Abstract
Communicating the design and results of Agent-Based Models (ABMs)
subject matter experts and other stakeholders can be challenging, which hinders participation and limits trust in the model. Advances in Generative AI
make it possible to create textual summaries to communicate ABMs, thus
complementing works on statistical and visualization techniques. While prior
work used Large-Language Models to translate conceptual models into text,
we extend this to simulation models such as ABMs.

We propose a fully

automated pipeline that extracts contextual information from ABMs in Netlogo and performs simulations, then summarizes them using state-of-the-art
LLMs along with abstractive and extractive summarization algorithms. Our
optimization considers three LLMs (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, DeepSeek)
and dierent prompt elements (setting a role, including an example, generating insights), which are analyzed via a factorial design of experiments over
ABMs previously published by various research groups. Our analysis reveals
that the summarization algorithm is the dominant driver of content-based
metrics (BLEU, METEOR, ROUGE), explaining up to 95% of variance,
while prompt elements such as insight or example mainly inuence the readability of the report.

Our work demonstrates the feasibility of generating

executive summaries from simulation models and exemplies the benets of
using the well-established techniques of designed experiments from the simulation literature to evaluate the interaction of prompt elements and LLMs.
While our method can contribute to making simulation insights more accessible to decision-makers and stakeholders beyond the modeling community,
our methods are currently limited to ABMs with sucient documentation to

1. Introduction
Explainability and transparency are important considerations in Modeling & Simulation (M&S) as they build trust and condence in the validity
of models, thus supporting the translation from model's insights into practice.

The creation of understandable narratives to accompany models is

particularly benecial for end-users who may not have the technical expertise to interpret raw simulation data directly or appreciate the limitations
of specic model-building processes [1]. Explainability and transparency are
multi-faceted, as they can be done at the local (e.g., sensitivity to changes
in individual parameters [2]) or global level (e.g., comparing point-estimates
from a simulation with ground truth), and they cover the production of a
model [3] (how was it created?) as well as the intended use of the nal product (Figure 1). When conceptual models strive to be comprehensive, they are
at risk of becoming spaghetti diagrams [4], thus turning into unintended
illustrations of complexity that oer little support for global explanations
of the model and hinder translations into practices. The Foresight Obesity
Map is a case in point, as it was depicted by researchers as an almost incomprehensible web of interconnectedness [in which] the scale and number
of interactions make it dicult to see how one might use it in any practical
way to develop systemic approaches [5], with others stating that the model
looks more like a spilled plate of spaghetti than anything of use to policymakers [6]. The solution is not necessarily to force a model into a reduced
scope or to omit mediating eects and multiple consequences [7], as oversimplied models may no longer be t for purpose [8]. For example, public
health models require the integration of biomedical, social, and environmental variables [9] so these models often need to be comprehensive and clearly
reect unintended consequences in order to serve their purpose, even if that
initially appears to come at the expense of explainability and transparency.
Given the necessity of keeping models t-for-purpose while addressing explainability and transparency, a possible solution could be that modelers provide all necessary descriptions. However, analyses of descriptions surround-

While ABMs are powerful tools to explore complex

2. Background
Our paper is motivated by the realization that simulation models such
as ABMs can be complex and produce large amounts of data, which need
to be succinctly conveyed to a variety of end-users (subsection 2.1).

We

propose using Large Language Models (LLMs), which have already been
employed in several simulation tasks (subsection 2.2).

We focus on using

LLMs to generate summaries, which can be evaluated both qualitatively
and quantitatively (subsection 2.3) in response to dierent prompt design
techniques (subsection 2.4).

2.1. Conveying Agent-Based Models to end-users
ABMs can capture interactions between multiple agents (characterized
by a number of dynamic attributes), over several steps (or `ticks'), through
a variety of scenarios that can be performed multiple times to account for
stochasticity.

The sheer volume and complexity of the data produced by

ABMs can overwhelm users, making it dicult to extract actionable insights
directly from the raw outputs [30]. In addition, the design of the ABM and
simulation can become complex, as it involves numerous agent attributes,
several data- and theory-driven rules, along with various scenarios consisting
of combinations of parameter values. This information can be comprehensively captured through standardized approaches to describe ABMs, such as

5

ODD [31] and its extensions [32, 33], and some aspects can be covered through
a UML diagram (emphasizing software design) or reporting standards such
as RAT-RS for data use in ABMs specically [34] or the STRESS guidelines
for simulations in general [35]. However, these standards and guidelines are
not systematically used by the modeling community [36] and they can be
challenging for stakeholders and model commissioners, who are not always
modelers but need to get a sense of what the model `does' and how to use it
without parsing large volumes of technical information.
Several works have sought to improve communication for the design of
ABMs and/or their simulation results, particularly for non-technical audiences. For example, Hall and Virrantaus used diagrams to convey the rules
of ABMs [37], which was more recently echoed by Szangolies and colleagues
in creating a visual version of the standardized ODD [38].

Visualizations

have been an important tool and research area to communicate ABM for
over a decade, across all phases of the modeling process [39], for instance
using the popular NetLogo software [40].

While statistical techniques are

commonplace in simulations (e.g., choosing a number of repeats for a target
condence interval), they often support modelers [41]. On occasions, statistics have been employed to convey ABMs to practitioners beyond M&S such
as statisticians [42] or a broader audience [27]. As shown in the next subsection, advances in GenAI have provided new opportunities to explain ABMs
as text, thus complementing studies on visualizations and statistics.

2.2. LLMs and Simulation
LLMs have intersected with simulations in several ways. While only one
task (simulation-to-text) is directly relevant to the present work, we briey
discuss the names and techniques involved in three related areas to avoid confusion. First, in

generative Agent-Based Modeling or `LLM-augmented social

simulations' [43], the decision-making modules of the agents are replaced or
extended with LLMs (Figure 2).

This allows agents to react to messages

and to explain their reasoning [44, 45], and it also transforms the challenge
of crafting behavioral rules onto a prompt engineering task that accounts
for the agents' attributes [46].

As pointed out by Larooij and Tornberg,

generative ABMs are still in a stage of early experimentation [47], with
prototypes emerging in late 2024 and early 2025. All ABMs used to generate
simulations in our paper are `traditional' as they use sets of rules to express
behavior rather than LLMs. Second, in

Agentic AI, the emphasis is on using

a set of specialized agents to interpret a textual request [48]. For example,

6

approach potential clients can trigger a market discovery agent that looks
for suitable companies, then another agent extracts email addresses, and a
third agent crafts tailored emails. In other words, agentic AI decomposes a
problem so that it can be solved by a set of specialized, autonomous entities.
The goals of the traditional ABMs studied in this paper are dierent, since
they provide simulations as decision-support tools so that end-users can estimate the eects of scenarios dened through input parameters. Third, there
is a wealth of studies on

generating simulations from text (Figure 2), drawing

from earlier works on extracting ABM concepts from narratives [49, 50], then
using questions and answering systems to automate the extraction [51, 52]
and nally generating simulation code in a target ABM platform such as

NetLogo [53, 54]. In this paper, we study the reciprocal transformation: instead of building an ABM from text, we use existing ABMs to generate a
textual description.

Figure 2: LLMs have been used to support several aspects of simulations, particularly to

As tools such as GPT make it possible to translate simulations into text,
the idea of using GPT to explain simulations was proposed [15, 55] and realized in a few studies. In particular, Jackson and Rolf noted the technical
complexity of simulations and translated them into natural language for domain experts, by translating the Python source of inventory management
systems into English. While the results use a repetitive style that does not
match the readability level expected of a report, this proof-of-concept demonstrated that GenAI could know enough about a simulation model in order to
depict it with factually correct sentences [56]. Gürcan also noted that agents
equipped with LLMs (i.e., generative ABM) could help with explainability,
as the agents can generate natural language explanations for their actions,
decisions, and the underlying mechanics of the simulation [43].

asking each agent to explain how it decided and acted [43]. There is thus a
need to distill the information, which we accomplish in this paper by using

2.3. Evaluating LLM-generated summaries
When the output from LLMs is too verbose for practical use, we can further request that the LLM summarizes it. Summarization algorithms fall into

extractive (copy-paste the most relevant parts of the
original text) or abstractive (synthesizing information by paraphrasing the
two broad categories:

text). Extractive summarization prioritizes factual content and oers computational eciency, making it well-suited for summarizing informative texts,
as illustrated by the well-known BERT [57] (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) algorithm. In contrast, abstractive summarization techniques aim to capture the essential meaning using novel phrasings,
as seen with BART [58] (Bidirectional and Autoregressive Transformers for
Pre-training).
Since summarizing is about communicating, it is important to identify
the audience and the format [59]: do we seek one paragraph for an email to a
modeler, a ve minutes elevator pitch to a potential model commissioner, or
a written overview for subject-matter experts?

Executive summaries are a

common format, found across application areas from military simulations [60]
to behavioral modeling [61]. Executive summaries typically require concise
and focused content yet powerful for the readers to take action [62], often
coming down to 1 or 2 pages with no more than 1000 words [63].
Evaluating the results produced by LLMs involves both quantitative (our
focus) and qualitative methods (described in supplementary section S1). To
eectively evaluate LLMs and avoid common pitfalls, it is essential to develop a comprehensive suite of metrics rather than relying on a single metric

Quantitative evaluation often use metrics such as BLEU, METEOR,
and ROUGE, which are standard in summarization tasks, to compare the
[64].

generated content against a ground truth [65, 66]. BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy) measures the precision of n-grams in the generated text
compared to a reference text, using a scale from 0 to 1, where higher scores
indicate better quality. Prior studies showed performances reaching 24.58%
for this metric on summaries from BART [67] and 63% from BERT [68] . METEOR (Metric for Evaluation of Translation with Explicit ORdering) evalu-

scores ranging from 0 to 1, with higher scores representing better quality.

the generated text and reference texts. It includes several variants, such as

2.4. Prompt engineering and experimental design
Eective prompt engineering is critical for leveraging the capabilities of
LLMs in generating high-quality explanations [73]. Rather than relying on
random attempts, prompts must be carefully designed to include essential
elements such as roles, examples, and insights. This structured approach ensures that the generated content is relevant and informative [74]. The design
of LLMs can be articulated along several dimensions [75], which can be binary
(e.g., whether to dening a role and style) or multi-valued (e.g., whether the
goal is expressed as looking-up information or creating). A notable challenge
is that having more prompt elements does not guarantee better results [76].
Intuitively, `throwing the kitchen sink' at the LLM may confuse it rather
than guide it.

Since we do not know

a priori which elements to include,

several prompts can be designed and their eects evaluated empirically.
The M&S studies that used prompt engineering have used several strategies to design their experiments. Several works have used the experimental
design of varying

one factor at a time [77, 78, 79], which involves systemat-

ically changing a single variable while keeping all other factors constant to

This simple design avoids the

complexity and costs of testing all possible combinations of multiple factors,
at the expense of missing how prompt choices inuence each other. Other

factors eciently at the expense of generating more prompts. For example,

binary factor) and whether to use a zero-shot approach that does not give
information or to provide metaproperties documentation (another binary fac2

3.1. Overview
Our methods are divided into three main parts (Figure 3).

First, we

obtain data about the ABM (subsection 3.2). Given a set of parameters, we
run several simulations to obtain results from the model. We also extract data
from the simulation le: we parse the documentation, the Graphical User

using this information (subsection 3.3). We transform simulation results into

A

summarization algorithm reduces the number of paragraphs to the length
suitable for an executive report. We optimize the prompt engineering process

Finally we evaluate the report

by human readers and by LLMs.

3.3.2. Post-processing
In line with best practices, we ask the LLM to rst generate a full response (i.e., without constraints on lengths), then we summarize it.

The

responses can be particularly long, since each variable is depicted as a visual
that gets transformed into text. For example, if an ABM has 15 variables,
then we create 15 CSV les (each comprised of several runs), leading to 15
complete descriptions through Prompt 2. Long document summarization is
its own challenge and has previously been addressed by a hierarchical approach, where each segment is summarized (e.g., 15 summaries) and then
pooled to create one summary [89].

We follow this two-step approach by

summarizing the description of each plot, which are further summarized into
a nal report consisting of one paragraph for the context and three paragraphs for the plots. We considered both extractive (BERT) and abstractive
(BART) techniques to generate the summaries (subsection 2.3). Because of
its extractive approach, BERT may combine `fragments' to form sentences.
This required further automated corrections including punctuation, spaces
in parentheses, hyphens, or setting the rst letter of each sentence to upper
case.

3.4. Evaluation
For each simulation model, a summary was jointly written by the authors
to serve as ground truth reference. We wrote the summary given the same
information as was available to the LLMs, including the time series from
each simulation run.

All summaries were written prior to generating text

via LLMs, thus ensuring that the ground truth reference provided by our

16

summary was not `contaminated' by sentences that we may read in the LLM
output.

The references are used to compute ve scores (subsection 2.3):

BLEU [90, 65, 91], METEOR [91], ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, ROUGE-L [90,
66, 91]. In addition to the ve reference-based scores, we use a reference-free
evaluation that computes the readability of the report.

This is evaluated

using the Flesch Readability Score [71].

5. Discussion

Our study automatically translated

well-documented ABMs into execu-

tive reports, which were evaluated both quantitatively (as detailed in this article) and qualitative (as available in the supplementary online material). Our
quantitative evaluations show that optimizing the conguration of LLMs,
summarization algorithms, and prompt elements can create report whose
readability oscillates between a college-educated audience or the general public (readability 50-70). From a quantitative standpoint, there is thus evidence
that the generated overviews of the ABMs are accessible, which was a challenge given that the readability of conceptual models once turned into text
was under 50 in a recent study [14]. We found that the more desirable reports were created through abstractive summarization, by taking a high-level
view of a model and rephrasing its content, rather than by extractive summarization in which existing bits and pieces of a long report are reorganized.
From a scoring viewpoint, abstractive summarization naturally results in
lower overlap scores between a generated summary and a target, since the

22

creative paraphrasing reduces the amount of direct matches even when the
meaning is preserved.

Our scores on n-gram matches (BLEU, METEOR,

ROUGE) are thus within expected ranges for abstractive summarization on
complex, technical content like ABM outputs.

Although we performed a

complementary qualitative evaluation to be comprehensive, the low consistency of ratings between human raters or between human raters and LLMs
on well-established metrics (coverage, faithfulness) reects documented challenges in the modeling literature, such as the notable dierences in parsing
information from reports and relating it to a model's structure [95].
While the literature on prompt engineering and the optimization of LLMbased systems predominantly uses simple experimental designs (e.g., varying
2
one factor at a time, or a limited 2 factorial design), we took a thorough
approach to analyzing the inuence of each design element and their conk
tributions through a factorial analysis with replication (2 r design). Our
analysis illustrates the benets of using a well-established approach to designed experiments in the eld of Generative AI, where it has not yet been
fully utilized.

Specic to our work, the analysis reveals that the choice of

summarization algorithms account for most of the output variance on n-gram
scores, which can be surprising given that the emphasis is often on the LLMs
or the prompt optimization.

The prompt elements aect readability and

interact with other controllable aspects of the pipeline, as some LLMs are
able to leverage examples in the prompt. Our evaluation showed that three
state-of-the-art LLMs (DeepSeek, GPT, Claude) have dierent benets, as
Claude's natural-sounding style favors readability, GPT's more formal descriptions tend to align with target summaries, and DeepSeek occasionally
provide unique performances.
The main limitation of our approach is that the ABMs must be accompanied by documentation that provides enough guidance to the generative
models. Solidly anchoring a summary in facts can only be achieved if the task
is strictly about translating rather than story-telling, which can hallucinate.
The simulation community regularly stresses the importance of documenting
the models [35] and repositories such as CoMSES provide a wealth of welldocumented ABMs. However, there is no guarantee that

any ABM comes

with sucient documentation, even if it is intended for use by a broad set
of stakeholders. An important objective for future works would be to identify documentations for models that are insuciently described. This may
be achieved in at least two ways.

First, clone detectors can realize that a

model (or parts thereof ) resembles other, well-known models thus some of

23

the documentation may be reused. The challenge would be to only reuse the
applicable parts of the documentation. Second, empirical studies on ABMs
have shown that related models (e.g., ABMs for social simulations) often
shared `building blocks' [96], for instance to simulate human mobility. Automatically identifying these building blocks within a simulation model could
thus generate some of the missing documentation.
While the integration of LLMs with simulation outputs is promising, our
study also raises two questions that can be investigated in future works.
First, our work used the documentation of the ABM as guidance for generative AI. However, a model may be described in a research article and/or
it may use the specialized vocabulary of its application domain.

The use

of retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) could organize this knowledge in a
database, thus providing supplemental guidance for generative AI. However,
additional studies are needed to identify which aspects to extract and store
in a database, or how to prioritize the database compared to the model's documentation or the LLM's own knowledge. Second, our analysis showed that
the optimal LLM depends on the ABM, for example as DeepSeek generates
summaries with the highest readability for one model while Claude is better
for the other two models. It would thus be valuable to identify

which features

of a ABM are best aligned with the capability of a LLM. Automatically supporting this alignment instead of performing a complete optimization study
each time would allow the audience of simulation models to quickly receive
the best report for the model of their choice.

6. Conclusion
By automatically translating well-documented Agent-Based Models into
concise executive reports, we lay the groundwork for integrating LLMs into
simulation workows to enhance transparency and stakeholder engagement.
Future work can explore expanding this approach and reuse our open-access
code for other simulation paradigms, or assess it across a broader range of
application domains. While our quantitative metrics show that the reports
have a sucient level of readability, empirical studies with stakeholders can
reveal whether the reports can bridge the gap between modelers and decisionmakers depending on the model and the readership.
