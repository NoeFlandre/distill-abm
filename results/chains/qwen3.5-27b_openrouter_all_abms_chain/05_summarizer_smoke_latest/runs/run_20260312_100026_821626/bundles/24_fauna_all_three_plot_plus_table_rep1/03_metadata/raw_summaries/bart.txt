 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based simulation . It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival . The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape .  The total energy available to forager bands begins at 2,000 units and follows a monotonic decline across the simulation timeline . The rate of energy depletion accelerates significantly after the initial 400 steps, averaging a decline of roughly 20 units per step in the latter half of the simulation .  The population of male megaherbivores begins at 125 individuals and undergoes a rapid decline over the first 500 time steps, dropping to fewer than 20 individuals in the majority of simulation runs . The population effectively reaches functional extinction by step 1000, with the aggregate mean dropping below 2.6 individuals . The oscillation period of roughly 63 steps suggests cyclic dynamics, potentially linked to seasonal resource regeneration or predator-prey cycles, which briefly sustain small populations before the system fails .  The total energy available in prey animals within the simulation begins at approximately 16,667 units at the start of the simulation . By 350 steps, the total energy falls to approximately 4,000 units, marking a period of high volatility where the population struggles to maintain metabolic stability . For decision makers, this suggests that in systems with high predation efficiency and limited refuge capacity, extinction events can occur almost deterministically .  The vegetation levels remain remarkably stable over the long term, with the final simulation value at 32.99%, nearly identical to the starting point . These dynamics imply that the current combination of 20 human foragers and initial megaherbivore density does not lead to landscape degradation .
