 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction . It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival . The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape .  The cumulative number of male prey killed exhibits a rapid, monotonic increase from the start of the simulation, rising from 0 to approximately 110 individuals within the first 600 time steps . The data indicates that the vast majority of male megaherbivores were removed from the population early in the simulation timeline, with the total mortality count plateauing around 119 to 120 individuals for the latter half .  The female megaherbivore population begins at an initial mean of 125 individuals and exhibits a rapid and consistent decline across all 40 simulation runs . By step 305, the population has already dropped sharply, indicating that the combined pressure of human hunting and environmental constraints led to an immediate reduction in the herd . The data culminates in a complete collapse where the minimum value of 0.00 is reached at the final index, signifying total extinction of the prey population .  At the initial time step (tick 0), the mean total energy across 120 simulation runs is approximately 16666.67 units . By tick 385, the mean energy decreases significantly to approximately 4102.18 units . The data series concludes at tick 2311 with the total energy reaching 0.0 units, indicating the complete loss of female energy reserves in the population by the end of the observed period .
