The cumulative number of male prey animals killed by forager bands increases steadily from the start, reaching approximately 100 by step 500. This rapid initial rise suggests high hunting efficiency and a strong initial foraging pressure on male prey. After step 500, the rate of increase slows, and the cumulative number stabilizes between 110 and 125 by step 2000. This stabilization indicates that hunting pressure on males either saturates or that the male population becomes depleted, limiting further kills. The observed dynamics suggest that early-stage hunting strategies significantly impact male prey populations, with diminishing returns as time progresses. Decision makers should consider the timing and intensity of initial hunting efforts, as they critically influence prey population dynamics and resource availability.
