The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against macro level data. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits an initial rapid increase from a starting value of 5.29 to 1456.59 by time step 32. This trajectory suggests agents initially adopted alternative milk options quickly, likely driven by early shifts in social influence or perception. The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits a consistent upward trend, starting at an initial value of 1.0 and gradually increasing to 1.0502. The mean percentage difference in choice function scores among agents exhibits a clear upward trend over time. The mean deviation between agents’ held environmental values and the implied environmental values from their milk choices begins at 0.0 at step 0, rises rapidly during the initial phase, reaches a peak of 13.4756 at step 13, and then remains relatively stable with minor fluctuations until step 32, where it ends at 13.4186. This pattern implies that long-term decisions are moderated by entrenched habits and social dynamics.
