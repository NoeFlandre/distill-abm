The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based simulation. It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival. The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape. The cumulative number of male prey killed begins at zero and increases sharply during the first 400 time steps, reaching approximately 100 individuals by step 400. The rate of increase slows significantly after step 400, as the cumulative count plateaus around 119 to 122 individuals between steps 1200 and 1800. The data shows a dominant oscillation period of 63 steps, which may correspond to seasonal cycles or behavioral periodicity in foraging activity. The population of male megaherbivores begins at 125 individuals and undergoes a rapid, precipitous decline over the first 500 time steps, dropping to fewer than 20 individuals in the majority of simulation runs. The population reaches absolute extinction (zero individuals) by approximately time step 2300. The total energy available in the male megaherbivore population begins at 12,500 units and exhibits a rapid decline over the initial 100 time steps. By step 2,284, the total energy reserves for the male population reach zero and remain there for the remainder of the simulation. For decision makers, this suggests that in systems with high predation efficiency and limited refuge capacity, extinction events can occur almost deterministically. Following this event the vegetation recovers, returning to levels near 33% by the 730th time step. Subsequent fluctuations remain much smaller in magnitude, generally staying above 30%. The overall stability observed after the first year indicates that the system reaches a dynamic equilibrium where the rate of grass consumption is balanced by its growth rate.
