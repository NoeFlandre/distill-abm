Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Hunters follow dynamic optimal foraging theory, balancing energy status, prey value, density, and costs. The cumulative number of female prey killed by foragers increases sharply from the start of the simulation, rising from 0 to approximately 100 within the first 500 time steps. The growth rate slows noticeably between 500 and 1500 steps, as the system approaches saturation. This pattern suggests that male-specific hunting pressure is intense initially but diminishes as resources become scarce. The population of male prey animals begins at 125 individuals and undergoes a rapid decline. By step 1159, the trend flattens completely, with the population remaining at zero for the remainder of the simulation. This total extinction suggests that the initial forager density and movement costs create a hunting pressure regime that the male population cannot withstand. Statistical analysis confirms a consistent downward trend throughout the simulation, with no significant recovery phases observed. The final energy state approaches zero, indicating total depletion of the female population's energy reserves. Decision-makers should consider these dynamics when evaluating hunting regulations and habitat management strategies to prevent similar outcomes.
