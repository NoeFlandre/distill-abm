The average total number of livestock, including destocked animals, across all households begins at 90 at the start of the simulation. Over the first 30 time steps, this number decreases sharply to approximately 40. This initial decline suggests an active response by farmers to reduce herd sizes, likely due to insufficient grass availability and the risk-averse nature of their decision-making processes as defined by the simulation settings. From time step 30 to 40, the livestock numbers remain relatively stable near the 40 level, indicating a period where farmers have adjusted their herd sizes to the prevailing grazing conditions. Between time steps 40 and 60, a divergence occurs in the trends observed across different simulation runs. Some runs show a gradual increase in livestock numbers, reaching up to approximately 60 by time step 80, while others remain stable around 45 to 50. This divergence implies that varying learning behaviors and interactions among farmers begin to influence herd management strategies differently as the simulation progresses. By the end of the simulation at time step 100, livestock numbers stabilize, with some runs maintaining around 60 animals and others hovering near 45 to 50. This stabilization suggests that farmers eventually find a balance between herd size and available grazing resources, influenced by their adaptive learning behaviors and interactions within the community. Decision makers should consider these trends to understand the potential outcomes of different learning and interaction strategies on livestock management and pasture sustainability.
