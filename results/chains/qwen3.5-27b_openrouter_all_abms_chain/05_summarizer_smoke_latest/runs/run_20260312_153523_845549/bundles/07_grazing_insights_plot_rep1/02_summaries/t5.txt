The RAGE model is a multi-agent simulation model that captures feedbacks between pastures, livestock and household livelihood in a common property grazing system. It implements three stylized household behavioral types (traditional TRAD, maximizer MAX and satisficer SAT) these types can be compared regarding their long-term social-ecological consequences. The mean household initial risk attitude across all social learning agents remains constant throughout the simulation period spanning 100 time steps. The recorded values range from approximately -0.11 to 0.17, with distinct clusters around 0.00, 0.05, 0.11, and 0.14. There is no variation over time, suggesting that initial risk dispositions are set as fixed parameters and do not evolve during the simulation. The average number of livestock destocked by households exhibits a sharp decline from the initial simulation steps to approximately step 10. Following this initial drop, the average destocking rate continues to decrease, albeit more gradually, until it stabilizes between steps 35 and 40. This stabilization indicates that households have likely adjusted their livestock management strategies to better align with the resource availability. Prolonged variability and decline in some runs could imply long-term sustainability of the E-RO strategy is influenced by other factors such as learning by doing or social learning (E-RO-SL1) strategies. Initial variability suggests the importance of early interventions or support to stabilize outcomes. Eventual stabilization indicates that under certain conditions, the strategy can remain viable.
