The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against macro level data. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at zero and remains at that level during the first time step. By the final time steps, consumption stabilizes at approximately 300 units, suggesting that the majority of agents have transitioned to alternative milk options. The overall trajectory shows a transition from low consumption to significantly higher levels, with an initial surge, a period of consolidation, and a final phase of increased adoption. The data across simulation runs shows very little variance throughout the timeline, indicating a highly consistent pattern of habit formation for these milk types among the agents. From step 10 onwards, deviation stabilizes and remains relatively constant around 13, suggesting that agents have reached a new equilibrium where their environmental values and milk choices are consistently misaligned. This stabilization may indicate that the habit and social influence factors have effectively balanced the agents' environmental perceptions.
