This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. The average deviation from the recommended number of livestock exhibits a distinct cyclical pattern. Initial decline is observed during the first twenty-six time steps, with the metric reaching its minimum value of 84,114.07 at time step 26. The rate of increase is particularly notable between time steps 44 and 54, where the slope reaches approximately 1,909.59. This pattern suggests an initial period of resource depletion followed by adaptive management strategies within the farming population. At the onset of the simulation, the inequality is negligible, starting at a value of approximately 0.0000. By the conclusion of the run at timestep 100, the coefficient rises to 0.0726, indicating a gradual divergence in livestock wealth among the agents. The mean number of households exhibiting social learning behavior across the simulation period is approximately 27.43, with a standard deviation of 8.92. The E-RO household count is statistically significant and increasing, reaching a maximum value of 21.1 households at timestep 8. The data indicates a long-term attrition of the non-learning strategy, with the population decreasing by roughly 26.5% from start to finish.
