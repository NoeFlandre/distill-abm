The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at 5.29 units at the start of the simulation, reflecting the initial market conditions in 1974 where whole milk was dominant. Consumption increases rapidly during the initial phase, exhibiting a significant upward trend from the first to the tenth time step. This early growth aligns with agents triggering their disposition to consider alternatives based on perceived health and environmental characteristics. Between time steps 10 and 24, the rate of adoption slows, with values fluctuating around a mean of approximately 1220 units. A notable peak occurs at time step 11, reaching 1265.47 units, followed by minor variations such as a local minimum of 1183.39 units at time step 18. This plateau suggests that habit strength and social conformity temporarily stabilize consumer choices, preventing further immediate substitution. In the final phase, from time step 25 to the end, the consumption volume resumes a significant increasing trend, reaching a maximum of 1456.59 units at time step 32. This late-stage acceleration implies that accumulated social influence and the evaluation of previous choices eventually reinforce the shift away from incumbent whole milk varieties. For decision makers, the trajectory highlights that while initial interventions yield quick adoption, policy support during the middle stability period is crucial to maintain momentum against entrenched habits before long-term social norms drive final market saturation.
