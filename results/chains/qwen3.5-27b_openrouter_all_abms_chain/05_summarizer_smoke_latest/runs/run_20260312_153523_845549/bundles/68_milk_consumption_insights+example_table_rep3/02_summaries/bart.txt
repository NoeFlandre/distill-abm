The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at 5.29 units at the start of the observation period. A rapid increase in consumption occurs over the first ten time steps, driven by the initial breaking of incumbent habits and the influence of health perception parameters. The data indicates that while initial health perceptions drive early adoption, sustained increases in alternative product consumption rely on reinforcing social norms. The average weekly consumption of whole milk begins at 2654.81 units at the start of the simulation, reflecting the dominant market position in 1974. Throughout the 33 time steps, the metric follows a sharp and sustained downward trajectory, ending at a minimum value of 253.10 at time step 32. The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents initiates at 0.0000 and follows a consistent upward trajectory, reaching a maximum value of 0.7508 by the final time step of 32. This trend implies that as agents engage with alternative milk options, the habitual reinforcement for these choices strengthens over time. The model suggests that without altering the strength of existing habits or the weight of social influence, consumer choices will continue to reflect a disconnect from environmental values. Agency agents may hold environmental concerns, but these are consistently overridden by other factors such as habit strength or health perceptions regarding milk types.
