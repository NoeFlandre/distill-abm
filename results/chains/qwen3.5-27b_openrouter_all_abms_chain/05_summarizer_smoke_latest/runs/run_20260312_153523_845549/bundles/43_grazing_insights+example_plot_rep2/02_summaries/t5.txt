The RAGE model is a multi-agent simulation model that captures feedbacks between pastures, livestock and household livelihood in a common property grazing system. It implements three stylized household behavioral types (traditional TRAD, maximizer MAX and satisficer SAT) these types can be compared regarding their long-term social-ecological consequences. The mean household initial risk attitude for all social learning agents remains constant across all 100 time steps. Multiple distinct levels of risk attitudes are observed, ranging approximately from -0.11 to 0.18. These initial risk attitudes serve as a static determinant of how agents perceive uncertainty and deviate from recommended livestock numbers. A gradual increase is observed, with the metric rising to approximately 50.0. The final values indicate that households have reached a new equilibrium in their livestock management practices. This trend highlights the importance of adaptive management practices to ensure herd recovery and stability. The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households increases steadily over the simulation period, starting from 0 at the initial time step. By time step 20, the rate of destocking slows down but continues to rise, reaching values between 60 and 170 by the end of the simulation at time step 100. The subsequent slower increase indicates a stabilization in livestock management practices.
