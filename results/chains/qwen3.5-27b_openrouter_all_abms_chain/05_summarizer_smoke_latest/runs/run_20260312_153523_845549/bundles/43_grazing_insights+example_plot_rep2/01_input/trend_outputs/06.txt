The average number of livestock destocked by households begins near zero and rises sharply in the first 5 time steps, peaking at approximately 7.5. This rapid increase is likely due to initial resource scarcity, as households with high livestock holdings face immediate pressure to adjust their herds to available pasture conditions. Following the peak, there is a steep decline over the next 15 to 20 time steps, with values falling below 2. This reduction indicates that households either adapted their herd sizes or benefited from resource recovery, possibly due to rainfall variability and vegetation regrowth dynamics inherent in the model. Between time steps 20 and 40, the destocking rate stabilizes with minor fluctuations, hovering between 1 and 2. This phase suggests a balance between resource availability and herd management practices, where households have adjusted their strategies to mitigate losses. From time step 40 onwards, the destocking rate remains relatively low and stable, with some runs showing near-zero values by time step 100. This long-term stabilization implies that households have successfully managed their livestock to align with pasture capacity, minimizing the need for forced destocking. The low variability in the later stages reflects a convergence toward sustainable herd sizes across most simulation runs. Decision-makers can infer that initial resource shocks may cause significant short-term losses, but adaptive behaviors and ecological recovery can lead to long-term stability. However, the presence of occasional spikes in destocking even after stabilization highlights the ongoing vulnerability of the system to environmental fluctuations, underscoring the importance of risk-aware management strategies.
