A distinct inflection occurs around timestep 49, marking a transition into a sustained period of growth. From timestep 33 through the final timestep 100, the biomass demonstrates a consistent and significant increasing trend. By the end of the simulation period, the average biomass reaches 60,535.28, representing a substantial recovery and increase from the initial state. In the context of the model, this trajectory indicates that the combination of behavioral types, including those with learning capabilities, led to improved pasture conditions over the long term despite the absence of enforced resting periods. The initial dip reflects the time required for agents to adapt their stocking decisions in response to uncertainty and resource state. The subsequent growth implies that learning mechanisms, such as adjusting livestock numbers based on observed pasture states, allow households to reduce grazing pressure sufficiently to promote vegetation recovery. For decision makers, these results highlight that adaptive management strategies can yield positive ecological outcomes even without rigid regulatory controls like mandatory resting, provided that agents possess the capacity to learn from past outcomes and environmental feedbacks. However, the initial period of decline underscores the importance of supporting stakeholders during the transition phase before the benefits of adaptive behavior materialize.
