The trajectory of the E-RO household count initiates at 20.00 and experiences a transient surge, peaking at 21.10 at time step 8. Following this initial rise, the series enters a prolonged phase of decline, stabilizing at 16.37 on average and concluding at 14.70 by time step 100. Statistical evaluation reveals a significant negative trend (p < 0.05) for the majority of the simulation period, confirmed by rolling Mann-Kendall tests which show significant decreasing slopes for the first approximately 70 timesteps. Afterward, the trend significance dissipates, indicating the system reaches a lower dynamic equilibrium. Structural change points are detected around timesteps 20 and 45, marking shifts in the rate of population change.

For grazing system management, the persistent attrition of E-RO agents suggests that baseline, non-adaptive management strategies are less persistent over time in this environment. The stabilization in the final quarter of the simulation implies that the system self-regulates to a state with a smaller proportion of non-learning agents. Decision-makers should interpret this as an indication that static management approaches may naturally diminish without intervention, suggesting a potential advantage to integrating adaptive learning mechanisms to maintain herd sizes and pasture utilization efficiency in the long term.
