 This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors . The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time .  The average total livestock population across all households follows a pronounced U-shaped trajectory over the 100-year simulation period . The average reserve biomass demonstrates a non-monotonic trajectory across the 100 timesteps . For land managers, this pattern highlights that adaptive management strategies may require an initial period of resource drawdown before achieving long-term sustainability gains .  The Gini coefficient measuring inequality in livestock distribution begins at zero and exhibits a consistent, statistically significant increasing trend throughout the 100-year period . The average cumulative livestock destocking metric initiates at 0.0 at the start of the simulation and concludes at 96.78 at timestep 100 . For decision makers, this pattern suggests that initial herd sizing exceeds carrying capacity .  The persistent attrition of E-RO agents suggests that baseline, non-adaptive management strategies are less persistent over time in this environment . Decision-makers should interpret this as an indication static management approaches may naturally diminish without intervention, suggesting a potential advantage to integrating adaptive learning mechanisms .
