 This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors . The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time . The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock .  The average total number of livestock, including healthy livestock and destocked animals, starts at 90 across all households . A significant decline occurs from the beginning of the simulation until approximately timestep 37, where the average reaches its lowest point of approximately 41.5 . The trajectory suggests that while the initial period involves substantial herd reduction, the system stabilizes and subsequently supports a modest recovery in livestock numbers .  The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households showed a consistently increasing trend over the 100 time steps . The data indicates a strong and significant upward trend throughout the first 45 time steps, followed by a phase of relative stability . This suggests that once social learning is adopted, it becomes a stable practice for a majority of the households within the simulation environment .
