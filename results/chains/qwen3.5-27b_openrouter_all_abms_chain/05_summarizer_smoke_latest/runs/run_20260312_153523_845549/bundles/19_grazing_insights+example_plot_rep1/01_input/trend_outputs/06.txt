The average number of livestock destocked by households begins near zero and rises sharply in the first 5 time steps, peaking at approximately 7.5 livestock per household. This rapid increase suggests that the initial livestock stocking levels exceeded the carrying capacity of the pastures under the simulated conditions, forcing immediate destocking across all households. Following this peak, there is a steep decline in destocking over the next 20 to 25 time steps, dropping from 7.5 to between 0.5 and 1.5 livestock per household. This decline indicates that households rapidly adjusted their stocking rates to better match pasture availability. Between time steps 25 and 100, the average destocking stabilizes at a low level, generally fluctuating between 0 and 1 livestock per household. The convergence of these trends suggests that households have reached a sustainable equilibrium in their stocking decisions relative to resource constraints. Some variability is observed in the first 30 steps, but this diminishes significantly afterward, pointing to a stabilization of decision-making processes. Decision-makers should interpret these dynamics as evidence that initial resource overuse is corrected relatively quickly when households have the capacity to adapt, leading to a steady and low level of livestock loss in the long term.
