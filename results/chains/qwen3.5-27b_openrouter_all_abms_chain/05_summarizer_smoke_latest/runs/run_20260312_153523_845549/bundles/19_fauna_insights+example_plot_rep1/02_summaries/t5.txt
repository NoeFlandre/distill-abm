The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Cumulative number of male prey animals killed starts at zero and rises sharply, reaching approximately 110 to 120 individuals by step 500 across simulation runs. This rapid increase suggests that the initial phase of the simulation is marked by intensive hunting pressure on male ungulates, likely due to their high food value relative to processing costs and their availability in the landscape. The trend plateaus between steps 500 and 1000, with only marginal increases in the number of females killed. From 400 to 800 time steps, the rate of increase in deaths slows. The cumulative death count plateaus between 225 and 250, suggesting that the prey population has reached a quasi-equilibrium state where predation and natural mortality are offset by reproduction or reduced forager impact. The total energy of female prey animals starts at approximately 12,000 units. This initial sharp decrease could be attributed to high consumption rates by foragers combined with limited fodder regeneration. However, this is followed by a continued decline, with most runs reaching near-zero levels by step 1,500.
