The RAGE model is a multi-agent simulation model that captures feedbacks between pastures, livestock and household livelihood in a common property grazing system. It implements three stylized household behavioral types (traditional TRAD, maximizer MAX and satisficer SAT) these types can be compared regarding their long-term social-ecological consequences. The average household risk attitude within the extension agent population starts at 0.0074 at the beginning of the simulation period. The metric maintains a steady value of approximately 0,0074, showing no deviation from this mean. Across all time steps, the variance is effectively zero. Between time steps 45 and 60, the values rise, with the mean climbing back toward 50 units. By time step 100, the metric stabilizes at approximately 47.5 units, while the aggregate mean follows a U-shaped trajectory, the individual simulation runs show increasing separation. This bifurcation likely reflects the heterogeneous risk dispositions. From time step 40 to 63, the metric continues to climb but at a reduced pace, stabilizing around a mean of 32.0667 households by the end of the simulation. There is no evidence of decline or plateauing after the initial rapid growth, suggesting persistent engagement in social learning behaviors.
