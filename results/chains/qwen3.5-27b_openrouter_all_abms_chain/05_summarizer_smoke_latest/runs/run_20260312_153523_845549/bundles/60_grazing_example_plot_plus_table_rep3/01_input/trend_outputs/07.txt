The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock, measured by the Gini coefficient, starts at 0.0000 and exhibits a consistent upward trend, reaching a final value of 0.0726 at the end of the simulation. The mean value across the simulation period is 0.0302, with a median of 0.0229, indicating that the distribution of inequality is skewed by a subset of outcomes that achieve higher divergence. Initially, between time steps 0 and 50, the increase in inequality is gradual, rising from 0.0000 to approximately 0.035. This early phase likely reflects the initial stabilization of herds as households with different learning capabilities (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) adjust their stocking rates based on feedback from their local environments and neighbors. 

From time step 50 onwards, the rate of inequality growth accelerates significantly, with the Gini coefficient climbing to approximately 0.06 by time step 80 and peaking near 0.09 in the upper range of simulation runs. This acceleration is marked by change points identified at time steps 51 and 71, where the trajectory of the data shifts to steeper slopes. This divergence suggests that as the simulation progresses, small differences in household risk attitudes (represented by the r-parameter) and learning mechanisms become amplified, leading to varying herd sizes and livestock health outcomes. The rolling Mann-Kendall tests confirm that this increasing trend is statistically significant across all time windows.

By the final time step of 100, the distribution of healthy livestock has become highly unequal, with some simulation runs showing a Gini coefficient exceeding 0.09, while others remain closer to 0.03. The presence of inflection points between time steps 8 and 25 indicates that the dynamics of herd growth and resource competition were not linear, likely driven by the interaction of rainfall variability (mean 200, std 100) and the varying vegetation growth rates. The oscillation indices observed near time step 85 suggest that towards the end of the simulation, the rate of inequality accumulation fluctuates, potentially due to saturation effects or the convergence of herd sizes among certain behavioral types as they approach carrying capacity.
