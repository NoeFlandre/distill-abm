The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent begins at 2654.81 at the initial time step and concludes at 253.10 by the final time step, representing a substantial decline over the 33 simulated periods. This trajectory reflects the model's aim to reproduce the historical shift in the British public's milk preferences from whole milk to lower-fat varieties between 1974 and 2005. The initial high value corresponds with the parameter setting of incumbent-initial-habit at 10.0, indicating strong existing preference for whole milk among the 1,000 agents at the start.

The decline is not monotonic; rolling trend analysis identifies significant decreasing trends in the first four windows, covering indices 0 through 14, followed by a period of less significant change before returning to a decreasing trend in the window covering indices 20 through 24. Inflection points occur at indices 5, 13, 16, 19, 24, 25, 27, and 30, marking shifts in the rate of consumption reduction. Local maxima appear at index 19 with a value of 773.96, index 27 with 620.98, and index 30 with 335.18, suggesting temporary resistances to the overall trend of adoption. Conversely, local minima occur at indices 18, 26, and 29.

These fluctuations may be attributed to the interplay between habit persistence, social influence within the Watts-Strogatz network, and the disposition mechanisms that trigger agents to consider alternatives. The dominant oscillation period of 15.00 indicates cyclical behavior in the collective decision-making process. Ultimately, the continuous downward pressure on whole milk consumption signifies that agents increasingly satisfy the disposition requirement to evaluate and switch to alternative milk types, influenced by perceived health and environmental characteristics and social norms.
