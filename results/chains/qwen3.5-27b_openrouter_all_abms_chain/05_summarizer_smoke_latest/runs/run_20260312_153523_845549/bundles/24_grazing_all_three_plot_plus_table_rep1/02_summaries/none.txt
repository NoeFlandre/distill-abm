Context and Goals

This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. In this version, each farmer stays on their own plot of land. They decide how many animals to keep based on how the grass is doing and what others are doing. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. There are three main ways farmers behave: baseline (E-RO), learning from their own results (E-LBD), and learning from neighbors (E-RO-SL1). The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock.

The average initial risk attitude for social learning agents remains fixed at approximately 0.0074 throughout the entire simulation duration. This metric exhibits zero variance, with no fluctuations, peaks, or trends observable over the 100 time steps. The stability of this value indicates that the initial disposition to deviate from recommended livestock numbers is a static parameter for these agents, established at the start of the simulation and not subject to change during the run. For decision makers, this implies that while social learning agents may adapt their behaviors based on environmental feedback and neighbor interactions, their fundamental risk tolerance baseline remains constant, ensuring a consistent underlying behavioral profile across the simulation period.

The average household risk attitude begins near 0.01 at the start of the simulation and climbs steadily over the first 33 time steps to reach a maximum value of approximately 0.38. This initial upward trend suggests that households are becoming more risk-seeking as they adapt to the grazing environment or react to peer behaviors. Following this peak around time step 33, the metric reverses course, entering a significant decline that stabilizes around time step 50. By the end of the simulation, the average risk attitude settles near 0.19. The transition from a high-risk posture to a more conservative stance likely indicates a shift in herd management strategies as farmers observe the consequences of overstocking and adjust their herd sizes to match pasture availability. Decision makers should note that initial high-risk behaviors are transient; households eventually converge toward lower-risk attitudes, implying that long-term sustainability relies on the system's capacity to correct risk-taking behaviors through experience.

The average reserve biomass in the grazing system begins at 90,000 and experiences a steady decline for the first 26 time steps, reaching a minimum of approximately 84,114. This initial reduction likely reflects the period where livestock grazing pressure exceeds biomass regeneration, particularly as the farmer agents adjust their strategies without immediate reinforcement from neighbors. Following this depletion phase, the biomass stabilizes briefly between steps 26 and 40 before initiating a sharp, non-linear growth phase around step 48. This rapid increase coincides with a shift in grazing behavior, where the agents likely reduced livestock numbers based on the initial scarcity of resources or the observation of neighboring strategies, allowing the vegetation to recover and regenerate. By the final time step, the biomass reaches a plateau of roughly 121,073, surpassing the initial conditions. For decision-makers, this trajectory implies that a period of resource scarcity and behavioral adjustment is necessary to trigger the positive feedback loops required for ecosystem recovery and sustainable stocking rates.

The average amount of actual available green biomass starts at 45000 and experiences a slight initial increase before declining to a minimum value of 42057 at approximately step 27. This early decline suggests that grazing pressure from the livestock population initially outpaced the regrowth capacity of the pasture. Following this minimum, the biomass exhibits a significant and continuous upward trend, accelerating notably between steps 33 and 54. This sustained increase implies that the farming strategies, particularly the E-RO behavior which prioritizes resting land or reducing herd sizes when conditions are poor, effectively reduced grazing pressure, allowing the ecosystem to recover and accumulate biomass. By the end of the simulation at step 100, the biomass reaches a maximum of 60535. The final values are substantially higher than the initial state, indicating that the system stabilizes at a high carrying capacity. The variance among individual simulation runs is observable, but the aggregate trend remains a robust recovery followed by stabilization, suggesting that these land management behaviors lead to long-term pasture health despite initial resource depletion.

The average total number of livestock, including destocked animals, starts at 90.0 at the beginning of the simulation and declines rapidly over the first 40 time steps, reaching a minimum value of approximately 41.0 around step 37. This initial sharp decline reflects a significant reduction in herd sizes as farmers adjust to the grazing conditions and management strategies. The decline is steep initially, with a Mann-Kendall slope of -3.470 in the first 10 steps, indicating a rapid decrease in livestock numbers. 

After reaching the minimum around step 37, there is a period of stabilization or slight fluctuation until about step 55. During this phase, the trend shows no significant direction, with a slope close to zero and a non-significant p-value. This suggests a temporary equilibrium in livestock numbers as farmers possibly reassess their strategies or adapt to the current conditions.

From step 55 onwards, the trend shifts to an increase, with a steady rise in livestock numbers continuing until the end of the simulation at step 100. The Mann-Kendall test indicates a significant increasing trend with a slope of 0.313 in the first phase of increase and maintaining a positive trend with decreasing slopes in subsequent phases. By the end of the simulation, the average livestock number rises to approximately 60.0.

The overall pattern suggests that farmers initially reduce their livestock numbers significantly, possibly due to resource constraints or the adoption of more conservative grazing strategies. After a period of adjustment, farmers begin to rebuild their herds, indicating a gradual return to more stable or increased livestock levels. This recovery could be attributed to improved resource management or the effectiveness of learning and adaptation strategies among the farmers.

For decision-makers, this trend highlights the importance of initial resource management and the potential for recovery and growth in livestock numbers through adaptive strategies. The initial decline suggests the need for support during the adjustment period, while the subsequent increase indicates that effective management and learning can lead to sustainable herd growth.

The average number of livestock destocked by households begins at zero and spikes sharply to a maximum of 6.61 animals at time step 3. This rapid escalation indicates an immediate and severe pressure on resources as households attempt to align their livestock numbers with initial forage availability. Following this peak, destocking levels decline steeply, reaching 3.37 animals by step 9 and 2.74 animals by step 12, before settling below 1.5 animals by step 17. This downward trend reflects a correction period where households adjust their herds to sustainable levels after the initial resource shock. By step 30, the destocking rate stabilizes, with values fluctuating around 0.5 to 1.0 for the remainder of the simulation, ending at 0.31 animals at step 100. The data indicates that after the initial adjustment phase, the system reaches a quasi-equilibrium where minor resource constraints persist but do not necessitate large-scale reductions in livestock. These findings suggest that early intervention strategies or initial resource assessments are critical, as the most significant livestock losses occur within the first few time steps before management behaviors stabilize.

The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock, measured by the Gini coefficient, starts at 0.0000 at the beginning of the simulation and rises to a value of 0.0726 by the final time step. The data exhibits a consistent and significant increasing trend throughout the 100 time steps, as indicated by the Mann-Kendall analysis across all rolling windows. In the initial phase (steps 1–40), the coefficient remains relatively low, hovering near zero, which suggests that livestock resources are initially distributed fairly equally among households. However, starting around step 40, the rate of increase accelerates, and by the final period, the inequality has grown notably. This shift is marked by several inflection points, notably at steps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, 37, and 57, where the slope of the trend changes, reflecting adjustments in the herd management behaviors of different farmers. By step 100, the spread between the lowest and highest inequality outcomes becomes visible, with some scenarios showing values exceeding 0.090, while others remain below 0.030. This divergence indicates that as the simulation progresses, the distinct behavioral strategies of farmers—ranging from baseline decision-making to learning from experience or neighbors—lead to increasingly divergent outcomes in herd health. The presence of change points at steps 51, 71, and 100 further highlights periods of structural shift in how inequality evolves. For decision-makers, this trend suggests that over time, reliance on varying learning strategies may lead to greater disparity in livestock health, potentially necessitating interventions to ensure equitable access to resources or support mechanisms for less successful farmers.

The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero across all simulation runs and rises sharply between time steps 7 and 30, reaching a peak average value of approximately 32 households by time step 63. This rapid increase suggests that, under the specified model conditions, social learning behavior spreads quickly among the population during the initial phase. The trend then slows, with the aggregate count stabilizing for the remainder of the simulation, indicating that social learning adoption reaches a saturation point. The inflection points at time steps 28 and 33 coincide with the transition from rapid growth to gradual stabilization, implying a shift in the dynamics of information exchange or behavioral adjustment among households. This pattern of initial rapid uptake followed by stability is consistent with scenarios where social influence drives early adoption of new practices, which then levels off as the majority of households adapt. For decision makers, this implies that social learning mechanisms are effective in the short term for spreading new grazing practices, but long-term strategies may need to address barriers preventing full adoption or encourage sustained engagement.

The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households increases steadily over the simulation period, starting at 0.00 at time step 0 and reaching 96.78 by time step 100. The trend shows a consistent upward trajectory with no detected peaks or valleys, indicating that destocking activities accumulate without significant pauses or reductions throughout the simulation. Multiple inflection points are identified between time steps 45 and 75, reflecting changes in the rate of destocking. These inflection points suggest that the pace of destocking may be influenced by dynamic factors such as varying livestock management decisions or environmental conditions. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms a statistically significant increasing trend across all time windows, with the initial rate of increase being higher in the early stages (slope of 4.956) and gradually stabilizing (slope of 0.311) as the simulation progresses. This deceleration in the rate of increase might indicate that households reach a saturation point in their destocking strategies or adapt to stable grazing conditions. The standard deviation of 20.30 underscores considerable variability among individual household trajectories, implying that decision-making heterogeneity plays a critical role in destocking patterns. Decision makers should note that while cumulative destocking is a robust trend, the significant variability suggests tailored interventions may be necessary to address specific household needs and behaviors.

The number of households following the non-learning E-RO strategy begins at 20 and experiences a slight increase during the initial phase, reaching a maximum of approximately 21.1 at step 8. Following this brief surge, the count enters a sustained downward trend that continues through step 70. By step 100, the average number of E-RO households stabilizes at a mean of approximately 14.7, with a range between 14.6 and 21.1 observed across the simulation. The initial increase suggests that the baseline non-learning approach may yield short-term stability or perceived benefits that attract adherence. However, the subsequent and significant decline indicates that, over time, agents utilizing learning strategies or experiencing negative outcomes associated with the non-learning baseline are likely shifting away from the E-RO strategy. Decision-makers should anticipate that while non-learning strategies may persist or grow initially, they are unlikely to remain the dominant approach in a dynamic system where adaptive behaviors evolve.
