 Simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors . The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time . The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock .  The average amount of available green biomass across all grazing patches initiates the simulation at 45,000.0 units . The average livestock count per household begins at 90.00 units at the onset of the simulation . A pronounced and statistically significant downward trend dominates the first 32 time steps . This decline continues until the metric reaches its minimum value of 41.01 units at time step 37 .  The average cumulative livestock destocking initiates at zero and progresses steadily to a final value of 96.7783 by the conclusion of the 100-step period . For decision makers, this highlights the importance of considering how different knowledge-sharing mechanisms impact long-term equity among farming communities, even when initial resources are distributed evenly .  The population of households utilizing the E-RO strategy begins at 20 and exhibits a minor increase during the first decade of the simulation . Starting at timestep 11, a pronounced and statistically significant downward trend takes hold . The decline reflects a systematic shift away from the non-learning baseline . Policy interventions should therefore prioritize supporting adaptive capacity .
