 The agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . Each agent evaluates milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics . Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms .  The total number of milk choices starts at 0.00 at the initial step and increases linearly, reaching 32,000 by the final recorded step . The trajectory shows a consistent upward trend with high linearity, supported by statistical measures indicating significant increasing trends across multiple rolling windows with tau values of 1.0 and slopes ranging from approximately 64.0 to 76.2 .  The data underscores a two-stage transition process: an initial uptake of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, followed by a period of stabilization, and finally, a secondary phase of sustained growth . For decision-makers, this pattern highlights the importance of early interventions targeting health perceptions and social influence to accelerate the adoption of healthier alternatives .  The mean deviation between agents' held environmental values and the implied values by their milk choices begins at 0.0 at time step 0 and increases rapidly during the initial phase of the simulation . After the initial peak, the metric stabilizes, remaining within a narrow range between approximately 13.41 and 13.48 . This high and persistent level of deviation suggests that once agents settle into new consumption routines, there is a sustained disconnect between their environmental priorities and the characteristics of products they choose .
