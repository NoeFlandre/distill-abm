The average amount of actual available green biomass starts at 45,000 units. Over the first several time steps, the biomass declines, reaching a minimum value of 42,057.04 at time step 27. This initial decrease reflects early pressure on the pasture resources as households adjust their livestock decisions. Following this low point, the biomass exhibits a consistent upward trajectory. A significant increasing trend is observed beginning around time step 33 and continuing through the remainder of the simulation. The metric surpasses its initial value, culminating at a maximum of 60,535.28 at time step 100. This long-term recovery and growth suggest that the adaptive behaviors implemented in the model, including learning-by-doing and social learning strategies, eventually facilitate sustainable grazing practices that enhance pasture regeneration. For decision makers, this pattern indicates that while resource availability may dip initially during strategy adoption, integrating learning mechanisms into household decision-making can lead to substantial long-term ecological improvements and increased biomass reserves.
