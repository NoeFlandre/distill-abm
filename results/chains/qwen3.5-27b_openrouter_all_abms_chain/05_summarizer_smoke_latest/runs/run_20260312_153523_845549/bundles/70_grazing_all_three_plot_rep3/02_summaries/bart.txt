This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock. The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches begins at approximately 45,000 units. The average number of livestock destocked by households begins near zero, then sharply rises to a peak of approximately 7.5 animals at around time step 2. After step 40 onwards, a robust recovery in biomass is observed, with levels increasing rapidly. The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households starts at zero and rises sharply in the first 20 time steps, reaching approximately 80. Farmers employing the baseline strategy (E-RO) show consistently higher destocking activity compared to those learning from their own results. For decision-makers, these trends underscore the importance of promoting adaptive learning strategies.
