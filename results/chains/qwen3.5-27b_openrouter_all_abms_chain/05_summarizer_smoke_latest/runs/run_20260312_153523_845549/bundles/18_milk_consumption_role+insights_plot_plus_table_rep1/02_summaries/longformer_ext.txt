Context and Goals: This agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. We aim to explore how individual perception, established habits, and social influence drive decision-making processes. The model measures the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person to validate against observed macro-level data. The average weekly consumption of each milk type per agent is approximately 2,655 units. The average weekly consumption of each milk type per agent exhibits a consistent and uniform upward trend across the simulation timeline. This steady accumulation suggests a continuous shift in consumer preference away from whole milk towards lower-fat alternatives. For decision-makers, this indicates that once the adoption process initiates, the transition to these specific milk varieties is persistent and linear, driven by the interplay of social influence and changing health perceptions within the consumer network. The trend remains steady from time steps 15 to 24, and then slightly moderates between time steps 25 and 32. The overall pattern indicates a growing influence of habit on the preference for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk varieties over time.
