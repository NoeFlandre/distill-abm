The total number of prey animals exhibits a consistent downward trend, starting from an initial value of approximately 167 and declining to zero by the end of the observation period. This reduction occurs rapidly during the initial phase, with values dropping below 50 by tick 500 and continuing to decrease until reaching extinction by tick 2311. The mean population size across the series is 25, while the median is significantly lower at 1.48, indicating a skewed distribution with most of the decline concentrated in the early stages. Minor fluctuations and temporary stabilization periods are observed, such as slight increases between ticks 1392 and 1623 and another between ticks 1856 and 2087, but these do not reverse the overall decline. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms a statistically significant decreasing trend throughout most of the simulation. The oscillation patterns suggest short-term variability, with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, possibly influenced by seasonal dynamics or resource regeneration. These results imply that, under the given socio-natural conditions, hunting pressure combined with environmental constraints leads to rapid and inevitable prey population collapse, highlighting the sensitivity of megafaunal systems to sustained external pressures.
