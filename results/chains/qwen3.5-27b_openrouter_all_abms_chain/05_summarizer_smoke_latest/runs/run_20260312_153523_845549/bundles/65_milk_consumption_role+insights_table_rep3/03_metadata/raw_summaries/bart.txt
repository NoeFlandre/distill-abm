 This agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 . The simulation consists of 1,000 agents representing consumers situated within a social network . Each agent evaluates milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics . Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms .  The simulation trajectory reveals a continuous accumulation of whole milk selections throughout the observed timeline . The slope of increase begins at approximately 76.199 during the early phase (indices 5-9) and diminishes to roughly 64.067 in the later phase . The slowing rate of cumulative choice aligns with the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed or semi-skimmed varieties .  The consumption trajectory reveals a structural break in consumer preference . Inflection points appear at inflection points corresponding to time steps 7, 12, and 28 . For decision makers, strategies to shift consumption patterns must account for an initial period of resistance before habit mechanisms can effectively sustain the new behavior .  Volatility becomes more pronounced in the final phase of the simulation, particularly between time steps 18 and 31 . This trajectory suggests that as health perceptions evolved during the transition from whole to lower-fat milk, consumer behavior lagged due to established habits and social dynamics . For decision makers, the widening gap highlights the need to address habit inertia alongside health information dissemination .
