 The agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 . The simulation consists of 1,000 agents representing consumers situated within a social network . Each agent evaluates milk choices based on health and environmental characteristics . Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms .  The total number of whole milk choices starts at 0.0 at the initial time step and increases steadily throughout the simulation period . The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk starts sharply at 529 units per person and increases during the simulation . The simulation captures the complex dynamics of consumer decision-making, highlighting the role of individual dispositions in driving macro-level changes in consumption patterns .  A significant rise occurs between time steps 0 and 10, where the value climbs from 5.29 to approximately 1265 units . This rapid growth suggests that a large number of agents initially shift their preferences towards lower-fat milk varieties . The overall trend shows a definitive move away from whole milk, reflecting the historical shift in British milk consumption driven by health consciousness and social dynamics .  The mean value environmental deviation begins at 0.00 at the initial step and increases sharply, reaching approximately 12.00 by step 4 . The metric continues to grow, peaking at a maximum value of 13.48 at step 13 . The values fluctuate slightly but stabilize, maintaining levels between 13.41 and 13.45 through the final observed step of 32.45 .
