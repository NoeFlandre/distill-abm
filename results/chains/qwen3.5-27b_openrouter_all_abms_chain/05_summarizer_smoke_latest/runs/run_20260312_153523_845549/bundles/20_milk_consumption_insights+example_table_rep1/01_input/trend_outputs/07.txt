Over the course of the simulation, which covers the period leading up to 2005, the consumption metric exhibits a substantial upward trajectory, culminating in a final value of 1456.60 units.

In the initial phase, from time step 0 to 9, the consumption rises sharply. The trend indicates significant increases during the first two five-step windows, with the metric climbing steadily from the baseline. This rapid adoption phase likely corresponds to the initial introduction of health-related perceptions regarding fat content, prompting agents to shift away from the incumbent whole milk choice despite strong initial habits.

Between time steps 10 and 24, the growth rate moderates, entering a period of relative stabilization where fluctuations occur without a significant directional trend. During this interval, values oscillate around a mean of approximately 1230 units, with local peaks observed at step 11 (1265.47) and step 13 (1240.35). This plateau suggests a tension between the agents' established habits and the emerging social norms favoring lower-fat options. The resistance to change during this middle period may be attributed to the high initial habit strength associated with the incumbent product, requiring sustained social influence to overcome.

From time step 25 onwards, the trend resumes a significant upward path, ending with the maximum recorded value at step 32. This late-stage acceleration indicates that the accumulated social influence and changing perceptions eventually overcame the inertia of habit. The presence of multiple inflection points throughout the simulation highlights the non-linear nature of adoption, driven by the interplay between individual disposition mechanisms and network interactions.

For decision makers, these dynamics suggest that policy interventions promoting healthier alternatives are most effective when timed to exploit the early adoption surge and the late-stage acceleration. The middle plateau indicates a critical juncture where consumer habits are resistant to change, implying that stronger incentives or targeted social campaigns are necessary to sustain momentum during periods of stagnation.
