The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at zero and rises sharply, reaching approximately 150 deaths by 400 time steps. This rapid increase suggests high predation pressure and/or environmental stressors leading to significant mortality early in the simulation. The rate of increase slows noticeably after 500 time steps, and the death counts stabilize between 200 and 250 by around 1,000 steps. Most simulation runs plateau close to 250 deaths, indicating near-complete turnover of the initial prey population, which was set at 250 individuals. A few runs show slower increases, reaching between 220 and 240 deaths later, suggesting variable predation efficiency or survival of certain subpopulations. The stabilization of death counts by 1,000 steps implies that the system has reached a state where few or no prey remain to die, reflecting conditions sufficient for near-total extinction under the given parameters. These findings highlight the model's sensitivity to early-stage pressures, and for decision-makers, emphasize the importance of mitigating hunting or environmental stressors during initial phases to prevent irreversible population loss.
