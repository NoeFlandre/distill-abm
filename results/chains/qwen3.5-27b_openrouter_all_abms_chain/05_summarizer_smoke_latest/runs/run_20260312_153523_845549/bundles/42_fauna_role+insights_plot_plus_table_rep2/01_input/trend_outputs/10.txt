The total number of prey animals declined consistently across all 120 simulations, beginning at approximately 167 at the start of the observation period and reaching zero by the final recorded time step. The mean population across the series was 25, while the median was 1.5, indicating a distribution heavily skewed toward lower values and a prolonged period of near-extinction conditions prior to total collapse. The rate of decline was most rapid during the initial phase, dropping significantly before the 400th time step, after which the rate of reduction slowed, transitioning into a phase of gradual attrition. Several minor oscillations in population levels occurred between time steps 700 and 1030, but these fluctuations were small in magnitude and did not represent sustained recoveries. The population trajectory eventually reached zero, indicating total extinction. This pattern suggests that under the specified model parameters, including a starting forager population of 20 and relatively high food values for both sexes, the combined pressures of human predation and environmental constraints are sufficient to drive the megaherbivore population to extinction. Decision-makers can infer that even moderate levels of predation pressure, when sustained over time, can result in the irreversible collapse of megaherbivore populations in the modeled ecosystem.
