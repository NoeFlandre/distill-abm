The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at zero and rises sharply, reaching approximately 150 deaths by 400 time steps. This rapid increase indicates a high initial mortality rate, potentially due to the combined effects of predation pressure from the foragers and internal mortality mechanisms within the prey population. From 400 to 800 time steps, the rate of increase in cumulative deaths slows, suggesting a stabilization of the death rate as the prey population adjusts to the predation pressure and environmental constraints. By 1000 time steps, the cumulative number of deaths approaches 200 and continues to rise more gradually, eventually plateauing near 250 deaths around 1500 time steps. This plateau indicates that the prey population has either been significantly reduced or has reached a state where the death rate balances with the birth rate and other survival factors. The variability in the cumulative death trajectories across different simulation runs suggests that small changes in initial conditions or random events can lead to different outcomes in prey mortality, highlighting the sensitivity of the system to these factors. These findings imply that decision-makers should be aware of the high initial impact of predation on prey populations and consider strategies to mitigate rapid population decline in the early stages of such socio-ecological systems.
