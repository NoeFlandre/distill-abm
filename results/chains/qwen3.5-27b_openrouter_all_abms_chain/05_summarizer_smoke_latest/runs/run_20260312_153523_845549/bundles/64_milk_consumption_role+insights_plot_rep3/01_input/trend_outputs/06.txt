The mean whole milk quantity consumed by agents in the agent-based model exhibits a clear downward trend over the simulated time steps, reflecting a significant shift in consumer behavior. At the initial step, the mean whole milk quantity consumed is approximately 2,600 units, indicating a strong preference for whole milk at the start of the simulation. This high initial consumption aligns with the historical context where whole milk was the dominant choice among British adults in 1974.  As the simulation progresses, there is a rapid decline in whole milk consumption. By the 10th time step, the mean quantity drops significantly to around 1,000 units. This sharp decrease suggests that factors such as increased health awareness, social influence, and the availability of alternatives began to impact consumer decisions early in the simulation period.  Between the 10th and 25th time steps, the decline in whole milk consumption continues but at a slower rate. The mean quantity gradually decreases from approximately 1,000 units to about 600 units. This period may indicate a phase where consumer habits are being reshaped more gradually, possibly influenced by persistent social norms and individual perceptions.  From the 25th time step onwards, there is a more pronounced drop in consumption. By the 30th time step, the mean whole milk quantity consumed falls to around 300 units. This sharp decline towards the end of the simulation could reflect a tipping point where alternative milk varieties become the preferred choice for a larger portion of the population.  Overall, the trends indicate a substantial shift away from whole milk consumption over the simulated period. Decision-makers can infer that strategies promoting healthier alternatives and leveraging social influence have been effective in changing consumer preferences. The model highlights the importance of addressing established habits and social norms in driving such behavioral changes.
