The total energy of all animals within the simulation begins at approximately 25,000 units at the initial time step. This value reflects the aggregate bioenergetic potential derived from the starting population of 250 prey individuals. A rapid, monotonic decline is observed from the first step, with the energy levels dropping precipitously to approximately 8,000 units by step 300. This initial phase of energetic loss correlates with the high predation efficiency of the 20 forager agents and the high caloric yield of adult males (food-value-males: 30) and females (food-value-females: 27) relative to the low processing costs. Following this steep descent, a transient plateau is evident between steps 300 and 500, where the total energy fluctuates between 4,000 and 9,000 units. This suggests a temporary stabilization where reproduction and foraging intake partially offset the mortality rates caused by hunting and internal mortality (0.025). However, the system fails to sustain this equilibrium. The aggregate energy resumes a downward trajectory, reaching near-zero levels by step 1,200 across all simulation runs. The trajectories converge at zero total energy by step 2,300, indicating the complete removal of biomass from the system. This outcome signifies that under these parameters, specifically with a fixed forager population and significant hunting pressure, the megafaunal population cannot replenish the energy extracted through predation, leading to total extirpation within roughly 2,300 time steps.
