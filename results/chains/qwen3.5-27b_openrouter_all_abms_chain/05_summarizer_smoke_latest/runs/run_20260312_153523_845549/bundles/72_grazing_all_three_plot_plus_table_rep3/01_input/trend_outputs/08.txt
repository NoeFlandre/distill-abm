The number of households practicing social learning starts at zero across all simulation runs. A rapid adoption phase occurs between time steps 5 and 25, where the average count of households engaging in social learning surges from near zero to approximately 40. This rapid accumulation suggests that once a threshold of information or successful outcomes is observed by the initial E-RO households, social learning spreads quickly among the remaining farmers. The rate of adoption slows significantly after time step 25, with the curve continuing to climb more gradually until it begins to plateau around time step 40. By time step 60, the average number of households engaging in social learning stabilizes at approximately 50 out of 60 total households. The simulation ends with the metric maintaining this plateau, indicating that the vast majority of the farming population has shifted to social learning. The variance among individual simulation runs is highest during the early adoption phase, reflecting stochastic differences in which neighbors influence whom first. However, this variance converges as the system stabilizes, showing that the long-term state is largely independent of initial random fluctuations. Decision-makers can expect that introducing social learning mechanisms will result in rapid system-wide adoption, typically stabilizing within the first half of the planning horizon.
