The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches initially fluctuates around 45,000 units during the first 10 time steps. A significant decline occurs between time steps 10 and 40, reaching a minimum of approximately 40,000 units. This reduction is likely attributed to increased grazing pressure from the livestock population. From time step 40 onwards, a substantial recovery in green biomass is observed, with values steadily increasing and eventually stabilizing between 55,000 and 63,000 units by time step 100. This recovery trend suggests that the adaptive behaviors of farmers, particularly those learning from their own results or neighbors, may have led to more sustainable grazing practices. The stabilization of green biomass at higher levels indicates a balance between livestock numbers and pasture health, which is crucial for long-term sustainability. Decision makers should consider promoting learning mechanisms among farmers to enhance pasture resilience and ensure sustainable grazing systems.
