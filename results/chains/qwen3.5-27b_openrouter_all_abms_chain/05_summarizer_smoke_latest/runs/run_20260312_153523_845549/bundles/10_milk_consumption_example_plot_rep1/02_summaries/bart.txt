The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at zero and remains at that level during the first time step. From time step 1, consumption rises sharply, reaching approximately 1,200 by time step 10. The growth rate slows between time steps 10 and 27, where consumption fluctuates around 1,000 to 1,250. After step 27, there is another noticeable increase, with consumption rising to about 1,450 by step 30. The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at zero and rises sharply during the initial time steps. This rapid increase suggests agents are quickly developing divergent preferences for milk types based on their initial perceptions, habits, and social influences. By step 25, the factor reaches approximately 1.45, reflecting a significant reinforcement of the choice function for skimmed/semi-skimmed milk. The mean value environmental deviation stabilizes, maintaining a value close to 13.5 throughout the remaining duration of the simulation. This stabilization indicates that the agents' milk choices have settled into a consistent pattern relative to their environmental values, with no further significant adjustments in behavior.
