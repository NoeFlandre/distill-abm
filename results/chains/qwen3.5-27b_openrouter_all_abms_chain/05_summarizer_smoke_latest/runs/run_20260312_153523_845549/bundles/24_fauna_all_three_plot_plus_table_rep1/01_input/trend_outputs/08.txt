The population of male megaherbivores begins at 125 individuals and undergoes a rapid, precipitous decline over the first 500 time steps, dropping to fewer than 20 individuals in the majority of simulation runs. This initial phase represents a catastrophic collapse, where the combined pressure of human foraging, defined by a high processing cost for males (6.0) relative to females (3.0) despite a higher food value (30.0), accelerates the removal of adult males from the system. Following this steep decline, the rate of decrease slows significantly between steps 500 and 1000, where the population hovers between 2 and 20 individuals. This transition period corresponds to a shift from mass depletion to a prolonged attrition phase, characterized by high variance among individual simulation runs. The population effectively reaches functional extinction by step 1000, with the aggregate mean dropping below 2.6 individuals. The statistical trend indicates a persistent downward trajectory with no recovery, confirmed by significant negative slopes in rolling trend analysis until the population stabilizes near zero. This stabilization near zero persists from approximately step 1500 through the simulation end at 2312 steps, marking the total loss of the male demographic. The implications suggest that under these specific parameter settings, particularly the high initial forager count and the specific energy economics of hunting males, the system lacks the resilience to sustain a breeding population, leading to deterministic extinction within a single decade (approx. 1000 ticks).
