The cumulative number of prey deaths begins at zero and exhibits a rapid, steep increase during the first 400 time steps, rising from 0 to approximately 170 deaths. This initial surge corresponds to a period of high mortality pressure, likely driven by the immediate interaction between the 20 immortal foragers and the 250 prey individuals within the landscape. Statistical analysis confirms a significant increasing trend with a slope of 0.346 in this initial window. The rate of accumulation remains elevated but gradually decelerates between time steps 400 and 1200, as the death count climbs from 170 to roughly 230. This plateauing effect suggests the prey population is becoming depleted, limiting the frequency of successful predation events. After time step 1200, the curve effectively flattens, with the death count oscillating narrowly around a mean of 235.0 and reaching a maximum peak of 240.33 at time step 1876. By the conclusion of the simulation, the cumulative deaths stabilize near 235, indicating that only a very small fraction of the initial 250 prey population (approximately 25 individuals) survives. The high variance in the early stages reflects stochastic differences in agent movement and resource discovery, which diminishes significantly as the system approaches equilibrium with a near-extinct prey population.
