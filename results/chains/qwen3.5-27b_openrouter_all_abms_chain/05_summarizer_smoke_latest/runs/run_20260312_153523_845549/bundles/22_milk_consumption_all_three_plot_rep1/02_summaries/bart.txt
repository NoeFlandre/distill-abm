The agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms. Total number of milk choices made by all agents begins at zero and increases steadily over time. By step 10, the cumulative choices reach approximately 10,000, reflecting an average rate of roughly 1,000 choices per time step. Trend implies a significant shift in consumer preference away from whole milk, driven by factors such as social influence and changing perceptions of health. The trend shows a clear and consistent increase in the habit factor over time, suggesting that consumer habits favoring skimmed and semi-skimmed milk become progressively stronger as the simulation progresses. Efforts to shift consumer behavior may require interventions that directly target the mechanisms of habit reinforcement or disrupt the conditions supporting habitual persistence. For decision-makers, this highlights the importance of addressing diverse consumer perceptions early in campaigns to counteract entrenched habits.
