The total energy of female prey animals starts at approximately 12,000 units and experiences a rapid decline over the first 400 time steps. This steep reduction suggests that the initial high energy levels are quickly depleted, potentially due to the combined effects of hunting pressure, movement costs, and insufficient food availability. The decline continues more gradually from 400 to 800 time steps, indicating a sustained period of energy loss, possibly as herds face ongoing environmental challenges and human predation. After 800 time steps, the total energy stabilizes at very low levels, hovering near zero. This stabilization suggests that the population of female animals has either been reduced to a minimal number or that the surviving individuals are operating at the lower end of their energy threshold. The variability among simulation runs is evident throughout the period, with some runs showing faster depletion rates than others, reflecting the stochastic nature of hunting behavior, environmental conditions, and herd dynamics.
