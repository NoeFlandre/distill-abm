 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore . It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival .  The trajectory indicates that forager energy deficits accumulate steadily without recovery, suggesting that the rates of energy expenditure from movement and processing costs consistently exceed energy acquisition from prey or storage mechanisms over the long term . The cumulative number of male prey animals killed by forager bands begins at zero and increases steadily over the initial phase of the simulation .  The reproductive dynamics show three primary phases: an initial lag phase with no births, a middle phase of active and increasing reproductive output, and a final phase marked by a slowdown and eventual plateau in birth events . The population of male megaherbivores begins at an initial mean of 125.0 and exhibits a rapid, sustained decline throughout the early simulation .  The total number of prey animals begins at approximately 167 and follows a steep, exponential decline during the initial phase of the simulation . As the population approaches extinction levels, the decline slows significantly, entering a period of stochastic fluctuation between 700 and 1700 time steps, where the mean population hovers near single digits . Ultimately, the population collapses to zero, demonstrating that under the given parameters of forager density and processing costs, the megaherbivore population cannot sustain itself .
