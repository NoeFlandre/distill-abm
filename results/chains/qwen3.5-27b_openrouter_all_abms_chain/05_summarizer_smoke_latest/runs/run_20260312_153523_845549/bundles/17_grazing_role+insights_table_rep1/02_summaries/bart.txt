This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock. At the onset, the system initializes with a biomass level of 90000.00 units. During the first 21 time steps, the metric undergoes a statistically significant decline, indicating an initial period of resource depletion or overgrazing pressure. By the end of the simulation at time step 100, the reserve biomass reaches its peak value of 121072.59 units. The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households demonstrates a continuous increasing trend over the 100-year simulation period. The overall mean engagement stands at 27.43 households with a standard deviation of 8.92. Social learning behaviors diffuse through the community of 60 smallholder farmers, eventually stabilizing at roughly half the population. The high initial destocking rate implies that farmers prioritize immediate herd alignment with land capacity or risk mitigation strategies early in the management cycle. Long-term planning should account for this front-loaded destocking behavior. Policy interventions should consider facilitating access to adaptive learning mechanisms to support farmers who might otherwise remain in less effective static strategies.
