The average reserve biomass across grazing patches exhibits a distinct non-linear trajectory over the 100 time step simulation period.

At the onset, the system initializes with a biomass level of 90000.00 units. This downward movement bottoms out at time step 26, where the minimum reserve biomass is recorded at 84114.07 units.

Following this valley, the trend shifts, entering a stabilization phase between time steps 22 and 32 where no significant directional trend is detected. Subsequently, from time step 33 onwards, a strong and statistically significant increasing trend emerges. This recovery phase is characterized by positive slopes that intensify through time step 54 before moderating slightly but remaining positive through the final period. By the end of the simulation at time step 100, the reserve biomass reaches its peak value of 121072.59 units, representing a net increase from the initial state.

This pattern suggests that while the grazing system experiences initial stress, the behavioral dynamics of the farmers, including those learning from experience and neighbors, eventually facilitate a shift toward sustainable herd management that allows pasture regeneration. For decision makers, this implies that short-term biomass losses are part of the system adjustment process and should not necessarily trigger panic interventions, as long-term indicators point toward substantial pasture recovery and improved resource availability.
