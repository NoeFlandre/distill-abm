The simulation output indicates a statistically significant upward trend in the number of households engaging in social learning over the 100-timestep period. Starting from zero households at timestep 0, the mean count rises steadily to a maximum of approximately 32.07 by timestep 63, maintaining this level through the end of the simulation. Rolling Mann-Kendall tests confirm a positive trend with high significance (p = 0.000) across all temporal windows, although the slope of increase diminishes over time, dropping from 1.43 in the initial window to 0.008 in the final observed window. Distinct inflection points occur at timesteps 7, 28, 30, 33, 38, 57, and 60, marking shifts in the rate of adoption. Periods of oscillation are detected between timesteps 22 and 32, and again from 51 to 59, introducing minor variance around the central tendency. The overall mean engagement stands at 27.43 households with a standard deviation of 8.92. In the context of the RAGE model, this trajectory suggests that social learning behaviors diffuse through the community of 60 smallholder farmers, eventually stabilizing at roughly half the population. For decision makers, this implies that social learning strategies have strong potential for adoption over long time horizons, potentially leading to synchronized grazing management. However, the plateau at 32 households indicates a saturation point under current conditions, suggesting that additional interventions may be required to engage the remaining non-learning households.
