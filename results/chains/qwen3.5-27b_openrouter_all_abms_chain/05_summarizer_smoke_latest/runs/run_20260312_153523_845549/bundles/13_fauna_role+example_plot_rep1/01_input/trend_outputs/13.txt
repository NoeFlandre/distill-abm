The total energy of all animals within the simulation begins at approximately 25,000 units at the initial time step. This value reflects the aggregate bioenergetic potential derived from the starting population of 250 prey individuals. A rapid, monotonic decline is observed from the first step, with the energy levels dropping precipitously to approximately 8,000 units by step 300. This initial phase of energetic loss correlates with the high predation efficiency of the 20 forager agents and the high caloric yield of adult males (food-value-males: 30) and females (food-value-females: 27) relative to the low processing costs. Following this steep descent, a transient plateau is evident between steps 300 and 500, where the total energy fluctuates between 4,000 and 9,000 units. This suggests a temporary stabilization where reproduction and foraging intake partially offset the mortality rates caused by hunting and internal mortality (0.025). However, the system fails to sustain this equilibrium. From step 500 onwards, the total energy resumes a downward trajectory, eventually reaching a critical threshold below 500 units by step 1,000. By step 1,200, the total energy across the vast majority of simulation runs approaches zero, indicating a systemic collapse of the prey population and a near-total depletion of available biomass. High variance is apparent across the different simulation runs, particularly during the decline phase between steps 100 and 1,200. This divergence suggests that stochastic elements in forager movement and prey distribution lead to different rates of population collapse, though the trajectory toward energy depletion is consistent. The simulation concludes with the total energy stabilizing at near-zero levels, signifying the extinction of the megaherbivore population under these specific parameter settings.
