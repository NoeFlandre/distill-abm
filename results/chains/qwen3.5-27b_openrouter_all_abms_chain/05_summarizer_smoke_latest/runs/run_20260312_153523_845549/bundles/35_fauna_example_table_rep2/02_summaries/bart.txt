Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Hunters follow dynamic optimal foraging theory, balancing energy status, prey value, density, and costs. Ungulates are sexually dimorphic, reproduce, age, and move as herds The model parameters suggest a scenario with 20 foragers and an initial prey population of 250. The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted by forager bands begins at zero at the start of the observation period. The accumulation of 123 female kills represents a significant portion of the initial cohort, yet the cessation of rapid accumulation implies a buffering effect, potentially driven by prey avoidance, reduced encounter rates, or the hunters' energy storage capabilities. The population of male prey animals starts at 125 and declines rapidly during the initial phase of the simulation. By time step 700, the population has already dropped to double-digit figures, and the count continues to dwindle. The population reaches zero at time step 2284 and remains at this minimum level for the remainder of the observation period. The total number of prey animals begins at approximately 167 individuals and follows a consistent downward trajectory throughout the simulation period. Population declines significantly within the first 125 time steps, indicating a rapid initial loss likely driven by the introduction of 20 foragers into the environment. Population stabilizes briefly around 11 individuals before exhibiting minor fluctuations characterized by peaks and valleys between steps 700 and 1000. The final change point occurs near tick 2310, leading to the complete extinction of the female prey cohort at tick 2311. During simulation, energy levels decrease rapidly with a steep negative slope. This suggests intense pressure on the herd, likely driven by the high efficiency of the 20 initial foragers operating under optimal foraging dynamics alongside seasonal environmental constraints. The dominant oscillation period of approximately 63 ticks further suggests cyclical pressures, possibly related to movement or feeding cycles.
