 This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors . The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time . The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock .  The average amount of reserve biomass starts at approximately 90,000 units and experiences a decline during the initial 15 time steps . This initial decrease suggests an early period of high grazing pressure or insufficient grass regrowth relative to livestock consumption . The stabilization period indicates that the grazing system has reached a new equilibrium, likely driven by adaptive livestock management behaviors .  The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero across all simulation runs and begins to rise sharply between time steps 5 and 10 . The growth rate of social learning slows down after time step 40, with many runs reaching a plateau between 18 and 24 households, while other runs continue to grow more steadily .
