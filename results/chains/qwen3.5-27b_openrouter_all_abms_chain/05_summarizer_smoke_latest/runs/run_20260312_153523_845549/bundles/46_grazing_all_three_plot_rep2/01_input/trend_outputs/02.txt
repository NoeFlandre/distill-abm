The mean household risk attitude begins near 0.5 for all households, reflecting the initial parameter setting. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid divergence in risk attitudes, with some households increasing their risk tolerance to values near 0.8 while others drop below 0. This divergence suggests that individual learning and decision-making processes are driving households toward varying levels of risk-taking. By time step 40, households with higher initial risk attitudes stabilize between 0.6 and 0.8, indicating a tendency to maintain higher risk levels once adopted. Conversely, households that adopt lower risk attitudes stabilize between -0.2 and 0, suggesting a cautious approach that persists over time. A notable trend is the sharp decline in risk attitude for some households after time step 40, dropping to values as low as -0.3. This could indicate a response to adverse conditions or negative feedback from livestock management decisions. Overall, the system exhibits significant variability in risk attitudes, reflecting diverse learning strategies and individual experiences. Decision-makers should consider this variability when designing policies, as uniform approaches may not address the diverse needs and behaviors of smallholder farmers.
