Context and Goals

This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. In this version, each farmer stays on their own plot of land. They decide how many animals to keep based on how the grass is doing and what others are doing. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. There are three main ways farmers behave: baseline (E-RO), learning from their own results (E-LBD), and learning from neighbors (E-RO-SL1). The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock.

The average initial risk attitude of all social learning agents remains constant throughout the 100 simulation steps, reflecting a static initial disposition to deviate from the recommended number of livestock. The risk attitudes vary across different agents, ranging from approximately -0.10 to 0.18. These fixed values suggest that each group of agents begins with a predetermined willingness to take risks or adhere to recommendations, which does not change over time in this specific context. The variation among initial attitudes indicates heterogeneity in decision-making predispositions, which could influence how different groups respond to environmental changes or social learning cues in more dynamic simulations. Decision makers can infer that initial risk attitudes set a baseline for behavior, but their static nature here suggests that external factors or internal learning mechanisms may be needed to alter these dispositions over time.

The mean household risk attitude begins near 0.5 for all households, reflecting the initial parameter setting. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid divergence in risk attitudes, with some households increasing their risk tolerance to values near 0.8 while others drop below 0. This divergence suggests that individual learning and decision-making processes are driving households toward varying levels of risk-taking. By time step 40, households with higher initial risk attitudes stabilize between 0.6 and 0.8, indicating a tendency to maintain higher risk levels once adopted. Conversely, households that adopt lower risk attitudes stabilize between -0.2 and 0, suggesting a cautious approach that persists over time. A notable trend is the sharp decline in risk attitude for some households after time step 40, dropping to values as low as -0.3. This could indicate a response to adverse conditions or negative feedback from livestock management decisions. Overall, the system exhibits significant variability in risk attitudes, reflecting diverse learning strategies and individual experiences. Decision-makers should consider this variability when designing policies, as uniform approaches may not address the diverse needs and behaviors of smallholder farmers.

The average amount of reserve biomass starts at approximately 90,000 units and experiences a decline during the initial 15 time steps, reaching a minimum around 80,000 units. This initial decrease suggests an early period of high grazing pressure or insufficient grass regrowth relative to livestock consumption. Following this initial decline, the reserve biomass begins a gradual increase from time step 15, accelerating significantly after time step 40. This rise continues until time step 60, where the biomass levels off, stabilizing between 110,000 and 128,000 units by the end of the simulation. The stabilization period indicates that the grazing system has reached a new equilibrium, likely driven by adaptive livestock management behaviors. The variance among simulation runs widens after time step 40, suggesting that differences in farmer learning strategies (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1) begin to significantly influence the outcome as the system approaches equilibrium. For decision-makers, the rapid recovery after time step 40 highlights the system's resilience, provided that farmers adapt their herding strategies effectively. However, the significant variance in final biomass levels underscores the importance of encouraging effective learning behaviors among farmers to optimize pasture health.

The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches begins at approximately 45,000 units. Over the first 40 time steps, there is a noticeable decline in biomass, reaching a minimum value around 40,000 units. This reduction likely reflects the initial grazing pressure as farmers adjust their livestock numbers without extensive learning or adaptation. Following this decline, the biomass levels stabilize briefly before initiating a substantial increase starting around step 40. By step 60, the biomass has risen significantly, reaching values above 60,000 units. This increase may indicate that farmers have adapted their practices, possibly through learning or reduced grazing pressure, allowing the pasture to recover and regenerate. After step 60, the biomass levels plateau, with most simulation runs stabilizing between 61,000 and 63,000 units by the end of the simulation at step 100. The consistency in the final values suggests that the system has reached a new equilibrium where grazing practices and pasture health are balanced. For decision-makers, this trend implies that initial grazing strategies may lead to short-term depletion of resources, but adaptive behaviors can restore and sustain pasture health over time. Monitoring and supporting such adaptive strategies could enhance long-term sustainability.

The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households begins at 90. This figure declines rapidly within the first 20 time steps, reaching approximately 50. The sharp reduction suggests an initial adjustment phase where households likely reduce livestock numbers in response to pasture conditions or resource constraints. From time step 20 to around 40, the decline slows, and the values stabilize between 40 and 50, indicating a period of relative equilibrium as households adapt their grazing strategies. After time step 40, the livestock numbers begin to recover, with some trajectories rising steadily to reach levels between 45 and 60 by time step 100. This recovery could reflect improved pasture management or adaptation to local conditions over time. Variability among trajectories increases after time step 40, suggesting divergent responses to environmental or social cues among households. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of early support for livestock management during the initial adjustment phase, as well as the potential for long-term sustainability through adaptive strategies.

The average number of livestock destocked by households begins near zero, then sharply rises to a peak of approximately 7.5 animals at around time step 2. Following this initial surge, the average destocking number drops steeply, reaching approximately 2 animals by time step 20. The decline continues at a slower rate until time step 40, where the values stabilize at a range between 0 and 1.5 animals for the remainder of the simulation. Throughout the 100 time steps, significant variance is observed among individual simulation runs, particularly in the initial phase, indicating high heterogeneity in household outcomes despite uniform behavioral settings. This suggests that early in the simulation, resource pressure forces widespread destocking, likely due to the initial overstocking relative to the pasture's carrying capacity under the baseline extension model. As households adjust their herd sizes, the pressure on the pasture diminishes, resulting in minimal destocking in later years. The stabilization indicates that the system reaches a new equilibrium where grazing levels are sustainable under the current management rules. The persistence of some destocking, even in later stages, implies that occasional resource shortfalls or management miscalculations still occur. For decision makers, the sharp initial destocking signals a critical need for intervention during the early adoption phase of grazing systems, possibly through better initial livestock advice or temporary support to avoid excessive herd reductions. The variance underscores the importance of considering individual farmer contexts and learning curves in policy design to prevent extreme losses in vulnerable households.

The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock starts at 0 across all simulations and remains near zero for the first 10 time steps. After this initial period, there is a gradual and steady increase in inequality, which becomes more pronounced around time step 40. By time step 100, the Gini coefficient ranges from approximately 0.03 to 0.09 across different simulation runs. This trend suggests that initially, healthy livestock are distributed fairly equally among households. However, as time progresses, differences in livestock management strategies and learning behaviors lead to increasing disparities in livestock health distribution. For decision makers, this indicates that while initial conditions promote equality, long-term outcomes may favor some households over others, potentially necessitating interventions to support less successful farmers.

The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero across all simulation runs and begins to rise sharply between time steps 5 and 10. By time step 20, approximately half of the simulation runs show between 18 and 28 households actively learning from their neighbors. The growth rate of social learning slows down after time step 40, with many runs reaching a plateau between 18 and 24 households, while other runs continue to grow more steadily, stabilizing around 30 to 54 households by time step 100. This trend suggests that initial conditions or stochastic factors in the simulation lead to varied outcomes in how quickly and extensively farmers adopt social learning behaviors. The diversity in final counts of socially learning households indicates that some configurations allow for widespread adoption, while others limit it to a subset of the community. For decision makers, this highlights the importance of facilitating early interactions and creating conditions that encourage the spread of beneficial behaviors within the farming community.

The number of households following the E-RO strategy starts at 20 in all simulation runs. Over the first 10 time steps, there is significant divergence among simulations, with some runs showing an increase to values between 28 and 40, while others show a decrease to values between 5 and 15. This early divergence suggests high sensitivity to initial conditions or stochastic elements within the model. By approximately 15 time steps, the counts stabilize into distinct bands. A subset of simulations maintains higher counts, fluctuating between 24 and 28 for the remainder of the simulation. Another subset settles into a lower range, oscillating between 16 and 18. Several simulation runs exhibit a decline to single-digit values, fluctuating between 0 and 5 by the end of the simulation period. The final state at 100 time steps indicates that the system tends to settle into one of these stable states, either retaining a substantial portion of non-learning agents, maintaining a moderate number, or seeing them nearly disappear from the population. This wide variability implies that without external interventions or changes in parameters, the persistence of non-learning strategies is highly uncertain and contingent on early interactions. Decision-makers should anticipate that policies assuming a steady adoption of learning strategies may face unpredictable resistance or persistence of non-learning behaviors depending on these early dynamics.
