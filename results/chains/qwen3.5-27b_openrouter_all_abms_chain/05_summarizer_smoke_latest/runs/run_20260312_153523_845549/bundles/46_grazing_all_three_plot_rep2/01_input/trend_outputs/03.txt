The average amount of reserve biomass starts at approximately 90,000 units and experiences a decline during the initial 15 time steps, reaching a minimum around 80,000 units. This initial decrease suggests an early period of high grazing pressure or insufficient grass regrowth relative to livestock consumption. Following this initial decline, the reserve biomass begins a gradual increase from time step 15, accelerating significantly after time step 40. This rise continues until time step 60, where the biomass levels off, stabilizing between 110,000 and 128,000 units by the end of the simulation. The stabilization period indicates that the grazing system has reached a new equilibrium, likely driven by adaptive livestock management behaviors. The variance among simulation runs widens after time step 40, suggesting that differences in farmer learning strategies (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1) begin to significantly influence the outcome as the system approaches equilibrium. For decision-makers, the rapid recovery after time step 40 highlights the system's resilience, provided that farmers adapt their herding strategies effectively. However, the significant variance in final biomass levels underscores the importance of encouraging effective learning behaviors among farmers to optimize pasture health.
