Context and goals this simulation extends the rage model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. They decide how many animals to keep based on how the grass is doing and what others are doing. There are three main ways farmers behave : baseline (e - ro), learning from their own results (e - lbd), and learning from neighbors (e - ro - sl1). Satisficing - threshold : range from 0. Behavioral - type : we set it to e - ro. Risk - att - init : we set it to 0. The average initial risk attitude of all social learning agents remains constant throughout the 100 simulation steps, reflecting a static initial disposition to deviate from the recommended number of livestock. These fixed values suggest that each group of agents begins with a predetermined willingness to take risks or adhere to recommendations, which does not change over time in this specific context. The variation among initial attitudes indicates heterogeneity in decision - making predispositions, which could influence how different groups respond to environmental changes or social learning cues in more dynamic simulations. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid divergence in risk attitudes, with some households increasing their risk tolerance to values near 0. The variance among simulation runs widens after time step 40, suggesting that differences in farmer learning strategies (e - lbd and e - ro - sl1) begin to significantly influence the outcome as the system approaches equilibrium. This increase may indicate that farmers have adapted their practices, possibly through learning or reduced grazing pressure, allowing the pasture to recover and regenerate. After step 60, the biomass levels plateau, with most simulation runs stabilizing between 61, 000 and 63, 000 units by the end of the simulation at step 100. For decision - makers, this trend implies that initial grazing strategies may lead to short - term depletion of resources, but adaptive behaviors can restore and sustain pasture health over time. From time step 20 to around 40, the decline slows, and the values stabilize between 40 and 50, indicating a period of relative equilibrium as households adapt their grazing strategies. After time step 40, the livestock numbers begin to recover, with some trajectories rising steadily to reach levels between 45 and 60 by time step 100. This suggests that early in the simulation, resource pressure forces widespread destocking, likely due to the initial overstocking relative to the pasture's carrying capacity under the baseline extension model. For decision makers, the sharp initial destocking signals a critical need for intervention during the early adoption phase of grazing systems, possibly through better initial livestock advice or temporary support to avoid excessive herd reductions. The growth rate of social learning slows down after time step 40, with many runs reaching a plateau between 18 and 24 households, while other runs continue to grow more steadily, stabilizing around 30 to 54 households by time step 100. Following this initial surge, the rate of destocking slows significantly, with values stabilizing between 60 and 70 for some simulation runs by time step 40, while others continue to rise more gradually. Over the first 10 time steps, there is significant divergence among simulations, with some runs showing an increase to values between 28 and 40, while others show a decrease to values between 5 and 15. A subset of simulations maintains higher counts, fluctuating between 24 and 28 for the remainder of the simulation. The final state at 100 time steps indicates that the system tends to settle into one of these stable states, either retaining a substantial portion of non - learning agents, maintaining a moderate number, or seeing them nearly disappear from the population. This wide variability implies that without external interventions or changes in parameters, the persistence of non - learning strategies is highly uncertain and contingent on early interactions. Decision - makers should anticipate that policies assuming a steady adoption of learning strategies may face unpredictable resistance or persistence of non - learning behaviors depending on these early dynamics.
