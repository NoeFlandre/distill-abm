 Simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors . The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time . The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock .  The average available green biomass across all patches starts at 45,000 units at the beginning of the simulation . The metric experiences a decline during the early phase, bottoming out at a minimum of 84,114.07 at time step 26 . The simulation concludes with the biomass reaching its maximum value of 60,535.28 at timestep 100 .  The number of households engaging in social learning initiates at zero and demonstrates a sustained increasing trend over the simulation duration . The Gini coefficient suggests that without intervention, wealth concentration in terms of healthy livestock tends to increase . For decision makers, this indicates that interventions relying on peer learning to improve grazing management will face a delay before achieving community-wide impact .  The simulation concludes at time step 100 with a value of 14.7, closely matching the minimum value . The decline suggests that non-learning management strategies become less prevalent as the grazing system evolves . Early intervention to encourage adaptive learning strategies could be beneficial before the baseline population diminishes significantly around the midpoint of the simulation timeline .
