The agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Each agent evaluates milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics. Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms. The total number of milk choices made by the agent population exhibits a strictly linear accumulation throughout the simulation duration. The average quantity of whole milk consumed per agent initiates at 2654.81 units, reflecting the strong established habits surrounding the incumbent product. By the final time step, the total cumulative count reaches 25,643.83 units. The consumption metric climbs from the mid-level plateau to reach the maximum observed value of 1456.60 units at the thirty-second time step. The trend shows a significant increase across all intervals, indicating a steady reinforcement of habitual purchasing behaviors associated with the incumbent milk variety. The mean percentage difference between maximum and minimum choice function scores commences at 0.0000 at the start of the simulation. The mean deviation between agents' held environmental values and the implied values of their milk choices begins at 0.0000 at the initial time step. Over the first 13 time steps, this deviation increases rapidly, reaching a maximum value of 13.4756 at time step 13. This sharp rise indicates a growing disconnect between what consumers value environmentally and the environmental impact of the milk products they select.
