The average reserve biomass across all patches begins at 90,000 units at the start of the simulation. Initially, the biomass condition deteriorates, showing a steady decline over the first quarter of the simulation period. This reduction continues until timestep 26, where the biomass reaches a minimum value of 84,114 units. This early depletion likely reflects the initial adjustment phase where households, possessing heterogeneous risk attitudes and behavioral strategies, establish their livestock numbers based on perceived resource availability and uncertainty.

Following the minimum at timestep 26, the biomass stabilizes briefly before shifting towards recovery. The trajectory shows a lack of clear trend between timesteps 22 and 32, marking a transition phase in the social-ecological system. Subsequently, a consistent upward trend emerges from timestep 33 onwards. An inflection point occurs around timestep 48, after which the rate of biomass accumulation sustains. By the end of the simulation at timestep 100, the reserve biomass recovers and exceeds the initial condition, reaching a maximum value of 121,072 units.

This long-term increase suggests that the learning mechanisms embedded in the behavioral types, such as learning-by-doing and social learning, eventually influence household decision-making towards more sustainable grazing practices. As agents observe pasture states and neighbor behaviors over time, stocking rates likely adjust to align with vegetation growth capacities, allowing the pasture biomass to regenerate and surpass baseline levels despite the stochastic climatic parameters and initial consumption pressure.
