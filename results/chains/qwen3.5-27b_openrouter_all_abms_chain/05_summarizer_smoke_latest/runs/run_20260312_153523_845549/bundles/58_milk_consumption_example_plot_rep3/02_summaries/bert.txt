Context and goals : the overall objective of the agent - based model is to reproduce adoption behaviours of milk consumption by the british public (1974 - 2005), by replicating individual preferences and decision factors. Decision - making follows a basic structure of : agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence, and finally, choice evaluation where agents consider the impact of their choices and may adjust their future decisions. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. Links between agents are unidirectional, and influence occurs as a function of interaction probability, with the degree of influence characterised by agent susceptibility. The average weekly consumption of whole milk starts at approximately 2, 650 units per person and remains stable during the first time step. Between time steps 10 and 18, the decline continues but at a slower rate, with consumption stabilizing around 750 units. A slight uptick in consumption is observed around time step 27, where levels briefly rise to approximately 650 units before declining again. Overall, the data demonstrates a clear trend of decreasing whole milk consumption over time, consistent with the model's goal of simulating the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi - skimmed varieties. The rate of increase slows significantly after this point, with consumption levels fluctuating between 1180 and 1280 units until time step 27. Following time step 27, consumption trends upward again, surpassing the previous peak and stabilizing between 1400 and 1450 units by the final time step. The total number of times agents chose skimmed or semi - skimmed milk begins at zero and increases steadily throughout the simulation. The total number of milk choices made by all agents begins at zero at time step 0 and increases linearly as the simulation progresses. The uniformity of the slope implies a constant rate of decision - making across the agent population, likely reflecting that the combined influences of habit, social interaction, and perception of milk attributes are sufficient to prompt a consideration of choices at every step without significant fluctuation. Following the tenth time step, the rate of decline moderates, with the mean quantity decreasing more gradually to approximately 750 by the twentieth time step. Subsequently, the decline resumes, dropping to roughly 650 by the twenty - fifth time step and reaching a minimum of approximately 250 by the thirty - second time step. This gradual upward trend reflects the accumulation of habit strength through repeated reinforcement of the choice over time, as agents continue to select whole milk and the habit modifier incrementally boosts the choice function for the incumbent product. The variance among simulation runs is minimal in the initial steps but widens slightly as the simulation progresses, indicating that while the overall habit formation mechanism is consistent, individual agent - level variations begin to influence the aggregate mean later in the process. This trend aligns with the model's representation of growing habitual reinforcement as agents increasingly adopt skimmed and semi - skimmed milk, consistent with broader shifts in consumer behavior during the modeled period. The trend suggests that while initial decision variability is high, the divergence in choice function scores among agents increases more gradually over time, indicating a progressive stratification of preferences as agents interact and adapt within the social network. This phase indicates a period of relative equilibrium where the initial adjustments have settled, and agents are maintaining a consistent level of discrepancy between their perceived health values and their actual choices. This rapid early increase followed by a stable plateau suggests that agents quickly adjust their choices or perceptions to create a consistent, steady - state deviation between their internal environmental values and their observed milk consumption choices.
