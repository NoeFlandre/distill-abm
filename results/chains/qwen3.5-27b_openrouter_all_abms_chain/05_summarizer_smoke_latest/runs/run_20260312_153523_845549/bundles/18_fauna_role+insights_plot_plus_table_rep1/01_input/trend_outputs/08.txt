The simulation demonstrates a robust and rapid decline in the male megaherbivore population, culminating in total extinction across all 40 experimental runs. The aggregate population, starting at 125 individuals, exhibits an immediate and steep downward trajectory. During the initial phase, specifically between tick 0 and tick 231, the population decreases with a significant slope of -0.149. The rate of decline accelerates between tick 232 and tick 463, where the slope steepens to -0.244, indicating that the interaction between hunter pressure and prey vulnerability is most lethal during this interval. By tick 500, the mean population has plummeted to a critical threshold where recovery is statistically impossible. A second distinct phase of decline occurs between tick 696 and tick 927 with a slope of -0.029, followed by a deceleration phase where the population hovers at low single digits before vanishing. Despite minor fluctuations and detected change points around tick 125 and 250, the overall trend remains consistently negative until the mean population reaches zero by tick 1300. The rapid collapse, combined with the absence of long-term stability, suggests that the configured hunting costs and foraging radii are sufficient to drive the prey species to extinction in under 2000 time steps.
