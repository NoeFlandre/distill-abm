The mean value health deviation begins at zero and increases sharply, reaching approximately 0.5 by the 10th step. This rapid rise indicates that, early in the simulation, agents' perceived health values of milk choices quickly diverge from the implied health values suggested by those choices. This initial divergence may be driven by the high initial habit strength and the low susceptibility to social norms, which delays immediate adjustments to choices. From step 10 to around step 25, the rate of increase slows, with the deviation stabilizing between 0.55 and 0.6. This plateau suggests a period where agents' choices and their underlying health perceptions reach a temporary alignment, possibly due to the gradual influence of social interactions and the adjustment of individual dispositions. In the final segment, from step 25 to step 32, the mean value health deviation rises again, reaching values close to 0.75. This renewed increase implies that agents begin to perceive a significant misalignment between their health evaluations and their milk choices, potentially triggering a reconsideration of habits. The steady rise without a decline indicates that the model does not achieve a complete realignment of perceived health values with choices within the observed time frame. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of habit strength and social influence in delaying or accelerating shifts in consumer behavior. Interventions that reduce habit strength or increase social susceptibility could lead to faster realignment of health perceptions with milk choices, potentially accelerating the shift towards healthier options.
