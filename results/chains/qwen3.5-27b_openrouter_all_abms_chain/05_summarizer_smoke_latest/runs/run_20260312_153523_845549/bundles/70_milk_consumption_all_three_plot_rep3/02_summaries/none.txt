Context and Goals: This agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. We aim to explore how individual perception, established habits, and social influence drive decision-making processes. The model measures the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person to validate against observed macro-level data.

Model Mechanics: The simulation consists of 1,000 agents representing consumers situated within a social network. Each agent evaluates milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics. Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms. Agents may adjust their choices if their disposition to consider alternatives is triggered.

As agents interact within the social network and receive information from their peers, their established habits for whole milk are challenged. The habit strength, set at a high initial value of 10, is gradually weakened by these interactions, leading to a significant shift in consumption patterns. Between 10 and 20 time steps, the decline continues but at a slower rate, stabilizing around 750 units. This phase reflects a period where the initial rapid change in consumer behavior begins to level off as the social network reaches a new equilibrium. Agents who have already shifted to alternative milk types reinforce this trend through their interactions, while those holding onto whole milk consumption face increasing social pressure to conform. From 20 to 30 time steps, the average consumption of whole milk further decreases to approximately 350 units, with minor fluctuations indicating ongoing adjustments in consumer preferences. These fluctuations may result from varying levels of social conformity and the persistence of some agents in maintaining their habits. The final decline towards the end of the simulation period, reaching about 250 units, suggests a nearly complete shift away from whole milk, driven by the cumulative effects of social influence, health perceptions, and habit modification over time. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the critical role of social networks and peer influence in driving consumer behavior changes. Interventions aimed at promoting healthier choices should leverage these social dynamics to accelerate the adoption of preferred alternatives. Understanding the interplay between habit strength and social conformity can help tailor strategies to effectively shift consumer preferences towards more sustainable and health-conscious options.
