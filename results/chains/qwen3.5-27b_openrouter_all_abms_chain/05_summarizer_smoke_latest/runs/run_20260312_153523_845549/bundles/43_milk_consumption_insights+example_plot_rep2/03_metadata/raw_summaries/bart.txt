 The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards . Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics of each alternative .  The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at zero and increases rapidly over the first 10 time steps . Consumption levels remain relatively stable between steps 10 and 26, fluctuating between 1,200 and 1,250 units . Consumption rises again to reach approximately 1,450 units by the end of the simulation at step 32 . For decision makers, the rapid initial adoption highlights the importance of early interventions to promote healthier milk choices .  The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed starts at 0 and increases rapidly, reaching approximately 1250 units by time step 11 . The rapid initial uptake underscores the impact of initial perceptions and social influence, while the subsequent stability and final increase suggest the need for continuous engagement to maintain and enhance healthy consumption habits .  The mean value environmental deviation begins at zero at the initial time step, indicating that agents' initial milk choices were perfectly aligned with their held environmental values . This rapid rise suggests that as agents interact within the social network and evaluate alternatives, a significant misalignment emerges between their environmental preferences and their actual consumption choices .
