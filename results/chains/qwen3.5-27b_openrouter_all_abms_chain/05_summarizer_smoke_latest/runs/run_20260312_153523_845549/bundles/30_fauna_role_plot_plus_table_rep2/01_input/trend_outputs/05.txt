The cumulative number of postpartum female megaherbivores hunted by forager bands exhibits a distinct pattern of rapid initial increase followed by a plateau. Over 40 simulation runs, the mean trajectory begins at 0 and rises sharply to approximately 117 individuals by step 1448, with individual simulation runs showing a final range between roughly 105 and 121. The rate of increase is highest in the initial phase, with a significant statistical trend (p=0.000) observed up to step 1391, where the slope is consistently positive. Following step 1391, the rate of hunting declines significantly; the trend analysis indicates a period of stability with no significant trend between steps 1392 and 1623. In the final observed window (steps 1856 to 2087), the cumulative metric shows a statistically significant decreasing trend (p=0.000), suggesting that the accumulation of kills ceases or slows drastically as the simulation approaches its end. The maximum aggregate value recorded is 119.0 at step 1933, after which the metric fluctuates slightly without further accumulation. This trajectory reflects a system where hunting pressure effectively depletes the targetable population of reproductive females within the first ~4 years (1460 steps) of the simulation, leading to a cessation of kills in the long term.
