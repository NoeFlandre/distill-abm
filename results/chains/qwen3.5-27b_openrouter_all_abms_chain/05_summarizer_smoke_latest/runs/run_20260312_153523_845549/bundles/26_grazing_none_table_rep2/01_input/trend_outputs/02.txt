The simulation monitors the average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock across the agent population. At the onset of the simulation (time step 0), the deviation is negligible, starting at 0.0074. This indicates that initially, the agents' livestock numbers are very closely aligned with the recommendations. However, the system evolves rapidly from this baseline. The deviation rises consistently, signaling a growing divergence from the recommended numbers as agents adapt to the environment and interact. This upward trend is statistically significant for the first half of the simulation period. The metric reaches its global maximum of 0.3814 at time step 33, marking the peak of behavioral or ecological adjustment following the initial conditions. Following this peak, the trajectory reverses. There is a sustained decline in deviation, driven by the learning capabilities of the different household types. The Mann-Kendall trend analysis confirms a significant decreasing trend from approximately time step 33 through to time step 65. While the deviation decreases from its peak, the system does not fully return to the initial near-zero state. By the final time step of 100, the value settles at 0.1911, remaining higher than the initial condition, which suggests a shift to a new operational equilibrium. The dynamics reveal a cyclic component, indicated by a dominant oscillation period of 49.00 time steps, likely driven by the feedback loops between the heterogeneous agent behaviors (E-RO, E-LBD, E-RO-SL1) and the stochastic environmental factors such as the high rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 against a mean of 200). The transition from rising to falling deviation around time step 33 to 50 coincides with the agents utilizing social learning and learning-by-doing to recalibrate their herd sizes in response to the environment.
