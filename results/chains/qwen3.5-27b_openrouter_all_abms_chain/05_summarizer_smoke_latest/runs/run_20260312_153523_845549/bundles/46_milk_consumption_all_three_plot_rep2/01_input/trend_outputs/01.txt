The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent begins at approximately 2,600 units and shows a rapid decline over the first 10 time steps, reaching around 950 units. This sharp decrease can be attributed to the initial susceptibility of consumers to social influence and the perceived health benefits of alternative milk types, which are modeled with a mean initial health value of 1.69. As agents interact within the social network and receive information from their peers, their established habits for whole milk are challenged. The habit strength, set at a high initial level of 10, begins to erode as the cognitive dissonance threshold is crossed, prompting agents to reconsider their choices. Between time steps 10 and 18, the consumption stabilizes around 800 units, suggesting a period of adjustment where some agents maintain their whole milk consumption while others transition to skimmed or semi-skimmed options. This plateau may reflect the interplay between habit persistence and the gradual influence of social norms, with the social susceptibility parameter set at 0.2. A second decline occurs between time steps 18 and 30, with consumption dropping to approximately 300 units by the end of the simulation. This final drop likely indicates a tipping point where the majority of agents have fully adopted alternative milk types, driven by continued social reinforcement and the low environmental value of whole milk, modeled with an initial mean of 1.12. The consistency of these trends across simulation runs underscores the robustness of the model in capturing the historical shift in milk consumption. For decision makers, this simulation highlights the importance of social influence and perceived health benefits in driving consumer behavior changes. It suggests that targeted interventions aimed at enhancing health awareness and leveraging social networks can effectively accelerate the adoption of healthier alternatives.
