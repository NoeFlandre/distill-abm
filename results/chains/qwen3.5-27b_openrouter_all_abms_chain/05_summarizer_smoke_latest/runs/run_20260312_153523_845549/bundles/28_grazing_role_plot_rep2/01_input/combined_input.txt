Context and Goals

This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. In this version, each farmer stays on their own plot of land. They decide how many animals to keep based on how the grass is doing and what others are doing. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. There are three main ways farmers behave: baseline (E-RO), learning from their own results (E-LBD), and learning from neighbors (E-RO-SL1). The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock.

The mean household initial risk attitude for social learning agents remains constant across all simulation steps, ranging from 0 to 100. The values observed span from approximately -0.12 to 0.18, with no significant variation or trend over time. This indicates that the initial risk attitudes of the agents are static throughout the simulation, reflecting their predefined dispositions to deviate from the recommended number of livestock without any adjustment based on experience or neighbor interactions.

The mean household risk attitude exhibits significant divergence among agents over time. Initially, risk attitudes cluster between -0.1 and 0.1 during the first five time steps. From approximately time step 10 to 25, a rapid increase occurs in most trajectories, with risk attitudes rising sharply. By time step 30, the mean household risk attitude for many agents stabilizes between 0.5 and 0.8. However, some trajectories show a marked decline starting around time step 40, with values dropping below 0.0 by time step 60. A subset of agents continues to decline, reaching negative values as low as -0.3 by the final time step. The spread between the highest and lowest risk attitudes widens significantly after time step 20, indicating increasing heterogeneity in household risk behavior.

Over the first 30 steps, the biomass consistently decreases, reaching its lowest point around step 30 to 40, where it averages between 40000 and 42000 across different simulations. This decline suggests a period of increased grazing pressure or reduced grass regeneration, likely due to the initial conditions and farmer behaviors. Following this trough, starting around step 40, there is a significant and rapid increase in green biomass. This upward trend accelerates until approximately step 60, with most simulation lines crossing the 50000 and 60000 thresholds. From step 60 to the final step of 100, the growth rate slows, and the biomass levels plateau. By step 100, the simulations stabilize at varying high levels, generally ranging between 55000 and 63000, indicating a new equilibrium where grazing and regeneration have balanced at a higher biomass level compared to the initial state.

After step 40, the values stabilize significantly, hovering between 0.0 and 1.2 for the remainder of the simulation up to step 100. Some individual simulation runs show slightly higher peaks around step 65, but the overall range remains below 1.5.

As the simulation progresses, inequality starts to increase gradually, reaching a Gini coefficient of approximately 0.015 by step 40. This initial slow increase suggests that differences in livestock management strategies begin to affect herd health outcomes unevenly among households. After step 40, the Gini coefficient begins to rise more sharply, indicating an acceleration in the divergence of livestock health across households. By step 80, the inequality has increased significantly, with the Gini coefficient reaching values around 0.06 to 0.09. The trend continues to rise towards step 100, where the Gini coefficient for some simulations exceeds 0.09, indicating high levels of inequality in livestock health distribution. This pattern suggests that over time, the initial homogeneity in livestock management evolves into a state where some households consistently maintain healthier herds than others, likely due to the compounding effects of their learning strategies and interactions.

The final counts at timestep 100 range from approximately 15 to 54 households, indicating a wide variability in the extent of social learning adoption across different simulation runs. This variability suggests that the dynamics of social learning are influenced by interactions among households and their specific circumstances within the grazing system.
