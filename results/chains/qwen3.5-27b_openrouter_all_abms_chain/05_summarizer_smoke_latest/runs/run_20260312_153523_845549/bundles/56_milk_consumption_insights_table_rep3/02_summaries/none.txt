Context and Goals:

The overall objective of the agent-based model is to reproduce adoption behaviours of milk consumption by the British public (1974-2005), by replicating individual preferences and decision factors. Specifically, the goal of the model is to explore the influence of perception, habits and social influence in an individual's decision-making process of milk choice. The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against observed macro level data.

In particular, the study conducts an experiment to compare the performance of two model variants in reproducing overserved milk consumption trends. These variants present different mechanisms for how agents become disposed to consider their choices, representing a threshold based, and a probability-based approach. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence, and finally, choice evaluation where agents consider the impact of their choices and may adjust their future decisions.

The agents in the model represent adult consumers who occupy a random position in an information environment. Each agent has a disposition to consider alternative milk choices. Two disposition mechanisms are tested in the model, a threshold-based approach, and a probability-based approach. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. These are computed at the initialisation of the simulation and then calculated at each time step. An agent's milk choice function is modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. Agents ascribe different relative importance to each constituent part of the choice function (health factors and environmental factors). If the disposition requirement has been met, consumption of each milk type is split proportionately by the size of each choice function, modulated by the other influences. If not, agents keep their existing choice.

Agents (n=1,000) start with an existing choice based on the dominant position of whole milk versus skimmed varieties in 1974 (start year of the data). All agents are part of a social network. Each agent in the network can sense and be influenced by the choice function of each milk alternative for other agents in their network. Links between agents are unidirectional, and influence occurs as a function of interaction probability, with the degree of influence characterised by agent susceptibility. Social norms are globally perceived by agents and impact the weightings of the choice function.

The simulation output demonstrates a pronounced and sustained reduction in the average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent across the modeled timeframe. Initial consumption at index 0 was recorded at 2654.81 units, establishing a high baseline consistent with historical 1974 preferences. By index 32, consumption had fallen to 253.10 units, indicating a near-complete substitution of whole milk with other varieties by the end of the period.

Trend analysis confirms a statistically significant decreasing trajectory during the initial phases. Between indices 0 and 14, the trend remained consistently downward, with steepest slopes observed between indices 5 and 9. However, the rate of decline exhibited variability in later stages. Periods between indices 15 and 19 and again between 25 and 29 showed intervals where the trend was not statistically significant, suggesting temporary stabilization or fluctuation in adoption rates. Despite these pauses, the overall direction remained negative.

Specific local fluctuations punctuated the general decline. Local peaks occurred at index 19 (773.96 units), index 27 (620.98 units), and index 30 (335.18 units). Conversely, local minima were identified at index 18 (760.00 units), index 26 (594.35 units), and index 29 (323.46 units). These oscillations align with a dominant periodicity of approximately 15 time steps, suggesting cyclical adjustments in consumer behavior driven by the interaction of habit, social influence, and perception. Multiple inflection points, including indices 5, 13, 16, 19, 24, 25, 27, and 30, mark shifts in the momentum of consumption change.

The substantial drop from the maximum value of 2654.81 units to the minimum of 253.10 units reflects the model's capacity to replicate the historical shift in milk preferences. For decision makers, the persistent downward trend underscores the strength of the transition away from whole milk, likely driven by entrenched social norms and health perceptions. The periods of stabilization indicate that while the long-term trend is clear, short-term consumption levels can resist change due to habit thresholds. Strategic interventions would need to account for these cyclical dynamics and the high initial inertia of established consumption habits to effectively influence future choices.

The average weekly consumption of alternative milk varieties exhibits a pronounced upward trajectory over the simulation period, initiating at 5.29 units and concluding at 1456.59 units. The initial phase, covering time steps 0 through 9, is characterized by a statistically significant increasing trend, reflecting a rapid transition in consumer behavior away from the incumbent whole milk dominance. During this interval, consumption levels rise sharply, establishing a foundation for the long-term adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed options.

Following the initial surge, the system enters a period of fluctuation between time steps 10 and 24. This oscillation implies a dynamic equilibrium where individual perception and social influence counterbalance each other.

This late-stage growth suggests that the initial barriers to adoption were eventually overcome, leading to near-saturation of alternative milk preferences within the agent population.

For decision makers, this pattern highlights that shifts in consumption habits are non-linear, featuring periods of rapid uptake interspersed with stabilization phases driven by existing habits and social networks. Interventions aimed at altering consumption patterns should account for these stabilization periods, recognizing that initial momentum may pause before a second wave of adoption occurs as social norms and individual evaluations evolve.
