The simulation results demonstrate a substantial long-term increase in the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. At the initial time step, consumption is minimal at 5.29 units, reflecting the historical dominance of whole milk. A period of significant growth occurs between time steps 0 and 9, characterized by consistent upward trends. Following this initial surge, the metric enters a phase of relative stability and fluctuation between time steps 10 and 24. During this interval, values oscillate around a mean of approximately 1220, with a local maximum recorded at time step 11 reaching 1265.47 units and a local minimum at time step 18 dropping to 1183.39 units. This plateau suggests a temporary equilibrium where habit and social conformity balance the drive toward alternative choices. From time step 25 onwards, the increasing trend resumes significantly, driving the metric to its maximum observed value of 1456.60 units at the final time step 32. The overall trajectory demonstrates a successful substitution effect from whole milk to lower-fat alternatives. For decision makers, this pattern implies that while early interventions yield rapid adoption gains, sustained long-term growth may require strategies to overcome mid-period habituation plateaus. The significant rise in alternative milk consumption underscores the effectiveness of health perception and social influence mechanisms in shifting consumer behavior over decades.
