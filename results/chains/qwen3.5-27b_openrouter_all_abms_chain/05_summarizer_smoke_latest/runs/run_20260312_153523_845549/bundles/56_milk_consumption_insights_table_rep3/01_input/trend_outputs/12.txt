The mean deviation between agents' held environmental values and the implied values by their milk choices follows a trajectory characterized by a rapid initial divergence followed by a prolonged plateau. The metric initiates at 0.0000 at index 0. During the first phase (indices 0-9), the system undergoes a statistically significant upward trend. Rolling Mann-Kendall tests confirm a significant increasing trend with a p-value of 0.027 for the intervals covering steps 0-4 and 5-9. This phase culminates in a peak deviation of 13.4756 at index 13. 

Following index 13, the system transitions into a state of relative stasis. Between indices 14 and 32, the deviation fluctuates narrowly within the range of 13.4153 to 13.4756, never returning to the initial baseline of zero. Statistical tests on later windows (indices 10-29) show p-values ranging from 0.086 to 0.221, indicating a lack of statistically significant trend direction once the peak is approached. A dominant oscillation period of 15.00 steps is observed overlaying this high-level plateau.

From a policy perspective, the persistence of high deviation scores suggests that a structural misalignment can form rapidly after the simulation begins and remain entrenched for the duration of the observation window. This indicates that for the agents modeled, internal environmental values do not naturally correct themselves to match consumption choices. For decision-makers, this implies that without active intervention to realign the choice environment, a persistent gap between consumer values and market behavior is the expected equilibrium, suggesting that market forces or individual will alone may be insufficient to close the values-action gap.
