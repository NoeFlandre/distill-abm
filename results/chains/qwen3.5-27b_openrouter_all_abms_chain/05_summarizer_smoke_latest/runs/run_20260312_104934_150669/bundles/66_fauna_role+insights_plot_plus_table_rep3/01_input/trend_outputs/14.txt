Vegetation availability remains remarkably stable across the 200-year simulation, with the average proportion of green patches hovering at approximately 32.7%. The system starts at 33.0% coverage and ends at 33.0%, showing only a minimal net decline of 0.0023 percentage points. A statistically significant minimum of 24.9% occurred at tick 365, representing a sharp, transient dip of about 24% relative to the initial state, before full recovery was achieved by the end of the simulation. While the long-term average is stable, the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis reveals a distinct structural shift: vegetation coverage experienced a significant increasing trend for the first 1,623 steps (approximately 4.4 years), followed by a sustained but statistically significant decreasing trend for the remainder of the simulation. The data exhibits a dominant oscillation period of roughly 63 steps, indicating a consistent, short-term cyclic pattern in plant growth and consumption. Decision-makers should note that while the environment demonstrates high resilience by returning to near-initial conditions, the shift from increasing to decreasing coverage after the four-year mark suggests that internal dynamics, likely linked to prey density, have exerted a gradual but persistent downward pressure on vegetation availability.
