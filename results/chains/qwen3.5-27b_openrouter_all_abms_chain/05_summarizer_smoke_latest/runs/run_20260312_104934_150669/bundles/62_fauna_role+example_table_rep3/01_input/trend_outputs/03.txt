This deceleration in the accumulation rate suggests that initial hunting pressure is intense but becomes less efficient over time, potentially due to declining prey density or increased search costs as the population is reduced. Between time steps 1160 and 1855, the trend becomes volatile, alternating between periods of stagnation and slight growth. Following this peak, a statistically significant decreasing trend emerges between steps 1856 and 2087. In the context of an extinction model, a decline in the aggregate cumulative mean implies that the subset of simulations contributing to the average is shrinking, likely because individual runs have terminated due to local herd extinction events, thereby removing higher cumulative kill counts from the subsequent mean calculations. By the final recorded time step, the mean value settles at 117.5. Given that there are 365 ticks per year in the model configuration, this oscillation likely corresponds to seasonal variations in foraging behavior or prey availability influencing the hunting success rate. The early change points identified at steps 125 through 850 reflect rapid adjustments in the system dynamics before the hunting pressure stabilizes near the carrying capacity of the environment or the limits of the forager bands.
