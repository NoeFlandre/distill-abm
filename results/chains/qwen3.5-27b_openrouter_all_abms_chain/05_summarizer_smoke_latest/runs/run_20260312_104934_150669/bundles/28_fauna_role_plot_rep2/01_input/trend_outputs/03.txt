The cumulative number of male prey animals killed by forager bands exhibits a consistent pattern of rapid increase followed by a plateau across all simulation runs. Initially, from step 0 to approximately step 500, the cumulative number of males killed rises steeply, with values ranging from approximately 10 to 110 males killed. This period reflects high hunting pressure, likely driven by the presence of a robust male population and effective foraging strategies. Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of increase slows, with the cumulative number of males killed reaching values between approximately 100 and 125. After step 1000, the cumulative count stabilizes, with most simulations converging to a range of 110 to 125 males killed. This plateau suggests that either the male population was significantly depleted, hunting efficiency declined, or environmental constraints limited further predation. The variability among simulations indicates stochastic influences, such as differences in herd distribution or forager behavior, but the overall trend underscores a saturation point in hunting activity.
