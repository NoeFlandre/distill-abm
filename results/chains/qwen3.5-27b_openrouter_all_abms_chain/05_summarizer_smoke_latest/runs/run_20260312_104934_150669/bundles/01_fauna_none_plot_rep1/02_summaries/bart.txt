The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Hunters follow dynamic optimal foraging theory, balancing energy status, prey value, density, and costs. The environment controls fodder density, regrowth, and seasonality. The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model begins at an initial value of approximately 125 across all simulation runs. The rapid rise in the early stages indicates high hunting pressure on postpartum females, likely due to their higher perceived value to foragers. The leveling off of the curve after step 1500 suggests the prey population may have reached a demographic equilibrium under the given environmental and hunting pressures. The total energy of male prey animals begins at approximately 12,000 units at the start of the simulation. Within the first 300 steps, there is a rapid and consistent decline across all simulations. By 1000 time steps, all trajectories converge to values close to zero, indicating near-complete extinction of the prey population.
