The total energy of male prey animals begins at approximately 12,500 units at the start of the simulation. This energy reserve drops precipitously, falling to roughly 3,500 units by time step 400. This initial rapid decline suggests a severe and immediate energetic deficit, likely driven by a combination of high foraging costs, seasonal resource scarcity, and predation pressure exceeding the energy intake of the male cohort. Following this initial crash, a brief plateau and minor fluctuation occur between steps 400 and 500, where energy levels hover between 3,000 and 5,000 units. This transient stability may represent a short-term buffer effect where remaining males exploit remaining resources, but it is insufficient to arrest the long-term decline. By step 750, the total energy has diminished to near zero, indicating the complete energetic collapse of the male population. This collapse precedes total extinction in the population, as individuals lose the capacity for mobility and reproduction before perishing. The high variance observed in the simulation runs after step 500 indicates that while the ultimate fate of the male population is extinction under these parameters, the timing of collapse is sensitive to stochastic environmental or behavioral factors. For decision-makers, this trend highlights the critical vulnerability of male megaherbivores to energetic stress; the rapid loss of male energy reserves acts as an early warning signal of impending population collapse. Management strategies aimed at preserving energy intake—such as habitat connectivity or reduced hunting pressure on males—could be crucial in preventing this trajectory.
