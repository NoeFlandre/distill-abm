The population of female megaherbivores begins at 125 individuals and undergoes a rapid, precipitous decline over the first 500 time steps, dropping to fewer than 20 individuals. This initial phase of severe attrition suggests that the immediate interaction between the 20 immortal foragers and the prey population creates unsustainable hunting pressure, depleting the reproductive segment of the herd much faster than natural birth or migration rates can compensate. Following this initial collapse, the decline continues but at a significantly reduced rate, characterized by a 'long tail' of extinction. Between time steps 500 and 1500, the population hovers near zero, with minor fluctuations indicating sporadic breeding events or temporary reprieves in predation pressure, yet ultimately failing to achieve population recovery. The population reaches absolute extinction (zero individuals) by approximately time step 2300. This trajectory implies that while high initial hunting efficiency rapidly decimates the herd, the remaining stressors—such as internal mortality and starvation thresholds—prevent the population from stabilizing even at low levels. For decision-makers, this indicates that under these specific parameters, the system lacks resilience; once the population falls below a critical threshold during the initial rapid decline, recovery becomes impossible, rendering the species extinct regardless of later environmental conditions.
