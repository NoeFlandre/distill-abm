The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The persistent energy deficit implies that the model configuration does not allow forager populations to survive long-term without significant intervention. The cumulative number of male prey killed by foragers increases sharply from 0 to approximately 100 over the first 500 time steps. This initial surge likely reflects the high density of available prey early in the simulation and the efficient foraging behavior of the agent bands. The population of male prey begins at approximately 125 individuals. A rapid decline is observed immediately, with the population dropping below 40 individuals by time step 200. This trend suggests that the socio-natural dynamics lead to a complete collapse of the male population. The total energy of the male population begins at 12,500 and experiences a rapid decline, dropping below 4,000 by approximately step 450. This initial phase is characterized by a steep descent across all simulation runs, indicating a systemic loss of biomass likely driven by high hunting pressure or starvation. The population effectively becoming extirpated by step 2,311, where the final energy value is 0.0. The vegetation percentage stabilizes between 32.96% and 33.00%. There are no sustained deviations from this range for the remainder of the simulation. The consistency in the metric underscores the system’s ability to maintain fodder availability.
