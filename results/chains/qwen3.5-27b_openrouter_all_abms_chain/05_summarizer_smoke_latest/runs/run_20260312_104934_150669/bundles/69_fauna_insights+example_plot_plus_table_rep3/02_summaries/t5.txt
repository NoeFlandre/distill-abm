The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The cumulative number of male prey killed by foragers increases sharply from 0 to approximately 100 over the first 500 time steps. This initial surge likely reflects the high density of available prey early in the simulation, enabling hunters to target males frequently without significant competition or resource depletion. Reducing hunting pressure becomes increasingly ineffective at altering cumulative outcomes. After 1600 time steps, the cumulative birth count stabilizes at a plateau, with minimal new births recorded. This stabilization implies that environmental constraints or internal herd dynamics have restricted further population expansion. The subsequent plateau indicates that the environment or predation pressure creates a strong equilibrium that prevents indefinite growth. A steady decrease in prey numbers indicates a system unable to buffer hunting pressure or maintain equilibrium under the given socio-natural conditions. The total energy of male prey animals begins at 12500 and declines rapidly over the first 400 time steps, falling to approximately 4000. This sharp reduction is indicative of high initial mortality or energy expenditure. This reduction in fodder availability aligns with the parameter seasonality set to 0.8, indicating that environmental conditions strongly influence vegetation dynamics. After this initial decline, the vegetated patch percentage recovers to around 33.0% within a short period, suggesting rapid grass regrowth.
