The cumulative number of male prey killed by foragers increases sharply from 0 to approximately 100 over the first 500 time steps, averaging a rise of about 0.2 animals per step. This initial surge likely reflects the high density of available prey early in the simulation, enabling hunters to target males frequently without significant competition or resource depletion. The trend continues to grow, albeit at a slower pace, reaching 110 animals by around 1,000 steps. As the cumulative count approaches 120 near the 1,500-step mark, the rate of increase diminishes significantly, indicating that the prey population or hunting efficiency has stabilized. Beyond 1,500 steps, the curve plateaus, with minimal changes observed until the final time steps, where the mean cumulative value settles around 117.5. Variance across simulation runs is highest during the initial growth phase but stabilizes as the total converges. This pattern suggests that early hunting success is highly variable due to stochastic interactions, while later dynamics are driven by predictable limits in prey availability or hunter saturation. For decision-makers, the rapid early rise implies that initial conservation interventions may have a stronger impact on long-term prey survival if implemented before 500 steps. Conversely, once the system stabilizes, reducing hunting pressure becomes increasingly ineffective at altering cumulative outcomes.
