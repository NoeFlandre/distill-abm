The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore. It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival. The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape. The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands begins at zero and rises sharply during the initial 500 time steps. By around 1,000 time steps, the rate of increase slows significantly, suggesting that the prey population is becoming more depleted. Decision makers should note that sustained hunting pressure on male prey may lead to rapid population depletion, emphasizing the need for strategies that balance predation with population sustainability. The total energy of male prey animals begins at approximately 12,500 units at the start of the simulation. This energy reserve drops precipitously. The total number of prey animals starts at 250 and undergoes a rapid decline, dropping below 100 by approximately 400 time steps. By around 1000 steps, the number of male animals in most runs falls to near zero. The total energy available in the population of female prey animals starts at approximately 12,500 units and experiences a rapid decline over the first 400 time steps. This sharp decrease is likely due to the combined effects of high predation pressure from human foragers and the energetic costs associated with movement and maintenance. After step 1000, the total energy continues to decline gradually, eventually reaching near-zero levels by step 2000.
