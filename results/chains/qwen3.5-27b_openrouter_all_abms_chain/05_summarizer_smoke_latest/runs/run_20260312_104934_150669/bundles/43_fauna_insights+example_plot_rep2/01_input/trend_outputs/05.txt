The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted begins at zero and shows a rapid increase during the initial 300 time steps, reaching values near 60 in some simulations. This rapid rise is followed by a continued, though slightly less steep, increase between 300 and 700 time steps, where the total reaches approximately 100 to 110. Postpartum female kills continue to accumulate more slowly until around 1,200 time steps, at which point the rate of increase significantly diminishes. By the final time steps, the cumulative number of postpartum female kills stabilizes between 100 and 122 across the different simulation runs. The observed pattern suggests that foragers initially exploit the readily available population of high-value targets, specifically reproductive females, which aligns with optimal foraging strategies prioritizing energy return. The eventual plateau indicates a potential depletion of this specific demographic class within the accessible population or a saturation of hunting effort. For decision makers, this trend highlights the critical vulnerability of reproductive females to early and intensive hunting pressure, which can rapidly impact herd demography and long-term population viability.
