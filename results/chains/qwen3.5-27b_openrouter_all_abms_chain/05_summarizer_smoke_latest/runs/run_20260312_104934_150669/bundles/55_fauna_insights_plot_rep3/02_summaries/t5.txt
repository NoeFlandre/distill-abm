The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The cumulative number of male prey animals killed by forager bands increases steadily from the start, reaching approximately 100 by step 500. This rapid increase indicates high hunting efficiency during the early phase, likely driven by abundant prey availability and optimal foraging behavior. After step 1000, the cumulative count stabilizes, fluctuating minimally between 115 and 120 until the end of the simulation. Extinction of female prey animals by around step 600 implies that the population could not sustain itself under the given conditions, likely due to the combined effects of environmental pressures, hunting, and the inability to reproduce sufficiently to counter mortality. Trend suggests a significant impact of hunting pressure and environmental factors on the survival of male prey populations. In the landscape initially fluctuates around 32% for the first few hundred steps. A sharp decline occurs between approximately steps 200 and 400. Similar patterns of sudden declines and recoveries are observed at later intervals.
