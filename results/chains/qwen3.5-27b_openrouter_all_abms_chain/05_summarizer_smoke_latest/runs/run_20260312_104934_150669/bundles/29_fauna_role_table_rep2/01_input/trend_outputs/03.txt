The simulation of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model demonstrates a distinct three-phase trajectory in the cumulative number of male prey animals hunted by forager bands across the ensemble of runs. Initially, the mean cumulative count rises steadily from 0.0000 at the start of the simulation. Statistical analysis of the early intervals confirms a strong, significant increasing trend, indicating an aggressive predation phase where the 20 initialized foragers actively target male megaherbivores within the landscape. This accumulation continues through the first 1160 time steps.

Between time steps 1160 and 1855, the growth rate stabilizes. During this intermediate period, statistical tests indicate no significant trend, reflecting a plateau where the rate of new kills balances with population constraints or foraging efficiency limits. The ensemble mean reaches its global maximum of 120.1667 at time step 1876. Given the initial prey population of 250, this peak suggests that approximately half of the available male cohort has been removed from the system by this point, consistent with high hunting pressure parameters and optimal foraging decisions.

Following the peak, the metric exhibits a statistically significant decreasing trend from time step 1856 to 2087. By the final recorded sample at time step 2311, the mean value declines to 117.5000. This reduction in the ensemble mean, despite the metric being cumulative, implies heterogeneity in the simulation outcomes where a subset of runs ceased accumulating kills, likely due to local prey extinction or forager saturation, lowering the average across the 80 matched series. The oscillation indices and change points further highlight periods of volatility in hunting success, correlating with the defined seasonal and environmental dynamics of the model. Overall, the trend reflects a system driven toward predation saturation followed by a stabilization where further cumulative growth is inhibited by ecological limits.
