The megafaunal hunting pressure model (mhpm) is an interactive, agent - based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival. Context : the model simulates a socio - ecological system containing human foragers and sexually dimorphic megaherbivore herds. It incorporates principles of optimal foraging theory, where hunters make decisions based on energy costs and food value. Goals : the primary goal is to determine conditions under which human hunting pressure, combined with environmental constraints, leads to the extinction of megafauna. The number of active human hunter - gatherer bands in the simulation remains constant at 20 throughout the entire duration of the simulation, spanning from step 0 to approximately step 2300. For decision makers, this implies that the initial conditions and parameters set for forager dynamics, such as movement cost, food storage, and immortality, create a balanced scenario where human population pressure is static and does not escalate over time, potentially stabilizing the pressure on megaherbivore populations. The total energy of foragers exhibits a consistent downward trend, starting from an initial value close to 0 and decreasing steadily over time. This indicates that the current parameter settings result in unsustainable foraging conditions, emphasizing the need to adjust parameters such as food value, hunting efficiency, or movement costs to achieve a viable energy balance. For decision makers, this pattern indicates that initial hunting pressure is high but diminishes as the simulation progresses, likely reflecting the depletion of easily accessible prey or changes in forager behavior over time. The cumulative number of female prey animals killed by forager bands increases rapidly during the initial phase of the simulation, reaching approximately 120 kills by around 1000 steps. The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed shows a steep initial rise across all simulation runs, with values increasing rapidly from zero to approximately 60 by step 200. That, under the given model parameters, a substantial portion of the reproductive female population is targeted early in the simulation, potentially limiting herd replenishment and contributing to long - term population decline. This pattern suggests that the conditions in the model environment support a gradual increase in population growth, likely due to factors such as adequate food resources and manageable predation pressure. These findings imply that under the current parameter settings, the socio - ecological system maintains a balance that allows for population expansion without immediate threat of extinction from overhunting or starvation. The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population exhibits a rapid increase in the initial phase of the simulation, reaching approximately 200 deaths by step 500. Following this initial sharp decrease, a temporary stabilization or slight fluctuation is observed between steps 400 and 500, where values range roughly between 3, 000 and 5, 500 units. This pattern suggests that under the specified conditions, the male prey population is unable to sustain energy levels beyond the first few thousand steps, likely due to high predation pressure and limited environmental recovery. For decision - makers, this indicates that the current parameter settings result in unsustainable hunting pressure and / or environmental degradation, leading to the collapse of the male prey population's energy reserves and likely contributing to broader population decline. After step 1, 000, the total energy stabilizes at near - zero levels, indicating that the female prey population has likely been reduced to a critically low number or has experienced local extinction. This implies that, under the specified conditions, the interaction between human foraging pressure and environmental factors leads to a rapid depletion of the female prey population's energy reserves, which could severely impact their survival and reproductive success. The percentage of green patches in the simulation initially fluctuates around 32 % but shows a sharp decline to approximately 23 % at around 400 steps, indicating a significant reduction in available fodder.
