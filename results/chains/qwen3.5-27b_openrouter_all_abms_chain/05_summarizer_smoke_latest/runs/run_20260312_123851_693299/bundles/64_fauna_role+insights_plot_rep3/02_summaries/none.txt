The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival. The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape.

Context:
The model simulates a socio-ecological system containing human foragers and sexually dimorphic megaherbivore herds. It incorporates principles of optimal foraging theory, where hunters make decisions based on energy costs and food value. The environment is dynamic, featuring grass growth and seasonal changes.

Goals:
The primary goal is to determine conditions under which human hunting pressure, combined with environmental constraints, leads to the extinction of megafauna. By manipulating parameters, users can test scenarios of environmental degradation and predation intensity.

The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands in the simulation remains constant at 20 throughout the entire duration of the observed time steps. This stability implies that the initial configuration of 20 foragers, combined with the setting where foragers are immortal, ensures no fluctuations in the human population. This constant human presence is critical for evaluating the persistence of megafauna under sustained, unvarying hunting pressure. Decision-makers should recognize that under these conditions, any changes in megafauna populations would be solely attributable to environmental factors and interaction dynamics rather than variations in human population size.

The total energy of foragers exhibits a consistent downward trend across all simulation runs. Starting at an initial value of approximately 0 at step 0, the energy declines steadily to around -12,000 by step 1,000. This decline accelerates, reaching approximately -32,000 by step 2,000. By the end of the recorded data at step 2,300, the total energy for foragers drops to approximately -38,000. This continuous reduction suggests that the foragers are expending more energy through movement and hunting activities than they are gaining from the food resources available. For decision-makers, this trend indicates that under the current model parameters, forager bands are in a state of persistent energy deficit, which could lead to unsustainable foraging practices and potential population decline if not addressed through changes in resource availability or foraging efficiency.

These findings highlight the importance of understanding early hunting dynamics and environmental carrying capacities in predicting long-term outcomes for megaherbivore populations.
