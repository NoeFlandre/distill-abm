The mean habit factor applied to the whole milk choice function demonstrates a consistent upward trajectory throughout the simulation period. Initiating at 1.0000 at time step 0, the metric ascends steadily to reach a maximum of 1.0502 by time step 32. The aggregate mean across the series is 1.0359, characterized by a low standard deviation of 0.0110, which reflects high stability across the thirty simulation runs. Statistical testing confirms a significant increasing trend across all rolling windows, with inflection points noted at indices 7 and 12. In the context of modeling the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed varieties, this increasing habit factor suggests that the psychological inertia associated with the incumbent product strengthens over time rather than diminishing. For decision makers, this implies that established consumption patterns are resilient forces. Even as social norms and health perceptions evolve to favor alternatives, the habitual reinforcement of the current product choice persists and grows. Strategies aimed at accelerating the market shift must therefore actively target and disrupt these strengthening habit loops, as informational changes alone may be insufficient to overcome a habit factor that is statistically increasing.
