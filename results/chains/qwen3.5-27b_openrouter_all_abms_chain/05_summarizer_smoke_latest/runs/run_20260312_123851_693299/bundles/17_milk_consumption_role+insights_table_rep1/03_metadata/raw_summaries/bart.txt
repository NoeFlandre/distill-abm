 The agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms .  The simulation output characterizes a decisive structural shift in aggregate whole milk consumption . The aggregate adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk alternatives exhibits a robust and monotonic increase throughout the simulation period . This acceleration suggests that as agents overcome initial habit inertia, represented by the high incumbent habit parameter, social influence and health perceptions drive a compounding shift in preferences .  The mean habit factor applied to the whole milk choice function demonstrates a consistent upward trajectory throughout the simulation period . For decision makers, this implies that marketing interventions should be designed to maintain momentum through the plateau phase to ensure long-term market penetration . The trajectory indicates that while the initial adoption of alternative milk varieties may require external triggers such as health perception or social norms .  The rate of change in choice function deviation can signal when consumer segmentation requires more granular marketing approaches to address the varied drivers of consumption habits . The system exhibits a consistent increasing trend in the mean deviation between health perceptions and consumption choices . This indicates a systematic divergence where consumer choices lag behind evolving health values .
