Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Hunters follow dynamic optimal foraging theory, balancing energy status, prey value, density, and costs. Ungulates are sexually dimorphic, reproduce, age, and move as herds During the initial phase, hunters actively target male prey, likely due to their higher food value compared to females and calves. The rate of accumulation slows significantly after step 600, with only marginal increases observed between steps 1000 and 2000. By the end of the simulation, the cumulative number of males killed stabilizes in the range of 110 to 125. The number of female prey animals begins at approximately 125 in all simulation runs, with the steepest rise occurring during the initial phase. After step 1000, the rate of increase slows significantly, and the number of deaths stabilizes near 230 to 250. This plateau suggests that the prey population has either been reduced to a level where predation pressure has diminished or that natural mortality has reached a steady state. The total energy of all prey animals within the simulation begins at approximately 25,000 units. The variability in energy levels across different simulation runs increases over time. This could be due to the interplay of various factors such as herd movement, reproduction rates, and environmental changes affecting prey population differently in each run.
