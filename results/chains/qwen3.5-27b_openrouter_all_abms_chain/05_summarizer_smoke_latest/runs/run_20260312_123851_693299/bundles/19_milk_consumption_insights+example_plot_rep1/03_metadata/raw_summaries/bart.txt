 The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards . The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person . The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against observed macro level data .  The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at zero and increases rapidly from step 1, reaching approximately 1,250 by step 10 . For decision makers, this trend suggests that the underlying factors driving switch from whole milk to lower-fat alternatives result in a predictable and continuous rate of adoption across the population .  The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at zero and rises sharply in the initial time steps, reaching approximately 0.5 by time step 4 . In the final segment, the mean value health deviation increases steadily but gradually, reaching a final value near 0.75 by step 32 . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial health messaging and social influence in driving consumer behavior .  The steady rise without a plateau suggests that the equilibrium is dynamic rather than static . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of ongoing health messaging and social influence strategies to guide milk consumption patterns effectively . The rapid initial shift underscores the immediate impact of health information, while the later fluctuations emphasize the need for sustained interventions to maintain long-term behavior changes .
