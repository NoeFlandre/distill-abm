The average total number of livestock begins at 90.0 across all households at the start of the simulation. During the initial phase, specifically from timestep 0 to timestep 32, the metric exhibits a significant decreasing trend, with the rate of decline gradually slowing after timestep 20. This downward trajectory reaches its lowest point of 41.01 at timestep 37. Following this minimum, the system enters a period of stabilization between timesteps 33 and 43, where no statistically significant trend is observed. Subsequently, starting from approximately timestep 44, the average total livestock count enters a sustained phase of recovery, characterized by a significant increasing trend that continues until the simulation concludes at timestep 100. By the final timestep, the metric reaches a value of 47.55. Overall, despite the recovery in the latter half of the simulation, the final livestock level remains significantly lower than the initial endowment, representing a net reduction of nearly half over the 100-timestep period. In the context of the extended RAGE model, this pattern suggests that the heterogeneous population of agents, comprising baseline (E-RO), learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1) types, initially responded to environmental variability and risk constraints by destocking. The risk mode being active, combined with a high rainfall standard deviation and no mandatory resting periods, likely pressured households to reduce herd sizes to avoid overgrazing or loss. The subsequent recovery indicates that the learning mechanisms enabled agents to adapt their strategies, rebuilding their herds to a new equilibrium that balances livelihood needs with the ecological limits imposed by the vegetation growth rates and rainfall parameters.
