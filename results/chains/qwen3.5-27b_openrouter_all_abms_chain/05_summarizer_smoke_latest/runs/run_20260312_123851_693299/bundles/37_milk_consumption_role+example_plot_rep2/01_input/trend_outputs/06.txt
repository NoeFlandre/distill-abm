The mean whole milk quantity consumed by agents begins at approximately 2,600 units at the initial time step. A rapid and steep decline is observed from time step 0 to 10, where the mean consumption drops to roughly 1,000 units. This sharp reduction suggests that the initial high habit strength (set at 10.0) for whole milk was overcome by other influencing factors, such as health perception and social influence, leading to a widespread shift in consumer behavior. After time step 10, the decline continues but at a slower pace, with the mean consumption reaching approximately 700 units by time step 20. This indicates a stabilization period where the remaining consumers of whole milk are more resistant to change, possibly due to higher cognitive dissonance thresholds or stronger individual habits. A further significant drop occurs between time steps 25 and 30, reducing the mean consumption to about 300 units. This could reflect a critical mass of agents influencing the remaining whole milk consumers through social norms and conformity, accelerating the shift away from whole milk. By the final time step, the mean consumption of whole milk stabilizes at around 250 units, indicating that the majority of agents have transitioned to alternative milk varieties. The low variance across simulation runs throughout the time steps suggests that the model parameters and agent interactions consistently drive the transition from whole milk to other milk types.
