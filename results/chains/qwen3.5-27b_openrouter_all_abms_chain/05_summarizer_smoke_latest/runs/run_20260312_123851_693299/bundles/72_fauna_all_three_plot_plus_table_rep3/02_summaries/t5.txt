The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape. It simulates a socio-ecological system containing human foragers and sexually dimorphic megaherbivore herds. Negative energy values and the sustained linear decline imply that the socio-ecological system forces the hunter-gatherer population into an increasingly unsustainable state. The cumulative number of male prey killed begins at zero and increases sharply during the first 400 time steps, reaching approximately 100 individuals. This rapid rise reflects high initial hunting pressure, likely driven by optimal foraging behavior where prey are abundant and easily accessible. The trend reverses after time step 1624, with the rate of cumulative births slowing and eventually declining by time step 1855. This downturn suggests that the initial reproductive momentum could not be sustained, likely due to the compounding effects of forager hunting pressure and resource competition. The cumulative number of prey deaths begins at zero and exhibits a rapid, steep increase during the first 400 time steps. The population is effectively extinct for the remaining duration of the simulation. This pattern indicates that the combined pressures of human hunting and environmental constraints rapidly overwhelm the reproductive capacity of the megaherbivores. The system shows multiple inflection points, suggesting non-linear phases of resource extraction and population dynamics. The consistent downward trend across all statistical windows confirms an irreversible trajectory toward total energy depletion.
