The total energy available in the male megaherbivore population begins at 12,500 units and exhibits a rapid decline over the initial 100 time steps. This sharp drop, averaging a loss of 14.8 units per step, suggests that the combined pressure of human foraging and environmental constraints immediately impacts the energetic reserves of the male cohort. As male animals are the primary target for foragers due to higher food value relative to processing costs, this initial depletion likely reflects the targeting of large, healthy individuals before the population structure is significantly altered. Between steps 100 and 400, the rate of decline fluctuates, with a noticeable plateau of lower energy levels stabilizing between 3,800 and 4,000 units. This period indicates a potential bottleneck where the remaining male population struggles to maintain energy intake against predation and seasonal resource limitation, possibly due to the disruption of herd dynamics and increased movement costs. The Mann-Kendall trend analysis confirms a statistically significant decrease in energy accumulation throughout the first 1,600 steps. Following this, brief intervals of stabilization or minor increases in energy are observed, but these are temporary. By step 2,284, the total energy reserves for the male population reach zero and remain there for the remainder of the simulation. This outcome signifies a total extinction of the male population, which would inevitably lead to the collapse of the entire megaherbivore herd as reproduction ceases. The rapid extinction timeline implies that under these parameters, specifically with a high forager count and significant predation costs, the socio-ecological system cannot sustain a viable breeding population. Decision makers should consider that reducing predation pressure or increasing food availability is critical to preventing such a rapid demographic collapse.
