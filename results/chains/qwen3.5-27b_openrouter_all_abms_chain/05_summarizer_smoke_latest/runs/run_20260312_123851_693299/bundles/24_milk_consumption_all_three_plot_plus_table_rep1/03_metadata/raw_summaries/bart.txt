 The simulation consists of 1,000 agents representing consumers situated within a social network . Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms . The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at a very low baseline of 5.29 units . The data indicates a robust and statistically significant downward trend throughout the majority of the simulation .  The total number of milk choices made by all agents starts at 0.00 at the initial step and increases linearly, reaching 32,000 by the final time step . The data demonstrates a clear, monotonic increase with no significant decline or stagnation . The trend suggests that marketing strategies focusing on health benefits and social norms can effectively drive long-term behavioral change .  The mean deviation between agents’ held health values and the implied health values from their milk choices begins at 0.0000 and rises steadily . This upward trajectory indicates a growing misalignment over time between what agents believe about the health benefits of milk types and the actual health implications embedded in their consumption choices . For decision-makers, this trend highlights that while initial consumer segmentation is volatile, preference differences eventually stabilize, suggesting that marketing strategies targeting specific consumer profiles become more effective .  The metric experiences slight fluctuations, with minor decreases observed at time steps 18 and 19, before stabilizing around 13.4153 by time step 31 . This pattern implies that initial shifts in consumer behavior are swift and driven by strong alignment with perceived environmental benefits, eventually settling into a stable consumption pattern .
