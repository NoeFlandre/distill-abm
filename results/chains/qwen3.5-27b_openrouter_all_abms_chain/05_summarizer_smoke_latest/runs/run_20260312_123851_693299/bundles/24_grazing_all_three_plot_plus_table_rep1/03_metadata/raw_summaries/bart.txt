 Simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors . The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time . The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock .  The average amount of actual available green biomass starts at 45000 and experiences a slight initial increase before declining to a minimum value of 42057 at approximately step 27 . By the final time step, the biomass reaches a plateau of roughly 121,073, surpassing the initial conditions . This trajectory implies that a period of resource scarcity and behavioral adjustment is necessary to trigger the positive feedback loops required for ecosystem recovery and sustainable stocking rates .  The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households increases steadily over the simulation period . The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero across all simulation runs and rises sharply between time steps 7 and 30 . The trend then slows, with the aggregate count stabilizing for the remainder of the simulation, indicating that social learning adoption reaches a saturation point .
