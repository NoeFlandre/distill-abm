The agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005. The simulation consists of 1,000 agents representing consumers situated within a social network. Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms. The overall pattern highlights a non-linear adoption curve characterized by an early rapid uptake, a period of stagnation, and a final consolidation of the new consumption norm. The resurgence suggests that as social networks adjust and health awareness solidifies, a larger portion of the population eventually transitions to lower-fat options. A dramatic expansion reflects the successful displacement of whole milk consumption by lower-fat alternatives over the modeled historical period. Initial acceleration is most pronounced during the first five time steps, where rate of adoption is statistically significant. For decision makers, the non-linear progression implies that health-led marketing is most effective at the onset of a trend, while mid-period strategies must focus on reinforcing social norms. The mean deviation between agents' held environmental values and the implied values by their milk choices begins at 0.0000 and increases rapidly to a maximum of 13.4756 at time step 13. Statistical analysis confirms a significant increasing trend during the first 10 time steps. For decision makers, this indicates that consumer habits and social norms quickly establish a persistent gap between values and actions.
