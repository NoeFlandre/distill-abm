Context and Goals

This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. In this version, each farmer stays on their own plot of land. They decide how many animals to keep based on how the grass is doing and what others are doing. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. There are three main ways farmers behave: baseline (E-RO), learning from their own results (E-LBD), and learning from neighbors (E-RO-SL1). The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock.

The mean household initial risk attitude for social learning agents remains constant across all simulation steps, ranging from -0.10 to 0.18. These static values indicate that the agents' initial dispositions to deviate from the recommended number of livestock do not change over time. The wide range of initial attitudes suggests that there is a significant diversity in risk tolerance among the agents, which could influence their decision-making processes. The lack of change in these values implies that initial conditions play a critical role in shaping agent behavior, and these predispositions persist throughout the simulation. Decision-makers should consider the impact of these initial risk attitudes on the overall system dynamics, as they may affect the stability and predictability of grazing patterns.

The mean household risk attitude begins near 0 at the start of the simulation and increases rapidly over the first 20 time steps, reaching values between 0.2 and 0.7. This initial rise suggests a period of heightened risk-taking, likely as farmers adjust to the model conditions and respond to initial pasture conditions. Following this initial phase, risk attitudes diverge into stable bands. The highest bands maintain risk attitudes between 0.6 and 0.8 for the duration of the simulation, while lower bands fluctuate or decline. Specifically, a subset of households shows a sharp decline in risk attitude beginning around time step 40, dropping to negative values by time step 60. These declines result in final risk attitudes ranging from -0.1 to -0.35. Conversely, households in the upper bands maintain their high risk posture throughout the simulation. This divergence indicates that while some farmers stabilize at high risk levels, others react strongly to accumulated experience or environmental feedback by adopting a significantly more conservative risk posture over time.

The average amount of reserve biomass starts at approximately 90,000 units and experiences a slight decline during the first 10 time steps, dropping to around 88,000 units. This initial decrease suggests that grazing pressure from the livestock population exceeds the initial regrowth capacity of the grass, leading to a temporary depletion of reserve biomass. Around time step 10, the biomass levels begin to stabilize, maintaining values between 85,000 and 90,000 units until approximately time step 40. This stabilization phase may indicate a balance between livestock consumption and grass regrowth, as farmers adjust their livestock numbers or grazing strategies based on initial observations. From time step 40 onward, the reserve biomass shows a rapid and sustained increase, rising sharply to reach values between 110,000 and 130,000 units by time step 100. This growth trend could reflect the combined effects of improved pasture management strategies, such as reduced grazing pressure or increased learning among farmers, allowing the grass to recover and accumulate biomass over time. The divergence in biomass levels across simulation runs during this phase suggests variability in how different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, E-RO-SL1) influence grazing decisions and resource outcomes. By the end of the simulation, the average reserve biomass stabilizes within a range of 110,000 to 130,000 units, indicating that the system has reached a new equilibrium with higher biomass levels compared to the initial conditions. This outcome highlights the potential benefits of adaptive learning and social influence in promoting sustainable grazing practices, which could inform decision-makers about the importance of incorporating these mechanisms in managing grazing lands effectively.

The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches begins at approximately 45,000 units. Over the first 40 time steps, there is a noticeable decline in biomass, reaching a minimum value of around 40,000 units. This reduction likely reflects the initial grazing pressure exerted by the livestock as the farmers, operating under the E-RO (baseline) behavioral type, adjust their herds without significant learning effects. From step 40 onwards, a robust recovery in biomass is observed, with levels increasing rapidly and crossing the initial 45,000 unit mark by step 50. The growth continues steadily until the simulation concludes at step 100, where the average green biomass stabilizes between 60,000 and 65,000 units. This significant increase implies that the initial reduction in livestock numbers or grazing intensity was sufficient to allow the pasture to regenerate, eventually leading to a surplus of available biomass. The divergence in trajectories among the individual simulation runs, which becomes most pronounced between steps 40 and 80, suggests that while the general trend of recovery is consistent, specific local dynamics and stochastic factors lead to varying outcomes in biomass accumulation. This highlights the importance of monitoring local conditions as they can influence the recovery rate of grazing lands.

Decision makers should focus on promoting strategies that enhance learning and adaptation, as these appear to lead to more resilient and productive livestock management.

The average number of livestock destocked by households begins near zero, then sharply rises to a peak of approximately 7.5 animals at around time step 2. Following this initial surge, the mean destocking value declines steadily, reaching roughly 2 animals by time step 20 and continuing to decrease to about 1 animal by time step 40. Between time steps 40 and 100, the values stabilize, hovering between 0.5 and 1 animal per household with minimal variation. This early peak in destocking suggests an initial adjustment period where households likely react to resource constraints before adapting their livestock management strategies. The subsequent decline and stabilization indicate that households progressively optimize their stocking levels relative to pasture capacity, reducing the need for destocking over time. For decision-makers, this pattern highlights the importance of supporting households during the initial years to manage resource shocks, while long-term interventions should focus on sustaining adaptive practices to maintain low destocking rates.

This stabilization implies that the initial fluctuations settle into a more predictable pattern, where the proportion of non-learning households remains relatively constant for the remainder of the simulation. 

A subset of simulations shows a more pronounced decline, with some reaching as low as zero households by time step 60. This extreme reduction suggests that under certain conditions, the E-RO strategy may become less viable or advantageous, leading households to adopt alternative strategies such as learning from their own experience or from neighbors. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the transient nature of the E-RO strategy's dominance in the early stages of the simulation. The eventual stabilization or decline of non-learning households suggests that external factors, such as resource availability or social influences, play a significant role in shaping long-term behavioral patterns. These insights emphasize the importance of considering adaptive strategies in managing grazing lands, as reliance on non-learning behaviors may limit resilience and sustainability over time.
