The average amount of reserve biomass starts at approximately 90,000 units and experiences a slight decline during the first 10 time steps, dropping to around 88,000 units. This initial decrease suggests that grazing pressure from the livestock population exceeds the initial regrowth capacity of the grass, leading to a temporary depletion of reserve biomass. Around time step 10, the biomass levels begin to stabilize, maintaining values between 85,000 and 90,000 units until approximately time step 40. This stabilization phase may indicate a balance between livestock consumption and grass regrowth, as farmers adjust their livestock numbers or grazing strategies based on initial observations. From time step 40 onward, the reserve biomass shows a rapid and sustained increase, rising sharply to reach values between 110,000 and 130,000 units by time step 100. This growth trend could reflect the combined effects of improved pasture management strategies, such as reduced grazing pressure or increased learning among farmers, allowing the grass to recover and accumulate biomass over time. The divergence in biomass levels across simulation runs during this phase suggests variability in how different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, E-RO-SL1) influence grazing decisions and resource outcomes. By the end of the simulation, the average reserve biomass stabilizes within a range of 110,000 to 130,000 units, indicating that the system has reached a new equilibrium with higher biomass levels compared to the initial conditions. This outcome highlights the potential benefits of adaptive learning and social influence in promoting sustainable grazing practices, which could inform decision-makers about the importance of incorporating these mechanisms in managing grazing lands effectively.
