The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households starts at zero and rises sharply in the first 20 time steps, reaching approximately 80. This initial surge suggests that farmers quickly adjust their herds in response to grazing conditions or early learning outcomes. After this period, the rate of increase slows, with values ranging between 80 and 170 by the final time step, depending on the specific behavioral type. The divergence in trends indicates that different learning strategies lead to varying levels of destocking activity. Farmers employing the baseline strategy (E-RO) show consistently higher destocking compared to those learning from their own results (E-LBD) or from neighbors (E-RO-SL1). These differences highlight the impact of learning mechanisms on herd management decisions. For decision-makers, these trends underscore the importance of promoting adaptive learning strategies to optimize livestock management and pasture sustainability. The varying slopes suggest that behavioral interventions could significantly influence long-term outcomes in resource allocation and environmental health.
