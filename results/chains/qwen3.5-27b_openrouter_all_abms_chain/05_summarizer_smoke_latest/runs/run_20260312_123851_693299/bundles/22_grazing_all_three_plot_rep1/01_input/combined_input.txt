Context and Goals

This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. In this version, each farmer stays on their own plot of land. They decide how many animals to keep based on how the grass is doing and what others are doing. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. There are three main ways farmers behave: baseline (E-RO), learning from their own results (E-LBD), and learning from neighbors (E-RO-SL1). The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock.

The mean household initial risk attitude for social learning agents remains constant across all simulation steps, ranging from -0.10 to 0.18. These static values indicate that the agents' initial dispositions to deviate from recommended livestock numbers do not change over time. For decision-makers, this suggests that social learning agents' risk attitudes are stable, which could imply consistent behavioral patterns in livestock management and potential predictability in resource use.

The mean household risk attitude begins near 0.5 for all households, reflecting the initial parameter setting. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid divergence in risk attitudes, with some households increasing their risk tolerance to values near 0.8 while others drop below 0. This divergence is likely driven by the behavioral types E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1, which cause households to respond differently to early outcomes. Between time steps 20 and 40, most households stabilize at higher risk attitudes, indicating a trend toward riskier livestock management. However, some households exhibit a sharp decline in risk tolerance after time step 40, reaching negative values by time step 60. This suggests that certain households are adopting more conservative strategies, possibly due to adverse outcomes or learning from neighbors. By the end of the simulation, the mean household risk attitude is highly variable, with some households maintaining high risk tolerance and others adopting very low or negative risk attitudes. This variability underscores the importance of behavioral diversity in grazing systems, as it influences pasture management and long-term sustainability.

The average amount of reserve biomass starts at approximately 90,000 units and experiences a slight decline during the initial 15 time steps, reaching values as low as 81,000 units. This early reduction likely reflects the initial overgrazing or insufficient rest periods before the farmers adjust their management strategies. Around time step 40, the reserve biomass begins to rise sharply across all simulations, with values exceeding 100,000 units by time step 50. This growth phase may indicate that farmers are adopting better grazing practices, either through individual learning or social influence, which allows the grass to recover and accumulate reserves. By time step 60, the rate of increase slows, and most simulations stabilize between 110,000 and 125,000 units by the end of the simulation period. This plateau suggests that the system has reached a new equilibrium where the grazing pressure is balanced with the regenerative capacity of the grass. The slight variation among simulation runs in the later stages highlights the role of individual farmer decisions or local environmental differences in influencing outcomes. For decision-makers, these trends emphasize the importance of promoting sustainable grazing practices early on to prevent biomass depletion and foster long-term pasture health.

The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches begins at approximately 45,000 units. Over the first 40 time steps, there is a noticeable decline in biomass, reaching a minimum value of around 40,000 units. This reduction likely reflects the initial grazing pressure exerted by the livestock as the farmers, operating under the E-RO (baseline) behavioral type, adjust their herds without significant learning effects. From step 40 onwards, a robust recovery in biomass is observed, with levels increasing rapidly and stabilizing between 60,000 and 64,000 units by step 80. The divergence in trajectories across simulation runs suggests that variations in farmer decision-making, particularly in adjusting livestock numbers in response to grass availability, contribute to differing levels of pasture recovery. For decision-makers, this highlights the importance of considering how initial grazing pressure impacts pasture health and the potential variability in recovery outcomes under baseline management strategies.

The average total number of livestock, including destocked animals, across all households begins at 90 at the start of the simulation. Over the first 30 time steps, this number decreases sharply to approximately 40. This initial decline suggests an active response by farmers to reduce herd sizes, likely due to insufficient grass availability and the risk-averse nature of their decision-making processes as defined by the simulation settings. From time step 30 to 40, the livestock numbers remain relatively stable near the 40 level, indicating a period where farmers have adjusted their herd sizes to the prevailing grazing conditions. Between time steps 40 and 60, a divergence occurs in the trends observed across different simulation runs. Some runs show a gradual increase in livestock numbers, reaching up to approximately 60 by time step 80, while others remain stable around 45 to 50. This divergence implies that varying learning behaviors and interactions among farmers begin to influence herd management strategies differently as the simulation progresses. By the end of the simulation at time step 100, livestock numbers stabilize, with some runs maintaining around 60 animals and others hovering near 45 to 50. This stabilization suggests that farmers eventually find a balance between herd size and available grazing resources, influenced by their adaptive learning behaviors and interactions within the community. Decision makers should consider these trends to understand the potential outcomes of different learning and interaction strategies on livestock management and pasture sustainability.

The average number of livestock destocked by households begins near zero and rises sharply in the first 5 time steps, peaking at approximately 7.5. This rapid increase likely reflects the initial overstocking of livestock relative to available resources, leading to immediate resource depletion. After this peak, there is a steady decline in destocking, with values dropping to around 3 by time step 20. This decline suggests that households adjust their livestock numbers or grazing strategies in response to initial resource constraints. By time step 40, the average destocking stabilizes at a low level, fluctuating between 0 and 1 for the remainder of the simulation. This stabilization indicates that households have reached a more sustainable balance between livestock numbers and resource availability. The variability across simulation runs is higher in the early stages, particularly before time step 20, but diminishes over time. This reduced variability implies that, despite differences in individual farmer behaviors, the system converges toward a stable equilibrium. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of early interventions to manage livestock numbers and prevent resource depletion, as well as the potential for learning mechanisms to promote long-term sustainability.

The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock starts at 0 across all simulation runs at timestep 0, indicating an initially equal distribution of livestock among households. Over the first 40 timesteps, the Gini coefficient remains relatively low, with values below 0.015, suggesting minimal inequality in livestock distribution during this period. From timestep 40 to 60, there is a marked increase in the Gini coefficient, as values rise sharply, indicating growing disparities in the distribution of healthy livestock. This rise is accompanied by increased variability across simulation runs, reflecting divergent outcomes based on farmer behaviors and environmental responses. By timestep 80, the Gini coefficient ranges between approximately 0.045 and 0.075, with some simulations showing higher inequality. In the final timesteps, the Gini coefficient continues to increase, reaching values as high as 0.090 in some runs, while others plateau around 0.030 to 0.045. This trend suggests that over time, certain learning and decision-making strategies lead to more unequal outcomes in livestock health distribution. For decision makers, this implies that without interventions, natural learning processes and behavioral dynamics among farmers may exacerbate disparities in livestock health, potentially affecting overall community resilience and productivity.

The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero across all simulation runs and begins to increase significantly between time steps 5 and 10. By time step 20, approximately half of the simulation runs show between 18 and 28 households actively learning from their neighbors. The rate of increase continues until around time step 40, after which the growth slows and stabilizes for most runs. By the end of the simulation at 100 time steps, the number of households engaged in social learning ranges between 16 and 54. The variation in final values suggests that the spread of social learning is highly sensitive to stochastic elements within the simulation. For decision-makers, this indicates that while social learning can eventually become a dominant behavior, the speed and extent of adoption vary considerably, requiring flexibility in policy design to accommodate different adoption trajectories.

The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households starts at zero and rises sharply in the initial phase, reaching approximately 80 by time step 20. This rapid increase suggests that farmers quickly adjust their herd sizes in response to early conditions, possibly due to risk-averse strategies or initial pasture degradation. Following this initial surge, the rate of destocking slows significantly, with values stabilizing between 60 and 70 for some simulation runs by time step 40, while others continue to rise more gradually. By the end of the simulation at time step 100, the cumulative destocked totals range widely, with some lines reaching as high as 170 and others plateauing around 60. The divergence in cumulative destocking trajectories across runs may reflect varying farmer learning behaviors and interactions within the model. These trends imply that while initial herd adjustments are uniform and pronounced, long-term strategies vary significantly, highlighting the importance of understanding farmer learning and social influences in designing effective livestock management policies.

The number of households following the E-RO strategy starts at 20 in all simulation runs. Over the first 10 time steps, there is significant divergence among simulations, with some runs showing an increase to values between 28 and 40, while others show a decrease to values between 5 and 15. This early divergence suggests high sensitivity to initial conditions or stochastic elements within the model. By approximately 15 time steps, the counts stabilize into distinct bands. A subset of simulations maintains higher counts, fluctuating between 24 and 28 for the remainder of the 100 time steps. Other simulations stabilize at lower levels, with counts ranging from 2 to 10. This stratification indicates that under the specified parameters, the population of non-learning agents does not converge to a single equilibrium state but rather persists in varying proportions across different simulation trajectories. The persistence of these agents, particularly in the higher-count bands, suggests that without social learning or individual learning incentives, a significant portion of the population may retain baseline behaviors despite environmental feedback or neighbor influence.
