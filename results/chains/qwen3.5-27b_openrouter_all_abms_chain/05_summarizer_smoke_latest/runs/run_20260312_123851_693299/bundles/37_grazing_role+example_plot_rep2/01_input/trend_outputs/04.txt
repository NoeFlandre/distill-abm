The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches begins near 45,000 units at the start of the simulation. Over the first 30 time steps, there is a general decline, with most simulation runs reaching a low point between 40,000 and 42,000 units around time step 35. This initial decrease is likely driven by early grazing pressure as farmers adjust their livestock numbers. From time step 35 to 60, the biomass levels rise sharply, with most runs increasing to approximately 55,000 to 60,000 units by time step 60. This growth phase may reflect adaptive management strategies by farmers, such as reducing livestock or learning from outcomes, allowing the pasture to recover. Beyond time step 60, the increase in biomass slows, and values stabilize in the range of 58,000 to 64,000 units by time step 100. The final biomass levels show variation across runs, with some reaching as high as 64,000 units while others remain closer to 58,000 units. This divergence likely results from differences in farmer behavior types, including learning from experience or neighbors, which influence grazing intensity and resource management over time. The overall trend indicates an initial period of resource depletion followed by recovery and stabilization as the system adapts.
