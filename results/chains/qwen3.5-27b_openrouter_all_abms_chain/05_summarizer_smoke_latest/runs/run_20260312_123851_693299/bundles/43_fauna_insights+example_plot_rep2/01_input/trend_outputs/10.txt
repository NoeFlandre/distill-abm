The total number of prey animals starts at 250 and undergoes a rapid decline, dropping below 100 animals by approximately 450 steps. By 600 steps, the population decreases further to roughly 50 animals, indicating that the combined effects of internal mortality and external hunting pressure are depleting the herd faster than reproduction can sustain it. The decline accelerates until the population stabilizes at zero for the majority of the simulation runs by 1,200 steps. Variance among simulation runs increases significantly between steps 400 and 800; while most runs converge toward extinction, a few maintain a small number of animals up to 1,500 steps, suggesting that specific stochastic interactions may temporarily buffer the population. The rapid loss of the herd suggests that under these parameters, the forager pressure and environmental constraints prevent population recovery, leading to inevitable extinction. Decision-makers should note that even with immortal foragers and food storage, the system fails to maintain a viable prey population, indicating the need to re-evaluate hunting efficiency or environmental support mechanisms to prevent total herd collapse.
