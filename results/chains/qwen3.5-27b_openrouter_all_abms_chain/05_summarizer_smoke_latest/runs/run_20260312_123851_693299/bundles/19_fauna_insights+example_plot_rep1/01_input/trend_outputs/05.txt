The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted begins at zero and shows a rapid increase during the initial 300 time steps, rising from 0 to approximately 100. This surge likely reflects the foragers' initial high engagement in hunting due to abundant prey availability and the energetic benefits of targeting reproductive females. After this initial phase, the rate of increase slows, and the curve begins to plateau around time step 500, with most simulation runs reaching cumulative values between 110 and 120 postpartum females killed by time step 700. The plateauing trend suggests that hunting pressure stabilizes as prey populations adjust or as foragers encounter diminishing returns due to lower prey density or increased hunting costs. This stabilization implies that the prey population is either adapting to hunting pressures or that foragers are shifting their foraging strategies to more efficient targets. Decision-makers should note that early interventions to manage hunting pressure could have significant impacts on the prey population, while later interventions may have limited effect due to the established equilibrium.
