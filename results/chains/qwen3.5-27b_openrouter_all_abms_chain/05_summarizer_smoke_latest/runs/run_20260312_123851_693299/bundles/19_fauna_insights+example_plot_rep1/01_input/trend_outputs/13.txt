The total energy of all animals starts at approximately 25,000 units and declines rapidly over the first 300 time steps. This sharp reduction likely reflects the combined effects of high initial hunting pressure and environmental resource constraints, which deplete the energy reserves of the prey population quickly. Between 300 and 500 time steps, the rate of decline slows, indicating a temporary stabilization as prey herds adapt or move to areas with better resource availability. However, a second significant drop occurs around 500 to 700 steps, potentially due to increased hunting efficiency or resource depletion in previously buffered areas. By 1000 steps, the total energy has diminished to near-zero levels, suggesting that the prey population has reached a critical threshold where energy reserves are insufficient to sustain the herd. The variability among simulation runs increases after 300 steps, indicating that stochastic factors such as herd movement patterns and individual hunting decisions contribute to divergent outcomes. For decision makers, this underscores the importance of balancing hunting pressure with resource regeneration rates to prevent rapid ecosystem collapse.
