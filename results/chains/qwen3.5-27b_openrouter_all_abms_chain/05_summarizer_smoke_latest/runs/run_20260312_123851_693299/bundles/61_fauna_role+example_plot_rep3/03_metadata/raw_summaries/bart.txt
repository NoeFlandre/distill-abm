 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore . It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival . The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape .  By 2000 steps, the total energy falls to approximately -30000, with the decline continuing to around -37000 by 2300 steps . The consistent downward trend across all simulation runs suggests that movement costs and hunting inefficiencies persistently outweigh energy acquisition from prey throughout the simulation duration . The cumulative number of female prey animals killed starts at zero and rises sharply during the first 500 steps .  There is a rapid decline in the population during the initial phase, with most simulations dropping below 20 individuals by 500 steps . By step 800, most runs show the male population dropping to fewer than 5 individuals, indicating a critical threshold of population collapse . Variance among simulation runs is high during the early decline (0 to 500 steps), indicating different rates of population loss, but converges significantly as the population approaches extinction .  A sharp and significant decline occurs between steps 200 and 400, with the percentage dropping to around 23% in some simulation runs . This indicates a rapid depletion of vegetation, possibly driven by high grazing pressure from the megaherbivore population . Following this decline, vegetation recovers quickly, returning to approximately 33% by step 600 . The percentage stabilizes near 33% with minor fluctuations .
