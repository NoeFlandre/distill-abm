This initial surge likely reflects a period of adjustment where households, particularly those using baseline or individual learning strategies, may overstock or misjudge carrying capacity, leading to rapid resource depletion and forced removals. Following this peak, the number of destocked livestock undergoes a steep decline, falling to around 3.4 by step 9 and further to roughly 2.7 by step 12. This downward trend continues through the early phase of the simulation, indicating that households are either adapting their stock levels to available resources or that the system is naturally correcting initial imbalances. By step 20, the average number of destocked livestock stabilizes near 1.0, and fluctuations become less pronounced. From step 30 onwards, the data exhibits a more stable pattern with minor oscillations, hovering between 0.5 and 1.0. The long-term stability suggests that most households have converged on sustainable stocking rates that align with the carrying capacity of their grazing land, minimizing the need for destocking. The variance across simulation runs decreases significantly after step 30, indicating a more uniform behavior among households as they adapt to the environmental conditions and social learning mechanisms within the model.
