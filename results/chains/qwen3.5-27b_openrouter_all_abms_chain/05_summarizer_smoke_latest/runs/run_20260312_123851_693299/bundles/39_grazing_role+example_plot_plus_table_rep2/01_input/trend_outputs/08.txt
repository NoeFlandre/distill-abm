The number of households practicing social learning starts at zero across all simulation runs. A rapid and significant increase occurs between time steps 0 and 40, indicating that social learning behaviors are quickly adopted by the population following an initial incubation period. During this early phase, the rate of adoption is highest, with households rapidly transitioning from non-learning to learning states as they observe the outcomes of neighbors or their own initial attempts. By approximately time step 63, the metric reaches its maximum aggregate mean of 32.07, signifying that roughly 53% of the total household population has engaged in social learning. Following this peak, the growth rate slows considerably, and the aggregate mean stabilizes between 30 and 32 households for the remainder of the simulation. This plateau suggests that while a critical mass of the population adopts social learning strategies to optimize livestock management, a substantial portion of the remaining households either stick to baseline behaviors or do not find sufficient incentive to switch strategies under the current model parameters. The data indicates a clear bifurcation in behavior, where about half the population eventually utilizes social information to guide grazing decisions, while the other half does not.
