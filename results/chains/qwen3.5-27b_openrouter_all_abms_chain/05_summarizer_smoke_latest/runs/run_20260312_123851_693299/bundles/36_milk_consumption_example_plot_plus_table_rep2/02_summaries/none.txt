Context and Goals:

The overall objective of the agent-based model is to reproduce adoption behaviours of milk consumption by the British public (1974-2005), by replicating individual preferences and decision factors. Specifically, the goal of the model is to explore the influence of perception, habits and social influence in an individual's decision-making process of milk choice. The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against observed macro level data.

In particular, the study conducts an experiment to compare the performance of two model variants in reproducing overserved milk consumption trends. These variants present different mechanisms for how agents become disposed to consider their choices, representing a threshold based, and a probability-based approach. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence, and finally, choice evaluation where agents consider the impact of their choices and may adjust their future decisions.

The agents in the model represent adult consumers who occupy a random position in an information environment. Each agent has a disposition to consider alternative milk choices. Two disposition mechanisms are tested in the model, a threshold-based approach, and a probability-based approach. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. These are computed at the initialisation of the simulation and then calculated at each time step. An agent's milk choice function is modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. Agents ascribe different relative importance to each constituent part of the choice function (health factors and environmental factors). If the disposition requirement has been met, consumption of each milk type is split proportionately by the size of each choice function, modulated by the other influences. If not, agents keep their existing choice.

Agents (n=1,000) start with an existing choice based on the dominant position of whole milk versus skimmed varieties in 1974 (start year of the data). All agents are part of a social network. Each agent in the network can sense and be influenced by the choice function of each milk alternative for other agents in their network. Links between agents are unidirectional, and influence occurs as a function of interaction probability, with the degree of influence characterised by agent susceptibility. Social norms are globally perceived by agents and impact the weightings of the choice function.

The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent begins at 2,654.81 units at time step 0. This initial high value reflects the historical dominance of whole milk in the market. The metric shows a sharp and consistent decline during the first 10 time steps, dropping to approximately 980.0 units. This rapid decrease suggests a strong initial shift in agent preferences, likely driven by early adjustments to the health and environmental characteristics of milk types as perceived by the agents. Following this steep drop, the rate of decline moderates. Between time steps 10 and 26, consumption slowly decreases from 980.0 to a local low of 594.35 units. This phase is characterized by several minor fluctuations and inflection points, indicating a stabilization period where agents balance established habits with evolving social influences. After time step 26, a second, more pronounced downward trend occurs, pushing consumption to its minimum value of 253.10 units by time step 32. The data indicates a steady reduction in the incumbent habit of whole milk consumption throughout the simulation, replaced by alternative choices as agents update their decision functions over time.

The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at a low level of 5.29 units per person at the initial time step. A rapid and sustained increase occurs during the first phase of the simulation, rising to approximately 1200 units by time step 9 and peaking near 1265 units at time step 11. This sharp ascent reflects a period where social influence and changing health perceptions drive a swift adoption of lower-fat milk alternatives, overcoming the strong initial habit of whole milk consumption. Following this initial surge, the growth trajectory slows significantly, entering a plateau phase between time steps 12 and 26 where consumption hovers between 1180 and 1240 units. This stability suggests a temporary equilibrium where existing habits and social norms balance the pressure to switch milk types. A distinct secondary increase begins after time step 26, driving consumption to a new peak of 1456 units at the final recorded time step. This resurgence indicates that the system re-evaluates, potentially overcoming previous habit thresholds to further entrench the preference for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk.

The total number of whole milk choices starts at 0.0 at time step 0 and increases steadily to 2194.57 by time step 32. The average value across the simulation period is 1130.88, with a standard deviation of 654.69, indicating moderate variability among simulation runs. The trend is consistently increasing throughout, with a statistically significant upward trend (p = 0.027) across all observed windows. Inflection points occur at time steps 1, 7, and 31, marking subtle shifts in the rate of increase, although no distinct peaks or valleys are detected. The rate of increase is most pronounced in the early steps, with a slope of approximately 76.2 between steps 5 and 9, gradually tapering to around 64.1 in the later steps (25–29). This suggests that while whole milk choices accumulate consistently, the pace of adoption slows slightly as the simulation progresses. The absence of oscillations beyond minor variations at steps 27–30 implies a relatively stable system without major fluctuations in collective consumer behavior.

The total number of choices for skimmed or semi-skimmed milk begins at zero at the start of the simulation and exhibits a continuous, monotonic increase throughout the entire period, concluding at approximately 25,644. The growth trajectory is initially slow; between time steps 0 and 5, the cumulative total rises modestly, reflecting the inertia imposed by the high initial habit parameter set to 10 for the incumbent choice. This initial lag suggests that agents are resistant to changing their milk preferences early in the simulation due to established habits. Following time step 5, the rate of adoption accelerates significantly, transitioning into a steep, linear-like ascent that persists until the final time step. By time step 15, the cumulative choices exceed 10,000, and by time step 30, the value surpasses 24,000. The absence of inflection points or valleys indicates a stable adoption process without periods of regression. The increasing trend is statistically significant across all evaluated windows, implying that the mechanisms of social influence and health perception eventually overcome initial habit barriers, driving a consistent shift in consumer behavior towards lower-fat milk alternatives as the simulation progresses.
