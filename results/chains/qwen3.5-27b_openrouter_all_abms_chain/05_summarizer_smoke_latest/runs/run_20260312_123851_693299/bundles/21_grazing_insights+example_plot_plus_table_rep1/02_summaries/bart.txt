RAGE model is a multi-agent simulation model that captures feedbacks between pastures, livestock and household livelihood in a common property grazing system. It implements three stylized household behavioral types (traditional TRAD, maximizer MAX and satisficer SAT) These types can be compared regarding their long-term social-ecological consequences. The average household risk attitude in the simulated population begins near zero, indicating an initial state of neutrality or balanced risk perception among agents. For decision makers, this implies a consistent baseline for risk-related behaviors in the context of pasture management and livestock allocation under the E-RO behavioral type. The average reserve biomass in the grazing system begins at approximately 90,000 units and experiences an initial decline, reaching a minimum value of roughly 84,114 units. The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero across all simulation runs and increases significantly during the initial phase of the simulation. For decision makers, the data highlights a critical adjustment period within the first 40 years where resource pressures are highest, followed by a system resilience that supports gradual herd rebuilding. The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households increases consistently throughout the simulation period. The rate of destocking is highest during the first 25 time steps, after which the slope decreases and stabilizes. The trajectory suggests that while some initial dynamics may favor non-learning behaviors, the system tends to reduce reliance on static strategies over time.
