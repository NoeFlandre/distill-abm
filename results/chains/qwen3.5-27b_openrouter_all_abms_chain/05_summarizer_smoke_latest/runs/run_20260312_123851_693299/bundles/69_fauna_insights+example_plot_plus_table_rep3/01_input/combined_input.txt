The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The landscape consists of an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges on a 211x211 patch matrix. The goals are to manipulate socio-natural dynamics to observe how environmental conditions, human hunting, and animal life history interact to either accelerate or buffer extinction or create equilibrium conditions. Hunters follow dynamic optimal foraging theory, balancing energy status, prey value, density, and costs. Ungulates are sexually dimorphic, reproduce, age, and move as herds. The environment controls fodder density, regrowth, and seasonality.

The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the simulation. This stability is maintained across all time steps, indicating that the human population does not experience demographic shifts, mortality, or migration under the current configuration where foragers are immortal and food storage is enabled. For decision makers, this suggests that the hunting pressure exerted on the environment is static, isolating the extinction dynamics to prey demography and environmental constraints rather than human population growth.

The total energy available to forager bands begins at 2,000 units and follows a consistent, monotonic decline throughout the simulation, terminating at approximately -38,229 units. The trajectory is characterized by a high degree of linearity, with statistical analysis confirming a significant decreasing trend across all rolling windows with near-perfect correlation coefficients. An initial phase of minor volatility is observed within the first 55 time steps, where values fluctuate between 1,263 and 1,990 units, suggesting a transient period of adaptation to hunting costs and prey availability before the system settles into a steady rate of energy loss. Subsequent fluctuations are minimal, with the slope of the decline remaining constant from roughly time step 360 onwards. This pattern indicates that the cumulative costs of movement and processing prey consistently outweigh the caloric intake derived from hunting under the current parameter configuration, such as the set foraging radius and animal densities. Decision makers should interpret this continuous downward trajectory as a signal of unsustainable resource management; without adjusting prey density, forager mobility costs, or food acquisition efficiency, the forager population faces inevitable energetic collapse.

The cumulative number of male prey killed by foragers increases sharply from 0 to approximately 100 over the first 500 time steps, averaging a rise of about 0.2 animals per step. This initial surge likely reflects the high density of available prey early in the simulation, enabling hunters to target males frequently without significant competition or resource depletion. The trend continues to grow, albeit at a slower pace, reaching 110 animals by around 1,000 steps. As the cumulative count approaches 120 near the 1,500-step mark, the rate of increase diminishes significantly, indicating that the prey population or hunting efficiency has stabilized. Beyond 1,500 steps, the curve plateaus, with minimal changes observed until the final time steps, where the mean cumulative value settles around 117.5. Variance across simulation runs is highest during the initial growth phase but stabilizes as the total converges. This pattern suggests that early hunting success is highly variable due to stochastic interactions, while later dynamics are driven by predictable limits in prey availability or hunter saturation. For decision-makers, the rapid early rise implies that initial conservation interventions may have a stronger impact on long-term prey survival if implemented before 500 steps. Conversely, once the system stabilizes, reducing hunting pressure becomes increasingly ineffective at altering cumulative outcomes.
