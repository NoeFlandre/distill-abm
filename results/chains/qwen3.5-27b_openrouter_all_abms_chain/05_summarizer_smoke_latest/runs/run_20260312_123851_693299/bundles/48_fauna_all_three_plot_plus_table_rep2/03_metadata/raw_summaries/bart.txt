 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore . It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival . The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape .  Statistical analysis confirms a strong, continuous downward trend with a significant negative slope throughout all examined rolling windows . The aggregate mean of the simulation runs mirrors this trajectory, decreasing from an initial value of 1,990 to a final deficit of approximately -38,233 at step 2,310 .  The cumulative number of births in the megaherbivore population begins at zero and remains there for the initial 725 time steps, a period corresponding to the gestational latency before the first offspring reach maturity . By the end of the simulation, the trend is characterized by a flattening curve, indicating that the population's reproductive output has been suppressed .  The total number of prey animals begins at approximately 167 and follows a steep, exponential decline during the initial phase of the simulation . This pattern indicates that the combined pressures of human hunting and environmental constraints rapidly exceeded the reproductive capacity of the prey population, leading to an irreversible collapse . The total energy available in the male megaherbivore population begins at 12,500 units .  The percentage of landscape patches that are currently vegetated begins at approximately 33%, reflecting the initial parameter setting for grass proportion . Within the first 365 time steps, corresponding to the first year of the simulation, the vegetation cover experiences a sharp decline, reaching a minimum of roughly 25% . This precipitous drop suggests a period of intense initial consumption by the megaherbivore population .
