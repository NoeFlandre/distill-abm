The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at a low value of 5.29 at the initial time step. This figure increases sharply over the first 10 time steps, rising to approximately 1200, indicating a rapid shift in consumer preferences away from whole milk. Inflection points are observed around time steps 5, 13, and 16, marking shifts in the slope of the decline. This progression mirrors the historical shift towards low-fat milk products. Skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents starts at a low value of approximately 5.29 at the beginning of the simulation. It experiences a rapid increase, reaching values above 1000 by the fifth time step and peaking near 1265.47 at the eleventh step. This sharp rise in alternative milk consumption likely reflects the initial adjustment period. The peak value of 13.4756 is recorded at time step 13.4.0 at the start of the simulation. This initial spike followed by a plateau suggests that agents quickly adjust their milk choices to align with the prevailing social norms and environmental perceptions. The collective misalignment between personal values and choices remains consistently high.
