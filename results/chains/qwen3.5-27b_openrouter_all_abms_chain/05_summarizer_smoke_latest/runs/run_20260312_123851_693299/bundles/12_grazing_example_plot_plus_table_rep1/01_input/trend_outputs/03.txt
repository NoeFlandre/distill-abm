The average reserve biomass in the simulation begins at approximately 90,000 units. Over the initial phase, spanning the first 26 time steps, the reserve biomass declines, reaching a minimum value of roughly 84,114 units at time step 26. This early reduction suggests that the initial stocking decisions and resource utilization strategies of the E-RO (baseline behavior), E-LBD (learning-by-doing), and E-RO-SL1 (social learning) agents result in a temporary drawdown of available forage reserves. Following this decline, the system enters a transition phase around time step 48. During this period, the downward pressure on the biomass stabilizes, and the trajectory begins to reverse. From this inflection point onwards, the reserve biomass exhibits a robust and sustained recovery. This trend is characterized by a rapid increase, where the biomass levels rise sharply through time steps 50 to 80. This recovery phase likely reflects the agents adjusting their livestock placement decisions, potentially due to the learning mechanisms inherent in the model, which lead to more sustainable grazing practices over time. By time step 100, the average reserve biomass stabilizes at its maximum observed value of approximately 121,073 units, indicating that the grazing system has reached a new equilibrium with higher carrying capacity and improved resource availability compared to the initial state.
