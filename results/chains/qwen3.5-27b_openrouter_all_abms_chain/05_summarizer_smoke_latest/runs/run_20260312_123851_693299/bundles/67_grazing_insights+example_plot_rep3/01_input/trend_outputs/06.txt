The average number of livestock destocked by households begins near zero and rises sharply in the first 5 time steps, peaking at approximately 7.5. This rapid increase is likely due to initial resource scarcity, as households with high livestock holdings face immediate pressure to reduce their herds in response to insufficient pasture biomass. From time step 5 to around 20, the mean destocking declines steadily, falling below 2 by time step 15. This trend suggests that households adjust their herd sizes more effectively after the initial shock, possibly through behavioral adaptations or the natural reduction of livestock populations. After time step 20, the mean destocking stabilizes at low levels, fluctuating between 0 and 1 for the remainder of the simulation. Some individual simulations show minor spikes around time steps 40 and 60, but these deviations are small and transient. These later fluctuations may reflect stochastic environmental variability, such as rainfall fluctuations, which temporarily strain resources and necessitate minor destocking actions. By the end of the simulation, at time step 100, the mean destocking remains consistently low, near 0.5 or less, indicating that the system has reached a new equilibrium where livestock numbers are better aligned with available resources. For decision-makers, the sharp initial destocking highlights the vulnerability of households during periods of resource scarcity, emphasizing the need for early intervention strategies. The subsequent stabilization suggests that long-term sustainability can be achieved if households have the capacity to adapt their practices effectively.
