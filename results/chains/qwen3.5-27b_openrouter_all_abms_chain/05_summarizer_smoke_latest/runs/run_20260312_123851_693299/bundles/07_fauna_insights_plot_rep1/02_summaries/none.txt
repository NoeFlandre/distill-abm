The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The landscape consists of an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges on a 211x211 patch matrix. The goals are to manipulate socio-natural dynamics to observe how environmental conditions, human hunting, and animal life history interact to either accelerate or buffer extinction or create equilibrium conditions. Hunters follow dynamic optimal foraging theory, balancing energy status, prey value, density, and costs. Ungulates are sexually dimorphic, reproduce, age, and move as herds. The environment controls fodder density, regrowth, and seasonality.

The number of human hunter-gatherer bands in the simulation remains constant at 20 throughout the entire duration of the simulation, spanning from step 0 to 2250. This stability is consistent with the parameter 'imortal-foragers' being set to true, indicating that foragers do not die out over time. The absence of fluctuations in the forager population suggests that socio-natural dynamics, such as resource availability or predation pressure, do not impact the hunter population in this specific simulation scenario. For decision-makers, this implies that under the given conditions, human populations can maintain a steady presence without experiencing growth or decline, potentially allowing for long-term environmental monitoring or intervention strategies.

The total energy of forager bands exhibits a consistent and significant decline over time. Starting from an initial energy level near 0 at step 0, the energy status decreases progressively. By step 500, the energy has dropped to approximately -5000. This downward trend continues, reaching around -15000 by step 1000. The decline accelerates, and by step 2000, the energy status is near -30000. This persistent negative trend suggests that the costs associated with foraging, including movement and potentially unsuccessful hunts, are outweighing the energy gains from successful hunting. For decision-makers, this indicates a critical imbalance in the foraging efficiency and resource availability, which could lead to unsustainable conditions for the forager bands if not addressed.

The cumulative number of male prey animals killed exhibits a rapid, steep increase from the initial step, reaching approximately 120 kills by step 500. Beyond step 500, the rate of increase decelerates significantly, causing the curves to plateau between 110 and 125 kills by step 2000. This pattern indicates that the forager bands achieve their maximum harvesting potential for male prey early in the simulation timeline, suggesting that the initial prey population density and accessibility were sufficient to saturate the hunting capacity of the 20 foragers quickly. Decision makers can infer that under these specific parameter settings, the pressure on the male segment of the prey population stabilizes rapidly, implying that long-term population decline due to hunting males is driven by the initial burst of activity rather than sustained extraction over time.

The cumulative number of female prey animals killed by forager bands increases rapidly from the start, reaching approximately 100 individuals by step 500 across all simulation runs. By step 1000, the majority of simulations stabilize, with the cumulative number of female kills plateauing between 120 and 125. A few simulations show a slower initial rise, reaching this plateau closer to step 1500. After step 1500, the number of cumulative kills remains constant in all runs, indicating that hunting of female prey animals ceases entirely. This trend suggests that early hunting pressure quickly depletes the available population of female prey or leads to a shift in hunting strategies, potentially impacting long-term herd demography and stability. Decision makers should consider the implications of high initial hunting pressure on herd sustainability and equilibrium conditions within the model.
