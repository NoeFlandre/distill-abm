The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches begins near 45,000 units at the start of the simulation. Over the first 30 time steps, there is a slight decline, with values reaching a low point of approximately 40,000 units around step 30. This initial reduction reflects the impact of grazing by livestock on the initial grass cover. Following this dip, the biomass shows a significant recovery trend. From step 30 to step 60, the green biomass increases rapidly, reaching around 55,000 units. This growth likely indicates that the rate of grass regrowth has outpaced the grazing pressure as farmers adjust their livestock numbers based on learning strategies. Beyond step 60, the increase in biomass continues but at a slower rate, plateauing around 60,000 to 63,000 units by step 100. This stabilization suggests that a balance has been achieved between grazing intensity and grass regeneration. Variations in the trajectories of the repeated simulations become more pronounced after step 40, indicating differences in how learning strategies among farmers affect biomass dynamics over time. By the end of the simulation, the biomass levels across runs remain relatively high, reflecting the overall health and sustainability of the grazing system under the applied learning behaviors.
