 This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors . The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time . The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock .  The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches begins near 45,000 units at the start of the simulation . The average total number of livestock, including healthy and destocked animals, begins at 90 across all households . The biomass levels across runs remain relatively high, reflecting the overall health and sustainability of the grazing system under the applied learning behaviors .  The number of households engaging in social learning begins at zero for all simulation runs at the initial time step . By step 100, the Gini coefficient spans a range from approximately 0.03 to 0.095, demonstrating substantial inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock by the end of the simulation .
