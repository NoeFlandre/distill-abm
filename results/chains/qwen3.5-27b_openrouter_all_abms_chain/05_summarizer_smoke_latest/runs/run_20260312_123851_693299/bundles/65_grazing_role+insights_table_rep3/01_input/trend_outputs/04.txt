The simulation output tracks the average available green biomass across all patches over a 100-timestep duration. Initially, the biomass stood at 45,000 units. A slight increase was observed at timestep 4, reaching 45,261 units, before the metric began to decline. Statistical analysis confirms a significant decreasing trend between timesteps 11 and 21, with a slope of -120.458. This decline continued until the system reached its lowest point of 42,057 units at timestep 27.

Following the minimum, the trend reversed significantly. A robust increasing trend emerged starting around timestep 33, maintaining statistical significance through the end of the simulation. The recovery was most pronounced between timesteps 44 and 54, where the slope reached 954.496. While the growth rate slowed in the later stages, with slopes ranging from 3.067 to 54.663 between timesteps 66 and 98, the upward trajectory remained consistent. By timestep 100, the biomass attained a maximum value of 60,535 units, significantly exceeding the initial level.

This U-shaped dynamic implies that initial management adjustments or grazing pressures reduced pasture cover, but subsequent adaptive learning behaviors among the households facilitated effective regeneration. For decision makers, these results highlight that supporting mixed learning strategies can yield long-term ecological benefits, ultimately restoring and enhancing biomass availability despite short-term reductions.
