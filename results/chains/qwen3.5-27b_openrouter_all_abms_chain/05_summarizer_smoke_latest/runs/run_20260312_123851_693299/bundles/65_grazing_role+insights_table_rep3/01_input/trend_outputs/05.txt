The average total livestock population across all households follows a pronounced U-shaped trajectory over the 100-year simulation period. A steep, statistically significant decline occurs immediately, characterized by a slope of -3.470 in the first decade, indicating rapid destocking. This negative trend continues with reduced intensity through timestep 37, where the metric reaches its global minimum of 41.01.

Between timesteps 33 and 43, the system stabilizes with no significant trend detected. Following an inflection point at timestep 46, the trajectory reverses into a sustained recovery phase. From timestep 44 to 100, the trend is statistically positive, with slopes ranging from 0.313 to 0.005, culminating in an end value of 47.55. Although the final population is nearly half the initial count, the consistent upward movement in the latter half suggests the system is converging toward a stable equilibrium rather than continuous degradation.

For decision makers, the initial collapse suggests the starting herd size was unsustainable given the rainfall variability and grass growth parameters, necessitating a correction in stocking rates. The eventual recovery indicates that the combination of farmer learning strategies allows for herd rebuilding once the land is relieved of pressure. This implies that while initial transitions may result in significant asset loss, the grazing system retains resilience and the capacity to support a reduced but stable livestock population over the long term.
