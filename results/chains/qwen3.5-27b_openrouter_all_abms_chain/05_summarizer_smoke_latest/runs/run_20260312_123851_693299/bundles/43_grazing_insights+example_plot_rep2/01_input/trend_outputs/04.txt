The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches begins at approximately 45,000 units. In the initial phase, from time step 0 to about time step 20, the biomass levels generally decline, with most simulation runs converging towards a low of roughly 41,000 units around time step 30. This initial reduction suggests a period of intensified resource extraction or slower regeneration rates relative to consumption, leading to a depletion of standing biomass. Following this minimum, a robust and rapid increase in biomass is observed starting around time step 40. By time step 60, the average biomass surges to approximately 60,000 units. The rate of growth decelerates between time steps 80 and 100, where the trajectories stabilize in a high range between roughly 55,000 and 63,000 units. While the general trend is consistent, there is notable divergence in the final state, with variance increasing over time. This variability implies that specific agent behaviors and local feedback loops create different equilibrium levels of pasture condition across simulations. For decision-makers, the sharp recovery indicates the system's high potential for regeneration once pressure is relieved or management practices improve, but the variance at the end suggests that achieving a uniform, optimal biomass level requires addressing the heterogeneity in individual agent responses to the environment.
