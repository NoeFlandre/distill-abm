 The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards . The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person . The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against macro level data .  The average weekly consumption of alternative milk varieties exhibits a pronounced upward trajectory over the simulation period, initiating at 5.29 units and concluding at 1456.59 units . The simulation output indicates a consistent upward trajectory in the total number of times agents selected whole milk throughout the observation period . For decision makers, the persistent downward trend underscores the strength of the transition away from whole milk .  The average weekly consumption of whole milk per person initiates at 2654.81 units at time step 0 . Throughout the 33 time steps of the simulation, corresponding to the period from 1974 to 2005, the metric exhibits a pronounced decline . For decision makers, the stabilization of the growth rate suggests that policies encouraging alternative milk adoption can expect a predictable, long-term cumulative increase in consumption .  The mean habit factor applied to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choice function begins at 1.0000 at time step 0 . At the conclusion of the period, the factor reaches its peak value of 1.5879 . This progression indicates that the influence of habit on consumer preference for alternative milk types strengthens over the simulated timeframe .  A persistent gap between consumer values and market behavior is the expected equilibrium . This implies that without active intervention to realign the choice environment, a persistent gap is expected to persist . Market forces or individual will alone may be insufficient to close the values-action gap .
