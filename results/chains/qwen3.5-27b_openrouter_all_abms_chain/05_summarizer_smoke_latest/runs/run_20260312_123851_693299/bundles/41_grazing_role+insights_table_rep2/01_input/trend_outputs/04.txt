The simulation tracks the average available green biomass over 100 timesteps. At the onset, biomass stands at 45,000.0. Over the initial period, the metric exhibits a decline, reaching a minimum value of 42,057.04 at timestep 27. This early reduction suggests an initial phase of grazing pressure exceeding regeneration rates as households establish their livestock strategies. Following this trough, the system stabilizes before entering a sustained recovery phase. Statistical analysis indicates no significant trend in the first decade, followed by a significant decreasing trend between timesteps 11 and 21. From timestep 33 onwards, a statistically significant increasing trend emerges and persists through the end of the simulation. This recovery is marked by an inflection point around timestep 49 and a change point at timestep 50. By the final timestep, the green biomass reaches its maximum recorded value of 60,535.28. The mean biomass across the entire period is 51,529.31. The trajectory implies that the mixed population of farmers, utilizing baseline, experience-based learning, and social learning strategies, initially degrades the pasture but eventually adapts their livestock holdings to allow for regrowth. For decision makers, this indicates that pasture recovery is possible through adaptive management, but early intervention might be necessary to prevent the initial dip below baseline levels. The convergence towards higher biomass suggests that the inclusion of learning mechanisms alongside baseline behavior facilitates long-term pasture sustainability.
