The agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The simulation consists of 1,000 agents representing consumers situated within a social network. Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms. The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent initiates at 2654.81 units at time step zero, reflecting strong incumbent habit and initial market dominance. The average number of milk purchasing decisions demonstrates a consistent linear accumulation across the simulation timeline. The aggregate count of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk alternatives begins at zero and shows a consistent upward trajectory, reaching a peak of 25643.83 by the final step. The mean factor applied to the alternative milk choice function due to habit begins at 1.0000 at the beginning of the simulation period. Over the course of the 33 time steps representing the historical timeline, this value exhibits a consistent upward trajectory, culminating in a maximum value of 1456.59 at the final time step. As agents interact within the Watts-Strogatz network, their perceptions of health and environmental benefits diverge based on unique exposure to social norms and personal history. The trend implies that awareness of healthier options, such as skimmed or semi-skimmed milk, does not instantly translate into market share changes.
