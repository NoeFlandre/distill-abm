By step 400, the total energy has dropped significantly to below 4,000 units. There is some variability among simulation runs, with some runs showing slightly higher energy retention, possibly due to stochastic differences in forager movements and prey encounters. Between steps 400 and 1000, the rate of decline slows, and the energy levels stabilize at lower values, generally between 0 and 2,000 units. This stabilization suggests that the population has reached a precarious equilibrium where the remaining females are either highly elusive or in areas with lower forager density. After step 1000, the total energy continues to decline gradually, eventually reaching near-zero levels by step 2000. This final decline indicates the impending extinction of the female prey population, as the remaining individuals are unable to sustain their energy levels against the combined pressures of predation and environmental constraints. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the critical importance of managing human-forager interactions and environmental factors to prevent the extinction of megaherbivore populations. Early intervention strategies that reduce hunting pressure or improve resource availability could help maintain higher energy levels and support population survival.
