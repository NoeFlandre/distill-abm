The average weekly consumption of whole milk per person begins at approximately 2,655 units and exhibits a sharp decline through the early stages of the simulation, reaching roughly 1,000 units by step 12. This precipitous drop mirrors the rapid behavioral shift expected as agents increasingly perceive health and environmental benefits in alternative milk options, overcoming initial habit inertia. The Mann-Kendall analysis confirms a statistically significant downward trend during the first 19 steps, with the steepest decline occurring between steps 5 and 9, where the slope reaches -221.1 units per step. This reflects the combined influence of social interaction and health perception triggering widespread adoption of substitutes. From step 15 to 19, the decline moderates significantly, indicating a slowdown in the rate of consumer transition as the most susceptible agents have already switched. Consumption continues to decrease gradually until step 27, where it stabilizes briefly around 620 units before a more pronounced drop occurs between steps 27 and 32, bringing consumption down to 253 units by the final recorded time step. This late-stage acceleration likely corresponds to the compounding effect of social norms and reduced cognitive dissonance, as the majority of the population has already adopted alternatives, reinforcing the new standard. The minimal oscillation and consistent downward trajectory across all simulation runs suggest that the model’s parameters—particularly high initial habit strength for whole milk and moderate social susceptibility—effectively capture the historical dynamics of consumer transition. For decision makers, this underscores the critical role of early adoption thresholds and social reinforcement in accelerating market shifts. Policies or marketing strategies targeting the first 20% of consumers may yield disproportionate long-term impact by triggering network effects that drive broader adoption.
