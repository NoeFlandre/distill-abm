 Simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors . The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time . The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock .  The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches initiates at 45,000 units . By time step 100, the reserve biomass peaks at 121,073, exceeding the starting value . This recovery suggests that farmer adaptation strategies, likely driven by learning from own results or neighbor interactions, allowed the pasture to regenerate .  The number of households engaging in social learning begins at zero and demonstrates a sustained increasing trend throughout the one hundred time steps of the simulation . The rate of destocking is not uniform; initially, there is a sharp increase in the first decade, with an average slope of 4.96 per time step . By step fifty-five, the growth rate approaches zero, and the count approaches zero .
