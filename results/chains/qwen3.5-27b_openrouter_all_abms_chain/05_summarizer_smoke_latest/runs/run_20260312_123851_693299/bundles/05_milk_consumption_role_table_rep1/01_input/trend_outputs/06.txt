The average weekly consumption of whole milk demonstrates a substantial and consistent decline over the 33 simulated time steps. At the beginning of the simulation, the mean quantity recorded was 2654.81 units. This initial value represents the baseline dominance of whole milk consumption before significant shifts in consumer behavior occurred. By the final time step, the metric decreased to 253.10, marking the lowest value observed in the series and indicating a near-total displacement of whole milk from the average consumption basket.

Statistical analysis of the trend reveals a predominantly negative slope throughout the duration. Rolling Mann-Kendall tests identified significant decreasing trends in the early phases, specifically within the windows covering time steps 0 to 4 and 5 to 9, with slope values reaching -115.85 and -221.11 respectively. A continued significant decrease was observed between time steps 10 and 14 and again from 20 to 24. However, periods of statistical stabilization occurred between time steps 15 and 19 and 25 and 29, where the trend was not statistically significant, suggesting temporary plateaus in the rate of consumption decline despite the overall downward movement.

The trajectory is punctuated by multiple inflection points at indices 5, 13, 16, 19, 24, 25, 27, and 30, indicating changes in the momentum of the decline. In the latter portion of the simulation, specifically after index 18, the observed values exhibit minor local oscillations. Peaks were recorded at indices 19, 27, and 30, while valleys appeared at indices 18, 26, and 29. These fluctuations occur around much lower consumption levels compared to the initial period. A dominant oscillation period of 15.00 was detected, suggesting cyclical adjustments in consumer choices potentially linked to social network dynamics or habit reinforcement cycles. The final change point at index 32 aligns with the minimum value, signifying the culmination of the transition. This pattern reflects the model parameters where high initial habit strength for the incumbent product is gradually overcome by social susceptibility and health perceptions, driving the agents toward alternative milk varieties.
