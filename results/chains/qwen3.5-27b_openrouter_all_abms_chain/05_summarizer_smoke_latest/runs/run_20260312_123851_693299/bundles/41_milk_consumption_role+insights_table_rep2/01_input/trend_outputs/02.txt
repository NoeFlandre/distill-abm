The simulated average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits a profound upward trajectory over the 33 recorded time steps. At the initial time step, consumption is negligible, recorded at 5.29 units per agent. This aligns with the historical baseline where whole milk was the dominant choice. Between time steps 0 and 9, the metric undergoes rapid expansion, with statistically significant increasing trends observed in both the first and second intervals, indicating a strong initial adoption phase driven by perceived health benefits and social interaction.

From time step 10 through step 24, the growth stabilizes. During this period, values oscillate between approximately 1183 and 1265, with no statistically significant trend detected. Notable fluctuations include peaks at indices 11 and 13, followed by minor valleys at 12 and 18. This plateau suggests a consolidation phase where new consumption habits solidify within the social network, and social norms reach a temporary equilibrium.

Following this stabilization, a renewed growth phase emerges from time step 25 to 29, characterized by a significant upward slope. The simulation concludes at time step 32 with a peak value of 1456.5991, representing the maximum consumption level across the series. This final surge indicates a late-stage reinforcement of the shift towards lower-fat milk varieties.

For decision makers, these dynamics highlight that behavioral shifts in consumption are not always linear. The early rapid uptake suggests high sensitivity to initial health messaging, while the middle plateau indicates the importance of sustained social reinforcement to maintain momentum. The late-stage increase implies that continued engagement with social networks is necessary to achieve market saturation. Strategies should account for periods of stability where habit formation occurs, requiring different interventions compared to the initial adoption phase.
