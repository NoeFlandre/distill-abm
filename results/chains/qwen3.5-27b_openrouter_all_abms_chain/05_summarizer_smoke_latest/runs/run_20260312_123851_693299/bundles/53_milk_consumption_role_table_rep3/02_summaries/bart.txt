The agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms. The simulation tracks the cumulative frequency of agents choosing skimmed or semi-skimmed milk over 33 time steps. At the inception of the simulation, the aggregate count is zero, indicating no initial preference for alternative milk types due to high incumbent habit strength. As the process unfolds, the total count exhibits a continuous and statistically significant upward trajectory, concluding at a value of 25,64383. The simulation demonstrates a marked increase in the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk throughout the timeline. At the initial time step, consumption was minimal at 5.29 units, indicating strong adherence to incumbent habits at the start of the period. The simulation output demonstrates a consistent increasing trend in the mean habit factor for the incumbent whole milk choice. The simulation tracks the mean percentage difference between maximum and minimum choice function scores across the agent population, serving as a proxy for heterogeneity in consumer preference evaluation. The metric exhibits a robust and consistent upward trajectory throughout the entire observation period. At the start of the simulation, the deviation is 0.0000, indicating initial alignment between health perceptions and consumption.
