This early reduction suggests that the initial stocking decisions and grazing pressure deplete the biomass reserves, as agents adjust to the system or face initial foraging constraints. Following this trough, the trajectory shifts significantly. Starting around time step 27, the reserve biomass begins to recover. This increase becomes particularly steep between time steps 40 and 60, where the mean biomass rises rapidly from approximately 85,000 to over 110,000 units. This sharp ascent indicates a substantial reduction in grazing pressure or a shift in agent behavior towards conservation, allowing the pasture to regenerate. After time step 60, the rate of growth slows, and the biomass levels off, gradually stabilizing at values above 120,000 units by the end of the simulation at time step 100. The final mean value stands at approximately 121,072 units. While the overall trend is a recovery to higher biomass levels, the trajectories show variance among the individual simulation runs during the recovery phase, likely reflecting differences in how households with different risk attitudes managed their pastures.
