The agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms. Understanding these dynamics can inform strategies to promote healthier consumption patterns. At step 0, the total number of choices is approximately 0.0 by step 10, indicating an average increase of about 75 choices per step during this initial phase. At the final recorded step, which is around 32, the number of whole milk choices spans a range between 2,000 and 2,300. This indicates a robust shift in consumer preference away from whole milk towards lower-fat alternatives over the simulated period. The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 1.0 at the initial time step. Trend suggests that once consumers begin adopting these alternatives, habitual reinforcement plays a significant role in sustaining and increasing their preference. For decision makers, this suggests that strategies to alter milk consumption habits would need to address entrenched preferences early in the process.
