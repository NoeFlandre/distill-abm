Analysis of the 80 matched simulation series reveals distinct phases in the predation pressure on male megafauna.

Phase 1: Initial Exploitation (Ticks 0–927). The simulation initiates at zero cumulative kills and enters a phase of sustained growth. Rolling Mann-Kendall tests confirm a statistically significant increasing trend across the first four windows (Ticks 0–927). The trajectory is steepest initially, indicating high encounter rates when the prey base is abundant. Specific change points identified at indices 125, 250, and 375 mark shifts in foraging efficiency.

Phase 2: Transition and Volatility (Ticks 928–1623). Growth continues but with varying intensity. Between ticks 1160 and 1391, the trend test indicates a period of stagnation, suggesting a temporary decoupling of forager effort and success. This is briefly interrupted by a renewed positive trend between ticks 1392 and 1623.

Phase 3: Saturation and Collapse Signals (Ticks 1624–2312). A critical shift occurs in the latter stage. The ensemble mean of cumulative kills reaches a peak value of approximately 120.1667 at tick 1876. Subsequent observations show the mean value declining to 117.5 by the final step at tick 2312. The Mann-Kendall test for the window 1856–2087 identifies a statistically significant decreasing trend. For a cumulative metric, a decrease in the aggregate mean across runs strongly implies that a significant proportion of simulation worlds have reached a state where no further kills occur, indicating local extinctions. The dominant oscillation period of 63 ticks persists, reflecting the underlying environmental seasonality.

Implications for Decision Makers: 1. Tipping Points: The transition from rapid accumulation to a plateau indicates a system approaching carrying capacity limits. 2. Extinction Risk: The statistical reversal in the average cumulative count (post-tick 1876) validates the hypothesis that sustained hunting pressure can deplete the resource to the point of local extinction. 3. Management: Mitigation strategies should target the early exploitation phase, as the system appears self-correcting only after the resource is critically depleted.
