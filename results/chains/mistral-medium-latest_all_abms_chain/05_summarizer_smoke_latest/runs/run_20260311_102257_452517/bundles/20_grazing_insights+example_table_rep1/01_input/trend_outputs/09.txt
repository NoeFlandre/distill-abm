The cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households starts at zero and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period. This rapid increase could be attributed to households adjusting to initial ecological and climatic conditions, leading to a higher rate of destocking as they adapt their herds to the available resources. Following this initial phase, the rate of increase slows down but remains steady, indicating a more stabilized decision-making process among households. Notable inflection points occur around time steps 45, 47, 53, 56, 57, 62, 67, 72, 74, and 75, suggesting periods where the rate of destocking experiences slight variations, potentially due to changes in environmental conditions or household strategies. The simulation ends with a cumulative number of approximately 96.78 destocked livestock, indicating that nearly all households have engaged in destocking by the end of the simulation period. The consistent upward trend, as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall test, underscores a continuous and significant increase in destocking activities throughout the simulation. For decision-makers, this trend highlights the importance of understanding the factors driving destocking and the need for strategies that can mitigate its impacts on household livelihoods and ecological sustainability.
