The total number of animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at an initial value of 250, which is the set parameter for init-prey. In the early stages of the simulation, the number of animals remains relatively stable, indicating a period where births and deaths are roughly balanced. However, as the simulation progresses, there is a noticeable decline in the total number of animals. This decline begins gradually but accelerates as time progresses, suggesting an increasing imbalance where deaths outpace births. By around 500 steps, the number of animals has significantly decreased, often falling below 100. This trend continues, and by approximately 1000 steps, the number of animals drops to near zero, indicating a potential extinction event. The decline in animal numbers can be attributed to various factors modeled in the MHPM, such as hunting pressure, internal and external mortality rates, and environmental conditions like grass availability and regrowth rates. For decision makers, this trend highlights the potential vulnerability of megaherbivore populations under sustained hunting pressure and varying environmental conditions. It underscores the importance of considering both direct and indirect factors influencing animal populations when developing conservation strategies or understanding historical extinction events.
