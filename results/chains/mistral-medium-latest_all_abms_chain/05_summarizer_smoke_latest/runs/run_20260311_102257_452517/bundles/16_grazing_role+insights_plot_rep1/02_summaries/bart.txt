 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households .  The agent-based model shows the average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, exhibits a notable trend over the 100 steps of the simulation . For decision makers, this trend indicates that while there may be an initial decline in green biomass due to grazing, the system has the potential to recover and even exceed initial levels .  The number of households engaging in social learning varies significantly across simulations . This variation indicates different rates of adoption and diffusion of social learning behaviors among households . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions to facilitate the adoption of beneficial behaviors, such as social learning, in grazing systems .
