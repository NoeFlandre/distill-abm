 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . It allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households .  The overall trend indicates a resilient system capable of recovery after initial stress, which is crucial for decision-makers focusing on long-term sustainability and adaptive management in grazing systems . For decision makers, this trend indicates the importance of initial conservative stocking rates followed by adaptive increases as conditions and management practices improve .  The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households starts at 0 and increases rapidly in the early stages, reaching approximately 60 by step 25 . The rate of destocking then slows down, indicating a possible stabilization as households adjust to new conditions or constraints . The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent increasing trend across all windows, reinforcing the steady rise in destocking .
