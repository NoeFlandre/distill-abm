The simulation of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decline in the number of female prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at 125 and exhibits a sharp decrease in the early stages of the simulation. This downward trend continues throughout the simulation, with the population eventually reaching zero by the end. The statistical analysis indicates several inflection points and change points, suggesting periods where the rate of decline varies. Notably, there are brief periods of slight increases or slower rates of decline, as indicated by some of the rolling Mann-Kendall windows showing increasing trends. However, the overall trajectory remains a significant decrease in the female prey population. The presence of multiple peaks and valleys suggests some oscillations in the population dynamics, but these are minor compared to the overall declining trend. For decision makers, this trend implies that under the given parameters and conditions, the female prey population is not sustainable and heads towards extinction. This insight could be crucial for understanding the impact of hunting pressure and environmental conditions on megaherbivore populations and for designing conservation strategies or further research into the factors influencing these dynamics.
