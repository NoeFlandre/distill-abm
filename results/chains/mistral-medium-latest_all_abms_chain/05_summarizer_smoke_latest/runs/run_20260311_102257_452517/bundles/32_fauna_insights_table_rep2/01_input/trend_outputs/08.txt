The simulation of male prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the population starts at 125 male prey animals. This number steadily declines throughout the simulation, eventually reaching zero by the end. The decline is marked by several inflection points and change points, indicating periods where the rate of decrease shifts. Early in the simulation, the decline is steep, but it gradually becomes more gradual, with minor fluctuations and small peaks and valleys. These minor oscillations suggest brief periods of stabilization or slight recovery, but the overall trend remains a steady decrease. The dominant period of these oscillations is approximately 63 ticks, indicating a cyclical pattern within the broader decline. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms significant decreasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with no significant trend detected in the later phases, suggesting the population stabilizes at very low numbers before ultimately reaching zero. For decision-makers, this trend indicates that under the given parameters, the male prey population is not sustainable and heads towards extinction. This insight could inform strategies related to conservation efforts, hunting regulations, or environmental management to prevent such outcomes in real-world scenarios.
