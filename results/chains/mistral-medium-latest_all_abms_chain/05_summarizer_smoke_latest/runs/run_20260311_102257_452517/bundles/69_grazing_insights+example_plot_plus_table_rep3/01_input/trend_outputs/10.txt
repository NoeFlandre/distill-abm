The number of non-learning households starts at 20 and exhibits a slight increase in the early stages, reaching a peak of approximately 21.1 at around the 8th time step. This initial rise could be attributed to the initial conditions of the simulation where households are still adapting to the environment and have not yet started to adopt learning strategies. Following this peak, there is a gradual decline in the number of non-learning households, dropping to around 14.7 by the end of the simulation at the 100th time step. This overall decreasing trend suggests that over time, households are increasingly adopting learning strategies, likely due to the benefits of adaptive management in response to ecological and climatic conditions. The decline is not uniform but shows several minor peaks and valleys, indicating periods where the rate of adoption of learning strategies fluctuates. Notable inflection points occur around the 5th, 12th, and 25th time steps, suggesting significant changes in the rate of decline during these periods. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant decreasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with the trend becoming less significant and stabilizing towards the end. This stabilization implies that the system may be reaching a new equilibrium where the number of non-learning households plateaus. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial conditions and the gradual adoption of learning strategies over time, suggesting that policies encouraging adaptive management could be beneficial in the long run.
