 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbivores . The model simulates a simplified grassland environment with human hunters and male and female ungulates . The landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, consisting of a 211x211 patch matrix .  The model allows users to manipulate these parameters to test various hypotheses related to extinction events . The temporal units used in the model are 'ticks,' which can be scaled to any temporal granularity . The number of human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant throughout the simulation .  The low variance among simulation runs suggests consistent hunting patterns and prey availability across different simulations . For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressure is high but stabilizes over time, which could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts to protect vulnerable prey populations .  The total number of animals in the simulation begins at 250, reflecting the initial population set by the parameters . The simulation ultimately trends towards zero animals, indicating a potential extinction event under the given parameters . For decision makers, these trends highlight the vulnerability of the female animal population under the current model parameters and the potential for rapid extinction under sustained pressure .  At around time step 800, there is a sudden and significant drop in the percentage of green patches, decreasing to approximately 24% . This decline could be due to increased grazing pressure, a seasonal die-off of vegetation, or other environmental factors affecting grass regrowth . Following this peak, the percentage  stabilizes again, maintaining a consistent level . This period of stability indicates a balance between grass consumption and regrowth rates .
