An agent-based model (ABM) is a computational framework used to simulate the actions and interactions of autonomous agents, with the goal of assessing their effects on the system as a whole. In this case, the ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005, focusing on the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk consumption at the beginning of the simulation. Over the first 10 time steps, there is a rapid decline in consumption, dropping to around 1,000 units. This sharp decrease suggests a significant shift in consumer behavior, potentially due to increasing awareness of health or environmental benefits of alternative milk types, or social influences encouraging a switch. Between time steps 10 and 20, the decline continues but at a slower rate, stabilizing around 800 units. This period indicates a phase where the initial rapid change slows down, and consumers are settling into new consumption patterns. From time step 20 to 30, the consumption rate remains relatively stable with a slight downward trend, hovering around 700 units. This stability suggests that the agents have largely adjusted to their new preferences and habits. The number of choices made by all agents combined starts at 0 and increases steadily over the 30 steps. Initially, the number of choices made is low, reflecting the initial stages of the simulation where agents are beginning to make decisions regarding their milk consumption. By step 10, approximately 10,000 choices have been made, indicating a gradual increase in agent activity and interaction. This trend continues linearly, with the total number of choices reaching around 20,000 by step 20. By the end of the simulation at step 30, the total number of choices made by all agents combined reaches approximately 30,000. This steady linear increase suggests a consistent rate of decision-making and interaction among the agents throughout the simulation period. This uniformity in the rate of choices made implies that the agents are continuously evaluating and possibly reevaluating their milk consumption choices, influenced by their perceptions, habits, and social interactions.
