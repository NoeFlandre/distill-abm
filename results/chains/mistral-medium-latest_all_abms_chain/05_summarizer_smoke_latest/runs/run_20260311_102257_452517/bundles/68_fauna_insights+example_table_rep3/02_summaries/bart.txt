 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbivores . The model simulates a simplified grassland environment with human hunters and male and female ungulates . The landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, consisting of a 211x211 patch matrix .  The model allows users to manipulate these parameters to test various hypotheses related to extinction events . The temporal units used in the model are 'ticks,' which can be scaled to any temporal granularity . The model includes summary statistics that are shown as continuous line graphs, reflecting data from each tick . The number of human hunter-gatherer bands in the simulation remains constant throughout the entire duration .  The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands begins at zero and exhibits a rapid increase during the initial phase of the simulation . Following this peak, the trend shows minor fluctuations but generally stabilizes, indicating a potential equilibrium between hunting pressure and prey availability . Understanding these dynamics can inform conservation strategies and hunting regulations to ensure sustainable prey populations .  The number of female prey animals in the simulation begins at 125 and exhibits a consistent decreasing trend throughout the entire duration of the simulation . For decision makers, these trends highlight the vulnerability of the male prey population under the current model parameters, suggesting that without intervention, the population is likely to face extinction .  The energy levels exhibit a rapid decline in the early time steps, dropping significantly within the first 500 ticks . This sharp decrease suggests a high initial consumption or depletion rate of energy resources among the female prey . For decision makers, these trends highlight the critical periods where interventions could potentially alter the trajectory of the male prey population's energy levels .
