The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population starts at zero and remains at this level until around 700 time steps. This initial period of no births could be attributed to the initial conditions of the simulation, where the population may be stabilizing or adjusting to the environmental parameters. Following this period, there is a gradual increase in the number of births, reaching a peak of approximately 5.6 births at around 1700 time steps. This rise in births suggests that the population has adapted to the environment and is experiencing a period of growth. The significant increasing trends identified by the rolling Mann-Kendall test during these time windows support this observation of population growth.

After reaching the peak, the number of births begins to decline, dropping to around 1 birth by the end of the simulation at 2312 time steps. This decline could indicate that the population is reaching a carrying capacity or that environmental factors are limiting further growth. The significant decreasing trends identified by the rolling Mann-Kendall test during the later time windows align with this decline in births. The oscillation indices suggest periodic fluctuations in the number of births, which could be due to seasonal effects or other cyclic environmental factors.

For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of considering both the initial conditions and the long-term dynamics of the population. The initial period of no births followed by a gradual increase suggests that interventions or changes in environmental parameters could have significant impacts on population growth. The subsequent decline in births indicates that carrying capacity and environmental limitations are crucial factors to consider in managing and conserving the population.
