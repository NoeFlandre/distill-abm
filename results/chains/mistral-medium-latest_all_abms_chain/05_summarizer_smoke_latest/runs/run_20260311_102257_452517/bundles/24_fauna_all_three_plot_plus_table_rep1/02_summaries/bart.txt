 The Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbiivores . Model considers various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, sexual dimorphism, and life history of animals . The model allows users to manipulate various parameters to establish the socio-natural dynamics of the experiment matrix .  The model's landscape consists of an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, existing on a 211x211 patch matrix with a relative spatial scale . Hunters are embedded with logic approximating dynamic optimal foraging theory, making foraging decisions based on environmental feedback . Animals can reproduce, and users can control sex ratios and reproduction probability .  The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 250 time steps, reaching approximately 100 . The rate of increase slows down significantly after this initial period, indicating a potential reduction in the number of available postpartum females or a stabilization in hunting success rates . The trends suggest that initial hunting pressure is high due to the abundance of prey, but as the prey population adjusts, the rate of hunting success stabilizes .  The number of male animals in the simulation starts at 125 and exhibits a rapid decline within the first 500 time steps . This sharp decrease suggests a high initial predation or mortality rate, potentially due to the combined effects of human hunting pressure and environmental factors . Understanding these dynamics can inform conservation strategies and hunting regulations to ensure the survival of vulnerable species .  The total energy of male animals in the simulation starts at 12,500 units and exhibits a rapid decline within the first 125 time steps, dropping to approximately 11,608 units . After 350 time steps the decline resumes but at a slower rate, reaching near zero around 2,310 time steps . This gradual decline suggests a prolonged period of attrition where the remaining male animals are either hunted down or succumb to other pressures such as starvation or environmental factors .  The rapid initial decline in energy levels highlights the vulnerability of prey populations to intense predation and environmental stress . The eventual stabilization and subsequent decline to zero energy reserves suggest that without intervention, such populations may face extinction . Understanding these dynamics can inform management practices aimed at maintaining sustainable levels of vegetation for prey animals .
