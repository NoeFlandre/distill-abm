 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices . The model includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where influence is a function of interaction probability and agent susceptibility .  Simulation starts at zero and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the time steps . Means value of whole milk choices across all steps is around 1130.88, with a median of 1158.97, indicating a relatively symmetric distribution around the mean . For decision makers, this trend implies a growing preference for whole milk among the agents .  The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents in the simulation shows distinct trends over the time steps . Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of approximately 5.29 units . This is followed by a rapid increase in consumption, reaching around 1265.47 units by the 11th time step . This period of rapid growth indicates a significant shift in milk consumption preferences .  The trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows a consistent increasing trend across all windows, with significant positive slopes ranging from 0.003 to 0.063 . This implies that interventions or policies aimed at influencing milk consumption behaviors may need to account for diversity in consumer preferences and the factors influencing their choices over time .
