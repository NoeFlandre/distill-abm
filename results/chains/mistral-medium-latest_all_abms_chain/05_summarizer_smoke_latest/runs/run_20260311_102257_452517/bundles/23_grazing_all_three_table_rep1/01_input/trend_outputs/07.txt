The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all households, as measured by the Gini coefficient, starts at a value of 0, indicating perfect equality. Over the course of the simulation, the Gini coefficient exhibits a consistent increasing trend, reaching a value of approximately 0.0726 by the end of the 100 time steps. This trend suggests a gradual increase in inequality among households in terms of healthy livestock distribution. The mean value of the Gini coefficient across the simulation period is 0.0302, with a median value of 0.0229, indicating that the central tendency of inequality remains relatively low but positive throughout the simulation. The standard deviation of 0.0239 and variance of 0.0006 suggest some variability in the inequality levels over time, but the overall trend remains upward. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this increasing trend across all windows, with significant positive trends in each window, indicating a steady and consistent rise in inequality. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points suggests that while the overall trend is upward, there are periods where the rate of increase in inequality changes. For decision-makers, this trend implies that over time, disparities in the distribution of healthy livestock among households are likely to grow. This could necessitate interventions aimed at promoting more equitable distribution of resources or supporting households that may be at a disadvantage in maintaining healthy livestock.
