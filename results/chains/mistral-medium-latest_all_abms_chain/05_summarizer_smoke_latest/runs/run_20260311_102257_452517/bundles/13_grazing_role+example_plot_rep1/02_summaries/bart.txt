 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . It allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households .  There is low variance across simulation runs in the first 40 steps, indicating consistent initial conditions and grazing patterns . The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, starts at approximately 90 . The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 20 time steps . This rapid increase indicates that households quickly adopt social learning behaviors early in the simulation .  The count of non-learning households starts with a wide range of values, with some simulations beginning with as few as 10 households and others with as many as 40 . Within the first 20 steps, there is a noticeable convergence trend where the number of non learning households in most simulations rapidly decreases . The steady increase over the long term suggests ongoing adaptive management practices where households continuously adjust their livestock numbers in response to changing conditions .
