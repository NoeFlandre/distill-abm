The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, starts at approximately 90. This high initial value rapidly declines within the first 10 time steps, dropping to around 45. This sharp decrease could be attributed to initial adjustments in the grazing system as households adapt their livestock management practices in response to environmental conditions and resource availability. Following this rapid decline, the number of livestock continues to decrease but at a slower rate, reaching a minimum of around 40 by approximately 40 time steps. This period of gradual decline may reflect ongoing adjustments and optimizations in livestock management as households respond to the carrying capacity of the pastures and other ecological constraints. After reaching the minimum, the average number of livestock begins to increase gradually. This upward trend continues steadily, reaching approximately 60 by the end of the simulation at 100 time steps. The increase in livestock numbers could be due to improved management practices, better adaptation to environmental conditions, and possibly the positive effects of learning strategies employed by the households. Throughout the simulation, there is some variability among the different runs, particularly noticeable after the initial rapid decline. This variability suggests that while the overall trend is consistent, individual household decisions and environmental stochasticity introduce some differences in livestock numbers across simulations.
