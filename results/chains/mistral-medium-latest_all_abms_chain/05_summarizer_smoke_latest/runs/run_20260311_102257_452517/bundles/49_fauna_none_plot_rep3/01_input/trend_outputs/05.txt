The simulations of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) show a consistent trend in the cumulative number of postpartum females killed over time. Initially, the number of postpartum females killed starts at zero. In the early stages, up to approximately 500 steps, there is a rapid increase in the number of postpartum females killed, rising steeply from zero to around 100. This rapid increase indicates a high initial hunting rate of postpartum females by the foragers. Following this initial phase, the rate of increase begins to slow down, and the curve starts to plateau. Between 500 and 1500 steps, the number of postpartum females killed continues to rise but at a much slower rate, eventually stabilizing around 120 postpartum females killed. This stabilization suggests that the hunting rate of postpartum females reaches an equilibrium where the number of postpartum females killed per unit time balances out, leading to a steady state. The overall trend indicates that while there is an initial surge in hunting activity, it eventually levels off, reflecting a dynamic equilibrium in the interaction between hunters and postpartum female prey.
