The average deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents begins at a low value of approximately 0.0074. This initial value suggests that households start with a risk attitude closely aligned with the recommended livestock numbers. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid increase in the deviation, reaching a peak value of around 0.3814 at time step 33. This trend indicates that households are increasingly deviating from the recommended livestock numbers, possibly due to initial learning and adaptation processes as they respond to ecological and climatic conditions. 

Following this peak, there is a gradual decline in the deviation, stabilizing around a value of 0.1911 by the end of the simulation at time step 100. This decline suggests that households are adjusting their risk attitudes closer to the recommended levels over time, potentially as a result of learning and experience gained through the simulation period. The presence of multiple inflection points and oscillations, particularly between time steps 20 and 33, indicates periods of adjustment and stabilization in household behavior. 

The rolling Mann-Kendall test results indicate significant increasing trends in the early phases of the simulation (time steps 0-32) and significant decreasing trends in the middle phases (time steps 33-65). The lack of significant trends in the later phases (time steps 66-98) suggests that the system reaches a relatively stable state where household risk attitudes do not exhibit strong trends in either direction. 

For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial interventions and support may be crucial in the early stages of adaptive co-management to guide households towards recommended livestock numbers. The stabilization observed in the later stages suggests that households can eventually align their risk attitudes with recommendations through learning and experience, but this process may require time and potentially supportive measures to facilitate this alignment.
