The cumulative number of postpartum females killed in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a rapid increase in the early stages, followed by a gradual stabilization. Initially, the number of postpartum females killed rises sharply from 0 to approximately 110 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase continues at a slightly slower rate until around 1000 steps, where the cumulative count reaches about 117. Beyond this point, the rate of increase significantly slows down, and the cumulative number of postpartum females killed stabilizes around 117 to 119, with minor fluctuations. The data shows that the majority of the increase occurs within the first 1000 steps, after which the system reaches a near-equilibrium state with minimal changes. The statistical analysis indicates several inflection points and change points, particularly around steps 245, 262, 285, and 475, which mark significant shifts in the trend. The rolling Mann-Kendall test reveals an increasing trend in the early stages, which transitions to no significant trend and slight decreases in later stages. The dominant oscillation period is around 63 steps, suggesting periodic fluctuations in the latter part of the simulation. For decision-makers, these trends imply that the initial phase of hunting has a critical impact on the postpartum female population, with the majority of the hunting pressure exerted early on. The stabilization phase indicates that after a certain point, the hunting pressure on postpartum females reaches a plateau, suggesting that further hunting efforts yield diminishing returns. This insight can guide strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts, emphasizing the need for early intervention and monitoring to prevent excessive depletion of the postpartum female population.
