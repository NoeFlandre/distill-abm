The number of male animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) simulations exhibits a clear and consistent declining trend over time. Initially, the number of male animals starts at 125 and shows a rapid decrease in the early stages of the simulation. By around 500 steps, the number of male animals drops significantly to approximately 20. This steep decline continues, and by around 1000 steps, the number of male animals further reduces to about 5. The trend persists, and the number of male animals approaches zero around 2000 steps, remaining at zero for the rest of the simulation period. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend with significant Mann-Kendall test results in the early windows, indicating a strong and consistent decline. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points suggests periods where the rate of decline changes, but the overall trend remains downward. The oscillation analysis indicates some periodic fluctuations, but these are minor compared to the overall declining trend. For decision-makers, this trend implies that under the given parameters and conditions of the MHPM, the male animal population is highly susceptible to extinction due to the combined effects of hunting pressure and environmental factors. This insight can guide conservation strategies and policy decisions aimed at mitigating the risk of extinction for megaherbivores.
