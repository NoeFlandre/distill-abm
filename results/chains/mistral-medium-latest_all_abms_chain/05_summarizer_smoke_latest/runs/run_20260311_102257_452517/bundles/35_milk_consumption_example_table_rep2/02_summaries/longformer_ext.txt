The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk alternatives. The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices, specifically the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The model uses both theoretical grounding and empirical data, with calibration performed against observed macro-level data. The main outcome is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The model compares two variants: a threshold-based approach and a probability-based approach for how agents become disposed to consider their choices. Agents represent adult consumers who make milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics, modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. The model includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where influence is a function of interaction probability and agent susceptibility. Social norms are globally perceived and impact the weightings of the choice function. The model monitors the number of agents consuming mostly whole milk or skimmed/semi-skimmed milk and plots the percentage of majority consumers and the average consumption of each milk type over time. Key parameters The mean percentage difference between the agents' held environmental values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.0000. This initial value indicates that at the beginning of the simulation, there is no deviation, suggesting that agents' perceptions of health values are perfectly aligned with their milk choices. The standard deviation of 401.0659 units and variance of 160853.8264 units indicate notable fluctuations in consumption patterns over time. Several peaks and valleys are observed throughout the simulation, suggesting periods of accelerated adoption followed by slight declines or plateaus. The mean deviation across the entire simulation period is 0.4965, with a median of 0.5584, showing that the central tendency of the deviation is around the midpoint of the observed range. The standard deviation of 0.1702 and variance of 0.0290 suggest moderate variability in the deviation values over time. Several inflection points are observed at time steps 4, 6, 16, 19, 22, 28, and 31, indicating periods where the rate of change in deviation experiences notable shifts. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent increasing trend in deviation across multiple time windows, with significant positive slopes ranging from 0.003 to 0.063.
