 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size . The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The average risk attitude of all social learning agents in the simulation remains constant throughout the entire duration of the model run .  Simulation of agent-based model shows distinct trends in the average total number of livestock across all households over the 100 timesteps . The model identifies several inflection points and change points, notably at timestep 9, 49, 50, and 100, which mark shifts in the trend dynamics .  Initially, at the start of the simulation, no households are engaged in social learning . By the 63rd timestep, the number of households engaged with social learning reaches its peak at approximately 32 households . The number stabilizes, maintaining a high level of engagement . The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households in the simulation shows a consistent increasing trend .
