The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands exhibits a rapid increase initially, followed by a gradual plateau. Initially, the number of females killed rises steeply from 0 to approximately 100 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase suggests a high initial hunting pressure on female prey, likely due to the high initial number of prey and the energy efficiency of hunting females as indicated by the lower processing cost and relatively high food value for hunters. 

Following this steep rise, the rate of increase slows down significantly, and the cumulative number of females killed begins to plateau around 120 females. This plateau is reached roughly between 1000 and 1500 steps, indicating a stabilization in the hunting dynamics. The plateau suggests that the hunting pressure on females has balanced out, possibly due to a reduction in the female prey population or changes in the foraging behavior of hunters as the simulation progresses. 

For decision makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressures can significantly impact female prey populations quickly. However, over time, the system reaches a new equilibrium where the rate of hunting stabilizes. This could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts to ensure that prey populations, particularly females crucial for reproduction, are not depleted beyond recovery. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions that balance the ecological impact with the needs of human hunters.
