The simulation of male prey animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear overall decreasing trend over time, starting from an initial value of 125 and eventually reaching zero. The decline is steep at first, with a significant drop in the population within the first few hundred ticks. This rapid decrease is evident as the mean value across the simulation is 21.0324, significantly lower than the starting point, indicating a sharp initial decline. The median value of 1.3462 further suggests that for the majority of the simulation duration, the population of male prey animals remains quite low. The standard deviation of 36.0441 and variance of 1299.1759 indicate substantial variability in the population numbers throughout the simulation. Key inflection points occur at various stages, notably around ticks 390, 755, and 817, suggesting periods where the rate of decline changes. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results indicate a consistent decreasing trend in the early phases of the simulation, which gradually shifts to no significant trend in the later phases, particularly after tick 1624. This suggests that the population stabilizes at a very low level before eventually reaching zero. Peaks and valleys identified in the data show minor fluctuations but do not significantly alter the overall downward trajectory. The dominant oscillation period of 63 ticks suggests some cyclical patterns, potentially linked to seasonal or reproductive cycles within the model. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the vulnerability of male prey populations under the given parameters, emphasizing the need for careful consideration of hunting pressures and environmental factors to avoid rapid declines and potential extinction.
