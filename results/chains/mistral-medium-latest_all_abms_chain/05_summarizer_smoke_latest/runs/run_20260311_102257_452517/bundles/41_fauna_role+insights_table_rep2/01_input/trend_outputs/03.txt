The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a general increasing trend over time, with specific dynamics and fluctuations. Initially, the number of males killed starts at zero and gradually increases. The trend is characterized by several phases of increasing rates, followed by periods of stabilization and slight decreases. Early in the simulation, there is a rapid increase in the number of males killed, with significant change points at ticks 125, 250, and 375, indicating shifts in the rate of increase. The most substantial growth occurs within the first 2000 ticks, where the cumulative count reaches near its peak values. The maximum value observed is approximately 120.17 males killed, occurring around tick 1876. Following this peak, the trend stabilizes with minor fluctuations and slight decreases, as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis showing periods of no significant trend and a slight decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation. The oscillation analysis indicates a dominant period of 63 ticks, suggesting regular fluctuations in the latter part of the simulation. The inflection points and peaks/valleys analysis further highlight the dynamic nature of the hunting pressure, with multiple points of significant change and minor fluctuations around the mean value of approximately 100 males killed. For decision-makers, these trends suggest that hunting pressure on male prey increases rapidly initially but stabilizes over time, with periodic fluctuations. This could imply that initial hunting efforts are highly effective but may reach a saturation point where the rate of increase slows down, potentially due to decreasing availability of male prey or other limiting factors within the model's environment.
