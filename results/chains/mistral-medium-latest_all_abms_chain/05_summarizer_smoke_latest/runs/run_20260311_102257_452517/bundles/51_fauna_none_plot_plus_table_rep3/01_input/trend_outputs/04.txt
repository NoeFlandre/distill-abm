The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, the number of females killed starts at zero and increases rapidly. This rapid increase continues until around 500 steps, where the cumulative count reaches approximately 100 females killed. Following this initial phase, the rate of increase slows down significantly, indicating a shift in the dynamics of the hunting process. By around 1000 steps, the cumulative number of females killed stabilizes, fluctuating around a value close to 120. This stabilization suggests that the system has reached a near-equilibrium state where the number of females killed does not change significantly over time. The statistical analysis indicates several inflection points and change points, highlighting key transitions in the trend. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms these observations, showing significant increasing trends initially, which then transition to a less pronounced increase and eventually to a decreasing trend towards the end of the observed period. The dominant oscillation period and various oscillation indices suggest minor periodic fluctuations around the stabilized value. Overall, the dynamics indicate an initial rapid hunting phase followed by a stabilization phase with minor fluctuations.
