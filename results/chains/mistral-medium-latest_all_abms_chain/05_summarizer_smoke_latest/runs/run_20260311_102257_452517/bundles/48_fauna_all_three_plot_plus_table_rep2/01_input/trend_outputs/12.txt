The total energy of female animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at 12,500 units and exhibits a rapid decline within the first 500 time steps. This initial sharp decrease suggests a high rate of energy depletion among female animals, potentially due to high hunting pressure, environmental stress, or a combination of both factors. The energy levels continue to decline steadily, reaching near-zero values by the end of the simulation at 2,312 time steps. This consistent downward trend indicates a continuous reduction in the energy reserves of female animals, likely leading to significant impacts on their survival and reproductive capabilities. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points suggests periods where the rate of energy depletion changes, possibly due to shifts in environmental conditions or hunting dynamics. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms a predominantly decreasing trend across various windows, reinforcing the overall decline in energy levels. The oscillation indices and dominant period indicate some periodic fluctuations, but these are minor compared to the overall declining trend. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the critical need to address factors contributing to the rapid energy depletion in female animals to prevent potential extinction scenarios. Understanding the underlying causes of these trends can inform conservation strategies and management practices aimed at mitigating the impacts on female animal populations.
