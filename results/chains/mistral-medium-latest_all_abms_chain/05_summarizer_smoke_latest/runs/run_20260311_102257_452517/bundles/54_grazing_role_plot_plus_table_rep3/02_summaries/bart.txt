 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The mean household initial risk attitude of all social learning agents in the grazing system model remains constant throughout the simulation period . The statistical analysis confirms this stability, showing no significant trends or changes .  The overall mean green biomass across the entire simulation period is about 51,529 units, with a median value of 51,197 units . The data shows a clear inflection point around time step 50, marking the transition from a decreasing trend to an increasing trend . The simulation exhibits some oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of 49 steps .  Mann-Kendall test shows a consistent increasing trend across all windows, with significant p-values, indicating a steady adoption of social learning behaviors among households . The data suggests a general trend of increasing engagement in social learning among households, with periods of faster and slower adoption rates .
