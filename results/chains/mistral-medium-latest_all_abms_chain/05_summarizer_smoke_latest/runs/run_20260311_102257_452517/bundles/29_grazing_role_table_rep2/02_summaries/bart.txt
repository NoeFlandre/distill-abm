 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The average risk attitude of all social learning agents in the simulation remained constant throughout the entire duration of the model run . The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size .  The agent-based model shows distinct trends in the average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households over 100 timesteps . The overall mean average of livestock across the entire simulation period is 48.7946, with a median of 47.3122, indicating a slight right skew in the distribution of healthy livestock .  The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households in the simulation exhibits a consistent increasing trend over the 100 timesteps . Initially, the destocking process is marked by a steady and significant increase, with periods of subtle rate changes and oscillations . The trend is characterized by a rapid initial increase, which then slows down over time .
