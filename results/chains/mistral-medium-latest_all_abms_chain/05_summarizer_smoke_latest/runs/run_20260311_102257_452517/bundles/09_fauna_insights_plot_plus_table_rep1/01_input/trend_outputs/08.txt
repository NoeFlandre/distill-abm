The number of male animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) simulation starts at 125 and exhibits a consistent decreasing trend over time, eventually reaching zero. Initially, the decline is steep, with the number of male animals dropping rapidly within the first few hundred ticks. This rapid decline continues until around 500 ticks, where the rate of decrease starts to slow down. By approximately 1000 ticks, the number of male animals has significantly reduced, and the decline becomes more gradual. Throughout the simulation, there are minor fluctuations and small peaks, but the overall trend remains a steady decrease. The statistical analysis indicates several inflection points and change points, suggesting shifts in the rate of decline at various stages. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this decreasing trend across multiple windows, with significant decreases noted in the early and middle phases of the simulation. The oscillation analysis reveals periodic minor fluctuations, but these do not alter the overall downward trajectory. For decision-makers, this trend suggests that under the given parameters, the male animal population is not sustainable and heads towards extinction. This insight could inform strategies related to conservation efforts, hunting regulations, and environmental management to prevent such outcomes in real-world scenarios.
