The data from the repeated simulations of the agent-based model show distinct trends in the number of non-learning households over 100 time steps. Initially, the number of non-learning households varies widely, with some simulations starting with as many as 40 households and others with as few as 10. This variability indicates a high degree of initial diversity in household strategies. By around 20 time steps, a noticeable convergence begins to occur. Many of the simulations show a sharp decline in the number of non-learning households, with several dropping to below 10. This trend continues, and by approximately 40 time steps, the majority of simulations stabilize, showing a consistent number of non-learning households ranging mostly between 5 and 20. Beyond this point, the number of non-learning households remains relatively stable, with minor fluctuations but no significant changes. This stabilization suggests that the system reaches a form of equilibrium where the proportion of non-learning households remains constant over time. The final time steps show that most simulations converge to a similar range, indicating a potential steady-state in the dynamics of household strategies within the grazing system.
