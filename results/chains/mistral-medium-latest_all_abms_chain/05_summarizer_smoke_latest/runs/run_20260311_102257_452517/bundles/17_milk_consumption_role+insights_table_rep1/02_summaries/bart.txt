 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  Simulation of agent-based model reveals key trends in the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent over the observed time steps . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial perceptions and social influence in driving the adoption of new products .  Understanding these trends can help in strategizing marketing and health campaigns aimed at influencing milk consumption behaviors . Initially, the adoption of alternative milk types starts at zero, reflecting the initial market conditions where whole milk was predominantly consumed . For decision-makers, these trends imply that interventions or policies aimed at promoting healthier or more environmentally friendly milk options could see gradual but consistent adoption over time .  Trend is evident from the start value of 5.29 units to a significant rise, with notable inflection points around time steps 4 and 12 . Rapid adoption phase suggests agents quickly become disposed to consider alternative milk types, influenced by health and environmental perceptions, social interactions, and initial marketing efforts .  Trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows consistent increasing trends across all windows . The oscillation analysis reveals periodic fluctuations with a dominant period of 15.00, indicating cyclical patterns in the data . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions to better align health perceptions with consumption choices, potentially through targeted education or marketing strategies .
