The cumulative number of postpartum females killed in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a rapid increase in the early stages, followed by a gradual stabilization. Initially, the number of postpartum females killed rises sharply from 0 to around 110 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase indicates a high hunting pressure on postpartum females during the initial phase of the simulation. Following this steep rise, the rate of increase slows down significantly, and the number of postpartum females killed stabilizes around 117, with minor fluctuations. The stabilization suggests that the hunting pressure reaches an equilibrium where the number of postpartum females killed does not change drastically over time. Key time steps include the initial 500 steps, where the most significant changes occur, and the subsequent steps where the metric stabilizes. The mean value of 102.44 and the median value of 116.86 further support the observation of a rapid initial increase followed by stabilization. The standard deviation of 30.00 indicates some variability in the number of postpartum females killed, but the overall trend remains consistent. The analysis of the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows significant increasing trends in the early windows, which later transition to no trend or slight decreases, reinforcing the observation of initial rapid changes followed by stabilization. For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressures are intense but eventually reach a steady state, which could inform strategies for managing hunting practices and conservation efforts.
