The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulated time steps. Initially, the number of deaths starts at zero and rapidly increases, reaching approximately 200 deaths by around 500 ticks. This rapid increase indicates a high initial mortality rate, likely due to a combination of predation by human hunters and natural causes. 

Following this initial surge, the rate of increase in the number of deaths slows down significantly. By around 1000 ticks, the cumulative number of deaths stabilizes, fluctuating slightly around a mean value of approximately 235 deaths. This stabilization suggests that the prey population has reached a dynamic equilibrium where the rate of deaths is balanced by the birth rate and other factors influencing population dynamics. 

Key inflection points and change points in the data indicate periods where the rate of deaths changes significantly. For instance, notable change points occur around 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850 ticks, suggesting shifts in the underlying dynamics of the model. These shifts could be due to changes in the interaction between predators and prey, environmental conditions, or other model parameters. 

The statistical analysis reveals several periods of increasing trends in the number of deaths, particularly in the early stages of the simulation. However, as the simulation progresses, periods of no significant trend and even slight decreases are observed, indicating fluctuations in the mortality rate. The presence of oscillations with a dominant period of 63 ticks suggests periodic variations in the number of deaths, possibly linked to seasonal changes or other cyclic factors in the model. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial conditions and early interventions in managing wildlife populations. The rapid initial increase in deaths underscores the vulnerability of prey populations to predation and environmental pressures. The subsequent stabilization suggests that, over time, populations may reach a new equilibrium, but this balance can be delicate and subject to periodic fluctuations. Understanding these dynamics can inform conservation strategies and policies aimed at maintaining sustainable wildlife populations in the face of hunting pressures and environmental changes.
