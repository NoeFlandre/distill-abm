The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 10 time steps, reaching approximately 14 households. This initial rapid adoption of social learning suggests that early in the simulation, households quickly recognize and adopt beneficial behaviors from their peers. The trend continues to rise more gradually, reaching around 32 households by time step 63. This indicates a steady but slower diffusion of social learning practices as the simulation progresses, possibly due to the saturation of easily adoptable practices or the increasing complexity of decision-making environments. After time step 63, the number of households engaging in social learning stabilizes, fluctuating slightly around the 32 household mark. This stabilization suggests that a significant portion of the population has integrated social learning into their decision-making processes, leading to a new equilibrium in the system. The consistent increase in social learning adoption across different time windows, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall test, underscores the robustness of this trend. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of facilitating early adoption of beneficial practices and understanding the factors that contribute to the stabilization of social learning behaviors. The initial rapid increase suggests that interventions aimed at promoting beneficial behaviors are most effective early in the timeline, while the subsequent stabilization indicates the need for ongoing support to maintain high levels of engagement.
