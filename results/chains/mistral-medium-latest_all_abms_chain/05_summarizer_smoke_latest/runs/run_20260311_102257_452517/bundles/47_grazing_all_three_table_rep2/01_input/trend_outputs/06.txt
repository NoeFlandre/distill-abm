The average number of livestock destocked across all households begins at zero and exhibits a sharp increase, peaking at approximately 6.61 livestock by the third time step. This initial spike suggests an immediate and significant response to initial conditions, possibly due to limited resources or high initial livestock numbers. Following this peak, there is a rapid decline to around 3.37 livestock by the ninth time step, indicating a quick adjustment period where households may be adapting their livestock numbers to better match available resources. Subsequent peaks and valleys occur at regular intervals, with notable peaks at time steps 12, 17, 19, 22, 24, and 26, each followed by a decline. These oscillations suggest periodic adjustments in livestock numbers, potentially due to fluctuating resource availability or household decision-making processes. The trend generally decreases over time, with significant decreasing trends identified in multiple rolling Mann-Kendall windows, particularly in the early and middle phases of the simulation. After the initial fluctuations, the number of destocked livestock stabilizes to lower values, ending at approximately 0.31 livestock by the final time step. This stabilization implies that households have reached a more sustainable balance in their livestock numbers relative to available resources. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial resource management and the potential for periodic adjustments in livestock numbers to maintain sustainability in grazing systems.
