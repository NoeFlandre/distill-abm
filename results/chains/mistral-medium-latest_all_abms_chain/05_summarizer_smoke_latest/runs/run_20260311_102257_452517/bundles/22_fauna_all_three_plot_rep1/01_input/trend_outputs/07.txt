The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at zero and remains low for the initial time steps. This period of low mortality is followed by a rapid increase in deaths, reaching approximately 150 deaths by around 500 time steps. This sharp rise in mortality could be attributed to the initial interactions between the prey population and human hunters, as well as natural mortality factors such as starvation and internal causes. As the simulation progresses, the rate of increase in the number of deaths begins to slow down, indicating a potential stabilization in the population dynamics. By around 1000 time steps, the cumulative number of deaths reaches approximately 200 and continues to increase gradually, eventually plateauing near 250 deaths. This plateau suggests that the population has reached a new equilibrium where the rate of deaths is balanced by the remaining population's reproductive and survival strategies. The variability among different simulation runs increases slightly after the initial rapid rise in deaths, indicating that while the initial mortality trends are consistent, the long-term dynamics introduce more variability due to the complex interactions between hunting pressure, environmental conditions, and herd demographics. For decision makers, these trends highlight the critical initial period where interventions could potentially mitigate the rapid increase in mortality. Understanding the factors contributing to the stabilization of deaths could also inform strategies to maintain a balanced ecosystem and prevent the extinction of megaherbivores.
