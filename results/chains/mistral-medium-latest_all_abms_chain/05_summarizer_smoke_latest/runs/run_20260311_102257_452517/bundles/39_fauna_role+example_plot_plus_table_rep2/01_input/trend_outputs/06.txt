This initial period of no births could be attributed to the initial conditions of the simulation, where the population might be stabilizing or adapting to the environment. Following this period, there is a rapid increase in the number of births, reaching approximately 5.6 births at around 1700 time steps. This significant rise suggests a period of favorable conditions for reproduction, possibly due to an abundance of resources or a stable environment. After reaching this peak, the number of births begins to decline gradually, stabilizing at around 1 birth per time step towards the end of the simulation. This decline could indicate a return to equilibrium as the population adjusts to the carrying capacity of the environment or as resources become limited. The simulation exhibits several inflection points and change points, indicating shifts in the trends of births over time. These points suggest periods of transition in the population dynamics, possibly due to changes in environmental conditions, predator-prey interactions, or internal population dynamics. The presence of multiple peaks and valleys further supports the idea of fluctuating conditions influencing birth rates.
