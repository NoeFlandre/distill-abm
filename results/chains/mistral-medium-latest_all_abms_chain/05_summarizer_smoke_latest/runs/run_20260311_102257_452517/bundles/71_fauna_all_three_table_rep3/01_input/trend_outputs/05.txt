The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the early time steps. This initial rise is steep, indicating a high hunting rate of postpartum females at the beginning of the simulation. The trend continues to increase but at a gradually slowing rate, suggesting that the hunting pressure remains high but the rate of increase in hunting success diminishes over time. By around 2000 time steps, the cumulative number of hunted postpartum females stabilizes, fluctuating around a mean value of approximately 117. This stabilization indicates that the hunting rate has balanced with the availability or vulnerability of postpartum females, reaching a near-equilibrium state. The peaks and valleys identified in the data are very close in value, reinforcing the idea of a stable equilibrium with minor fluctuations. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, followed by a period of no significant trend, and eventually a slight decreasing trend towards the end. This suggests that while the initial phases are marked by consistent increases in hunting success, the latter phases see a stabilization and slight decline, possibly due to reduced availability of postpartum females or changes in their vulnerability. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points further supports the idea of dynamic shifts in the hunting success rate over time. For decision makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressures are highly impactful but tend to stabilize over time, which could inform strategies related to wildlife management and conservation efforts. The slight decline towards the end of the simulation might indicate the need for adaptive management practices to sustain hunting pressures without leading to potential declines in prey populations.
