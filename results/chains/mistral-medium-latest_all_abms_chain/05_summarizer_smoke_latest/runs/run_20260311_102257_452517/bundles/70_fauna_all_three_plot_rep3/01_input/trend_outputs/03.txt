The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 80 males killed. This rapid initial increase suggests that male prey are highly accessible or vulnerable to hunting pressure early in the simulation. The rate of increase begins to slow down between 500 and 1000 time steps, indicating a potential reduction in the availability of male prey or increased difficulty in hunting them. By around 1000 time steps, the cumulative number of males killed stabilizes around 120, with minimal increases observed beyond this point. This stabilization suggests that the system may have reached a state where the remaining male prey are either less accessible, better at evading hunters, or that the hunters have shifted their focus to other prey types. The low variance among simulation runs indicates consistent hunting patterns and outcomes across different scenarios. For decision makers, these trends highlight the initial vulnerability of male prey to hunting pressure and the eventual stabilization of hunting success, which could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts.
