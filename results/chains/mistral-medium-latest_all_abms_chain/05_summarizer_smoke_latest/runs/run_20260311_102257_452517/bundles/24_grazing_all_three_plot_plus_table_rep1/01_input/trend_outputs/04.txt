The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches starts at approximately 45,000 units. Initially, there is a slight increase, reaching around 45,261 units by the fourth time step. Following this, the biomass experiences a gradual decline, hitting a low point of about 42,057 units at the 27th time step. This initial decline could be attributed to the grazing pressure exerted by livestock, which consumes the available biomass faster than it can regenerate in the early stages of the simulation. After reaching this low point, the biomass begins a steady recovery. This recovery phase is marked by a consistent increase in biomass, indicating that the vegetation growth rate surpasses the grazing pressure, allowing the biomass to accumulate. By the 100th time step, the biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units, the highest value observed in the simulation. The overall trend shows a significant recovery and growth in biomass after the initial decline, suggesting that the system stabilizes and the vegetation adapts to the grazing pressure over time. The mean biomass across the simulation period is around 51,529 units, with a standard deviation of approximately 8,000 units, indicating some variability but a general trend towards recovery and growth. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates various trends within different windows of the simulation. Initially, there is no significant trend, followed by a decreasing trend, and then a consistent increasing trend in the latter part of the simulation. This analysis suggests that while the initial phases of the simulation show some instability and decline in biomass, the long-term trend is towards recovery and growth, which is a positive indicator for the sustainability of the grazing system. Decision makers can infer that while initial grazing pressure may lead to a temporary decline in biomass, the system has the capacity to recover and stabilize over time, provided that the ecological and climatic conditions remain favorable.
