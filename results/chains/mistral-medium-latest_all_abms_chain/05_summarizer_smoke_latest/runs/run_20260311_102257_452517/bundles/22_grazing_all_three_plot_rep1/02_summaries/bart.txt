 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households . The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems .  The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, starts at approximately 90 across all households . The mean destock of households starts at a high value of approximately 7 livestock per household . Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid decline in livestock numbers, dropping to around 40 .  The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 20 time steps . This rapid initial increase indicates a quick adoption of social learning behaviors among households . For decision makers, this implies the need for interventions to ensure more equitable distribution of resources and knowledge to mitigate growing disparities in livestock health .
