The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, the number of deaths starts at zero and remains relatively low for a brief period. As time progresses, there is a rapid increase in the number of deaths, indicating a phase of high mortality among the prey population. This rapid increase continues until around 500 steps, where the rate of increase starts to slow down. By approximately 1000 steps, the cumulative number of deaths begins to plateau, suggesting a stabilization in the mortality rate. The number of deaths eventually reaches a maximum value of around 240, after which it remains relatively constant with minor fluctuations. The statistical analysis indicates several inflection points and change points, highlighting key transitions in the mortality rate. The rolling Mann-Kendall test reveals periods of increasing trends followed by phases with no significant trend and a final period of decreasing trend. The oscillation analysis suggests a dominant periodicity in the later stages of the simulation, indicating regular fluctuations in the number of deaths. Overall, the dynamics of the prey population's mortality show an initial rapid increase followed by a stabilization phase with minor oscillations.
