The most substantial decrease occurs early on, as indicated by the first change point at tick 125, followed by subsequent change points at ticks 350, 500, 625, and 875. These change points mark significant shifts in the rate of energy decline.  

The energy levels experience several peaks and valleys, particularly around the mid-range of the simulation. Notable peaks are observed at ticks 65, 377, 380, 388, 385, 390, 393, and 408, with corresponding valleys closely following these peaks. These fluctuations suggest periodic variations in the energy levels, potentially due to seasonal effects or other cyclic factors within the model. The dominant oscillation period of 63 ticks further supports the presence of regular cyclic patterns in the energy levels.  

Despite these oscillations, the overall trend remains a steady decline in energy. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms this decreasing trend across multiple windows, with significant decreases noted in the early and mid-stages of the simulation. The trend briefly shifts to increasing in some windows, such as between ticks 1392 and 1623, and ticks 1856 and 2087, but these increases are minor and do not reverse the overall downward trajectory.  

The energy level eventually approaches zero, with the minimum value of 0.0000 reached at tick 2284. This indicates a complete depletion of energy in the male prey population by the end of the simulation. The inflection points scattered throughout the simulation highlight periods where the rate of energy decline changes, but the consistent downward trend underscores the overall reduction in energy availability.  

For decision-makers, these trends suggest that under the given parameters, the male prey population experiences a significant and steady decline in energy levels. This could imply increasing stress on the population, potentially leading to reduced survival rates or other adverse outcomes. Understanding these dynamics can help in assessing the impact of various parameters on the sustainability of the prey population and the broader ecosystem.
