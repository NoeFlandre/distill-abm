The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear trend over the simulation period. Initially, the number of females killed starts at zero and gradually increases over time. The trend shows a rapid increase in the early stages, with significant inflection points around ticks 245, 262, and 285, indicating periods where the rate of increase changes notably. The most substantial growth occurs within the first 2000 ticks, where the cumulative count rises steeply. 

Following this initial phase, the rate of increase slows down, with additional inflection points observed at ticks 1204, 1336, 1487, 1631, 1725, 1800, 1940, and 1943. These points suggest periods where the hunting rate stabilizes or changes slightly. The cumulative number of females killed reaches a peak around tick 1851, with a value of approximately 123.67, indicating the highest hunting activity observed in the simulation. 

After reaching the peak, the trend shows a slight decline or stabilization, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, which identifies a decreasing trend in the later stages of the simulation (window 9: start=1856 end=2087). The oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation period suggest minor fluctuations in the cumulative count, but these are relatively insignificant compared to the overall trend. 

The statistical analysis reveals that the mean cumulative number of females killed is around 104.56, with a median of 119.06, indicating that the central tendency of the data is skewed towards the higher end of the observed range. The standard deviation of 31.10 and variance of 967.08 highlight the variability in the hunting activity over different simulation runs. 

For decision-makers, these trends suggest that hunting pressure on female prey increases rapidly initially and then stabilizes, with minor fluctuations in the later stages. This pattern could imply that as the forager bands become more established and efficient in their hunting strategies, the rate of prey capture stabilizes, leading to a more consistent but lower rate of increase in the cumulative count of females killed. Understanding these dynamics can help in assessing the long-term impact of hunting on prey populations and the potential for equilibrium or extinction events.
