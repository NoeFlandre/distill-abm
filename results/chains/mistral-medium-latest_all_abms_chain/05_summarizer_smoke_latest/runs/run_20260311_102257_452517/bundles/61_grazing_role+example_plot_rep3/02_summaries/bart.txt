 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . It allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households .  The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 20 time steps . The Gini coefficient for total livestock healthy starts at 0 across all simulations, indicating an equal distribution of healthy livestock among households at the beginning . Over time, disparities in livestock health distribution become more pronounced and stabilize at a higher inequality level .  Between time steps 20 and 40, the number of non-learning households fluctuates but generally trends downward, stabilizing around 5 households . This period of fluctuation and gradual decline indicates ongoing adjustments and strategy shifts among households as they respond to changing conditions or learn from their experiences . After time step 40, stabilization suggests that the system has reached a new equilibrium .
