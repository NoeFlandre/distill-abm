The average reserve biomass in the grazing system exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the reserve biomass starts at 90,000 units and experiences a decreasing trend until around the 21st timestep, reaching a minimum value of approximately 84,114 units at the 26th timestep. This initial decline suggests a period of increased grazing pressure or reduced vegetation growth. Following this decline, the reserve biomass begins to recover, showing a significant increasing trend starting from the 33rd timestep onwards. This recovery phase is marked by a steady rise in biomass, culminating in a maximum value of around 121,073 units at the 100th timestep. The inflection points at the 8th and 48th timesteps indicate changes in the rate of biomass accumulation, suggesting shifts in the underlying dynamics of the grazing system. The presence of significant increasing trends in multiple rolling Mann-Kendall windows highlights a robust recovery and growth in reserve biomass during the latter half of the simulation period. For decision makers, these trends imply that while initial grazing pressures may reduce reserve biomass, adaptive management practices can lead to significant recovery and growth over time. Understanding these dynamics can aid in developing strategies to balance grazing intensity with vegetation recovery, ensuring long-term sustainability of the grazing system.
