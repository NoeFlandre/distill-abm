The average amount of available green biomass across all patches begins at 45,000 units. Initially, there is a slight increase, reaching approximately 45,261 units by the fourth time step. Following this, the biomass experiences a notable decline, hitting a low of around 42,057 units at the 27th time step. This decline could be indicative of initial heavy grazing pressure as livestock agents adapt to the available resources. Post this decline, the biomass starts to recover gradually. From the 33rd time step onwards, there is a consistent increasing trend in the green biomass. This upward trajectory continues steadily, reaching a peak of 60,535 units by the final time step of 100. The consistent increase in biomass after the initial decline suggests that the grazing system may be reaching a more sustainable equilibrium, where the regrowth of vegetation can keep pace with or exceed the grazing pressure. The presence of significant increasing trends in multiple rolling Mann-Kendall windows further supports this observation of recovery and growth. For decision makers, this trend indicates that while initial grazing pressures can significantly impact biomass levels, over time, the system can recover and potentially reach higher levels of biomass, suggesting resilience in the pastoral system under the given conditions.
