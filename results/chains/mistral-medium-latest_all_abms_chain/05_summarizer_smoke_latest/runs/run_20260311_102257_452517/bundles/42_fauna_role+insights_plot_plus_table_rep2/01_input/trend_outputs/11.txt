The total energy of male animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a consistent and significant decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12,500 units. This high energy level rapidly declines within the first few hundred steps, indicating a sharp reduction in the energy available in the male animal population. By around 232 steps, the energy level has already dropped significantly, and this decreasing trend continues steadily throughout the simulation. Key inflection points and change points in the data highlight periods where the rate of decline varies, but the overall trend remains a decrease in total energy. Notable change points occur at steps 125, 350, 500, 625, 875, and 2310, marking significant shifts in the rate of energy decline. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms that the trend is consistently decreasing across various windows, with significant statistical evidence supporting this observation. The presence of oscillations with a dominant period of 63 steps suggests periodic fluctuations in the energy levels, but these do not alter the overall downward trajectory. The energy levels eventually approach zero, indicating a near-complete depletion of energy in the male animal population by the end of the simulation. For decision-makers, this trend underscores the critical impact of the model's parameters on the sustainability of the male animal population, highlighting the need for careful consideration of factors such as hunting pressure, environmental conditions, and animal life history in conservation efforts.
