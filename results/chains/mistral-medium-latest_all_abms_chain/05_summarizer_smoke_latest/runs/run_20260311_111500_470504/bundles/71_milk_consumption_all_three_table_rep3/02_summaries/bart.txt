 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  The total number of times agents chose whole milk starts at zero and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation . The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at a very low value of 5.29 units . This initial value suggests that at the start of the simulation, the adoption of alternative milk types is minimal .  Total number of milk choices made by all agents combined starts at zero and increases steadily over the entire simulation period . Means quantity of whole milk consumed by agents starts at a high value of 2654.81 units at the beginning of the simulation . Consumption then exhibits a rapid and consistent decline over time, reflecting a significant shift in consumption patterns .  The overall trend shows a clear and steady increase in the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk over the simulation period . For decision makers, this trend indicates that habits play an increasingly significant role in consumer choices, which could be crucial for strategies aimed at promoting healthier milk alternatives .  The mean deviation between the agents' held environmental values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.0000 . This initial value indicates that, at the beginning of the simulation, there is no discrepancy between the environmental values held by the agents and those implied by milk choices . The rapid increase in deviation highlights the importance of early interventions to align consumer choices with environmental values . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions to better align health perceptions with actual consumption behaviors .
