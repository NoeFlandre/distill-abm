The simulation results show the average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents over 101 time steps. Initially, the deviation starts at a low value of 0.0074. The trend exhibits a rapid increase in the early stages, reaching a peak value of approximately 0.3814 around time step 33. This period of increase is followed by a gradual decline, with the deviation value decreasing to around 0.1911 by the end of the simulation. The data exhibits several peaks and valleys, indicating fluctuations in the deviation values throughout the simulation period. Notably, there are significant increasing trends in the early phases (time steps 0-32) and decreasing trends in the middle phases (time steps 33-65), as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall test. The latter part of the simulation (time steps 66-100) shows no significant trend, suggesting a stabilization in the deviation values. The dominant oscillation period of approximately 49 time steps indicates cyclical patterns in the data. For decision makers, these trends suggest that initial adaptive management strategies may lead to higher deviations from recommended livestock numbers, but these deviations tend to stabilize over time. Understanding these patterns can help in designing interventions that account for initial fluctuations and aim for long-term stability in livestock management.
