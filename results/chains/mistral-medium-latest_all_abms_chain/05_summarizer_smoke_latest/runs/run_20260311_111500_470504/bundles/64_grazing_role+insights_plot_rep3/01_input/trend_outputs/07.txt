The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock starts at 0 for all simulations, indicating an equal distribution of livestock among households at the beginning. Over the first 20 steps, the Gini coefficient increases rapidly, reaching values between 0.015 and 0.045. This suggests a quick divergence in livestock distribution among households. From step 20 to step 60, the Gini coefficient continues to rise but at a slower pace, with values ranging from approximately 0.030 to 0.060. This period indicates a continued but less rapid increase in inequality. Beyond step 60, the Gini coefficient stabilizes for most simulations, with values plateauing between 0.045 and 0.090. This stabilization suggests that the inequality in livestock distribution has reached a steady state. For decision makers, the rapid initial increase in inequality highlights the importance of early interventions to promote equitable livestock distribution. The eventual stabilization suggests that while inequality may be inevitable, it can be managed to some extent over the long term.
