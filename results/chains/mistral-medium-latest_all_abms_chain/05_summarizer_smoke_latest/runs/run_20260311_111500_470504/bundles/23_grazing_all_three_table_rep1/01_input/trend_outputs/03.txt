The average amount of reserve biomass available across all patches in the grazing system begins at 90,000 units. Initially, there is a slight increase, reaching approximately 90,523 units by the third time step. Following this, the reserve biomass experiences a notable decline, hitting a low of around 84,114 units at the 26th time step. This decline could be attributed to the initial grazing pressure exerted by the households as they start utilizing the pastures without significant adaptive management strategies in place. After this low point, the reserve biomass begins a gradual recovery. The recovery phase is marked by a steady increase, with significant upward trends observed from the 33rd time step onwards. The biomass continues to rise, reaching its peak at the final time step of 121,073 units. This long-term increase suggests that the grazing system may be adapting over time, possibly due to the learning behaviors of the households, even though the specific learning mechanisms are not directly observable in this data. The overall trend indicates an initial stress on the biomass reserves followed by a recovery and eventual growth, which could imply that the system is moving towards a more sustainable state as households potentially learn and adapt their grazing strategies. For decision makers, this trend highlights the importance of initial management practices and the potential benefits of adaptive strategies in maintaining and enhancing biomass reserves over time.
