 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbivores . The model simulates a simplified grassland environment with human hunters and male and female ungulates . The landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, consisting of a 211x211 patch matrix .  The model allows users to manipulate these parameters to test various hypotheses related to extinction events . The temporal units used in the model are 'ticks,' which can be scaled to any temporal granularity . The model includes summary statistics that are shown as continuous line graphs, reflecting data from each tick . The number of human hunter-gatherer bands in the simulation remains constant at 20 throughout the entire duration .  Understanding these dynamics can help in managing hunting practices to ensure the sustainability of prey populations . For decision-makers, this suggests that initial hunting pressures have a significant impact on postpartum females, but over time, the system reaches a balance . The number of male animals in the simulation starts at 125 and declines rapidly over the first 500 time steps . This initial decline could be attributed to the high initial number of hunters and the relatively high energy value and low processing cost of male prey, making them a prime target for hunters . By around 1000 time steps, the number of animals drops significantly, reaching near zero, indicating a near-complete extinction event .  Total energy of all animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model starts at 12,500 units and exhibits a rapid decline within the first 500 time steps . The energy levels continue to decline steadily, albeit at a slower rate, indicating ongoing stress on the female animal population . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the factors contributing to the rapid decline in energy levels .  Low variability and minimal fluctuations suggest that the grass regrowth and grazing dynamics are well-balanced, providing a stable environment for the herbivores .
