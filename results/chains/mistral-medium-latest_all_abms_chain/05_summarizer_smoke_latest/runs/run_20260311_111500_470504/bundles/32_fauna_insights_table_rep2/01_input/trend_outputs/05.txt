The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear trend over the simulation period. Initially, the count starts at zero and shows a rapid increase in the early time steps, indicating a phase of intense hunting activity. This rapid increase continues until around the 850th tick, where the rate of increase starts to slow down significantly. By the 2310th tick, the trend stabilizes, and the cumulative count reaches a plateau, fluctuating around a mean value of approximately 117 postpartum females hunted. The data shows minor oscillations with a dominant period of 63 ticks, suggesting periodic variations in hunting success or prey availability. The peaks and valleys identified in the data are relatively close in value, indicating that these oscillations are not drastic but rather subtle fluctuations around the plateau. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms these observations, showing significant increasing trends in the early phases and a shift to no significant trend or even a slight decreasing trend in the later phases, indicating stabilization. For decision makers, these trends suggest that initial hunting pressure is high but eventually stabilizes, which could imply that the prey population reaches a point where further hunting does not significantly increase the cumulative count of hunted postpartum females. This stabilization could be due to various factors such as reduced prey availability, changes in prey behavior, or environmental constraints.
