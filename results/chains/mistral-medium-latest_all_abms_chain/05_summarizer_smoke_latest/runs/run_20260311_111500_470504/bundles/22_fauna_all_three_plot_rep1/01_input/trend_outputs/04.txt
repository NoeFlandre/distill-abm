The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 100 females killed. This rapid initial increase indicates a high initial hunting pressure on female prey, likely due to the abundance of prey and the energy requirements of the hunters. Around the 500 to 1000 time steps mark, the rate of increase begins to slow down, suggesting a possible reduction in the female prey population or a shift in hunting strategies. By 1000 time steps, the cumulative number of females killed stabilizes around 120, indicating that a near equilibrium state has been reached where the number of females killed plateaus. This stabilization could imply that the remaining female prey population has reached a level where it is more challenging for hunters to find and kill additional females, or that the hunters have shifted their focus to other prey types. The low variance among simulation runs suggests consistent hunting patterns and outcomes across different simulations, implying predictable dynamics in the interaction between hunters and female prey. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial conditions and early interventions in managing hunting pressures to prevent rapid declines in prey populations. Understanding the stabilization point can also inform sustainable hunting quotas and conservation strategies.
