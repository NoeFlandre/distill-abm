The cumulative number of postpartum females killed starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 100. This rapid increase suggests that postpartum females are highly vulnerable to hunting pressure immediately after giving birth, likely due to their reduced mobility and increased visibility. The rate of increase slows down significantly after this initial period, indicating that the most susceptible individuals are targeted early in the simulation. By around 1000 time steps, the cumulative number of postpartum females killed stabilizes, fluctuating slightly around 120. This stabilization suggests that a balance is reached where the remaining postpartum females are either less accessible or better able to avoid predation. The low variance across simulation runs in the initial time steps indicates a consistent pattern of vulnerability among postpartum females. However, as time progresses, slight deviations become noticeable, reflecting the increasing complexity and variability in the interactions between hunters and prey. For decision makers, these trends highlight the critical vulnerability period for postpartum females and the importance of early intervention strategies to mitigate initial hunting pressures. Understanding these dynamics can inform conservation efforts aimed at protecting vulnerable populations during their most susceptible life stages.
