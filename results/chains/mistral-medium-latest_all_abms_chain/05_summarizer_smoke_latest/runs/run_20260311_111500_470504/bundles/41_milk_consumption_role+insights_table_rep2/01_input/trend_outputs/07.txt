The simulation of the agent-based model reveals several key trends in the mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents over time. Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of 5.29 units. This low initial consumption is followed by a rapid and significant increase, indicating a swift adoption of alternative milk types among the agents. By the end of the simulation, the mean consumption reaches a peak value of approximately 1456.60 units, showing a substantial shift in milk consumption preferences over the simulated period. The mean consumption across the entire simulation period is 1053.21 units, with a median value of 1220.21 units, suggesting that the data is somewhat skewed with several lower values early in the simulation. The standard deviation of 401.07 units indicates considerable variability in consumption levels over time. The trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows distinct phases in the adoption process. Initially, there are two windows of significant increasing trends, indicating rapid adoption phases. The first significant increasing trend has a slope of 112.80 units per time step, and the second has an even steeper slope of 139.01 units per time step. These phases are followed by periods of no significant trend, suggesting stabilization in consumption patterns. Towards the end of the simulation, another significant increasing trend is observed with a slope of 48.54 units per time step, indicating a renewed phase of adoption or increased consumption. The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of 15 time steps, suggesting cyclical patterns in the adoption behavior. These oscillations could imply periodic influences on consumption, possibly due to social interactions or other temporal factors embedded in the model. The presence of multiple inflection points and peaks further supports the idea of a dynamic and evolving adoption process. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial conditions and the rapid adoption phase in the early stages of introducing alternative milk types. The periods of stabilization and renewed growth suggest that continuous efforts might be necessary to sustain and increase consumption over time. Understanding the cyclical patterns can also help in planning periodic interventions or campaigns to boost consumption.
