 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents in the simulation exhibits distinct trends over the 100 timesteps .  The simulation of the agent-based model shows a clear trend in the average total number of livestock across all households over the 100 timesteps . The oscillation analysis reveals periodic fluctuations with a dominant period of 49 time steps, suggesting cyclical patterns in biomass availability . For decision makers, these trends highlight the potential for stabilization over time, which could inform strategies for managing livestock and resources more effectively .  The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households exhibits a consistent increasing trend over the entire simulation period of 100 timesteps . For decision makers, this trend suggests that social learning behaviors can become widely adopted over time in a grazing system, potentially leading to more adaptive and cooperative management practices among households .
