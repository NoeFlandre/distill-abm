 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent begins at a high value of 2654.81 units . This initial value indicates a strong preference for whole milk consumption among the agents at the start of the simulation . The consumption then exhibits a rapid and significant decline in the early time steps, dropping sharply within the first few time steps . This steep decline suggests a substantial shift in consumer behavior .  Understanding these dynamics can help in designing strategies to promote healthier and more environmentally friendly milk choices . The fluctuations and cyclical patterns indicate that continuous efforts may be needed to sustain high levels of adoption . Understanding these oscillations could help in timing targeted interventions to maximize their impact .  The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per person begins at a low value of approximately 5.29 units . This initial value suggests that at the start of the simulation in 1974, the consumption of alternative milk types was minimal . The consumption then increases rapidly, reaching around 1265.47 units by the 11th time step . This sharp rise indicates a significant shift in consumer preferences . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing policies or campaigns aimed at influencing dietary habits  The mean deviation between the agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.0000 . This initial alignment suggests that agents' initial choices are strongly influenced by their health perceptions . The deviation then increases steadily over time, reaching a value of 0.7251 by the end of the simulation . This trend suggests that interventions aimed at aligning health perceptions with actual choices may need to be dynamically adjusted .
