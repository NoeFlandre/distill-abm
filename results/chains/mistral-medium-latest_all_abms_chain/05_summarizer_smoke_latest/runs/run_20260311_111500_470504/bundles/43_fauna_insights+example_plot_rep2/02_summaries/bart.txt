 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbivores . The model simulates a simplified grassland environment with human hunters and male and female ungulates . The landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, consisting of a 211x211 patch matrix .  The model allows users to manipulate these parameters to test various hypotheses related to extinction events . The model includes summary statistics that are shown as continuous line graphs, reflecting data from each tick . These statistics include the number of prey items taken per 10 ticks, the proportion of grazed to ungrazed grass, forager energy, and number of animals in the environment . The number of human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant throughout the simulation .  The number of postpartum females killed starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps of the simulation . This rapid increase in the number of deaths suggests a high initial mortality rate . By around 1000 time steps, the rate of increase begins to slow, indicating that the initial surge in hunting success is tapering off . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the initial adaptation period of the prey population and factors influencing reproductive success .  The total number of animals in the simulation starts at 250, reflecting the initial population set by the parameters . Over the first 500 time steps, there is a rapid decline in the total energy of male animals . This sharp decrease suggests a significant reduction in the male animal population, potentially due to high hunting pressure or other environmental factors .  The trend implies that under the given conditions, the prey population struggles to sustain itself, leading to a significant reduction in total energy over time . Understanding these patterns can inform management strategies to maintain sustainable grassland ecosystems, ensuring adequate food availability for prey animals while preventing overgrazing . For decision-makers, these trends highlight the dynamic interplay between grazing pressure and grass regrowth .
