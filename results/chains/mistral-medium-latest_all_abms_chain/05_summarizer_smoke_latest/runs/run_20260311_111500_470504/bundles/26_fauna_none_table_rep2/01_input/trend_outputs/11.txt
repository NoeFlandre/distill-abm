The combined total energy available in the population of male prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear overall decreasing trend throughout the simulation. Initially, the energy level starts at 12,500 units. This high initial value is followed by a rapid decline in the early stages of the simulation. By the 65th tick, the energy level drops to approximately 11,608 units, marking the first significant peak. This downward trend continues with several smaller peaks and valleys, indicating periods of slight recovery followed by further declines. Notable peaks occur around ticks 377, 380, and 388, with energy values around 4,002, 3,986, and 3,975 units respectively. These peaks are relatively minor compared to the initial energy levels, suggesting brief periods of stabilization or slight recovery in the male prey population's energy. The energy levels continue to decrease steadily, with several inflection points indicating changes in the rate of decline. These inflection points are observed at ticks 316, 453, 516, and several others, suggesting shifts in the dynamics of energy availability. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms significant decreasing trends in most windows, with only a few windows showing increasing trends or no significant change. The dominant oscillation period of 63 ticks suggests a cyclical pattern in the energy levels, possibly related to seasonal variations or other periodic factors in the model. Ultimately, the energy level reaches zero by the 2,284th tick, indicating the complete depletion of energy in the male prey population. This trend reflects the interactions between environmental conditions, hunting pressure, and the life history of the animals, leading to the eventual extinction of the male prey population in this simulation.
