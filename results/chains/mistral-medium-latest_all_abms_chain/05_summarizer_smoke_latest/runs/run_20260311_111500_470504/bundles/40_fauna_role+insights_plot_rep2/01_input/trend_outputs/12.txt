The total energy of female animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a rapid decline in the initial phase of the simulation. Starting from an initial value of approximately 12,000 units, the total energy of female animals decreases sharply within the first 500 steps, dropping to around 2,000 units. This steep decline suggests a significant reduction in the energy reserves of the female population, potentially due to high hunting pressure, environmental factors, or a combination of both. 

Following this initial sharp decline, the total energy continues to decrease but at a slower rate, stabilizing around 1,000 steps. By approximately 2,000 steps, the total energy of female animals approaches near-zero levels, indicating a critical depletion of energy reserves within the female population. This trend implies that the female animals are facing severe survival challenges, which could lead to population collapse or extinction if the conditions persist. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the vulnerability of the female animal population under the given model parameters. The rapid initial decline in energy reserves suggests that immediate interventions may be necessary to mitigate the factors contributing to this energy loss. Potential strategies could include reducing hunting pressure, enhancing food availability, or modifying environmental conditions to support the survival and energy retention of female animals. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for developing effective conservation and management policies to prevent the extinction of megaherbivores in similar ecological contexts.
