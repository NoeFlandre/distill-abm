The simulation of the agent-based model reveals several key trends in the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent over the observed time steps. Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of 5.29. This low initial value suggests that at the beginning of the simulation, agents predominantly consume whole milk, with very little adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives.  \n\nThe consumption of alternative milk types exhibits a rapid increase in the early stages of the simulation. This trend is evident from the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, which shows significant increasing trends in the initial windows. For instance, from time steps 0 to 4 and 5 to 9, the consumption increases sharply with slopes of approximately 140.433 and 139.015 respectively. This rapid adoption phase indicates that agents quickly begin to consider and adopt alternative milk types, likely influenced by the initial perceptions of health and environmental benefits, as well as social interactions. \n\nFollowing this initial surge, the consumption levels enter a phase of stabilization. From time steps 10 to 14 onwards, the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates no significant trend, suggesting that the consumption levels have reached a plateau. During this phase, the consumption values oscillate around a mean of approximately 1220.21, with minor fluctuations. The peaks and valleys identified in the data, such as peaks at time steps 11, 13, and 21 with values around 1265.47, 1240.35, and 1226.86 respectively, and valleys at time steps 12, 20, and 23 with values around 1233.62, 1214.46, and 1209.70 respectively, indicate minor variations but overall stability in consumption patterns. \n\nTowards the end of the simulation, there is a slight resurgence in the increasing trend of consumption. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis for time steps 25 to 29 shows a significant increasing trend with a slope of 48.544, indicating a renewed uptake in the consumption of alternative milk types. This late-stage increase could be attributed to cumulative social influence and habit formation over time, reinforcing the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. \n\nThe dominant oscillation period of 15 time steps suggests a cyclical pattern in the consumption data, possibly reflecting periodic social interactions or habitual reevaluations among the agents. The inflection points and oscillation indices further support the presence of these cyclic trends within the stabilized consumption phase. \n\nFor decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial perceptions and social influence in driving the adoption of new products. The rapid initial increase underscores the impact of early interventions and marketing efforts. The stabilization phase suggests that once a critical mass of adoption is achieved, consumption levels can be maintained with relatively minor fluctuations. The late-stage increase indicates the potential for long-term habit formation and sustained behavioral change, emphasizing the need for continuous engagement and reinforcement strategies.
