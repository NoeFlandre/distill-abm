The simulation of the agent-based model reveals several key trends in the mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents over time. Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of 5.29 units. This low initial consumption suggests that agents primarily favored whole milk at the beginning of the simulation period, reflecting the initial habits and perceptions aligned with the incumbent milk type. 

In the early stages, there is a rapid increase in the consumption of alternative milk types. This trend is evident from the start value of 5.29 units to a significant rise, with notable inflection points around time steps 4 and 12. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a strong increasing trend in these early windows, with significant slopes of 112.802 and 139.015, respectively. This rapid adoption phase suggests that agents quickly become disposed to consider alternative milk types, influenced by health and environmental perceptions, social interactions, and initial marketing efforts. 

Following this rapid increase, the consumption of alternative milk types stabilizes around a higher mean value. Between time steps 10 and 24, the trend stabilizes with no significant increases or decreases, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall test showing no trend and non-significant p-values. The mean consumption during this period hovers around 1220.2054 units, with minor fluctuations. This stabilization phase indicates a period where the adoption of alternative milk types has become more normalized among the agents, with habits and social norms playing a significant role in sustaining the consumption levels. 

Towards the end of the simulation, there is a slight resurgence in the consumption of alternative milk types. The rolling Mann-Kendall test for the window between time steps 25 and 29 shows a significant increasing trend with a slope of 48.544. This late-stage increase could be attributed to cumulative social influence and reinforcement of positive perceptions about alternative milk types over time. The final consumption value reaches 1456.5991 units, indicating a strong overall shift towards alternative milk types by the end of the simulation period. 

The oscillation analysis reveals periodic fluctuations in consumption, with dominant periods around 15 time steps. These oscillations suggest periodic influences, possibly due to cyclical social interactions or varying degrees of social conformity and susceptibility among the agents. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial perceptions and social influence in driving the adoption of alternative milk types. The rapid early increase underscores the impact of initial marketing and awareness campaigns, while the stabilization phase suggests the role of habits and social norms in maintaining consumption levels. The late-stage resurgence indicates the potential for long-term reinforcement strategies to further increase adoption.
