This divergence suggests that households quickly adapt their risk attitudes based on initial conditions and early experiences in the simulation. Between 20 and 40 steps, the risk attitudes continue to evolve, with some households stabilizing at higher risk attitudes around 0.7 to 0.8, while others stabilize at lower risk attitudes around 0.1 to 0.2. This period indicates a phase of adjustment where households are fine-tuning their strategies based on the outcomes of their initial decisions. After 40 steps, the risk attitudes generally stabilize, with minor fluctuations observed until the end of the simulation at 100 steps. The stabilization suggests that households have settled into their respective adaptive strategies, reflecting a balance between risk-taking and risk-averse behaviors. The implications for decision-makers are significant. The rapid initial divergence highlights the importance of early interventions and support to guide households towards sustainable risk attitudes. The stabilization phase indicates that long-term policies should focus on maintaining this balance and providing resources for households to adapt to changing conditions without drastic shifts in risk behavior.
