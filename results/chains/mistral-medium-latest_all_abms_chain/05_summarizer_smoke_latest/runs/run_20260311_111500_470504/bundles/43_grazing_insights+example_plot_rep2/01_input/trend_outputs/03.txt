The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system starts at approximately 90,000 units. It initially experiences a slight increase, reaching around 92,000 units by the 10th time step. This decline could be attributed to the initial grazing pressure exerted by the livestock as they begin to utilize the available biomass. After reaching this low point, the mean reserve biomass begins to recover steadily. By the 50th time step, it surpasses the initial value and continues to increase, indicating a period of biomass regeneration and growth. This recovery phase suggests that the grazing system is resilient and capable of regenerating biomass under the given conditions. The mean reserve biomass stabilizes around 120,000 units from the 70th time step onwards, showing a plateau that indicates the system has reached a new equilibrium where biomass production matches the consumption by livestock. The trends observed in the mean reserve biomass provide insights into the sustainability and resilience of the grazing system. The initial decline followed by a recovery and stabilization suggests that while the system can withstand initial grazing pressure, it requires time to regenerate and reach a stable state. For decision-makers, this implies the importance of managing grazing intensity and allowing sufficient time for biomass recovery to maintain the long-term health and productivity of the grazing system.
