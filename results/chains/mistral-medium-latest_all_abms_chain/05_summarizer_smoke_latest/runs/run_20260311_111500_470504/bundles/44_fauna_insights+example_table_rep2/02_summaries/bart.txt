 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbivores . The model simulates a simplified grassland environment with human hunters and male and female ungulates . The landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, consisting of a 211x211 patch matrix .  The model allows users to manipulate these parameters to test various hypotheses related to extinction events . The model includes summary statistics that are shown as continuous line graphs, reflecting data from each tick . These statistics include the number of prey items taken per 10 ticks, the proportion of grazed to ungrazed grass, forager energy, and number of animals in the environment .  The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model begins at zero and exhibits a series of fluctuations throughout the simulation period . Initial rise indicates a high hunting pressure on postpartum females, likely due to their vulnerability and the high energy value they provide to hunters . Following this peak, the trend shows minor fluctuations but generally stabilizes, indicating a possible equilibrium between hunting pressure and prey availability . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the dynamics of hunting pressure .  The number of male prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model begins at 125 and exhibits a rapid decline initially . This initial trend suggests a high predation or mortality rate early in the simulation . Following this rapid decline, the population experiences a series of smaller fluctuations with minor peaks and valleys . The oscillations observed towards the end of the simulation suggest minor fluctuations around the equilibrium state .  The total energy available in prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model begins at a high value of 16,666.67 units . The energy levels exhibit a rapid decline in the early time steps, dropping to approximately 11,608 units by time step 65 . This initial decline suggests a high rate of energy consumption or loss among the male prey, possibly due to high predation pressure .  The percentage of vegetated landscape patches in the simulation begins at approximately 33% and remains relatively stable throughout the duration of the simulation, ending at around 32.99% . The rolling Mann-Kendall test reveals periods of both increasing and decreasing trends in vegetation coverage, with significant changes detected at various intervals .
