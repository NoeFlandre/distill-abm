 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  The total number of times agents chose whole milk starts at zero and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation . The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at a very low value of 5.29 . This initial value suggests that at the start of the simulation, the adoption of alternative milk types is minimal . As the simulation progresses, the rate of increase in whole milk choices accelerates .  The total number of milk choices made by all agents combined starts at zero and increases steadily over the entire simulation period . This steady increase reflects a consistent rise in the cumulative number of decisions made by agents regarding their milk choices . The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent increasing trend across all analyzed windows, with a significant positive slope of 1000.000 .  The habit factor shows a steady increase with minor periodic fluctuations, reflecting a gradual strengthening of the habit influence on whole milk consumption over the simulation period . The trend analysis shows a consistent increase in the habit factor, with significant trends detected in multiple rolling windows . The mean deviation between the agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.0000 .  The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a significant increasing trend in the early time steps . However, in the later time steps, there is no significant trend, indicating that the system has reached a stable state . The dominant oscillation period of 15 time steps suggests that there are regular, minor fluctuations in the mean deviation .
