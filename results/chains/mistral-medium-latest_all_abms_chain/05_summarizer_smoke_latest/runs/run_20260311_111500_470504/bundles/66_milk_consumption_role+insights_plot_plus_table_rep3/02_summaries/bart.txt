 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  Simulation results indicate a significant decline in the average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent over the observed period . The statistical analysis confirms a consistent decreasing trend across multiple time windows, with significant declines noted in the early and middle phases of the simulation . For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the factors driving the initial rapid decline and the eventual stabilization .  Understanding these dynamics can help in designing targeted interventions or marketing strategies to influence milk consumption patterns . For decision-makers, these trends imply a growing preference for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk over time, influenced by the parameters governing agent behavior such as memory lifetime, health perceptions, habit thresholds, and social influences .  The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents in the simulation exhibits distinct trends over the observed time steps . Initially, consumption starts at a very low value of approximately 5.29 units . This is followed by a rapid increase in consumption, reaching around 1265.47 units by the 11th time step . Following this peak, consumption levels exhibit minor fluctuations but generally stabilize around 1220.21 units .  A model simulating milk consumption behaviors shows a clear trend of rapid initial increase followed by a gradual stabilization . For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial interventions or changes in parameters can lead to significant shifts in consumer behavior, but these changes tend to stabilize over time as agents adapt and fine-tune their choices .
