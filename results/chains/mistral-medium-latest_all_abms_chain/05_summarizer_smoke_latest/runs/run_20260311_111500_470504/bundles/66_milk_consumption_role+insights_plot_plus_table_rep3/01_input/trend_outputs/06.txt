The simulation of the agent-based model reveals a clear and consistent downward trend in the mean quantity of whole milk consumed by agents over the time steps. Initially, the mean whole milk quantity starts at approximately 2654.81 units. This high starting value indicates a strong initial preference or habit for whole milk consumption among the agents.  

In the early stages, there is a steep decline in whole milk consumption, dropping to around 800 units by step 18. This rapid decrease suggests that agents are quickly adopting alternative milk types, likely influenced by the initial perceptions of health and environmental benefits, as well as social interactions and susceptibility to social norms. The inflection points around steps 5, 13, and 16 further highlight significant changes in the rate of decline during this period. 

Following this steep decline, the rate of decrease in whole milk consumption slows down, stabilizing around 800 units between steps 18 and 26. This stabilization period indicates a phase where the agents' habits, social influences, and justifications for their choices are balancing out, leading to a slower rate of change in consumption patterns. The inflection points at steps 19, 24, and 25 mark subtle shifts in this stabilization phase. 

After step 26, there is a renewed decline in whole milk consumption, albeit at a more gradual pace compared to the initial steep drop. By the final step (step 32), the mean whole milk quantity reaches approximately 253.10 units. This final value represents a significant reduction from the initial consumption levels, indicating a substantial shift in milk preferences among the agents over the simulation period. 

The rolling Mann-Kendall test results confirm the overall decreasing trend in whole milk consumption, with significant decreases observed in multiple windows (e.g., steps 0-4, 5-9, 20-24). The periods where no significant trend is detected (e.g., steps 15-19, 25-29) correspond to the stabilization phases observed in the data. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial perceptions and social influences in driving significant changes in consumption behaviors. The steep initial decline suggests that interventions targeting health and environmental perceptions, as well as leveraging social networks, can be highly effective in promoting the adoption of alternative milk types. The subsequent stabilization and gradual decline phases indicate the need for sustained efforts to reinforce these changes and overcome entrenched habits.
