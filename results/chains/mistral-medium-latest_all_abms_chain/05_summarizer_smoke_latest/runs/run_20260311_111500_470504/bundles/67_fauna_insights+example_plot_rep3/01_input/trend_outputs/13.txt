The total energy of all animals begins at approximately 25,000 units. It declines rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching around 5,000 units. This sharp decline suggests a significant reduction in the energy reserves of the prey population, potentially due to high initial predation pressure or limited food resources. Following this rapid decline, the energy levels continue to decrease more gradually, stabilizing near zero around the 2,000 time step mark. This stabilization indicates that the prey population has either been significantly reduced or has adapted to the environmental pressures, resulting in a new equilibrium with much lower energy reserves. The low variance across simulation runs in the initial steps suggests a consistent pattern of energy depletion, while the slight deviations observed later on could be attributed to varying dynamics in predator-prey interactions or environmental conditions. For decision makers, this trend highlights the critical initial period where interventions could be most impactful in preserving the energy reserves of the prey population. Understanding the factors contributing to the rapid energy decline could inform strategies to mitigate predation pressure or enhance food availability during these crucial early stages.
