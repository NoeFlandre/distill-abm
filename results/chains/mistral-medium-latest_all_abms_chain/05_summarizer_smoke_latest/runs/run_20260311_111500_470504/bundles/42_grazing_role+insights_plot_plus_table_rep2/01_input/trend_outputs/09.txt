The analysis of the simulation results reveals several key trends and insights regarding the mean destock total of households over a period of 100 steps. Initially, the mean destock total starts at 0 and exhibits a rapid increase in the early stages, particularly within the first 25 steps. This initial phase is characterized by a steep slope, indicating a high rate of destocking. 

Following this initial surge, the rate of increase in the mean destock total begins to slow down, as evidenced by the inflection points and the results of the rolling Mann-Kendall test. The inflection points, occurring at steps 45, 47, 53, 56, 57, 62, 67, 72, 74, and 75, suggest changes in the rate of increase. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent increasing trend across all windows, although the slope of this trend decreases over time. For instance, the slope drops from 4.956 in the first window (steps 0-10) to 0.311 in the later windows (steps 66-100), indicating a deceleration in the rate of destocking. 

The change points identified at steps 25, 60, and 100 further highlight significant shifts in the trend. The first change point at step 25 marks the transition from a rapid increase to a more gradual rise. The second change point at step 60 indicates another shift, possibly reflecting a stabilization phase where the rate of destocking becomes more consistent but slower. The final change point at step 100 corresponds to the end of the simulation period. 

The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of 49 steps, with multiple oscillation indices suggesting periodic fluctuations in the destocking process. These oscillations may reflect underlying cyclic patterns in household decision-making or ecological factors influencing destocking practices. 

For decision-makers, these trends suggest that while destocking increases over time, the rate of this increase slows down, indicating potential adaptation or stabilization in household behaviors and ecological conditions. The initial rapid destocking phase may require closer monitoring and intervention to manage potential socio-ecological impacts, while the later stabilization phase could indicate a period where households have adapted to the conditions, leading to more predictable and manageable destocking rates.
