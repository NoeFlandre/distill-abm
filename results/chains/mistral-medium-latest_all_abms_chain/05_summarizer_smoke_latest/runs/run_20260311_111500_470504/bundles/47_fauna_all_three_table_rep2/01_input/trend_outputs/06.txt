The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model starts at zero and exhibits a series of fluctuations throughout the simulation. Initially, there is a gradual increase in the number of births, reaching a peak of approximately 5.6 around the midpoint of the simulation. This peak is followed by a series of smaller peaks and valleys, indicating periodic fluctuations in birth rates. Key peaks are observed at around 1702, 1392, 1214, and 1073 time steps, with values of 5.6, 4.3, 3.45, and 2.875 respectively. The valleys, indicating lower birth rates, are observed at around 1422, 1281, and 1097 time steps, with values of approximately 3.8889, 3.0588, and 2.871 respectively. The simulation ends with the cumulative number of births stabilizing around 1. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in birth rates during the early and middle phases of the simulation, followed by significant decreasing trends in the later phases. This suggests that the birth rate initially increases, likely due to favorable conditions and lower predation pressure, but eventually decreases as the population dynamics stabilize and environmental or predation pressures increase. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points further supports the notion of dynamic shifts in birth rates over time. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of considering the temporal variability in birth rates when designing conservation strategies or assessing the impact of hunting pressures on megaherbivore populations.
