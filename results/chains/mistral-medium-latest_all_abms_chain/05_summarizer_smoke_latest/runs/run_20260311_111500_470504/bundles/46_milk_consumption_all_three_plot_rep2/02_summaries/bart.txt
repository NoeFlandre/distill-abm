 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  The mean weekly whole milk consumption per agent starts at approximately 2,600 units . There is a rapid decline in consumption within the first 10 steps, dropping to around 1,000 units . This sharp decrease suggests a significant shift in consumer behavior, possibly due to initial perceptions of health and environmental benefits of alternative milk types . After step 20, there is a gradual increase in consumption, culminating in a value close to 1,400 units .  The total number of milk choices made by all agents combined starts at 0 and increases linearly over the 30 steps of the simulation, reaching a value of approximately 32,000 . The linear trend suggests that the rate at which agents make choices remains constant throughout the simulation period . For decision makers, this implies a predictable and steady pattern of consumer behavior .  The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk starts at a value of 1.0 at the beginning of the simulation . At step 5 to step 10, it is relatively stable, hovering around 1.05 . By step 20, it shows a more substantial impact of habits on milk choice . The steady increase in habits over the simulation period implies that habits become progressively more influential in the decision-making process .  The mean value environmental deviation starts at 0, indicating that initially, there is no discrepancy between the agents' environmental values and their milk choices . The deviation then increases rapidly, reaching approximately 13 by around 5 steps . After this rapid increase, the deviation stabilizes and plateaus around the value of 13 for the remainder of the simulation, up to 30 steps .
