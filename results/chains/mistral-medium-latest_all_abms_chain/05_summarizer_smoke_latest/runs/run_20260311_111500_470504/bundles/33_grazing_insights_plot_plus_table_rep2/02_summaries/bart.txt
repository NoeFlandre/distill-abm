 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The goal is to evaluate how different behavioral types and learning strategies impact the resilience and sustainability of pastoral systems . The model allows for the adjustment of a range of parameters to observe their effects on social, ecological, and economic outcomes .  Simulation results indicate that the mean household risk attitude in the agent-based model exhibits distinct trends over the 100 time steps . Initial decline and subsequent recovery of the biomass have important implications for decision-makers . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions and policies that support sustainable and resilient pastoral systems .  The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock starts at 0 and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period of 100 steps . The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly in the initial steps, reaching around 30 households by step 20 . The average number of destocked livestock across households shows a sharp initial increase, peaking at approximately 6.61 livestock at the third time step .  The number of non-learning households in the simulation starts at 20 and exhibits an initial increase, reaching a peak of approximately 21.1 households around the 8th step . Throughout the entire simulation, the trend is consistently increasing . Understanding these trends can help . anticipate the long-term impacts of different behavioral types and learning strategies on the resilience and sustainability of pastoral systems .
