The number of female animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a consistent and significant decreasing trend over time. Initially, the number of female animals starts at 125 and gradually declines to zero by the end of the simulation period. The decline is steep at the beginning, with a rapid reduction in the first few hundred steps, followed by a more gradual decrease. Key inflection points occur at various stages, indicating changes in the rate of decline. Notable inflection points are observed around steps 305, 740, 833, and several others, suggesting periods where the rate of decline changes. The statistical analysis reveals multiple windows where the trend is significantly decreasing, with only a few instances of slight increases. The dominant period of oscillation is around 63 steps, indicating some periodic fluctuations within the overall declining trend. The implications for decision-makers suggest that under the given parameters, the female animal population is under significant stress, leading to eventual extinction. This trend highlights the potential impact of hunting pressure and environmental conditions on the survival of megaherbivores, emphasizing the need for careful consideration of conservation strategies and hunting regulations.
