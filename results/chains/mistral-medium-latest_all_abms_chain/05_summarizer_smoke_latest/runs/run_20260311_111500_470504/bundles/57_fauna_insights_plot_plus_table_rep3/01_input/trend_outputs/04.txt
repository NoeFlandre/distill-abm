The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a rapid increase initially, followed by a gradual stabilization. The number of females killed starts at 0 and rises sharply within the first 500 steps, reaching approximately 100. This rapid increase continues more gradually until around 1000 steps, where the cumulative count stabilizes around 120. The trend then shows minor fluctuations but generally remains steady around this value for the remainder of the simulation. Key inflection points occur around steps 245, 262, and 285, indicating significant changes in the rate of increase. Additionally, there are notable change points at steps 125, 375, 475, 625, 850, 1600, and 2310, suggesting shifts in the dynamics of hunting pressure or prey availability. The data also indicates a dominant oscillation period of 63 steps, reflecting minor periodic variations in the number of females killed. The initial rapid increase in the number of females killed suggests a high initial hunting pressure or vulnerability of the female prey, which stabilizes as the system reaches a form of equilibrium. For decision-makers, this trend implies that initial hunting pressure significantly impacts female prey populations, but over time, the system may reach a more stable state. Understanding these dynamics can help in formulating strategies for conservation or sustainable hunting practices.
