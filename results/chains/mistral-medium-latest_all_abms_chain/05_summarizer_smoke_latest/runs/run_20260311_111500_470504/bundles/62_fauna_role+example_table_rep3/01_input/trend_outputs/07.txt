The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at 0 and exhibits a rapid increase in the initial time steps. This early rise in deaths can be attributed to the initial interactions between the human hunters and the prey population, as well as natural mortality factors. By around 250 time steps, the rate of increase begins to slow down, indicating a potential stabilization in the dynamics between predators and prey.

The trend continues to rise steadily, reaching a peak value of approximately 240 deaths around the 1876th time step. This peak suggests a critical point where the cumulative effect of predation and natural mortality has reached its maximum within the simulation period. Following this peak, the cumulative death count exhibits minor fluctuations but generally stabilizes around the value of 239 to 240 deaths.

The simulation shows several inflection points, notably around time steps 362, 1241, and 1330, indicating significant changes in the rate of deaths. These inflection points could correspond to shifts in the population dynamics, such as changes in the prey population size or hunter behavior adaptations. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with a notable shift to no significant trend around the middle time steps, followed by a decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation.

Oscillations in the death count are observed with a dominant period of approximately 63 time steps, particularly noticeable towards the end of the simulation. These oscillations suggest periodic variations in the death rate, potentially due to cyclical environmental factors or predator-prey interactions. The overall pattern indicates a complex interplay between hunting pressure, natural mortality, and environmental factors influencing the prey population dynamics.
