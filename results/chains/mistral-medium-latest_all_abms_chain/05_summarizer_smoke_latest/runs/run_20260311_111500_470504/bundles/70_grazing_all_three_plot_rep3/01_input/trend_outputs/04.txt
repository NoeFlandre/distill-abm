The mean green biomass across all patches starts at approximately 45,000 units. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a slight decline, indicating initial grazing activity by the livestock. This initial decline could be attributed to the immediate consumption of readily available green biomass as households begin their grazing activities. Following this initial period, the mean green biomass continues to decrease gradually until around the 40th time step, reaching a lower point of approximately 43,000 units. This period of gradual decline suggests a steady consumption rate as livestock graze across the patches. After the 40th time step, the mean green biomass begins to increase steadily. This increase continues until around the 80th time step, where the mean green biomass reaches a plateau at approximately 63,000 units. The steady increase in green biomass during this period indicates a recovery phase where vegetation regrowth outpaces the consumption by livestock, possibly due to favorable climatic conditions or reduced grazing pressure. From the 80th time step to the end of the simulation at the 100th time step, the mean green biomass stabilizes around 63,000 units. This stabilization suggests that the system has reached a dynamic equilibrium where the rate of biomass consumption by livestock is balanced by the rate of vegetation regrowth. For decision makers, the initial decline in green biomass highlights the importance of managing initial grazing pressure to avoid overconsumption. The subsequent recovery and stabilization phases indicate the resilience of the vegetation under the given conditions, suggesting that sustainable grazing practices can lead to a stable equilibrium in biomass availability. This insight can guide policies aimed at maintaining long-term ecological balance and household livelihoods in grazing systems.
