 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The mean household initial risk attitude in the simulation remains constant throughout the 100 steps . The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies .  The simulation begins with an initial average of 90 livestock per household . The overall mean of livestock across the simulation period is approximately 48.79, with a median value of 47.31, indicating a slight skew in the distribution of livestock . By the end of the simulation at step 100, the average number of livestock stabilizes around 47.55 .  The mean destock total of households in the grazing system model shows a consistent increasing trend over the 100 steps of the simulation . Trend is consistent upward throughout the simulation, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall test, which shows significant increasing trends across all windows . The model's parameters, such as the number of households, learning behaviors, and ecological settings, contribute to the observed dynamics .
