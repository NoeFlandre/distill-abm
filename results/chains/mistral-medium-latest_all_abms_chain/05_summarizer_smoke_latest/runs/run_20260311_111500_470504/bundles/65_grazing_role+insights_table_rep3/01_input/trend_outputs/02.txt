The simulation results indicate that the average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents exhibits distinct trends over the 100 timesteps. Initially, the deviation starts at a low value of 0.0074 and shows a rapid increase, reaching a peak value of approximately 0.3814 around timestep 33. This upward trend is statistically significant, as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall test, which shows increasing trends with high significance in the early windows. 

Following this peak, the deviation enters a phase of gradual decline. This decreasing trend is also statistically significant, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall test for the middle windows. The deviation continues to decrease, stabilizing around a value of 0.1911 by the end of the simulation. The latter part of the simulation shows no significant trend, suggesting a stabilization in the deviation from the recommended number of livestock. 

The presence of multiple inflection points and oscillation indices suggests that the system experiences several changes in the rate of deviation, reflecting the dynamic interactions between households and their adaptive behaviors. The dominant oscillation period of 49 timesteps indicates a cyclical pattern in the deviation, which could be influenced by the adaptive learning mechanisms and environmental factors simulated in the model. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of considering the adaptive behaviors of households and the potential for significant deviations from recommended livestock numbers over time. The initial rapid increase in deviation suggests that households may initially struggle to align with recommendations, possibly due to learning curves or environmental variability. The subsequent stabilization indicates that over time, households may adapt their behaviors to better align with recommendations, although some deviation persists. This insight underscores the need for policies that support adaptive learning and provide mechanisms for households to better manage livestock numbers in response to changing conditions.
