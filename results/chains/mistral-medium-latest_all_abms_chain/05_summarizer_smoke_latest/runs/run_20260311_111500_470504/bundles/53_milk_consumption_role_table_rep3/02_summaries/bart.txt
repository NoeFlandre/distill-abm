 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  Simulation of the agent-based model shows a clear and consistent downward trend in the average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent from 1974 to 2005 . The data also exhibits some oscillatory behavior, with dominant periods of oscillation identified around specific time steps, suggesting periodic fluctuations in the rate of decline .  Simulation of agent-based model shows distinct trends in the mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents over time . Initially, the mean consumption starts at a very low value of 5.29 . This value rapidly increases in the early time steps, indicating a swift initial adoption of alternative milk types . By the final time step, the total number of choices reaches its maximum value, suggesting a substantial shift in consumer behavior towards skimmed milk .  The whole milk choice function due to habit in the agent-based model shows a consistent increasing trend over the simulation period . Initially, the mean factor starts at a value of 1.0000, indicating no initial influence of habit on the choice of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk . The trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent upward trend across multiple windows .  The mean deviation between the agents' held environmental values and their milk choices exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period . Initially, the deviation starts at 0.0000 and rapidly increases, indicating a significant divergence . However, as the simulation progresses, the trend stabilizes, and no significant trends are detected in the later windows .
