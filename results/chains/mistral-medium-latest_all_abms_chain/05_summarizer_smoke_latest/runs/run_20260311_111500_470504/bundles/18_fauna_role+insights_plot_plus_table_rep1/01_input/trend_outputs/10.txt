The simulation of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent trend of decreasing total number of prey animals over time. Initially, the number of animals starts at approximately 166.67 and exhibits a steep decline within the first few hundred time steps. By around 500 time steps, the number of animals has significantly reduced, continuing to decrease steadily thereafter. The rate of decline slows down as the number of animals approaches zero, with the population effectively reaching extinction by the end of the simulation at 73000 time steps. 

Key time steps and values highlight the rapid initial decline: 
- At the start (time step 0), the population is at its maximum with approximately 166.67 animals. 
- By time step 500, the population has dropped significantly. 
- The trend continues with smaller fluctuations and a general downward trajectory until the population reaches zero. 

The statistical analysis indicates several inflection points and change points, suggesting periods where the rate of decline changes. Notably, the rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent decreasing trend across various windows, with significant negative slopes indicating a steady decline in the animal population. The presence of peaks and valleys at specific time steps suggests minor temporary stabilizations or slight recoveries in the population, but these are not sustained. 

For decision makers, these trends imply that under the given parameters and conditions of the MHPM, the prey population is highly susceptible to extinction due to the combined pressures of hunting and environmental factors. The rapid initial decline suggests that early intervention might be crucial to prevent population collapse. Understanding the specific parameters that contribute most significantly to this decline could help in designing strategies to mitigate the risk of extinction for megaherbivores in similar ecological settings.
