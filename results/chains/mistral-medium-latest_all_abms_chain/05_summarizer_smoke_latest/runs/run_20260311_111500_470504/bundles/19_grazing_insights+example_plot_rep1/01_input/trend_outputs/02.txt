The mean household risk attitude starts with a value of approximately 0.5 across all simulations. In the initial time steps, there is a rapid divergence in the risk attitudes, with some households increasing their risk attitude sharply while others decrease it. By around 20 time steps, the risk attitudes have diverged significantly, with some households reaching a risk attitude close to 0.8 and others dropping to around 0.2. This divergence indicates that households are quickly adapting their risk attitudes based on initial experiences and learning within the model. Between 20 and 40 time steps, the risk attitudes continue to adjust but at a slower rate, showing a period of stabilization where households fine-tune their strategies. After 40 time steps, the risk attitudes generally stabilize, with minor fluctuations observed until the end of the simulation at 100 time steps. The final risk attitudes range from approximately -0.2 to 0.8, indicating a wide spectrum of risk attitudes among households by the end of the simulation. This stabilization suggests that households have settled into their adaptive strategies based on the learned behaviors and environmental interactions over the simulation period. For decision makers, the initial rapid divergence in risk attitudes highlights the importance of early interventions to guide household strategies towards more sustainable practices. The eventual stabilization of risk attitudes suggests that long-term policies should focus on reinforcing positive adaptive behaviors and mitigating the impacts of more risky strategies.
