 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbivores . The model simulates a simplified grassland environment with human hunters and male and female ungulates . The landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, consisting of a 211x211 patch matrix .  The model allows users to manipulate these parameters to test various hypotheses related to extinction events . The temporal units used in the model are 'ticks,' which can be scaled to any temporal granularity . The model includes summary statistics that are shown as continuous line graphs, reflecting data from each tick .  Understanding these dynamics can help in designing conservation strategies or managing hunting practices to ensure the sustainability of both predator and prey populations . For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the initial rapid increase in hunting success and the subsequent stabilization could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices .  The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a consistent decreasing trend in the number of male prey animals across most time windows . The oscillation indices indicate periodic minor variations in the population, but these do not alter the overall declining trend . For decision makers, these trends highlight the vulnerability of the prey population under sustained hunting pressure .  The total energy of all animals in the simulation starts at approximately 16,667 units . The energy levels decline rapidly within the first 500 time steps, dropping to around 4,000 units . This sharp decline suggests a significant reduction in the prey population, potentially due to high predation pressure or limited food resources . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing strategies to prevent rapid energy depletion .
