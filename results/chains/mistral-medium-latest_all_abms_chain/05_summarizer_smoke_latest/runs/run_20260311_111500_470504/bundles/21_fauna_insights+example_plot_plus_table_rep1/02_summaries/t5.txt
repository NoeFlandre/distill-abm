the model simulates a simplified grassland environment with human hunters and male and female ungulates . the goal is to understand how environmental conditions, human hunting behavior, and animal life history interact to influence extinction events or create equilibrium conditions . the model allows users to manipulate these parameters to test various hypotheses related to extinction events . the temporal units used in the model are 'ticks,' which can be scaled to any temporal granularity . small peaks and valleys indicate slight variations in hunting success or prey availability over time . these fluctuations are relatively minor, suggesting a stable long-term dynamic between the hunters and the prey population . over time, the system stabilizes, and the rate of increase in the number of females killed diminishes . the number of male prey animals in the simulation starts at 125 and declines rapidly over the first 500 time steps . this initial decline could be attributed to the high initial number of hunters and the immediate pressure they exert on the female prey population . however, these fluctuations are not significant enough to reverse the overall declining trend . the total energy of female animals in the simulation starts at 12,500 units . around 1,500 time steps, the decline rate further slows, and the energy levels stabilize near zero . this indicates that the male animal population has been nearly depleted . the rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant trends in different windows . initial increasing trends followed by decreasing trends, suggesting dynamic changes in vegetation cover over time . for decision makers, these trends imply that the landscape maintains a relatively stable vegetation cover .
