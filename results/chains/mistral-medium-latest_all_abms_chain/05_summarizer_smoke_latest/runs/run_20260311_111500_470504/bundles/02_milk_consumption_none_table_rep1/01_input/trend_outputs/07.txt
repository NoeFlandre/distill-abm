The simulation of the agent-based model tracking the mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents from 1974 to 2005 reveals several notable trends and patterns. Initially, the mean consumption starts at a very low value of approximately 5.29 units. This value rapidly increases in the early time steps, indicating a swift initial adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk among the agents. By the time step corresponding to the early 1980s, the mean consumption reaches around 1265.47 units, marking the first significant peak. This period of rapid increase is followed by a phase of stabilization where the consumption values exhibit minor fluctuations around a high mean value. During the mid-1980s to the early 1990s, the mean consumption values oscillate mildly, with notable peaks around 1240.35 units and 1226.86 units, and valleys around 1233.62 units and 1209.70 units. These oscillations suggest periodic variations in consumption, potentially reflecting underlying cyclic behaviors in agent decision-making or external influencing factors. Towards the later time steps, corresponding to the mid-1990s onwards, the mean consumption generally trends upwards again, culminating in a maximum value of approximately 1456.60 units by the final time step in 2005. The overall trend demonstrates a significant and sustained increase in the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk over the simulated period, with intermittent periods of stabilization and minor fluctuations. The inflection points and rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicate significant increasing trends in the early and late phases of the simulation, with periods of no significant trend in between, reflecting phases of rapid adoption followed by stabilization and minor adjustments in consumption patterns.
