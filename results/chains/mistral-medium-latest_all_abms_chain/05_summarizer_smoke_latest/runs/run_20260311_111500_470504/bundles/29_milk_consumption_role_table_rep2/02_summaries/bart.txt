 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent in the simulation exhibits a clear and consistent downward trend over time . By the end of the simulation, the consumption drops significantly to around 253.10 units . The dominant period of oscillation is around 15 time steps, indicating cyclical patterns within the broader trend . The consumption reaches its maximum value of 1456.60 at the final time step .  Initially, the total number of choices starts at zero, reflecting the baseline where no agents have yet chosen these options . As the simulation progresses, there is a steady increase in the adoption rate . The trend remains consistently increasing throughout the simulation, with no detected peaks or valleys, suggesting a smooth and continuous adoption process without periods of decline or stagnation .  The habit factor for whole milk choice shows a steady and significant increase over the simulation period . The mean factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choice function due to habit exhibits a clear increasing trend over time . This increasing trend becomes statistically significant and is consistently observed across multiple rolling Mann-Kendall windows .  The mean percentage difference between choice function scores suggests a growing divergence in agent preferences and decision-making processes over time, influenced by the model's parameters and the interactions among agents . The mean deviation between the agents' health values and their milk choices shows a clear and consistent upward trend with periodic fluctuations .
