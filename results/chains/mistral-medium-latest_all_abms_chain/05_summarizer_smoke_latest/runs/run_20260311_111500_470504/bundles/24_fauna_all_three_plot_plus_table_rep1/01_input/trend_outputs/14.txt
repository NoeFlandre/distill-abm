The percentage of green patches, representing the proportion of vegetated landscape available as fodder for prey animals, begins at approximately 33%. This initial value indicates a relatively stable environment with a significant amount of available grass. The percentage then experiences a sharp decline, reaching a minimum of about 25% around the 365th time step. This decline suggests a period of intense grazing or environmental stress that significantly reduces the available vegetation. Following this decline, the percentage of green patches gradually recovers, showing a steady increase and stabilizing around 33% again. This recovery phase indicates a period of regrowth and stabilization of the grass patches, suggesting that the landscape has the capacity to regenerate given the conditions set in the model. Throughout the simulation, there are minor fluctuations, but the overall trend after the initial decline is a return to the initial levels of vegetation. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points suggests periods of dynamic adjustment in the landscape's vegetation cover. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results indicate significant trends in the data, with initial increasing trends followed by periods of decreasing trends, reflecting the complex interactions between grazing, regrowth, and environmental conditions. For decision makers, these trends highlight the resilience of the landscape under the given conditions but also underscore the potential vulnerability to intense grazing pressures or environmental stressors that can rapidly deplete vegetation cover. Understanding these dynamics can inform management practices aimed at maintaining sustainable levels of vegetation for prey animals.
