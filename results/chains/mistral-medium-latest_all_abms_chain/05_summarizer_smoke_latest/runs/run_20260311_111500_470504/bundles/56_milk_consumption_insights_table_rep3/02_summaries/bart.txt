 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices . The model includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where influence is a function of interaction probability and agent susceptibility .  Trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a significant increasing trend across all windows, with consistent positive slopes ranging from approximately 64 to 76 . For decision-makers, these trends suggest a growing preference for whole milk among agents over time . Understanding these trends can help in designing interventions or strategies to promote or mitigate the consumption of whole milk based on public health or market objectives .  The simulation of the agent-based model shows distinct trends in the mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents over time . For decision makers, these trends imply a substantial shift away from whole milk consumption over time, influenced by the modeled factors such as perception, habits, and social influence .  The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents in the simulation starts at 0.0000 and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the time steps . This trend suggests that as the simulation progresses, the factors influencing agents' decisions, such as perception, habits, and social influence, lead to greater divergence in their choice behaviors .
