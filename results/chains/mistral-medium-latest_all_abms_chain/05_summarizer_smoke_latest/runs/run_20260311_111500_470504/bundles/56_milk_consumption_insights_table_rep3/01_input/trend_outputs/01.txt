The simulation results indicate a significant and consistent decline in the average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent over the observed period from 1974 to 2005. Initially, the average consumption starts at a high value of approximately 2654.81 units per week. This value experiences a sharp decline in the early years, dropping substantially within the first few time steps. Throughout the simulation, the trend continues to decrease, albeit with varying rates of decline and occasional minor fluctuations. Key time steps highlight notable values, such as around 773.96 units and 620.98 units, which represent local peaks in the declining trend. The consumption reaches its lowest point of approximately 253.10 units by the end of the simulation period. The data exhibits several inflection points, indicating changes in the rate of decline, and periods of oscillation where minor increases and decreases occur around the overall downward trend. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms significant decreasing trends in multiple windows, reinforcing the overall decline in whole milk consumption. For decision makers, these trends suggest a strong shift away from whole milk consumption over the studied period, likely influenced by changing perceptions, habits, and social norms regarding milk choices. This insight can inform strategies related to public health campaigns, marketing of milk products, and understanding consumer behavior shifts.
