The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices, specifically the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The model uses both theoretical grounding and empirical data, with calibration performed against observed macro-level data. The main outcome is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The model compares two variants: a threshold-based approach and a probability-based approach for how agents become disposed to consider their choices. Agents represent adult consumers who make milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics, modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. The model includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where influence is a function of interaction probability and agent susceptibility. Social norms are globally perceived and impact the weightings of the choice function. The model monitors the number of agents consuming mostly whole milk or skimmed/semi-skimmed milk and plots the percentage of majority consumers and the average consumption of each milk type over time. Key parameters include: memory-lifetime (range: 1.0 to 10.0, set to 5), alt-health-mean-initial (range: 1.0 to 3.0, set to 1.69), habit-threshold (range: 1.0 to 10.0, set to 2), p-interact (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0.3), social-susceptibility (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0.2), incumbent-initial-habit (range: 0.0 to 10.0, set to 10), alt-env-mean-initial (range: 0.0 to 3.0, set to 1.12), social-blindness (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0.51), justification (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0.61), cognitive-dissonance-threshold (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0.15), network-parameter (range: 2.0 to 10.0, set to 8), number-of-agents (set to 1000), habit-on? (set to True), network-type (set to 'watts-strogatz'), norms (set to True), and social-conformity (set to 0.28).

This sharp decline suggests a significant shift in consumer preferences, likely influenced by changing perceptions of health and environmental impacts associated with whole milk consumption. 

Following this rapid decline, the consumption rate stabilizes somewhat, fluctuating slightly but maintaining a general downward trend. Notable points include minor peaks at steps 19, 27, and 30, where consumption briefly increases to around 773.96, 620.98, and 335.18 units respectively. These peaks are followed by slight declines, indicating temporary reversions or slowdowns in the overall trend of reducing whole milk consumption. 

The trend continues to decrease steadily, with significant drops observed at various inflection points, particularly around steps 5, 13, 16, 19, 24, 25, 27, and 30. By the end of the simulation period, the average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent falls to approximately 253.10 units. This substantial reduction over the simulation period highlights a major shift in consumption patterns, likely driven by the cumulative effects of health awareness, social influence, and changing habits among the agents. 

The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a consistent decreasing trend across most windows, with significant decreases noted in several intervals. This statistical evidence supports the observation of a steady decline in whole milk consumption over time. The presence of multiple inflection points and minor oscillations suggests periods of adjustment and temporary stabilization, but the overall trajectory remains downward. 

For decision makers, these trends imply that interventions aimed at reducing whole milk consumption, such as health campaigns or environmental awareness programs, could be effective. The initial rapid decline suggests that once consumers are exposed to new information or influences, they may quickly adjust their consumption habits. However, the minor peaks and fluctuations indicate that sustained efforts may be necessary to maintain the downward trend and prevent temporary reversions to higher consumption levels.
