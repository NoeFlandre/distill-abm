The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a general increasing trend over the simulation period, starting from 0 and reaching a maximum value of approximately 240.33. The initial phase of the simulation, up to around 1,200 ticks, exhibits a steady increase in the death count, indicating a consistent rise in mortality rates among the prey population. This trend continues with some fluctuations, but the overall trajectory remains upward until it stabilizes around the maximum value.

Key time steps highlight significant changes in the trend. For instance, the first change point at 125 ticks marks the beginning of a noticeable increase in deaths. Subsequent change points at 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850 ticks further emphasize the accelerating mortality rate. The most substantial inflection points occur around 1,241 and 1,330 ticks, suggesting periods where the rate of increase in deaths undergoes significant shifts. The trend stabilizes around 2,310 ticks, indicating a potential equilibrium in the death count.

The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis reveals distinct phases in the trend. Initially, there is a strong increasing trend in the death count, which gradually weakens over time. Between 1,160 and 1,391 ticks, there is no significant trend, suggesting a period of stability. This is followed by another increasing trend and then a decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation period. The oscillation analysis indicates a dominant period of 63 ticks, suggesting cyclical patterns in the death count data.

For decision makers, these trends imply that the prey population experiences significant pressure from both natural causes and predation, leading to a steady increase in mortality. The stabilization of the death count towards the end of the simulation suggests a potential equilibrium state, which could be crucial for understanding the long-term dynamics of the ecosystem under the given parameters. The cyclical patterns and inflection points provide insights into the periodic nature of mortality events and the critical time steps where significant changes occur.
