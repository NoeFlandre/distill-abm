 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices, specifically the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk .  The total number of milk choices made by agents combined starts at 0 and increases steadily over the entire simulation period . The trend is consistently upward, with significant increases detected in multiple rolling windows . This consistent rise could be attributed to agents' evolving preferences, influenced by factors such as social norms, habits, and perceived characteristics of whole milk over the simulated time frame .  The trend shows an initial rapid adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, followed by a period of stability with minor fluctuations, and ending with a slight increase in consumption . This pattern reflects the complex interplay of individual decision-making processes influenced by health perceptions, social norms, and habit formation .  The mean deviation between the agents' held environmental values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.4756 . The deviation reaches its maximum value of 0.7251 at the final time step, indicating that by the end of the simulation, there is a substantial discrepancy between their health perceptions and their actual milk choices .
