 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices . The model includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where influence is a function of interaction probability and agent susceptibility .  The total number of whole milk choices by agents in the simulation starts at zero and increases steadily over time . The data shows a consistent upward trend without any detected peaks or valleys, indicating a steady increase in the adoption of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk . For decision-makers, these trends imply that, despite introduction of alternative milk choices and the influence of social and health factors, a significant portion of the population continues to choose whole milk consistently .  The mean quantity of whole milk consumed by agents shows a clear and consistent decreasing trend over the simulation period . For decision makers, these trends highlight a significant shift away from whole milk consumption over time, suggesting effective influences of the modeled factors such as health perception, social influence, and habit changes on consumer behavior .  The mean value health deviation in the simulation of milk consumption behaviors starts at 0.0000 and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the time steps . For decision makers, this trend implies that as agents become more accustomed to evaluating their choices based on health, environmental factors, and social influences, their decisions diverge more significantly from one another initially .
