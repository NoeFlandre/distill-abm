 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . It allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households .  The overall mean number of livestock across the simulation period is about 48.79, with a median of 47.31, indicating a relatively balanced distribution around these central values . The Gini coefficient consistently increases throughout the simulation, reflecting a growing disparity in the distribution of healthy livestock among households . After step 63, the number of households engaging in social learning behaviors among the households continues to increase gradually .  The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households starts at zero and increases rapidly in the early stages of the simulation . By the 25th time step, the destocking rate begins to slow down, indicating a change point where the initial surge in destocking starts to stabilize . After step 45, the number of non-learning households fluctuates slightly but generally remains stable around the mean value of 14.7 .
