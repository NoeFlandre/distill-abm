The average cumulative number of destocked livestock across all households exhibits a consistent increasing trend over the entire simulation period. Starting from an initial value of 0, the metric steadily rises, reaching a final value of approximately 96.78 by the end of the simulation. The trend is characterized by a series of inflection points and change points, indicating variations in the rate of increase. Notably, the rate of destocking is higher in the initial phases of the simulation and gradually stabilizes as the simulation progresses. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a significant increasing trend across all windows, with the slope of the trend decreasing over time. This suggests that while the cumulative number of destocked livestock continues to rise, the rate of increase slows down. The presence of multiple inflection points and oscillation indices indicates periodic fluctuations in the destocking rate, although these do not reverse the overall increasing trend. For decision makers, this trend implies a continuous and gradual reduction in livestock numbers over time, which could have significant implications for the sustainability and resilience of pastoral systems. Understanding these dynamics can help in formulating adaptive management strategies to mitigate the impacts of destocking on household livelihoods.
