The combined total energy available in the population of female prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12500 units. This high initial value suggests a robust and healthy population of female prey animals at the beginning of the simulation. However, the energy levels begin to decline rapidly and consistently throughout the simulation period. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant decreasing trends across various segments of the time series, with notable slopes of -22.616, -26.687, and -5.738 in the early phases, highlighting a sharp decline in energy levels. Despite some minor fluctuations and brief periods of stabilization or slight increase, the overall trajectory remains predominantly downward. The inflection points and change points identified in the data further emphasize the shifts in the rate of decline, but the primary trend remains a decrease in total energy. The dominant period of oscillation is relatively short, suggesting frequent but minor fluctuations around the overall decreasing trend. By the end of the simulation, the total energy available in the population of female prey animals drops to zero, indicating a complete depletion of energy resources within this group. For decision makers, this trend underscores the importance of considering the long-term sustainability of prey populations under current model parameters and the potential need for interventions to prevent energy depletion and potential extinction.
