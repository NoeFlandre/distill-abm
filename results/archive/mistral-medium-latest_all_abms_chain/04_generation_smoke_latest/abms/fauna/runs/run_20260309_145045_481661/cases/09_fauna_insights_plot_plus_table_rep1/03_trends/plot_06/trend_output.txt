The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits several distinct trends over the simulated time steps. Initially, the number of births remains at zero, indicating no births at the start of the simulation. As the simulation progresses, there is a gradual increase in the number of births, with notable inflection points around steps 613, 733, and 903, suggesting periods of accelerated growth in the birth rate. The number of births peaks significantly around step 1702, reaching a value of 5.6, indicating a high birth rate at this point. Following this peak, there is a noticeable decline in the birth rate, with valleys observed around steps 1422, 1281, and 1097. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates significant increasing trends in the birth rate from steps 696 to 1623, followed by significant decreasing trends from steps 1624 to 2087. This suggests a shift in the dynamics of the prey population, possibly due to changes in environmental conditions or predator-prey interactions. The oscillation summary reveals a dominant period of 50 steps, indicating periodic fluctuations in the birth rate. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of monitoring and managing environmental and hunting pressure factors to maintain a stable prey population. The initial growth phase suggests favorable conditions for reproduction, while the subsequent decline indicates potential stressors that need to be addressed to prevent population crashes.
