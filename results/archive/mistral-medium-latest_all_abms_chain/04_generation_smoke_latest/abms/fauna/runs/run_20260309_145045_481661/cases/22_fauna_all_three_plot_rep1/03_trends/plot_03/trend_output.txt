The cumulative number of male prey animals killed starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 80 males killed. This rapid initial increase suggests that male prey are highly susceptible to hunting pressure early in the simulation, possibly due to their higher visibility or lower initial wariness. By around 1000 time steps, the number of males killed stabilizes around 120, indicating a plateau where the rate of hunting balances with the remaining male population's ability to evade or sustain themselves. This stabilization suggests that the initial surge in hunting success is followed by a period where the remaining males become harder to hunt, potentially due to learned behaviors or reduced population density. The low variance among simulation runs indicates consistent hunting patterns and outcomes across different simulations, implying that the dynamics of male prey hunting are robust to initial conditions. For decision-makers, this trend highlights the vulnerability of male prey to initial hunting pressures and the importance of sustainable hunting practices to prevent rapid depletion of male populations.
