The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits several distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, there is a consistent and significant increasing trend in the number of males killed from the start up to approximately one-third of the simulation period. This is evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, which shows strong increasing trends with high Kendall's tau values and significant p-values in the early phases. Specifically, from ticks 0 to 927, the trend is strongly increasing, indicating a rapid accumulation of hunted males. This phase suggests that forager bands are highly active and successful in hunting male prey during this period.  \n\nFollowing this initial phase, the trend begins to stabilize. Between ticks 1160 and 1623, the data shows no significant trend, indicating a period of equilibrium where the number of males killed plateaus. This stabilization could imply that the forager bands have reached a balance in their hunting efficiency or that the male prey population has been significantly reduced, leading to a slower rate of accumulation. \n\nTowards the latter part of the simulation, from ticks 1856 to 2087, there is a significant decreasing trend in the number of males killed. This suggests a potential decline in the male prey population or a shift in the hunting behavior of the forager bands. The change points identified around ticks 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, 850, and 2310 further highlight the shifts in the dynamics of the hunting patterns. \n\nThe oscillation summary indicates a dominant period of 63 ticks, suggesting cyclical patterns in the hunting success of forager bands. These oscillations could be influenced by various factors such as seasonal changes, prey availability, or forager movement patterns. \n\nFor decision-makers, these trends provide insights into the dynamics of hunting pressure and its impact on male prey populations. The initial rapid increase in hunting success followed by a stabilization period and eventual decline suggests that hunting pressure may not be sustainable over long periods without affecting prey populations. This information can be crucial for developing strategies to manage hunting practices and ensure the long-term viability of both prey and forager populations.
