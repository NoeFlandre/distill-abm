In the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM), the cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, the number of deaths starts at zero. During the early stages, up to approximately 500 steps, there is a rapid increase in the number of deaths, rising steeply from zero to around 150 deaths. This phase represents a period of high mortality, likely due to initial interactions between prey and predators, as well as natural causes. Following this rapid increase, the rate of deaths begins to slow down significantly. Between 500 and 1000 steps, the cumulative number of deaths continues to rise but at a much slower pace, reaching around 200 deaths. This deceleration suggests a stabilization in the prey population dynamics, where the initial surge in mortality has subsided, and the system starts to approach an equilibrium. Beyond 1000 steps, the cumulative number of deaths plateaus, indicating that the rate of new deaths has significantly reduced. The number of deaths stabilizes around 225 to 250, showing minimal increases over time. This plateau phase suggests that the prey population has reached a relatively stable state, with deaths occurring at a much slower rate, potentially balancing out with births and other factors influencing population dynamics. Overall, the trend indicates an initial period of high mortality followed by a stabilization phase where the number of deaths levels off, reflecting the dynamic interactions within the model's socio-natural system.
