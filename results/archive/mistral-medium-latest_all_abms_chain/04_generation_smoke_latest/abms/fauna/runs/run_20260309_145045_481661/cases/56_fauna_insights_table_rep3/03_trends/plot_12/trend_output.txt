The combined total energy available in the population of female prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12500 units. This high initial value suggests a robust starting population with ample energy reserves. However, the energy levels begin to decline rapidly and consistently throughout the simulation. The mean energy level across the simulation period is approximately 1569 units, with a median of 100 units, indicating that the majority of the time, the energy levels are relatively low. The standard deviation of 3083 units and a variance of approximately 9,505,022 units highlight significant fluctuations in energy levels, despite the overall downward trend. The minimum energy level reaches zero at the final time step, indicating a complete depletion of energy reserves in the female prey population by the end of the simulation. The peaks and valleys identified in the data show minor fluctuations around specific time steps, but these do not significantly alter the overall decreasing trend. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results confirm this decreasing trend across various segments of the simulation, with significant negative slopes indicating a steady decline in energy levels. The change points identified at time steps 375, 500, 625, 875, and 2310 mark significant shifts in the rate of energy decline, but the overall trajectory remains downward. The oscillation summary indicates a dominant period of 63 time steps, suggesting some cyclical patterns within the broader decline. For decision makers, these trends imply that under the given parameters and conditions, the female prey population experiences a continuous and significant reduction in energy reserves, ultimately leading to complete depletion. This insight could be crucial for understanding the dynamics of prey populations under hunting pressure and environmental conditions, aiding in the formulation of conservation strategies or further hypothesis testing.
