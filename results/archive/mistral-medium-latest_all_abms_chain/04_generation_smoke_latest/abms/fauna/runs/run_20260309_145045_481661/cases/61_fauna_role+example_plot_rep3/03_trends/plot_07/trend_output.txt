The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at zero and remains low for the first 100 time steps. This initial period of low mortality could be attributed to the initial stability of the population, with ample resources and low predation pressure. Around the 100th time step, the number of deaths begins to increase rapidly, reaching approximately 150 deaths by the 500th time step. This sharp rise in mortality could be due to a combination of factors such as increased predation pressure from hunters, environmental stress, and the natural aging process of the animals. By the 1000th time step, the cumulative number of deaths stabilizes around 225 to 250 deaths. This stabilization suggests that the population has reached a new equilibrium, where the remaining animals are better adapted to the environmental conditions and predation pressure. The variance across simulation runs is low initially but increases slightly as the number of deaths approaches the equilibrium, indicating some variability in the dynamics of predator-prey interactions and environmental factors. This variability could be due to differences in the spatial distribution of resources, the movement patterns of animals and hunters, and the stochastic nature of mortality events.
