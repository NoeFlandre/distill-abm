The simulation of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decreasing trend in the total number of prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at approximately 166.67 animals and gradually declines to zero by the end of the simulation period. The decline is marked by several key phases and inflection points, indicating varying rates of decrease. Early in the simulation, from ticks 0 to 1159, the population experiences a steep and significant decline. This is followed by a brief period of stabilization and slight increase between ticks 1392 and 1623, before the decreasing trend resumes. Notable peaks and valleys occur around ticks 700 to 1030, where minor fluctuations are observed, but the overall trajectory remains downward. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms these trends, showing significant decreases in most intervals, with only brief periods of non-significant change or slight increase. The dominant period of oscillation is around 63 ticks, suggesting cyclical patterns within the broader decline. For decision makers, these trends imply that under the given parameters, the prey population is not sustainable and heads towards extinction. This insight can guide further exploration of parameter adjustments to understand better the conditions that might lead to different outcomes, such as equilibrium or sustained populations.
