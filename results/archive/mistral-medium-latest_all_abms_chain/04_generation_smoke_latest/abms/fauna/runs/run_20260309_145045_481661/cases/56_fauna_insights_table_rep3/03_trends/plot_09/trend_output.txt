The simulation of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decreasing trend in the number of female prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at 125 and exhibits a steady decline throughout the simulation, ultimately reaching zero by the end of the observed period. The mean number of female prey animals across the simulation is approximately 16.48, with a median value of 1, indicating that the population is generally low and skewed towards the lower end. The standard deviation of 31.55 and variance of 995.63 suggest significant fluctuations in the population at various points in the simulation. Several peaks and valleys are observed, with notable peaks at ticks 730, 710, and 803, where the population temporarily increases before continuing its overall decline. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant decreasing trends in most intervals, with only a few periods showing slight increases. The change points identified at ticks 475, 600, 875, and 2310 mark significant shifts in the trend, often corresponding to more rapid declines. The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of 63 ticks, indicating cyclical patterns within the overall downward trend. For decision makers, these trends suggest that under the given parameters, the female prey population is under consistent pressure, leading to eventual extinction. This insight can inform strategies related to conservation efforts, hunting regulations, and environmental management to potentially alter the outcome and promote sustainability within the model's ecosystem.
