The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at zero and exhibits a gradual increase over time. The initial phase shows a slow rise in births, reflecting the initial establishment and growth of the prey population. Around the mid-point of the simulation, there is a noticeable acceleration in the birth rate, reaching a peak value of 5.6. This period of increased births could be attributed to the prey population reaching a more stable and favorable state, possibly due to adequate food availability and lower predation pressure. Following this peak, there is a slight decline in the birth rate, indicating a potential stabilization of the population or the onset of limiting factors such as resource competition or increased predation. The simulation ends with the cumulative number of births stabilizing around 1, suggesting a balance between births and other demographic factors such as mortality and predation. The trends observed in the cumulative number of births provide insights into the dynamics of prey population growth and stability. For decision-makers, understanding these trends can help in assessing the impact of various parameters on prey population dynamics and in formulating strategies for conservation or management of megaherbivore populations.
