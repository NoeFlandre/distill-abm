The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands begins at zero and exhibits a consistent increasing trend over time. Initially, the rate of increase is rapid, with significant growth observed in the early time steps. This early phase of rapid increase can be attributed to the initial abundance of prey and the optimal foraging behavior of the hunters, who likely target the most accessible and valuable prey first. As time progresses, the rate of increase gradually slows down, indicating a potential reduction in the availability of postpartum female prey or a shift in the foraging strategy of the hunters. Around the mid-point of the simulation, the trend stabilizes, showing minimal fluctuations and a near-plateau effect. This stabilization suggests that the system may be approaching an equilibrium where the number of postpartum female prey hunted reaches a steady state. Towards the end of the simulation, there is a slight decrease in the cumulative number, which could be due to the depletion of the prey population or changes in the environmental conditions affecting prey availability. The overall trend highlights the dynamic interaction between the foragers and the prey population, reflecting the complex socio-natural system modeled by the MHPM.
