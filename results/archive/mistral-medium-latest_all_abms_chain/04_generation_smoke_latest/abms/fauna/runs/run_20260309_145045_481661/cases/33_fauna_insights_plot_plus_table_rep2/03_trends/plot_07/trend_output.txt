The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population, resulting from both natural causes and predation by foragers, exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the number of deaths, starting from zero and rising steeply. This phase of rapid increase continues until around 500 steps, where the number of deaths reaches approximately 150. Following this initial surge, the rate of increase in deaths begins to slow down, and the curve starts to plateau. By around 1000 steps, the number of deaths stabilizes, fluctuating around 230 to 240 deaths. This stabilization indicates that the rate at which new deaths occur has significantly reduced, suggesting a potential equilibrium in the population dynamics. The statistical analysis reveals several key points, including peaks and valleys in the death count, with notable peaks around 1876 steps and valleys around 1958 steps. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in the early phases, followed by periods of no trend and a slight decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation. The change points identified at various steps further emphasize the shifts in the dynamics of the death count over time. For decision makers, these trends suggest that initial conditions and early interventions could have substantial impacts on the prey population. The early rapid increase in deaths highlights a critical period where the population is most vulnerable, while the later stabilization indicates a potential balance between births and deaths, which could inform long-term management strategies.
