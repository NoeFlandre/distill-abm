The total number of males killed starts at zero and increases rapidly over the first 500 time steps. This initial increase could be attributed to the initial abundance of prey and the high energy needs of the foragers, leading to a high hunting rate. Around 500 steps, the rate of increase starts to slow down, likely due to a reduction in the male prey population as hunting continues. The number of males killed reaches a plateau at around 120 males killed at approximately 1000 steps. This plateau suggests that the hunting pressure and the prey population have reached a temporary equilibrium, where the number of males being killed stabilizes. After this point, the number of males killed remains relatively constant with minor fluctuations, indicating a balance between the hunting efforts of the foragers and the remaining male prey population. There is some variance across simulation runs, particularly noticeable after the initial rapid increase. This variability could be due to differences in the spatial distribution of prey, the movement patterns of both hunters and prey, and the stochastic nature of encounters and hunting success.
