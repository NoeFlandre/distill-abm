The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits several distinct trends over the simulated time period. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the death count, with significant upward trends observed from the start up to approximately one-third of the total time steps. This period is characterized by a steep rise in deaths, indicating a phase of high mortality among the prey population. The death count continues to rise, albeit at a slower rate, through the middle portion of the simulation, suggesting a gradual stabilization in the rate of prey mortality. Around the midpoint of the simulation, the trend in the death count plateaus, indicating a period where the number of deaths stabilizes and fluctuates around a mean value. This stabilization suggests a potential equilibrium between prey births and deaths, as well as the impact of predation by foragers. Towards the latter part of the simulation, there is a slight decrease in the death count, indicating a potential reduction in mortality rates. This could imply that the prey population has adapted to the hunting pressure or that the foragers have reached a saturation point in their hunting efficiency. The dominant period of oscillation towards the end of the simulation suggests regular fluctuations in the death count, possibly due to cyclical environmental factors or periodic changes in hunting behavior. For decision makers, these trends highlight the dynamic interplay between hunting pressure and prey population dynamics. The initial rapid increase in deaths underscores the immediate impact of hunting on prey populations, while the subsequent stabilization and slight decrease suggest potential long-term equilibrium points. Understanding these trends can inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts to maintain ecological balance.
