The total energy of female animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a consistent and rapid decline over time. Initially, the total energy of female animals starts at a high value of approximately 12,000 units. This high initial energy level indicates a healthy and robust population of female prey animals at the beginning of the simulation. 

Within the first 500 steps, there is a steep decline in the total energy, dropping to around 2,000 units. This sharp decrease suggests a significant stressor on the female population, likely due to a combination of hunting pressure, environmental factors, and resource limitations. The rapid reduction in energy levels implies that female animals are either being hunted extensively or are unable to replenish their energy reserves due to limited food availability or high energy expenditure. 

Beyond 500 steps, the decline in total energy continues but at a slower rate, eventually tapering off and approaching zero around 2,000 steps. This trend indicates that the female population is gradually being depleted, with fewer individuals surviving over time. The tapering off suggests that the remaining female animals are either being hunted to extinction or are unable to sustain their energy levels due to ongoing environmental pressures. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the critical impact of hunting pressure and environmental conditions on the survival of female prey animals. The rapid initial decline in energy levels underscores the vulnerability of the female population to intensive hunting and resource scarcity. The gradual depletion of energy reserves over time suggests that without intervention, the female population is at risk of extinction. These insights can inform conservation strategies and policy decisions aimed at protecting vulnerable species and managing hunting practices to ensure sustainable populations.
