The number of female prey animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) simulation exhibits a consistent and significant decreasing trend throughout the entire duration of the simulation. Initially, the population starts at 125 female prey animals and steadily declines to zero by the end of the simulation. The mean number of female prey animals over the entire period is approximately 16.48, with a median of 1, indicating a skewed distribution with a few higher values early in the simulation. The standard deviation of 31.55 and variance of 995.63 reflect substantial variability in the population numbers, particularly in the early stages. The data series shows several peaks and valleys, with notable peaks occurring around ticks 730, 710, 803, and 820, where the population temporarily increases before continuing its overall decline. The most prominent peaks and valleys are relatively minor compared to the initial population size, indicating a general downward trajectory with small fluctuations. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms the decreasing trend across various segments of the simulation, with significant decreases noted in all segments except for a brief increasing trend between ticks 1160 and 1391, and another between ticks 1856 and 2087. These brief increases, however, do not alter the overall declining pattern. The change points identified at ticks 475, 600, 875, and 2310 mark significant shifts in the rate of decline. The oscillation summary indicates a dominant period of 63 ticks, suggesting periodic fluctuations within the overall decline. For decision-makers, these trends imply that under the given parameters, the female prey population is under consistent pressure, leading to eventual extinction. This insight could be crucial for understanding the dynamics of megaherbivore populations under similar conditions and for designing conservation strategies or further research into the factors driving these trends.
