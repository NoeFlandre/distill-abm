The number of female animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a consistent declining trend across all simulations. Initially, the number of female animals starts at approximately 120. This number remains relatively stable for a brief period before beginning a steady decline. By around 500 steps, the number of female animals drops to about 60. The decline continues more gradually, reaching approximately 20 female animals by around 1000 steps. Beyond this point, the number of female animals continues to decrease at a slower rate, approaching near-zero values by around 2000 steps. This trend indicates a significant reduction in the female animal population over time, which could have implications for the overall herd dynamics and sustainability. For decision-makers, this trend suggests that under the current model parameters, the population of female animals is not sustainable in the long term, which could lead to potential extinction. This insight is crucial for understanding the impact of various factors such as hunting pressure, environmental conditions, and demographic parameters on the survival of the species. Adjustments to parameters such as internal mortality, external mortality, and reproduction rates might be necessary to achieve a more stable population dynamic.
