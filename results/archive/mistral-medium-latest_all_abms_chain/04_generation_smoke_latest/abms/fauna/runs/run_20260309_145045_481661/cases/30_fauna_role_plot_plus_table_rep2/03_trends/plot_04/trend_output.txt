The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear trend over time. Initially, the number of females killed starts at zero and remains constant for a brief period. Following this initial phase, there is a rapid increase in the number of females killed, which begins around the 125th tick and continues steeply until approximately the 625th tick. During this period, the rate of increase is quite pronounced, indicating a significant rise in hunting activity or success. 

After this rapid increase, the rate of accumulation begins to slow down, transitioning into a more gradual increase. This phase lasts until around the 1600th tick, where the curve starts to plateau, suggesting that the rate at which females are being killed is stabilizing. The number of females killed reaches a peak value of approximately 123.6667 around the 1826th tick. Following this peak, the trend shows minor fluctuations but generally maintains a steady state with slight oscillations. The oscillations have a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, indicating a regular pattern of minor increases and decreases in the number of females killed. 

The statistical analysis reveals several key inflection points and change points, notably at ticks 125, 375, 475, 625, 850, 1600, and 2310, which mark significant shifts in the trend. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates that the initial phases are characterized by strong increasing trends, which gradually weaken as the curve approaches the plateau. The final phase shows a slight decreasing trend, although it is not as pronounced as the initial increases. Overall, the model demonstrates a rapid initial increase in the number of females killed, followed by a stabilization phase with minor oscillations around a steady state.
