The simulation of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decline in the number of female prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at 125 and exhibits a steady decrease throughout the simulation, ultimately reaching zero by the final tick. The trend is predominantly decreasing, as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall statistics, which indicate significant decreasing trends across most of the time steps. The mean number of female prey animals is approximately 16.48, with a median of 1, highlighting the skewed distribution towards lower values as the simulation progresses. The standard deviation of 31.55 and variance of 995.63 further emphasize the variability in the population size, particularly in the earlier stages of the simulation. Several peaks and valleys are observed, notably around ticks 730, 710, and 803, but these fluctuations are relatively minor compared to the overall downward trend. The dominant period of oscillation is 63 ticks, suggesting some cyclical patterns in the population dynamics, potentially linked to seasonal variations or other periodic factors within the model. Change points identified at ticks 475, 600, 875, and 2310 indicate significant shifts in the trend, with the final change point marking the near-extinction of the female prey population. For decision makers, these trends suggest that under the given parameters, the female prey population is under significant pressure, leading to a steady decline and eventual extinction. This insight could inform conservation strategies and highlight the need for interventions to mitigate factors contributing to the decline.
