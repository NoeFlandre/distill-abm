The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population, from both natural causes and predation by foragers, exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, the number of deaths increases rapidly, with the count rising from 0 to approximately 200 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase is followed by a more gradual rise, reaching around 235 deaths by step 1000. The trend then stabilizes, with the number of deaths fluctuating slightly around this value for the remainder of the simulation, up to step 73000. Key inflection points occur around steps 362, 1241, 1330, 1397, 1402, 1419, 1455, 1474, 1595, 1713, 1828, and 2040, indicating periods where the rate of change in the number of deaths shifts. Statistical analysis reveals significant increasing trends in the early phases of the simulation, followed by periods of no significant trend and a slight decreasing trend towards the end. The dominant period of oscillation is 63 steps, suggesting a cyclical pattern in the number of deaths. For decision makers, these trends highlight the initial rapid impact on the prey population followed by a stabilization phase, which could inform strategies related to hunting pressure and environmental management.
