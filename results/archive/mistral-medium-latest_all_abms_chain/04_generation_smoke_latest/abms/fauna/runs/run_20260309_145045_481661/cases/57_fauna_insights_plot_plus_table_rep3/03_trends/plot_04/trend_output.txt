The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a rapid initial increase followed by a gradual stabilization. Initially, the number of females killed rises sharply from 0 to approximately 115 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase continues at a slightly slower rate until around 1000 steps, where the cumulative count reaches about 120. Beyond this point, the rate of increase slows significantly, and the cumulative number of females killed stabilizes around 123. The data shows minor fluctuations around this value, indicating a plateau in the hunting activity. Key inflection points are observed around steps 245, 262, and 285, where the rate of increase starts to decline. The trend analysis indicates a consistent increase in the number of females killed up to around step 1855, after which a slight decrease is noted, although the overall trend remains relatively stable. The dominant period of oscillation is around 63 steps, suggesting periodic minor variations in the hunting activity. For decision makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressure is intense but eventually stabilizes, indicating a potential equilibrium in the predator-prey dynamics within the model's parameters. This stabilization could inform strategies related to sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts.
