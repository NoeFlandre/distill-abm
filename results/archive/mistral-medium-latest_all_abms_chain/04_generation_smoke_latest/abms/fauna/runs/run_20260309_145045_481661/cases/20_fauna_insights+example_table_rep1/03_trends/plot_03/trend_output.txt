The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the initial time steps. This early rise indicates a high initial hunting success rate, likely due to the abundance of prey and the optimal foraging conditions at the start of the simulation. The trend continues to increase steadily, reaching a peak value of approximately 120 around the mid-point of the simulation. This peak suggests a period of maximum hunting efficiency or a high density of male prey animals available for hunting. Following this peak, the cumulative number of males hunted stabilizes, showing minimal fluctuations and maintaining a relatively constant value. This stabilization implies that the system may have reached an equilibrium where the hunting pressure is balanced by the remaining male prey population and their reproductive rates. The slight oscillations observed towards the end of the simulation suggest minor periodic variations in hunting success or prey availability, potentially due to seasonal effects or dynamic changes in the environment. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial conditions and the potential for the system to reach a stable equilibrium under the given parameters. The early rapid increase in hunting success could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices, while the stabilization phase suggests the potential for long-term coexistence between hunters and prey under certain conditions.
