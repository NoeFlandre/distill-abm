The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and increases rapidly over the first 500 time steps. This initial rise can be attributed to the high initial density of prey and the optimal foraging behavior of hunters, who target the most accessible and valuable prey first. The number of males killed reaches around 100 by approximately 500 time steps, indicating a significant initial hunting pressure. The rate of increase slows down between 500 and 1500 time steps, suggesting a reduction in the availability of male prey due to the depletion of the most accessible individuals. The cumulative number of males killed stabilizes around 120 after 1500 time steps, indicating that the system has reached a dynamic equilibrium where the remaining males are either harder to hunt or the hunting pressure has balanced with the prey's reproductive and survival rates. The variability among simulation runs increases slightly after the initial rapid rise, reflecting the stochastic nature of hunting success and prey availability. The dominant period of oscillation around 63 time steps suggests cyclical patterns in hunting success, possibly due to seasonal variations or periodic changes in prey behavior. Decision makers can infer that initial hunting pressure significantly impacts male prey populations, but over time, the system reaches a new equilibrium. This insight can guide strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts.
