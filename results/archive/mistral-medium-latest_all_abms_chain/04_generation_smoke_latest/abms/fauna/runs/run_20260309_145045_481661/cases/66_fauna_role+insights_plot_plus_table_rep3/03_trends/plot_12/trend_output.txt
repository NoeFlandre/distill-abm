The total energy of female animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a pronounced decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12,500 units. This high initial energy level rapidly declines within the first few hundred time steps. By around 500 time steps, the total energy has dropped significantly to approximately 2,000 units. This steep decline continues, albeit at a slower rate, until around 1,000 time steps, where the total energy stabilizes at lower values, fluctuating around a few hundred units. Beyond 1,000 time steps, the energy levels continue to decrease gradually, eventually approaching zero by the end of the simulation at 2,312 time steps. The statistical analysis indicates a consistent and significant decreasing trend throughout the simulation, with occasional minor fluctuations and periods of slight increase, but the overall trajectory remains downward. The dominant period of oscillation is around 63 time steps, suggesting some periodic behavior in the energy levels, though the overall trend is a steady decline. For decision makers, this trend implies that under the given parameters, the energy reserves of female prey animals are consistently depleted over time, which could lead to potential extinction or severe population decline. This insight is crucial for understanding the impact of hunting pressure and environmental factors on the sustainability of megaherbivore populations.
