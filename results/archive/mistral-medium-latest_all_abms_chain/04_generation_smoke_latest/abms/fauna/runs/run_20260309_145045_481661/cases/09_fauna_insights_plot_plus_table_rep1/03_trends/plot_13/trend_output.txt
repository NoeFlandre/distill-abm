The total energy of all animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of approximately 16,666.67 units. This high initial value indicates a large amount of energy collectively held by the prey animals at the beginning of the simulation. As the simulation progresses, there is a rapid decline in the total energy. By around 500 steps, the total energy has dropped significantly to approximately 4,100 units. This sharp decline continues, and by around 1,000 steps, the total energy further reduces to about 800 units. Beyond this point, the decline becomes more gradual, eventually approaching zero as the simulation reaches its later stages around 2,312 steps. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend with significant negative slopes in the rolling Mann-Kendall test across various segments of the simulation. The change points identified at 350, 500, 625, 875, and 2310 steps highlight key moments where the rate of decline shifts, indicating different phases in the energy depletion process. The oscillation summary suggests some periodic fluctuations, but these are minor compared to the overall downward trend. For decision makers, this trend implies that under the given parameters and conditions of the MHPM, the energy reserves of the prey animals are being depleted over time, potentially leading to a scenario where the prey population's energy levels are critically low. This could inform strategies related to conservation efforts, hunting regulations, or environmental management to mitigate the rapid depletion of energy reserves in prey populations.
