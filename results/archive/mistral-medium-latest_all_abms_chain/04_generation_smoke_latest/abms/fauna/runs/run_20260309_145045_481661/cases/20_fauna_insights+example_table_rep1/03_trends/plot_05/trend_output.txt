The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at zero and exhibits a consistent increasing trend over the simulation period. The initial phase shows a rapid increase, reaching significant values around the 2000 time steps mark. This rapid rise can be attributed to the initial abundance of prey and the optimal foraging behavior of hunters, who target the most accessible and valuable prey first. The trend continues to rise steadily, albeit at a slower rate, indicating a gradual depletion of the prey population over time. The cumulative count reaches its peak values around the 1826 time steps, with the highest recorded value being 119. The latter part of the simulation shows minor fluctuations around the peak values, suggesting a stabilization in the hunting success rate as the system approaches an equilibrium. The minor oscillations towards the end of the simulation indicate periodic variations in hunting success, possibly due to seasonal changes or dynamic adjustments in prey and hunter behaviors. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the long-term impacts of hunting pressure on prey populations and the potential for stabilization in socio-natural systems. The initial rapid increase in hunted prey underscores the vulnerability of postpartum females, which could be critical for conservation strategies.
