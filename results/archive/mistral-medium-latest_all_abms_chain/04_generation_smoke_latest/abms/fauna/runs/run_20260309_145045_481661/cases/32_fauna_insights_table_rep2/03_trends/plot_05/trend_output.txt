The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a general increasing trend over the simulation period. Initially, the count starts at zero and steadily rises, reaching a maximum value of 119 at around the midpoint of the simulation. The trend analysis indicates several phases of significant increase, particularly in the early stages up to approximately one-third of the simulation duration. During these phases, the rate of increase is relatively high, as evidenced by the slopes of 0.213 and 0.226 in the initial segments. As the simulation progresses, the rate of increase slows down, with slopes decreasing to 0.015 and 0.003 in the middle phases. Towards the latter part of the simulation, the trend stabilizes, showing no significant trend between indices 1392 and 1623, followed by a slight increase and a subsequent decrease. The dominant period of oscillation is 63 ticks, indicating periodic fluctuations in the latter part of the simulation. The implications for decision-makers suggest that hunting pressure on postpartum female prey animals increases over time, with significant initial growth that stabilizes and fluctuates periodically in the long run. This trend could inform strategies related to hunting regulations and conservation efforts to manage prey populations sustainably.
