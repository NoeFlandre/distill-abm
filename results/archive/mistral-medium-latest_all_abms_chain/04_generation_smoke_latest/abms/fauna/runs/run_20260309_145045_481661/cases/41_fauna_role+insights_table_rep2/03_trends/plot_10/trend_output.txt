The simulation of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear overall decreasing trend in the total number of prey animals over time. Starting from an initial value of approximately 166.67 animals, the population declines steadily to zero by the end of the simulation. The mean number of animals across the simulation period is approximately 25.01, with a median of 1.48, indicating a skewed distribution with many time steps having very low animal counts. The standard deviation of 44.90 and variance of 2015.71 further highlight the variability and dispersion in animal numbers throughout the simulation. The minimum value of zero animals is reached at the final time step, while the maximum value of approximately 166.67 animals is observed at the beginning. Several peaks and valleys are identified at various time steps, but these fluctuations are relatively minor compared to the overall downward trend. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results indicate significant decreasing trends in animal numbers across most of the simulation periods, with only a few intervals showing slight increases. Change points are detected at time steps 125, 475, 625, 875, and 2310, suggesting shifts in the rate of decline. The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of 63 time steps, indicating cyclical patterns within the data. For decision makers, these trends suggest that under the given parameters, the prey population is under significant pressure, leading to a steady decline and eventual extinction. This insight could inform conservation strategies and highlight the need for interventions to mitigate factors contributing to the decline of megaherbivore populations.
