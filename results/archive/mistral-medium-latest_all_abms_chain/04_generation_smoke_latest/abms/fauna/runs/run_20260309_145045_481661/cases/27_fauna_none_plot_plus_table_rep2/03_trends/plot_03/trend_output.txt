The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the number of males killed, starting from zero and rising steeply. This phase of rapid increase continues until around 500 steps, where the number of males killed reaches approximately 60. Following this, the rate of increase begins to slow down, and by around 1000 steps, the number of males killed stabilizes around 116. This stabilization indicates a plateau where the number of males killed remains relatively constant, fluctuating slightly around this value. The data shows minor oscillations around this plateau, with small peaks and valleys, but the overall trend remains stable. The dominant period of these oscillations is approximately 63 steps. The initial rapid increase can be attributed to the high initial prey population and the optimal foraging behavior of the hunters, while the subsequent stabilization suggests an equilibrium state where the hunting pressure balances the prey population dynamics. The model parameters, such as the initial number of foragers, processing costs, and food values for males and females, play a crucial role in shaping these trends.
