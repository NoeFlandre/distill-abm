The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at zero and remains relatively low for the first 500 time steps. This initial period of low mortality could be attributed to the initial stability in the population, where both prey and foragers are adapting to the environment and resources are relatively abundant. Around the 500 time steps mark, there is a rapid increase in the number of deaths, which continues steeply until approximately 1000 time steps. This sharp rise in mortality could be due to a combination of factors such as increased predation pressure from foragers, depletion of grass resources leading to starvation, and natural mortality catching up as the population ages. After this rapid increase, the rate of deaths starts to plateau, indicating a stabilization in the population dynamics. Between 1000 and 2000 time steps, the cumulative number of deaths continues to rise but at a much slower rate, eventually leveling off. This plateau suggests that the system may have reached a new equilibrium where the birth rate balances the death rate, and the remaining prey population is more stable. The variability among different simulation runs increases notably after the initial 500 time steps, indicating that while initial conditions are consistent, the interactions and feedback loops within the model introduce complexity and variability over time.
