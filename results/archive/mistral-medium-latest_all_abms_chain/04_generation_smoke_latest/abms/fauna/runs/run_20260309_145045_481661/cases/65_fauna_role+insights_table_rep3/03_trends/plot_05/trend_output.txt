The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the number of postpartum females hunted, starting from 0 and rising sharply. This trend continues with a high growth rate until around the 475th tick, where the rate of increase begins to slow down. By the 625th tick, the growth rate further diminishes, indicating a deceleration in the hunting rate of postpartum females. Around the 850th tick, the trend stabilizes, and the cumulative number of hunted postpartum females approaches a plateau. This plateau is maintained with minor fluctuations until approximately the 2310th tick, after which the trend remains relatively stable with no significant changes. The final value of the cumulative number of postpartum females hunted reaches around 117 by the end of the simulation. The data suggests that the hunting pressure on postpartum females increases rapidly initially but stabilizes over time, possibly due to a balance being reached between the hunting efforts and the availability of postpartum females. For decision makers, this trend implies that initial hunting pressures can significantly impact postpartum female populations, but over time, the system may reach a dynamic equilibrium where the rate of hunting stabilizes.
