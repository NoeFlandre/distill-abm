The percentage of green patches in the landscape starts at approximately 33% and remains relatively stable throughout the simulation. There is a notable dip around the 365th time step, where the percentage of green patches drops to about 24.91%. This dip could be attributed to seasonal effects or a temporary imbalance in the grazing and regrowth dynamics of the grass. Following this dip, the percentage of green patches gradually recovers and stabilizes back to around 33%. The system exhibits minor fluctuations but generally maintains a steady state, indicating a balance between grass consumption by prey animals and grass regrowth. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates periods of increasing and decreasing trends, but these changes are minimal and the system largely remains in equilibrium. The dominant oscillation period of 63 time steps suggests a cyclical pattern in the grass regrowth and consumption dynamics, possibly linked to seasonal variations or periodic grazing patterns. For decision makers, this stability in the percentage of green patches suggests that the current parameters of the model maintain a sustainable balance between the prey animals and their food resources, which is crucial for the long-term survival of the megaherbivores in the simulated environment.
