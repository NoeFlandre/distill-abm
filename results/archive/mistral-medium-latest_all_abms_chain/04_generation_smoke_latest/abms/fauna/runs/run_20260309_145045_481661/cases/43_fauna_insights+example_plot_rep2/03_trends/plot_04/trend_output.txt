The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 100. This rapid initial increase suggests that female prey are highly accessible and vulnerable to hunting pressure early in the simulation. The rate of increase slows down significantly after this initial period, indicating a potential reduction in the availability of female prey or increased difficulty in hunting them. By around 1000 time steps, the cumulative number of females killed stabilizes around 120, with minor fluctuations observed thereafter. This stabilization suggests that the system may have reached a dynamic equilibrium where the remaining female prey are either less accessible or the hunting pressure has balanced with the prey's reproductive and survival strategies. For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial hunting efforts are highly effective but that sustained hunting pressure leads to diminishing returns. This insight could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts to maintain prey populations.
