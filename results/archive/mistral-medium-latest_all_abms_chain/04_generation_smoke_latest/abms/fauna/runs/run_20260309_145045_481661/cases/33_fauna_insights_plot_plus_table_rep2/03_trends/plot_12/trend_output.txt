The total energy of female animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12500 units. This high initial value indicates a large population of female animals with substantial energy reserves at the beginning of the simulation. The energy levels then rapidly decline, showing a steep drop within the first few hundred time steps. This rapid decline suggests a high rate of energy consumption or loss among the female animals, potentially due to factors such as hunting pressure, environmental changes, or high metabolic costs. As the simulation progresses, the rate of decline in total energy slows down, but the overall trend remains downward. By the end of the simulation, the total energy of female animals approaches zero, indicating a significant reduction in the population or energy reserves of female animals. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend, with significant negative slopes and tau values across various time windows. The presence of multiple change points and inflection indices further supports the notion of a dynamic but consistently declining energy landscape. The oscillation summary indicates periodic fluctuations, but these are minor compared to the overall downward trend. For decision makers, this trend implies that under the given parameters and conditions, the female animal population faces substantial pressure, leading to a continuous decline in their total energy. This insight could be crucial for understanding the factors contributing to the extinction or survival of megafaunal species and for designing interventions to mitigate these pressures.
