The total energy of male animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a consistent declining trend over time. Initially, the total energy of male animals starts at a high value of approximately 12,000 units. This high initial energy level suggests that male animals begin with substantial energy reserves, which could be crucial for their survival and reproductive success early in the simulation. 

As the simulation progresses, there is a sharp decline in the total energy of male animals within the first 500 steps, dropping to around 2,000 units. This rapid decrease indicates a significant consumption of energy, likely due to factors such as hunting pressure, movement costs, and environmental conditions. The steep decline suggests that male animals are expending energy at a high rate, which could be critical for decision-makers to consider when evaluating the sustainability of male animal populations under current model parameters. 

Following this sharp decline, the total energy of male animals continues to decrease but at a slower rate, gradually approaching zero by around 2,000 steps. This gradual decline implies that while the initial energy reserves are being depleted quickly, the remaining energy is being consumed more slowly, potentially due to a reduction in the number of male animals or adaptive behaviors that conserve energy. 

By the end of the simulation, the total energy of male animals stabilizes near zero, indicating that the population of male animals has either been significantly reduced or has adapted to a low-energy state. This stabilization suggests that the system may have reached a new equilibrium, albeit with much lower energy reserves for male animals. For decision-makers, this trend highlights the importance of understanding the long-term impacts of hunting pressure and environmental conditions on the energy dynamics of male animal populations.
