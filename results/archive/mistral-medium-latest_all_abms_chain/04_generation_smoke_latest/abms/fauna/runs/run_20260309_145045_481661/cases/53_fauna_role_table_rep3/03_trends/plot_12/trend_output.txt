The combined total energy available in the population of female prey animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear overall decreasing trend throughout the simulation. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12,500 units. This high initial value rapidly declines in the early stages of the simulation, with significant decreases observed consistently across multiple time steps. 

In the early phase, from tick 0 to approximately tick 927, the energy levels drop sharply. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a strong decreasing trend with high significance in these initial periods. For instance, between ticks 0 and 231, the energy decreases with a slope of -22.616 units per tick, and this rate of decline continues, albeit at varying slopes, through to tick 927. 

Following this rapid decline, there is a brief period of stabilization and minor fluctuations. Between ticks 1160 and 1391, there is a slight but significant increasing trend in energy levels, as indicated by a positive slope of 0.019 units per tick. However, this increase is short-lived and not substantial. The trend reverses again to a decreasing pattern from tick 1392 onwards, though the rate of decline is much slower compared to the initial phase. 

Throughout the simulation, several change points are identified where the trend in energy levels shifts. Notable change points occur around ticks 375, 500, 625, and 875, marking transitions in the rate of energy decline or brief periods of stabilization. Despite these fluctuations, the overarching trend remains a decrease in total energy. 

Oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks are observed, indicating periodic fluctuations in the energy levels. However, these oscillations do not alter the overall decreasing trend. The energy levels eventually approach zero by the end of the simulation at tick 2311, indicating a complete depletion of energy in the female prey population. 

In summary, the simulation shows a consistent and significant decline in the total energy of female prey animals, with initial rapid decreases followed by periods of slower decline and minor fluctuations, ultimately leading to the depletion of energy in the population.
