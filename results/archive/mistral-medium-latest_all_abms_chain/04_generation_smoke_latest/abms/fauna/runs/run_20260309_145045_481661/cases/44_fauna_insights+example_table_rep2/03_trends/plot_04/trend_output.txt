The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at zero and exhibits a consistent increasing trend over time. The initial phase shows a rapid increase, reaching approximately 119 by around 1,826 time steps. This rapid rise can be attributed to the initial abundance of prey and the optimal foraging behavior of hunters, who target the most accessible and valuable prey first. The trend continues to rise gradually, reaching a peak value of approximately 123.67 at around 1,851 time steps. This peak suggests a period where hunting pressure is at its highest, possibly due to the high density of prey and efficient foraging strategies. After reaching the peak, the cumulative number of female prey hunted stabilizes, indicating a potential equilibrium where the rate of hunting matches the rate of prey reproduction and other dynamic factors in the model. The model shows significant increasing trends in various time windows, as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall test, which indicates consistent growth in the number of prey hunted. The change points identified at various time steps suggest shifts in the dynamics of hunting pressure, possibly due to changes in prey density, environmental conditions, or hunter behavior. The oscillation summary indicates a dominant period of 63 time steps, suggesting cyclic patterns in hunting success, which could be influenced by seasonal variations or other periodic factors in the model. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the dynamics of hunting pressure and prey population interactions. The initial rapid increase in hunting success underscores the vulnerability of prey populations to intense hunting pressure, while the subsequent stabilization suggests the potential for sustainable hunting practices under certain conditions. The cyclic patterns and change points provide insights into the periodic nature of hunting success and the need for adaptive management strategies.
