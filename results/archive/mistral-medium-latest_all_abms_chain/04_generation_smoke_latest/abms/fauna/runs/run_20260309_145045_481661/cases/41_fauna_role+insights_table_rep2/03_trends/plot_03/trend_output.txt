The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear trend over the simulation period. Initially, the number of males killed starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase. This rapid increase is evident in the early stages, with significant growth observed up to around 1,159 ticks, where the trend starts to stabilize. The mean number of males killed across the simulation is approximately 100, with a median of 116.5, indicating that the central tendency of the data is skewed slightly towards higher values. The maximum number of males killed reaches a peak of approximately 120.17 at around 1,876 ticks. After this peak, the trend shows a slight decrease, indicating a potential stabilization or slight reduction in the hunting pressure on male prey. The data exhibits several peaks and valleys, suggesting periods of increased and decreased hunting activity. Notably, the rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, followed by periods of no significant trend and a final phase of decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation. The presence of strong oscillations with a dominant period of 63 ticks suggests cyclical patterns in the hunting behavior or prey availability. For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressure is high but tends to stabilize over time, with potential cyclical variations that could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices or conservation efforts.
