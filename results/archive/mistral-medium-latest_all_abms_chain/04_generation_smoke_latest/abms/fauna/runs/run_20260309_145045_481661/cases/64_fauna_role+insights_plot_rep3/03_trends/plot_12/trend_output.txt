The total energy of female animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a consistent decline over time across all simulations. Initially, the total energy of female animals starts at approximately 12,000 units. This high initial energy level indicates a healthy and robust population of female prey at the beginning of the simulation. 

Within the first 500 steps, there is a steep decline in the total energy, dropping to around 2,000 units. This rapid decrease suggests a significant stressor impacting the female prey population early in the simulation, potentially due to high hunting pressure or environmental factors. Between 500 and 1,000 steps, the decline continues but at a slower rate, with the total energy reducing to near 0 units. This gradual decline indicates ongoing pressures that continue to deplete the energy reserves of the female prey population. 

After 1,000 steps, the total energy of female animals stabilizes at near 0 units, indicating that the population has been nearly or completely depleted. This stabilization at a low energy level suggests that the population has reached a critical point where recovery is unlikely without significant changes to the model parameters or external interventions. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight the vulnerability of the female prey population under the given conditions. The rapid initial decline and subsequent stabilization at near-zero energy levels suggest that current hunting pressures and environmental conditions are unsustainable for the female prey population. This information can guide policy decisions aimed at conservation efforts, such as adjusting hunting quotas, enhancing habitat protection, or introducing measures to support the recovery of the prey population.
