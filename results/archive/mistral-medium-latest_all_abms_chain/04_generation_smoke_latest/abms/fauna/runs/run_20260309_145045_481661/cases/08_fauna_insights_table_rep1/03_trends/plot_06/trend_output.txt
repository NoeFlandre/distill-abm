The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits several distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the number of births starts at zero and gradually increases. The most significant increases occur in distinct phases: from time step 696 to 927, from 928 to 1159, from 1160 to 1391, and from 1392 to 1623, with the rate of increase becoming more pronounced over time. The peak number of births is observed at time step 1702, reaching a value of 5.6. Following this peak, there is a noticeable decreasing trend in the number of births from time step 1624 to 1855 and from 1856 to 2087. The model also identifies several inflection points and change points, indicating shifts in the trend dynamics. Additionally, there is evidence of strong oscillations with a dominant period of 50 time steps, suggesting periodic fluctuations in the birth rate. For decision makers, these trends imply that the prey population experiences significant growth phases followed by declines, which could be critical for understanding the dynamics of prey population sustainability and extinction risks under varying conditions of hunting pressure and environmental factors.
