The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands exhibits a rapid initial increase followed by a gradual plateau. Initially, the number of females killed rises steeply from 0 to approximately 100 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase indicates a high initial hunting success rate or a high density of female prey that are easily accessible to the foragers. Following this steep rise, the rate of increase slows down significantly, and the cumulative number of females killed begins to plateau around 120 by approximately 1000 steps. This plateau suggests that the rate at which female prey are being hunted stabilizes, potentially due to a balance being reached between the hunting pressure and the remaining female prey population. The trend implies that after an initial period of high hunting success, the foragers may face diminishing returns due to reduced prey availability or increased difficulty in hunting the remaining females. For decision makers, this trend highlights the importance of understanding the initial high impact of hunting on the female prey population and the subsequent stabilization, which could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts.
