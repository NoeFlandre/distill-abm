The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and increases rapidly over the first 500 time steps. This initial rise can be attributed to the high initial density of prey and the optimal foraging behavior of hunters, who target the most accessible and valuable prey first. Around 500 steps, the rate of increase begins to slow as the number of males killed approaches approximately 100. This deceleration suggests a reduction in the availability of male prey due to hunting pressure and possibly environmental constraints. Between 500 and 1000 steps, the cumulative number of males killed continues to rise but at a slower rate, indicating that hunters are encountering fewer males or facing higher costs to locate and hunt them. After 1000 steps, the curve starts to plateau, with the number of males killed stabilizing around 120. This plateau indicates that the system is reaching an equilibrium where the remaining male prey are either harder to find or the costs of hunting outweigh the benefits, leading to a stabilization in the cumulative number of males killed. There is some variability across different simulation runs, particularly noticeable after 500 steps, reflecting the stochastic nature of the model and the variability in hunter success and prey availability.
