The total energy of male animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a consistent and significant decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12500 units. This high initial value indicates a robust population of male prey animals with substantial energy reserves. The energy levels drop sharply in the early stages, with a notable decrease observed within the first few hundred time steps. By around 500 time steps, the total energy has already reduced significantly, indicating a rapid decline in the energy reserves of the male prey population. This rapid decline continues, albeit at a slightly reduced rate, through subsequent time steps. Around the 1000 time step mark, the total energy levels off somewhat, showing a slower rate of decline. However, the overall trend remains a decrease, albeit with minor fluctuations. These fluctuations suggest periodic variations in the energy levels, potentially due to seasonal changes or other cyclic factors in the model. By the end of the simulation period, the total energy of male animals approaches zero, indicating a near-complete depletion of energy reserves in the male prey population. The implications for decision-makers are significant. The rapid initial decline in energy levels suggests that early intervention might be crucial to prevent a drastic reduction in the male prey population. The periodic fluctuations, although minor, indicate that there might be specific times or conditions under which the energy reserves stabilize temporarily. Understanding these periods could be key to implementing effective conservation strategies or adjusting hunting pressures to allow for population recovery.
