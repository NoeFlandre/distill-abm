The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, there is a period of no births, followed by a gradual increase starting around 725 time steps. The number of births continues to rise steadily, reaching a peak of approximately 5.6 births at around 1702 time steps. This increasing trend is statistically significant and consistent, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, which shows strong increasing trends in multiple intervals (e.g., 696-927, 928-1159, 1160-1391, and 1392-1623 time steps).

After reaching the peak, there is a notable decrease in the number of births, with a significant decreasing trend observed between 1624 and 1855 time steps, and continuing from 1856 to 2087 time steps. The number of births stabilizes at a lower level towards the end of the simulation period. The oscillation analysis indicates a dominant period of 50 time steps, suggesting regular fluctuations in the birth rate throughout the simulation.

Key change points in the trend are identified at approximately 725, 1325, 1850, 2050, and 2310 time steps, marking significant shifts in the birth rate dynamics. These trends provide insights into the reproductive behavior of the prey population under varying conditions of hunting pressure, environmental changes, and demographic factors. For decision-makers, understanding these trends can help in assessing the impact of different parameters on the prey population's sustainability and in formulating strategies to manage and conserve the species within the model's context.
