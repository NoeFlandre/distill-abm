The total energy of all animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of approximately 16,666.67 units. This high initial energy level rapidly declines within the first few hundred time steps. By around 500 time steps, the total energy has dropped significantly to roughly 5,000 units. This steep decline continues, albeit at a slower rate, until around 1,000 time steps, where the total energy stabilizes at lower values, generally below 1,000 units. Beyond 1,000 time steps, the total energy continues to decrease gradually, approaching near-zero values by the end of the simulation at 2312 time steps. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend with significant negative slopes in the rolling Mann-Kendall test across various segments of the time series. The presence of oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 time steps suggests some periodic fluctuations, but the overall trend remains a steady decline. For decision-makers, this trend indicates a rapid and substantial reduction in the energy levels of prey animals over time, which could imply significant stress on the animal population within the model's parameters. This insight could be crucial for understanding the dynamics of megaherbivore extinction and the impact of various factors such as hunting pressure and environmental changes.
