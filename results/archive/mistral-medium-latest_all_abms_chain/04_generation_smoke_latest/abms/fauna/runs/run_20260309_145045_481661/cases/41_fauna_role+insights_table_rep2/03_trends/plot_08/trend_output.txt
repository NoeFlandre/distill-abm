The simulation of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decline in the number of male prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at 125 male prey animals. The trend is characterized by a rapid decrease in the early stages, followed by a more gradual decline and eventual stabilization at zero by the end of the simulation period.  \n\nKey observations include several notable peaks and valleys in the population count. Early peaks occur around ticks 730 and 709, with values of approximately 10.54 and 8.85 male prey animals, respectively. Subsequent peaks are observed at ticks 803, 820, and 893, with values around 6.76, 6.24, and 4.25 male prey animals. These peaks are followed by corresponding valleys, indicating periodic fluctuations in the population. However, the overall trend remains a steady decline. \n\nThe rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant decreasing trends in the early phases of the simulation, with the most substantial declines observed between ticks 0-231, 232-463, and 464-695. The rate of decline slows down in later phases, with periods of no significant trend detected between ticks 1624-1855 and 1856-2087. This suggests that while the initial phases are marked by rapid population reduction, the later stages see a stabilization as the population approaches zero. \n\nThe oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, indicating cyclical patterns in the population dynamics. These oscillations are relatively strong, suggesting periodic influences on the male prey population, possibly due to seasonal variations or other cyclic factors within the model. \n\nFor decision-makers, these trends highlight the critical impact of initial conditions and ongoing pressures on the male prey population. The rapid early decline underscores the vulnerability of the population to initial hunting pressures and environmental factors. The periodic fluctuations suggest that while there are temporary recoveries, the overall trajectory is towards extinction under the given parameters. This information can be crucial for understanding the dynamics of megaherbivore populations under similar pressures and for designing conservation strategies that address both immediate threats and long-term sustainability.
