The total energy of male animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a consistent and significant decline over time. Initially, the total energy of male animals starts at approximately 12,000 units. This high energy level is maintained briefly before a sharp decline begins around 500 steps. By approximately 1,000 steps, the total energy drops steeply to around 2,000 units. Following this sharp decline, the energy levels continue to decrease more gradually, approaching near-zero values by around 2,000 steps. This trend suggests a rapid reduction in the energy reserves of male animals, likely due to a combination of hunting pressure, environmental factors, and the animals' movement and metabolic costs. For decision-makers, this trend indicates that under the given model parameters, male animals experience a rapid depletion of energy, which could lead to significant impacts on their survival and reproductive success. This insight could be crucial for understanding the dynamics of megaherbivore extinction and the factors contributing to the decline in male animal populations.
