The simulation of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear overall decreasing trend in the total number of prey animals over time. Initially, the number of animals starts at approximately 166.67 and consistently declines to zero by the end of the simulation. The mean number of animals throughout the simulation is around 25.01, with a median of 1.48, indicating that the population is generally low and skewed towards the lower end. The standard deviation of 44.90 and variance of 2015.71 suggest significant variability in the animal population at different time steps. The minimum value of zero at the end of the simulation and the maximum value of approximately 166.67 at the start highlight the extent of the decline. Several peaks and valleys are observed at various time steps, but these fluctuations are relatively minor compared to the overall downward trend. Key inflection points occur at multiple time steps, indicating changes in the rate of decline. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results show significant decreasing trends across most intervals, with only a couple of intervals showing slight increasing trends. Change points are identified at time steps 125, 475, 625, 875, and 2310, marking significant shifts in the population dynamics. The oscillation summary indicates a dominant period of 63 time steps, suggesting periodic fluctuations within the overall decline. For decision makers, these trends imply that under the given parameters, the prey population is under significant stress and ultimately faces extinction. This could inform strategies aimed at conservation or understanding the impacts of hunting pressure and environmental factors on megaherbivore populations.
