The total number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at 0 and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 200 deaths. This initial rapid increase in deaths can be attributed to the combined effects of predation by human hunters and natural mortality factors, as the prey population is initially exposed to high levels of stress from both environmental conditions and hunting pressure. Following this rapid increase, the rate of deaths slows down significantly, and the cumulative number of deaths gradually approaches an asymptote around 250 deaths. This plateau suggests that the prey population has reached a more stable state, where the remaining individuals are either less susceptible to predation and natural mortality or have adapted to the environmental pressures. The variance among simulation runs is relatively low initially but becomes more pronounced as time progresses, indicating that while the initial mortality trends are consistent across simulations, the long-term dynamics introduce more variability. This variability could be due to differences in individual prey survival strategies, hunter behavior, and environmental fluctuations.
