The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, there is a period of no births, followed by a gradual increase starting around 500 steps. The number of births rises steadily, reaching a peak of approximately 5.6 births around 1700 steps. This increasing trend is statistically significant, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, which shows strong positive trends in multiple intervals (e.g., 696-927 steps with tau=0.833, slope=0.001, p=0.000). After reaching the peak, there is a noticeable decline in the number of births, with a significant decreasing trend observed between 1624-1855 steps (tau=-0.495, slope=-0.005, p=0.000). The number of births stabilizes somewhat but remains lower than the peak values. The model also identifies several change points, notably around 725, 1325, 1850, 2050, and 2310 steps, indicating shifts in the trend dynamics. Additionally, there is evidence of periodic oscillations with a dominant period of 50 steps, suggesting regular fluctuations in the birth rates. For decision makers, these trends highlight the dynamic nature of prey population growth and the potential impacts of various factors such as environmental conditions, hunting pressure, and demographic changes on birth rates over time. Understanding these patterns can inform strategies for managing wildlife populations and assessing the sustainability of hunting practices.
