The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct phases over time. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the number of deaths, starting from 0 and rising steeply. This phase is characterized by a significant upward trend, with the number of deaths increasing quickly. By around 500 time steps, the rate of increase begins to slow down, indicating a transition phase where the steep rise starts to level off. This trend continues until approximately 1000 time steps, where the number of deaths reaches around 200. Following this, the rate of increase further diminishes, and the curve starts to plateau, suggesting a stabilization in the number of deaths. Between 1000 and 2000 time steps, the number of deaths fluctuates slightly but generally remains stable, hovering around 235 deaths. The data shows minor oscillations and small peaks and valleys, but the overall trend remains relatively flat, indicating that the system has reached a near-equilibrium state where the number of deaths does not change significantly over time. The initial rapid increase can be attributed to the initial conditions and interactions within the model, while the subsequent stabilization suggests a balance between the various factors influencing prey mortality, such as predation by foragers and natural causes.
