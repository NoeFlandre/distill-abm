The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population, from both natural causes and predation by foragers, exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the number of deaths, starting from 0 and rising steeply. This phase of rapid increase continues until around 500 time steps, where the number of deaths reaches approximately 150. Following this initial surge, the rate of increase in deaths begins to slow down, indicating a transition phase. By around 1000 time steps, the cumulative number of deaths stabilizes, reaching a plateau near 235 deaths. This plateau persists with minor fluctuations, indicating a steady-state where the number of deaths remains relatively constant. The statistical analysis reveals that the initial phase is characterized by a strong increasing trend, which gradually weakens as the system approaches equilibrium. The inflection points and change points identified in the data further support this transition from rapid increase to stabilization. The dominant period of oscillation suggests minor periodic variations around the steady-state value. For decision makers, these trends imply that the initial conditions and early dynamics are critical in shaping the long-term outcomes of the prey population. The rapid increase in deaths early on highlights the vulnerability of the prey population to initial hunting pressure and environmental factors. The subsequent stabilization suggests that the system reaches a balance where the rate of deaths is matched by other demographic processes, such as reproduction and survival. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions aimed at managing prey populations and ensuring their sustainability in the face of hunting pressure and environmental changes.
