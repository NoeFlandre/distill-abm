The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and remains constant for the first 100 steps. This initial period of inactivity could be attributed to the initial conditions of the simulation where hunters and prey are getting acclimated to the environment. After this initial period, there is a rapid increase in the number of females killed, reaching approximately 100 by 500 steps. This sharp rise indicates a phase where hunting activities intensify, possibly due to the hunters becoming more efficient or the prey becoming more accessible. Between 500 and 1000 steps, the rate of increase slows down, stabilizing around 120 females killed. This stabilization suggests that a balance is reached where the hunting pressure equals the prey's ability to evade or reproduce. The simulation ends with the number of females killed plateauing, indicating that the system has reached a dynamic equilibrium. For decision makers, this trend highlights the importance of understanding the initial conditions and the rapid adaptation phase in hunting dynamics. It also underscores the need to consider the long-term equilibrium in conservation efforts and resource management.
