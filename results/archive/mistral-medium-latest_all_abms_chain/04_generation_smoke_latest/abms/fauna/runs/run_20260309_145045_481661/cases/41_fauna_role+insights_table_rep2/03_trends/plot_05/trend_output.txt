The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear trend over the simulation period. Initially, the count starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase. This rapid rise continues until around the 2310th tick, where the cumulative count reaches approximately 117. The most significant increases occur early in the simulation, with the steepest slopes observed between the 0-231 and 232-463 tick intervals, indicating a high initial hunting rate of postpartum females.\n\nFollowing this rapid increase, the trend begins to stabilize, with the rate of increase slowing down significantly. Between the 1392-1623 tick interval, there is no significant trend, suggesting a period of equilibrium where the number of postpartum females hunted stabilizes around the mean value of approximately 117. This stabilization is followed by a slight decrease in the trend starting from the 1856th tick onwards, although the overall cumulative count remains high.\n\nThe data also exhibits some oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of 63 ticks, particularly noticeable towards the end of the simulation. However, these oscillations are relatively minor compared to the overall trend. The peaks and valleys identified in the data are closely clustered around the mean value of 117, indicating that while there are minor fluctuations, the system largely remains in a steady state after the initial rapid increase.\n\nFor decision makers, these trends suggest that initial hunting pressure on postpartum females is high but stabilizes over time. This stabilization could imply that the forager bands reach a carrying capacity or equilibrium in their hunting behavior, which might be influenced by the availability of prey or other environmental factors. Understanding these dynamics can help in assessing the impact of hunting pressure on the population of megaherbivores and in designing conservation strategies.
