The simulation of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decline in the number of male prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at 125 male prey animals. The most significant decreases occur early in the simulation, with notable drops around time steps 125, 250, and 375. The trend of decline continues steadily, with the population experiencing periodic minor fluctuations. These fluctuations are relatively small and do not significantly alter the overall downward trajectory. The peaks and valleys identified in the data indicate minor temporary increases and decreases in the population, but these are not sustained. For instance, slight increases are observed around time steps 730, 709, and 803, but these are followed by subsequent declines. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms the decreasing trend in the population, with significant decreases noted in the early and middle phases of the simulation. Towards the latter part of the simulation, the trend stabilizes, showing no significant increase or decrease, indicating that the population has nearly diminished. The dominant oscillation period of 63 time steps suggests regular but minor fluctuations in the population count. By the end of the simulation, the number of male prey animals reaches zero. For decision makers, this trend indicates that under the given parameters and conditions, the male prey population is not sustainable and heads towards extinction. This insight can be crucial for understanding the dynamics of megaherbivore populations under similar simulated conditions and for making informed decisions regarding conservation strategies and the impact of hunting pressures.
