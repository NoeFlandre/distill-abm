The total energy of female animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12,500 units. This high initial value indicates a large population of female prey animals with substantial energy reserves at the beginning of the simulation. The energy levels drop sharply in the early stages, with a significant decrease observed within the first few hundred time steps. By around 500 time steps, the total energy has already reduced considerably, indicating a rapid decline in the energy reserves of the female prey population. This rapid decline continues, albeit at a slower rate, as the simulation progresses. Around the 2,310th time step, the total energy of female animals approaches zero, suggesting that the population has been nearly depleted of energy. Throughout the simulation, there are minor fluctuations and small peaks and valleys in the total energy levels, but the overall trend remains a steady decline. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend, with significant negative slopes in the rolling Mann-Kendall test across various segments of the simulation. The implications for decision-makers are that under the given parameters and conditions, the female prey population experiences a continuous and significant reduction in energy reserves, leading to potential extinction or severe depletion over time. This trend highlights the impact of the model's parameters on the sustainability of the prey population and underscores the need for careful consideration of factors such as hunting pressure, environmental conditions, and prey demographics in conservation and management strategies.
