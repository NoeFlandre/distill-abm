The percentage of vegetated landscape patches in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits several notable trends and patterns over the simulation period. Initially, the proportion of green patches starts at approximately 33% and remains relatively stable throughout the simulation, ending at around 32.998%. The mean percentage of vegetated patches across the simulation is approximately 32.656%, with a median value of 32.970%, indicating a slight skew in the distribution. The standard deviation of 0.950 and variance of 0.903 suggest low variability in the data. 

The minimum value observed is 24.911%, occurring at tick 365, which is a significant drop from the initial value. This dip represents the most substantial deviation from the overall stable trend. The data series exhibits several peaks and valleys, although the differences in values at these points are minimal, indicating minor fluctuations around the mean. The peaks and valleys are closely clustered, suggesting a pattern of minor oscillations. 

The rolling Mann-Kendall test reveals several significant trends within different segments of the simulation. From ticks 0 to 1623, the percentage of vegetated patches generally exhibits an increasing trend, with varying strengths of significance and slopes. Notably, the strongest increasing trend is observed between ticks 696 and 927, with a high Kendall tau value of 0.931. However, from tick 1624 onwards, the trend shifts to a decreasing pattern, with significant decreases noted between ticks 1624 to 1855 and 1856 to 2087. 

Change points identified at ticks 150, 300, 475, 725, and 2310 indicate moments where the trend in the data undergoes notable shifts. These change points may correspond to underlying changes in the model dynamics or interactions among the agents. The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, with strong oscillations observed at regular intervals. This periodicity suggests a cyclical pattern in the vegetation coverage, potentially linked to seasonal variations or other periodic factors within the model. 

Overall, the percentage of vegetated patches in the MHPM simulation remains relatively stable with minor fluctuations and a slight decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation period. The identified trends, change points, and oscillations provide insights into the dynamic interactions within the model, reflecting the complex interplay between environmental factors and agent behaviors.
