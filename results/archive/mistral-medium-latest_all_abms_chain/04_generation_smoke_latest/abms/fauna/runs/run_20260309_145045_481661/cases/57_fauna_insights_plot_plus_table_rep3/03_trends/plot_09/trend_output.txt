The number of female animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the number of female animals starts at 125 and rapidly declines. This decline continues steadily throughout the simulation, with the number of female animals approaching zero as the simulation progresses towards 73000 ticks. The mean number of female animals across the simulation is approximately 16.48, with a median of 1, indicating that for most of the simulation, the number of female animals is quite low. The standard deviation of 31.55 and variance of 995.63 suggest significant variability in the early stages of the simulation, which diminishes as the number of female animals decreases. The minimum value of zero is reached at the end of the simulation, indicating the potential extinction or near-extinction of female animals. The peaks and valleys identified in the data show minor fluctuations but do not significantly alter the overall decreasing trend. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this decreasing trend across various segments of the simulation, with significant decreases noted in multiple intervals. The change points identified at ticks 475, 600, 875, and 2310 mark notable shifts in the rate of decline, but the overall trajectory remains downward. The oscillation summary indicates periodic fluctuations with a dominant period of 63 ticks, but these oscillations are relatively minor compared to the overall trend. For decision-makers, this trend suggests that under the given parameters and conditions, the population of female animals is under significant stress, leading to a continuous decline. This insight could be crucial for understanding the dynamics of megaherbivore extinction and the impact of various factors such as hunting pressure, environmental changes, and demographic variables. The consistent decline highlights the need for potential interventions or adjustments in parameters to explore scenarios that might lead to more sustainable population levels.
