The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the number of males killed, starting from 0 and rising sharply. This steep increase continues until around 500 steps, where the rate of increase begins to slow down. By approximately 1000 steps, the number of males killed reaches around 116, indicating a significant hunting activity during this period. After this point, the trend stabilizes, and the number of males killed fluctuates slightly around a mean value of approximately 117.5. The data shows minor oscillations with a dominant period of around 63 steps, suggesting periodic variations in hunting success or prey availability. Key inflection points occur at various steps, notably around 362, 1241, and 1330, indicating changes in the rate of increase. The trend analysis reveals significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases, followed by periods of no significant trend and a slight decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation. The maximum number of males killed is recorded at around 120.17, while the minimum remains at 0, as expected at the start of the simulation. The implications for decision-makers include understanding the dynamics of hunting pressure and its stabilization over time, which can inform strategies for managing prey populations and hunter activities in similar socio-natural systems.
