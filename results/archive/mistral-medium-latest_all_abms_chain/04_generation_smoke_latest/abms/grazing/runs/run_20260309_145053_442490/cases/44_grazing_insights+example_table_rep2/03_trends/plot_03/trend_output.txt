The average amount of reserve biomass available across all patches begins at 90,000 units. Initially, there is a slight increase, followed by a notable decline reaching a minimum of approximately 84,114 units around the 26th time step. This decline could be attributed to the initial grazing pressure exerted by livestock as households adapt their strategies in response to observed pasture conditions and neighbor behaviors. Following this decline, the reserve biomass exhibits a steady and consistent increase throughout the remainder of the simulation, culminating in a peak of around 121,073 units at the final time step. This upward trend suggests an overall improvement in biomass conditions, potentially due to adaptive management practices and favorable ecological interactions over time. The presence of strong oscillations, particularly around the middle time steps, indicates periodic fluctuations in biomass levels, which may reflect seasonal variations or adaptive responses to changing environmental conditions. For decision makers, the initial decline highlights the importance of monitoring and managing grazing practices during early stages to prevent overuse of pastures. The subsequent recovery and growth in biomass suggest that adaptive co-management strategies can lead to sustainable improvements in ecological conditions, emphasizing the value of long-term planning and adaptive learning in household decision-making processes.
