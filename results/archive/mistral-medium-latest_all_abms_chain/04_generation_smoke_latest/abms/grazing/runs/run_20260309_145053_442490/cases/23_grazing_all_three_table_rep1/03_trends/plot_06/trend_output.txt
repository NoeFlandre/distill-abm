Subsequent fluctuations show a series of smaller peaks and troughs, with notable peaks at time steps 12 (2.74), 17 (1.47), and 19 (1.38), interspersed with declines. These oscillations suggest periodic stresses and recoveries, potentially reflecting seasonal variations or intermittent resource availability affecting livestock management. The trend generally decreases over time, with significant decreasing trends identified in the initial phases (time steps 0-32) as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis. This indicates that, despite periodic stresses, the overall pressure to destock livestock reduces gradually. 

After the initial volatile period, the system appears to stabilize somewhat, with less pronounced fluctuations and a more gradual decline in destocking rates. The detection of change points at time steps 30 and 100 suggests shifts in the underlying dynamics, possibly due to long-term adaptations by households or changes in environmental conditions. The dominant oscillation period of 49 time steps hints at cyclical patterns in livestock destocking, which could be linked to recurring climatic or resource availability cycles. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of preparing for initial severe resource shortages and the need for strategies to manage periodic stresses. The gradual stabilization suggests that long-term planning and adaptive management practices could mitigate the impacts of these cyclic challenges on livestock numbers.
