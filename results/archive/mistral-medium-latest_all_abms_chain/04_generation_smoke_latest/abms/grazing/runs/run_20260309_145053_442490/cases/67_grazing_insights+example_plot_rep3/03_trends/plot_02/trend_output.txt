The mean household risk attitude starts with a value of approximately 0.5 across all simulations. In the initial 20 time steps, there is a rapid divergence in the risk attitudes among households. Some households exhibit an increase in risk attitude, reaching values as high as 0.8, while others show a decrease, dropping to values as low as -0.2. This divergence suggests that households are quickly adapting their risk attitudes based on initial observations and interactions within the model. Between time steps 20 and 40, the risk attitudes tend to stabilize, with households maintaining their respective risk attitudes without significant fluctuations. This stabilization indicates that households have settled into their adaptive strategies based on the initial learning phase. After time step 40, the trends in risk attitude remain relatively constant, with minor fluctuations. Households that increased their risk attitude maintain higher values, while those that decreased their risk attitude maintain lower values. This consistency implies that the adaptive strategies and learning behaviors have been established and are being maintained over the long term. For decision-makers, the rapid initial divergence in risk attitudes highlights the importance of early interventions and support to guide households toward beneficial adaptive strategies. The stabilization of risk attitudes after the initial phase suggests that once adaptive strategies are established, they are likely to persist, underscoring the need for effective initial guidance and resources.
