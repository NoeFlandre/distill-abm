The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households starts at 90. This initial value reflects the starting conditions of the simulation where each household begins with a set number of livestock. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid decline in the total number of livestock, reaching a low point around time step 37 with a value of approximately 41. This sharp decrease could be attributed to initial adaptive behaviors of households as they respond to environmental conditions and possibly destock livestock to align with carrying capacities of their pastures. Following this decline, the number of livestock stabilizes and begins to exhibit a gradual increasing trend starting around time step 44. This recovery phase suggests that households are adjusting their management strategies effectively, possibly benefiting from learning mechanisms incorporated in the model. The increase continues steadily, though at a decreasing rate, indicating a potential stabilization in the system as households reach a new equilibrium in their livestock management practices. The presence of strong oscillations, particularly in the early and middle time steps, suggests periodic adjustments and responses to varying conditions, which could be climatic or social factors influencing decision-making. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial adaptive strategies and the potential benefits of incorporating learning mechanisms in livestock management to achieve long-term stability.
