The mean green biomass starts at approximately 45,000 units. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a slight decline, followed by a more pronounced decrease until around the 40th time step, where the biomass reaches a minimum value of approximately 42,000 units. This initial decline could be attributed to the initial grazing activities of the livestock as they are introduced to the pastures. After this period, the mean green biomass begins to increase steadily. This recovery phase continues until around the 80th time step, where the biomass levels off and stabilizes at approximately 63,000 units. The increase in biomass suggests that the pastures are recovering and possibly benefiting from adaptive management practices or favorable climatic conditions that promote vegetation growth. The stabilization indicates that the system may have reached a dynamic equilibrium where the rate of biomass production matches the rate of consumption by livestock. For decision makers, the initial decline in biomass highlights the importance of managing initial grazing pressures to avoid overgrazing. The subsequent recovery and stabilization suggest that adaptive management practices can lead to sustainable pasture use over time. Understanding these trends can help in planning for sustainable livestock management and ensuring long-term productivity of the pastures.
