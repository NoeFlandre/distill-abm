The average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents begins at a low value of 0.0074. This initial value suggests that households start with livestock numbers closely aligned to the recommended levels. The deviation increases rapidly, reaching a peak of approximately 0.3814 around the 33rd time step. This significant rise indicates a period where households increasingly deviate from the recommended livestock numbers, potentially due to adaptive strategies or responses to environmental and social factors. Following this peak, there is a noticeable decline in deviation, stabilizing around a value of 0.1911 by the end of the simulation. This trend suggests that over time, households may be adjusting their livestock numbers closer to the recommended levels, possibly due to learning and adaptation processes. The presence of multiple peaks and valleys between the 20th and 50th time steps indicates fluctuations in household decisions, reflecting periods of adjustment and response to changing conditions. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis reveals significant increasing trends in deviation during the initial phases (0-32 time steps), followed by significant decreasing trends (33-65 time steps), and eventually stabilizing with no significant trends (66-98 time steps). This pattern highlights the dynamic nature of household decision-making and the impact of learning and adaptation over time. For decision makers, these trends underscore the importance of understanding the adaptive behaviors of households and the factors influencing their deviations from recommended practices. The initial rapid increase in deviation suggests a need for interventions or support during early stages to guide households towards sustainable practices. The subsequent stabilization indicates potential long-term benefits of adaptive learning and social interactions in achieving more sustainable livestock management.
