This sharp decrease could be attributed to initial adaptive strategies employed by households in response to environmental or climatic stressors, leading to a reduction in livestock numbers as households adjust to sustainable levels. The lowest point is reached at around 41 livestock at time step 37, indicating a critical phase where households have significantly reduced their livestock holdings, possibly due to harsh conditions or strategic destocking to prevent overgrazing. Following this low point, the trend reverses, and the number of livestock begins to increase gradually. This recovery phase suggests that households start to rebuild their livestock numbers as conditions improve or as they adapt better to the environment. The increase is steady and consistent from time step 44 onwards, indicating a period of stabilization and growth in livestock numbers. By the end of the simulation at time step 100, the average total number of livestock reaches approximately 47.55. The initial rapid decline followed by a gradual recovery highlights the resilience and adaptive capacity of the households in managing their livestock under varying conditions. For decision makers, this trend underscores the importance of supporting households during the initial adaptive phase to mitigate sharp declines in livestock numbers and to facilitate recovery and growth in the long term.
