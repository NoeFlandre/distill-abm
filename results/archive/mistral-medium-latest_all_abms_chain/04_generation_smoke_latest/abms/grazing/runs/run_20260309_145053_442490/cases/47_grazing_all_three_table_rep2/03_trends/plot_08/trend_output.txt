The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases steadily over the simulation period, reaching a maximum of approximately 32 households by the midpoint of the simulation. This trend indicates a gradual adoption of social learning behaviors among households. The initial increase is rapid, suggesting that early adopters quickly recognize the benefits of social learning. The rate of increase slows down as the number of households engaging in social learning approaches its peak, indicating a saturation point where most households that are likely to adopt social learning have already done so. The inflection points suggest periods where the rate of adoption changes, potentially due to varying levels of interaction or changes in the perceived benefits of social learning. The consistent increase across all rolling Mann-Kendall windows indicates a robust trend towards greater social learning engagement over time. For decision makers, this trend suggests that interventions aimed at promoting social learning could be effective, particularly in the early stages of implementation. The saturation point indicates that there may be a limit to the number of households that will adopt social learning, which could inform the targeting and scaling of such interventions.
