The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all households, as measured by the Gini coefficient, exhibits a consistent increasing trend over the entire simulation period. Initially, the Gini coefficient starts at a value of 0.0, indicating perfect equality in the distribution of healthy livestock. Over time, the inequality gradually increases, reaching a value of approximately 0.0726 by the end of the simulation. This trend is statistically significant and consistent across all time intervals, as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall test results, which show a significant increasing trend with a tau value of 1.000 and a p-value of 0.000 in each interval. The change points detected at time steps 51, 71, and 100 suggest notable shifts in the rate of increase in inequality. The dominant period of oscillation is 49.00, indicating a cyclical pattern in the data. For decision makers, this trend implies that over time, the distribution of healthy livestock becomes increasingly unequal among households. This could have significant implications for the sustainability and equity of the grazing system, potentially requiring interventions to address the growing disparity.
