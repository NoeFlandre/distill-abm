The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period. Starting from an initial value of 0, the metric rises steadily, reaching a final value of approximately 96.78 by the end of the simulation at time step 100. The trend is characterized by a rapid initial increase, with a slope of 4.956 in the first 10 time steps, followed by a gradual slowing in the rate of increase. The slopes for subsequent decades of time steps are 1.466, 0.687, and so on, indicating a decelerating yet persistent upward trend. The Mann-Kendall test confirms the increasing trend across all time windows, with significant p-values. Change points are detected at time steps 25, 60, and 100, suggesting shifts in the rate of destocking. The presence of oscillations with a dominant period of 49 time steps indicates periodic fluctuations in the destocking rate. For decision makers, this trend suggests a continuous and accelerating need for destocking over time, which may reflect underlying pressures on the grazing system. The periodic oscillations hint at cyclical factors influencing destocking decisions, possibly related to climatic or seasonal variations. Understanding these trends can help in planning adaptive management strategies to mitigate the impacts of increasing destocking rates.
