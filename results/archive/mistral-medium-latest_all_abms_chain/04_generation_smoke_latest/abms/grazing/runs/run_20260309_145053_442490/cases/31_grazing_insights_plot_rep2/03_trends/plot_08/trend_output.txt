The data from the repeated simulations of the agent-based model show distinct trends in the number of households engaging in social learning over time. Initially, all simulations start with zero households participating in social learning. By around step 10, there is a noticeable divergence in the number of households adopting social learning, with some simulations showing a rapid increase while others exhibit a more gradual uptake. By step 20, the number of households engaging in social learning ranges widely from approximately 10 to 40 households. This variability continues until around step 40, where the trends begin to stabilize. By step 50, the number of households engaging in social learning in most simulations has plateaued, with values ranging from about 15 to 50 households. This stabilization continues through to step 100, indicating that the adoption of social learning behaviors reaches a steady state in the long term. For decision makers, these trends suggest that the adoption of social learning among households does not follow a uniform pattern and can vary significantly in the initial phases. However, over time, the number of households engaging in social learning tends to stabilize, indicating a potential equilibrium in the diffusion of this behavior. This insight can help in planning interventions and understanding the dynamics of behavioral adoption in similar contexts.
