The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households starts at 90. Initially, there is a rapid decline in the number of livestock over the first 20 time steps, dropping to around 60. This sharp decrease could be attributed to initial adjustments in household decision-making and environmental factors impacting livestock survival. Following this decline, the number of livestock continues to decrease at a slower rate until around time step 37, reaching a minimum value of approximately 41. This period of decline may reflect ongoing adaptations to pasture conditions and household strategies. After time step 37, the trend reverses, and the number of livestock begins to increase gradually. This increase continues steadily through to the end of the simulation, reaching a value of approximately 47.55 by time step 100. The initial rapid decline followed by a gradual recovery suggests that households may have faced initial challenges but eventually adapted their strategies to better manage their livestock. For decision-makers, this trend indicates the importance of supporting households during initial periods of adjustment to prevent significant livestock losses and to promote strategies that facilitate recovery and growth in livestock numbers over time.
