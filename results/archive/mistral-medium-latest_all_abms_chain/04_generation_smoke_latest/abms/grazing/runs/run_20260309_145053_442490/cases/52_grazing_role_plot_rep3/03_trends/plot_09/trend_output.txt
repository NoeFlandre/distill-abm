The trends in the data from the repeated simulations of the agent-based model show distinct patterns in the average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households over 100 time steps. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the mean destock total of households, with values rising steeply from 0 to approximately 60 within the first 20 time steps. This initial phase indicates a period of significant adjustment where households are likely responding to immediate ecological and climatic conditions by reducing their livestock numbers. Following this rapid increase, the rate of destocking slows down considerably. Between time steps 20 and 40, the mean destock total continues to rise but at a more gradual pace, reaching around 80. This suggests a stabilization phase where households are adapting their strategies based on initial observations and learning. From time step 40 onwards, the destocking rate further slows, and the curves begin to plateau. By time step 100, the mean destock total values range between approximately 80 and 160, indicating a wide variability in the long-term strategies and outcomes among different households. Some households continue to destock at a steady rate, while others stabilize their livestock numbers. This variability highlights the diverse adaptive strategies employed by households, influenced by their learning capabilities and interactions with neighbors. The overall trend demonstrates an initial reactive phase followed by a more strategic and adaptive management phase, reflecting the complex decision-making processes in response to ecological and climatic parameters.
