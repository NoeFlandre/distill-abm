The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases steadily over the first 40 time steps, reaching approximately 28 households. This initial rapid adoption of social learning suggests that households quickly recognize and adopt beneficial strategies from their neighbors. The rate of increase slows down between time steps 40 and 100, with the number of households engaging in social learning stabilizing around 32 households. This stabilization indicates a potential saturation point where most households that can benefit from social learning have already adopted it. The trend shows a consistent upward trajectory with no significant declines, implying a robust diffusion of social learning practices among households. The strong oscillations detected in the middle time steps suggest periods of heightened interaction and strategy adoption among households. For decision makers, this trend highlights the importance of facilitating early adoption of beneficial practices and understanding the factors that contribute to the stabilization of social learning uptake. The insights can inform policies aimed at enhancing information dissemination and cooperative behaviors in pastoral communities.
