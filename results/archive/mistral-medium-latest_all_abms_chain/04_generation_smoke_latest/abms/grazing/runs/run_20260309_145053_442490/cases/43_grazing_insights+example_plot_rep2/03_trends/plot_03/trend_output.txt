The mean reserve biomass starts at approximately 90,000 units. It initially exhibits a slight increase, reaching a peak around 92,000 units within the first few time steps. This initial rise could be attributed to the initial growth of vegetation due to favorable conditions such as adequate rainfall and low grazing pressure at the start of the simulation. Following this peak, there is a noticeable decline in mean reserve biomass, dropping to around 85,000 units by time step 20. This decline may indicate the onset of grazing activities by livestock, which reduces the available biomass as animals consume the vegetation. After time step 20, the mean reserve biomass begins to recover, showing a steady increase. This recovery phase continues until around time step 60, where the biomass levels reach approximately 120,000 units. The steady increase suggests a period of vegetation regrowth, possibly due to reduced grazing pressure or improved environmental conditions that promote plant growth. From time step 60 onwards, the mean reserve biomass stabilizes, fluctuating slightly around the 120,000 units mark. This stabilization indicates that the system has reached a dynamic equilibrium where the rate of biomass production is balanced by the rate of consumption by livestock. The trends in mean reserve biomass provide insights into the resilience and sustainability of the grazing system. The initial decline followed by recovery and stabilization suggests that the system can withstand initial grazing pressures and recover over time. For decision-makers, this implies that managing grazing intensity and ensuring periods of rest for pastures can help maintain biomass levels and support long-term sustainability. Monitoring biomass levels and adjusting grazing practices accordingly can help prevent overgrazing and ensure the availability of forage for livestock throughout the year.
