The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households shows a consistent increasing trend over the entire simulation period of 100 time steps. Starting from an initial value of 0, the metric steadily rises, reaching a final value of approximately 96.78. The trend is characterized by a continuous increase without any detected peaks or valleys, indicating a steady accumulation of destocked livestock over time. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this increasing trend across all time windows, with significant positive slopes and tau values of 1.000, indicating strong and consistent upward movement. The slope of the increase is steepest in the initial time steps, with a slope of 4.956 in the first 10 time steps, and gradually becomes more moderate, stabilizing around a slope of 0.311 in the later time steps. The change points detected at time steps 25, 60, and 100 suggest shifts in the rate of increase, although the overall trend remains upward. The oscillation analysis indicates some periodic fluctuations with a dominant period of 49 time steps, suggesting cyclical patterns in the rate of destocking. For decision makers, this trend implies a continuous and significant reduction in livestock numbers over time, which could have substantial implications for household livelihoods and the overall resilience of the pastoral system. Understanding the underlying causes of this trend and the periodic fluctuations could be crucial for developing strategies to mitigate the impacts of destocking on the community.
