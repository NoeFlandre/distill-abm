The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, starts at approximately 90 across all households. This value declines sharply within the first 10 time steps, reaching around 45. This rapid decline could be attributed to initial adjustments in the grazing system as households adapt their livestock numbers to the carrying capacity of the pastures and environmental conditions. Between time steps 10 and 40, the number of livestock continues to decrease but at a slower rate, stabilizing around 40. This period likely represents a phase of stabilization where households fine-tune their livestock numbers based on observed pasture conditions and initial learning experiences. After time step 40, there is a gradual increase in the average number of livestock, which continues until around time step 70, where it peaks at approximately 55. This increase suggests that households are successfully adapting their management strategies, possibly through learning-by-doing and social learning, leading to improved pasture conditions and livestock health. From time step 70 to the end of the simulation at time step 100, the average number of livestock stabilizes, fluctuating slightly around 50. This stabilization indicates that the system has reached a dynamic equilibrium where the number of livestock is balanced with the available resources and management practices.
