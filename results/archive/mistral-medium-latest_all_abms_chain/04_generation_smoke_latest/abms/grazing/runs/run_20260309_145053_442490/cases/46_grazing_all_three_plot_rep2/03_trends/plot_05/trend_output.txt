The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, starts at approximately 90. This high initial value indicates that households begin with a substantial livestock population. There is a rapid decline in the first 10 steps, dropping to around 50. This sharp decrease suggests an initial adjustment period where households may be destocking livestock to align with their carrying capacity or in response to initial environmental conditions. From step 10 to step 40, the number of livestock continues to decline but at a slower rate, stabilizing around 40. This period indicates a phase of stabilization where households are likely adapting their livestock numbers to sustainable levels given the ecological and climatic conditions. After step 40, there is a gradual increase in the number of livestock, with some simulations showing a rise to around 60 by step 100. This upward trend suggests that households are gradually rebuilding their livestock populations as they learn and adapt to the conditions, possibly improving their management strategies over time. The variability among simulation runs increases notably after step 40, indicating diverse outcomes based on different adaptive strategies and learning processes among households. For decision makers, the initial rapid decline highlights the importance of preparing for significant early adjustments in livestock numbers. The subsequent stabilization and gradual increase suggest that long-term strategies should focus on sustainable management practices that allow for gradual recovery and growth of livestock populations.
