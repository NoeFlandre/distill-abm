The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, starts at approximately 90. This high initial value indicates that households begin with a substantial livestock population. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid decline in the total number of livestock, dropping to around 45. This sharp decrease suggests an initial period of adjustment where households may be destocking livestock to align with their carrying capacity or in response to initial environmental conditions. From time step 20 to around time step 50, the number of livestock remains relatively stable, fluctuating slightly around the 45 mark. This period of stability indicates that households have reached a temporary equilibrium in their livestock management practices. After time step 50, there is a gradual increase in the total number of livestock, reaching approximately 60 by time step 100. This upward trend suggests that households are gradually increasing their livestock numbers, possibly due to improved environmental conditions, better management practices, or successful adaptation strategies. The variability among different simulation runs increases slightly over time, indicating that while the general trend is an increase in livestock numbers, individual household decisions and environmental conditions lead to some divergence in outcomes. For decision makers, the initial rapid decline highlights the importance of preparing for significant adjustments in livestock numbers during the early stages of management. The subsequent stabilization and gradual increase suggest that long-term strategies can lead to recovery and growth in livestock populations, emphasizing the need for sustainable and adaptive management practices.
