The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households exhibits a rapid increase during the initial time steps, followed by a gradual leveling off. Initially, from step 0 to around step 20, there is a steep rise in the mean destock total, indicating a significant amount of livestock being destocked early in the simulation. By step 20, the destocking values range between approximately 40 and 80 livestock. Beyond step 20, the rate of increase slows down considerably, and the curves begin to plateau. From step 40 to step 100, the destocking values stabilize, with most simulations showing a mean destock total between 80 and 140 livestock. The highest destocking value reaches around 160 livestock by the end of the simulation period. This trend suggests that the majority of destocking decisions occur early in the simulation, likely as households respond to initial conditions and immediate observations. The subsequent stabilization indicates a period where households adjust to a more sustainable level of livestock management. For decision-makers, this implies that initial conditions and early interventions are critical in influencing long-term livestock management strategies. Understanding the factors driving early destocking can help in designing policies that promote sustainable livestock management from the outset.
