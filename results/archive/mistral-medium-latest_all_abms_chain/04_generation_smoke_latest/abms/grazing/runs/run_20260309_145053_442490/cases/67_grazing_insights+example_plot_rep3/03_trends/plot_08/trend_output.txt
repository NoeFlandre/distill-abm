The count of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and shows a rapid increase within the first 20 time steps. This initial phase indicates a quick adoption of social learning behaviors among households, likely due to the initial conditions and the immediate need for adaptive strategies in response to environmental and social factors. By around 20 time steps, the number of households with social learning stabilizes into distinct groups, reflecting different behavioral types and their respective adoption rates. The majority of households reach a steady state between 30 and 54 households engaging in social learning, with some variability observed across different simulation runs. This stabilization suggests that after the initial rapid adoption phase, the diffusion of social learning behaviors slows down as households settle into their adaptive strategies. The variability in the steady state values indicates differences in the effectiveness and spread of social learning behaviors among different household types. For decision makers, the rapid initial increase in social learning adoption highlights the importance of early interventions and support to facilitate adaptive behaviors. The subsequent stabilization suggests that while social learning is widely adopted, there are inherent differences in how various household types integrate these behaviors, necessitating tailored approaches to maximize the benefits of social learning across the population.
