The mean household risk attitude across various simulations exhibits distinct trends over the 100 steps. Initially, the risk attitudes start around a neutral value of 0.0. Within the first 20 steps, there is a rapid divergence in the risk attitudes, with some households showing an increase and others a decrease in their risk attitude. By step 20, the risk attitudes stabilize into three general trends: an increase to values around 0.6 to 0.8, a stabilization around the neutral value, and a decrease to values around -0.2 to -0.4. These trends remain relatively stable for the remainder of the simulation, indicating that households' risk attitudes become consistent over time. For decision-makers, this suggests that initial conditions and early interactions significantly influence long-term risk attitudes, which could impact the overall resilience and adaptive capacity of the grazing system. Understanding these trends can help in designing interventions that either mitigate negative risk attitudes or enhance positive ones, thereby promoting more sustainable grazing practices.
