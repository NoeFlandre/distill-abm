The mean reserve biomass in the agent-based model exhibits distinct trends over the 100 steps of the simulation. Initially, the mean reserve biomass starts at approximately 90,000 units. Within the first 10 steps, there is a noticeable decline in the mean reserve biomass, reaching a low point of around 85,000 units. This initial decrease suggests a period of increased grazing or reduced growth conditions, potentially due to the initial placement of livestock and their immediate impact on the pastures. 

Following this decline, there is a gradual recovery phase that begins around step 10 and continues until approximately step 40. During this period, the mean reserve biomass steadily increases, indicating a recovery in pasture conditions. This recovery could be attributed to adaptive management practices, such as reduced grazing pressure or improved pasture management techniques adopted by the households. 

From step 40 onwards, the mean reserve biomass continues to rise more sharply, eventually plateauing around step 60. The biomass stabilizes at a higher level, approximately between 120,000 and 130,000 units, for the remainder of the simulation. This stabilization at a higher biomass level suggests that the grazing system has reached a new equilibrium, where the growth of vegetation matches the grazing pressure exerted by the livestock. 

For decision makers, the initial decline in biomass highlights the importance of careful initial stocking rates and the need for adaptive management practices to prevent overgrazing. The subsequent recovery and stabilization phases demonstrate the potential for sustainable grazing systems when adaptive co-management strategies are effectively implemented. The final plateau indicates that with appropriate management, it is possible to maintain high levels of reserve biomass, ensuring the long-term health and productivity of the grazing system.
