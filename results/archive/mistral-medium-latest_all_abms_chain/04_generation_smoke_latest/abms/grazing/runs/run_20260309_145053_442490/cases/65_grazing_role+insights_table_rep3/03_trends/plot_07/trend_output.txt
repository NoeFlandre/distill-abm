The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all households, as measured by the Gini coefficient, exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period. Initially, the Gini coefficient starts at a value of 0.0000, indicating perfect equality in the distribution of healthy livestock. Over time, the inequality gradually increases, reaching a value of 0.0726 by the end of the simulation. The mean Gini coefficient across the simulation period is 0.0302, with a median of 0.0229, suggesting that while the inequality is generally low, there is a slight skew towards higher inequality values. The standard deviation of 0.0239 and variance of 0.0006 indicate relatively low variability in the inequality measures over time. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent and significant increasing trend in inequality across all time windows, with a significant p-value of 0.000 in each window. Change points are detected at time steps 51, 71, and 100, suggesting notable shifts in the trend at these points. The dominant period of oscillation is 49.00, with strong oscillations observed towards the latter part of the simulation period. For decision makers, these trends imply that over time, the distribution of healthy livestock becomes increasingly unequal among households. This growing inequality could have significant implications for the resilience and sustainability of the pastoral system, potentially leading to disparities in household livelihoods and well-being. Understanding these trends can help in designing interventions and policies aimed at promoting more equitable distribution of resources and enhancing the overall resilience of the system.
