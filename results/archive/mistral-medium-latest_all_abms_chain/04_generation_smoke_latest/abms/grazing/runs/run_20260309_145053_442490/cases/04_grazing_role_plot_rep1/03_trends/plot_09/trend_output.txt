The trends in the data from the repeated simulations of the agent-based model show distinct patterns in the mean destock total of households over 100 steps. Initially, the mean destock total starts at zero for all simulations. In the early steps, there is a rapid increase in the mean destock total, with significant variability among the different simulations. By around step 20, the mean destock total ranges approximately between 40 and 80 livestock across the simulations. This rapid increase continues until about step 40, where the mean destock total stabilizes and the rate of increase slows down. At this point, the values range roughly between 60 and 120 livestock. Beyond step 40, the mean destock total continues to rise but at a much slower and more consistent rate. By step 100, the mean destock total values range from approximately 70 to 170 livestock. Throughout the simulations, there is a noticeable divergence in the trajectories, indicating variability in the outcomes based on different parameters or initial conditions. Some simulations show a higher cumulative destock total, suggesting more intensive destocking practices or more challenging ecological conditions, while others show lower totals, indicating less destocking. This variability highlights the impact of different household behaviors, ecological parameters, and learning strategies on the long-term social-ecological consequences in the grazing system.
