The average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents begins at a low value of approximately 0.0074. This metric shows a rapid increase in the early stages, reaching a peak value of around 0.3814 at around the 33rd time step. This initial rise suggests that households are initially adjusting their livestock numbers significantly as they adapt to the model's conditions and learn from their experiences. Following this peak, the deviation metric exhibits a general decreasing trend, indicating that households are gradually aligning their livestock numbers closer to the recommended values over time. The metric stabilizes towards the latter part of the simulation, ending at a value of approximately 0.1911 at the 100th time step. The presence of several smaller peaks and valleys between the 20th and 50th time steps suggests periodic adjustments and corrections in livestock numbers as households continue to learn and adapt. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates significant increasing trends in the early phases, followed by significant decreasing trends, and eventually, no significant trends towards the end of the simulation. This pattern implies that the initial learning and adaptation phase is crucial, with households making significant adjustments early on, followed by a period of stabilization where fewer adjustments are needed. For decision-makers, this trend highlights the importance of initial conditions and early interventions in guiding households towards sustainable livestock management practices. The stabilization phase suggests that over time, households can achieve a balance closer to the recommended livestock numbers, potentially leading to more sustainable and efficient livestock management.
