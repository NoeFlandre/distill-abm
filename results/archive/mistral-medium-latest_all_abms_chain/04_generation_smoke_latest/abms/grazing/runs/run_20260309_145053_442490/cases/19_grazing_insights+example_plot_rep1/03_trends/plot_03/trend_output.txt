The mean reserve biomass starts at approximately 90,000 units. It initially exhibits a slight increase, reaching a peak around 92,000 units by the 10th time step. Following this peak, there is a notable decline, with the biomass dropping to its lowest point of around 85,000 units by the 30th time step. This decline could be attributed to the initial grazing pressure exerted by livestock as households begin their grazing activities. After reaching this low point, the mean reserve biomass begins to recover steadily. By the 50th time step, it surpasses the initial value and continues to rise, indicating a period of recovery and growth in the pasture biomass. This recovery phase could be due to adaptive management practices and favorable climatic conditions promoting vegetation regrowth. The mean reserve biomass stabilizes around 120,000 units from the 70th time step onwards, suggesting that the grazing system has reached a new equilibrium where biomass production matches the grazing pressure. The stabilization at a higher biomass level implies that the adaptive co-management strategies and learning behaviors of the households have positively influenced the sustainability of the grazing system. For decision-makers, the initial decline highlights the importance of careful management during the early stages of grazing to prevent overuse of pasture resources. The subsequent recovery and stabilization phases suggest that adaptive strategies and learning behaviors can lead to sustainable grazing practices over time.
