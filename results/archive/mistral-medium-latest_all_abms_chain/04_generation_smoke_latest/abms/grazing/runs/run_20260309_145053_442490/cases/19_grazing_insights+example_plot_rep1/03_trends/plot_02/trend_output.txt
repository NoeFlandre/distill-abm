The mean household risk attitude starts with a value of approximately 0.5 across all simulations. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid divergence in the risk attitudes among households. Some households exhibit an increase in risk attitude, reaching values as high as 0.8, while others show a decrease, dropping to values as low as -0.2. This divergence indicates that households are quickly adapting their risk attitudes based on initial observations and interactions within the model. By around 40 time steps, the risk attitudes begin to stabilize, with distinct groups of households settling into different risk attitude ranges. Households with higher risk attitudes tend to stabilize around 0.7 to 0.8, while those with lower risk attitudes stabilize around -0.2 to 0.3. This stabilization suggests that households have reached a steady state in their risk attitudes based on their learning and adaptation processes. The middle range of risk attitudes, around 0.3 to 0.5, shows less variability and stabilizes more quickly, indicating a more consistent adaptation process among these households. The trends in risk attitude provide insights for decision-makers regarding the variability in household risk perceptions and the potential for different groups to exhibit distinct risk management strategies. Understanding these trends can help in designing interventions that cater to the diverse risk attitudes within the community, ultimately supporting more effective and targeted adaptive co-management strategies.
