The trends in the data from the repeated simulations of the agent-based model show distinct patterns in the average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households over 100 time steps. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the mean destock total of households, with values rising steeply from 0 to approximately 60 within the first 20 steps. This initial phase indicates a period of significant adjustment where households are actively destocking livestock, likely in response to initial conditions and early observations of pasture and neighbor behaviors. Following this rapid increase, the rate of destocking slows down, and the curves begin to level off, showing a more gradual increase. Between steps 20 and 60, the mean destock total continues to rise but at a slower pace, reaching values ranging from approximately 80 to 120. This phase suggests a stabilization period where households are fine-tuning their livestock numbers based on ongoing observations and learning. Beyond step 60, the destocking trends further stabilize, with the mean destock total showing minimal increases and converging towards a range of approximately 100 to 140 by step 100. This final phase indicates a long-term equilibrium where households have adapted their livestock numbers to a relatively stable state, reflecting the outcomes of their learning and adaptive strategies over time. The variations among different simulation runs highlight the heterogeneity in household behaviors and the impact of different learning strategies on the destocking process.
