The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households starts at 90. It declines rapidly over the first 20 time steps, reaching a value of approximately 45. This initial decline could be attributed to the households adjusting their livestock numbers in response to initial conditions and constraints. The metric reaches its minimum value of around 41 at time step 37. After this point, the number of livestock begins to increase gradually, showing a steady upward trend from time step 44 onwards. By the end of the simulation at time step 100, the average total number of livestock stabilizes around 47.5. The initial rapid decline suggests that households might be reducing their livestock numbers to align with available resources or environmental conditions. The subsequent gradual increase indicates a period of recovery and adaptation, where households possibly optimize their livestock management practices. For decision makers, the initial sharp decline highlights the importance of preparing for significant adjustments in livestock numbers during the early stages of adaptive management. The later stabilization suggests that long-term strategies can lead to a more balanced and sustainable livestock population.
