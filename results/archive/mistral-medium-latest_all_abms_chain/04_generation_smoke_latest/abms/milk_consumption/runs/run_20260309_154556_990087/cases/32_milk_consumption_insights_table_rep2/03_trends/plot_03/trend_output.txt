The total number of times agents chose whole milk in the simulation shows a consistent increasing trend from 1974 to 2005. The mean value over the entire period is around 1130.88, with a median of 1158.97, indicating a relatively balanced distribution of values around the mean. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a continuous increasing trend throughout the simulation, with significant positive slopes in all tested intervals. This consistent rise suggests that, despite the overall shift towards healthier milk options like skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, whole milk consumption still occurred frequently among the agents. The dominant period of oscillation is around 15 time steps, indicating some periodic fluctuations within the overall increasing trend. For decision makers, this trend implies that while there is a clear movement towards healthier milk choices, whole milk remains a significant part of consumption habits, possibly due to entrenched habits and social influences. This insight can guide public health campaigns to further encourage the adoption of healthier milk options by addressing these underlying factors.
