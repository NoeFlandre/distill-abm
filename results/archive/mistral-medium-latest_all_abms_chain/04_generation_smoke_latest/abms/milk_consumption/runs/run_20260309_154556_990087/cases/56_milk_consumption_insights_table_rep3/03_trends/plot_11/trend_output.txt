The mean deviation between the agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices shows a consistent increasing trend over the simulation period from 1974 to 2005. Initially, the deviation starts at 0.0000 and gradually rises to 0.7251 by the end of the period. The mean deviation across the entire period is 0.4965, with a median of 0.5584, indicating that the deviation tends to be slightly higher than the mean in the latter half of the period. The standard deviation of 0.1702 and variance of 0.0290 suggest moderate variability in the deviation values. The trend analysis reveals a steady increase in deviation, with significant increasing trends detected in multiple rolling Mann-Kendall tests across different time segments. The inflection points at various time steps indicate changes in the rate of increase, but the overall trend remains upward. The oscillation summary suggests a dominant period of 15.00, with strong oscillations observed towards the latter part of the simulation. For decision makers, this trend implies that over time, there is a growing discrepancy between the health values held by agents and the health implications of their milk choices. This could indicate a need for interventions or policies aimed at aligning consumer perceptions more closely with the actual health attributes of their choices.
