The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents in the simulation of milk consumption behaviors shows a consistent increasing trend from 1974 to 2005. Initially, the deviation starts at 0.0000 and gradually rises to 0.7508 by the end of the simulation period. The mean value of this deviation over the entire period is 0.6323, with a median of 0.6729, indicating that the deviation tends to be higher more frequently. The standard deviation of 0.1460 and variance of 0.0213 suggest moderate variability around the mean. The trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a consistent and significant increasing trend throughout the simulation, with varying slopes at different time intervals. The initial increase is more pronounced with a slope of 0.128, which gradually decreases to 0.004 towards the end of the simulation. Inflection points are noted at indices 7, 12, and 28, suggesting changes in the rate of increase at these points. The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of 15.00, with several strong oscillation indices, indicating periodic fluctuations within the overall increasing trend. For decision makers, these trends imply a growing divergence in the choice function scores among agents over time, reflecting increasing diversity in the factors influencing milk consumption choices. This could suggest a need for targeted strategies to address varying consumer preferences and behaviors as they evolve over time.
