The simulation results indicate a consistent and significant increasing trend in the total number of times agents chose skimmed or semi-skimmed milk over the entire period from 1974 to 2005. Initially, the adoption of these milk types starts at zero, reflecting the initial conditions of the model where agents presumably begin with a preference for whole milk. The mean value of the total choices over the simulation period is approximately 11,157, with a median of 10,679, indicating a steady rise in adoption. The standard deviation of 8,241 and variance of 67,919,370 suggest considerable variability in the adoption rates across different time steps. The minimum value remains at zero at the start, while the maximum value reaches 25,643.83 at the end of the simulation, highlighting a substantial shift in milk preferences over time. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent increasing trend across all time windows, with significant slopes ranging from 201.463 to 935.933, indicating a robust and steady adoption pattern. The inflection point at index 31 and the change point at index 32 suggest a notable shift in the adoption rate towards the latter part of the simulation. The dominant oscillation period of 15 and strong oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation period indicate some periodic fluctuations in the adoption rates, possibly reflecting underlying social or behavioral dynamics. For decision makers, these trends imply a clear and sustained shift towards healthier milk options over the studied period, driven by the interplay of perception, habits, and social influence among the agents.
