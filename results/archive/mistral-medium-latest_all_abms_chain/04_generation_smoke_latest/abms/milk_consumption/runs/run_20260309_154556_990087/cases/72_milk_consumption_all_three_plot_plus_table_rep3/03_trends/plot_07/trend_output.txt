The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents starts at approximately 5.29 units. The consumption then increases rapidly, reaching around 1220.21 units by the 11th time step, suggesting a significant shift in consumer preferences towards healthier milk options. This rapid increase could be attributed to the influence of social interactions and the initial health perceptions of alternative milk types. 

Between the 11th and 21st time steps, the consumption quantity stabilizes, fluctuating slightly around the mean value of approximately 1220 units. This period of stabilization indicates that the agents have reached a temporary equilibrium in their milk consumption habits, influenced by their social networks and personal health perceptions. The peaks and valleys during this period show minor variations but no significant trends, reflecting a steady state in consumer behavior. 

After the 25th time step, the mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed begins to increase again, reaching a peak of approximately 1456.60 units by the end of the simulation at the 32nd time step. This second phase of increase suggests a renewed shift towards healthier milk options, possibly due to reinforced social norms and sustained positive health perceptions. The significant increasing trend detected by the rolling Mann-Kendall test in the later time steps supports this observation. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial health perceptions and social influence in driving the adoption of healthier milk options. The rapid initial increase suggests that targeted campaigns and social interactions can significantly impact consumer behavior. The stabilization period indicates that once a certain level of adoption is achieved, consumption habits become more stable, but continuous reinforcement through social norms can lead to further increases in healthier milk consumption.
