The simulation results indicate a consistent and significant upward trend in the total number of times agents chose whole milk over the observed time steps. Starting from an initial value of 0, the metric steadily increases, reaching a peak of approximately 2194.57 at the final time step. The mean value across all time steps is 1130.88, with a median of 1158.97, suggesting a relatively symmetric distribution around the central tendency. The standard deviation of 654.69 and variance of 428622.51 highlight substantial variability in the data. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent increasing trend across all time windows, with significant p-values and high Kendall tau coefficients, indicating strong and statistically significant upward movements throughout the simulation. Inflection points are noted at time steps 1, 7, and 31, suggesting changes in the rate of increase. Additionally, the oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of 15 time steps, with strong oscillations detected towards the end of the simulation period. For decision makers, these trends suggest a growing preference or habit formation towards whole milk among the agents over time. The consistent increase and significant upward trends imply that interventions or policies aimed at influencing milk consumption patterns should consider the strong habitual and social influences driving this behavior.
