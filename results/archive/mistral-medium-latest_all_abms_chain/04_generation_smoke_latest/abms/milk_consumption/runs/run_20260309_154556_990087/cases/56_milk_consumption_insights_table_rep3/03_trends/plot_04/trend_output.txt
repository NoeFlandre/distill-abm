The simulation results show a consistent and significant increasing trend in the total number of times agents chose skimmed or semi-skimmed milk over the entire period from 1974 to 2005. Starting from an initial value of 0, the adoption of these milk types steadily rises, reaching a final value of approximately 25,643.83. The mean number of choices across the simulation period is about 11,157.35, with a median of 10,679.43, indicating a right-skewed distribution. The standard deviation of 8,241.32 and variance of 67,919,370.31 suggest substantial variability in the adoption rates over time. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent increasing trend across various segments of the simulation period, with significant slopes ranging from 201.463 to 935.933, all statistically significant with p-values of 0.027. The model identifies an inflection point at index 31 and a change point at index 32, indicating a notable shift in the adoption rate around these time steps. Additionally, the oscillation summary reveals a dominant period of 15, with strong oscillations detected towards the end of the simulation period. For decision makers, these trends imply a growing preference for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk among the British public over the studied period. The consistent increase suggests that interventions or policies promoting these milk types could be effective, and the variability indicates potential periods of accelerated adoption that may warrant further investigation.
