The simulation results indicate a consistent and significant upward trend in the total number of times agents chose whole milk over the observed time steps. Initially, the value starts at 0 and steadily increases, reaching a peak of approximately 2194.57 at the final time step. The mean value across all time steps is around 1130.88, with a median of 1158.97, suggesting a relatively symmetric distribution of values around the mean. The standard deviation of 654.69 and variance of 428622.51 indicate substantial variability in the data. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent increasing trend across all time windows, with significant slopes ranging from approximately 64 to 76, indicating a steady rise in whole milk consumption choices. Inflection points are noted at time steps 1, 7, and 31, suggesting changes in the rate of increase at these points. Additionally, there is evidence of strong oscillations towards the end of the time series, with a dominant period of 15 time steps. The consistent increase in whole milk consumption choices suggests that, despite the introduction and promotion of alternative milk types, a significant portion of the population continues to prefer whole milk. This trend could imply that initial habits and perceptions are strong determinants of consumer behavior, and that social influence and other factors may not be sufficient to overcome these established preferences. For decision makers, this highlights the importance of understanding deep-rooted consumer habits and the potential challenges in shifting these behaviors through social influence or other interventions.
