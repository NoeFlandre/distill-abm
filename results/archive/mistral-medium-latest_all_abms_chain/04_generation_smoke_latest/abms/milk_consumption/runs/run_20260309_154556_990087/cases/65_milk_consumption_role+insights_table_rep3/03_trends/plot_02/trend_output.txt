The simulation of the agent-based model reveals several key trends in the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent over the observed time period. Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of 5.29. This low initial consumption suggests that at the beginning of the simulation, agents predominantly consumed whole milk, reflecting the initial habit strength set for the incumbent milk type.\n\nThe consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits a rapid increase in the early stages of the simulation. This trend is evident from the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, which shows significant increasing trends in the initial time steps. Specifically, from time steps 0 to 4 and 5 to 9, there is a strong and significant upward trend in consumption, with slopes of approximately 140.433 and 139.015 respectively. This rapid adoption phase indicates a shift in consumer behavior, likely influenced by the parameters related to health perception, social influence, and cognitive dissonance.\n\nFollowing this initial surge, the consumption levels enter a phase of stabilization. From time steps 10 to 24, the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates no significant trends, suggesting that the consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk has reached a plateau. During this period, the consumption values oscillate around a mean of approximately 1220.21, with minor fluctuations. The peaks and valleys identified during this phase, such as the peak at time step 11 with a value of 1265.47 and the valley at time step 12 with a value of 1233.62, indicate minor variations but overall stability in consumption patterns.\n\nTowards the end of the simulation, there is another significant increasing trend observed from time steps 25 to 29. This late-stage increase, with a slope of 48.544, suggests a renewed shift towards higher consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The final consumption value reaches 1456.60, indicating a substantial overall increase from the initial value.\n\nThe dominant period of oscillation is identified as 15 time steps, with several strong oscillation indices scattered throughout the simulation. This periodic behavior suggests recurring patterns in consumer adoption, possibly influenced by social interactions and network effects.\n\nFor decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial habit strength and the impact of social influence and health perceptions on consumer behavior. The rapid adoption phase followed by stabilization and a late-stage increase underscores the potential for targeted interventions to shift consumption patterns significantly. Understanding these dynamics can inform strategies to promote healthier milk choices and leverage social networks to influence consumer decisions.
