The trend in the mean quantity of whole milk consumed by agents shows a significant decline over the simulated time steps. Initially, the mean whole milk quantity starts at approximately 2,600 units. This high initial value reflects the strong initial habit strength for whole milk consumption among the agents, as indicated by the Incumbent-Initial-Habit parameter set to 10.0. 

In the first few time steps, there is a steep decline in the mean whole milk quantity, dropping to around 1,000 units by the 10th time step. This rapid decrease suggests that agents are quickly adopting alternative milk types, influenced by their health and environmental perceptions, social interactions, and susceptibility to social influence. The parameters Alt-Health-Mean-Initial and Alt-Env-Mean-Initial, set to 1.69 and 1.12 respectively, indicate that agents have a moderate initial perception of the health and environmental benefits of alternative milk types. 

Following this steep decline, the rate of decrease in whole milk consumption slows down, stabilizing around 800 units between the 15th and 25th time steps. This stabilization period indicates a phase where the adoption of alternative milk types has slowed, and a significant portion of agents have already switched from whole milk. The parameters Social-Susceptibility and Social-Conformity, set to 0.2 and 0.28 respectively, suggest that agents are somewhat influenced by their social network but not overwhelmingly so. 

After the 25th time step, there is a gradual decline in whole milk consumption, eventually reaching around 400 units by the 30th time step. This gradual decline indicates a continued but slower shift away from whole milk, possibly due to the remaining agents being more resistant to change or having stronger habits and justifications for their choices. The parameters Habit-Threshold and Justification, set to 2.0 and 0.61 respectively, support this interpretation. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial perceptions and social influences in driving the adoption of alternative milk types. The steep initial decline suggests that interventions targeting health and environmental perceptions can be highly effective in the short term. However, the stabilization and gradual decline phases indicate that long-term strategies should also consider habit formation and social conformity to sustain the shift away from whole milk.
