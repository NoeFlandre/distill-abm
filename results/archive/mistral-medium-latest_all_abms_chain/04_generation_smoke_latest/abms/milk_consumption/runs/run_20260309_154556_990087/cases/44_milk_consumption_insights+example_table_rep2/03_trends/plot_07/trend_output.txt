The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents starts at a very low value of 5.29 units. This initial value suggests that at the beginning of the simulation period in 1974, the consumption of these milk types was minimal among the British public. The consumption then increases rapidly, indicating a significant shift in consumer preferences and behaviors. By the end of the simulation period in 2005, the mean quantity reaches 1456.5991 units, showing a substantial rise in the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. This trend reflects a major transition in milk consumption habits over the 31-year period. The rapid increase in the early stages of the simulation could be attributed to growing awareness of health benefits associated with lower-fat milk options, as well as the influence of social networks in spreading new consumption habits. The mean consumption value over the entire period is 1053.2062 units, with a median of 1220.2054 units, indicating that the latter half of the simulation period saw higher consumption levels. The standard deviation of 401.0659 units and variance of 160853.8264 units suggest considerable variability in consumption patterns across different time steps. The data shows several peaks and valleys, with notable peaks at time steps 11, 13, 21, and 19, where consumption values are relatively high. These peaks could correspond to periods where health campaigns or social influences were particularly effective in promoting the consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. Conversely, the valleys at time steps 12, 20, 23, and 18 indicate slight dips in consumption, which might reflect temporary setbacks or market fluctuations. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in the early (0-4 and 5-9) and late (25-29) stages of the simulation, with periods of no significant trend in the middle stages (10-14, 15-19, and 20-24). This suggests that the initial adoption phase and the later consolidation phase were periods of significant change, while the middle period saw more stable consumption patterns. The dominant oscillation period of 15 time steps indicates cyclical patterns in consumption, possibly due to recurring social or marketing influences. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial awareness campaigns and the role of social networks in driving the adoption of healthier milk options. The variability in consumption patterns also suggests the need for continuous engagement strategies to maintain and increase consumption levels over time.
