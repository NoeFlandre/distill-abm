The simulations of the agent-based model show a general trend of increasing mean destock total of households over time. Initially, the mean destock total starts near zero and exhibits a rapid increase within the first 20 steps, reaching approximately 60 to 80 livestock. This initial phase indicates a period of significant adjustment where households are likely responding to immediate ecological and economic pressures by destocking livestock. Following this rapid increase, the rate of destocking slows down, and the curves begin to plateau, suggesting a stabilization phase. Between steps 20 and 100, the mean destock total gradually increases at a slower rate, eventually reaching values between 80 and 160 livestock. This trend implies that households continue to adjust their livestock numbers, but at a more measured pace, possibly reflecting a balance between maintaining livelihoods and adapting to environmental conditions. The variation among different simulation runs indicates diverse outcomes based on the interactions of households with their environment and each other. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial conditions and early interventions in managing livestock numbers. The rapid early destocking suggests that immediate measures may be necessary to stabilize household livelihoods, while the subsequent slower increase indicates the need for long-term strategies to sustainably manage livestock in response to ongoing environmental and social dynamics.
