The biomass reaches its lowest point at around time step 26, with a value of approximately 84,114 units. Following this low point, the trend reverses, and the reserve biomass begins to increase steadily. This increase is significant and consistent from time step 33 onwards, indicating a recovery phase where biomass regeneration outpaces consumption. The biomass continues to rise, reaching its highest value of approximately 121,073 units at the final time step of 100. The steady increase in biomass after the initial decline suggests that the grazing system may have adapted to a sustainable level of consumption, allowing for biomass regeneration and accumulation over time. The presence of strong oscillations, particularly around the middle time steps, indicates periodic fluctuations in biomass levels, which could be due to seasonal variations or adaptive management practices. For decision makers, the initial decline in biomass highlights the importance of careful monitoring and management during the early stages of grazing to prevent overconsumption. The subsequent recovery and steady increase in biomass suggest that adaptive management strategies can lead to sustainable grazing practices, ensuring long-term biomass availability and system resilience.
