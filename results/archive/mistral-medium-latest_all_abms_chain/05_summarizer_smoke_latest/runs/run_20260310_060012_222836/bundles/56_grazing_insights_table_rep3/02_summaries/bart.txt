 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes .  The average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents in the simulation shows distinct trends over the 100 timesteps . For decision makers, these trends imply that initial strategies may lead to increasing deviations from recommended livestock numbers, but over time, adaptive behaviors or external factors may contribute to a reduction of these deviations . Understanding these patterns can help in designing interventions that mitigate early increases in deviation .  The average number of livestock destocked across all households exhibits notable trends over the observed time steps . For decision makers, these trends suggest an initial period of livestock reduction possibly due to environmental or management factors . Understanding these trends can help in planning interventions and policies to manage livestock resources more effectively .  The number of households following the E-RO strategy, which represents non-learning agents, exhibits a general decreasing trend over the observed time steps . The data exhibits strong oscillations with a dominant period of 49 timesteps . The consistent increase in destocked livestock implies that households are continually adjusting their livestock numbers .
