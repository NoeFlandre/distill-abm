This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system. The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies. The model aims to evaluate the long-term social-ecological consequences of different household behavioral types and the impact of various ecological and climatic parameters on the system. The model is applied to smallholder farmers' decision-making, where each pasture is the private property of the household placed on it, and households make decisions based on observations of their pasture and neighbors. The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes. The overall trend indicates an initial period of biomass reduction followed by a recovery phase, suggesting an adaptive response in the grazing system. The variability in the data decreases over time, indicating more stable biomass levels as the system progresses. The strong oscillations observed in certain periods suggest fluctuations in biomass recovery rates, potentially due to varying climatic conditions or grazing patterns.
