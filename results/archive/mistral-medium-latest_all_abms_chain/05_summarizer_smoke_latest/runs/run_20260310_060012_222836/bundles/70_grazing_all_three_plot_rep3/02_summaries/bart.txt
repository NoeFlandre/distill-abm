 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning . The model is applied to smallholder farmers' decision-making, where each pasture is the private property of the household placed on it .  The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system starts at approximately 90,000 units . Within the first 20 steps, there is a slight decline followed by a sharp decrease, reaching a minimum value of around 85,000 . After step 60, most households' risk attitudes converge to a range between 0.2 and 0.4 . This convergence suggests a collective adaptation towards a moderate risk attitude . Social learning and adaptive management strategies can lead to more uniform and potentially sustainable decision-making processes .  The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase within the first 20 time steps . This initial surge indicates a quick adoption of social learning behaviors among households . By around 40 time steps, the Gini coefficient shows a more pronounced increase in livestock health distribution . This trend suggests certain households are becoming significantly more successful in maintaining healthy livestock compared to others .  A few groups of non-learning households show a rapid decline to near zero within the first 20 steps . The remaining groups exhibit more gradual changes, with some fluctuations but generally maintaining their numbers within specific ranges . Understanding the factors that lead to the quick adoption of learning strategies can inform policies aimed at promoting adaptive management practices .
