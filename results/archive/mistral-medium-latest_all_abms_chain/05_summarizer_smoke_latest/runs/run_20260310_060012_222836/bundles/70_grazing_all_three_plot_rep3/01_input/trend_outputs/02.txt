The mean household risk attitude starts with a value of approximately 0.5, indicating a neutral risk attitude at the beginning of the simulation. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid divergence in risk attitudes among households. Some households increase their risk attitude to around 0.8, while others decrease it to approximately -0.2. This divergence suggests that households quickly adapt their risk attitudes based on initial observations and interactions within the grazing system. Between time steps 20 and 40, the risk attitudes stabilize somewhat, with households maintaining their respective risk attitudes with minor fluctuations. This stabilization period indicates a phase where households have gathered sufficient information and are making more consistent decisions. After time step 40, there is a noticeable convergence in risk attitudes. Households with higher risk attitudes gradually decrease their values, while those with lower risk attitudes increase theirs. By time step 60, most households' risk attitudes converge to a range between 0.2 and 0.4. This convergence suggests a collective adaptation towards a moderate risk attitude, possibly due to shared learning and social influences within the community. From time step 60 to the end of the simulation at time step 100, the risk attitudes remain relatively stable with minor fluctuations. This stability indicates that households have reached a steady state in their risk attitudes, reflecting a balanced approach to managing their livestock and pastures. For decision makers, the initial rapid divergence highlights the importance of early interventions to guide household risk attitudes towards sustainable practices. The subsequent convergence towards moderate risk attitudes suggests that social learning and adaptive management strategies can lead to more uniform and potentially sustainable decision-making processes over time.
