The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households starts at approximately 90. This high initial value indicates that households begin with a substantial livestock base, which is crucial for their livelihood and economic stability. There is a rapid decline in the first 20 steps, dropping to around 45. This sharp decrease suggests an initial adjustment period where households might be destocking due to immediate environmental pressures or initial strategy implementations. Between steps 20 and 40, the number of livestock stabilizes around 45, indicating a period of equilibrium where households have adapted their livestock numbers to sustainable levels given the environmental and economic conditions. After step 40, there is a gradual increase in livestock numbers, reaching approximately 60 by step 100. This trend suggests that households are gradually rebuilding their livestock herds, possibly due to improved management strategies, better environmental conditions, or successful adaptation to the initial pressures. The variability among simulation runs increases slightly after step 40, indicating that different households or different conditions lead to varied outcomes in livestock management. This variability highlights the importance of adaptive strategies and the impact of individual household decisions on livestock numbers. For decision-makers, the initial rapid decline underscores the need for robust initial strategies to manage livestock during early periods of environmental or economic stress. The subsequent stabilization and gradual increase suggest that long-term planning and adaptive management can lead to recovery and growth in livestock numbers, which is vital for the sustainability of pastoral systems.
