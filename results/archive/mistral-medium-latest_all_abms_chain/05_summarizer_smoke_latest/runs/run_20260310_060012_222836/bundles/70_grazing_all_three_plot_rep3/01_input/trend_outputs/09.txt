The mean destock total of households starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 20 steps, reaching approximately 60 livestock. This initial rise indicates an immediate response to initial conditions or stressors in the grazing system, prompting households to reduce their livestock numbers quickly. Following this rapid increase, the rate of destocking slows down and the curves begin to level off, showing a more gradual increase. By around 40 steps, the mean destock total reaches about 80 livestock, suggesting a period of stabilization where households adjust to the new conditions and destock at a slower rate. Beyond 40 steps, the destocking continues at a steady but slower pace, with the mean destock total reaching approximately 100 livestock by 100 steps. The variance among simulation runs increases slightly over time, indicating that while the general trend of destocking is consistent, individual household decisions and external factors introduce some variability. This trend suggests that households are continuously adapting their livestock management strategies in response to ongoing conditions, with significant initial adjustments followed by more measured changes. For decision makers, this implies that interventions or policies aimed at supporting households should consider the immediate need for support during the initial rapid destocking phase, as well as ongoing assistance to manage the slower, steady adjustments that follow.
