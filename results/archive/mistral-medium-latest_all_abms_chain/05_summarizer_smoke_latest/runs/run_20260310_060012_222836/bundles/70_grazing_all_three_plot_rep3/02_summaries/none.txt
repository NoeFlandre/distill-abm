This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system. The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies. The model aims to evaluate the long-term social-ecological consequences of different household behavioral types and the impact of various ecological and climatic parameters on the system. The model is applied to smallholder farmers' decision-making, where each pasture is the private property of the household placed on it, and households make decisions based on observations of their pasture and neighbors. The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes. The model parameters are categorized into extension model parameters and original model parameters used by the extension model. The extension model introduces new behavioral types and allows for the study of strategy diffusion in a heterogeneous population of learning and non-learning agents. The model also incorporates a risk parameter to represent agents' dispositions to act under uncertainty. The context and goals of the model are to understand the implications of behavioral change for the resilience of pastoral systems and to evaluate the impact of various learning strategies and social learning on the system's performance. The key parameters of the model are as follows: number-households (range: 1.0 to 100.0, set to 60), satisficing-threshold (range: 0.0 to 100.0, set to 0), satisficing-trials (range: 0.0 to 100.0, set to 21), knowledge-radius (range: 0.0 to 5.0, set to 1), resting-threshold (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0.1), intrinsic-preference (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0), social-susceptibility (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0), global-rain? (set to True), start-on-homepatch? (set to True), resting? (set to False), homog-behav-types? (set to True), extension-model? (set to True), SL2-strategy-switch? (set to False), risk-mode? (set to True), behavioral-type (set to E-RO), number-SAT (set to 0), number-E-RO (set to 20), number-TRAD (set to 0), b (set to 0), number-MAX (set to 0), rain-mean (set to 200), risk-att-init (set to 0.5), gr1 (set to 0.5), number-E-LBD (set to 20), gr2 (set to 0.1), number-E-RO-SL1 (set to 20), timesteps (set to 100), w (set to 0.8), number-random (set to 0), livestock-init (set to 90), and rain-std (set to 100).

The mean household initial risk attitude remains constant throughout the simulation, indicating that the initial disposition to deviate from the recommended number of livestock does not change over time for social learning agents. The values of the mean household initial risk attitude are distributed across a range from approximately -0.1 to 0.17, suggesting a varied initial disposition among households. This range of values implies that while some households are initially more risk-averse, others are more inclined to take risks regarding livestock management. The consistency of these values over time suggests stability in the initial risk attitudes of households, which could be crucial for decision-makers to understand the baseline risk perceptions within the community. This stability can help in predicting household behaviors and designing interventions that consider the diverse risk attitudes present in the population. The lack of change over time also indicates that external factors or learning processes within the simulation do not significantly alter the initial risk attitudes of households, providing a clear picture of the inherent risk dispositions that decision-makers need to account for in their strategies.

The mean household risk attitude starts with a value of approximately 0.5, indicating a neutral risk attitude at the beginning of the simulation. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid divergence in risk attitudes among households. Some households increase their risk attitude to around 0.8, while others decrease it to approximately -0.2. This divergence suggests that households quickly adapt their risk attitudes based on initial observations and interactions within the grazing system. Between time steps 20 and 40, the risk attitudes stabilize somewhat, with households maintaining their respective risk attitudes with minor fluctuations. This stabilization period indicates a phase where households have gathered sufficient information and are making more consistent decisions. After time step 40, there is a noticeable convergence in risk attitudes. Households with higher risk attitudes gradually decrease their values, while those with lower risk attitudes increase theirs. By time step 60, most households' risk attitudes converge to a range between 0.2 and 0.4. This convergence suggests a collective adaptation towards a moderate risk attitude, possibly due to shared learning and social influences within the community. From time step 60 to the end of the simulation at time step 100, the risk attitudes remain relatively stable with minor fluctuations. This stability indicates that households have reached a steady state in their risk attitudes, reflecting a balanced approach to managing their livestock and pastures. For decision makers, the initial rapid divergence highlights the importance of early interventions to guide household risk attitudes towards sustainable practices. The subsequent convergence towards moderate risk attitudes suggests that social learning and adaptive management strategies can lead to more uniform and potentially sustainable decision-making processes over time.

The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system starts at approximately 90,000 units. Over the first 20 steps, there is a slight decline followed by a sharp decrease, reaching a minimum value of around 85,000 units. This initial decline could be attributed to the immediate consumption of biomass by livestock as households begin their grazing activities. Following this decline, there is a rapid increase in mean reserve biomass starting around step 20, continuing steeply upwards until approximately step 60, where it stabilizes around 120,000 units. This sharp increase suggests a period of significant biomass regrowth, potentially due to favorable climatic conditions or effective grazing management practices that allow vegetation to recover. After step 60, the mean reserve biomass levels off and remains relatively stable for the remainder of the simulation, indicating that the system has reached a dynamic equilibrium where biomass consumption by livestock is balanced by regrowth. The stabilization of biomass at a higher level than the initial value suggests that the grazing system has adapted to sustain higher levels of reserve biomass over time. For decision makers, this trend implies that initial grazing pressures can lead to a temporary decline in biomass, but with appropriate management and favorable conditions, the system can recover and stabilize at higher biomass levels, indicating resilience in the grazing system.
