The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits several distinct trends over the simulated time period. Initially, the number of births starts at zero and gradually increases. The early phase of the simulation, up to around the 927th tick, shows a steady but slow increase in births, with a significant upward trend detected by the rolling Mann-Kendall test. This trend continues and becomes more pronounced between the 928th and 1159th ticks, where the rate of increase becomes slightly steeper. From the 1160th to the 1391st tick, the upward trend persists but at a reduced rate. A notable peak in the number of births occurs around the 1673rd tick, reaching a maximum value of 5.6. Following this peak, there is a significant change point around the 1850th tick, after which the trend shifts to a decreasing pattern. This downward trend is evident from the 1624th to the 1855th tick and continues until the 2087th tick, indicating a reduction in the cumulative number of births. The simulation also exhibits strong oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of 50 ticks, suggesting regular fluctuations in the birth rate throughout the simulation. Overall, the dynamics of the birth count show initial growth, a peak, and subsequent decline, with notable change points and oscillatory patterns throughout the simulation period.
