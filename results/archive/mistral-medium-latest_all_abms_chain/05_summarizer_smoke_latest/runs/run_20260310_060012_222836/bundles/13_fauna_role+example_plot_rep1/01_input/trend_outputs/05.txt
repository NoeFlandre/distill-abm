The cumulative number of postpartum females killed starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase suggests that postpartum females are highly vulnerable to predation immediately after giving birth, likely due to their reduced mobility and increased visibility. Around the 500-step mark, the rate of increase begins to slow, indicating that either the initial susceptible population has been significantly reduced or that the remaining postpartum females have adapted or found safer environments. By approximately 1000 steps, the cumulative number of postpartum females killed reaches a plateau around 120, suggesting that the rate of predation has stabilized and fewer postpartum females are being targeted. This plateau could be due to a balance being reached between the birth rate of new offspring and the predation rate, or it could indicate that the forager bands have shifted their focus to other prey types. The variance among simulation runs is relatively low initially but increases slightly as the number of steps progresses, indicating some variability in the dynamics of predation and prey adaptation across different simulations.
