The total number of animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a consistent and significant decreasing trend over time. Initially, the number of animals starts at approximately 166.67 and rapidly declines to near zero by the end of the simulation period. The decline is steep at the beginning, with the number of animals dropping sharply within the first few hundred steps. This rapid decrease continues, albeit at a slower rate, as the simulation progresses. Key time points indicate notable changes in the rate of decline, such as around steps 125, 475, 625, and 875, where the slope of the decrease changes, suggesting different phases in the extinction process. The statistical analysis reveals that the overall trend is consistently decreasing, with significant Mann-Kendall test results indicating strong negative trends across various segments of the simulation. The presence of minor peaks and valleys suggests brief periods of slight recovery or stabilization, but these are not sustained, and the overall trajectory remains a decline to extinction. For decision makers, these trends highlight the sensitivity of the animal population to the model parameters and the potential for rapid extinction under the given conditions. The insights suggest that even with varying parameters, the model consistently leads to a significant reduction in animal numbers, emphasizing the critical impact of hunting pressure and environmental factors on megaherbivore populations.
