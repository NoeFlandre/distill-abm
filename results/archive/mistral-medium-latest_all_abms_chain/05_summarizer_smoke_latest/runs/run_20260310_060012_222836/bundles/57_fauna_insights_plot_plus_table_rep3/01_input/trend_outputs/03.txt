The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the number of males killed, starting from zero and rising sharply. This steep increase continues until around 500 steps, where the rate of increase begins to slow down. By approximately 1000 steps, the cumulative number of males killed reaches a plateau, stabilizing around 120 males. This plateau indicates a phase where the number of males killed remains relatively constant, with minor fluctuations. The data shows that the maximum number of males killed is around 120, with slight variations but no significant further increase. The initial rapid increase suggests a high hunting pressure at the beginning of the simulation, which gradually stabilizes as the system reaches an equilibrium. The stabilization phase implies that the hunting pressure and the prey population dynamics have reached a balance, where the number of males killed does not significantly change over time. For decision-makers, this trend indicates that initial hunting pressure can lead to a rapid reduction in the male prey population, but over time, the system may stabilize, suggesting the importance of monitoring and managing hunting practices to maintain ecological balance.
