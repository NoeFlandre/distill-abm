 An agent-based model (ABM) is a computational framework used to simulate the actions and interactions of autonomous agents to assess their effects on the system as a whole . The model consists of 1,000 agents, each representing an adult consumer . These agents are part of a social network and make decisions based on various parameters that influence their milk choice .  The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent starts at a very low value of approximately 5.29 units . The consumption then increases rapidly over the first 10 steps, reaching around 1265.47 units . This sharp rise suggests a significant shift in consumer behavior, likely driven by increasing awareness and adoption of healthier milk alternatives .  Total number of milk choices made by all agents combined starts at 0 and increases linearly over the 33 time steps, reaching a final value of 32,000 . This consistent linear growth indicates a steady accumulation of decisions made by the agents over time, reflecting a continuous and uniform engagement in the decision-making process regarding milk consumption .  The mean habit factor for whole milk starts at a baseline value of 1.0000 and exhibits a consistent upward trend throughout the simulation period . The rolling Mann-Kendall test results consistently show increasing trends across all time windows, with a significant p-value of 0.027 . Understanding this gradual increase in habit strength can help in designing more effective interventions and policies .  The mean deviation between the agents' held environmental values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.0000 and increases rapidly in the first few steps . The trend of increasing mean value health deviation implies that agents are continually adjusting their behaviors based on social interactions and personal evaluations . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions and campaigns that leverage social networks and reinforce positive health habits .
