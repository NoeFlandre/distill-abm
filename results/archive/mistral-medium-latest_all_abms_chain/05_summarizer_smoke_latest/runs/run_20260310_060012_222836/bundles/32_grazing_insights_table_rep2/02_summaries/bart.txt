 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes .  There were no fluctuations, peaks, or valleys in the data, indicating a complete lack of variability . The average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents exhibits distinct trends over the 100 timesteps . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of managing initial grazing pressures and understanding the resilience of the grazing system .  Initially, the number of livestock starts at 90.0000. There is a consistent and significant decreasing trend in the early time steps, with the most substantial drop occurring between time steps 0 and 32 . The trend stabilizes briefly around time step 33 to 43, showing no significant trend . For decision makers, these trends highlight the initial struggle in maintaining livestock numbers, possibly due to environmental or management challenges .  Trend in the average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households shows a consistent increase over the entire simulation period of 100 time steps . Trend suggests a progressive reduction in livestock numbers over time, which could have implications for household livelihoods and pasture management strategies . The steady increase in destocking may reflect adaptive behaviors in response to changing ecological or climatic conditions .
