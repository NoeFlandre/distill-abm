The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands exhibits a rapid increase initially, followed by a gradual plateau. Between 500 and 1000 steps, the rate of increase begins to slow, with the number of males killed reaching around 100 to 110. Beyond 1000 steps, the trend continues to flatten, and by around 1500 steps, it stabilizes, indicating that the number of males killed has reached a plateau at approximately 120. This stabilization suggests that the hunting pressure has balanced out, possibly due to a reduction in the male prey population or other limiting factors in the environment. For decision makers, the initial rapid increase in the number of males killed suggests that early intervention strategies might be crucial to prevent significant reductions in the male prey population. The eventual plateau indicates that there may be natural limits to hunting success, which could inform sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts.
