 The MHPM is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbivores . It incorporates various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, sexual dimorphism, and life history of animals . The model allows users to manipulate parameters to establish socio-natural dynamics and observe how these interactions influence extinction events .  The model's landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, consisting of a 211x211 patch matrix . The temporal units are 'ticks,' which can be scaled to any temporal granularity . Hunters and animals move and interact based on the set parameters, and the model provides summary statistics such as the number of prey items taken, the proportion of grazed to ungrazed grass .  The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population starts at zero and remains at this level until around 700 time steps . After this initial period of no births, the number of deaths begins to plateau, indicating a stabilization in the death rate . The overall trend indicates an initial high mortality rate that stabilizes over time, reflecting a dynamic equilibrium between prey population and predation pressure .  The simulation depicts a scenario where the female prey population undergoes a rapid initial decline followed by a more gradual decrease, ultimately leading to near-extinction . The total number of animals in the simulation starts at approximately 167 and declines rapidly over the first 500 time steps . The steep drop suggests that the initial conditions are highly stressful for the animal population, leading to a rapid reduction in their numbers .  Between 1000 and 2000 time steps, the total energy of the prey animals approaches zero . This suggests that the prey population has been nearly or completely depleted . The system exhibits minor fluctuations but generally maintains a steady state . The percentage of green patches in the landscape starts at approximately 33% and remains relatively stable throughout the simulation .
