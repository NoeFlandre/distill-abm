The simulation of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decline in the number of male prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at 125 male prey animals and exhibits a steady decrease throughout the simulation, ultimately reaching zero by the end of the observed period. The mean number of male prey animals across the simulation is approximately 21, with a median of about 1.35, indicating a skewed distribution with most values being relatively low. The standard deviation of 36.04 and variance of 1299.18 suggest significant variability in the population numbers at different time steps. Key time points show notable values, such as a peak at tick 730 with 10.54 male prey animals and subsequent smaller peaks and valleys, indicating minor fluctuations amidst the overall downward trend. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results indicate significant decreasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with periods of no significant trend observed later on. The change points identified at ticks 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, 875, and 2310 suggest shifts in the rate of decline. The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of 63 ticks, indicating cyclical patterns within the data. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the continuous pressure on the male prey population, leading to eventual extinction in the simulated environment. This suggests that under the given parameters, the hunting pressure and environmental conditions are not sustainable for the male prey population over the long term.
