The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits several distinct trends and patterns over the simulation period. Initially, the number of births starts at zero and gradually increases. The mean number of births across the simulation is approximately 2.18, with a median of 2.49, indicating a relatively balanced distribution around these central values. The standard deviation of 1.83 suggests moderate variability in the number of births over time. The minimum number of births remains at zero at the start of the simulation, while the maximum peaks at 5.6 around the midpoint of the simulation. Key peaks in the number of births are observed at various points, notably at simulation steps 1702, 1392, 1214, and 1073, with values of 5.6, 4.3, 3.45, and 2.875 respectively. These peaks are followed by valleys, indicating periods of lower birth rates, such as at simulation steps 1422, 1281, and 1097, with values of approximately 3.89, 3.06, and 2.87 respectively. The rolling Mann-Kendall test identifies significant increasing trends in the number of births from simulation steps 696 to 1623, with varying strengths of increase. This is followed by significant decreasing trends from simulation steps 1624 to 2087. Change points are detected at simulation steps 725, 1325, 1850, 2050, and 2310, indicating shifts in the underlying dynamics of the birth rates. The oscillation summary reveals a dominant period of 50 simulation steps, with strong oscillations observed around simulation steps 625 to 810. For decision-makers, these trends suggest that the prey animal population experiences periods of growth and decline in birth rates, influenced by the model's parameters and interactions. Understanding these patterns can help in assessing the impact of hunting pressure and environmental factors on the sustainability of the prey population.
