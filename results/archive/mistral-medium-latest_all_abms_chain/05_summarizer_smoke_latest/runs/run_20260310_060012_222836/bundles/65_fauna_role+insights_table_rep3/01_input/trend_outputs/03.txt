The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows distinct trends over the simulation period. As the simulation progresses, the rate of increase in the number of males killed begins to slow down. Between ticks 1160 and 1391, the trend stabilizes, indicating no significant change in the number of males hunted. This plateau suggests a potential equilibrium where the hunting pressure is balanced by the remaining male prey population's ability to sustain or evade predation. Following this stable period, there is a brief resurgence in the hunting trend, with a significant increase observed again between ticks 1392 and 1623, although the slope of this increase is much gentler compared to the initial phase.

This decline is statistically significant and suggests a reduction in the availability of male prey or a decrease in hunting efficiency. The data also exhibits periodic oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, indicating regular fluctuations in the hunting success rate. These oscillations become more pronounced towards the end of the simulation, suggesting cyclic patterns in prey availability or hunter success rates.

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the dynamic interplay between hunting pressure and prey population sustainability. The initial rapid increase in hunting success may inform strategies on early intervention or monitoring. The subsequent stabilization and eventual decline could indicate the need for adaptive management practices to maintain ecological balance and prevent potential prey population collapse.
