 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005, focusing on the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk . Agents represent adult consumers who make milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics, modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices .  The number of choices made by all agents combined starts at 0 and increases linearly over the 30 steps . The total number of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk choices starts at zero and increases gradually over the first 5 steps . From step 15 to step 30, the growth continues but at a slower, more linear rate, eventually reaching around 25,000 choices .  The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed starts at a low value near zero . It then increases rapidly over the first 10 steps, reaching approximately 1,200 units . At step 25, there is a noticeable increase in consumption, reaching 1,400 units by step 30 . This second rise could be attributed to additional factors such as renewed health campaigns, increased social influence, or changes in market availability .  The mean value environmental deviation starts at a low value near zero, indicating that initially, the agents' health values align closely with their milk choices . Over the first 10 steps, there is a rapid increase in the mean value health deviation, reaching approximately 0.4 . This sharp rise suggests that as agents begin to make milk choices, their perceptions of health values start to diverge significantly from their environmental values .
