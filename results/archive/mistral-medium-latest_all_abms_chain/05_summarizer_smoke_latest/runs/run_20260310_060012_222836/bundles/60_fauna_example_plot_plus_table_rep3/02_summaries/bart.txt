 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to megaherbivore extinction . It considers various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and life history . The landscape is a simplified grassland environment with patches of grass, ungulates, and human hunters .  The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and increases rapidly over the first 500 time steps . The rate of increase begins to slow down around 1000 time steps, indicating a reduction in the number of available male prey due to hunting pressure and possibly environmental factors . The system has reached a dynamic equilibrium where hunting pressure is balanced by the prey's reproductive capacity and environmental carrying capacity . The total number of female prey animals follows a similar pattern across multiple simulation runs . The system's complexity introduces more variability as time progresses .  The total energy of all animals in the simulation begins at approximately 16,667 units . This value experiences a rapid decline within the first 500 time steps, dropping to around 4,000 units . The variability in the energy levels increases as the simulation progresses, suggesting that the interactions between the animals and their environment become more complex over time .
