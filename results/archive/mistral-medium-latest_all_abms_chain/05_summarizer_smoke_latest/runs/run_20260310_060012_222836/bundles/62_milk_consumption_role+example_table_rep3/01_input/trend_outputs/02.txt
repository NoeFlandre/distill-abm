The consumption then exhibits a rapid and significant increase over the early time steps, reaching a peak of approximately 1265.47 around the 11th time step. This sharp rise indicates a substantial shift in consumer behavior, likely driven by increasing awareness and acceptance of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. 

Following this peak, the consumption levels exhibit some fluctuations but generally stabilize around a high value. Notable peaks are observed at time steps 13, 21, and 19, with values around 1240.35, 1226.86, and 1221.57, respectively. These peaks suggest periods of heightened consumption, possibly due to marketing campaigns, social influences, or other external factors promoting the consumption of these milk types. 

The valleys observed at time steps 12, 20, 23, and 18, with values around 1233.62, 1214.46, 1209.70, and 1183.39, respectively, indicate temporary declines in consumption. These declines could be attributed to various factors such as market saturation, temporary shifts in consumer preferences, or external economic conditions.

The later time steps (25-29) also show an increasing trend, albeit with a smaller slope, indicating a continued but slower growth in consumption. The periods with no significant trend (10-14, 15-19, and 20-24) suggest phases of stabilization where consumption levels plateau. 

The dominant oscillation period of 15.00 and the strong oscillation indices suggest that there are regular fluctuations in consumption patterns, possibly due to cyclical consumer behaviors or periodic external influences.
