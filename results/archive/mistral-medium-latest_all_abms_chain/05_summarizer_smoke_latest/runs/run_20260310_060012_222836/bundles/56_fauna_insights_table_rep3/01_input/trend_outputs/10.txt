The simulation of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decreasing trend in the total number of prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at approximately 166.67 animals and gradually declines to zero by the end of the simulation period. The decline is marked by several key phases and inflection points, indicating varying rates of decrease. Early in the simulation, from the start to around tick 730, there is a notable peak where the population briefly stabilizes around 11.37 animals before continuing its decline. This period is followed by a series of smaller peaks and valleys, suggesting periodic fluctuations in the population. Significant change points are observed at ticks 125, 475, 625, and 875, where the rate of decline shifts. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates significant decreasing trends throughout most of the simulation, with brief periods of increase around ticks 1392-1623 and 1856-2087. The oscillation summary reveals a dominant period of 63 ticks, indicating regular intervals of population fluctuation. For decision makers, these trends suggest that under the given parameters, the prey population is under consistent pressure leading to eventual extinction. The periodic fluctuations and brief stabilization phases may indicate temporary equilibriums or responses to environmental and hunting pressures, but the overall trajectory is a steady decline. This information can be crucial for understanding the dynamics of megaherbivore extinction and the impact of various factors modeled in the MHPM.
