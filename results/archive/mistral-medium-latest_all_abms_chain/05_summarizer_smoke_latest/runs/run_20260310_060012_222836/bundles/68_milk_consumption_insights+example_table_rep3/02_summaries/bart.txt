 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model explores how perception, habits, and social influence affect individual decision-making processes regarding milk choices . The main outcome is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person, using data for calibration against observed macro-level data .  The total number of milk choices made by all agents combined starts at 0 and increases steadily over the entire simulation period, reaching a final value of 32,000 . The trend can be attributed to the growing adoption of healthier milk options among the British public, driven by increasing awareness of health and environmental benefits .  The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents starts at a very low value of approximately 5.29 units . Consumption then increases rapidly, indicating a significant shift in consumer preferences and behaviors . The overall trend is characterized by a steep increase in the early time steps, followed by periods of stabilization and minor fluctuations .  The mean deviation between the agents' health values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.0000 and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period . This trend indicates a growing discrepancy between the perceived health values of milk types and the actual choices made by the agents over time . For decision makers, this trend highlights the importance of understanding the factors that contribute to this growing discrepancy .
