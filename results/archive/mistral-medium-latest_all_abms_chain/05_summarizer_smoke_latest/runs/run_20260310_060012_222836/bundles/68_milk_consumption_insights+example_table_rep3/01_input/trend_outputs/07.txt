This initial value suggests that at the beginning of the simulation period, the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk was minimal. The consumption then increases rapidly, indicating a significant shift in consumer preferences and behaviors. By the end of the simulation, the mean quantity reaches approximately 1456.60 units, showing a substantial rise in the adoption of these milk types over the studied period. The overall trend is characterized by a steep increase in the early time steps, followed by periods of stabilization and minor fluctuations. Key inflection points and peaks suggest intervals of accelerated adoption and temporary plateaus, reflecting the complex interplay of social influence, habit formation, and perception changes among the agents. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates significant increasing trends in the early and later stages of the simulation, with periods of no significant trend in the middle stages, suggesting phases of rapid adoption followed by stabilization. The dominant oscillation period of 15 time steps highlights recurring patterns in the adoption behavior, possibly due to cyclical social influences or periodic reassessment of habits. For decision makers, these trends underscore the importance of initial adoption phases and the potential for periodic interventions to sustain long-term behavioral changes in milk consumption.
