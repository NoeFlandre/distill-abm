The total energy of all animals starts at approximately 25,000 units. This high initial value indicates a large and healthy population of prey animals with substantial energy reserves. This sharp decrease suggests a significant reduction in the energy reserves of the prey population, potentially due to high predation pressure, limited food availability, or a combination of both. Between 500 and 1,000 time steps, the decline continues but at a slower rate, stabilizing around 1,000 units of total energy. This period of slower decline indicates that the remaining prey animals are either more resilient, better at avoiding predation, or that the predation pressure has decreased due to a reduction in the prey population. After 1,000 time steps, the total energy levels off and remains close to zero for the remainder of the simulation. This stabilization at near-zero energy levels suggests that the prey population has been nearly or completely depleted, with very few animals remaining in the environment. The trends indicate that the initial conditions and parameters of the model lead to a rapid and substantial decline in the energy reserves of the prey population, ultimately resulting in a near-complete depletion of prey animals. For decision-makers, this implies that under the given parameters, the prey population is not sustainable in the long term, and significant interventions may be required to prevent the collapse of the prey population.
