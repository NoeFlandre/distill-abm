The percentage of green patches starts at approximately 32% and remains stable for the first 200 time steps. This initial stability suggests that the grass patches are not immediately affected by the presence of herbivores or other factors, indicating a period of equilibrium in the ecosystem. Around time step 200, there is a sharp decline in the percentage of green patches, dropping to about 25%. This decline could be attributed to increased grazing pressure from the megaherbivores as their population stabilizes and they consume more grass. Following this decline, the percentage of green patches quickly recovers to around 32% by time step 500. This recovery suggests a period of regrowth and regeneration of grass patches, possibly due to seasonal changes or reduced grazing pressure. The percentage of green patches remains relatively stable again until around time step 1000, where another sharp decline is observed, dropping to approximately 25%. This second decline could indicate another phase of increased grazing or environmental stress on the grass patches. After this decline, the percentage of green patches recovers once more to around 32% by time step 1500, indicating another period of regrowth and ecosystem recovery. From time step 1500 to the end of the simulation, the percentage of green patches remains stable at around 32%, suggesting a long-term equilibrium in the availability of grass patches despite periodic fluctuations. For decision makers, these trends highlight the resilience of the grassland ecosystem to periodic grazing pressures and the importance of understanding the cyclical nature of grass regrowth and consumption. This information can be crucial for managing grazing lands and ensuring the sustainability of both the herbivore populations and the vegetation they rely on.
