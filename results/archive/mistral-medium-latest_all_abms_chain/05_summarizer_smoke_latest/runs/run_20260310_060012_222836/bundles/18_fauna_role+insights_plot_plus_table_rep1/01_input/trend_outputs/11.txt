The total energy of male animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent downward trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12,500 units. This high energy level rapidly declines within the first few hundred time steps, dropping to approximately 1,300 units by around 500 time steps. This initial phase of rapid decline is followed by a more gradual decrease, with the total energy continuing to diminish steadily. By around 2,000 time steps, the total energy of male animals approaches zero and remains there for the rest of the simulation. Throughout the simulation, there are minor fluctuations and small peaks, but the overall trend is a significant and continuous decline in the total energy of male animals. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend with significant Mann-Kendall test results across various intervals, indicating a consistent reduction in energy levels. The implications for decision-makers suggest that under the given parameters, the male animal population experiences a severe and sustained decline in energy, which could be indicative of increased hunting pressure, environmental stress, or a combination of factors leading to a potential extinction scenario. This trend highlights the need for careful consideration of hunting practices and environmental management to ensure the sustainability of the male animal population.
