The number of female prey animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a consistent and significant decreasing trend over time. Initially, the number of female prey animals starts at 125 and rapidly declines within the first few hundred time steps. This sharp decline continues, albeit at a slightly reduced rate, until around 2000 time steps, where the number of female prey animals approaches zero. Throughout the simulation, there are minor fluctuations and small peaks, but the overall trend remains a steady decrease. Key time points include the initial rapid decline within the first 500 time steps and the continued decline with minor oscillations up to 2000 time steps. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend with significant Mann-Kendall test results across various intervals, indicating a strong and consistent decline in the number of female prey animals. The implications for decision-makers suggest that under the current model parameters, the population of female prey animals is highly susceptible to extinction due to the combined pressures of hunting and environmental factors. This trend highlights the potential need for interventions to mitigate the decline and preserve the population of megaherbivores.
