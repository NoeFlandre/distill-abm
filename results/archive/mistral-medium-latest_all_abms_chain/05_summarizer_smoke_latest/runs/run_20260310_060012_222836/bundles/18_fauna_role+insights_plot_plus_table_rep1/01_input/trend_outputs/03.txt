The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the number of males killed, starting from 0 and rising sharply. This steep rise continues until around 500 steps, where the number of males killed reaches approximately 100. Following this, the rate of increase slows down, and the number of males killed gradually approaches an asymptote near 120. This trend stabilizes around 1000 steps, with minor fluctuations observed thereafter. The data shows that the maximum number of males killed is around 120, with very slight variations and no significant trends in the later stages of the simulation. The inflection points and change points indicate shifts in the rate of increase, particularly around 500 steps, where the trend starts to plateau. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms these observations, showing significant increasing trends initially, followed by periods of no significant trend and a slight decreasing trend towards the end. The dominant period of oscillation suggests minor periodic fluctuations in the later stages. For decision makers, these trends imply that the initial phase of hunting pressure is intense, leading to a rapid increase in the number of males killed. However, over time, this pressure stabilizes, indicating a potential equilibrium in the hunting dynamics or a saturation point in the hunting efficiency of the forager bands.
