 The MHPM is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbivores . It incorporates various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, sexual dimorphism, and life history of animals . The model allows users to manipulate parameters to establish socio-natural dynamics and observe how these interactions influence extinction events .  The model's landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, consisting of a 211x211 patch matrix . The number of human hunter-gatherer bands in the simulation remains constant throughout the entire duration . Initially, there are 20 bands, and this number does not change over time, maintaining a steady value of 20 .  The number of postpartum females killed in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model exhibits a rapid increase in the early stages of the simulation, followed by a gradual stabilization . The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates that the initial phases are characterized by strong increasing trends, which gradually weaken as the curve approaches the plateau . The final phase shows a slight decreasing trend, although it is not as pronounced as the initial increases .  The number of female prey animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear trend of decreasing total number of animals over time . By approximately 2,300 steps, the number of male animals reaches zero, indicating complete extinction within the simulation timeframe . Peaks and valleys in the data suggest minor temporary recoveries and subsequent declines in the animal population, but these are not sustained .  The total energy of all animals in the simulation starts at a high value of approximately 16,666.67 units . This initial energy level rapidly decreases within the first few hundred steps, indicating a sharp decline in the overall energy of the prey population . The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend with significant negative slopes in the rolling Mann-Kendall test .
