The Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) simulations show a consistent trend in the total number of prey animals over time. Initially, the total number of animals starts at approximately 250, which aligns with the set value for the initial number of prey. In the early stages, up to around 500 steps, there is a steep decline in the number of animals, dropping to about 100. This rapid decrease suggests a high initial hunting pressure or other mortality factors significantly impacting the prey population. 

Following this initial decline, the rate of decrease slows down, and the number of animals continues to drop more gradually. By around 1000 steps, the total number of animals is typically reduced to below 50. This phase indicates a continued but less intense pressure on the prey population, possibly due to a combination of hunting and environmental factors. 

As the simulation progresses beyond 1000 steps, the decline in the number of animals becomes even more gradual. By around 2000 steps, the number of animals approaches near-zero levels, indicating a trend towards extinction. This phase suggests that the remaining animals are either being systematically hunted down or succumbing to other mortality factors such as starvation or environmental changes. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight the critical impact of initial conditions and ongoing pressures on prey populations. The steep initial decline underscores the vulnerability of the prey to early hunting pressures, while the gradual decline towards extinction suggests that even sustained low-level pressures can lead to significant long-term impacts. Understanding these dynamics can inform conservation strategies and policies aimed at mitigating the factors leading to the decline and potential extinction of megaherbivores.
