The simulations of the agent-based model show a general trend of increasing inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households over time. Initially, the Gini coefficient starts at a value close to zero, indicating a relatively equal distribution of livestock among households. During the early steps, up to around step 20, the Gini coefficient remains low but begins to rise gradually. This period suggests that households are starting to experience slight differences in their livestock health, possibly due to initial variations in decision-making and environmental conditions. As the simulation progresses from step 20 to step 60, the Gini coefficient continues to increase more noticeably, indicating a growing disparity in livestock health among households. This phase likely reflects the cumulative effects of household decisions, learning processes, and environmental interactions. Beyond step 60, the rate of increase in the Gini coefficient starts to vary more widely among different simulations. Some simulations show a steeper increase, while others level off or increase more gradually. By the final steps, around step 100, the Gini coefficient values range approximately between 0.03 and 0.09. This indicates a significant divergence in the distribution of healthy livestock across households, highlighting the impact of different behavioral strategies, learning mechanisms, and environmental factors over the long term.
