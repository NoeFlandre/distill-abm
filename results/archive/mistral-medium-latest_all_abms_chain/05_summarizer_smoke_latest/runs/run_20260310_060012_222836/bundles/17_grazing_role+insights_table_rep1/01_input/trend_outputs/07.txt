The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all households, as measured by the Gini coefficient, exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period. Initially, the Gini coefficient starts at a value of -0.0000, indicating perfect equality in the distribution of healthy livestock. Over time, the inequality gradually increases, reaching a value of 0.0726 by the end of the simulation at time step 100. The mean Gini coefficient across the simulation period is 0.0302, with a median of 0.0229, suggesting that the distribution of healthy livestock becomes slightly more unequal over time. The standard deviation of 0.0239 and variance of 0.0006 indicate relatively low variability in the Gini coefficient values. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a significant increasing trend in inequality across all time windows, with a consistent tau value of 1.000 and significant p-values. Change points are detected at time steps 51, 71, and 100, suggesting notable shifts in the trend at these points. The dominant period of oscillation is 49.00, with strong oscillation indices observed towards the latter part of the simulation. For decision makers, these trends imply that over time, the distribution of healthy livestock becomes more unequal among households. This increasing inequality may necessitate interventions or policies aimed at promoting more equitable distribution of resources and supporting households that may be at a disadvantage in the grazing system.
