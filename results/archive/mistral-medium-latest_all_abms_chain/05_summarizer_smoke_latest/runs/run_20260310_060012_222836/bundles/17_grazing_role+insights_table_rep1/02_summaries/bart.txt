 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes . For decision makers, these trends suggest that initial deviations from recommended livestock numbers can increase significantly but tend to correct over time . This insight could inform policies or interventions aimed at managing livestock numbers more effectively .  A significant peak occurs early on at time step 3, where the average number of destocked livestock sharply rises to 6.6144 . Over time, there is a general decreasing trend with some fluctuations . For decision makers, these trends imply that over time, the distribution of healthy livestock becomes more unequal among households . This increasing inequality may necessitate interventions or policies aimed at promoting more equitable distribution of resources .  The number of households following the E-RO strategy, which represents non-learning agents, shows a decreasing trend over the simulated time steps . For decision makers, this trend implies a continuous reduction in livestock numbers over time . Understanding the underlying causes of this trend and the periodic fluctuations could be crucial for developing strategies to mitigate the impacts of destocking on the community .
