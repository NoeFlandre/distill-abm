The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the number of males killed, starting from 0 and rising sharply. This steep increase continues until around 1,000 steps, where the rate of increase begins to slow down. By approximately 1,250 steps, the cumulative number of males killed reaches a plateau, stabilizing around 120 males. This plateau is maintained with minor fluctuations until the end of the simulation period at 73,000 steps. The data shows that the number of males killed peaks at around 120, with slight variations but no significant downward or upward trends after the initial rise. The inflection points and change points indicate shifts in the trend dynamics, particularly around 1,250 steps where the trend transitions from increasing to stabilizing. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms these observations, showing significant increasing trends initially, followed by periods of no significant trend and a slight decrease towards the end. The oscillation summary indicates periodic fluctuations with a dominant period of 63 steps, suggesting some regularity in the minor variations observed in the plateau phase. For decision makers, these trends imply that the initial phase of hunting is highly impactful, leading to a rapid increase in the number of males killed. However, over time, the rate of hunting stabilizes, indicating a possible equilibrium or saturation point in hunting pressure. This stabilization could be due to various factors such as reduced availability of male prey, changes in hunter behavior, or environmental constraints. Understanding these dynamics can help in formulating strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts.
