The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a rapid increase initially, followed by a gradual stabilization. The number of females killed starts at 0 and rises sharply within the first 500 steps, reaching approximately 100. This rapid increase continues more gradually until around 1000 steps, where the cumulative number stabilizes around 120. The trend then plateaus, with minor fluctuations observed beyond 1000 steps, indicating a near-equilibrium state. Key inflection points are noted at steps 245, 262, 285, 1204, 1336, 1487, 1631, 1725, 1800, 1940, 1943, and 2040, suggesting periods of significant change in the rate of increase. The statistical analysis indicates a strong increasing trend initially, which weakens over time, eventually showing a slight decrease towards the end of the simulation period. The dominant oscillation period is 63 steps, with strong oscillations noted towards the latter part of the simulation. For decision makers, these trends suggest that initial hunting pressure is intense but stabilizes over time, potentially indicating adaptive foraging behavior or prey population dynamics reaching a balance. The stabilization phase implies that after a certain point, the rate of hunting does not significantly increase, which could be crucial for understanding long-term sustainability and conservation strategies.
