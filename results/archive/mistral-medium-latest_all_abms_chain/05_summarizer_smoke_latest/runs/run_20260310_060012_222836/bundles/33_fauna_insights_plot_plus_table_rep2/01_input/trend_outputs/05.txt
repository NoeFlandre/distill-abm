The cumulative number of postpartum females killed in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a rapid increase initially, followed by a gradual stabilization. Initially, the number of postpartum females killed rises sharply from 0 to approximately 117 over the first 2000 steps. This rapid increase indicates a high hunting pressure on postpartum females at the beginning of the simulation. Following this initial phase, the rate of increase slows down significantly, and the number of postpartum females killed stabilizes around 117 to 119. This stabilization suggests that the hunting pressure reaches a dynamic equilibrium where the number of postpartum females killed does not change drastically over time. The statistical analysis reveals that the initial phase of rapid increase is characterized by strong increasing trends, as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall test results showing significant positive trends in the early steps. As the simulation progresses, the trend weakens and eventually stabilizes, indicating a balance between hunting pressure and the availability of postpartum females. The inflection points and change points identified in the analysis mark transitions in the rate of increase, further supporting the observation of an initial rapid rise followed by stabilization. The dominant oscillation period of 63 steps suggests some periodic fluctuations in the number of postpartum females killed, but these oscillations are relatively minor compared to the overall trend. For decision makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressure has a significant impact on postpartum females, but over time, the system reaches a state of equilibrium. This equilibrium could be influenced by various factors such as the availability of prey, hunter behavior, and environmental conditions. Understanding these dynamics can help in formulating strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts.
