The total energy of male animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent downward trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12,500 units. This high initial value indicates a robust population of male animals with substantial energy reserves at the beginning of the simulation. The energy levels then rapidly decrease, showing a steep decline within the first few hundred time steps. This rapid decline suggests a significant reduction in the male animal population or their energy reserves, potentially due to high hunting pressure, environmental factors, or a combination of both. Around the 500th time step, the rate of decline begins to slow, indicating a possible stabilization in the factors affecting the male animals. However, the overall trend remains a decrease in total energy. By the 2,300th time step, the total energy of male animals approaches zero, indicating near-complete depletion of the male animal population or their energy reserves. Throughout the simulation, there are minor fluctuations and small peaks, but these do not significantly alter the overall downward trend. The implications for decision-makers are that the current parameters and conditions in the model lead to a rapid and sustained decline in the energy reserves of male animals, which could be indicative of unsustainable hunting practices or adverse environmental conditions. This trend suggests that without intervention or changes in the model parameters, the male animal population may face severe depletion or extinction.
