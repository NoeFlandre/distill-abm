The total energy of male animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12,500 units. This high initial energy level rapidly declines within the first few hundred time steps. By around 500 time steps, the total energy has dropped significantly to approximately 4,000 units. This downward trend continues, albeit at a slower rate, with the total energy gradually approaching zero. By the end of the simulation at 73,000 time steps, the total energy of male animals reaches zero, indicating a complete depletion of energy in the male animal population. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend with significant negative slopes in the rolling Mann-Kendall test across various segments of the time series. The implications for decision-makers suggest a critical examination of factors contributing to the rapid energy depletion in male animals, which could be indicative of high hunting pressure, environmental stressors, or insufficient food resources. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for developing strategies to mitigate the decline and potential extinction of megaherbivores in the modeled ecosystem.
