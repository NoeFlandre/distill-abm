The percentage of green patches in the landscape, representing the availability of grass for prey animals, exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the percentage of green patches starts at approximately 33% and remains relatively stable for a short period. Around time step 150, there is a noticeable decline in the percentage of green patches, reaching a minimum of about 25% at time step 365. This decline indicates a significant reduction in grass availability, which could be due to increased grazing pressure from the prey animals or other environmental factors. Following this decline, there is a gradual recovery phase where the percentage of green patches increases again, stabilizing around 32% by time step 725. This recovery suggests a period of regrowth and reduced grazing pressure, allowing the grass patches to recover. After time step 725, the percentage of green patches remains relatively stable with minor fluctuations around the 32% mark until the end of the simulation at time step 2312. The stability in the latter part of the simulation indicates a potential equilibrium between grazing and regrowth rates. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the dynamics of grass regrowth and grazing pressure. The initial decline and subsequent recovery of green patches suggest that management practices could be implemented to ensure sustainable grazing levels, preventing overgrazing and promoting landscape recovery. The stability observed in the latter part of the simulation indicates that with appropriate conditions, a balance can be achieved, supporting both prey populations and landscape health.
