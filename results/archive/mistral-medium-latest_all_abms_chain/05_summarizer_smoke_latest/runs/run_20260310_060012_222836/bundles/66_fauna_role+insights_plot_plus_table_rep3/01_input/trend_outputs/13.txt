The total energy of all animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of approximately 16,666.67 units. This high initial energy level rapidly declines within the first few hundred time steps. By around 500 time steps, the total energy has dropped significantly to roughly 4,000 units. This steep decline continues, albeit at a slower rate, reaching around 1,000 units by 1,000 time steps. Beyond this point, the rate of decline further slows, and the total energy gradually approaches zero, effectively reaching it by the end of the simulation at 73,000 time steps. Throughout the simulation, there are minor fluctuations and small peaks, but the overall trend remains a steady decrease in total energy. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend with significant Mann-Kendall test results across various segments of the time series. The implications for decision-makers suggest that under the given parameters, the energy reserves of the prey population are consistently depleted over time, leading to a potential extinction scenario. This trend indicates that the current settings of the model may not support a sustainable prey population, which could be critical for understanding the dynamics of megaherbivore extinction and the impact of hunting pressure.
