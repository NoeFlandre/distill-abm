 An agent-based model (ABM) is a computational framework used to simulate the actions and interactions of autonomous agents to assess their effects on the system as a whole . The model consists of 1,000 agents, each representing an adult consumer . These agents are part of a social network and make decisions based on various parameters that influence their milk choice . The ABM is designed to replicate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 .  The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent exhibits distinct trends over the simulated time steps . The simulations show a consistent upward trend in the total number of whole milk choices made by agents . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions and policies that effectively promote healthier or more environmentally friendly choices .  The total number of milk choices made by all agents combined exhibits a linear growth trend over the 30 steps of the simulation . For decision makers, this trend suggests a continuous engagement of agents in the decision-making process regarding milk consumption . The linear growth pattern suggests a consistent and predictable adoption rate, which can aid planning for future demand and supply chain adjustments .  The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits a consistent upward trend over the 30 steps of the simulation . For decision-makers, this trend highlights the importance of habit formation in consumer behavior . Understanding that habits strengthen over time can inform strategies aimed at either reinforcing or disrupting these habits .  The rapid increase in health deviation early on underscores the impact of initial perceptions and social influences . The subsequent stabilization indicates that once habits are formed, they tend to persist, but there is still room for gradual adjustments . This insight can inform strategies aimed at introducing new products or changing consumer habits .
