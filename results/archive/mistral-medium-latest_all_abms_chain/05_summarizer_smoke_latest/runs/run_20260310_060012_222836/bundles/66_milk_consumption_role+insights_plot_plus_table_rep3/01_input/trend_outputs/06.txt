The simulation of the agent-based model reveals a clear and consistent trend in the consumption of whole milk over time. Initially, the mean quantity of whole milk consumed is approximately 2654.81 units per agent. This high level of consumption sharply declines in the early steps of the simulation, dropping to around 800 units by step 10. This rapid decrease indicates a significant shift in consumer behavior away from whole milk. 

Between steps 10 and 20, the rate of decline slows, and the mean consumption stabilizes somewhat, fluctuating around 800 units. This period suggests a phase where the initial rapid adoption of alternative milk types slows down, and the remaining consumers of whole milk are more resistant to change. 

After step 20, the trend of decreasing whole milk consumption resumes but at a slower pace. By step 32, the mean quantity of whole milk consumed further reduces to approximately 253.10 units. This continued decline, albeit at a reduced rate, indicates a steady but gradual shift away from whole milk consumption over the long term. 

Key insights from the statistical analysis include several periods of significant decreasing trends, particularly notable in the early steps (0-4, 5-9, and 10-14) where the decline is steepest. There are also periods with no significant trend (15-19 and 25-29), suggesting temporary stabilization in consumption patterns. The presence of multiple inflection points and oscillations indicates variability and potential points of intervention where consumer behavior could be influenced. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of early intervention strategies to accelerate the shift towards healthier milk alternatives. The initial rapid decline suggests that consumers are quite responsive to changes in perception and social influences early on. However, the stabilization periods indicate that a subset of the population may require more targeted strategies to encourage further reduction in whole milk consumption. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing more effective public health campaigns and policy measures to promote healthier dietary choices.
