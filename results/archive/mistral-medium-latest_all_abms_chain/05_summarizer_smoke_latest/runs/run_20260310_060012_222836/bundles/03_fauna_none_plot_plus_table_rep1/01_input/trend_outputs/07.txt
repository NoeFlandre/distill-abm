The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population, resulting from both natural causes and predation by foragers, exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the number of deaths, starting from zero and rising sharply. This steep increase continues until around 500 time steps, where the number of deaths reaches approximately 150. Following this period, the rate of increase begins to slow down, and the curve starts to plateau. By around 1000 time steps, the number of deaths stabilizes around 230 to 240, indicating a significant deceleration in the rate of deaths. This stabilization suggests that the population has reached a state where the number of deaths per time step is relatively low, and the cumulative count fluctuates slightly but remains largely constant. The data indicates that the initial phase is characterized by a high mortality rate, which gradually decreases, leading to a steady-state where the number of deaths per time step is minimal, and the cumulative count exhibits minor oscillations around a stable value.
