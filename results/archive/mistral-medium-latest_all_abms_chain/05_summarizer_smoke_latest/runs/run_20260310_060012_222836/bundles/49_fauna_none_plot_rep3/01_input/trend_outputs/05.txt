The data from the repeated simulations of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) show a consistent trend in the cumulative number of postpartum females killed over time. Initially, the number of postpartum females killed starts at zero. As the simulation progresses, there is a rapid increase in the number of postpartum females killed, with a steep rise observed in the early stages, specifically within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase continues until approximately 1000 steps, where the rate of increase begins to slow down. By around 1500 steps, the cumulative number of postpartum females killed starts to plateau, indicating a stabilization in the rate of killings. The values generally stabilize around 120 postpartum females killed, with minor variations observed across different simulations. This trend suggests that the hunting pressure on postpartum females intensifies initially but eventually reaches a point of equilibrium where the rate of increase in killings diminishes significantly.
