The mean destock total of households starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 20 steps, reaching approximately 60. This initial rapid increase indicates that households are quickly reducing their livestock numbers, possibly due to immediate environmental pressures or initial strategic decisions. Following this rapid increase, the rate of destocking slows down and transitions into a more gradual linear increase from step 20 to step 100. During this period, the mean destock total rises steadily from around 60 to approximately 170. This suggests a consistent, albeit slower, ongoing reduction in livestock numbers over time. The linear trend indicates a sustained response to long-term conditions rather than immediate pressures. The relatively low variance among the simulation runs implies a high level of predictability and consistency in household decision-making processes regarding destocking. For decision-makers, the initial rapid destocking phase highlights the need for interventions that can support households during periods of immediate environmental or economic stress. The subsequent linear increase suggests that ongoing support and adaptive strategies are necessary to manage long-term sustainability and resilience in livestock management.
