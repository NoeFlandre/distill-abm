 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes .  The mean household risk attitude starts with a value of approximately 0.5 across all simulations . Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid divergence in the risk attitudes among households . Some households exhibit an increase in risk attitude, reaching values as high as 0.8, while others show a decrease, dropping to values as low as -0.2 . This initial high value indicates that households begin with a significant number of livestock that they cannot sustain . For decision makers, this trend highlights the importance of initial resource management and the need for adaptive strategies to quickly stabilize livestock numbers .  After step 40, there is a noticeable decline in the count of non-learning households, dropping to around 25 by step 50 . This decline could be attributed to changing environmental or social conditions . Long-term decline suggests that long-term policies could effectively reduce the prevalence .
