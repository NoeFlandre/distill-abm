The average green biomass across all patches exhibits distinct trends over the 100 time steps. Initially, the mean green biomass starts at approximately 45,000 units. There is a slight increase in the early stages, followed by a notable decrease reaching a minimum value of around 42,057 units at time step 27. After this point, the trend reverses, and the mean green biomass begins to increase steadily. This upward trend continues for the remainder of the time steps, culminating in a maximum value of approximately 60,535 units at the final time step. The overall mean green biomass across all time steps is about 51,529 units, with a median of 51,196 units, indicating a relatively symmetric distribution around the mean. The standard deviation of approximately 8,006 units suggests some variability in the green biomass values over time. The analysis of trends using the rolling Mann-Kendall test reveals several phases: an initial phase with no significant trend, followed by a significant decreasing trend, and then a series of increasing trends that persist until the end of the time steps. The presence of strong oscillation indices and a dominant period of 49 time steps suggest periodic fluctuations in the green biomass levels. For decision makers, these trends imply that while there are initial fluctuations and a temporary decline in green biomass, the long-term trend is one of recovery and growth. This could indicate resilience in the grazing system, where adaptive management strategies potentially contribute to the recovery and sustained increase in green biomass over time.
