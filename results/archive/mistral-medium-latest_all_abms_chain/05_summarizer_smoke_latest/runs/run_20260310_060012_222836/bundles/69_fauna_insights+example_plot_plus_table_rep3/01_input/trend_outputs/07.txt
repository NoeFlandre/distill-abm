The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps. This initial rise can be attributed to the initial interactions between predators and prey, as well as natural mortality, which are most pronounced when the population sizes are at their highest. The rate of increase in deaths slows down significantly after this initial period, indicating a potential stabilization in the population dynamics as the number of prey decreases and the system approaches an equilibrium. The cumulative deaths reach a plateau around 235 deaths by approximately 2000 time steps, suggesting that most of the mortality events have occurred by this point. The plateau indicates that the remaining prey population has stabilized, and further deaths are minimal. The variability among different simulation runs is relatively low, indicating consistent dynamics across different instances of the model. This consistency suggests that the initial conditions and parameters of the model lead to predictable outcomes in terms of prey mortality. For decision makers, these trends imply that initial conditions and early interactions are critical in determining the long-term stability of the prey population. Understanding these dynamics can help in assessing the impact of various parameters on extinction risks and in designing strategies for conservation or management of similar ecological systems.
