 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to megaherbivore extinction . It considers various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and life history . The landscape is a simplified grassland environment with patches of grass, ungulates, and human hunters .  The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and increases rapidly over the first 1,000 time steps . This initial rapid increase indicates a high initial hunting pressure on postpartum females, likely due to their vulnerability and the high food value they represent to the foragers . By the 730th time step, the energy level drops to around -1186.65 units, indicating a continuous depletion of energy reserves among the forager population . Total energy of male animals begins at 12,500 units and declines rapidly within the first 500 time steps . Energy levels drop steeply to around 2,000 units by the 500th time step, indicating a significant reduction in the population or individual energy reserves . After around 1000 time steps, the population continues to decrease but at a slower rate, suggesting that remaining animals might be reaching a more stable, albeit lower, population level .  The percentage of green patches in the landscape starts at approximately 33% and remains relatively stable throughout the simulation . However, a notable dip around the 365th time step highlights a potential vulnerability that could be investigated further to ensure the resilience of the grassland environment .
