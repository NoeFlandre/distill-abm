The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, there is a consistent and significant increasing trend in the number of males killed from the start up to approximately one-third of the simulation duration. Following this peak, the trend stabilizes, showing no significant increase or decrease for a substantial portion of the simulation. During this middle phase, the number of males killed fluctuates slightly but remains relatively constant, indicating a potential equilibrium between hunting pressure and prey availability. Towards the latter part of the simulation, there is a slight but significant decreasing trend in the number of males killed. This decline, although modest, suggests a reduction in hunting success or prey availability. Throughout the simulation, there are periodic oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, indicating regular fluctuations in the hunting dynamics. Key time steps highlight significant changes in the trend, particularly around the early, middle, and late phases of the simulation. The overall dynamics suggest an initial rapid exploitation of male prey, followed by a stabilization period, and a slight decline towards the end, reflecting the complex interactions within the model's socio-natural system.
