The total energy of all animals in the simulation begins at approximately 25,000 units. This high initial value indicates a robust and thriving population of prey animals at the start of the simulation. Over the first 500 time steps, there is a rapid and steep decline in total energy, dropping to around 5,000 units. This sharp decrease suggests a significant reduction in the prey population, potentially due to high hunting pressure and environmental factors that limit energy intake. 

Between 500 and 1,000 time steps, the decline in total energy continues but at a slower rate, stabilizing around 1,000 units. This phase indicates that the prey population is still under stress but the rate of energy loss has decreased, possibly due to a reduction in the number of prey available for hunting or an adaptation in prey behavior to avoid hunters. 

After 1,000 time steps, the total energy of all animals gradually approaches zero, with the population nearly extinct by 2,000 time steps. This trend suggests that the combined effects of hunting pressure, environmental conditions, and possibly internal dynamics of the prey population lead to a near-complete collapse. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight the critical impact of hunting pressure and environmental factors on the sustainability of prey populations. The initial rapid decline in energy levels underscores the vulnerability of the prey population to intense predation and environmental stress. The subsequent stabilization and eventual near-extinction provide insights into the long-term effects of sustained pressure on wildlife populations. This information can guide conservation efforts and policy-making aimed at preserving endangered species and maintaining ecological balance.
