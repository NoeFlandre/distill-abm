 The MHPM is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbivores . It incorporates various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, sexual dimorphism, and life history of animals . The model allows users to manipulate parameters to establish socio-natural dynamics and observe how these interactions influence extinction events .  The model's landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, consisting of a 211x211 patch matrix . The temporal units are 'ticks,' which can be scaled to any temporal granularity . Hunters and animals move and interact based on the set parameters, and the model provides summary statistics such as the number of animals in the environment over time .  The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period . The trends suggest that initial hunting efforts are highly effective but diminish over time as the prey population adapts or as the most vulnerable individuals are removed . Understanding these trends can help decision-makers in conservation efforts .  The number of animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model starts at 250, reflecting the initial population set for the simulation . Over the first 500 time steps, there is a rapid and steep decline in the total energy, dropping to around 5,000 units . By around 2000 steps, the number of female animals approaches zero and remains there for the rest of the simulation, suggesting that the population has collapsed . Understanding these dynamics can inform policies aimed at sustainable hunting quotas and habitat preservation  After 1,000 time steps, the total energy of all animals gradually approaches zero, with the population nearly extinct by 2,000 . This trend suggests that the combined effects of hunting pressure, environmental conditions, and possibly internal dynamics of the prey population lead to a near-complete collapse .
