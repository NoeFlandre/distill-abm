This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system. The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies. The model aims to evaluate the long-term social-ecological consequences of different household behavioral types and the impact of various ecological and climatic parameters on the system. The model is applied to smallholder farmers' decision-making, where each pasture is the private property of the household placed on it, and households make decisions based on observations of their pasture and neighbors. The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes. The model parameters are categorized into extension model parameters and original model parameters used by the extension model. The extension model introduces new behavioral types and allows for the study of strategy diffusion in a heterogeneous population of learning and non-learning agents. The model also incorporates a risk parameter to represent agents' dispositions to act under uncertainty. The context and goals of the model are to understand the implications of behavioral change for the resilience of pastoral systems and to evaluate the impact of various learning strategies and social learning on the system's performance. The key parameters of the model are as follows: number-households (range: 1.0 to 100.0, set to 60), satisficing-threshold (range: 0.0 to 100.0, set to 0), satisficing-trials (range: 0.0 to 100.0, set to 21), knowledge-radius (range: 0.0 to 5.0, set to 1), resting-threshold (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0.1), intrinsic-preference (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0), social-susceptibility (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0), global-rain? (set to True), start-on-homepatch? (set to True), resting? (set to False), homog-behav-types? (set to True), extension-model? (set to True), SL2-strategy-switch? (set to False), risk-mode? (set to True), behavioral-type (set to E-RO), number-SAT (set to 0), number-E-RO (set to 20), number-TRAD (set to 0), b (set to 0), number-MAX (set to 0), rain-mean (set to 200), risk-att-init (set to 0.5), gr1 (set to 0.5), number-E-LBD (set to 20), gr2 (set to 0.1), number-E-RO-SL1 (set to 20), timesteps (set to 100), w (set to 0.8), number-random (set to 0), livestock-init (set to 90), and rain-std (set to 100).

The mean household initial risk attitude remains constant throughout the simulation period. It starts at a value of approximately 0.0074 and maintains this value without any deviation across all 100 time steps. There is no observable trend or variability in the data, as indicated by the statistical analysis showing a mean, median, standard deviation, and variance all equal to approximately 0.0074. The absence of peaks and valleys, along with the lack of significant trends in the rolling Mann-Kendall test, further confirms the stability of this metric. This consistency suggests that the initial risk attitude of households does not change over time in this simulation, indicating a steady disposition towards risk among the agents from the beginning to the end of the simulation period.

The mean household risk attitude starts at a low value of approximately 0.0074. It increases rapidly over the first 20 time steps, reaching a value of around 0.2200. This initial rise indicates that households are quickly adapting their risk attitudes in response to initial conditions or early interactions within the grazing system. The risk attitude continues to increase, peaking at around 0.3814 at time step 33. This peak suggests a period where households are most inclined to take risks, possibly due to accumulated knowledge or observed successes in the system. After reaching this peak, the mean household risk attitude begins to decline gradually. By time step 48, it drops to approximately 0.2723, indicating a reduction in risk-taking behavior as households possibly start to stabilize their strategies. The decline continues steadily, with minor fluctuations, until it reaches a value of around 0.1911 by the end of the simulation at time step 100. This overall trend suggests that households initially increase their risk tolerance as they learn and adapt, but eventually, they reduce their risk attitudes, possibly due to achieving a more stable and predictable state within the grazing system. The variability in risk attitudes decreases over time, indicating a convergence towards more uniform risk attitudes among households as the simulation progresses.
