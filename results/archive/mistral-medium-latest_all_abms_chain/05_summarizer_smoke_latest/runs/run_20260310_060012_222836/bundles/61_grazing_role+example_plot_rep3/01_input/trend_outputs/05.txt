The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, starts at approximately 90 across all households. This value declines sharply within the first 20 time steps, dropping to around 45. This rapid decline could be attributed to initial adjustments in the grazing system as households adapt to the environmental conditions and manage their livestock accordingly. Between 20 and 40 time steps, the number of livestock continues to decrease but at a slower rate, reaching a minimum of around 40. This period likely represents a stabilization phase where households are fine-tuning their livestock management strategies based on the available pasture and observed conditions. After 40 time steps, the trend reverses, and the number of livestock begins to increase gradually. By around 60 time steps, the livestock numbers start to diverge more noticeably across different simulation runs, indicating variability in household strategies and environmental responses. From 60 to 100 time steps, the average number of livestock continues to rise, with some simulations showing livestock numbers reaching up to 60, while others stabilize around 50. This variability suggests differing levels of success in adaptive management strategies among households, influenced by learning capabilities and environmental conditions.
