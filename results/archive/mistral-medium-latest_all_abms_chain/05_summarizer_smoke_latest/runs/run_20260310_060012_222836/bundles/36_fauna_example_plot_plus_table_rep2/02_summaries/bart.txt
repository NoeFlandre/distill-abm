 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to megaherbivore extinction . It considers various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and life history . The landscape is a simplified grassland environment with patches of grass, ungulates and human hunters .  The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands begins at zero and increases rapidly in the early stages . The number of females killed continues to increase steadily, reaching around 120 by approximately 1,800 time steps . The system has reached a dynamic equilibrium where hunting pressure and prey availability are in balance . The rate of increase in deaths begins to slow down significantly around 1000 time steps, indicating a potential stabilization in the population dynamics . The variability in the number of animals decreases as the simulation progresses, suggesting that the system becomes more stable with fewer animals remaining .  Total energy of male animals eventually approaches zero, indicating a significant reduction in the male population's energy reserves over the simulation period . The total energy of female animals begins at 12,500 units and exhibits a rapid decline within the first 500 time steps . After 1000 steps, the energy levels approach zero indicating that the majority of the female animals have either perished or their energy reserves have been significantly depleted .
