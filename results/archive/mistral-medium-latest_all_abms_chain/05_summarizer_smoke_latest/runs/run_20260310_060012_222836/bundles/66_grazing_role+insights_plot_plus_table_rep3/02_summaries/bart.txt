 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies .  The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system exhibits distinct phases over the 100 steps of the simulation . Initially, the biomass starts at 90,000 and experiences a slight increase followed by a notable decline, reaching a minimum value of approximately 84,114 at step 26 . The trend analysis reveals significant decreasing trends in the early steps (0-21), followed by consistent increasing trends from step 33 onwards .  The Gini coefficient for the distribution of healthy livestock across households starts at 0, indicating perfect equality, and increases steadily over 100 time steps to a value of approximately 0.0726 . This trend suggests a gradual rise in inequality among households in terms of health livestock distribution . Understanding these patterns can help in planning and managing livestock resources more effectively, particularly in anticipating periods of higher destocking activity .  The dominant period of oscillation is 49 steps, suggesting cyclical patterns in the data . For decision-makers, these trends imply that the proportion of non-learning households decreases over time . This shift could have implications for the resilience and sustainability of the grazing system, as households adapt their behaviors in response to ecological and climatic conditions .
