The Gini coefficient for the distribution of healthy livestock across households starts at 0, indicating perfect equality, and increases steadily over 100 time steps to a value of approximately 0.0726. This trend suggests a gradual rise in inequality among households in terms of healthy livestock distribution. The mean Gini coefficient over the entire period is 0.0302, with a median of 0.0229, indicating that while the inequality is increasing, it remains relatively low on average. The standard deviation of 0.0239 and variance of 0.0006 show that there is some variability in the inequality levels over time, but the changes are generally consistent and gradual. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a continuous and statistically significant increasing trend throughout the entire period, with no detected peaks or valleys, reinforcing the steady rise in inequality. The inflection points and change points suggest some shifts in the rate of increase, particularly around time steps 51, 71, and 100. The dominant oscillation period of 49 time steps and the strong oscillation indices towards the end of the period indicate some periodic fluctuations in the latter half of the simulation. For decision makers, these trends imply that while the overall inequality in livestock distribution is increasing, the rate of increase is steady and predictable, allowing for potential interventions to mitigate inequality before it becomes more pronounced.
