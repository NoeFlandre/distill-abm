The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands exhibits a rapid increase initially, followed by a gradual plateau. Initially, from 0 to approximately 500 steps, the number of males killed rises steeply from 0 to around 80. This rapid increase indicates a high hunting success rate during the early stages of the simulation. Between 500 and 1000 steps, the rate of increase begins to slow, reaching around 100 to 110 males killed. This suggests that as time progresses, the hunting pressure remains high but the rate of successful kills starts to stabilize. Beyond 1000 steps, the trend continues to plateau, with the number of males killed leveling off between 110 and 120. This plateau implies that the system may be reaching an equilibrium where the number of males killed stabilizes due to factors such as reduced prey availability, increased difficulty in hunting, or other environmental constraints. For decision makers, these trends suggest that initial hunting efforts are highly effective but over time, the efficiency of hunting decreases, potentially due to declining prey populations or adaptive behaviors of the remaining prey. This insight can inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts to prevent over-exploitation of prey populations.
