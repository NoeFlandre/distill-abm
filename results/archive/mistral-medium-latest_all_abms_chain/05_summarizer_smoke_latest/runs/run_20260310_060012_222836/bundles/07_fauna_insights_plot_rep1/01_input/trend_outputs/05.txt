The data from the repeated simulations of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) show a clear trend in the cumulative number of postpartum females killed over time. Initially, the number of postpartum females killed increases rapidly from 0 to around 120 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase suggests a high initial hunting pressure on postpartum females. After this initial surge, the rate of increase slows down significantly, and the number of postpartum females killed stabilizes around 120, with minor fluctuations. This stabilization indicates that the hunting pressure reaches a plateau, potentially due to a balance between the number of available postpartum females and the hunting capacity of the foragers. For decision-makers, this trend implies that initial hunting pressure is intense but eventually stabilizes, which could inform strategies for managing hunting practices and conservation efforts. The stabilization phase suggests that there might be a carrying capacity or equilibrium point in the system, which could be critical for understanding the long-term dynamics of the prey population.
