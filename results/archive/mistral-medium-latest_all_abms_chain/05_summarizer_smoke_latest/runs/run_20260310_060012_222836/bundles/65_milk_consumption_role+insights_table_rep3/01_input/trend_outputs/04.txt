The simulation results indicate a consistent and significant upward trend in the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk by agents over the observed time period. Initially, the number of times agents chose these milk types started at zero. However, there was a rapid increase in adoption, reaching a final value of approximately 25,643.83 at the end of the simulation. The mean number of choices across the simulation period was about 11,157.35, with a median of 10,679.43, indicating a skewed distribution with a few very high values towards the end. The standard deviation of 8,241.32 and variance of 67,919,370.31 further highlight the substantial variability and increasing trend in the data. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirmed a consistent increasing trend across various segments of the simulation, with significant slopes ranging from 201.463 to 935.933, all statistically significant with p-values of 0.027. This suggests a strong and steady shift in consumer behavior towards skimmed and semi-skimmed milk over time. The detection of a dominant oscillation period of 15.00 and strong oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation suggest some periodic fluctuations in the adoption rate, possibly indicating cycles of influence or behavioral patterns within the agent population. The inflection point at index 31 and the change point at index 32 mark significant stages in the adoption process, where the rate of increase may have stabilized or reached a saturation point. For decision-makers, these trends imply that interventions or marketing strategies aimed at promoting healthier milk options have a strong potential for success, given the clear upward trajectory in adoption. The consistent increase also suggests that social influence and habit formation play crucial roles in shaping consumer choices, which can be leveraged to further drive the adoption of healthier alternatives.
