 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies .  The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system exhibits distinct trends over the simulated time steps . Initial decrease suggests a period where biomass is being utilized more rapidly than it is being replenished . The recovery could be attributed to favorable conditions such as reduced grazing pressure, improved climatic conditions, or effective management practices that allow biomass to regenerate .  The data shows a variety of trends in the adoption of social learning among households over time . Initially, all simulations start with zero households engaging in social learning . By step 20, the mean destock total ranges approximately between 40 and 80 livestock across the simulations .
