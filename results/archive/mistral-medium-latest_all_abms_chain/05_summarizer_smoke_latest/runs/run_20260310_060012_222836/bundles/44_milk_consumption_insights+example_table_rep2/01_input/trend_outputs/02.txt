This initial value suggests that at the start of the simulation in 1974, the consumption of these milk types was minimal. The consumption then exhibits a rapid increase over the initial time steps, indicating a swift adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk by the agents. This trend could be attributed to the growing awareness and perception of the health benefits associated with these milk types, as well as the influence of social networks and habits forming around these choices. By around the 400th time step, the consumption reaches a peak, reflecting a significant shift in consumer preferences and behaviors. The simulation shows several peaks and valleys in consumption, suggesting periods of increased and decreased adoption rates. Notable peaks occur around time steps 11, 13, and 21, with values approximately 1265.47, 1240.35, and 1226.86 units respectively. These peaks may indicate periods where social influence, health perceptions, or other factors strongly favored the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. Conversely, valleys around time steps 12, 20, and 23, with values approximately 1233.62, 1214.46, and 1209.70 units respectively, suggest temporary declines in adoption, possibly due to counter-influences or market saturation effects. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates significant increasing trends in the early and later stages of the simulation, with periods of no significant trend in between. This suggests that while the initial and final phases of the simulation show clear trends in adoption, the middle phases exhibit more complex dynamics with no clear trend. The simulation ends with the highest consumption value of approximately 1456.60 units, indicating that by 2005, the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk had become widespread among the agents. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial adoption phases and the role of social influence and health perceptions in driving consumer behavior. The variability in adoption rates suggests that while overall trends can be predicted, specific interventions may be necessary to sustain growth during periods of decline.
