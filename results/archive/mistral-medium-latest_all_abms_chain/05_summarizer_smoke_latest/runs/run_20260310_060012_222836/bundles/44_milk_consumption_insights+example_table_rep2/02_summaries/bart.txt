 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model explores how perception, habits, and social influence affect individual decision-making processes regarding milk choices . The main outcome is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person, using both theoretical grounding and empirical data for calibration against observed macro-level data .  The mean quantity of whole milk consumed by agents begins at approximately 2654.81 units at the start of the simulation . There is a rapid and significant decline in consumption within the first few time steps, dropping sharply . This initial steep decline suggests a swift shift in consumer preferences or behaviors .  At the beginning of the simulation period in 1974, the consumption of these milk types was minimal among the British public . The consumption then increases rapidly, indicating a significant shift in consumer preferences and behaviors . The data shows several peaks and valleys, with notable peaks at time steps 11, 13, 21, and 19 where consumption values are relatively high .  The mean deviation between the agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.0000 and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period . For decision makers, these trends imply that over time, the choices made by agents become more varied, which could reflect increasing individualization in preferences . Understanding these trends can help in anticipating how consumer preferences might evolve and in designing interventions that account for growing diversity in choice behaviors .
