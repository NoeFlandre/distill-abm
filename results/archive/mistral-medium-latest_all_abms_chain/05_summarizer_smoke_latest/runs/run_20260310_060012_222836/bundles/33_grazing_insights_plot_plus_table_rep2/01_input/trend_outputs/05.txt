The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households shows a distinct trend over the simulated time steps. Initially, the mean livestock count starts at 90. This value sharply decreases in the early time steps, reaching a minimum of approximately 41 at around time step 37. Following this decline, the livestock count gradually increases, stabilizing around a mean value of approximately 47.55 by the end of the simulation at time step 100. The data exhibits a significant decreasing trend in the initial phase, followed by a period of stabilization and a subsequent gradual increase. The inflection points at time steps 3 and 46 indicate notable changes in the trend direction. The rolling Mann-Kendall test highlights significant decreasing trends in the early phases (time steps 0-32) and significant increasing trends in the later phases (time steps 44-98). The oscillation analysis suggests a dominant period of 49 time steps, indicating cyclical patterns in the livestock count. For decision makers, these trends imply that initial livestock management strategies may lead to a sharp decline in livestock numbers, but adaptive strategies can stabilize and gradually improve livestock counts over time. Understanding these dynamics can help in formulating policies that support sustainable livestock management and household livelihoods in common property grazing systems.
