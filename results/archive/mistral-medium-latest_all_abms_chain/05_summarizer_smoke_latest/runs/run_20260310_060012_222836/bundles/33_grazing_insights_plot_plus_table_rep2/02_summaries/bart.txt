 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes .  The mean, median, standard deviation, and variance are all consistent with a constant value of 0.0074 . The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates no trend across different segments of the time series . The average deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents in the simulation exhibits distinct trends over the 100 steps .  The trend in the average number of livestock destocked across households shows a sharp initial peak followed by a rapid decline and eventual stabilization . For decision makers, these trends imply that initial livestock management strategies may lead to a sharp decline in livestock numbers but adaptive strategies can stabilize and gradually improve livestock counts over time .  The number of non-learning households in the simulation starts at 20 and exhibits a general decreasing trend over the 100 steps . For decision makers, these trends imply that destocking is an ongoing process that accelerates initially and then stabilizes into a steady increase . Understanding these patterns can help in planning and managing livestock resources more effectively .
