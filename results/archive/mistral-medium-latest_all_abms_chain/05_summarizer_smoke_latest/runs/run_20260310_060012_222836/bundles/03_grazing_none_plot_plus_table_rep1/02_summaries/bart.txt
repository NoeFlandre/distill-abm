 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes .  The mean household risk attitude in the simulation exhibits distinct phases over the 100 steps . Initial increase, subsequent decrease, and final stabilization reflect the dynamic adaptation and learning processes of households in response to the model's conditions . The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system exhibits distinct trends over the simulated time steps .  The model's dynamics reflect an initial adjustment phase with high destocking rates, likely due to households adapting to initial conditions and learning from their environment and neighbors . The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly in the early stages . The trend is consistently upward, with no detected peaks or valleys, and several inflection points suggesting changes in the rate of increase .  The statistical analysis indicates significant decreasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with no significant trends detected in the later phases . The dominant period of oscillation is around 49 time steps, suggesting periodic fluctuations within this broader decreasing trend .
