The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the early time steps. This initial rise is steep, indicating a high hunting pressure on postpartum females at the beginning of the simulation. By around 2000 ticks, the cumulative count reaches approximately 117, showing a significant accumulation of hunted postpartum females. The trend continues to increase but at a much slower rate, indicating a gradual approach towards a saturation point. The maximum value of 119 is reached around the 1826th tick, after which the cumulative count stabilizes with minimal fluctuations. The data shows several peaks and valleys, but these variations are minor, suggesting a steady state in the latter part of the simulation. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a strong increasing trend in the early phases, which gradually weakens and transitions to a period with no significant trend, followed by a slight decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation. This pattern suggests that the initial hunting pressure is high but tapers off as the simulation progresses, potentially due to a reduction in the availability of postpartum female prey or changes in the dynamics of the predator-prey interactions.
