 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies . The model is applied to smallholder farmers' decision-making, where each pasture is the private property of the household placed on it .  The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system starts at approximately 90,000 units . Initially, there is a slight increase, reaching around 90,522 units by the third time step . The biomass experiences a notable decline, reaching its lowest point of about 84,114 units at the 26th time step. After this low point, the biomass begins a steady and significant increase. The increase in biomass can be segmented into several phases of growth, each characterized by varying rates of increase .  The number of livestock destocked across all households begins at zero and rapidly increases, peaking at 6.61 livestock at around the third time step . The Gini coefficient for the distribution of healthy livestock across households starts at 0.0, indicating perfect equality at the beginning of the simulation . The trend shows a consistent increase in inequality over time .  The mean destock total of households starts at 0 and exhibits a rapid increase in the early time steps, reaching approximately 80 by step 25 . This initial surge indicates a significant and immediate response to initial conditions of the simulation . The rate of destocking slows down, showing a more gradual increase from step 25 to step 60 . This period suggests a stabilization phase where households are making more measured adjustments to livestock numbers .
