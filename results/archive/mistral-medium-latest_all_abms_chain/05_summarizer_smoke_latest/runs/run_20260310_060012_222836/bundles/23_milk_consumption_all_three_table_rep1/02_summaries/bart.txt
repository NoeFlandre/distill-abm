 An agent-based model (ABM) is a computational framework used to simulate the actions and interactions of autonomous agents to assess their effects on the system as a whole . The model consists of 1,000 agents, each representing an adult consumer . These agents are part of a social network and make decisions based on various parameters that influence their milk choice .  The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at a very low value of 5.29 per agent . The overall trend is a significant and steady decrease in whole milk consumption over time . For decision makers, this trend underscores the importance of understanding the factors driving the shift away from whole milk and the potential influence of social dynamics and perceptions on consumer behavior .  Over the first 300 time steps, there is a steady and significant increase in the adoption of skimmed or semi-skimmed milk . This trend suggests that agents are gradually shifting their preferences, likely influenced by the parameters governing health perception, social influence, and habit formation . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of health and environmental perceptions in driving consumer behavior changes .  The overall trend shows a steady increase in the consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, with periods of stabilization and minor fluctuations . This trend implies that the adoption of alternative milk types is a gradual process influenced by multiple factors, including social norms, health perceptions and environmental concerns .  The mean deviation between the agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.0000 and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation . The presence of strong oscillations, particularly towards the latter part of the simulation, suggests periodic fluctuations in how agents reconcile their health perceptions with their choices . For decision makers, this trend highlights the importance of addressing the growing gap between perception and behavior .
