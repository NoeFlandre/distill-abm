The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent starts at a very low value, close to zero. This initial state reflects the low adoption of these milk types at the beginning of the simulation period in 1974. Over the first 10 time steps, there is a rapid increase in consumption, reaching approximately 1,000 units. This sharp rise indicates a significant shift in consumer behavior, likely driven by initial health and environmental perceptions, as well as social influences. By around the 15th time step, the consumption stabilizes at around 1,200 units, suggesting that a substantial portion of the population has adopted skimmed and semi-skimmed milk as their regular choice. This stabilization period lasts until about the 25th time step, where consumption remains relatively constant with minor fluctuations. After the 25th time step, there is another noticeable increase in consumption, reaching a peak of around 1,400 units by the end of the simulation period in 2005. This final increase could be attributed to the cumulative effects of social influence, habit formation, and possibly changes in health and environmental perceptions over time. The overall trend demonstrates a clear shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, driven by a combination of individual and social factors.
