 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes .  The mean household risk attitude starts with a value of approximately 0.5 across all simulations . The values of the metric are spread across a range from approximately -0.1 to 0.17 . This spread implies that while some households are more risk-averse, others are more inclined to take risks . Understanding this diversity can help tailor strategies that cater to different risk preferences .  The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and shows a rapid increase in the first 20 time steps . The mean destock of households starts at a value of 0 and rapidly increases to a peak of approximately 7 within the first few time steps. This sharp rise indicates an initial period of significant stress on household resources, leading to a high rate of livestock destocking . The variability among different simulation runs increases slightly over time, indicating diverse outcomes based on differing adaptive strategies or environmental conditions  The count of non-learning households starts at 20 and exhibits a rapid increase in the initial time steps, reaching a peak of around 40 households by step 10 . After step 40, there is a noticeable decline in the number of households, dropping to around 25 by step 60 . This decline could imply that households are shifting away from non-learners strategies .
