The combined total energy available in the population of female prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a consistent and significant decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12500 units. This is followed by a rapid decline in the early stages, with notable peaks and valleys occurring around the 375 to 410 tick range, where energy values fluctuate between approximately 2094 and 2232 units. Despite these fluctuations, the overall trend remains a steady decrease. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates significant decreasing trends across various segments of the simulation, with the rate of decline varying in different phases. For instance, the decline is steepest between ticks 232 and 463, with a slope of -26.687, and more gradual in later phases, such as between ticks 928 and 1159, with a slope of -0.561. There are brief periods of increase or stabilization, such as between ticks 1160 and 1391, and ticks 1856 and 2087, but these are followed by continued decreases. The energy levels eventually approach zero by the end of the simulation at tick 2311. The dominant period of oscillation is 63 ticks, indicating periodic fluctuations within the overall decreasing trend. For decision makers, these trends suggest that under the given parameters, the energy reserves of female prey animals are consistently depleted over time, which could have significant implications for the sustainability of the prey population and the broader ecosystem dynamics.
