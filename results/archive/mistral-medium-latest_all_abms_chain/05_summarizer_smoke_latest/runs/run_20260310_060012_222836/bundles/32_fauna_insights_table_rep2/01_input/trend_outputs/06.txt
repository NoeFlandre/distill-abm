The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits several distinct phases and trends over the simulation period. Initially, the number of births starts at zero and begins to increase gradually. This upward trend accelerates significantly around the mid-point of the simulation, reaching a peak value. The most notable peak occurs around the middle of the simulation period, where the cumulative number of births reaches its highest value. Following this peak, there is a noticeable decline in the birth rate, indicating a decreasing trend in the latter part of the simulation. The data also shows several smaller peaks and valleys, suggesting periodic fluctuations in the birth rate. The analysis of change points and inflection indices further supports the identification of these distinct phases, highlighting key transitions in the birth rate dynamics. The dominant period of oscillation suggests a cyclical pattern in the birth rate, which could be influenced by various factors such as environmental conditions, predator-prey interactions, or intrinsic population dynamics. For decision makers, understanding these trends can provide insights into the factors influencing prey population dynamics and the potential impacts of different management strategies on birth rates and overall population sustainability.
