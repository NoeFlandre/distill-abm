The simulation of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decline in the number of male prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at 125 male prey animals. This number steadily decreases throughout the simulation, eventually reaching zero by the end of the observed period. The decline is marked by several key phases, with significant decreases noted at various intervals, particularly around the 125th, 250th, 375th, 500th, 625th, and 875th time steps. The most rapid declines occur in the early phases of the simulation, with the rate of decrease slowing down as the population diminishes. There are minor fluctuations and brief periods of stabilization, particularly around the 1392nd to 1623rd time steps, where a slight increase is observed. However, these periods are short-lived, and the overall trend remains a steady decline to extinction. The model exhibits strong oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of 63 time steps, indicating cyclical patterns in the population dynamics. For decision makers, these trends suggest that under the given parameters, the male prey population is highly susceptible to extinction over time. This insight could be crucial for understanding the dynamics of megaherbivore populations under similar conditions and for making informed decisions regarding conservation strategies and the management of hunting pressures.
