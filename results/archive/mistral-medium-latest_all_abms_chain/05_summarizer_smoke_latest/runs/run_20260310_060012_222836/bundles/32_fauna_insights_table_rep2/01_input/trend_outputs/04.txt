The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear increasing trend over time. Initially, the count starts at zero and gradually rises, indicating a steady accumulation of hunted females. The trend is consistently upward, with significant increases noted at various intervals. Early on, from time steps 0 to 1159, the increase is quite pronounced, with a high tau value indicating a strong upward trend. This initial phase suggests a rapid accumulation of hunted females as foragers actively pursue prey. As time progresses, the rate of increase begins to slow down, particularly noticeable from time step 1160 onwards, where the tau values decrease, indicating a less steep but still significant upward trend. The peak values are observed around time step 1826, where the cumulative count reaches its maximum of approximately 123.67. Following this peak, there is a slight decrease in the trend, as indicated by the negative tau value from time step 1856 to 2087, suggesting a stabilization or slight reduction in the number of females hunted. The data also shows some oscillation with a dominant period of 63 time steps, indicating periodic fluctuations in the hunting rates. These trends imply that over the long term, the hunting pressure on female prey animals increases, which could have significant implications for the population dynamics of the prey species. Decision makers might consider these trends when evaluating the sustainability of hunting practices and the potential impacts on prey populations over extended periods.
