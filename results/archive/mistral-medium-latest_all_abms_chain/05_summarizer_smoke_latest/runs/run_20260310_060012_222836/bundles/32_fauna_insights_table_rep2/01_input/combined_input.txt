The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to megaherbivore extinction. It considers various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and life history. The model allows users to manipulate parameters to observe how different conditions interact to influence extinction or equilibrium outcomes. The landscape is a simplified grassland environment with patches of grass, ungulates, and human hunters. The model uses 'ticks' as temporal units, which can be scaled to any temporal granularity. The landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, existing on a 211x211 patch matrix with relative spatial scale. The model is interactive, allowing users to manipulate variables to test a wide range of hypotheses related to extinction in a socio-natural system. Hunters in the model follow dynamic optimal foraging theory, making decisions based on environmental feedback, their energy status, prey food value, prey density, and the cost of hunting. Ungulate herds are sexually dimorphic, with distinct food values and processing costs for males and females. Animals can reproduce, and users can control sex ratios and reproduction probability. The environment can be controlled by adjusting grass density and regrowth time, with options for seasonality and static environments. The model provides summary statistics such as the number of prey items taken, the proportion of grazed to ungrazed grass, forager energy, and the number of animals in the environment.
