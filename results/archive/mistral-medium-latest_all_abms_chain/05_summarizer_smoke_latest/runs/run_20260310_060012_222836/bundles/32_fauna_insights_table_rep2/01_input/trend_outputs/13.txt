The total energy available in all prey animals within the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) simulation exhibits a consistent and significant decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of approximately 16,666.67 units. This value rapidly declines in the early stages of the simulation, with notable decreases observed at various key time steps. For instance, significant drops are evident around time steps 385, 388, 402, and 408, where the energy values are approximately 4102.18, 4092.42, 3994.85, and 3961.70 units respectively. These early fluctuations indicate initial instability in the prey population's energy levels. As the simulation progresses, the rate of decline in total energy slows down, but the overall trend remains downward. By the midpoint of the simulation, around time step 730, the total energy available in prey animals is significantly lower, approximately 1083.15 units. This downward trend continues, with minor fluctuations, until the total energy approaches zero by the end of the simulation at time step 2311. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend, with multiple significant periods of decline identified through the rolling Mann-Kendall test. The dominant period of oscillation is around 63 time steps, indicating periodic fluctuations within the overall downward trend. For decision makers, these trends suggest that under the given parameters, the prey population's energy reserves are consistently diminishing over time. This could imply increasing hunting pressure, environmental stress, or other factors leading to a decline in prey energy levels. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for making informed decisions about conservation strategies, hunting regulations, and environmental management policies.
