 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes .  The mean household risk attitude starts with a value of approximately 0.5 across all simulations . The constancy of these values over time suggests that the initial risk attitudes of households do not change during the simulation period . The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system starts at approximately 90,000 units .  The Gini coefficient for total livestock healthy starts at 0 for all simulations, indicating an initially equal distribution of healthy livestock across households . After step 20 to around step 40, the rate of increase in the mean destock total begins to slow down . This suggests a period of adjustment where the initial surge of destocking stabilizes, and households may be finding a balance in livestock management practices .  A smaller, yet consistent, portion of households maintain non-learning strategies . This final stabilization suggests a long-term equilibrium where a smaller, but consistent, part of households remain non-learned strategies . The study suggests that this may be the case for a long time in the U.S.
