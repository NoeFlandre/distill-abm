This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system. The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies. The model aims to evaluate the long-term social-ecological consequences of different household behavioral types and the impact of various ecological and climatic parameters on the system. The model is applied to smallholder farmers' decision-making, where each pasture is the private property of the household placed on it, and households make decisions based on observations of their pasture and neighbors. The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes. The model parameters are categorized into extension model parameters and original model parameters used by the extension model. The extension model introduces new behavioral types and allows for the study of strategy diffusion in a heterogeneous population of learning and non-learning agents. The data shows significant increasing trends in multiple intervals, particularly from time step 0 to around time step 21, indicating a consistent recovery and growth in biomass The rolling Mann-Kendall test results indicate a consistent increase in the distribution of healthy livestock over time. The trend is consistently increasing throughout the entire period, with no detected peaks or valleys, suggesting a steady and continuous rise in inequality. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this increasing trend across all time windows, with significant positive slopes and tau values of 1.000, indicating a strong and consistent upward trend. The change points detected at time steps 51, 71, and 100 suggest notable shifts in the rate of increase in inequality. The dominant period of oscillation is around 49 time steps, with strong oscillation indices observed in the later stages of the simulation.
