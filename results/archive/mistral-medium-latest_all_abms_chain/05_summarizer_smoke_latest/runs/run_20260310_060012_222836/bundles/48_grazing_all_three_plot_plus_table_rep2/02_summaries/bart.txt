 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system starts at approximately 90,000 units . Initially, there is a slight increase, reaching around 45,261 units by the fourth time step . The biomass reaches its peak at the final time step, approximately 121,073 units . The increase in green biomass suggests a recovery and growth phase where the vegetation adapts and thrives .  The Gini coefficient for the distribution of healthy livestock across households starts at 0.0, indicating perfect equality in the initial distribution . The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 20 time steps, reaching approximately 30 households . The average cumulative number of destocked livestock across all households increases rapidly in the first 25 time steps . The trend analysis reveals a consistent increasing pattern in the adoption of social learning across different time windows .  There is a noticeable decline in the number of E-RO households, dropping to around 20.1 by the 7th time step . This decline continues more gradually over the subsequent time steps . The dominant period of oscillation is around 49 time steps, suggesting cyclical patterns in the adoption and abandonment of the strategy . For decision makers, these trends highlight the dynamic nature of household strategies .
