The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 20 time steps, reaching approximately 30 households. This initial rapid increase suggests that households quickly adopt social learning behaviors as they begin to interact and observe their neighbors. The number of households engaging in social learning continues to increase, albeit at a slower pace, reaching around 32 households by time step 63. This indicates a gradual diffusion of social learning practices among the households over time. After time step 63, the number of households engaging in social learning stabilizes, showing little to no further increase. This stabilization suggests that a saturation point has been reached, where most households that are likely to adopt social learning have already done so. The trend analysis reveals a consistent increasing pattern in the adoption of social learning across different time windows, with significant positive trends indicated by the Mann-Kendall test. This consistent increase underscores the importance of social interactions and observations in the decision-making processes of households. The presence of strong oscillation indices suggests periodic fluctuations in the adoption rate, potentially reflecting cycles of interaction and observation among households. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the critical role of social learning in the diffusion of adaptive strategies among households. The rapid initial adoption followed by a gradual increase and eventual stabilization suggests that interventions aimed at promoting social learning could be most effective in the early stages of implementation. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing policies that leverage social learning to enhance the resilience and adaptability of pastoral systems.
