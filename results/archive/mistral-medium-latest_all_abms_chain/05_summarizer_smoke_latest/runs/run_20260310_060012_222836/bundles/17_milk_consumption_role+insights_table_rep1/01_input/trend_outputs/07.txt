The simulation of the agent-based model reveals several key trends in the mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents over time. Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of approximately 5.29 units. This low initial consumption reflects the strong initial habit strength for whole milk, which is set to its maximum value, indicating a strong preference or habit for whole milk among the agents at the beginning of the simulation. 

In the early stages of the simulation, there is a rapid and significant increase in the consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. This trend is evident from the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, which shows a strong increasing trend with a significant slope of 112.802 in the initial time steps (0-4) and an even steeper slope of 139.015 in the subsequent time steps (5-9). This rapid adoption could be attributed to the influence of social interactions and the initial health and environmental perceptions of alternative milk types, which likely start to sway agents' preferences away from whole milk. 

Following this initial surge, the consumption levels enter a phase of stabilization. Between time steps 10-24, the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates no significant trend, suggesting that the consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk has reached a plateau. During this period, the mean consumption values oscillate around a central value, with minor fluctuations. The peaks and valleys identified in the data, such as the peak at time step 11 with a value of 1265.47 and the valley at time step 12 with a value of 1233.62, indicate minor variations but overall stability in consumption patterns. 

Towards the later stages of the simulation, there is a resurgence in the increasing trend of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis for time steps 25-29 shows a significant increasing trend with a slope of 48.544. This late-stage increase could be influenced by the cumulative effects of social susceptibility and conformity, as well as the justification and cognitive dissonance parameters, which might lead agents to increasingly adopt and justify their choice of alternative milk types over time. 

The simulation concludes with the highest mean consumption value of approximately 1456.60 units, indicating a substantial shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk over the simulated period. The dominant oscillation period of 15 time steps suggests a cyclical pattern in the adoption behavior, possibly reflecting periodic social interactions or reinforcement of habits and perceptions. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial perceptions and social influences in driving the adoption of healthier or more environmentally friendly products. The rapid initial increase underscores the potential impact of targeted campaigns or interventions that leverage social networks and health perceptions. The stabilization phase suggests that once a critical mass of adopters is reached, consumption patterns can become self-sustaining. Finally, the late-stage increase indicates that long-term strategies that reinforce positive perceptions and social norms can further enhance adoption rates.
