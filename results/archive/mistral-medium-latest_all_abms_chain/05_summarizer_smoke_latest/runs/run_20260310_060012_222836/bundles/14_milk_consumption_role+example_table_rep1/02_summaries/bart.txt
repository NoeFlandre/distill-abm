 An agent-based model (ABM) is a computational framework used to simulate the actions and interactions of autonomous agents to assess their effects on the system as a whole . The model consists of 1,000 agents, each representing an adult consumer . These agents are part of a social network and make decisions based on various parameters that influence their milk choice .  The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent per agent begins at a very low value of approximately 5.29 units . This initial value suggests that at the start of the simulation, skimmed milk is minimal among the agents . Over the subsequent time steps, there is a consistent and significant increase in the number of whole milk choices . This trend is evident as the consumption value rises sharply, indicating a growing preference .  The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this increasing trend across all segments of the simulation, with a consistent slope of 1000 and significant p-values, indicating a strong and statistically significant upward trend throughout the entire period . The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents begins at approximately 2654.81 units .  The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at 0.0000, indicating no initial deviation in choice functions . Over the course of the simulation, this metric exhibits a consistent increasing trend, reaching a final value of 0.7508 . The mean deviation between the agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices is consistent . The overall trend demonstrates a clear and sustained growth in the habit factor .  The lack of significant trends in the latter part of the simulation indicates that the system has reached a steady state, with the agents' decisions being influenced by a complex interplay of their perceptions, habits, and social interactions . The agents then settle into a stable pattern of deviation .
