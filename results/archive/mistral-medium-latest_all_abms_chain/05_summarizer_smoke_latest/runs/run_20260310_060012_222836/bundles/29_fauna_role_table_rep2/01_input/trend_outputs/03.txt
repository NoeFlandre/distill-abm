The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the number of males killed starts at zero and shows a rapid increase. This upward trend is statistically significant and consistent through the early phases of the simulation, with notable increases in the cumulative count observed at regular intervals. Specifically, the data indicates strong increasing trends from the beginning up to approximately one-third of the simulation period, as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall test results which show significant positive slopes and tau values close to 1.0. 

As the simulation progresses beyond the midpoint, the rate of increase in the cumulative number of males killed begins to slow down. This deceleration is marked by periods where no significant trend is detected, interspersed with brief intervals of slight increases. Notably, around the three-quarter mark of the simulation, there is a shift towards a decreasing trend, although this decline is modest. The highest cumulative values are observed around the middle of the simulation, with peaks reaching just above 120 males killed. These peaks are followed by slight declines, indicating minor fluctuations but generally stabilizing around a high cumulative count. 

The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant periodicity, suggesting regular intervals of slight increases and decreases in the cumulative count of males killed. However, these oscillations are relatively minor compared to the overall trend. The change points identified in the data further corroborate the shifts in trend dynamics, particularly highlighting significant changes around the early, middle, and very late stages of the simulation. Overall, the data suggests an initial rapid accumulation of male prey killed, followed by a stabilization phase with minor fluctuations and a slight decline towards the end of the simulation period.
