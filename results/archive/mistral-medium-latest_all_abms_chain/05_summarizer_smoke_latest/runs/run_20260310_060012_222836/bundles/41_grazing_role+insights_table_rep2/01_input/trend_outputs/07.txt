The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all households, as measured by the Gini coefficient, exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period. Initially, the Gini coefficient starts at a value of 0.0000, indicating perfect equality in the distribution of healthy livestock. Over time, the inequality gradually increases, reaching a value of 0.0726 at the end of the simulation. The mean Gini coefficient across the simulation period is 0.0302, with a median of 0.0229, suggesting that the distribution is slightly skewed towards higher inequality values. The standard deviation of 0.0239 and variance of 0.0006 indicate relatively low variability in the inequality measures over time. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a significant increasing trend in the Gini coefficient across all time windows, with a consistent and significant positive slope. This suggests a steady increase in inequality throughout the simulation. Change points are detected at time steps 51, 71, and 100, indicating notable shifts in the trend at these points. The dominant period of oscillation is 49.00, with strong oscillation indices observed towards the latter part of the simulation. For decision makers, the steady increase in inequality implies that over time, the distribution of healthy livestock becomes more uneven across households. This trend could have significant implications for the resilience and sustainability of the pastoral system, as increasing inequality may lead to disparities in household livelihoods and potentially affect the overall stability of the system. Understanding these trends can help in designing interventions aimed at promoting more equitable distribution of resources and enhancing the adaptive capacity of households.
