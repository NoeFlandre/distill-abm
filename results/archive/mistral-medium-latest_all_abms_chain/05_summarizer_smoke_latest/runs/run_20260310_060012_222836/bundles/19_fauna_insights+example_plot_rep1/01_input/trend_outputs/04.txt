The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 100. This rapid initial increase indicates a high initial hunting success rate, likely due to the abundance of prey and the optimal foraging strategies employed by the hunters. Around the 500 to 1000 time steps mark, the rate of increase begins to slow down, suggesting a possible reduction in the availability of female prey or increased difficulty in hunting due to environmental factors or prey adaptation. By 1500 time steps, the cumulative number of females killed stabilizes around 120, indicating that the system may have reached an equilibrium where the hunting pressure is balanced by the prey's reproductive rate and other survival strategies. The low variance across simulation runs in the initial time steps suggests predictable hunting success, while the slight deviations observed later could imply varying environmental conditions or stochastic events affecting hunting efficiency. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of sustainable hunting practices and the need to consider long-term ecological balance to prevent over-exploitation of prey populations.
