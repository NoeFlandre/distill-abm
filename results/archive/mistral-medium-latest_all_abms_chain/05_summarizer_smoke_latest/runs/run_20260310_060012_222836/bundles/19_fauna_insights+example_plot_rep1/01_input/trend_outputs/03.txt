The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and increases rapidly over the first 500 time steps. This initial rapid increase indicates a high initial hunting success rate, likely due to the abundance of prey and the optimal foraging strategies employed by the hunters. Around the 500-step mark, the rate of increase begins to slow down, suggesting that the prey population is becoming depleted or that the remaining prey are harder to hunt. By approximately 1000 steps, the cumulative number of males killed reaches a plateau around 120, indicating that the hunting pressure has balanced with the remaining prey population's ability to evade or sustain themselves. The plateau suggests a potential equilibrium where the number of successful hunts stabilizes, reflecting a balance between predator efficiency and prey availability. There is some variability across different simulation runs, particularly noticeable after the initial rapid increase phase. This variability could be due to differences in the spatial distribution of prey, variations in hunter success rates, or stochastic elements in prey behavior and environmental conditions. For decision-makers, the initial rapid increase in hunting success highlights the effectiveness of early hunting strategies but also underscores the need for sustainable practices to prevent prey depletion. The subsequent plateau indicates that continued hunting pressure may not yield significantly higher returns, suggesting the importance of managing hunting efforts to maintain ecological balance.
