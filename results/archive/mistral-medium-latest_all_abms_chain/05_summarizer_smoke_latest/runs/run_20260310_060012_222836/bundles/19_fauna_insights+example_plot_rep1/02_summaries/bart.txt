 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to megaherbivore extinction . It considers various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and life history . The landscape is a simplified grassland environment with patches of grass, ungulates, and human hunters . Ungulate herds are sexually dimorphic, with distinct food values and processing costs  The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and increases rapidly over the first 500 time steps . The rate of increase begins to slow down, suggesting that the prey population is becoming depleted or that the remaining prey are harder to hunt . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of sustainable hunting practices and the need to balance energy expenditure with energy gain .  The number of animals in Megafaunafaunas simulation starts at approximately 120 and declines rapidly over the first 1000 time steps . The total number of male animals follows a consistent pattern of decline, reflecting a systematic reduction in the male population over time . By 2000 steps, the number of female animals approaches near zero, suggesting a critical threshold where the population struggles to sustain itself under the current trend .  The total energy of male animals begins at approximately 12,000 units at the beginning of the simulation . It declines rapidly within the first 500 steps, dropping to around 5,000 . This sharp decline suggests a high initial predation or mortality rate, possibly due to high hunting pressure or environmental stress factors . After 1,000 steps, the energy levels gradually approach zero, indicating a near-complete depletion of energy reserves in the female animal population .  The stability in the latter part of the simulation indicates a possible equilibrium between grazing pressure and grass regrowth rates . For decision makers, these trends suggest that while the grassland environment can experience significant fluctuations in vegetation cover due to grazing and regrowth dynamics, it has the potential to stabilize over time . Understanding these dynamics can help in managing grazing pressures .
