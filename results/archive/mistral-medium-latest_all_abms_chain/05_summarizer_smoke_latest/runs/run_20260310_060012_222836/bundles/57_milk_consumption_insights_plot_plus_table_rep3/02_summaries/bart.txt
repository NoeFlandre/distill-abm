 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . It includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where they can influence and be influenced by others . Agents represent adult consumers who make milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics, modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices .  There are no detected peaks, valleys, or inflection points, suggesting a smooth and uninterrupted growth in the total number of choices made . Trend analysis indicates a continuous and significant increase in the adoption of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk choices throughout the simulation period .  Trend highlights impact of social influence, habit formation, and perceived health benefits on the adoption of new milk types . For decision makers, these trends underscore the importance of initial adoption phases and the potential for periodic reinforcements to sustain or increase consumption levels . Trend implies that interventions aimed at reducing whole milk consumption may need to account for the strengthening habit factors .  Trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent increasing trend across all segments of the time steps . From steps 10 to 20, the rate of increase slows down, suggesting a stabilization period where the agents' health perceptions are adjusting more gradually .
