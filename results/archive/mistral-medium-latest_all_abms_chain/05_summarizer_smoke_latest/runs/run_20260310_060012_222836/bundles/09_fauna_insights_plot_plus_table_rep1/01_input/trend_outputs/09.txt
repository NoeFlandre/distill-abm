The number of female prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) simulations exhibits a consistent and significant decreasing trend over time. Initially, the number of female animals starts at 125 and rapidly declines within the first few hundred steps. This sharp decline continues, albeit at a slightly reduced rate, until around 500 steps, where the number of female animals drops to approximately 50. Beyond this point, the decline becomes more gradual, with occasional minor fluctuations. By around 1000 steps, the number of female animals stabilizes at a very low level, approaching zero. Throughout the simulation, there are minor peaks and valleys, but these oscillations are not substantial enough to alter the overall downward trend. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend with significant Mann-Kendall test results across various intervals, indicating a consistent reduction in the number of female animals. The dominant period of oscillation is around 63 steps, but these oscillations do not significantly impact the overall trend. The key insight for decision-makers is the rapid and sustained decline in the number of female prey animals, which could inform strategies related to conservation efforts, hunting regulations, and environmental management to mitigate such declines in real-world scenarios.
