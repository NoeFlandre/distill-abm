The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a rapid initial increase followed by a gradual stabilization. Initially, the number of females killed rises sharply from 0 to approximately 120 within the first 1000 steps. This rapid increase indicates a high hunting pressure at the beginning of the simulation. Following this initial surge, the rate of increase slows down significantly, and the number of females killed stabilizes around 120 to 123. This stabilization suggests that the system reaches a dynamic equilibrium where the hunting pressure is balanced by other factors such as prey reproduction and environmental constraints. Key time steps include the initial phase up to 1000 steps, where the most significant changes occur, and the subsequent phase where the system stabilizes. The mean number of females killed is approximately 104.56, with a median of 119.06, indicating that the majority of the simulation runs converge to a similar equilibrium value. The standard deviation of 31.10 reflects some variability in the outcomes, but the overall trend is consistent across different runs. The implications for decision-makers include understanding that initial hunting pressure can significantly impact prey populations, but over time, the system may reach a balance. This insight can help in designing conservation strategies and managing hunting practices to ensure the sustainability of prey populations.
