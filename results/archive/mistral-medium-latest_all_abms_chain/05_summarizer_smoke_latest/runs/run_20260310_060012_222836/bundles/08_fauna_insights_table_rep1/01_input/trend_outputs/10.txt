The simulation of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear overall decreasing trend in the total number of prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at approximately 166.67 animals and gradually declines to zero by the end of the simulation period. The decline is marked by several distinct phases, each characterized by varying rates of decrease. Early in the simulation, from the start to around tick 700, there is a steep decline in the prey population. This phase is followed by a series of smaller fluctuations and a more gradual decline. Notable peaks and valleys occur around ticks 700 to 1000, where the population briefly stabilizes or slightly increases before continuing its downward trend. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates significant decreasing trends in most intervals, with only a few periods showing slight increases. The inflection points and change points suggest shifts in the rate of decline, with notable changes around ticks 125, 475, 625, and 875. The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of 63 ticks, indicating cyclical patterns within the overall decline. For decision makers, these trends highlight the sensitivity of prey populations to the modeled parameters and the potential for cyclical fluctuations even within a general decline. Understanding these dynamics can inform strategies for conservation and management of wildlife populations under similar conditions.
