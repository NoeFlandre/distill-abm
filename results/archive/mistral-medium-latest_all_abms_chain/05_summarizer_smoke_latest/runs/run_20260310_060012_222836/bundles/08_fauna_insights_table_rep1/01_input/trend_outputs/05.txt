The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a general increasing trend over time, with specific dynamics and fluctuations. Initially, the count starts at zero and increases rapidly, reaching a value of 117 by the end of the simulation. The mean number of postpartum females killed is approximately 102.44, with a median of 116.86, indicating a skewed distribution. The standard deviation of 30.00 and variance of 900.13 suggest moderate variability in the data. The minimum value remains at zero, while the maximum value peaks at 119. The data shows several peaks and valleys, with notable peaks at values around 119 and valleys around 117. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with periods of no trend and slight decreases towards the end. Change points are identified at ticks 475, 625, 850, and 2310, suggesting shifts in the dynamics of hunting pressure. The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of 63 ticks, indicating cyclical patterns in the hunting behavior. For decision makers, these trends imply that hunting pressure on postpartum female prey animals increases over time with periodic fluctuations, which may be influenced by environmental factors, prey availability, and forager behavior. Understanding these patterns can help in assessing the impact of hunting on prey populations and in designing conservation strategies.
