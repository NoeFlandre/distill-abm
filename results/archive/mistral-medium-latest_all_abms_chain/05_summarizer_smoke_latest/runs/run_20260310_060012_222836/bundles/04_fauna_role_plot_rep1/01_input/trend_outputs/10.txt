The simulations of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) show a consistent trend in the total number of animals over time. Initially, the total number of animals starts at approximately 250, which aligns with the set value for the initial number of prey. In the early stages, up to around 500 steps, there is a sharp decline in the number of animals. This rapid decrease suggests a high rate of predation or mortality early in the simulation. 

By around 1000 steps, the number of animals has significantly reduced, with most simulations showing fewer than 50 animals remaining. This indicates a critical phase where the population experiences substantial pressure, likely due to a combination of hunting by human agents and environmental factors. As the simulation progresses beyond 1000 steps, the decline continues but at a slower rate, with the number of animals gradually approaching zero. 

By approximately 2000 steps, the majority of the simulations show that the animal population has either completely died out or is very close to extinction. This trend suggests that under the given parameters, the population of megaherbivores is not sustainable over the long term, leading to eventual extinction within the simulated environment. The consistent pattern across multiple simulations indicates robust dynamics driven by the interaction of hunting pressure, environmental conditions, and demographic factors.
