The simulations of the agent-based model show distinct trends in the mean green biomass over the 100 steps. Initially, the mean green biomass starts at approximately 45,000 units. In the first 20 steps, there is a slight decline in the mean green biomass, reaching a low point of around 44,000 units. This initial decline suggests a period of adjustment where grazing impacts are beginning to be felt across the pastures. Following this decline, there is a gradual increase in mean green biomass from step 20 to step 60, indicating a recovery phase where the biomass starts to regenerate despite ongoing grazing activities. This recovery could be attributed to adaptive management practices by the households, such as rotating pastures or adjusting livestock numbers. From step 60 onwards, the mean green biomass exhibits a more rapid and consistent increase, reaching values close to 65,000 units by step 100. This significant upward trend in the latter part of the simulation suggests that the adaptive strategies employed by the households are effective in promoting vegetation growth and sustainability over the long term. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of adaptive management practices in maintaining and enhancing green biomass in grazing systems. The initial decline underscores the need for careful monitoring and intervention in the early stages of pasture management. The subsequent recovery and growth phases demonstrate the potential benefits of adaptive strategies, which could inform policies aimed at sustainable livestock management and pasture conservation.
