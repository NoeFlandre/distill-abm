The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase within the first 20 steps across all simulations. This initial phase indicates a quick adoption of social learning behaviors among households, likely driven by the immediate need to adapt to new information or environmental conditions. By around 20 steps, the number of households involved in social learning stabilizes, with values ranging between 12 and 54 households. This wide range suggests variability in the adoption rates and effectiveness of social learning strategies among different households. The stabilization phase indicates that households have reached a point where the benefits of social learning have plateaued, and further adoption is minimal. For decision-makers, this trend highlights the importance of initial conditions and early interventions in promoting social learning. The rapid increase in the early steps suggests that households are quick to adopt new strategies when faced with new information or changes in their environment. The variability in the number of households engaging in social learning after stabilization implies that different households may have varying capacities or inclinations towards adopting social learning behaviors. This insight can guide policies aimed at enhancing the diffusion of beneficial practices and ensuring that all households have the opportunity to engage in social learning.
