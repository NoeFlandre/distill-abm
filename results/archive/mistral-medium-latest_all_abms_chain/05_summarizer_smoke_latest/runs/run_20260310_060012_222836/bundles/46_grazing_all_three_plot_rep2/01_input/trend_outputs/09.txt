The mean destock total of households begins at zero and exhibits a rapid increase during the initial 20 steps, reaching approximately 60. This sharp rise indicates an immediate response to initial conditions or stressors within the grazing system, prompting households to reduce their livestock numbers quickly. Following this initial surge, the rate of destocking slows down significantly, with the mean destock total gradually increasing and approaching a plateau around 80 to 100 by step 100. This trend suggests that after the initial rapid adjustment, households continue to destock but at a much slower and more stable rate, indicating a period of stabilization and adaptation to the prevailing conditions. The variability among different simulation runs increases slightly over time but remains relatively constrained, implying consistent decision-making patterns among households despite some differences in individual responses. For decision-makers, this trend highlights the importance of anticipating significant initial adjustments in livestock numbers when implementing new grazing management strategies or responding to ecological changes. The stabilization phase suggests that while households adapt over time, continuous monitoring and support may be necessary to maintain system resilience.
