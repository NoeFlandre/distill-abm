 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning . The model is applied to smallholder farmers' decision-making, where each pasture is the private property of the household placed on it .  The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system starts at approximately 90,000 units . Initial rise could be attributed to the initial growth phase of vegetation, where the biomass accumulates due to favorable conditions at the start of the simulation . After this peak, there is a rapid decline in biomass, reaching a low point of around 85,000 by time step 20 . By time step 40, biomass levels start to stabilize and continue to rise steadily . This long-term stability indicates that households have adapted to the environmental conditions and are maintaining their livestock numbers at sustainable levels . The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase within the first 20 steps .  Learning households maintain higher counts around 35 to 40 households, while others drop to lower counts around 5 to 10 households . The persistence of these distinct groups suggests that certain households find stability in non-learning strategies . Understanding the factors that contribute to this stability could help in designing interventions that encourage adaptive behaviors .
