This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system. The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies. The model aims to evaluate the long-term social-ecological consequences of different household behavioral types and the impact of various ecological and climatic parameters on the system. The model is applied to smallholder farmers' decision-making, where each pasture is the private property of the household placed on it, and households make decisions based on observations of their pasture and neighbors. The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes. The model parameters are categorized into extension model parameters and original model parameters used by the extension model. The extension model introduces new behavioral types and allows for the study of strategy diffusion in a heterogeneous population of learning and non-learning agents. The model also incorporates a risk parameter to represent agents' dispositions to act under uncertainty. The context and goals of the model are to understand the implications of behavioral change for the resilience of pastoral systems and to evaluate the impact of various learning strategies and social learning on the system's performance. The key parameters of the model are as follows: number-households (range: 1.0 to 100.0, set to 60), satisficing-threshold (range: 0.0 to 100.0, set to 0), satisficing-trials (range: 0.0 to 100.0, set to 21), knowledge-radius (range: 0.0 to 5.0, set to 1), resting-threshold (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0.1), intrinsic-preference (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0), social-susceptibility (range: 0.0 to 1.0, set to 0), global-rain? (set to True), start-on-homepatch? (set to True), resting? (set to False), homog-behav-types? (set to True), extension-model? (set to True), SL2-strategy-switch? (set to False), risk-mode? (set to True), behavioral-type (set to E-RO), number-SAT (set to 0), number-E-RO (set to 20), number-TRAD (set to 0), b (set to 0), number-MAX (set to 0), rain-mean (set to 200), risk-att-init (set to 0.5), gr1 (set to 0.5), number-E-LBD (set to 20), gr2 (set to 0.1), number-E-RO-SL1 (set to 20), timesteps (set to 100), w (set to 0.8), number-random (set to 0), livestock-init (set to 90), and rain-std (set to 100).

The mean household initial risk attitude remains constant throughout the simulation, indicating that the initial disposition of households to deviate from the recommended number of livestock does not change over time. The values of the mean household initial risk attitude are distributed across a range from approximately -0.1 to 0.15. This distribution suggests a diversity in the initial risk attitudes among households, with some households being more risk-averse and others more risk-tolerant. The fact that these values remain constant over the 100 time steps indicates stability in the initial risk attitudes of households, implying that external factors or interactions within the model do not influence this particular parameter during the simulation period. For decision-makers, this stability suggests that initial risk attitudes are intrinsic and do not fluctuate due to short-term changes or interactions within the system. Understanding this can help in designing interventions that account for a static distribution of risk attitudes among households.

The mean household risk attitude starts at a neutral value of approximately 0.5, indicating an initial balanced disposition towards risk among households. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid divergence in risk attitudes, with some households becoming significantly more risk-averse and others more risk-tolerant. This divergence suggests that households quickly adapt their risk attitudes based on initial experiences and observations. By around 20 time steps, the risk attitudes stabilize into distinct groups, with some households maintaining a high risk tolerance above 0.6, while others exhibit low risk tolerance below 0.2. A few households even develop negative risk attitudes, indicating a strong aversion to risk. This stabilization implies that households have settled into their respective risk strategies based on their learning and interactions within the system. Throughout the remaining time steps, the risk attitudes remain relatively stable with minor fluctuations, indicating that the initial learning phase significantly influences long-term risk attitudes. For decision makers, this trend highlights the importance of early interventions and support to guide households towards sustainable risk management strategies, as initial experiences greatly influence long-term behavior.

This initial rise could be attributed to the initial growth phase of vegetation, where the biomass accumulates due to favorable conditions at the start of the simulation. Following this peak, there is a rapid decline in biomass, reaching a low point of around 85,000 units by time step 20. This decline may indicate the onset of grazing activities by livestock, which consume the available biomass more quickly than it can regenerate. After this decline, the biomass begins to recover gradually, showing a steady increase. By time step 40, the biomass levels start to stabilize and continue to rise steadily, reaching approximately 120,000 units by time step 60. This recovery and stabilization phase suggests that the grazing pressure has been balanced by the regenerative capacity of the vegetation, possibly due to adaptive management practices or environmental conditions that favor biomass growth. From time step 60 onwards, the mean reserve biomass levels off, maintaining a steady state around 120,000 units for the remainder of the simulation. This stabilization indicates that the system has reached an equilibrium where the biomass production matches the consumption rate, implying a sustainable balance in the grazing system. For decision makers, the initial decline in biomass highlights the importance of managing grazing intensity to prevent overconsumption. The subsequent recovery and stabilization suggest that adaptive management strategies can effectively maintain biomass levels, ensuring the long-term sustainability of the grazing system.
