The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the initial time steps. This early rise indicates a high initial mortality rate, which could be attributed to the combined effects of natural mortality and predation pressure from foragers. By around 231 time steps, the death count reaches approximately one-third of its maximum value, suggesting a significant initial impact on the prey population. The trend continues to increase steadily, though at a slower rate, until around 1159 time steps, where the growth rate begins to plateau. This plateauing suggests that the prey population may be reaching a more stable state, potentially due to a balance between birth rates and mortality factors. After this point, the death count exhibits minor fluctuations but generally stabilizes around a mean value of approximately 200 deaths. The minor oscillations observed towards the end of the simulation period indicate periodic variations in mortality, which could be influenced by seasonal changes or dynamic interactions between prey and predators. The simulation ends with the death count near 235, indicating that while the population experiences continuous mortality, it may have reached a dynamic equilibrium. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial conditions and early interventions in managing prey populations. The rapid initial increase in deaths underscores the vulnerability of the prey population to early predation and natural mortality, suggesting that conservation efforts should focus on mitigating these early losses to promote long-term stability.
