The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at zero and exhibits a consistent increasing trend over time. Initially, the rate of increase is rapid, with significant growth observed in the early time steps. This rapid increase can be attributed to the high initial density of prey and the optimal foraging behavior of hunters, who target the most accessible and valuable prey first. The cumulative count reaches a peak value of approximately 123.67 around the mid-point of the simulation, indicating a high level of hunting activity. Following this peak, the rate of increase slows down, and the cumulative count stabilizes around this peak value with minor fluctuations. The minor oscillations observed towards the end of the simulation suggest a dynamic equilibrium where hunting pressure and prey availability reach a balance. The inflection points and change points identified in the data indicate shifts in the rate of hunting success, possibly due to changes in prey density, hunter energy status, or environmental conditions. The dominant period of oscillation suggests a cyclical pattern in hunting success, which could be influenced by seasonal variations or other periodic factors in the model. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial prey density and hunter behavior in determining the overall hunting pressure on female prey animals. The stabilization of the cumulative count suggests that long-term hunting pressure may reach a sustainable equilibrium, but the initial rapid increase underscores the potential for significant short-term impacts on prey populations.
