The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the number of males killed starts at zero and shows a rapid increase. This upward trend is statistically significant and consistent, with the number of males killed rising steadily. The early phase of the simulation, up to around 1,159 ticks, is characterized by a strong and significant increasing trend, as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall test results, which show high tau values and significant p-values. The slope of the increase is steep initially and gradually becomes less pronounced but remains positive. 

Following this initial phase, the trend stabilizes, and from approximately 1,160 to 1,391 ticks, there is no significant trend detected, indicating a period of equilibrium where the number of males killed plateaus around a mean value. This equilibrium is temporary, as a subsequent increasing trend resumes from around 1,392 to 1,623 ticks, although this increase is less steep compared to the initial phase. 

After this period, from around 1,624 to 1,855 ticks, the trend once again stabilizes with no significant changes. However, towards the end of the simulation, from approximately 1,856 to 2,087 ticks, a significant decreasing trend is observed. This indicates a reduction in the number of males killed, suggesting a possible decline in the male prey population or changes in the dynamics of hunting pressure and prey availability. 

Throughout the simulation, several change points are identified, marking significant shifts in the trend dynamics. These change points occur at approximately 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, 850, and 2,310 ticks, indicating key moments where the underlying dynamics of the model experience notable transitions. 

The data also exhibits periodic oscillations, particularly towards the end of the simulation period, with a dominant period of 63 ticks. These oscillations suggest regular fluctuations in the number of males killed, potentially reflecting cyclic patterns in the model's dynamics, such as seasonal variations or periodic changes in hunting behavior or prey availability. 

Overall, the simulation results highlight complex interactions between hunting pressure and prey dynamics, with phases of rapid increase, stabilization, and eventual decline in the cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed.
