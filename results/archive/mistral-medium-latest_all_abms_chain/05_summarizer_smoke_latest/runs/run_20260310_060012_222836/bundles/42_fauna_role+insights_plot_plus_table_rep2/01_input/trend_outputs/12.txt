The total energy of female animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a pronounced decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12,500 units. This high initial energy level rapidly declines within the first few hundred time steps, indicating a sharp reduction in the energy available within the female prey population. By around 500 time steps, the total energy has decreased significantly to approximately 2,000 units. This rapid decline continues more gradually, with the energy levels dropping to near zero by around 2,311 time steps. Throughout the simulation, there are minor fluctuations and small peaks and valleys, but the overall trend remains a steady decrease in total energy. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend with significant negative slopes in the rolling Mann-Kendall test across various segments of the time series. The presence of change points and inflection indices further supports the observation of a consistent downward trajectory in the energy levels of female prey animals. For decision makers, this trend suggests a critical reduction in the energy reserves of female prey over time, which could have significant implications for the sustainability and survival of the prey population under the given model parameters.
