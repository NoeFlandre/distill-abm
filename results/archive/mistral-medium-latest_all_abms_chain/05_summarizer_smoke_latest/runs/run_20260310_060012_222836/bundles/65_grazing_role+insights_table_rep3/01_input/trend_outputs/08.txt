The number of households engaging in social learning, as indicated by the variable household-SL being true, exhibits a consistent increasing trend over the simulation period. Initially, no households are engaged in social learning at the start of the simulation. The number of households participating in social learning gradually increases, reaching a maximum of approximately 32 households around the mid-point of the simulation timeline. The trend analysis shows a significant and steady increase in social learning adoption throughout the entire simulation period, with the most rapid increases occurring in the early stages. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this increasing trend across various segments of the timeline, with high statistical significance. The inflection points and change points suggest periods where the rate of increase shifts, indicating phases of accelerated or decelerated adoption. The dominant oscillation period and strong oscillation indices highlight periodic fluctuations in the adoption rate, suggesting intervals of more rapid or slower uptake of social learning practices. For decision makers, these trends imply that social learning gradually becomes more prevalent among households over time, which could influence the overall adaptive management strategies and resilience of the pastoral system. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions that either support or leverage social learning to enhance system performance and sustainability.
