 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies . For decision makers, these trends suggest that initial deviations from recommended livestock numbers can increase significantly but tend to correct over time . Understanding these dynamics can aid in developing sustainable grazing practices and policies that support long-term biomass health and system resilience .  The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households shows consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period . Trend analysis shows a significant and steady increase in social learning adoption throughout the entire simulation period, with the most rapid increases occurring in the early stages . For decision makers, these trends imply that social learning gradually becomes more prevalent among households over time, which could influence the overall adaptive management strategies and resilience of the pastoral system .  The number of households following the E-RO strategy, which represents non-learning agents, shows a clear overall decreasing trend over the 100 time steps . The periodic oscillations suggest that there may be underlying cyclic factors influencing destocking decisions, which could be important for planning and resource allocation .
