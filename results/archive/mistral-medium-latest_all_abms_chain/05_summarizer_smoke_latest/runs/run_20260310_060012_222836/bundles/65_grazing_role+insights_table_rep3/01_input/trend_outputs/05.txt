The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households shows a distinct trend over the simulation period. This decreasing trend continues significantly until around time step 33, where the number of livestock reaches a low point of approximately 41 at time step 37. Following this low point, the trend stabilizes briefly with no significant changes observed. From time step 44 onwards, there is a consistent increasing trend in the number of livestock, although the rate of increase diminishes over time. The inflection points at time steps 3 and 46 indicate notable changes in the trend dynamics. The oscillation analysis suggests a dominant period of 49 time steps, with strong oscillations observed in the early and middle phases of the simulation. For decision makers, these trends imply that initial grazing strategies may lead to a significant reduction in livestock numbers, but adaptive management practices could eventually stabilize and even increase livestock numbers over time. The early sharp decline suggests a need for careful initial stocking rates and monitoring, while the later recovery indicates potential resilience in the system given appropriate adaptive strategies.
