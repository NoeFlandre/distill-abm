 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies . The model is applied to smallholder farmers' decision-making, where each pasture is the private  The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system exhibits distinct trends over the simulated time steps . These trends suggest that over time, households develop varying risk attitudes, which could influence their decision-making processes . Understanding these variations can help in designing interventions that cater to different risk profiles among households .  The data from repeated simulations of the agent-based model show distinct trends in the number of households engaging in social learning over time . For decision makers, this trend highlights the potential for growing inequality in livestock distribution over time, which could have implications for the economic stability and resilience of households within the grazing system . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions that promote adaptive management strategies in pastoral systems .
