 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies .  The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system begins at approximately 90,000 units . Initial decline highlights the importance of monitoring and potentially limiting grazing intensity at the outset to prevent overconsumption . The trends indicate that while initial grazing reduces the available biomass, the system adapts over time, leading to a recovery and eventual stabilization of green biomass at a higher level .  The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock starts at 0, indicating an initially equal distribution of livestock among households . The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 20 time steps, reaching approximately 50 livestock . The number of households engaging in social learning increases rapidly .  By around 20 time steps, the number of non-learning households stabilizes across different simulations, with values ranging from near zero to around 30 . This stabilization indicates a diversification in household strategies, where non-learners either find a niche or are outcompeted by learning households .
