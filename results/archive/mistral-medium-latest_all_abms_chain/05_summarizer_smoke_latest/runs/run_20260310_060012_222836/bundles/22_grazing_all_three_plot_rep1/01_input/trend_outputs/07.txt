By around 40 time steps, the Gini coefficient reaches approximately 0.015, indicating a modest increase in inequality. This period may represent the point where initial differences in household strategies start to have more pronounced effects on livestock accumulation. From 40 to 60 time steps, the Gini coefficient continues to rise more steeply, reaching values around 0.03. This suggests a phase of rapid divergence in livestock distribution, potentially due to the compounding effects of successful strategies and the beginning of more significant disparities among households. Beyond 60 time steps, the rate of increase in the Gini coefficient starts to slow down, but the coefficient continues to rise, indicating ongoing but decelerating inequality. By the end of the simulation at 100 time steps, the Gini coefficient values range between approximately 0.03 and 0.09, showing a wide spread of final inequality levels across different simulation runs. This variability suggests that while some households manage to maintain or achieve a more equal distribution of livestock, others experience significant disparities. The overall trend indicates that over time, the distribution of healthy livestock becomes increasingly unequal among households. For decision makers, this implies that interventions may be necessary to mitigate growing inequalities and support households that are falling behind in livestock accumulation. Understanding the factors contributing to these disparities can help in designing targeted strategies to promote more equitable livestock distribution.
