 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes .  The mean household risk attitude starts at a low value of approximately 0.0074 and exhibits a rapid increase during the initial time steps, reaching a peak of around 0.3814 at time step 33 . The presence of strong oscillations, particularly in the early and middle phases of the simulation, suggests periodic adjustments in household risk attitudes . For decision-makers, these trends highlight the dynamic nature of risk attitude adaptation .  The average number of livestock destocked across households begins at zero and rapidly increases to a peak of approximately 6.61 at the third time step . Following this peak, there is a steep decline to around 3.37 by the ninth time step, indicating a rapid adjustment phase where households adapt to the initial shock . For decision makers, understanding these trends can highlight the importance of initial management practices and the need for adaptive strategies to maintain livestock numbers in a sustainable manner .  The number of non-learning households in the simulation starts at 20 and exhibits a slight increase in the early stages, peaking at around 21.1 at step 8 . The trend analysis indicates significant decreasing trends in multiple segments of the simulation . Understanding this shift can help in designing interventions that promote more effective household behaviors in response to environmental and social changes .
