Following this, the reserve biomass experiences a notable decline, hitting a low of around 84,114 units at the 26th time step. This initial decline could be attributed to the immediate utilization of biomass by livestock as they graze, reducing the available reserve biomass rapidly. After this low point, the reserve biomass begins to recover, showing a steady increase over the remaining time steps. By the end of the simulation at the 100th time step, the reserve biomass reaches approximately 121,073 units, indicating a significant recovery and growth phase. The trend analysis reveals several phases: an initial decrease, followed by a period of no significant trend, and then a consistent increase in reserve biomass. This pattern suggests an initial depletion of biomass due to grazing activities, followed by a recovery phase where biomass regeneration outpaces consumption. The strong oscillations detected around specific time steps indicate periods of dynamic interaction between biomass consumption and regrowth. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of managing initial grazing intensity to prevent excessive depletion of biomass reserves. The recovery phase demonstrates the resilience of the grazing system, suggesting that with appropriate management, biomass levels can be restored and even enhanced over time.
