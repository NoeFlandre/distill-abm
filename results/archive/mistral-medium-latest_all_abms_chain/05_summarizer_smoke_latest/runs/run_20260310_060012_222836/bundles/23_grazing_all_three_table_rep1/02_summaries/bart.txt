 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model is applied to smallholder farmers' decision-making, where each pasture is private property of the household placed on it .  The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches in the grazing system begins at 45,000 units . The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households starts at 90.0353 units . After this low point, the reserve biomass begins to recover, showing a steady increase over the remaining time steps .  The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 10 time steps, reaching approximately 14 households . The dominant oscillation period of 49 time steps hints at cyclical patterns in livestock destocking . For decision-makers, understanding these trends can help in designing interventions to mitigate increasing inequality and promote more equitable distribution of resources among households .  The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis shows significant decreasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation . This suggests that the decline in E-RO households is more pronounced initially and stabilizes later on . For decision makers, the steady decline in non-learning households suggests a shift towards other behavioral strategies .
