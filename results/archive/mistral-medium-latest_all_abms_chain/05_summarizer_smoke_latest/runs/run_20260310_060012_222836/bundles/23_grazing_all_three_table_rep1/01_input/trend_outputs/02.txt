The average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents begins at a low value of approximately 0.0074. This metric shows a rapid increase in the early stages of the simulation, reaching a peak value of around 0.3814 at time step 33. This initial rise suggests that households are initially adjusting their livestock numbers significantly as they respond to the initial conditions and start learning from their environment and neighbors. Following this peak, there is a general decreasing trend in the deviation, indicating that households are gradually aligning their livestock numbers closer to the recommended values as they gain more experience and information. The deviation stabilizes and shows no significant trend in the later stages of the simulation, suggesting a period of equilibrium where households have adapted their strategies effectively. The dominant oscillation period of 49 time steps indicates cyclical patterns in the deviation, which could be due to periodic environmental changes or recurring decision-making cycles among households. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial conditions and early interventions in guiding households towards sustainable livestock management practices. The stabilization phase suggests that continuous learning and adaptation can lead to more consistent and recommended livestock numbers over time.
