 The MHPM is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbivores . It incorporates various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, sexual dimorphism, and life history of animals . The model allows users to manipulate parameters to establish socio-natural dynamics and observe how these interactions influence extinction events .  The model's landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, consisting of a 211x211 patch matrix . The temporal units are 'ticks,' which can be scaled to any temporal granularity . Hunters and animals move and interact based on the set parameters . The number of human hunter-gatherer bands in the simulation remains constant throughout the entire duration .  Understanding dynamics can help in designing conservation strategies or adjusting hunting practices to ensure ecological balance within the modeled environment . Understanding these dynamics can inform strategies for conservation and management of megaherbivore populations in the face of hunting pressures and environmental changes . For decision-makers, understanding these trends can provide insights into the reproductive dynamics of the prey population and factors influencing their birth rates .  The number of female prey animals in the simulation starts at 125 and exhibits a rapid decline in the initial phase, dropping significantly within the first 730 time steps . By around 730 ticks, the population drops to about 11 animals, indicating a critical phase where the population is severely diminished . The population continues to decrease but at a slower rate, with minor fluctuations . The simulation ends with the prey population reaching zero, indicating complete extinction .  The energy level declines rapidly in the early stages, dropping significantly within the first few hundred time steps . This rapid decline suggests a high initial predation pressure or resource consumption rate . The rapid initial decline underscores the vulnerability of the population to sudden changes, while the subsequent fluctuations suggest the need for sustainable management practices to prevent extinction .  The change points identified at ticks 150, 300, 475, 725, and 2310 mark significant shifts in the trend of vegetation cover . For decision makers, the overall stability with minor fluctuations suggests a resilient grassland environment . However, the notable dip at around 365 ticks and the cyclical patterns indicate periods of stress and recovery that may need further investigation .
