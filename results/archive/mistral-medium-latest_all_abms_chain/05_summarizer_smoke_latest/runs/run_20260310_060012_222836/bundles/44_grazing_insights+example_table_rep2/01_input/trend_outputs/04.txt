The average amount of available green biomass across all patches begins at 45,000 units. Initially, there is a slight increase, reaching a peak of 45,261.3681 units at time step 4. Following this peak, the biomass experiences a notable decline, hitting a low of 42,057.0353 units at time step 27. This decline could be attributed to initial grazing pressures as households begin to utilize the pastures more intensively. After this low point, the biomass starts to recover and exhibits a consistent increasing trend. This recovery phase can be segmented into several periods of growth with varying rates. From time steps 33 to 98, the biomass shows a steady increase, indicating a possible adaptation in grazing practices or an improvement in pasture management by the households. The simulation concludes with the biomass reaching 60,535.2808 units at time step 100, the highest value observed throughout the simulation. The overall trend suggests an initial period of adjustment followed by a sustained recovery and growth in biomass. For decision makers, this trend implies that while initial grazing pressures can lead to a temporary decline in biomass, adaptive management practices can facilitate recovery and long-term sustainability of pasture resources.
