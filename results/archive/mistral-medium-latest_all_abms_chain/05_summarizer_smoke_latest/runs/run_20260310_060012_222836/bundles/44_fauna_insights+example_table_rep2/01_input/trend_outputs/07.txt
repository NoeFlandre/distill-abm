The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at zero and exhibits a consistent increasing trend over time. Initially, the rate of increase is relatively rapid, with significant growth observed in the early time steps. This early rise in deaths can be attributed to the initial interactions between the prey and foragers, as well as natural mortality factors. The rate of increase in deaths begins to slow down as time progresses, indicating a potential stabilization in the prey population dynamics. Around the mid-point of the simulation, the cumulative death count reaches a plateau, suggesting that the system may be approaching an equilibrium where the rate of deaths stabilizes. This plateau is characterized by minor fluctuations, reflecting the dynamic but balanced interactions between prey, foragers, and environmental factors. Towards the end of the simulation, the cumulative death count shows a slight decrease, indicating a potential reduction in mortality rates. This could be due to a variety of factors such as a decrease in predator efficiency, changes in prey behavior, or environmental adjustments that favor prey survival. The overall trend suggests that while initial conditions lead to a rapid increase in deaths, the system eventually moves towards a more stable state with lower mortality rates. For decision makers, this trend highlights the importance of considering long-term dynamics in predator-prey interactions and the potential for system stabilization over time.
