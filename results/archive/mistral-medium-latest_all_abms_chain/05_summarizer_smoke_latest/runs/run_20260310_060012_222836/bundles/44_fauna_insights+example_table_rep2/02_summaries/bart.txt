 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to megaherbivore extinction . It considers various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and life history . The landscape is a simplified grassland environment with patches of grass, ungulates, and human hunters . Ungulate herds are sexually dimorphic, with distinct food values and processing costs  The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows significant trends in various time windows as evidenced by the rolling MannKendall test . The model shows significant growth in the number of prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands . The insights from these trends can help decision-makers understand dynamics of hunting pressure and prey population interactions .  The cyclic patterns and change points provide insights into the periodic nature of hunting success and the need for adaptive management strategies . For decision-makers, these trends highlight the continuous vulnerability of postpartum females to hunting pressure, which could have significant implications for the population dynamics and conservation strategies of the prey species .  The initial rapid decline and eventual extinction suggest that current parameters related to hunting pressure, environmental factors, and prey demographics may not be sustainable for the male prey population . This information could be crucial for developing conservation strategies or adjusting hunting regulations to prevent such outcomes in real-world scenarios .  The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model simulation begins at approximately 16,667 units . The energy levels eventually approach zero by the 2,284th time step, suggesting a near-complete depletion of energy resources within the male prey population . The rapid initial decline and subsequent fluctuations underscore the importance of managing hunting pressure and environmental factors to prevent the depletion .  The rolling Mann-Kendall test shows periods of both increasing and decreasing trends in vegetation cover . This shift could imply changes in environmental conditions or grazing patterns affecting vegetation regrowth . The dominant oscillation period of 63 time steps suggests regular fluctuations in vegetation .
