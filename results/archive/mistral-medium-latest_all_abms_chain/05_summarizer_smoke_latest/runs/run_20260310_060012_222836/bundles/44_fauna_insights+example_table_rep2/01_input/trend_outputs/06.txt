The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at zero and exhibits a gradual increase over time. The initial phase shows a slow rise in births, indicating a period of adaptation and stabilization within the prey population. Around the mid-point of the simulation, there is a noticeable acceleration in the birth rate, reaching a peak value. This peak suggests a period of optimal conditions for reproduction, possibly due to a balance between available resources and population density. Following this peak, the birth rate begins to decline gradually, indicating a potential stabilization or a response to changing environmental or population dynamics. The simulation ends with the cumulative number of births stabilizing, implying that the prey population has reached a dynamic equilibrium. The presence of strong oscillations around the mid-point of the simulation suggests periodic fluctuations in birth rates, which could be influenced by seasonal environmental changes or predator-prey interactions. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of considering both short-term fluctuations and long-term stabilization in population dynamics when evaluating the impact of various parameters on megaherbivore extinction.
