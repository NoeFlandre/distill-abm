 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005, focusing on the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk . Agents represent adult consumers who make milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics, modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices .  The number of times agents chose whole milk starts at zero and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period . This steady rise indicates a growing preference or habit formation towards whole milk among the agents over time . The rapid increase in consumption suggests that once the benefits of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk were recognized, there was a swift change in consumer behavior .  These trends suggest a clear shift away from whole milk consumption over the simulated period . This insight could inform strategies related to marketing, product development, and public health campaigns . For decision makers, interventions aimed at changing milk consumption habits may need to account for the increasing influence of habit over time .  The mean deviation between the agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.0000 . It increases rapidly in the initial time steps, reaching a value of 13.4756 by the 13th time step . The deviation reaches a maximum value of 0.7251 at the final time step, suggesting a growing misalignment .
