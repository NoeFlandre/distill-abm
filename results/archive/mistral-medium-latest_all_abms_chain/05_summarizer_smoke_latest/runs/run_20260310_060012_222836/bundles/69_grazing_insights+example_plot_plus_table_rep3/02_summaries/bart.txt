 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes .  The mean household risk attitude starts at a low value of approximately 0.0074 and exhibits a rapid increase over the first 30 time steps . The absence of peaks and valleys, along with the lack of significant change points, underscores the static nature of this metric . For decision makers, this implies that the initial risk attitude of households does not fluctuate .  The average number of livestock destocked across households begins at zero and rises sharply to a peak of approximately 6.61 at the third time step . This initial spike suggests a rapid response to immediate environmental or resource constraints, leading to a significant reduction in livestock numbers early in the simulation . The trend of declining destocking rates suggests that households are gradually stabilizing their livestock numbers in response to the initial constraints .  Trend shows significant decreasing phases, particularly between time steps 11 to 65, indicating a consistent reduction in the number of non-learning households during this period . After time step 65, trend stabilizes with no significant changes, suggesting that the system has reached a more stable state regarding the distribution of behavioral types .
