An agent-based model (ABM) is a computational framework used to simulate the actions and interactions of autonomous agents to assess their effects on the system as a whole. In this context, the ABM is designed to replicate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005, focusing on the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The simulation ends with the average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent stabilizing around 400 units. This final stabilization indicates that a new equilibrium has been reached, with a significant reduction in whole milk consumption compared to the initial values. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial interventions and social influences in driving significant changes in consumer behavior. The rapid initial decline suggests that targeted campaigns or policy changes can have a substantial impact in the short term. However, the slower stabilization phase indicates the need for sustained efforts to achieve long-term behavioral changes. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing more effective strategies for promoting healthier or more sustainable consumption patterns.
