The mean deviation between the agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices shows a consistent increasing trend over the simulation period from 1974 to 2005. Initially, the deviation starts at 0.0000 and gradually rises to a final value of 0.7251. The mean deviation across the entire period is 0.4965, with a median of 0.5584, indicating that the data is relatively evenly distributed around the mean. The standard deviation of 0.1702 and variance of 0.0290 suggest moderate variability in the data. The trend analysis reveals a steady increase in deviation, with significant increasing trends detected in multiple rolling Mann-Kendall tests across different time segments. The absence of detected peaks and valleys, along with the presence of inflection points at various indices, indicates a smooth but non-linear upward trajectory. The oscillation summary suggests some periodic fluctuations with a dominant period of 15.00, but the overall trend remains upward. For decision makers, this implies that over time, there is a growing discrepancy between the health values perceived by the agents and the health implications of their milk choices. This trend could be useful for understanding how public perception and actual consumption patterns diverge, potentially informing public health campaigns or policy interventions aimed at aligning perceptions with healthier choices.
