The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, starts at approximately 90. This high initial value indicates that households begin with a substantial livestock population. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid decline in the total livestock, dropping to around 45. This sharp decrease suggests an initial period of adjustment where households may be destocking livestock due to limited pasture resources or other constraints. From time step 20 to around time step 50, the number of livestock remains relatively stable, fluctuating slightly around the 45 mark. This period of stability indicates a balance between livestock reproduction and destocking practices. After time step 50, there is a gradual increase in the total number of livestock, reaching approximately 60 by time step 100. This upward trend suggests that households are adapting their management practices, possibly due to improved pasture conditions or more effective livestock management strategies. The insights from these trends imply that decision-makers should focus on strategies to mitigate the initial sharp decline in livestock numbers and support practices that lead to long-term stability and growth in livestock populations.
