 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies .  Early stages, up to approximately step 20, there is a noticeable decline in the mean reserve biomass, reaching a low point of around 85,000 units . This initial decrease suggests a period of increased grazing activity or reduced biomass growth, possibly due to the initial setup and adaptation phase of the households and their livestock . Following this period, the mean green biomass begins to increase steadily .  The data shows a variety of trends in the number of households engaging in social learning over time . The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households exhibits a rapid increase in the initial time steps . This information can be crucial for understanding the dynamics of strategy diffusion in pastoral systems and for designing interventions that promote adaptive management strategies .
