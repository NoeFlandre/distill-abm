 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . It explores how perception, habits, and social influence affect individual decision-making processes regarding milk choices . The model includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where they can influence and be influenced by others .  Total number of whole milk choices starts at 0 and increases steadily over the 33 time steps . Means value of 1130.88 and median value of 1158.97 indicate that, on average, agents choose whole milk around 1131 times cumulatively by the midpoint of the simulation period . For decision makers, these trends imply that interventions aimed at influencing milk consumption habits should consider the steady and cumulative nature of adoption behaviors .  The data shows significant variability across the simulation runs, particularly noticeable after the initial rapid decline . The peaks and valleys identified in the data suggest minor temporary increases and decreases in consumption at specific steps, but the overall trend remains a steady decline . For decision makers, the consistent decline in whole milk consumption highlights a significant shift in consumer behavior .  The model captures a dynamic process where individual differences in milk consumption choices become more distinct over time due to the interplay of personal habits, social influence and continuous evaluation of choices . For decision makers, this trend highlights the dynamic nature of consumer perceptions and the importance of considering social influences and habitual behaviors in understanding milk consumption patterns .
