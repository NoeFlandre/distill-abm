The total number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at zero and remains relatively low for the first 500 time steps. This initial period of low mortality could be attributed to the stable initial conditions of the environment and the prey population, where resources are abundant and predation pressure is relatively low. Around the 500-step mark, there is a rapid increase in the number of deaths, reaching approximately 200 deaths by the 1000-step mark. This sharp rise in mortality could be due to a combination of factors such as increased predation pressure as foragers optimize their hunting strategies, environmental changes reducing resource availability, and natural mortality within the prey population. After this rapid increase, the number of deaths stabilizes and plateaus around 250 deaths, maintaining this level for the remainder of the simulation. This stabilization suggests that the system has reached a new equilibrium where the rate of deaths balances with the birth rate and other factors influencing the prey population. There is some variance across simulation runs, particularly noticeable after the initial 500 steps, indicating that while the initial conditions lead to predictable outcomes, the complexity of interactions among various factors introduces variability in the long-term dynamics of the system.
