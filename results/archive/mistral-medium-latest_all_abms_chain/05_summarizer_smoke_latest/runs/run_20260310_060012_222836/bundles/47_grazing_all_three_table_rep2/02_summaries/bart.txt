 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model aims to evaluate the long-term social-ecological consequences of different household behavioral types and the impact of various ecological and climatic parameters on the system .  The average amount of reserve biomass available across all patches in the grazing system begins at 90,000 units . Initial decline could be attributed to the immediate consumption of biomass by livestock . The subsequent stabilization indicates potential long-term benefits of adaptive learning and social interactions in achieving more sustainable livestock management .  The average cumulative number of destocked livestock starts at 0 and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the entire simulation period of 100 time steps . For decision makers, the steady increase in the Gini coefficient implies that interventions may be needed to address growing disparities in livestock health among households . Understanding the factors contributing to these change points and oscillations could provide insights into the dynamics of inequality and inform targeted policies .  The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant decreasing trends in the initial phases, particularly from time steps 11 to 65 . The trend implies a gradual shift away from the E-RO strategy among households . The dominant oscillation period of 49 time steps suggests some cyclical behavior, but the overall trend remains a decline .
