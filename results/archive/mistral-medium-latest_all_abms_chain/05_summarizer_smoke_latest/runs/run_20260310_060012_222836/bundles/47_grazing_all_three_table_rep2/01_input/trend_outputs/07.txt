The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all households, as measured by the Gini coefficient, starts at a value of 0.0000, indicating perfect equality. Over the course of the simulation, the Gini coefficient increases steadily, reaching a value of 0.0726 at the final time step. This trend suggests a gradual increase in inequality among households in terms of healthy livestock distribution. The mean value of the Gini coefficient across all time steps is 0.0302, with a median of 0.0229, indicating that the distribution is slightly skewed towards higher inequality values. The standard deviation of 0.0239 and variance of 0.0006 suggest low variability in the inequality measures over time. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a consistent and significant increasing trend in inequality throughout the entire simulation period. Change points are detected at time steps 51, 71, and 100, suggesting notable shifts in the rate of increase in inequality at these points. The dominant oscillation period of 49 time steps and strong oscillation indices towards the latter part of the simulation suggest some periodic fluctuations in the rate of change of inequality. For decision makers, the steady increase in the Gini coefficient implies that interventions may be needed to address growing disparities in livestock health among households. Understanding the factors contributing to these change points and oscillations could provide insights into the dynamics of inequality and inform targeted policies to promote more equitable distribution of healthy livestock.
