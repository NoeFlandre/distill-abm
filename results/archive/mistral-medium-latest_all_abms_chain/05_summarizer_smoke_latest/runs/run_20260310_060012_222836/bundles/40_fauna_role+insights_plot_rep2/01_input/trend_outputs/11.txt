The total energy of male animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a consistent declining trend across all simulations. Initially, the total energy of male animals starts at approximately 12,000 units. This high energy level is maintained for a brief period, indicating a stable and healthy population of male animals at the beginning of the simulations. 

Around the 500-step mark, a noticeable decline in total energy begins. This decline continues steeply until approximately the 1,000-step mark, where the total energy drops to around 2,000 units. This sharp decrease suggests a significant reduction in the population or health of male animals, potentially due to increased hunting pressure, environmental changes, or other factors affecting their energy levels. 

After the 1,000-step mark, the decline in total energy slows down but continues to decrease gradually. By around the 1,500-step mark, the total energy levels off and stabilizes at a very low value, close to zero. This stabilization at a low energy level indicates that the male animal population has either been significantly reduced or has reached a new equilibrium under the prevailing conditions of the model. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight the critical periods around the 500 to 1,000-step marks where interventions could be most impactful. Understanding the factors contributing to the steep decline in male animal energy during this period could inform strategies to mitigate the effects of hunting pressure or environmental changes, potentially preserving the male animal population and their ecological roles.
