The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the death count starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase. This increasing trend is statistically significant and can be divided into several phases based on the rate of increase. From the beginning to approximately one-sixth of the simulation period, the death count rises sharply with a slope of about 0.346. This is followed by another phase of rapid increase, albeit at a higher rate with a slope of 0.438. The trend continues to rise significantly but at a slower pace in the subsequent phases, with slopes of 0.170 and 0.059 respectively. Around the midpoint of the simulation, the rate of increase slows down considerably, with a slope of 0.017, indicating a plateauing trend. This period of stabilization continues until about three-fifths of the simulation, where the death count fluctuates with no significant trend. Towards the end of the simulation, there is a slight but significant decreasing trend, with a slope close to zero. The death count reaches its peak values around the three-quarter mark of the simulation, with the highest value being approximately 240.33. The data also shows some periodic oscillations towards the end, with a dominant period of 63 ticks. For decision makers, these trends suggest that the initial phases of the simulation are critical, with high mortality rates among the prey population. The plateauing and subsequent slight decrease in death counts towards the end of the simulation may indicate a potential stabilization in the prey population dynamics, which could be crucial for understanding the long-term impacts of hunting pressure and environmental factors on megaherbivore populations.
