The number of households engaging in social learning in the agent-based model shows a clear increasing trend over the 101 time steps. Initially, no households are engaged in social learning, as indicated by the starting value of 0. The number of households adopting social learning increases rapidly in the early stages, with significant growth observed within the first 10 time steps, where the count rises sharply. This rapid increase continues, albeit at a slightly reduced rate, through the subsequent time steps. By time step 63, the number of households engaging in social learning reaches its peak at approximately 32 households. After this point, the number of households engaging in social learning stabilizes and remains relatively constant for the remainder of the simulation. The mean number of households engaging in social learning across all time steps is approximately 27, with a median of 32, indicating that for the majority of the simulation period, around 32 households are consistently engaging in social learning. The standard deviation of 8.92 suggests some variability in the number of households adopting social learning, particularly in the early stages of the simulation. The statistical analysis indicates a consistent increasing trend in social learning adoption, with significant Mann-Kendall trends across various rolling windows, reinforcing the observation of steady growth. The presence of strong oscillation indices suggests periodic fluctuations in the adoption rates, although these do not detract from the overall increasing trend. For decision makers, these trends imply that social learning strategies can gain significant traction over time within a community of smallholder farmers. The rapid initial adoption followed by stabilization suggests that once a critical mass of households adopts social learning, it becomes a sustained practice within the community. This insight can inform policies aimed at promoting adaptive management strategies, highlighting the potential for widespread adoption of beneficial practices through social learning mechanisms.
