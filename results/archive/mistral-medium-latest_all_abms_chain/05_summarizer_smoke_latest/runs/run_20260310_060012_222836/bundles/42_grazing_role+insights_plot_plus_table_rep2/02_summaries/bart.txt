 This agent-based model is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension focuses on incorporating learning capabilities into the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive (co-)management strategies . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system exhibits distinct trends over the simulated time steps . Initially, the biomass starts at approximately 90,000 units . The overall trend indicates an initial decline in biomass followed by a robust recovery and growth phase . For decision makers, these trends imply that while there may be initial variability and a temporary decline in green biomass, the long-term trend indicates a recovery .  The number of households engaging in social learning in the agent-based model shows a clear increasing trend over the 101 time steps . For decision makers, these trends imply that social learning strategies can gain significant traction over time within a community of smallholder farmers . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions to promote more equitable distribution of livestock health benefits .  The number of non-learning households fluctuates but generally follows a downward trajectory . The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis highlights significant decreasing trends in multiple intervals, particularly between steps 11 to 65 . The dominant period of oscillation is 49 steps, suggesting cyclical patterns in the data .
