This sharp rise suggests a significant shift in consumer behavior, likely influenced by initial perceptions of health benefits and environmental concerns associated with alternative milk types. By around the 10th time step, the mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed stabilizes at approximately 1,200 units. This stabilization indicates that a new equilibrium has been reached, where a substantial portion of the population has adopted the consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. Between the 10th and 25th time steps, the consumption remains relatively stable with minor fluctuations. This period of stability suggests that the agents have settled into their new consumption patterns, influenced by their social networks and personal habits. After the 25th time step, there is another noticeable increase in the mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed, reaching around 1,400 units by the 30th time step. This second rise could be attributed to reinforced social norms and continued influence within the social network, further solidifying the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The subsequent stabilization phases indicate the importance of reinforcing social norms and habits to sustain long-term changes in consumer behavior.
