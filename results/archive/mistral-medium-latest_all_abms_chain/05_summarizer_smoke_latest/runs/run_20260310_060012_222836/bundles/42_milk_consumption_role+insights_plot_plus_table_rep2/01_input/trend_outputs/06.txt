The mean quantity of whole milk consumed by agents in the simulation shows a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the mean whole milk quantity starts at approximately 2654.81 units. This high initial value indicates a strong preference or habit for whole milk consumption among the agents at the beginning of the simulation.  

In the early steps, there is a rapid decline in the mean quantity of whole milk consumed. By around the fifth step, the mean quantity drops significantly to approximately 2000 units. This sharp decrease continues, and by the tenth step, the mean quantity further reduces to about 1000 units. This trend suggests that agents are quickly shifting away from whole milk, likely influenced by the model parameters such as social influence, health perceptions, and habit formation thresholds. 

Between steps 10 and 20, the rate of decline slows down, and the mean quantity stabilizes around 800 units. This period of stabilization indicates a phase where the initial rapid shift has slowed, and the remaining agents consuming whole milk are doing so more out of habit or resistance to change. 

After step 20, the mean quantity of whole milk consumed continues to decrease but at a slower and more gradual pace. By step 30, the mean quantity reaches approximately 253.10 units. This final value represents a significant reduction from the initial quantity, indicating a substantial shift in consumption patterns among the agents. 

The statistical analysis confirms these observations, with significant decreasing trends identified in multiple segments of the simulation. The rolling Mann-Kendall test highlights periods of significant decline, particularly in the early and middle steps of the simulation. The presence of inflection points and change points further supports the interpretation of a dynamic shift in consumption behavior. 

For decision-makers, these trends imply that interventions aimed at reducing whole milk consumption can be highly effective, especially when leveraging social influence and health perceptions. The initial rapid decline suggests that once agents are exposed to alternative milk types and their benefits, a significant portion of the population can quickly adapt to new consumption habits. However, the stabilization period indicates that a core group of consumers may require additional incentives or information to fully transition away from whole milk. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing targeted strategies to further reduce whole milk consumption and promote healthier alternatives.
