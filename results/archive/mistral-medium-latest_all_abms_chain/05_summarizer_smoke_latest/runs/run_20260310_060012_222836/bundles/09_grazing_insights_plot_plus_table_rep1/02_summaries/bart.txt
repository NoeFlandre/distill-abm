 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes .  The average household risk attitude in the simulation shows distinct trends over the 100 steps . For decision-makers, these trends highlight the dynamic nature of household risk attitudes over time and the importance of considering short-term fluctuations and long-term stabilization in risk management strategies .  The Gini coefficient for the distribution of healthy livestock across households starts at 0, indicating perfect equality . Initially, the destocking rate rises sharply in the early stages, indicating a rapid increase in the number of livestock being destocked . The subsequent decline and stabilization suggest that households adapt over time, reducing the need for destocking as they potentially optimize their livestock management strategies . Understanding these trends can help help manage livestock management practices, authors say .  The rolling Mann-Kendall test highlights decreasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with no significant trends observed in the later phases . For decision makers, this implies that the number of non-learning households tends to decrease over time, suggesting a potential shift towards learning behaviors among households .
