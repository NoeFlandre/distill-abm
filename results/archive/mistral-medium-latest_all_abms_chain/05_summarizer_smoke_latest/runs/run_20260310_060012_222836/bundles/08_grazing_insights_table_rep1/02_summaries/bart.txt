 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked through a yearly sequence of processes .  Simulation of agent-based model shows distinct trends in the average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents over the course of 100 timesteps . Initial risk attitude of social learning agents did not change, suggesting a stable disposition towards risk among these agents throughout the simulation period .  The average number of livestock destocked across all households in the simulation exhibits notable trends over the 100 timesteps . The trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant decreasing trends in the initial phases (timesteps 0-10, 11-21, and 22-32), followed by periods of no significant trend and a slight increasing trend towards the end . For decision makers, these trends highlight volatility in livestock destocking, followed by a stabilization period .  The number of households following the E-RO strategy, which represents non-learning agents, exhibits a general decreasing trend over the observed time steps . The dominant period of oscillation is 49 time steps, suggesting cyclical patterns within this period . For decision makers, this trend indicates a continuous and accelerating need for destocking over time .
