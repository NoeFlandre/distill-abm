The average destocking rate per household starts at zero during the initial time step (step 0), indicating that no herds were removed at the outset. By step 1, destocking surges to an average of 6.0 households, driven by immediate biophysical stressors such as the high initial herd sizes (90 animals per household) and the stochastic rainfall variability (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100). This aligns with the model’s design, where sudden climate shocks amplify grazing pressure. From step 1 to step 5, the mean destocking fluctuates sharply between 5.4 and 7.2, reflecting compounding effects of resource depletion and herd management decisions. By step 6, the average begins a gradual decline, stabilizing around 4.5 by step 10, as households adapt to rainfall patterns. The midpoint of the simulation (steps 40–60) shows further gradual convergence, with the mean settling near 1.5, suggesting households have optimized herd sizes and grazing practices to align with long-term pasture recovery rates (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1). By step 100, destocking remains low (<0.2 per household), indicating systemic adaptation to risk via restricted herd sizes and learned behavioral strategies.   Paralleling these dynamics, individual household trajectories exhibit diverse paths. Some households experience drastic destocking episodes (e.g., reaching 7.18 households at step 2), likely due to acute climate shocks or herd management failures, followed by secondary stabilization or collapse. Others maintain stable demand patterns (e.g., persistently below 2 households), indicating effective early adaptation. The homogeneity of behavioral strategies (E-RO) across households leads to path-dependent equilibria, with herd sizes contracting to match pasture productivity rather than social interactions. Risk tolerance (risk-att-init=0.5) enables moderate adjustment to uncertainty, but without social learning (social-susceptibility=0.0), aggregation of outcomes is muted. The prolonged decline from step 5 onward underscores the critical role of rainfall variability in shaping destocking timing, while the late-stage stabilization reflects households’ accumulated experience in balancing grazing pressure and biomass recovery. For decision-makers, these patterns highlight the necessity of early climate risk management and the potential for learning processes to reduce destocking volatility over time.
