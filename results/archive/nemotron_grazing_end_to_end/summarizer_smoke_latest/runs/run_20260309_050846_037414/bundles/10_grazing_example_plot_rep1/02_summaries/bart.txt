 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The simulation demonstrates stable average risk attitudes across all agent types throughout the 100-step timeline . Climate-driven biomass fluctuations might indirectly influence risk perceptions, but the absence of strategy switching (SL2-strategy-switch?=False) and intrinsic preference weighting (0.0) prevents measurable behavioral adaptation . The mean reserve biomass follows a distinct three-phase trajectory across 100 timesteps .  The average livestock stock per household begins at approximately 90, reflecting the initial condition of 90 livestock per household (`livestock-init`) under global rainfall variability . Over the first 30–40 steps, there is a sharp decline in the metric, dropping to ~40–41 . This rapid depletion aligns with the absence of resting (`resting? = False`), which allows continuous grazing without recovery periods .
