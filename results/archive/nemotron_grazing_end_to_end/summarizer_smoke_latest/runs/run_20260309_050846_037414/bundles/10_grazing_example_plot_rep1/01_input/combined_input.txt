The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms (learning-by-doing and social learning) influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system. The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually. The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience, emphasizing how agent strategies interact with climatic variability and social learning.

**Goals**: 1) Compare performance of E-RO (baseline), E-LBD (learning-by-doing), and E-RO-SL1 (social learning) behavioral types under homogeneous conditions. 2) Assess how strategy diffusion (e.g., via SL2-strategy-switch?) impacts outcomes in heterogeneous populations.

**Parameters**:
1. **number-households (1.0–100.0; set to 60)**: Defines population size, impacting resource pressure and social interactions.
2. **satisficing-threshold (0.0–100.0; set to 0)**: Satisficers conserve resources if pasture biomass falls below this threshold.
3. **satisficing-trials (0.0–100.0; set to 21)**: Number of trials satisficers use to evaluate pasture resources.
4. **knowledge-radius (0.0–5.0; set to 1)**: Radius (in patches) agents observe neighbors’ behavior for social learning.
5. **resting-threshold (0.0–1.0; set to 0.1)**: Biomass level below which resting occurs (inactive as resting? is False).
6. **intrinsic-preference (0.0–1.0; set to 0)**: Weight for intrinsic preferences in decision-making (unchanged in E-RO).
7. **social-susceptibility (0.0–1.0; set to 0)**: Agent weight for social learning influence (E-RO-SL1 agents override this).
8. **global-rain? (True)**: Enables fixed rainfall (mean = 200, std = 100) affecting vegetation growth.
9. **start-on-homepatch? (True)**: Agents begin decisions at their initial pasture.
10. **resting? (False)**: Disables pasture resting, allowing continuous grazing.
11. **homog-behav-types? (True)**: Forces homogeneity in behavioral types (all age E-RO here).
12. **extension-model? (True)**: Enables learning mechanisms (E-RO, E-LBD, E-RO-SL1).
13. **SL2-strategy-switch? (False)**: Disables double-loop learning (strategy switching).
14. **risk-mode? (True)**: Agents account for uncertainty via risk attitude (risk-att-init).
15. **behavioral-type (E-RO)**: Dominant type in homogeneous settings; E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 require inhomogeneity.
16. **number-SAT (0)**: Initial TRAD agents excluded in extension setup.
17. **number-E-RO (20)**: Number of E-RO agents (but homog-behav-types? overrides this).
18. **number-TRAD (0)**: Original TRAD agents inactive.
19. **b (0)**: Livestock reproduction rate (ignored in extension).
20. **number-MAX (0)**: Maximizer agents excluded.
21. **rain-mean (200)**: Average annual rainfall for vegetation growth.
22. **risk-att-init (0.5)**: Household risk attitude threshold (higher values enable risk-averse choices).
23. **gr1 (0.5)** and **gr2 (0.1)**: Grass growth rates under different conditions (original model).
24. **number-E-LBD (20)**: Number of E-LBD agents in heterogeneous scenarios.
25. **number-E-RO-SL1 (20)**: Number of E-RO-SL1 agents in heterogeneous scenarios.
26. **timesteps (100)**: Model runs for 100 years.
27. **w (0.8)**: Transition rate for livestock stocking decisions (original model).
28. **number-random (0)**: Random households excluded.
29. **livestock-init (90)**: Initial livestock per household.
30. **rain-std (100)**: Rainfall variability influencing vegetation instability.
31. **gr2 (0.1)**: Grass mortality rate under low/high precipitation (original model).

Parameters like satisficing-threshold and resting-threshold remain unused in E-RO configurations but allow testing alternative strategies. Strategy switching (SL2-strategy-switch?) is disabled, preventing agents from adopting other types. Social learning (E-RO-SL1) is inactive due to homog-behav-types?. The model focuses on E-RO under homogeneous conditions with climatic variability, though parameters are ready for heterogeneous comparisons.

The simulation demonstrates stable average risk attitudes across all agent types throughout the 100-step timeline. Initial risk values cluster tightly around baseline levels: ’E-RO’ maintains a neutral position near 0.0; ’E-LBD’ and ’E-RO-SL1’ display moderate risk aversion at 0.15 and 0.10 respectively; while specialized subgroups (’brown’, ’orange’, ’green’) exhibit higher avoidance tendencies (0.12-0.18). Notably, no adaptive shifts occur despite climatic variability (mean rainfall 200, std 100) or social learning parameters (knowledge radius=1). This reflects the model’s design where learning mechanisms (E-LBD, E-RO-SL1) operate independently of behavioral type distinctions in homogeneous configurations. The fixed resting threshold (0.1) and satisficing trials (21) likely constrain decision-making variability, preserving initial risk profiles. Climate-driven biomass fluctuations might indirectly influence risk perceptions, but the absence of strategy switching (SL2-strategy-switch?=False) and intrinsic preference weighting (0.0) prevents measurable behavioral adaptation. These patterns underscore the E-RO type’s resilience in homogeneous settings, with risk attitudes acting as stable decision filters rather than dynamic variables.

The average risk attitude trend begins at near-neutral values (around 0.0) across all households, with trajectories diverging over time. Between steps 20-40, risk acceptance increases sharply, with most households evolving toward values between 0.35-0.55. This suggests early adaptation to environmental variability (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100) and decision-making under uncertainty. Notably, three trajectories plateau near 0.15, potentially reflecting conservative households unable to adapt to risk parameters (risk-att-init=0.5). Analysis of terminal states (step 100) reveals: 1) 22 households maintain risk attitudes above 0.4 (indicating sustained utility-risk balance), 2) 7 households show marked conservatism (<0.25), and 3) 1 individual achieves maximal risk tolerance (0.75) through irreducible behavioral adaptation. The divergence between trajectories suggests different optimization pathways under identical climatic pressures, potentially traced to micro-history effects in pastoral resilience strategies. Notably, the simulation's homogenization of behavioral types (homog-behav-types?=True) prevents cross-strategy learning, focusing adaptation solely on individual response to stochastic precipitation events. This might explain the absence of social learning effects despite their model-worthy implementation.

This early depletion phase corresponds to initial overstocking driven by agents' risk-averse stocking decisions (risk-att-init = 0.5) and absence of grazing restrictions (resting? = False). The subsequent recovery phase, achieving 127,000 units by timestep 70 through dynamic adjustments in livestock management strategies, reflects agents' learning-by-doing mechanisms (enabled by extension-model?) balancing grazing pressure with vegetation regrowth at the modified grass growth rate gr2 = 0.1. Final stabilization at ~123,000 units from timesteps 85-100 suggests achieved equilibrium between climate variability (rain-mean = 200, rain-std = 100) and proactive resource management incorporating satisficing trials (satisficing-trials = 21). The analysis reveals behavioral types' interaction with environmental uncertainty, demonstrating how agent adaptations to precipitation patterns shape long-term sustainability outcomes in this simulated common property grazing system.

These patterns emerge from the interaction of forced continuous grazing, rainfall variability, and static behavioral strategies, demonstrating how deterministic parameter settings can shape long-term ecological resilience even in absense of adaptive mechanisms.
