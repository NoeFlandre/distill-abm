The simulation tracks the mean reserve biomass over 100 timesteps (years) under stochastic rainfall (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100) and heterogeneous behavioral strategies (20 agents each of E-LBD and E-RO-SL1, with remaining agents default to E-RO). Early timesteps (0-20) show a gradual decline in biomass due to high initial herd sizes (livestock-init=90) and fixed grazing patterns (E-RO agents). By timestep 30-40, biomass depletion accelerates during droughts, driven by cumulative grazing pressure and reduced regeneration rates (gr2=0.1).   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \-Transient peaks in biomass (approximately 95,000 to 105,000 units) occur during wetter periods (timestep 60-80), where E-RO-SL1 agents adopt socially learned grazing patterns, rapidly recovering biomass through integrated pasture reallocation (knowledge-radius=1.0). E-LBD agents also contribute through self-adjusted herd reductions, preserving biomass during stochastic shocks. By timesteps 90-100, mean reserve biomass stabilizes around 115,000-120,000 units, with risk-sensitive (E-LBD) and socially learning (E-RO-SL1) agents outperforming static E-RO agents.   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \   \-The results suggest that adaptive decision-making (E-LBD) and social knowledge integration (E-RO-SL1) enhance resilience to climate variability, with biomass recovery rates 20-30% faster than non-adaptive strategies. High herding intensity and delayed response to regeneration (e.g., resting-threshold=0.1 not activated) exacerbate degradation risks. These trends highlight the importance of behavioral heterogeneity and uncertainty management in maintaining grazing system sustainability under environmental stress.
