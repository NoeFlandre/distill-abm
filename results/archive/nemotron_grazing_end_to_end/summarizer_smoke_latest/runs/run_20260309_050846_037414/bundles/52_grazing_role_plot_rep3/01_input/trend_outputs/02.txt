The analysis of the simulated outcomes across 100 steps reveals distinct trajectory patterns among the agent-based households in the addressed pasture-based system simulation. The data demonstrates how different behavioral strategies interact with integrated climate variability and adaptive learning mechanisms over extended periods.

Three agent groups emerge from the initial configuration: 20 households employing reference-based decision-making (E-RO), 20 with learning-by-experience mechanisms (E-LBD), and 20 utilizing social learning approaches (E-RO-SL1). The trajectories show systematic divergence from the reference herd size baseline across the simulation.

Early divergence (steps 0-25) reflects initial climate variability impacts. E-LBD households demonstrate faster adaptation through personal historical data integration, with deviation patterns showing earlier corrective actions during low-rainfall periods. E-RO-SL1 households exhibit slightly delayed but more consistent deviation adjustments, suggesting social learning effectiveness through observation-based experiences. The E-RO group maintains fixed reference-level decisions longer before collapse, indicating inflexible decision-making patterns.

Post-initial-adaptation trends (steps 25-60) reveal climate resilience patterns. Learning-based strategies show compounding effects through iterative decision refinement. Social learners maintain moderate deviations through observational learning, while pure reference agents escalate deviation during pasture stressors before collapse events. Recovery phases (steps 60-80) demonstrate how learning houses maintain previously achieved deviation stabilization, while reference agents experience delayed recovery penalties.

Terminal stabilization (steps 80-100) shows learning agents establishing equilibrium around lower deviation ranges, while reference agents either collapse or enter oscillation patterns. Stochastic rainfall effects (standard deviation 100) create characteristic deviation fluctuation patterns, particularly pronounced during multi-year drought sequences. Risk sensitivity mechanisms manifest most clearly in the stabilization levels of learning-based households post-shock.

The behavioral heterogeneity embedded in the simulation manifests strongly in the deviation dynamics. Learning agents demonstrate adaptive capacity to stochastic environmental pressures through continuous strategy refinement, maintaining mean deviation ranges that avoid extreme outcomes. In contrast, the reference group's fixed behavioral framework reveals systemic vulnerability to environmental variability, resulting in higher median deviation values and more pronounced terminal system failure scenarios. The average across all extension agents captures these differential behavioral impacts through its central tendency trajectory, illustrating the systemic benefits of integrated learning and risk-aware decision frameworks in grazing system management.
