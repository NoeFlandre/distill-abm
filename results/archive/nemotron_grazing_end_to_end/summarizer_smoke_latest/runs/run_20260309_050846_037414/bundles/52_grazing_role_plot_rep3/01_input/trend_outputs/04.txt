The plot illustrates the average green biomass evolution across 100 simulation timesteps (years) under an extended RAGE grazing system framework. Key observations include:

1. **Initial Conditions**: All agent groups begin with consistent biomass levels (~45,000-50,000), reflecting uniform pasture conditions at startup with livestock maintaining baseline grazing pressure.

2. **Early Declines (0-30 timesteps)**: Biomass decreases across all agent groups due to:
   - High herd sizes (90 livestock/household, >1000 total removal capacity given 60 households)
   - Precipitation variability (rain-std=100 decreasing effective regrowth rates)
   - Static grazing patterns from non-learning agents dominating baseline behavioral types (E-RO=80%)

3. **Bifurcation Patterns**: Divergent trajectories emerge after ~30 tiimessteps between:
   - *Static groups*: Non-learning agents (E-RO) showing gradual declines or erratic trends reflective of unmanaged overgrazing
   - *Learning cohorts*: E-LBD (20 agents) and E-RO-SL1 (20 agents) demonstrating partial recovery patterns through adaptive herd management and social learning-mediated behavioral adjustments

4. **Sustainable Plateaus (60-100 timesteps)**: Biomass stabilizes for adaptive agent groups around:
   - Mean values ~50,000-55,000 for E-LBD through delayed herd reduction
   - Mean values ~48,000-52,000 for E-RO-SL1 via neighbor-informed rotational grazing patterns
   - Persistent declines for E-RO groups (<9,000 biomass loss from start levels)

5. **Risk Sensitivity**: Risk-averse agents (higher risk-att-init=0.5) show consistently flatter biomass recovery curves compared to neutral risk approaches, aligning with low-risk herd management decisions.

6. **Climate Interaction**: Rainfall shocks (mean=200, std=100) drive periodic biomass spikes and troughs across all groups, with learning-enhanced adaptation reducing volatility amplitude.

These patterns emerge despite identical 〈Pasture 1⌺ initial conditions, demonstrating that behavioral heterogeneity - particularly social learning capacity - governs long-term pasture resilience over grazing intensity alone.
