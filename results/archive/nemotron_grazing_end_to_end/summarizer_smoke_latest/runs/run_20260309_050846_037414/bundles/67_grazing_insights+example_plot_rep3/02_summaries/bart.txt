 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The average initial risk attitude of all households remains consistently around 0.5 throughout the 100-step simulation, reflecting a moderate aversion to uncertainty in decision-making under risk-prone conditions . Minor variations across individual households suggest localized adaptations to environmental shocks, particularly during periods of extreme precipitation . Decision-makers might leverage these dynamics to prioritize social learning frameworks or adaptive thresholds that mitigate overshoot risks in volatile climates .  The model series exhibits four distinct phases across the 100-year simulation . Biomassible biomass consistently begins between 44,000-45,000 units across all scenarios . At step 60, a universal inflection point occurs, with biomass surging to 60,000 by step 100 . This post-60 acceleration pattern indicates a tipping point where risk-averse stocking behaviors create more sustainable grazing practices .  The equilibration failure reflects systemic vulnerabilities in homogeneous, risk-attuned households maintaining fixed stocking policies under predictable stressors . Rural communities relying on uniform herd management approaches demonstrate heightened inequality when exposed to correlated shocks without adaptive behavioral flexibility . Mitigation requires diversified strategies that balance climate resilience with risk-aware resource distribution .  The stabilization suggests E-RO agents adapt to environmental feedback through fixed rules rather than dynamic learning . The model implicitly demonstrates that risk-averse decision frameworks (risk-att-init=0.5) within rigid rule sets (E-RO) can mitigate but not eliminate long-term degradation under climatic variability .
