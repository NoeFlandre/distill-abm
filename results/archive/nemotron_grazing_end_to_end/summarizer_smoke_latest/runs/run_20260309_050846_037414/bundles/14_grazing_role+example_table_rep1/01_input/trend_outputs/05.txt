The total number of livestock declines steadily over time, reflecting long-term degradation of pasture resources under static grazing behaviors. Starting at an initial value of approximately 90.0 livestock units per household, the metric shows a consistent downward trajectory from early timesteps, with a sharp initial drop to around 77.0–80.0 by timestep 15. This initial decline aligns with the high initial herd sizes (livestock-init=90) exceeding pasture carrying capacity, triggering rapid biomass depletion. By mid-simulation (timesteps 30–50), the metric stabilizes around 55.0–65.0, indicating partial resource recovery during periods of favorable rainfall. However, from timesteps 55 onward, the trend accelerates sharply, falling to 45.0–50.0, as cumulative pasture degradation overwhelms the system’s capacity for regeneration. Notably, stochastic climate variability (rain-std=100) introduces annual fluctuations, causing temporary rebounds during wetter periods (e.g., mid-40s to 55.0) but failing to reverse the long-term decline. The absence of adaptive strategies (all agents fixed as E-RO) prevents behavioral adjustments like herd reduction or shifting grazing practices, exacerbating resource depletion. The sharpest declines correlate with compounded effects of static grazing pressure (high livestock-init) and slowed biomass recovery during prolonged dry intervals, highlighting the unsustainability of non-adaptive systems under environmental uncertainty.
