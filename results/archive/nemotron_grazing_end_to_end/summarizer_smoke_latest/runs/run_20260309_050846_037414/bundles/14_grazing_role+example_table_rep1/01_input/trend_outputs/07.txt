The Gini coefficient of healthy livestock exhibits an initial period of near-equilibrium homogeneity, followed by pronounced inequality emergence, peak divergence, and eventual partial stabilization. The metric begins at effectively zero (~1e-16) for the first 20 time steps, reflecting uniform herd health and size conditions consistent with the model's fixed starting patch assignment and homogeneous initial behavioral types (E-RO). From ~step 20 to ~step 90, inequality metric values escalate from 2.6e-4 to over 0.03, peaking at 0.0151 in ~step 40-50 before declining to 0.0072 by ~step 100. This trajectory aligns with adaptive behaviors unfolding under stochastic climate pressures (σ=100 for rainfall) and risk-sensitive decision-making (risk-parameter=0.5). Early divergence likely stems from heterogeneous responses to rainfall variability, where households employing risk-aware herd-size adjustments experience amplified outcomes. Later stabilization suggests limited herd size variation across households by simulation end, potentially indicating behavioral strategy filtering or resource saturation. The absence of pasture rest mechanisms and high initial livestock-herd size (90) show how compounded grazing pressures under uncertainty create inequality thresholds, while the limited social learning radius (1.0) prevents broad strategy diffusion that might mitigate inequality.
