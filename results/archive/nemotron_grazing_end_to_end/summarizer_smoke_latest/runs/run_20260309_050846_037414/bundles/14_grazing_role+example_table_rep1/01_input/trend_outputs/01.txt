Initial values cluster tightly around the model’s baseline risk tolerance (risk-att-init=0.5, mean≈0.075), but deviations emerge as simulations progress. Within the first 10–20 time steps, mean values decline asymmetrically: some households show mild risk aversion (negative values down to -0.11), while others adopt risk-seeking behaviors (up to 0.18), likely responding to stochastic rainfall effects (global-rain?=True, rain-mean=200, rain-std=100). This divergence suggests heterogeneous outcomes from co-pasture grazing pressures, with no pasture rest mechanisms active (resting?=False).  

After 20–30 steps, values stabilize around -0.05 to 0.11, indicating partial convergence in risk attitudes. Negative values may reflect agents penalized by drought years (rain-std=100), while positive values could stem from households rewarded for opportunistic grazing before rainfall shocks. The lack of social-susceptibility (social-susceptibility=0.0) implies these shifts are driven by individual learning-by-doing (E-RO-SL1) rather than direct neighbor influence.  

Mid-to-late simulation phases (30–100 steps) exhibit minor fluctuations (±0.03), suggesting equilibria where adaptive strategies mitigate extreme resource depletion risks. The dataset’s long-term stability aligns with the model’s goal of evaluating how E-RO-SL1 agents balance learning and tradition in volatile environments. Variability across simulations (e.g., occasional outliers near -0.2 or 0.2) likely reflects uncorrelated climate shocks dispersed across herds, exacerbated by fixed initial livestock densities (livestock-init=90) and no reproduction-based behavioral feedbacks (b=0.0).  

The absence of satisficing thresholds (satisficing-threshold=0.0) ensures agents exhaustively explore options, amplifying responsiveness to environmental variability. Overall, the trends underscore the role of risk sensitivity in sustaining grazing systems under stochastic climate stress, with social learning agents dynamically recalibrating herd sizes to align with ecosystem resilience. This pattern reinforces the model’s objective to analyze how behavioral heterogeneity shapes socio-ecological outcomes in common-pool resource settings.
