The data reveals a sustained decline in the average total livestock numbers per household over a 61-year simulation period. Initially, livestock numbers remained stable at 90.0 for the first two years, followed by a gradual reduction beginning in year 3. The decline accelerates incrementally over time, with annual averages dropping from 84.0 in year 3 to approximately 45.7 by the final timestep. This downward trend persists despite climatic fluctuations (e.g., rainfall variability with \( \text{rain-mean} = 200, \text{rain-std} = 100 \)), which introduce year-to-year oscillations in livestock numbers but do not reverse the long-term trajectory. The uniform reduction across households aligns with the E-RO strategy’s static decision-making under homogeneous conditions, where agents do not employ learning or adaptive mechanisms. The model’s lack of pasture resting and absent social learning prevents resource recovery, leading to irreversible degradation of grazing capacity. Decision makers should prioritize integrating dynamic strategies like learning-by-doing or social learning to mitigate resource depletion risks in variable environments.
