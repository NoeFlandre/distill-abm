 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The model focuses on E-RO under homogeneous conditions with climatic variability, though parameters are ready for heterogeneous comparisons . Most individuals exhibit moderate risk attitudes, clustering near zero, though a subset demonstrates distinctively risk-averse (-0.04 to -0.11) tendencies .  Simulations demonstrate resilience or recovery, indicating systemic susceptibility to degradation under static decision-making . Decision-makers should note that homogeneous, non-adaptive strategies (e.g., E-RO) fail to sustain biomass in variable climates, suggesting urgency for integrating learning mechanisms (LBD, SL) to improve resource management outcomes .  Homogeneous satisficing strategies may fail to buffer against climatic extremes, necessitating flexibility mechanisms like social learning (e.g., E-RO-SL1 activation) to stabilize outcomes in real-world grazing systems . The Gini coefficient of livestock health in the early stages of the simulation suggests relatively equitable livestock health distribution in early stages .  The data shows a gradual increase in the average number of destocked livestock across all households over time . The trend suggests that agents respond to environmental variability, likely driven by climatic fluctuations affecting pasture availability . For decision-makers, the trend underscores the potential need for adaptive strategies to manage resource use under unstable environmental conditions .
