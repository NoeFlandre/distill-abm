The average reserve biomass across all patches remains stable at approximately 90,000 units during the initial 20–30 timesteps, reflecting equilibrium conditions where grazing pressure (livestock-init=90) and biomass regeneration rates (gr1=0.5 for wet seasons, gr2=0.1 for dry seasons) balance resource consumption. Around timestep 30–40, biomass begins fluctuating between 85,000 and 91,000 units, coinciding with the activation of stochastic rainfall variability (global-rain?=True) and risk-aware decision-making (risk-mode?=True). These shocks create temporary stress on pastures, causing biomass to dip but recover as agents adapt grazing strategies.  

By timesteps 50–100, biomass stabilizes around 85,000–91,000 units, indicating emergent equilibrium under climate uncertainty. The extended model’s learning strategies (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 agents) likely contribute to this stabilization, as agents adjust herd sizes and grazing intensity based on historical outcomes (learning-by-doing) and neighbor observations (social learning within knowledge-radius=1.0). However, the absence of resting (<resting?=False) and satisficing behavior (<satisficing-threshold=0.0) means pasture recovery remains dependent on exogenous rainfall patterns and endogenous learning dynamics.  

After timestep 100, biomass shows minor oscillations but maintains a slight upward trend toward ~90,000 units. This suggests that risk-tolerant agents (risk-att-init=0.5) and learning mechanisms (particularly E-RO-SL1) may dampen long-term degradation by promoting adaptive herd management. However, the persistent heterogeneity in biomass values across patches (e.g., peaks at ~108,000 and troughs at ~85,000 in later timesteps) indicates localized variability driven by uneven learning adoption and climate shocks. The data imply that delaying satisficing and enabling heterogeneous behavioral strategies could improve system resilience to climate variability, though the extended simulation would be needed to confirm these hypotheses.
