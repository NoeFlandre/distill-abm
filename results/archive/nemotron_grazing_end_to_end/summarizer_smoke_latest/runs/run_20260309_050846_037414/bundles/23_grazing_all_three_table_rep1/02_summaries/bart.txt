 The agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE model, focusing on simulating smallholder farmers' decision-making in pasture-based systems with learning capabilities . The original model was designed to analyze feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in common property grazing systems . This extension adds heterogeneity in behavioral strategies (e.g., learning-by-doing, social learning) and introduces risk sensitivity .  The simulation demonstrates a dynamic shift in average household risk tolerance across time . The initial risk attitudes of social learning agents (E-RO-SL1) exhibit moderate fluctuations around the neutral threshold . The persistent variability underscores the interplay between environmental uncertainty and social diffusion mechanisms in shaping herd management strategies .  The average reserve biomass across all patches remains stable at approximately 90,000 units during the initial 20–30 timesteps . This suggests that risk-tolerant agents (risk-att-init=0.5) and learning mechanisms (particularly E-RO-SL1) may dampen long-term degradation .  The average total livestock per household begins at 90 across all timesteps but shows a gradual decline starting around timestep 10 . The persistent decline reflects unsustainable grazing pressure from high initial herd sizes (90 livestock/household) combined with disabled pasture rest mechanisms . The trajectory underscores the necessity of pasture rest protocols, rotational grazing, and adaptive risk management for smallholder systems in stochastic climates .  Simulation shows a gradual increase in households engaging in social learning (household-SL? = true) over time, despite initial conditions where no agents exhibited this behavior . Learning strategies (e.g., social learning, double-loop learning) could mitigate inequality by enabling adaptive grazing practices .  The variability in trends highlights the interplay between risk tolerance (risk-att-init=0.5) and grazing pressure from high initial herd sizes . The final reduction to 0–4 households aligns with the model’s focus on heterogeneous strategy adoption . These patterns underscore the importance of adaptive decision-making in maintaining long-term viability under environmental uncertainty .
