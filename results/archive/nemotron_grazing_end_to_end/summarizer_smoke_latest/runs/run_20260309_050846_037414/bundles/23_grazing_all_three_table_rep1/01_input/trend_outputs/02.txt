The simulation demonstrates a dynamic shift in average household risk tolerance across time, with agents initially exhibiting low risk-taking behavior (mean risk-att clustering around 0.075 in early timesteps) that gradually intensifies to peak at 0.35 by the final timestep, followed by a slower stabilization phase. The lowest values occur at timestep 16 (0.018) and timestep 8 (0.045), while the highest cluster around 0.35 in timesteps 24 and 30. This pattern reflects the interplay between risk-sensitive decision-making, climate variability (rain-std=100), and behavioral strategy interactions. Early in the simulation, agents maintain conservative herd sizes (high risk-att values) amid stochastic rainfall shocks, likely prioritizing resource conservation. By timestep 20, increased risk-taking emerges (mean risk-att=0.18), correlating with observations of agents adopting riskier grazing strategies during simulated drought conditions. The final stabilization phase (post-timestep 25) shows reduced variance in risk attitudes (0.18–0.25 range), suggesting emergent consensus patterns as social learning-based agents (E-RO-SL1) propagate adaptive strategies within their knowledge-radius. Notably, the absence of satisficing constraints (satisficing-threshold=0.0) enables prolonged exploration of herd size decisions, creating feedback loops where early survival-critical risk-taking becomes institutionalized. These trends underscore the model's capacity to model how behavioral heterogeneity and environmental uncertainty shape long-term risk management strategies in pastoral systems.
