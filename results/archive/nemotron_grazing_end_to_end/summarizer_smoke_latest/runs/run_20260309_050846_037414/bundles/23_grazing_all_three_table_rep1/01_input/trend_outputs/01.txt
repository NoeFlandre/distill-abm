The initial risk attitudes of social learning agents (E-RO-SL1) exhibit moderate fluctuations around the neutral threshold, with notable temporal variability influenced by environmental stochasticity and peer learning dynamics. Values oscillate between approximately -0.18 and +0.18 across 29 observed time steps, reflecting adaptive adjustments to pasture conditions and social influence. Early in the simulation (time steps 1-4), agents show slight risk-seeking tendencies (mean ~0.07-0.08), likely due to baseline herd sizes (90 animals) exceeding pasture carrying capacity under average rainfall conditions (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100). By time steps 5-7, several herds reduce risk exposure (values < -0.06), possibly as biomass regeneration (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1) stabilizes pasture pressure temporarily. A pronounced surge in risk-taking (0.18 average at time step 8) coincides with elevated rainfall variability, suggesting agents exploit favorable conditions through social learning, amplified by their receptive knowledge-sharing radius (1.0). Later dynamics (time steps 18-21) show sustained risk-aversion (-0.06 to -0.1), indicating recovery from overexploitation or climate shocks. The persistent variability, despite homogeneous starting conditions (behavioral-type=E-RO-SL1), underscores the interplay between environmental uncertainty and social diffusion mechanisms in shaping herd management strategies.
