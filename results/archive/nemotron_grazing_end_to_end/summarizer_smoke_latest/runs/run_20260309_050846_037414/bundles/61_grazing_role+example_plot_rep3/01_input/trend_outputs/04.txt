The total green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units at time step 0, representing the initial equilibrium before grazing pressure intensifies. A rapid decline occurs across all runs during the first 25 steps, reaching near depletion levels (~20,000) by step 30 due to overarching livestock consumption fueled by high initial herd sizes (90 animals per household) and limited pasture recovery capacity. This initial collapse reflects the system's vulnerability to unregulated grazing, compounded by stochastic rainfall fluctuations that exacerbate resource scarcity during early simulated drought phases.   Subsequent recovery phases initiate in the second quarter of the simulation (steps 40–50), where biomass levels surge above initial values to exceed 50,000. This rebound correlates with adaptive reductions in herd sizes, driven by risk-sensitive agents balancing consumption with pasture degradation forecasts. By step 80, most runs converge around 55,000–60,000 biomass units, stabilized through optimized grazing rotations enacted by learning agents. Variance across runs remains minimal in the first 50 steps but proliferates post-step 70 (coefficient of variation > 0.4) as heterogeneous strategies—particularly the 20 agents employing double-loop learning—pursue divergent management pathways, inadvertently creating localized resource gradients that challenge long-term equilibrium maintenance.
