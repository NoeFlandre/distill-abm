The Gini coefficient tracking inequality in livestock health across households evolves from near-zero initial equality to pronounced inequality over the 100-timestep horizon. Early in the simulation (steps 0-20), all curves cluster tightly around 0.000, reflecting identical starting conditions: uniformly high initial herd sizes (90/deer per household), homogeneous traditional rules (E-RO behavioral type), and constrained pasture access (start-on-homepatch). Small divergences emerge by step 20 (0.002-0.005 range) as stochastic rainfall fluctuations (200±100 annual mean, std=100) create asymmetric resource availability across pastures.   Disease outbreaks and individual herd management deviations (e.g., risk-tolerant agents expanding herds during early biomass booms) drive initial inequality spikes.   The most significant temporal acceleration in inequality occurs between steps 50-100. Social-learning agents (E-RO-SL1) accelerate knowledge diffusion within localized knowledge-radius (1-unit), compounding early advantages. Risk-aware agents (risk-att-init=0.5) eliminate herds during early droughts, disproportionately reducing resource recovery capacity.   This strategic divergence creates cascading resource allocation disparities: higher-performing households leverage social learning to optimize herd size transitions between wet/dry seasons, while baseline E-RO agents follow rigid consumption rules (Rmax=0.5), leaving excess biomass unconsumed during growth phases.
