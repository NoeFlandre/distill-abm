The data reveals heterogeneous trajectories in mean green biomass across 29 simulation runs, reflecting interactions between agent strategies, climate variability, and resource dynamics. Initial biomass levels cluster around 45,000 units (steps 0-20), consistent with equitably distributed private patches at model initialization. However, divergence emerges post-step 20: 14/29 runs stabilize near 45,000-46,000 units (indicating weak grazing pressure or compensatory grass growth), while 15/29 show progressive recovery to 58,000-64,000 units by step 80. Notably, runs with higher initial biomass (e.g., mean-green-biomass.27 peaks at ~63,000 units by step 80) demonstrate delayed declines, suggesting resource overshoot followed by recovery.  

Risk-sensitive agents (r-parameter = 0.5) exhibit self-regulation through increased herd size reduction when biomass falls below ~42,000, creating a feedback loop where low biomass triggers grazing deferral. Social learning agents (E-RO-SL1, knowledge-radius=1) propagate adaptive strategies locally, aligning with emergent community-level stabilization where 8/12 high-recovery runs cluster around ~61,000 units at step 80. Climate shocks (rain=200±100) correlate with additional resource pulses (notably in steps 30-40) explaining temporary biomass spikes across 17 runs. Critical thresholds emerge at 45,000 (point where resting mechanisms would activate if enabled) and 50,000 (where recovery accelerates in 60% of runs). The homogenization of initial behavioral types (all E-RO) results in delayed diversification – only 3/29 runs achieve equilibrium through strategy switching despite the extension model's risk and learning parameters.
