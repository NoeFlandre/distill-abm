 The agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE model, focusing on simulating smallholder farmers' decision-making in pasture-based systems with learning capabilities . The original model was designed to analyze feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in common property grazing systems . This extension adds heterogeneity in behavioral strategies (e.g., learning-by-doing, social learning) and introduces risk sensitivity .  The average initial risk attitudes of social learning agents (E-RO-SL1) across households display a bimodal distribution with central clustering around ±0.05, slight positive skew toward higher risk tolerance (mean 0.07–0.08) and notable variability (±0.02) in 2–4 households . The simulation data reveals temporal patterns in household risk attitudes and livestock management strategies .  The modeled system exhibits a dynamic interplay between grazing pressure, pasture recovery, and adaptive behavioral strategies across the 100-year simulation period . Stochastic rainfall variability creates cumulative pasture stress, forcing herd reductions despite initial overshooting . Long-term trends revealed diminishing returns in biomass accumulation after ~80 years, potentially from pasture saturation thresholds (Ρ_max=0.5)  Initial biomass levels cluster around 45,000 units (steps 0-20), consistent with equitably distributed private patches at model initialization . But divergence emerges post-step 20, suggesting resource overshoot followed by recovery . Climate shocks (rain=200±100) correlate with additional resource pulses (notably in steps 30-40) explaining temporary biomass spikes across 17 runs .  The Gini coefficient for total livestock health begins near-zero at time step 0, indicating initial equity in herd health across households . The model’s design favors inequality through consolidated grazing territories (start-on-homepatch=True) and limited information flow (knowledge-radius=1.0) Risk-aware agents with r=0.5 balance exploration and exploitation but lack adaptive capacity to counteract entrenched inequality . Heterogeneous behavioral strategies emerge naturally due to stochastic rainfall  At steps 0-6, all households exhibit no destocking activity . At step 6, universal destocking begins, with each household uniformly increasing their total by 6.0 livestock . By step 29, subtle divergence emerges at step 29 suggesting early signs of non-uniform decision-making . From step 100 onward, differences between households propagate sharply, with total destocking escalating exponentially .
