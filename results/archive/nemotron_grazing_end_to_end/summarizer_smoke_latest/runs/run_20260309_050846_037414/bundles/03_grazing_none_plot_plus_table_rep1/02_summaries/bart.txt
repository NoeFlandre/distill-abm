 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The mean initial risk attitudes of households remain constant across all reported simulations, with no variation observed between time steps or simulation iterations . The homogeneity in behavioral type (all E-RO agents under homog-behav-types?) appears to manifest as fixed risk parameters rather than social learning aggregation .  The data shows a consistent pattern across simulations: an initial stability, followed by a significant decline, then a recovery phase converging to a stable equilibrium . The dynamics reflect agent-based equilibrium-seeking behavior - initial conditions within the "satisficing" zone lead to gradual exploration (dip) followed by reinforced conservation behaviors . The model's focus on learning mechanisms (extension-model? = True) is partially constrained by the parameters, emphasizing ecological feedbacks over behavioral innovation .  The data reflects the Gini coefficient of 'Total Livestock Healthy' over 100 simulation steps, indicating inequality in livestock health outcomes across households managing a common-grazing system . The sharpest Gini increases occur between steps 5–10, aligning with the model's focus on resilience under climate-induced grazing challenges . Long-term outcomes (steps 80–100) suggest persistent inequality beyond initial divergences .  Consistent resting-threshold (0.1) and satisficing-trails (21) parameters suggest households with sustained pasture health (e.g., Households 5, 9) maintain lower destocking through adaptive grazing management . Top destocking values (168-172) suggest critical resource collapse thresholds .
