The data reveals a sharp initial decline in livestock stocking across all households, peaking between steps 5-10, followed by a gradual stabilization. By step 100, households maintain consistent destocking levels near 0.5-1.0, suggesting a long-term equilibrium. The rapid reduction in steps 1-10 aligns with raster pressure reduction due to climatic variability (high rainfall standard deviation), while the stabilization phase reflects adaptive adjustments to precipitation dynamics. The presence of satisficing strategies with no resting threshold (only active grazing parameter enabled) explains the persistent destocking near thresholds. These patterns indicate that behaviorally homogeneous households effectively mitigate ecological stress through individual learning but do not exhibit aggregate oscillations, suggesting the absence of herding behaviors in this parameter configuration. For decision makers, this highlights the immediacy of short-term responses to environmental shocks and the importance of maintaining flexible livestock plans to match climatic realities.
