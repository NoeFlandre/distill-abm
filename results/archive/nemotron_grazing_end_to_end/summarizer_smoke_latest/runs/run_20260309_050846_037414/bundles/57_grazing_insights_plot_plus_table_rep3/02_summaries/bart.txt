 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The model focuses on E-RO under homogeneous conditions with climatic variability, though parameters are ready for heterogeneous comparisons . The average initial risk attitudes of households remain constant across all time steps, with no observed deviations or shifts in values as steps progress . This suggests that once agents begin making risk-attitude-related decisions, their behavior stabilizes quickly . Gini coefficient values show a clear trend of increasing inequality in livestock health across households over the 100-step simulation period . By step 100, households maintain consistent destocking levels near 0.5-1.0, suggesting a long-term equilibrium . For decision makers, this highlights the importance of maintaining flexible livestock plans to match climatic realities .  Despite disabled social learning, E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 parameters hint at latent learning capacity .
