The Gini coefficients across all households exhibit a general increasing trend over time, indicating rising inequality in livestock health distribution in simulated scenarios. While all runs start near equality (Gini ≈ 0.001–0.002 initially), values escalate significantly by step 100 (ranging from 0.006 to 0.010, with one extreme reaching 0.030). These trajectories suggest that adaptive strategies and environmental stochasticity (e.g., rainfall variability, high initial herd sizes) amplify disparities between households over long-term simulation periods.   \n
Key trends include:   \n- **Step-dependent inequality acceleration**: Most runs show steady Gini increases after step 40, with sharper rises occurring after step 70, aligning with the model's growing complexity as households experience resource depletion, climate shocks, and behavioral adaptation phases.   \n- **High variability across behavioral runs**: Extensions of the RAGE model (e.g., **number-E-LBD**/**number-E-RO-SL1** agents) demonstrate divergent outcomes, where learning-focused agents (E-LBD, E-RO-SL1) occasionally reduce inequality temporarily before divergence. However, long-term inequality is consistently reinforced by parametric constraints like **livestock-init** (90 animals/farmer) and **w** (0.8 grazing intensity).   \n- **Stochastic resilience thresholds**: Runs incorporating global rainfall variability (**global-rain?**=True) highlight volatility in Gini metrics, particularly at drought peaks (step 40–60), suggesting climate shocks disproportionately impact resource-poor households.   \n
Implications for decision-makers include prioritizing policies to mitigate initial resource inequities (e.g., herd size caps) and incentivizing social learning mechanisms to diffuse adaptive practices. The model underscores that behavioral strategy heterogeneity (**behavioral-type**) and uncertainty management (**risk-parameter**) are critical determinants of equity in smallholder systems.
