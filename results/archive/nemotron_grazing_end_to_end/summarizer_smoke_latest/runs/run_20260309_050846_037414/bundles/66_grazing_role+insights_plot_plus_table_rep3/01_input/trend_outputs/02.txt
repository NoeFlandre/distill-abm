The data reveals several key patterns in mean household risk attitudes across simulated households. Initially (first ~20 steps), values show moderate divergence from baseline risk attitude, with most households maintaining positive attitudes between 0.01-0.06. A significant shift occurs between steps 40-60 where 12 households (rows 23-26) exhibit sharp declines, with values dropping to -0.09 to -0.19 range. This pattern coincides with periodic rainfall variability parameters (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100) likely reflecting drought impacts. By final steps (rows 55-65), 8 households demonstrate sustained negative risk attitudes below -0.3, correlating with extended dry season recovery rates (gr2=0.1). Learning strategies (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 agents) show distinct trajectories - social learning agents (SL1) maintain relatively stable mid-range attitudes (0.03-0.07) while learning-by-doing agents display greater volatility, with 4 households (rows 58-61) experiencing complete strategy collapse. Non-learning agents (baseline E-RO) show the most consistent mid-range patterns (-0.03 to 0.05). The extended timestep (100 years) allows these adaptive dynamics to emerge, with morning pasture depletion events (start-on-homepatch=True) potentially accelerating risk attitude deterioration in high-pressure herding environments.
