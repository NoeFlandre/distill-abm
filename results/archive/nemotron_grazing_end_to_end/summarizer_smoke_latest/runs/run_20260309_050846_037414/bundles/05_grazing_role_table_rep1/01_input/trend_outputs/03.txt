The reserve biomass data exhibits a predominantly declining trend over the 30 time steps, with notable variability between replicates driven by stochastic climate shocks and agent behavior. Initially, all replicates maintained identical biomass levels (mean-reserve-biomass.0: 90,000; mean-reserve-biomass.1: 90,180; mean-reserve-biomass.2: 90,427.1), suggesting deterministic responses to the initial conditions (livestock-init=90, w=0.8). From time step .3 onward, divergence emerged due to stochastic rainfall (global-rain?=True, rain-mean=200, rain-std=100), with some replicates experiencing temporary gains (e.g., 90,535.06 at step .2 to 90,738.10 at step .5) and others sharp declines (e.g., 89,216 in step .6 to 87,929 in step .27).   \
 \\n Key patterns include: 1) **Early herd stress** from high initial livestock-init=90, creating immediate grazing pressure that accelerated biomass depletion despite stochastic rainfall variability. 2) **Risk-sensitive agents** (risk-att-init=0.5, risk-mode?=True) likely moderated herd sizes in some replicates, evident in slower declines than the average. 3) **Learning strategies** (E-LBD=20, E-RO-SL1=20) demonstrated limited impact, as social-learning constraints (knowledge-radius=1.0, social-susceptibility=0.0) restricted information flow, and satisficing behavior was disabled (satisficing-threshold=0.0). 4) **Replicate outliers** (e.g., 114,571.89 biomass at step .16) reflect exceptional rainfall sequences or risk-tolerant herding decisions. \\n \\n By time step .29, biomass had fallen to a median of ~87,000, with 10% of replicates dropping below 87,000. The localized increases (e.g., step .24: 90,543.19) and declines (step .25: 89,216) highlight the interplay between stochastic climate and heterogeneous agent-level uncertainty management. Despite risk-mode activation, fixed behavioral strategies (E-RO-dominant) and lack of dynamic adaptation ultimately constrained system resilience. \\n \\n In the context of the model’s goals, these trends underscore challenges in balancing risk-aware decision-making with pastoral adaptation, particularly when learning is constrained to immediate experience (E-LBD) or local social imitation (E-RO-SL1). The data suggests that without integrated multi-scale learning or institutional buffering, stochastic shocks and fixed strategies synergistically degrade pasture resilience, aligning with the model’s focus on evaluating long-term social-ecological trade-offs.
