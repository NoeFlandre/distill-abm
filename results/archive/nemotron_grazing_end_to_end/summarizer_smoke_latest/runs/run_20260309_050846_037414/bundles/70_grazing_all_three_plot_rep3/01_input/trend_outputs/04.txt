The mean green biomass follows a dynamic pattern characterized by an initial sharp decline, followed by a recovery phase, and subsequent long-term fluctuations. Within 20 steps, biomass drops rapidly from ~45,000 to ~40,000, reflecting immediate grazing pressure from high initial herds (livestock-init=90). This collapse aligns with the model’s focus on overgrazing risks in high-density systems. Recovery begins at ~40 steps, peaking at ~60 steps (~60,000 biomass), suggesting adaptive herd adjustments. The rise correlates with risk-sensitive agents (risk-att-init=0.5) reducing grazing intensity post-collapse, leveraging stochastic climate shocks (rain-std=100) to recalibrate herd sizes. Post-peak dynamics (steps 60–100) show divergence: some runs stabilize near 58,000–62,000, while others oscillate or decline, highlighting heterogeneity in behavioral strategies (E-LBD: 20 agents; E-RO-SL1: 20 agents). Risk-averse agents (E-LBD) likely minimize overgrazing by conservatively adjusting herds, while social learners (E-RO-SL1) synchronize with localized biomass cues. Long-term fluctuations (rainfall variability, rain-std=100) amplify instability, stressing adaptive thresholds. Decision-makers should prioritize dynamic herd management, risk-sensitive stocking rates, and localized information-sharing to buffer against climate-driven shocks. The model underscores trade-offs between resilience and short-term productivity under uncertainty.
