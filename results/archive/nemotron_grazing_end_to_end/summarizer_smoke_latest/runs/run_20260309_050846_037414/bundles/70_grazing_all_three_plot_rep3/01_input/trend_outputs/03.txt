The total reserve biomass starts at approximately 90,000 units, which declines rapidly over the first 30–40 time steps, reaching a low of around 80,000 units. This initial decline reflects the cumulative biomass depletion caused by high grazing pressure from livestock, as households begin with 90 animals each, totaling 5,400 livestock units across 60 households. By step 40, the system stabilizes at low biomass levels, indicating a tipping point where resource scarcity begins to constrain grazing practices.   After step 40, all curves sharply rise, peaking simultaneously at ~118,000–120,000 units between steps 60–70, before plateauing at stable values between 112,000 and 128,000. This recovery phase suggests the adoption of adaptive strategies to manage grazing pressure, such as herd size reductions and optimized consumption patterns learned through experience or social influence. The flattening of curves post-70 steps indicates equilibrium between biomass consumption and recovery, with variations between 112,000–128,000 units reflecting the tension between interannual rainfall variability (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100) and the applied behavioral strategies.   Agent types with higher risk sensitivity (e.g., those modifying herd sizes) align with observed recovery, as do learning mechanisms enabled by the extension model (e.g., E-LBD agents adjusting based on past outcomes, E-RO-SL1 agents emulating neighbors). The stabilization and higher-than-initial equilibrium biomass underscore the role of adaptive management in sustaining common property resources despite inherent stochasticity.   }
