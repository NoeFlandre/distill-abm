 The agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE model, focusing on simulating smallholder farmers' decision-making in pasture-based systems with learning capabilities . The original model was designed to analyze feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in common property grazing systems . This extension adds heterogeneity in behavioral strategies (e.g., learning-by-doing, social learning) and introduces risk sensitivity .  The plot illustrates the temporal evolution of the mean initial risk attitude across all social learning agents (20 E-RO-SL1 households) over 100 years in a pasture-based grazing system . Agents’ risk tolerance (r-parameter) begins at 0.5 (risk-neutral) but shows distinct deviations as shaped by environmental stochasticity and learning dynamics .  The average deviation from the recommended number of livestock begins near zero at the start of the simulation, indicating alignment with the initial target herd size . The mean green biomass follows a dynamic pattern characterized by an initial sharp decline, followed by a recovery phase, and subsequent long-term fluctuations . After step 40, the system stabilizes at low biomass levels, indicating a tipping point where resource scarcity begins to constrain grazing practices .  The model underscores trade-offs between resilience and short-term productivity under uncertainty . The Gini coefficient of healthy livestock per household evolves through three distinct phases across the 100-step simulation . By step 60, localized adaptation minimizes uniform pasture degradation, but the system shows a bifurcation . Traditional herds show declining herd health, while social learners aggregate in productive micro-regions .
