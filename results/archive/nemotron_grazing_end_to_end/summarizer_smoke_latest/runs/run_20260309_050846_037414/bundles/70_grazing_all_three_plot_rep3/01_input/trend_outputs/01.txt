The plot illustrates the temporal evolution of the mean initial risk attitude across all social learning agents (20 E-RO-SL1 households) over 100 years (steps) in a pasture-based grazing system. Agents’ risk tolerance (r-parameter) begins at 0.5 (risk-neutral) but shows distinct deviations as shaped by environmental stochasticity and learning dynamics. Three key trends emerge:   \begin{enumerate}
  <li><b>Early destabilization (steps 0–40):</b> A sharp drop to ~0.1 (risk-averse) occurs due to initial pasture collapse under high livestock pressure (livestock-init=90) and drought-linked biomass decay (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100). This pressure was apparent as pasture recovery lagged during stochastic dry spells, depleting commons and increasing coordinatorogenomic-per-household livestock losses. Despite inheriting fixed rules, E-RO-SL1 agents began adjusting their r-parameter downward as individual failures compounded.
  <li><b>Post-50 step recovery (50–70):</b> The mean rebounds toward 0.4 (moderate risk tolerance) during extreme wet seasons (e.g., step 50–55 rainfall spike), driven E-RO-SL1 agents implicitly learning from exogenous climate shocks. Social learning via knowledge-radius=1.0 enabled observation of neighbors’ successful herd-reduction choices, though no explicit strategy switching occurred (SL2-strategy-switch?=False).
  <li><b>Late-phase convergence (70–100):</b> Mean r stabilizes near 0.3–0.4 as agents equilibrate with the carrying capacity limited by resting?=False rules (no automated pasture rest) and persistent mortality (mr=0.05). E-LBD agents reinforced risk-aversion through repeated trial-and-error herd crashes, while E-RO-SL1 agents historically anchored decisions in short-term neighbor comparisons, creating diffuseness in risk alignment.
\end{enumerate} These patterns highlight three policy implications: first, E-RO-SL1 agents’ social learning alone cannot counteract systemic degradation without intrinsic resting mechanisms, suggesting the need for institutional resting protocols (resting?=True). Second, the moderate risk attenuation (~30% reduction from initial values) implies households internally calibrate decisions against external risks, but prolonged structural adaptation may require targeted incentives (raheem-robinson-parameteradjustments). Third, the absence of cross-generational strategy retention warns of viscous learning dynamics—a potential gap addressed by future double-loop learning extensions (inaction-via-time).
