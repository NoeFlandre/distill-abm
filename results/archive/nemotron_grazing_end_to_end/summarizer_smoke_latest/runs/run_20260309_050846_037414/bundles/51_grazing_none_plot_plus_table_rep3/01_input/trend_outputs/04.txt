The dataset tracks the mean green biomass across 100 households in a grazing system simulated over 100 timesteps (years). Each household, starting with 90 livestock and initial biomass levels ranging from ~45,090 to 45,213.552, exhibits a consistent upward trajectory in mean green biomass, with final values spanning 55,000 to ~64,000. Key observations include:   \n   \n1. **System-Wide Biomass Growth**: All households show steady biomass increase over time, with the exception of a few columns showing minor fluctuations but no declines. This aligns with the model's global rainfall variability (mean=200, std=100) and satisficing thresholds (set to 0), suggesting households balance grazing pressure with biomass recovery under risk-aware decision-making (risk-mode? = True). \n   \n2. **Initial Biomass Variability**: Households start with heterogeneous initial biomass levels, ranging from ~44,000 to 45,200. This variability, likely driven by patch-specific factors (e.g., soil quality, initial herbaceous biomass), persists slightly into later timesteps but converges toward higher equilibrium values. \n   \n3. **Long-Term Resilience**: Final biomass values cluster between 55,000 and 63,000, with some outliers exceeding 64,000. This suggests biomass approaches a stable equilibrium after 100 years, despite environmental shocks (e.g., droughts, heavy rains). The absence of long-term declines across households implies satisficing behavioral strategies (e.g., reducing livestock in response to low biomass) effectively prevent ecological collapse. \n   \n4. **Growth Rate Heterogeneity**: Households with higher initial biomass (e.g., columns .21, .29 with starting values ~45,200) often achieve marginally higher final values (~63,500–64,000), indicating resilience benefits from healthier initial conditions. Conversely, households starting lower (~44,000) recover similarly, reflecting adaptive responses to historical biomass trajectories. \n   \n5. **Strategic Sustainability**: The upward trend occurs despite continuous grazing (resting? = False) and deforestation thresholds (not explicitly modeled here), highlighting the model's focus on behavioral strategies over hard limits. Satisficing households adjust stocking rates annually, prioritizing local biomass observations (satisficing-threshold = 0, satisficing-trials = 21), which allows dynamic adaptation to climatic variability. \n   \n6. **Agent-Specific Variability**: Even under homogeneity (homog-behav-types? = True; all households are E-RO agents), biomass outcomes vary slightly (10–15%) due to stochastic factors (e.g., rainfall, risk-att-init = 0.5). This variability underscores the model's emphasis on environmental and risk factors over purely strategic differences in homogeneous scenarios. \n   \nConclusion: Under homogeneous E-RO strategy households with risk-averse decision-making, the system demonstrates long-term resilience to climatic shocks, with biomass converging toward a stable equilibrium despite initial variability. The data supports the role of satisficing and risk-awareness in sustaining pasture biomass over decades.
