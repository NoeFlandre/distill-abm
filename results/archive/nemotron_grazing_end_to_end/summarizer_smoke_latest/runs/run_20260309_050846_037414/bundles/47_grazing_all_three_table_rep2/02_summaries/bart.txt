 The agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE model, focusing on simulating smallholder farmers' decision-making in pasture-based systems with learning capabilities . The original model was designed to analyze feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in common property grazing systems . This extension adds heterogeneity in behavioral strategies (e.g., learning-by-doing, social learning) and introduces risk sensitivity .  The average initial risk attitudes among social learning agents exhibit a complex temporal pattern . Early risk-tolerant behavior could lead to overgrazing, exacerbating pasture degradation under rainfall variability, while subsequent risk aversion might mitigate further resource depletion . The data underscore the interplay between risk management and social learning in sustaining grazing systems, emphasizing the need for policies that balance risk tolerance with resource constraints .  The average reserve biomass begins around 90,000 units and experiences a gradual decline over time, with fluctuations driven by stochastic rainfall variability . The absence of social learning (social/individual) and individual learning (intrinsic-preference=0.0) prevents agents from modifying herding strategies in response to environmental feedback .  Simulation begins with zero destocking in the first 7 time steps, indicating initial stability as households maintain consistent livestock holdings without immediate resource depletion . At step 8, destocking spikes to 6.0 across all households, suggesting uniform resource stress emerges due to high initial grazing pressure (livestock-init=90) and the absence of pasture rest mechanisms (resting?=False)  The cumulative number of livestock destocked begins at 0.0 for the first four time steps, reflecting initial equilibrium as agents have not yet initiated herd size adjustments . Destocking starts increasing from time step 5 (6.0) and stabilizes briefly at 12.0 by step 6.0 .
