The analysis of the destocking metrics across households reveals distinct patterns influenced by behavioral strategies, environmental volatility, and risk management. Initially, households with large herds experience elevated destocking rates (<15 steps) due to overgrazing and stochastic droughts (low precipitation events <100 threshold). By mid-simulation (~25 steps), adaptive agents (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1) begin reducing herd sizes preemptively during unfavorable rainfall cycles, aligning with their risk-sensitive decision-making. Fixed-rule E-RO agents persist with higher cumulative destocking until ~60 steps, when their conservative thresholds (>70% pasture biomass) fail to mitigate prolonged dry spells, triggering sharper destocking spikes. Learning agents demonstrate proactive adaptation: E-LBD agents gradually reduce herd sizes following personal negative outcomes (herbivory efficiency <0.3), while E-RO-SL1 agents leverage neighbor observation (within 1-unit radius) to mimic successful stabilization strategies. By final timesteps (~100), cumulative destocking averages converge between adaptive and fixed-strategy groups, suggesting homogenized herd management practices. Risk-seeking phenotypes (higher r-parameters) consistently exhibit greater herd resilience during volatility but incur higher destocking post-drought recovery, whereas risk-averse types exhibit earlier destocking but slower recovery. Spatial heterogeneity in pasture recovery rates (gr1 vs gr2) further exacerbates regional destocking disparities, with arid zones showing 25-30% higher destocking peaks. Long-term trajectories indicate that behavioral plasticity mitigates baseline overgrazing pressures but amplifies inter-agent destocking disparities, particularly under compound meteorological shocks.
