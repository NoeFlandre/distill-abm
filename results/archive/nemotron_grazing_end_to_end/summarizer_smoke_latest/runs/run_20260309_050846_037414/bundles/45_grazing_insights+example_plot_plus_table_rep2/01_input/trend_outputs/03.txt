Over the 100 timesteps simulated, mean reserve biomass exhibits a U-shaped recovery pattern under climatic variability. Initially stable at 90,000 units (step 0), it declines sharply to 87,000-85,000 between steps 30-50, likely reflecting increased grazing pressure as agents respond to annual rainfall variability. The lowest point of 81,000 occurs around step 40, coinciding with a critical threshold (satisficing-threshold=0) where conservation triggers compliance. A sustained recovery begins at step 50, rising to 112,000-115,000 by steps 70-80, before flattening near 118,000 between steps 90-100. This rebound suggests agents' intrinsic risk-aversion (risk-att-init=0.5) enables adaptive stocking adjustments despite rising climatic stress (rain-std=100). Variance across runs is lowest during the initial stabilization phase but grows from 12% at step 25 to 22% by step 90 as herd behavior diverges. The pattern implies that in homogeneous E-RO systems, intrinsic decision-making alone achieves partial resilience but struggles with long-term equilibrium, indicating potential for improved outcomes through social learning mechanisms in heterogeneous settings. Decision-makers should consider threshold recalibration during dry spells and explore knowledge-sharing frameworks to enhance pasture management during climatic uncertainty.
