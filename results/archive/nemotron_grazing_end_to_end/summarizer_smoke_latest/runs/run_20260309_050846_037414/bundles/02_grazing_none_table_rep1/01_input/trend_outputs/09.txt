The data shows a consistent, accelerating increase in average cumulative livestock destocking across all households over the simulation period. Destocking starts at 0.0 in the initial years, then progresses through gradual increments (e.g., 6.0 at early time steps) before accelerating sharply into sustained and steeper rises (e.g., 70.0–100.0+ in later decades). This pattern reflects a delayed but mounting response to resource depletion in a homogeneous E-RO behavioral type configuration under climatic variability. Within the model's context of fixed rainfall (mean=200, std=100) and continuous grazing (resting?=False), the gradual deficit in pasture resilience forces households to reduce livestock stocking to avoid overgrazing. The acceleration implies compounds environmental pressures as pasture biomass declines progressively, forcing households to respond more aggressively once thresholds are crossed. Notably, the absence of social learning or strategy switching (homog-behav-types?=True, SL2-strategy-switch?=False) prevents adaptive mitigation, leading to a linear but increasing pressure on resources and a proportional rise in destocking to maintain herd viability. The risk-averse behavior of E-RO agents (risk-att-init=0.5) likely triggers cautious destocking as environmental uncertainty increases. These trends align with expectations for baseline conditions where solitary decision-making fails to counteract climatic shocks until systemic resource stress becomes unavoidable.
