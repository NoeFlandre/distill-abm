The data shows that from the initial steps, the risk attitudes of different households start moderately high (~0.075-0.15). Over time, there is a divergence in behavior among households: some increase their risk attitude (peaking at 0.7-0.8 by step 80), while others decrease (dropping to -0.3 by step 100). This divergence suggests varied strategies in overstocking or understocking compared to recommendations. At early stages, agents with higher risk attitudes may have displayed aggressive stocking, while those with lower values adopted more cautious approaches. Later, a plateau phase occurs for some households, maintaining higher risk attitudes (0.6-0.7), while others remain at lower levels (-0.2 to 0.4), indicating stabilized strategies. The variability in outcomes highlights how interactions between agents and climatic unpredictability lead to divergent adaptation patterns, even under homogeneous conditions. For decision-makers, this signifies that incentivizing moderate risk attitudes and social learning could enhance stability in grazing practices, as observed in the plateau phases.
