 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The data shows that from the initial steps, the risk attitudes of different households start moderately high (~0.075-0.15) Over time, there is a divergence in behavior among households . Some increase their risk attitude among households, while others decrease . This divergence suggests varied strategies in overstocking or understocking compared to recommendations .  The data exhibits a pattern of sharp decline, stabilization, secondary dip, recovery in mean livestock + destock values . The data shows an initial sharp decline in mean destock across all households, consistent with overgrazing and pasture degradation, followed by stabilization at lower levels . The recovery phase accelerates between timesteps 50–80 in the fastest-recovering runs .  Some simulations exhibit stabilization in inequality around mid-timesteps, hinting at potential tipping points where forced overgrazing or resource depletion finally equalizes outcomes through system collapse . Fixed rainfall parameters with high variability (std=100) likely accelerate inequality by creating patchy resource availability .
