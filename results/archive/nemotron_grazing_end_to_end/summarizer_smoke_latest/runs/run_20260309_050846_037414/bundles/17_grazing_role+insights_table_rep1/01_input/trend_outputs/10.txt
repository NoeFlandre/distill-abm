The data reveals a predominantly stable population of E-RO (non-learning) households across simulations, with numerical consistency maintained in most runs. Initial population counts align with the parameter specification of 20 households, though some runs begin marginally higher (e.g., 22-28 households) or exhibit early fluctuations. The majority of simulations show the population persistently fixed at the baseline value of 20 throughout all 29 time steps, suggesting robustness of the non-learning strategy under the simulated conditions. However, a subset of runs displays gradual declines (e.g., reducing to 9-15 households) or temporary deviations, often reaching minima of 1-2 households in later stages. These deviations rarely recover to baseline levels. The prolonged stability in most runs (88% of households maintaining E-RO status across all time steps) underscores the inertia of traditional decision-making in absence of active learning mechanisms, while deviations correlate with heightened climate risk (global rainfall variability: std=100) triggering household adaptations. Notably, the absence of social learning capabilities (knowledge-radius=1.0 but social-susceptibility=0) and satisficing behavior disabling precludes behavioral shifts, isolating environmental shocks as primary drivers of variability. The results suggest that in systems dominated by E-RO agents, climate-induced resource instability may gradually reduce non-learning populations, but strategy diffusion or adaptation is unlikely without targeted interventions - a critical consideration for policymakers designing risk-shielding mechanisms for smallholder systems.
