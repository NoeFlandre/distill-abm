The data reveals dynamic trends in herd size deviations from recommendations across agent types, shaped by learning strategies and environmental feedback. **Early-time trends** (rows 0-15) show low, stable deviations (±0.2) for most households, with some E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 agents exhibiting minor gains (0.1-0.2) due to initial synchronized grazing. **Mid-transition** (rows 16-30) reveals escalating deviations: high-risk-tolerance agents (E-LBD) experience amplified positive deviations (up to 0.5-0.7) from overstocking during rainfall booms (e.g., rows 20-25), followed by sharp negative declines (-0.2 to -0.4) as pasture degradation accumulates during drought-like shocks (rows 25-30). In contrast, non-learning agents (E-RO) maintain smaller deviations (±0.1-0.3), reflecting slower adaptation. **Social learning dynamics** emerge in later stages (rows 25-30): E-RO-SL1 agents show clustered deviations tied to local neighborhood conditions, suggesting localized strategy adoption despite global social-learning suppression. The asymmetric response to risk (risk-parameter=0.5) and delayed pasture recovery (gr2=0.1) exacerbate herding pressures, with deviations peaking during simulated climate stress. These patterns highlight the interplay between behavioral heterogeneity, environmental variability, and systemic resource constraints in shaping grazing outcomes.
