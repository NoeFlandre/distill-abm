The Gini coefficients describe the inequality in healthy livestock distribution among 60 households across 30 simulations. Initial simulations (columns 1–2) show near-zero inequality (-6.66e-16 to 1.37e-4), suggesting almost perfect equilibrium in resource access at the outset. By simulations 3–4, inequality remains minimal (up to 2.02e-4), reflecting stable outcomes under the baseline ‘E-RO’ behavioral strategy.   Some simulations (columns 5–8) show slight declines (<5e-5), while later time steps (columns 13–16) exhibit localized increases (up to 8.70e-4), potentially linked to learned collaborative behaviors or risk-driven herd adjustments. Over time, heterogeneity in behavioral types (‘E-RO’, ‘E-LBD’, ‘E-RO-SL1’) appears to dampen inequality, with social learning agents (‘E-RO-SL1’) possibly enhancing equitable resource sharing through localized knowledge exchange. Risk-aware agents (risk-att-init=0.5) may reinforce stability by moderating herd sizes during stochastic shocks (e.g., rainfall variability=100), preventing extreme disparities. Overall, the system maintains low inequality (<0.001 Gini), though minor deviations suggest adaptive trade-offs between exploration and exploitation in grazing decisions.
