The data reveals a gradual decline in mean livestock numbers (including destocked animals) across households over time, starting from an initial uniform value of 90.0 livestock per household. The first significant deviation occurs at timestep 11, where variability begins to emerge, with some households showing proportional reductions (e.g., ~70% of initial herd size by timestep 20). By timestep 60, the average livestock biomass per household stabilizes around 68% of initial levels, though inter-household variability increases markedly. This trajectory aligns with the model's activation of stochastic rainfall (rain-mean = 200 ± std 100), simulating climate-driven resource stress.   Human behaviors interacting with stochastic environmental conditions determine adaptive responses: the 20 households engaged in learning-by-doing (E-LBD) and social learning (E-RO-SL1) exhibit slower aggregate declines than the fixed-rule E-RO majority following initial climate shocks. However, by mid-simulation (timesteps 30-50), learning agents demonstrate improved proportional maintenance of livestock biomass compared to baseline types, suggesting risksensitive strategy adaptation to climate variability. The absence of pasture rest mechanisms (resting? = False) and high grazing pressure parameters (livestock-init = 90, w = 0.8) likely underlie the systemic degradation trend, as uncoordinated herding depletes pasture biomass during low-rainfall periods, triggering destocking under stress.   Management implications stress the long-term viability of static stocking levels under climate variability and highlight adaptive learning strategies (E-RO-SL1/E-LBD) as potential buffers for localized resilience. However, indirect herd declines suggest persistent ecological and social pressures exceeding innate behavioral flexibility, emphasizing the need for policy interventions to recalibrate land-use thresholds or enforce grazing coordination to mitigate systemic degradation.   The temporal pattern of gradual decline punctuated by accelerated reductions during climate shocks underscores the nonlinear interplay between stochastic events and adaptive capacity, with learning agents offering incremental resilience but still facing existential thresholds under compounded stressors.
