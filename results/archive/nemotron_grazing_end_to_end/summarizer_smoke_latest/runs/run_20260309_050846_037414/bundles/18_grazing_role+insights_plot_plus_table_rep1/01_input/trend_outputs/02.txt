The trends in the mean household risk attitude metric over 100 timesteps reveal dynamic adjustments to climate and pasture conditions. Early timesteps (0-20) show risk attitudes oscillating between 0 and 0.2, with peaks at 0.18 and 0.2167, indicating initial herd overexpansion under high rainfall uncertainty (risk_att-init=0.5, risk-mode?=True). By timesteps 8 and 20, risk attitudes rise to 0.16-0.19, reflecting adaptive overstocking during favorable biomass conditions (rainfall variability regime). Post-timestep 40, a marked decline to negative values (-0.09 to -0.08) suggests herd reductions due to chronic pasture degradation from cumulative grazing pressure and stochastic rain stress. By the final timesteps (80-100), risk attitudes stabilize near -0.06 to 0.1, implying a system equilibrium where deviations comply with the recommended livestock range. Risk-averse agents (risk-att-init=0.5) dominate, with E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 strategies showing slower adaptation due to disabled social learning (social-susceptibility=0). The data underscores the importance of dynamic risk sensitivity in managing grazing systems under climate shocks, with self-correcting deviations toward sustainable stocking levels after initial overexploitation.
