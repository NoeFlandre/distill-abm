The mean reserve biomass data reveals a complex dynamic pattern influenced by initial conditions, behavioral strategies, and stochastic climate variability. The presence of global rainfall variability (rain-mean = 200, rain-std = 100) introduces significant biomass fluctuations, with droughts periodically reducing pasture productivity and wet seasons enabling recovery at a slower rate (gr2 = 0.1 for droughts vs. gr1 = 0.5 for wet seasons). The high initial herd size (livestock-init = 90) likely caused immediate grazing pressure, reducing biomass levels early in the simulation.  
   
Agents using the baseline E-RO strategy (fixed rules without adaptive learning) show limited capacity to adjust grazing intensity despite climatic shocks. This results in persistent biomass depletion during droughts, with partial recovery only in favorable rainfall years. The absence of resting mechanisms (resting? = False) and satisficing behavior (satisficing-threshold = 0.0) further exacerbates unsustainable grazing pressure.  
   
Interestingly, the data indicates some simulations stabilize or even increase biomass beyond initial levels (e.g., reaching ~128,000), suggesting occasional equilibrium emergence when wet seasons and moderate rainfall variability (risk-att-init = 0.5) balance pasture degradation. However, most runs demonstrate cyclical declines and recoveries, highlighting system sensitivity to climate shocks and static decision-making. The homogenization of behavioral strategies (homog-behav-types? = True) prevents strategy diffusion benefits, limiting adaptive responses to environmental stressors.
