The data reveals a progressive decline in average livestock numbers across all households over the 100-year simulation period, with accelerating degradation over time. Initially, all households start with 90.0 livestock, reflecting high initial herd sizes that strain pasture resources. From timesteps 0–10 (years), livestock numbers remain stable within ±1% of the starting value, suggesting robustness to early stochastic rainfall events (mean=200, std=100). However, by timesteps 11–30, a gradual but consistent downward trend emerges, with average herd sizes dropping to 83.0–87.0 range, indicating increasing grazing-induced degradation of pastures. This decline correlates with the absence of native pathways for afforestation (A0) and the active extension-model (extension-model?=True), which introduces behavioral heterogeneity despite the simulation focusing on E-RO agents (number-E-RO=20).   

Between timesteps 30–80, the decline accelerates, with herd sizes falling below 73.0 and remaining suppressed for ~10–15 years. This period aligns with the model's climate regime (dry-season regrowth rate gr2=0.1), which limits pasture recovery capacity under prolonged stress. The absence of automatic pasture rest mechanisms (resting?=False) prevents natural recovery, exacerbating biomass depletion. By timesteps 90–100, average livelihoods fall below 40.0, approaching critical thresholds for food security collapse. Despite homogeneous behavioral types (homog-behav-types?=True), social connectivity through knowledge-radius=1.0 fails to mitigate declines, as E-RO agents cannot share adaptive strategies to adjust herd sizes. The final collapse indicates a system-wide tipping point where degradation outpaces recovery, driven by compounded effects of initial overstocking, climate variability, and lack of behavior change. Decision-makers should note how fixed-risk strategies (risk-parameter=0.5) without adaptive learning or governance mechanisms exacerbate systemic risks in marginal grazing systems.
