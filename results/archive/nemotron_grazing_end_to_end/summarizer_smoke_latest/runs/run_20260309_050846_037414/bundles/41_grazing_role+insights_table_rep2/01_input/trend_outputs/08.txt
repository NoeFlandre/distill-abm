The data reveals a strong temporal pattern in social learning adoption among households. Initially, no households engaged in social learning during the first four time steps. At time step 5, adoption begins in limited numbers across simulations (ranging from 0 to 4 households). Between time steps 6 and 21, the number of households engaging in social learning exhibits dynamic growth, with fluctuations reflecting simulation variability. Notably, by time steps 25–29, adoption reaches high levels, with counts ranging from 33 to 51 households across simulations. This upward trend persists through the final recorded time steps, with counts clustering between 40 and 50 households.  The pattern suggests that the proportion of households adopting social learning increases across time, though not uniformly, as some simulations show sharper increases while others plateau at lower levels. This trend aligns with the model's design where heterogeneous behavioral strategies (particularly E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 agents) interact with stochastic climate shocks (global rainfall variability) and adaptive decision-making. The delayed onset and accelerating adoption curve indicate that social learning becomes increasingly prevalent as environmental uncertainties (e.g., variable rainfall impacting pasture biomass) create learning opportunities through neighbor observations. Implications suggest that deterministic social learning mechanisms in grazing systems may serve as adaptive tools for managing resource variability, with potential for targeted interventions to enhance knowledge-sharing networks. The data supports the hypothesis that social learning adoption scales with temporal and environmental complexity in this context.
