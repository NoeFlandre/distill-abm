The data reveals variable adoption and retention of the E-RO (Baseline) strategy across simulations, influenced by interactions between decision-making dynamics and climatic conditions. In most simulations, E-RO households initially dominate early time steps (1–10), with counts ranging from 0 to 40 households depending on the run. For example, in one scenario, E-RO households transiently peak at 32 in the first five time steps before declining steeply to 20 by time step 30, while in another, they remain stable at 20 by time step 18 and stabilize thereafter. Some runs exhibit abrupt collapses, with E-RO counts dropping to 0 by time step 15 (e.g., simulations with initial counts of 9 households), suggesting rapid abandonment of the baseline strategy under adverse conditions.   \n

Satisficers, with a threshold of 0.1 and limited trials (21), may contribute to this trend by triggering resource-conserving behaviors in later time steps, reducing E-RO prevalence. Notably, no learning mechanisms (E-LBD, E-RO-SL1) are active in these simulations, ruling out social or experiential learning as drivers of strategy shifts. The absence of strategy switching (SL2-strategy-switch?=False) implies changes in E-RO counts stem from intrinsic model dynamics or environmental pressures rather than agent adaptation. Over time, simulations with higher initial E-RO adoption (32–39 households) demonstrate slower declines, suggesting baseline strategies may delay but fail to sustain equilibrium under prolonged climatic stress. \n
The variability in E-RO retention underscores the fragility of risk-averse decision-making in isolated, homogeneous societies facing stochastic environmental shocks. While E-RO households temporarily stabilize resource use early on, long-term sustainability becomes untenable without adaptive strategies or social learning, highlighting the critical role of behavioral flexibility in social-ecological resilience.
