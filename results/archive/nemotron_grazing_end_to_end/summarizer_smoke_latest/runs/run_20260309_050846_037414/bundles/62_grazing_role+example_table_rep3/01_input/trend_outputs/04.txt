The mean green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units across all patches and declines gradually over the first 20–30 time steps (simulation years), reaching a low point around 44,800–44,900 units by step 30. This initial drop reflects unsustainable grazing pressure from high initial herd sizes (90 livestock per household) combined with stochastic rainfall variability (mean=200, std=100), which exacerbates pasture stress during dry periods. Between steps 25 and 45, the biomass fluctuates moderately within a narrow range (~44,800–45,200 units), indicating adaptive herd management responses. From step 45 onward, biomass stabilizes at ~45,000 units, suggesting equilibrium between grazing pressure and pasture recovery rates (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1). The absence of significant fluctuations post-step 40 implies that learning strategies (e.g., E-LBD or E-RO-SL1 agents adjusting grazing intensity, though not explicitly quantified here) and risk-aware decision-making (risk-att-init=0.5) mitigate long-term degradation. The model’s focus on behavioral adaptation through social and individual learning aligns with the observed stabilization, as agents likely optimize herd sizes to balance short-term resource use and long-term pasture resilience.
