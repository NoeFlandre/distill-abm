 The agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE model, focusing on simulating smallholder farmers' decision-making in pasture-based systems with learning capabilities . The original model was designed to analyze feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in common property grazing systems . This extension adds heterogeneity in behavioral strategies (e.g., learning-by-doing, social learning) and introduces risk sensitivity .  The average degree of livestock deviation from recommended levels evolves dynamically across time steps, reflecting complex interactions between environmental variability and adaptive agent behavior . The data reveals a dynamic pattern in the initial risk attitudes of social learning agents across multiple simulations . The values under household.1 to households show systematic variation, with initial values in simulations 1-3 clustering around moderate positive levels .  The mean total number of livestock across households starts at 90.0 at time step 0 and exhibits a gradual but persistent decline over time, ending at 46.9 livestock units by time step 61 . The mean green biomass remains stable at approximately 45,000 units during the initial phase, reflecting equilibrium grazing pressure from households with high livestock numbers (90 per herd). From time step 4 onward, biomass increases gradually, reaching a peak around 45,276 units (~time step 25) Post-peak  The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock exhibits a dynamic pattern of increasing inequality over the 100-timestep simulation . The number of households engaged in social learning increases gradually over the simulation period, starting from zero and reaching a peak of 52 households by the final timestep . The trend aligns with the model's focus on how learning strategies diffuse in common property systems .  The number of households following the E-RO strategy exhibits stable but fluctuating patterns throughout the simulation . The final timesteps show deceleration in annual destocking rates (e.g., +10.0 between t=169–170 vs. +20.0 earlier in t=159–160), possibly indicating system saturation or adaptive management stabilizing herd sizes .
