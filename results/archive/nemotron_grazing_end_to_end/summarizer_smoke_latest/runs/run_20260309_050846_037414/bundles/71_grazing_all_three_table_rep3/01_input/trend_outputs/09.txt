The average cumulative livestock destocked across all households exhibits a gradual increase in magnitude over time, with variability increasing towards later timesteps. Initially (t=0–5), no destocking occurs (0.0). By t=6, destocking begins at 6.0, remaining stable for the first 6 years before rising to 12.2–13.2 in years 7–12. A sharp divergence occurs between t=13–18, where values fluctuate between 11.4–18.8, suggesting localized responses to environmental or social pressures. From t=19 onward, destocking intensifies nonlinearly: values reach 22.8 by t=21, peak at 169.7 by t=169, and culminate at 171.8 by t=170, with sharp spikes in some timesteps (e.g., 131.7 at t=157, 158.3 at t=162, 169.7 at t=169).  
\nThe increasing variability in destocking patterns over time (e.g., 4.0–5.0 differences in early years vs. 20.0–16.0 differences in t=140–150) indicates growing sensitivity to stochastic shocks and behavioral adaptations. Early destocking aligns with the model’s high initial herd size (90 livestock/household) straining pastures, amplified by stochastic rainfall variability (std=100). Learning-by-doing (E-LBD) and social learning strategies (E-RO-SL1) likely influence the uneven adoption of destocking behaviors, as seen in the gradual divergence among households. For instance, the plateau periods (e.g., t=12–13 at ~12.0–13.3) and delayed increases (e.g., 20.0 at t=23) may reflect risk-aversion or delayed social learning diffusion.  
\nThe final timesteps show deceleration in annual destocking rates (e.g., +10.0 between t=169–170 vs. +20.0 earlier in t=159–160), possibly indicating system saturation or adaptive management stabilizing herd sizes. Risk-aware decision-making (risk-att-init=0.5) and the absence of satisficing behavior (threshold=0.0) suggest agents prioritize strategic reductions in herd pressure to sustain long-term pasture resilience under recurrent climate shocks.
