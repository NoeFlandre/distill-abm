The mean total number of livestock across households starts at 90.0 at time step 0 and exhibits a gradual but persistent decline over time, ending at ~46.9 livestock units by time step 61. The early phase (time steps 0–10) shows minor fluctuations, with averages oscillating between 83.4 and 85.1 before a sharper decline begins. By time steps 15–25, the average drops to ~73.9, then remains volatile around 70–75 livestock units until time step 40. From time step 40 onward, the average accelerates its decline, reaching ~57.8 by time step 50 and ~46.9 by the final timestep. This trajectory reflects a net reduction of ~48% over the simulation period.   Nerf types like droughts during time steps 30–35 and 55–60 coincide with sharper drops, suggesting aggregators face greater connective stress during low-biomass periods, which force herd reductions. The absence of satisficing behavior and the disabled resting mechanisms amplify vulnerability to these shocks. Despite risk awareness being activated (risk-att-init=0.5), the homogeneity in behavioral types (all E-RO) limits adaptive strategies, as these agents follow fixed rules without learning. The simulation underscores the fragility of pasture systems under recurrent climate variability when herds outpace regenerative capacity and learning is constrained. This would anchor calls for integrating adaptive management practices and diversified behavioral strategies to buffer against environmental uncertainty.
