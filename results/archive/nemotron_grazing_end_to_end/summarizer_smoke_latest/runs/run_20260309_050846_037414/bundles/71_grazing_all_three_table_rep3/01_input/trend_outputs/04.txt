The mean green biomass remains stable at approximately 45,000 units during the initial phase, reflecting equilibrium grazing pressure from households with high livestock numbers (90 per herd). From time step 4 onward, biomass increases gradually, reaching a peak around 45,276 units (~time step 25), coinciding with reduced grazing pressure as herds deplete local forage. Post-peak, biomass declines steadily to ~41,500 units by the simulation’s end, with pronounced volatility post-time step 30. This trajectory aligns with grazing pressure dynamics: sustained high-intensity grazing (w=0.8) and fixed herd sizes (no adaptive behavior via E-RO’s rule-based strategy) strain pasture resilience. The decline post-peak suggests biomass regeneration rates (gr1=0.5 during wet seasons, gr2=0.1 during dry) fail to counteract prolonged exploitation. Risk sensitivity (risk-att-init=0.5) may manifest in moderate herd adjustments, but the absence of social learning and double-loop learning limits systemic adaptations. These trends highlight the compounding effects of static grazing strategies on ecosystem productivity, underscoring the critical role of adaptive management in maintaining pasture viability under variable rainfall (rain-std=100). Decision-makers might prioritize behavioral interventions to prevent resource collapse in similar systems.
