The simulation tracks mean reserve biomass across time steps, revealing growth patterns conditioned by stochastic rainfall and agent behavior. Initial values peak sharply within the first 30 steps, likely driven by favorable climate conditions (**rain-mean**=200, **rain-std**=100) and default grazing pressure (**livestock-init**=90, **w**=0.8). This corresponds to a critical threshold where pasture resilience exceeds grazing impacts. Biomass plateaus around step 50-60 (approximately 115,000) before declining steeply by step 90, suggesting systemic overextraction under persistent dry conditions.   Original RAGE dynamics emerge post-step 70, with biomass dropping below 100,000—a reflection of **Rmax**=0.5 limiting resource recovery during droughts (controlled by **gr2**=0.1). Stochastic rainfall explains inter-run variability, as agents encounter unpredictable precipitation switches.   Risk-aware decision-making (**risk-att-init**=0.5) amplifies conservative herd size adjustments after step 40, delaying but not preventing stocking rate surges that overwhelm pastures.   Learning agents (E-LBD/E-RO-SL1) exhibit delayed but sharper corrections than risk-neutral E-RO types, though homogeneous initial conditions limit behavioral diversity.   Final biomass stabilization at ~90,000—despite active management strategies—implies strong stochastic forcing (**rain-std**=100) outweighs agent responsiveness.   Parameter sensitivities dominate here, particularly **gr1**=0.5 (wet-season recovery) and **mr**=0.05 (livestock mortality), acting as nonlinear circuit breakers between growth and collapse states.
