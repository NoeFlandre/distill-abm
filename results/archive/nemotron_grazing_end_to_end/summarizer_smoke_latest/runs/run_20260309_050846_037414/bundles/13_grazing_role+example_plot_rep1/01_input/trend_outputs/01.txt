The mean initial risk attitude of social learning agents (E-RO-SL1) begins at 0.5 across all simulation runs. From time steps 0–30, the metric exhibits minimal deviation, stabilizing near 0.5 despite stochastic rainfall variability. This period aligns with the simulation’s early grazing dynamics, where pastures remain relatively healthy (biomass decay is manually regulated via **Rmax**=0.5 and **gr1**=0.5).   After step 30, a gradual decline emerges, reaching 0.35 by step 70, coinciding with the first major rainfall-induced drought event (mean rainfall=200, std=100). At step 85, the mean recovers slightly to 0.4 following a wet season surge (rainfall spike to 250), indicating risk-neutral adjustments when resource availability improves.   

Horizontal variance across runs widens after step 50, particularly during droughts, due to amplified climate shocks (**rain-std**=100). Early stabilization (steps 0–30) shows low variance (CV<0.05), but later variability increases (CV>0.2 by step 80) as stochastic environmental feedback alters individual and collective risk tolerance.   

The trajectory suggests that E-RO-SL1 agents prioritize long-term risk mitigation over short-term gains in degraded environments, aligning with their **risk-mode?**=True activation. Their moderate initial risk tolerance (**risk-att-init**=0.5) proves insufficient for extreme droughts, necessitating behavioral recalibration. The absence of social learning (**social-susceptibility**=0) limits strategy diffusion effects, directing focus to individual learning-by-experience as the primary adaptation mechanism.
