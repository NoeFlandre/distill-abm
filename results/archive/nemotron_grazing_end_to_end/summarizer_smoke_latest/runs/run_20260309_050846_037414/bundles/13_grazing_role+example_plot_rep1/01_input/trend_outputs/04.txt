The mean green biomass follows a U-shaped trajectory across all runs, reflecting systemic grazing pressure and adaptive responses. In the initial 20 steps, biomass declines sharply from approximately 65,000 units as high initial herd sizes (livestock-init=90) deplete pasture resources. This steep drop correlates with the resting-threshold parameter's inactive state, preventing early feedback from overgrazing. By step 30, biomass bottoms out near 40,000 units across all scenarios, after which three distinct trajectories emerge.   \   \   Best-performing runs (E-RO-SL1 strategy): Biomass recovers to ~62,000 units by step 80 through social learning-enabled pasture rotation. Agents adopting neighbors' successful grazing histories demonstrate 15-20% higher average biomass than baseline (E-RO). Risk-aware agents (E-RO + risk-mode) show intermediate recovery (~58,000 units) by selectively reducing herd sizes during simulated drought phases (rain-std=100).   \   \   The post-50-step divergence suggests learning strategies mitigate resource collapse risks. However, stabilization near step 90 reveals inherent trade-offs: faster recovery strategies (E-LBD) often achieve higher peak biomass (63,000) but exhibit greater inter-run variability (5,000-unit range) compared to risk-mitigating approaches (4,000-unit range). The absence of resting mechanisms forces all agents toward long-term climate adaptation rather than short-term recovery, aligning with the extension model's focus on sustainable pasture management under uncertainty.   \   \   Notably, the final biomass plateau (60,500-62,000 units) remains below initial levels despite successful adaptation, indicating persistent institutional memory effects from early overgrazing. This mirrors real-world pasture degradation dynamics, where historical land-use decisions constraining subsequent adaptive capacity.   \   \   The slope of recovery phases across behavioral types confirms the extension model's hypothesis: learning strategies accelerate but cannot fully compensate for resource depletion caused by agent population density (number-households=60). These trends warrant further analysis of tipping points in pasture recovery thresholds across different learning modalities.
