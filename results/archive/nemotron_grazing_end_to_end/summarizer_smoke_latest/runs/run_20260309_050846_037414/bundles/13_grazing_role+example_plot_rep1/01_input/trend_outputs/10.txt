The total number of households maintaining the E-RO (non-learning) strategy demonstrates two distinct phases of decline across the 100-year simulation. Initially, 58 of the 60 households persist with this strategy, showing near-complete stability through year 10. However, a critical threshold occurs between years 35 and 40 when the count abruptly drops to 28 households. This inflection point coincides with increased herd sizes straining carrying capacities during periods of below-average rainfall (e.g., years 30-36 where rainfall deviated -150 or greater from the mean). By year 70, the survivor count reaches 12 households, which then oscillates between 10-14 through the simulation's final decades due to recurring pasture degradation and stochastic climate shocks. The declining trajectory reflects the fixed-strategy limitation of E-RO agents, which cannot adapt to deteriorating pasture conditions through behavioral modification. This pattern suggests that without learning capabilities, E-RO strategies become overspecialized to initial environmental conditions, making them particularly vulnerable to compounding stressors. The final population distribution indicates that risk-modulated responses only partially mitigate systemic pressures, with remaining households likely operating at reduced scale rather than truly adapting. However, the absence of reinforcement in household numbers throughout the final 30 years suggests that any resilience observed stems from the model's fixed parameters rather than strategic adaptation.
