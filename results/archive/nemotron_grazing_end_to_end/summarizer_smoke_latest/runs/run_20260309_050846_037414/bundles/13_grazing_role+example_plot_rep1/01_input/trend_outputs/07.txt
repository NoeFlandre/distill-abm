The Gini coefficient metric evolves through three distinct phases across the 100-step simulation. During the initial 50 steps, inequality grows marginally but unevenly, with Gini values remaining below 0.03 across runs. This period reflects the compounding effects of fixed-rule grazing (E-RO) and nascent learning behaviors in E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 households, as stochastic rainfall variability (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100) introduces spatial heterogeneity in pasture productivity. By step 50, convergence suggests initial equilibration of grazing pressures and herd adjustments, with most households maintaining Gini values between 0.00–0.02.   

Between steps 50–70, a sharp nonlinear rise occurs, with Gini coefficients surging to 0.07–0.08. This phase aligns with heightened climate shocks from rainfall variability and the introduction of active learning strategies. E-LBD households, adjusting herd sizes based on personal outcomes, and E-RO-SL1 households, imitating neighbors, create divergent outcomes: some households build resilience through adaptive management, while others experience pasture degradation from mismatched strategies. Risk-sensitive decision-making (risk-att-init=0.5) exacerbates disparities, as herds in drought-prone patches face disproportionate losses.   

Post-70 steps, Gini values plateau near 0.08–0.09, reflecting a stabilized but persistently unequal distribution. This equilibrium suggests that learning strategies and risk aversion fail to fully mitigate the compounding impacts of fixed grazing areas (start-on-homepatch?=True) and recurring climatic extremes. The observed patterns indicate that heterogeneity in behavioral types and environmental stochasticity are critical drivers of spatial inequality in pasture-based systems.
