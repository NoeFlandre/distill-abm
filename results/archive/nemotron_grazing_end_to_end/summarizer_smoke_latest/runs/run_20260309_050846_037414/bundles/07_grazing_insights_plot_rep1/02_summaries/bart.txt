 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The average initial risk attitude of all social learning agents is represented by parallel lines . The stability of the mean values indicates a static behavioral profile in homogeneous populations . For decision makers, this implies that behavioral homogeneity could lead to consistent herding outcomes, potentially reducing system resilience during climate variability .  The steep decline in ending biomasss shows the need for proactive policies consisting of habitat conservation, sustainable land-use planning, and efficient herding practices to ensure water reserves . Social learning and climate-resilient herding tactics allowed groups to mitigate biodiversity loss results .  Initial herding behavior weakens when grazing land drops to 41 after about 8 years of grazing . Waiting for the land to regenerate to higher levels early in the season would allow for more grazers as well as a more permitting environment for adequate regrowth and prevent exhaustion of the pasture and risk to animal survival .
