The mean destock of households across all simulations begins at values between approximately 5.9 and 7.4 at Step 0, driven by initial high livestock stocking (90 animals/household) and climatic variability (rainfall mean = 200, std = 100). This creates rapid resource depletion in early Steps, triggering immediate destocking responses. A sharp initial decline occurs between Steps 0-10, with most simulations reducing mean destock to ~3–4 individuals by Steps 3–7 and ~1.5–2.5 by Steps 10–15. Values then gradually decrease further, plateauing between ~0.2–0.5 at Steps 60–100. However, some simulations retain higher near-homepatch variability, with persistent small fluctuations (e.g., ~0.7–0.9 at Step 80–90), reflecting localized resource availability and risk-based decision-making (risk-att-init = 0.5). The overall downward trend correlates with pasture biomass recovery, as liter of grass produced decreases under restrictive resting protocols. No meaningful behavioral type differentiation is observed due to homogenized strategies (homog-behav-types = True) disallowing cross-learning between E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 agents. Risk-aversion dynamics and persistent low social learning thresholds (social-susceptibility = 0) contribute to conservative household behavior in later Steps.
