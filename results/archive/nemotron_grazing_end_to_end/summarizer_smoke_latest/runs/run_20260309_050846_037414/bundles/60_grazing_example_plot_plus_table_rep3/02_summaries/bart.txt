 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The model focuses on E-RO under homogeneous conditions with climatic variability . Risk attitudes cluster around mild positive values (0.01–0.075) for 15 households, with a minority displaying pronounced risk aversion (~-0.11 to -0.12) The model's trajectory reflects agents dynamically adjusting livestock stocking decisions in response to environmental uncertainty, with risk attitudes converging toward conservatism .  The model's focus on climatic impact over strategy is evident in the consistent biomass recovery pattern across runs . The average livestock + destock values across households follow a distinct pattern of collapse and recovery:  collapse and recovery . The absence of strategy switching (SL2-strategy-switch?=False) suggests adaptation is limited to within-behavioral-type adjustments rather than systemic shifts .  Gini coefficients for total livestock health consistently increase across all household runs over the 100-step simulation period . Starting values cluster near zero (0.0000–0.0003) in early steps, with visible divergence emerging around step 20 . Risk-aversion dynamics and persistent low social learning thresholds (social-susceptibility = 0) contribute to conservative household behavior in later Steps .  Steps 50–60 mark a critical turning point, with coefficients surging to 0.0060–0.0080 across most runs . This acceleration coincides with the model's fixed rainfall parameter (mean = 200, std = 100) amplifying stochastic shocks . Some households adopt conservative stocking, while others overexploit pastures deepening inequity .  Risk-att-init = 0.5 may amplify volatility, with risk-averse agents adapting more slowly to scarcity . E-RO strategies become unsustainable under persistent environmental stress . Remaining households may transition to alternative behaviors (despite homog-behav-types? constraints) or face intrinsic collapse .
