 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The average degree of deviation from recommended livestock stocking levels begins near zero at time step 0, indicating baseline adherence to optimal stocking . Over time, deviations exhibit spatially heterogeneous patterns, with persistent negative values (understocking) emerging in northern zones around time step 25 . The declining trend suggests potential long-term unsustainability in the pasture reserves under these baseline conditions .  The E-RO strategy, which lacks social learning (social-susceptibility=0) and double-loop learning (SL2-strategy-switch?=False) fails to adapt to recurring pasture biomass fluctuations, leading to suboptimal stocking adjustments during periods of low rainfall . The long-term trajectory reveals a net **30% reduction in average livestock** from t=0 to t=90 .  Early stabilization (timesteps 1–5) gives way to dynamic shifts, with the most significant changes occurring in the later simulation phases . By time steps 70–99, destocking reaches its peak, with average values exceeding 60 units per household, culminating in some households reaching 100+ cumulative destocked livestock by simulation end .
