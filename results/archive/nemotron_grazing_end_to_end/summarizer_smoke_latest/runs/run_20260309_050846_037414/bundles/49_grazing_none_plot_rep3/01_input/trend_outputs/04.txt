The analysis of the mean green biomass trends across simulations reveals consistent patterns in pasture recovery dynamics over 100 model years. All behavioral configurations show a decline in biomass during the initial 20-30 steps, from baseline levels (~45,000) to troughs near (~40,000), suggesting initial herd overexploitation or satisficing behavior under stressful conditions. Notably, stochastic rainfall (std = 100) amplifies short-term variability in this decline phase.

After step 20-30, biomass recovery accelerates sharply, reaching ~63,000 by step 60 across all simulations. This rapid rebound phase aligns with agents progressively reducing livestock stock in response to declining biomass (satisficing-threshold = 0 triggers grazing reductions), allowing vegetation recovery. The uniform peak value (~63,000) observed in all E-RO configurations indicates homogenized herd adjustments outperforming variable-type responses.

Post-peak stabilization at ~62,000 in steps 80-100 demonstrates equilibrium between grazing pressure and climatic resilience (average rainfall = 200). The near-asymptotic convergence of all lines to this plateau highlights E-RO's capacity for synchronized management under homogeneous conditions. Risk-aversion modulation (risk-att-init = 0.5) likely tempers recovery phase intensity, balancing exploitation and conservation pressures. These outcomes validate the extension model's ability to detect critical transition dynamics in property systems under behavioral homogeneity.
