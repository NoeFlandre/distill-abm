The cumulative total of livestock destocked across households begins at zero for all agents at timestep 0.0 and progressively increases over time. By timestep 6.0, each household reports an initial destocking of 6.0, indicating the onset of systematic herd reduction. This baseline rate persists through timestep 12.0, suggesting a consistent pressure on pasture resources during the early phases of the simulation. At timestep 17.9, deviations emerge: some households experience intermediate destocking levels (~18–20.6), while others lag behind (~12–19.7), reflecting early heterogeneity in adaptive behaviors. By timestep 22.83, the divergence widens, with destinations ranging from 21.25 to 26.95, correlating with the activation of learning and risk sensitivity mechanisms. 

From timestep 27.05 onward, the cumulative destocking accelerates non-linearly, driven by compounding stressors: global rainfall variability (rain-std=100), biomass regeneration disparities (gr1=0.5 for wet, gr2=0.1 for dry), and heterogeneous agent strategies. For example, households 9–19 show sharper increases (rising from ~27 to ~44 by timestep 42.3), likely influenced by social learning among E-RO-SL1 agents and individual risk adjustments among E-LBD agents. The widest disparities appear after timestep 80.0, where cumulative destocking spans 80–111.9 across households, aligning with the propagation of risk-aware strategies. Risk-averse agents (risk-att-init=0.5) exhibit delayed destocking, as their decisions hinge on uncertainty thresholds tied to pasture degradation rates. 

Long-term trends reveal stochastic convergence around high-level destocking, with an upper bound of ~120–172 across households by timestep 103.58. This suggests systemic collapse of carrying capacity, amplified by stochastic climate shocks and divergent learning dynamics. While E-RO agents (non-adaptive) follow a rigid pathway, E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 agents modulate their rates based on feedback loops (own outcomes or neighbor observations), creating bifurcated trajectories. The final values exceed ecological thresholds, underscoring the risk of irreversible pasture degradation in common property systems under variable climate pressures.
