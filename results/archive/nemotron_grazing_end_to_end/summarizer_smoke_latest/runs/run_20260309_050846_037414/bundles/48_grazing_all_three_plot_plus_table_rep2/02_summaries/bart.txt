 The agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE model, focusing on simulating smallholder farmers' decision-making in pasture-based systems with learning capabilities . The original model was designed to analyze feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in common property grazing systems . This extension adds heterogeneity in behavioral strategies (e.g., learning-by-doing, social learning) and introduces risk sensitivity .  The mean initial risk attitude of social learning agents (E-RO-SL1) exhibits relative stability across simulated households . This stability suggests strong reinforcement of baseline risk behaviors despite the presence of dynamic environmental stressors (e.g., global rainfall variability with rain-mean=200, rain-std=100)  CSV data shows the average deviation from the recommended livestock numbers for households . Initial deviations range from minor adjustments (e.075, 0.045) to severe understocking . By mid-simulation, values stabilize closer to neutral deviations (-0.0875–0.0925), suggesting iterative learning and risk-adjusted decisions dampen volatility .  The average mean destock of households begins at 6.0 during the first time step, after which it declines rapidly over the first 20 steps . As the simulation progresses, a plateau emerges between steps 50 and 100, with values stabilizing within a range of 0.4 to 1.0 across replicates . This equilibrium suggests ongoing resource limitations but stabilized herd sizes under adaptive management .  Long-term trends reveal stochastic convergence around high-level destocking, with an upper bound of ~120–172 across households by timestep 103.58 . This suggests systemic collapse of carrying capacity, amplified by climate shocks and divergent learning dynamics . The final values exceed ecological thresholds, underscoring the risk of irreversible pasture degradation in common property systems .
