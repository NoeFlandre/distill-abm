 The agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE model, focusing on simulating smallholder farmers' decision-making in pasture-based systems with learning capabilities . The original model was designed to analyze feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in common property grazing systems . This extension adds heterogeneity in behavioral strategies (e.g., learning-by-doing, social learning) and introduces risk sensitivity .  The mean household risk attitude (risk-att) deviations show dynamic shifts across timesteps, reflecting adaptive responses to stochastic climate shocks and learning strategies . The data imply heterogeneous learning strategies and collectively mitigate risks collectively . The absence of resting? (automatic pasture recovery) amplifies the need for strategic herd management .  Green biomass begins at a stable equilibrium of approximately 45,000 units, reflecting initial system allocation of resources under baseline grazing pressure . At mid-simulation (~Time Step 24), a gradual decline emerges, reaching a nadir of 44,400–44,700 units by Time Step 48 . Initial phase of decline correlates with the unchecked impact of high initial herd sizes (90 livestock per household) and stochastic rainfall shocks .  The mean destocking across all households begins at approximately 6.0 livestock units per timestep (year 0-20) and remains relatively stable during this period . This reduction suggests adaptive herd management, as households reduce grazing pressure following pasture recovery following initial destocking . The absence of satisficing behavior and purely rule-based decision-making (behavioral-type=E-RO) likely constrained proactive adaptation .  The number of earthquakes recorded in the region starts at 5,000 then declines rapidly over the first 400 time steps . The total seismic activity follows the same pattern, starting at 10,000 events and declining steadily . Destocking trends highlight the unsustainable equilibrium of the model's design, underscoring the role of static policies in driving sequential ecological depletion .
