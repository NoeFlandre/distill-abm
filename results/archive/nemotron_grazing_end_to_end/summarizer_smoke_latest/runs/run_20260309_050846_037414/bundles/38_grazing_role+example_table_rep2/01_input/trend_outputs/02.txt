The mean household risk attitude (risk-att) deviations show dynamic shifts across timesteps, reflecting adaptive responses to stochastic climate shocks and learning strategies. Initially (timesteps 0–10), risk-att values are moderately positive (0.075–0.18), suggesting overstocking relative to recommended levels. This aligns with the high initial livestock-init (90) and variable rainfall (rain-std=100) straining pasture biomass. By timesteps 20–30, risk-att deviations oscillate between -0.09 and 0.32, indicating households adjusting herd sizes downward (risk-aversion) as pasture productivity becomes constrained by stochastic droughts (gr2=0.1). Later timesteps (40–50) see narrower ranges (-0.09–0.24), suggesting convergence toward stabilized adaptations, possibly due to emergent social norms or dominant learning strategies. E-LBD agents likely drive experimentation in risk adjustment, while E-RO-SL1 agents accelerate diffusion of risk-aware practices within knowledge-radius=1.0, countering E-RO’s static behavior. The absence of resting? (automatic pasture recovery) amplifies the need for strategic herd management, with risk-mode?=True enabling agents to factor climate uncertainty into decisions. The data imply heterogeneous learning strategies and risk sensitivity collectively mitigate overgrazing risks but delayed equilibrium suggests persistent vulnerability to climate variability.
