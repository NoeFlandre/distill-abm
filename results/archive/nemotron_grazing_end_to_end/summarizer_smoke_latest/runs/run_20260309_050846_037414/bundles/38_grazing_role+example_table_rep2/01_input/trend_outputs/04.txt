The green biomass begins at a stable equilibrium of approximately 45,000 units, reflecting initial system allocation of resources under baseline grazing pressure. This stability persists until mid-simulation (~Time Step 24), where a gradual decline emerges, reaching a nadir of 44,400–44,700 units by Time Step 48. The initial phase of decline correlates with the unchecked impact of high initial herd sizes (90 livestock per household) and stochastic rainfall shocks (std=100), which amplify grazing pressure and reduce biomass regeneration capacity. The lack of pasture rest mechanisms (resting?=False) accelerates degradation, as biomass decay outpaces recovery in wet (gr1=0.5) and dry (gr2=0.1) seasons.   Inset, at Time Step 24, a shift in biomass dynamics becomes apparent. While the absolute value of the decline is modest in percentage terms (~1–1.5%), it signals the system’s transition into a new state of resource allocation. This phase aligns with the emergence of behavioral adaptation among learning agents (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1). E-LBD agents, constrained by satisficing-threshold=0.0, progressively adjust herd sizes or grazing intensity based on historical outcomes, while E-RO-SL1 agents, limited by disabled social learning (social-susceptibility=0.0, SL2-strategy-switch=False), remain isolated in their behavioral inertia. These localized adjustments create heterogeneous regrowth patterns, with biomass stabilizes at ~45,000 units by Time Step 60. The recovery is incomplete, suggesting persistent system constraints: high livestock density, constrained social learning, and stochastic climate variability prevent a return to pre-similarity baseline levels. The simulation concludes with biomass values skewed toward mid-range stability, reflecting a dynamic equilibrium between grazing pressure, risk-sensitive decision-making, and stochastic climate shocks.   Theoretical drivers for these trajectories include the extended RAGE model’s focus on adaptive management through learning-by-doing and risk-aware strategies, though the absence of social learning frameworks (homog-behav-types?=True, heterogeneous strategies disabled) limits broader behavioral diffusion. The persistent biomass levels below initial values underscore the interplay between individual heterogeneity in risk tolerance (risk-att-init=0.5) and the absence of institutionalizing management practices (e.g., resting-threshold policies).
