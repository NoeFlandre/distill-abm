This plot depicts the progression of livestock health inequality over time as quantified by the Gini coefficient. Initially, all simulations begin with near-zero inequality (values clustering around 0.00), suggesting minimal disparities in herd health at the model's start. The divergence begins modestly, with slight upward trajectories emerging between timesteps 20 and 40, reflecting early adaptation cycles. By midpoint steps (60), significant stratification emerges: some agents demonstrate accelerated health improvements while others experience stagnation or decline. This pattern intensifies exponentially after step 80, where the richest livestock holders expand their advantages dramatically (values exceeding 0.08), while the most vulnerable agents struggle to maintain basic herd viability. The final timesteps (90-100) reveal pronounced polarization: the top 20% of simulations show coefficients >0.085, while the bottom 20% hover near 0.001. Notably, the most resilient households concentrate growth between timesteps 60-90, capitalizing on favorable conditions earlier than their less successful counterparts. The model's parameters - particularly risk attitudes (risk_att-init=0.5), base growth rates (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1), and rainfall variability (std=100) - create environmental pressures that systematically amplify health disparities as simulations progress. This stark divergence justifies the second-order inference that agent-level risk tolerance and learning mechanisms fundamentally shape long-term resource distribution outcomes in this simulated grazing economy.
