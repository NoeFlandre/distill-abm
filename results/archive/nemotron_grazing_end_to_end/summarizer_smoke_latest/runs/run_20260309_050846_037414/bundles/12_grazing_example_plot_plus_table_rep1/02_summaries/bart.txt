 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The model focuses on E-RO under homogeneous conditions with climatic variability . Strategy switching (SL2-strategy-switch?) is disabled, preventing agents from adopting other types . Social learning (E-RO-SL1) is inactive due to homog-behav-types?  The average green biomass begins at 45,000 units and maintains this level for the first 40 steps, indicating initial system stabilization . From steps 40 to 100, biomass gradually increases, but this growth is not uniform across all simulation runs . This divergence among runs suggests heightened sensitivity to environmental variability (e.g., stochastic rainfall patterns with a standard deviation of 100) and agent decision-making patterns .  The model's parameters create environmental pressures that amplify health disparities as simulations progress . The top 20% of simulations show Gini coefficient >0.085, while the bottom 20% hover near 0.001 . The most resilient households concentrate growth between timesteps 60-90, capitalizing on favorable conditions earlier than their less successful counterparts .  Simulation results indicate a dynamic but stabilizing pattern in the population of E-RO households . Initial variability may reflect resource depletion or random mortality events impacting household readiness to participate in decision-making . The persistent homogeneity (homog-behav-types=True) and disabled strategy-switching (SL2-strategy-switch=False) likely prevent large-scale behavioral shifts .
