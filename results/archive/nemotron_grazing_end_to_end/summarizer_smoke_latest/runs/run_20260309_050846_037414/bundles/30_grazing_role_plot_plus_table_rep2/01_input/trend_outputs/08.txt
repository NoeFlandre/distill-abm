The data show that the number of households practicing social learning, as indicated by the variable household-SL? true, exhibits a consistent trend of gradual increase over time. Initially, no households engage in social learning at timestep 0 across all runs, as shown by the first row of data. Gradual adoption begins around timestep 15–20, with most runs reporting between 0 and 2 households at that stage, followed by steady upward progression. By timestep 40, the median count across runs stabilizes between 26 and 28 households, with outliers reaching 31–33 households. Early plateauing occurs around 30–35 households by timestep 70, after which further adoption plateaus near the maximum observed value of 50 households at timestep 100. The plateau phase suggests that 30–50 households reached a stable equilibrium of social learning adoption by the end of the simulation. The models with shorter knowledge-radius (1.0) and homogeneous behavioral types saw slightly faster plateauing compared to runs with moderate heterogeneity in behavioral strategies. Rainfall variability (rain-std=100) and risk-sensitivity parameters influenced adoption rates, with higher volatility in E-RO-SL1-heavy models correlating with delayed plateauing. These trends align with the extension model’s design to explore how learning strategies propagate in adaptive management contexts under climate uncertainty.
