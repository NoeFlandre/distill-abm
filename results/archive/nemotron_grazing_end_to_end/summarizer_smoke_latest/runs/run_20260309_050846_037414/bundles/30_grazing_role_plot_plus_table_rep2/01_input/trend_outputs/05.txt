The simulation data reveals three distinct phases in livestock and destocking trends. Initially, from time step 0 to 20, the average livestock + destock value plunges from 90 to 40-45 across all households, indicating a rapid depletion of pasture resources due to high initial herds (livestock-init=90) exceeding carrying capacity. This steep decline reflects immediate grazing pressure overwhelming biomass regeneration rates (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1) under stochastic rainfall conditions (rain-std=100, global-rain?=True). Between time steps 20-60, a gradual recovery occurs, with values rising from ~40-45 to ~45-50, driven by adaptive herd size adjustments. This recovery phase aligns with stochastic recovery of vegetation under variable precipitation and agents' risk-sensitive behavior (risk-att-init=0.5), where some households reduce herds below initial levels while others maintain grazing pressure. After time step 60, values plateau around 45-50, suggesting a new equilibrium where adaptive strategies (E-RO-SL1 social learning, E-LBD trial-and-error learning) stabilize grazing practices. The plateau values are lower than initial levels due to persistent climate uncertainty (rainfall variability) and legacy effects of early resource depletion. Heterogeneous learning strategies enable the system to self-regulate, with risk-averse agents (lower herds post-collapse) buffer against climatic shocks. The observed U-shaped temporal pattern highlights the interplay between ecological capital degradation, strategic adaptation, and long-term system resilience in pasture-based systems under uncertainty.
