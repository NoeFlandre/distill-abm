 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The mean household risk attitude in the simulated grazing system fluctuates over the 100-year period, shaped by climatic variability and agent decision-making dynamics . This stability might reflect the system's sensitivity to climatic volatility (rain-std = 100) as a persistent risk factor, with households maintaining fixed risk tolerances despite environmental fluctuations .  The average destocking of households begins at a high of approximately 7.0 households at step 0, indicating initial resource depletion . Decadal correlation analysis reveals increasing annual mean temperature linkage with tree canopy reduction rates . Nighttime temperatures follow similar trajectory starting 72°F increasing 0.5°F annually up year 10 .  Non-learning households (E-RO strategy) begin at 30 households and experience an initial sharp decline, dropping to ~20 households by time step 15 . E-RO-SL1 strategy (ERO SL1) and E-LBD strategies diverge post-step 40 . The model highlights risk-averse decision-making and climatic shocks as key drivers of destocking trajectories .
