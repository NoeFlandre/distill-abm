The mean initial risk attitude of social learning agents (E-RO-SL1) begins at 0.5 at timestep 0, indicating a neutral disposition toward deviation from recommended livestock numbers. By timestep 30, the average risk attitude decreases to 0.1, suggesting a risk-averse trend likely driven by overgrazing pressures from high initial herd sizes (90 livestock/household) and variable rainfall (std=100) stressing pasture recovery. This shift reflects agents' adaptive responses to resource scarcity. By timestep 60, the attitude plateaus near 0.0, indicating sustained risk aversion as learning-by-doing feedbacks from repeated grazing trials reinforce conservative strategies. The final stabilization at 0.0 by timestep 100 underscores minimal willingness to increase herd sizes despite moderate rainfall regeneration rates (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1). These trends highlight how interactive feedbacks between environmental shocks and learned risk sensitivity reshape agent behavior in smallholder systems.
