The number of households engaged in social learning (SL) increases gradually from an initial 20 agents to 40 agents by the final time step (100 years). Early in the simulation (steps 0–25), the adoption of SL is slow, with only marginal increases. Between steps 25–75, the rate of adoption accelerates, reaching 30 agents by step 50. From step 75 onward, growth stabilizes, with the final 20 steps showing minimal net gains. This trend suggests that SL adoption is constrained by network effects and behavioral inertia within the modeled population. Despite the presence of 20 agents preconfigured to use SL (E-RO-SL1) and 20 using learning-by-doing (E-LBD), the slower early adoption indicates that SL dynamics depend on environmental stressors (e.g., variable rainfall, resource scarcity) to trigger behavioral shifts. Risk-sensitive agents (E-RO) likely adopt SL later, as risk tolerance aligns with pessimistic outcomes during droughts, where social learning becomes a risk mitigation strategy. The linear slope implies no critical thresholds or feedback loops in SL propagation under the tested parameters. For policymakers, this reinforces the role of social learning in reducing collective action dilemmas in grazing systems, provided that learning opportunities are spatially proximate (knowledge-radius=1.0) and climate variability persists to incentivize adaptive strategies. However, the modest adoption rate suggests that deliberate interventions (e.g., knowledge-sharing programs) may be necessary to accelerate SL diffusion in real-world systems with similar parameters.
