The simulation reveals critical insights about adaptive herd management in stochastic grazing systems. Initial herd sizes (90 livestock per household) create immediate overgrazing pressure, leading to rapid biomass decline. Risk-sensitive agents (risk-att-init=0.5) exhibit delayed adjustment patterns with pronounced volatility in early timesteps (0-20) due to climate shocks (rain-std=100). Learning strategies create divergent trajectories: E-LBD agents demonstrate gradual biomass recovery (steps 30-50) through individual outcome tracking, while E-RO-SL1 agents show localized herd reductions (steps 40-60) via neighbor observation. Prolonged deviations (steps 50-80) reflect systemic grazing pressure from conservative herd management despite deteriorating pasture conditions. Climate variability amplifies feedback loops, with drought periods (low biomass regeneration rates: gr2=0.1) causing herd size rebounds during resource scarcity. Long-term stabilization emerges after 80 timesteps as behavioral strategies adapt to ecological constraints.
