The data reveals a nonlinear decline in mean destocking events from initial maximums to stabilized low levels across all households. At simulation start, volitional destocking rapidly peaks at approximately 6 stocked animals per household, suggesting immediate responses to resource scarcity triggered by high initial livestock numbers (90/herd) combined with environmental stochasticity (rainfall variability: mean=200, std=100). This acute phase corresponds to the model's initial grazing pressure under fixed starting patches and global rainfall shocks.

Decadal-scale reductions falling to 0.4-0.6 persistent destocking events indicate adaptive management stabilization. The E-LBD agents (20 households) and E-RO-SL1 learners (20 households) likely drive long-term equilibration through localized strategy experimentation. E-LBD agents' outcome-based adjustments explain early destocking responsiveness, while E-RO-SL1 agents' neighbor observation (knowledge-radius=1.0) facilitates consensus around sustainable herd sizes. Risk-sensitivity (r-parameter=0.5) tempers behavioral extremes, preventing oscillatory destocking patterns despite rainfall volatility.

The absence of pasture rest mechanisms and high baseline grazing tolerance (w=0.8) necessitate voluntary destocking as the primary adaptive mechanism. Successful strategy dissemination reduced herd numbers to ~0.4-0.6 destocking events by timestep 100, demonstrating resilience to climate uncertainty. The homogeneity in destocking trajectories despite initial behavioral heterogeneity suggests emergent social coordination around risk-aware destocking thresholds, aligning with the RAGE model's focus on dynamic carrying capacity adjustments.
