The mean household risk attitude deviations during the simulation show a distinct trend of gradual decline and stabilization, reflecting systemic adjustments in pasture management under stochastic environmental and social pressures. Initially, deviations start near neutral values (0.02–0.1 at timestep 0) but exhibit increasing magnitude of negative deviations over time, with the majority of households shifting toward reduced livestock holdings compared to recommendations by approximately 0.08–0.2 risk units by the final timestep.   \ 

Key observable patterns include: \ \- **Early-phase divergence (0–20 years):** Values bifurcate, with some households maintaining positive deviations (-0.04 to 0.08) while others show sharper declines (-0.15 to -0.25). This suggests initial heterogeneity in responses to environmental shocks, particularly rainfall variability (std=100) and high initial herd sizes (90 units), which likely strained pasture biomas. \ \- **Mid-simulation inflection points:** At timestep 40, deviations peak in negative territory (-0.12 to -0.27 for most households), coinciding with intensified risk perception from compounding environmental stressors. Recovery attempts emerge in subgroups (e.g., 0.17–0.27 deviations at timestep 60), potentially reflecting adaptive strategies among agents utilizing learning mechanisms (E-LBD: 20 agents, E-RO-SL1: 20 agents). However, these adjustments do not reverse the aggregate trend, as the mean-deviation remains below zero thereafter. \ \- **Late-phase stabilization:** By timestep 80+, deviations cluster tightly around -0.08–0.01, indicating equilibrium between risk mitigation (lower herd sizes) and resource recovery. This aligns with the model's fixed grazing rules (no rest) and slow biomass regrowth in dry seasons (gr2=0.1), suggesting resource depletion outpaces recovery potential. \ \nThe dominance of E-RO behavioral types (baseline fixed-rule agents) likely limits systemic adaptation, as their inability to update strategies perpetuates suboptimal herd decisions.
