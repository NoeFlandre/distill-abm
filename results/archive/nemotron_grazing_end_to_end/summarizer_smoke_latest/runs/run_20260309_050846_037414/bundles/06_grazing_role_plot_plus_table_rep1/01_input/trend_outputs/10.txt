The simulation data reveals that non-learning E-RO households maintain a relatively stable population across all time steps, with values fluctuating narrowly between 20-32 throughout the 100-step period. This stability suggests that E-RO agents resist strategy shifts even in dynamic environmental conditions (rainfall variability: mean=200, std=100), likely due to their fixed rule-based decision-making framework. Initial conditions showing 20 E-RO households increased marginally (up to 32) early in the simulation before converging to a ~20 baseline, indicating partial adoption of learning strategies by some agents. Parameters governing risk sensitivity (risk-att-init=0.5), restricted grazing mobility (start-on-homepatch?=True), and disabled resting mechanisms (resting?=False) likely contributed to disciplined resource use patterns that prevented collapses or rebellions in E-RO populations. The absence of satisficing behaviors (satisficing-threshold=0.0) and strictly enforced grazing rules suggest that E-RO agents consistently maintained herd sizes below critical thresholds for pasture degradation. The trivial fluctuation pattern (unchanged values after step 25) implies equilibrium reached by late simulation phase, reflecting the model's heterogeneously stable outcomes under extended timesteps (timesteps=100). Notably, risk-aware decision-making (risk-mode?=True) appears insufficient to disrupt E-RO dominance despite stochastic shocks, given their minimal behavioral adaptivity.
