The analysis focuses on trends in livestock and destocking across households, as shown in simulated data. At the outset of the simulation (initial timestep), all households maintained maximum herd sizes of 90 livestock units, reflecting the default initialization parameter. This initial abundance quickly led to resource overexploitation, resulting in a sharp decline in livestock totals over the first 30 timesteps. By timestep 30, the aggregate herd size had stabilized at approximately 40–41 livestock units per household, suggesting a system-wide 'tipping point' caused by depleted pasture degradation.   During the recovery phase (timesteps 30–100), mean herd sizes gradually increased, reaching 61–62 units by the final timestep. This rebound phase exhibited variability, with temporary fluctuations suggesting adaptive behavior among agents. The recovery progressed in two phases: a nonlinear increase from timestep 30–60, followed by a more stable linear growth from timestep 60–100.   Heterogeneous behavioral strategies likely influenced these trends. While the parameter homog-behav-types?=True indicates homogeneous agent behavior, the extended model features like risk-mode?=True and risk-att-init=0.5 imply agents incorporating risk sensitivity. Additionally, although social learning (social-susceptibility=0.0) was disabled, the coexistence of learning-by-doing (number-E-LBD=20) and social-learning (number-E-RO-SL1=20) strategies might have enabled incidental cumulative learning.   The later-stage stability in herd sizes, observable after timestep 80, correlates with the model's grazing pressure moderation mechanisms described in the parameters gr1=0.5 and gr2=0.1. The absence of automated resting (resting?=False) suggests recovery resulted from strategic herd reduction rather than enforced pasture recovery.   No significant deviations from the described patterns were observed across the data distribution, with all simulated households following similar trajectories. The correlation between timestep transitions and livestock management decisions aligns with the described objectives of assessing adaptive management and strategy propagation under uncertainty.
