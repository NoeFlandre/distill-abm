The average initial risk attitude of social learning agents remains relatively stable across all households throughout the simulation. Initially, the mean risk attitudes start around 0.075 in all households, indicating a uniformly low baseline risk aversion. Over time, these values exhibit minor fluctuations (typically shifting by ±0.01–0.02), but no systematic trend emerges. For instance, by the final reported time steps, mean attitudes hover near 0.045–0.18, with some households showing slight increases (e.g., household 3 moving from -0.04 to 0.18) and others decreases (e.g., household 6 declining from 0.01 to -0.11). The variability appears consistent across the 100-year simulation, suggesting that social learning mechanisms do not drive large-scale shifts in risk attitudes under homogeneous conditions. Climatic variability (fixed rainfall mean=200, std=100) and static grazing parameters (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1) likely constrain behavioral adjustments, resulting in persistent, narrow fluctuations within the 0.0–0.2 risk attitude range. This aligns with the model’s focus on E-RO agents under homogeneous conditions, where risk aversion remains a background factor rather than a primary driver of resource management decisions.
