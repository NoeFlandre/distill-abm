After timestep 60, biomass stabilizes temporarily at ~90,136.62 (timestep 70) before resuming a downward trajectory, reaching 89,570.52 (timestep 90) and ultimately collapsing to 72,068.3 (timestep 100).   \nThe initial decline likely reflects agents' overstocking decisions under E-RO strategies, overwhelming pasture recovery capacity. The stabilization near timestep 30 may indicate partial adaptation via risk-adjusted grazing (risk-att-init=0.5), where agents cautiously reduce stocking after observing resource depletion. However, the re-acceleration of decline post-timestep 60 suggests ineffective strategy synchronization. By timestep 90, biomass enters a critical threshold (~87,000), with volatility intensifying due to climatic variability (rain-std=100) and uncoordinated agent responses. The final crash to 72,068.3 by timestep 100 implies irreversible degradation of grazing resilience.   \nThese trends highlight the failure of homogeneous E-RO agents to mitigate climate-driven resource stress, underscoring the need for behavioral diversity or social learning mechanisms (e.g., E-RO-SL1) to enhance adaptive capacity. Decision makers should prioritize enabling cooperative strategies and climate-informed resting policies to prevent systemic collapse.
