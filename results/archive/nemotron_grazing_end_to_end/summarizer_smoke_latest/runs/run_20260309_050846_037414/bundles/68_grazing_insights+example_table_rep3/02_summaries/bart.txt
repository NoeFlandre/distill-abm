 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The initial risk attitudes of households (measured by `household-risk-att-init`) show a narrow distribution, clustering around 0.045 to 0.075 . The lack of extreme outliers implies that agent strategies under homogeneous behavioral types (E-RO) do not diverge significantly in risk tolerance . Early understocking (negative values) may stem from agents’ caution in response to rainfall variability (rain-std = 100)  The findings underscore how homogeneous risk-seeking behaviors can exacerbate resource depletion . The absence of strategy adaptation (homogeneous E-RO agents without social learning) correlates with unsustainable stocking decisions, as agents maintain Livestock numbers above ecologically collaborative thresholds, leading to progressive pasture degradation .  The number of households following the E-RO (non-learning) strategy exhibits distinct patterns across simulation runs, reflecting the interplay of environmental variability and behavioral rigidity . The cumulative destocking of livestock begins at 0.0 across all households at timestep 0 and undergoes significant fluctuations in the early simulation phases . Peaks between Years 22-30 indicate periods of lower pasture biomass recovery during adverse climates .  The model’s parameters (homog-behav-types? = True, no strategy switching) theoretically enforce uniformity . This implies that ecological variability, rather than behavioral flexibility, drives demographic shifts among non-learning agents . The absence of social learning (social-susceptibility = 0, knowledge- radius = 1) limits opportunities for collective risk-sharing .
