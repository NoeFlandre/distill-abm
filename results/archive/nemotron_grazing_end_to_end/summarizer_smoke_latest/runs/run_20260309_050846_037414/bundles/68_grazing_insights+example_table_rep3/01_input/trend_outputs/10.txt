The number of households following the E-RO (non-learning) strategy exhibits distinct patterns across simulation runs, reflecting the interplay of environmental variability and behavioral rigidity. In the majority of simulations (e.g., the first 14 rows), counts remain stable at approximately **20 households** across all timesteps, consistent with the homogeneous condition where all agents are initialized as E-RO (and SL2-strategy-switch? is disabled). This stability suggests that E-RO agents maintain fixed stocking rates regardless of climatic shocks, as observed in the raw data where timesteps 1–29 rarely deviate from the baseline.  

However, in a subset of simulations (e.g., rows 15–29), the number of E-RO households declines significantly during early to mid-simulation timesteps (between 5 and 15 years). For instance, several runs show a sharp drop from 30–32 to below 20 within the first 10–15 years, with some trajectories collapsing to near zero by timesteps 20–25. Conversely, a few runs (rows 1–5) display marginal fluctuations (e.g., ±3 households) but stabilize quickly. These divergences likely stem from stochastic rainfall patterns (global-rain? = True, rain-std = 100), which exacerbate pasture degradation over time. E-RO agents, constrained by fixed stocking decisions and no adaptive learning, may overgraze during droughts, triggering resource collapse and household attrition. 

Notably, the model’s parameters (homog-behav-types? = True, no strategy switching) theoretically enforce uniformity, yet the data reveal heterogeneity in outcomes. This implies that ecological variability—rather than behavioral flexibility—drives demographic shifts among non-learning agents. The persistence of most runs at 20–25 households reflects the buffer provided by initial herd sizes (livestock-init = 90) and baseline agricultural resilience, while crashes highlight systemic fragility under prolonged climatic stress. 

For decision-makers, these trends underscore that non-learning strategies may perform adequately under stable conditions but face heightened failure risks in variable environments. Interventions such as adaptive policies, contingency planning for drought years, or promoting climate-resilient practices could mitigate risks of resource depletion linked to static decision-making. The absence of social learning (social-susceptibility = 0, knowledge-radius = 1) in E-RO configurations limits opportunities for collective risk-sharing, further emphasizing the need for strategies to reduce systemic vulnerability.
