The Gini coefficient of healthy livestock across households begins near zero, indicating near-identical distributions of livestock health among all 60 households in the initial simulation phase. This uniformity persists through the first 15 time steps, with values remaining statistically indistinguishable (e.g., 1.37×10⁻⁴ at step 2, 6.97×10⁻⁵ at step 6). A gradual divergence emerges around time step 20, as the Gini coefficient starts to climb consistently. By step 30, values surpass 0.0015 in most households, reflecting accelerating inequality.   After a temporary plateau between steps 40–50, the Gini coefficient experiences a marked upward shift, reaching 0.0072 by step 60 and peaking at 0.095 in the final recorded step. This escalating trend suggests growing disparities in livestock resilience, potentially driven by climatic variability (high rainfall variance) and the E-RO agents’ fixed, non-learning decision-making framework. The absence of strategy switching (SL2-disabled) and social learning mechanisms (E-RO-SL1 inactive) may limit systemic adaptation, causing marginalized households to disproportionately underperform. Decision makers might interpret this divergence as evidence that uniform behavioral policies in variable environments risk entrenching inequities, urging exploration of more flexible institutional or economic structures to mitigate resource-based disparities.
