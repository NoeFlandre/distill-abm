 The agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE model, focusing on simulating smallholder farmers' decision-making in pasture-based systems with learning capabilities . The original model was designed to analyze feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in common property grazing systems . This extension adds heterogeneity in behavioral strategies (e.g., learning-by-doing, social learning) and introduces risk sensitivity .  The mean initial risk attitudes of social learning agents exhibit a pattern of stability with minor variations across the simulation . Most agents in the cohort maintain a value hovering around **0.075** throughout all time steps . The rising mean in the outlier group hints at alternative pathways for behavioral adaptation under uncertainty .  Minimal resource variability ensures ecological stability at 90,000 biomass units . Adaptive management strategies maintain productivity without significant resource deterioration . Moderate resource management effectiveness aligns with similar studies focusing on mean-field ecological balance scenarios . By step 100, terminal biomass stabilizes in most simulations between 45,500 and 46,500 units .  The Gini coefficient for total livestock health begins near zero at the start of the simulation and increases consistently over time . The rising inequality reflects feedbacks between social learning (know radius: 1.5) and resource variability . The upper bound across simulations remains constant at 54 households by step 29 . Simulation termination at step 29 corresponds to persistent platform-level stability .  The mean destock totals of households begin at 0.0 and show a steady progression toward stabilization around 120 households by the final timestep . The count of households following non-learning strategies (E-RO) demonstrates remarkable stability throughout the simulation, maintaining values consistently at 20 across all runs . The simulation’s 100-year time horizon allows long-term patterns to emerge .
