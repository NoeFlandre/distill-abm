The destocking metric reveals a pronounced initial decline in cattle numbers across households, followed by gradual stabilization. In the early simulation steps (0-20), nearly all households experienced significant destocking, with mean values averaging ~6.0 between steps 0-20, then stabilizing at ~5.5 between 20-40 steps. This initial sharp drop likely reflects the immediate impact of high herd sizes (livestock-init=90) compounded with stochastic rainfall variability (global-rain?=True, rain-std=100), which created resource shortages in initial conditions. By steps 60-80, destocking values clustered below 1.5, suggesting partial recovery in pasture resources enabled by biomass regeneration rates (gr1=0.5 for wet seasons, gr2=0.1 for dry seasons). However, lingering uncertainty from climate shocks (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100) maintained persistent variability in minor culling events. The model's risk-sensitive decision-making (risk-att-init=0.5) and absence of adaptive learning (satisficing-threshold=0.0, knowledge-radius=1.0) likely constrained behavioral adaptation, keeping destocking below 2.0 for most steps beyond step 100. This pattern aligns with the model's design to test long-term social-ecological performance under fixed behavioral strategies in variable environments.
