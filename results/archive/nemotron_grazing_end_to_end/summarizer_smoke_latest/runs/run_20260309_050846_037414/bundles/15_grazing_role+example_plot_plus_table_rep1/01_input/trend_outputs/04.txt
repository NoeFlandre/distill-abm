The total mean green biomass across all simulations initiates at approximately 45,000 to 46,000 units, with early fluctuations framing a dynamic range of 44,500 to 45,500 units within the first 20 steps. This initial variability reflects immediate responses to stochastic rainfall shocks (average annual rainfall = 200, standard deviation = 100) and uniformly high initial herd sizes (90 units per agent), which amplify grazing pressure. By step 40, biomass trends diverge: simulations with proactive adaptation by E-LBD agents begin stabilizing around 45,000 units, while others decline to 44,000–44,500 due to overgrazing from delayed strategy adjustment. The introduction of climatic instability accelerates variability starting at step 60, with biomass ranges expanding to 43,500–46,000 units across runs, driven by divergent risk sensitivity (risk-att-init = 0.5) and neighborhood social learning among E-RO-SL1 agents. By step 100, terminal biomass stabilizes in most simulations between 45,500 and 46,500 units, with three outliers exceeding 47,000 units, demonstrating long-term coadaptation between risk-aware herd management and pasture regeneration rates (gr1 = 0.5, gr2 = 0.1). The cascading effects of behavioral homogeneity (all agents default to E-RO) and absence of social-susceptibility (0.0) limit systemic strategy diffusion, resulting in persistent variability rather than convergent outcomes.
