The mean reserve biomass begins at 90,000 units and exhibits a pattern of initial stability, followed by gradual declines interspersed with periods of fluctuation. Early phases (first ~15 time steps) show minor increases up to 90,550 units, reflecting baseline grazing pressures under homogeneous E-RO agents. After this period, biomass stabilizes briefly before declining steadily over time, reaching ~83,000 units by the final steps. Declines accelerate at ~30-40 time steps, coinciding with increased rainfall variability (mean=200, std=100), as agents’ risk-averse stocking decisions amplify pasture degradation during low-precipitation events. The absence of resting mechanisms and strategy homogenization prevents corrective recovery, while risk-mode influences amplify short-term conservation but fail to counter long-term resource depletion. This trajectory suggests that single-loop E-RO decision-making under climatic uncertainty fosters unsustainable resource use in isolated populations, highlighting the limitations of behavioral homogeneity in adaptive grazing management. The final biomass level (~83,000 units) represents a critical threshold, aligning with model parameters suggesting thresholds for resource collapse outcomes.
