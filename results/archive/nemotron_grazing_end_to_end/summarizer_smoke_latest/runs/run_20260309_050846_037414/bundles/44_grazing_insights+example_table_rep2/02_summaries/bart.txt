 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The mean initial risk attitude of households starts consistently around 0.075 across all simulations . Negative values (-0.04083) indicate scenarios where agents begin with more conservative attitudes, potentially leading to reduced livestock stocking . Conversely, higher values (0.18167) suggest riskier starts, which might align with agents prioritizing immediate gains over sustainability .  The average total number of livestock begins at 90.0 and experiences a sharp decline from time step 21 onward . This initial decline reflects the depletion of pasture biomass due to sustained overstocking by households . The absence of social learning mechanisms (inactive due to homogenization) and the maintaining risk-averse behavior (risk-att-init = 0.5) amplifies stress on pastures .  The absence of social learning mechanisms (homog-behav-types? = True) and strategy-switching (SL2-strategy-switch? = False) in the baseline E-RO group likely limits adaptive responses to environmental shocks . The simulation ends with persistent inequality, underscoring the challenge of achieving equitable outcomes in systems dominated by stochastic environmental drivers and static behavioral strategies .  Risk-averse decision-making (risk-att-init=0.5) likely buffers against catastrophic herd collapses but does not prevent mild population dips during climatic stress . These dynamics imply that while homogeneous E-RO agents provide predictable stability, abrupt environmental shocks can induce fleeting destabilization .
