Preliminary analysis of the data reveals dynamic patterns in household-level destocking behavior across a simulated 30-year period (time steps 0–29) within a grazing system governed by climatic variability and uniform behavioral strategies.   Step 0 shows no destocking activity, aligning with initial baseline conditions before environmental pressures emerge.   From time step 1 onward, households begin destocking livestock at near-uniform levels (mean ~6.0), reflecting synchronized responses to initiating pasture management decisions.   However, divergence emerges by time step 3–5, where select households exhibit spikes up to ~7.5 livestock removed, suggesting localized resource stress or competitive pressure to accelerate reductions in stocking levels.   This divergence persists through time step 20, with household-level destocking rates fluctuating between 4.0 and 7.5, creating a bifurcated pattern: half of households maintain or increase removals while the other half gradually reduce theirs.   By time step 25 and beyond, a sharp decline dominates, with 80% of households averaging ≤3.0 destocked animals annually.   Notably, 15% of households reach zero destocking (indicated by 0.0 values), signaling herd depletion.   These trends imply that homogeneous behavioral strategies (E-RO agents) initially generate coordinated responses to resource scarcity, but climatic stress and agent interactions lead to long-term heterogeneity in resilience trajectories.   Decision-makers should recognize that uniform interventions upfront may temporarily unify decisions, but long-term outcomes rely on adapting to localized feed stress and agent-specific mortality risks under climatic volatility.   Epistemic uncertainty in agent-scale responses beyond time step 20 suggests the need for monitoring mechanisms to track emergent heterogeneity.   }
