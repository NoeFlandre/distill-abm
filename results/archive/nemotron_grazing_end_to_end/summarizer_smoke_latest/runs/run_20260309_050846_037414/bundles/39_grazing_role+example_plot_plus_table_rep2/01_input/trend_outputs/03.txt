The mean reserve biomass exhibited a U-shaped trajectory over the first 50 steps, starting near 90,000, declining to 80,000 by ~step 10, then recovering to 90,000–100,000 by ~step 40. This initial decline likely reflects herd overshoot due to high initial livestock (90 units) coupled with stochastic rainfall shocks (mean=200, std=100), which stress resource regeneration. The subsequent recovery aligns with pasture stabilization as alarming grazing pressures reduced unsustainable herds. Biomass then surged to ~120,000 by ~step 60—a likely equilibrium where residual herds coexist with regenerated biomass. Later phases (beyond ~70 steps) showed stabilization near 120,000 with dampened variability, suggesting system resilience emerges despite the absence of explicit learning strategies. The lack of adaptive behaviors (fixed E-RO strategies) implies that biomass persistence relies on self-regulating environmental pressures rather than proactive management. No clear monotonic trends persist, as periodic adjustments reflect grazing-vegetation feedback loops. Risk sensitivity (risk-att-init=0.5) modulated herd decisions but did not enable strategy shifts. Early resilience likely stems from system-level equilibria, while mid-phase oscillations highlight vulnerability to stochastic events.
