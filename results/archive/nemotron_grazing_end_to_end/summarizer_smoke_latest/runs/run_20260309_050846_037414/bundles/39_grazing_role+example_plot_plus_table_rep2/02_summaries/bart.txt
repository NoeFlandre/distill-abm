 The agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE model, focusing on simulating smallholder farmers' decision-making in pasture-based systems with learning capabilities . The original model was designed to analyze feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in common property grazing systems . This extension adds heterogeneity in behavioral strategies (e.g., learning-by-doing, social learning) and introduces risk sensitivity .  The agent-based model's mean household initial risk attitude reveals a consistent pattern of stability across the simulated population . The simulation shows dynamic adjustments in household risk attitudes across 100 timesteps . The absence of exogenous shocks overwhelming the system reinforces steady behavior, suggesting the system preserves a steady equilibrium state .  Mean attitudes below zero in final steps may reflect institutional constraints not explicitly modeled, warranting further validation against observed farmer decision patterns . The absence of converging strategies among extension agents highlights the complexity of adaptive learning dynamics and limitations of static behavioral initialization in multi-agent systems .  The Gini coefficient for total livestock health across households evolves from near-zero values at early time steps to reach its maximum asymptotic levels by 100 time steps . From steps 20–60, the coefficient increases exponentially in magnitude for most behavioral types, though E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 exhibit slower growth rates compared to risk-seeking variants . Risk-sensitive agents achieve maximum inequality (0.090–0.095) through asymmetric herd-size  The total count of non-learning households (E-RO strategy) begins at approximately 20–25 across most simulation runs and experiences a decline over time . The decline may reflect households exiting the system or transitioning strategies due to environmental stress or risk aversion . Risk-sensitive agents (risk-att-init=0.5) may temper restocking decisions under uncertainty .
