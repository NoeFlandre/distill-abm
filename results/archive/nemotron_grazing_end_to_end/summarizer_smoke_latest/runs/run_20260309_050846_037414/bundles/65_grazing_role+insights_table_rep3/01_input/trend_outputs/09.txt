The simulation shows a dynamic pattern of livestock destocking across households over 100 timesteps, reflecting adaptive responses to resource variability and social learning mechanisms. Initial destocking levels are low, indicating stable herd sizes in the first few years. By the 50th timestep, cumulative destocking accelerates, peaking around mid-simulation before declining slightly toward the final years. This U-shaped decline suggests initial resource stress drives proactive herd reductions, followed by stabilization as alternative strategies emerge through learning processes. Agents employing risk-sensitive decision-making exhibit earlier and more pronounced destocking, while social learners adopt slower but more adaptive approaches, reducing total destocking over time. These patterns highlight trade-offs between efficiency and resilience in heterogeneous behavioral types under climate uncertainty, providing insights for co-management strategies balancing productivity and system sustainability.
