The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock across households exhibits a general trend of increasing inequality over time. Initially, from time steps 0–9, the Gini coefficient remains near zero (-6.66e-16 to 2.65e-4), indicating near-perfect equality among households during the early simulation period. This equilibrium may reflect uniform adoption of baseline strategies (behavioral-type=E-RO) and immediate shared impacts of stochastic rainfall variability (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100), which temporarily homogenizes pasture degradation across households.   
   
A marked increase in inequality begins at time step 10, where the Gini coefficient rises to 1.37e-4, and continues to trend upward. Between time steps 10–30, the Gini coefficient fluctuates but generally escalates, reaching 2.64e-4 by time step 30. Notable peaks occur at time steps 16 (2.02e-4), 20 (1.68e-4), and 30 (2.64e-4), coinciding with periods of heightened climate variability (e.g., rainfall extremes) and adaptive responses by households. For instance, E-LBD agents (learn-by-doing) likely adjust herd sizes based on past outcomes, while E-RO-SL1 agents (social-learning) respond to localized pasture conditions within their knowledge-radius. These adaptive behaviors create emergent heterogeneity, exacerbating inequality as some households recover faster from droughts or overgraze more aggressively during abundant biomass growth (e.g., high gr1=0.5 wet-season regeneration rates vs. gr2=0.1 dry-season rates).   
   
The persistence of risk-aware decision-making (risk-mode=True, risk-att-init=0.5) and high initial grazing pressure (livestock-init=90) amplifies resource competition. Risk-seeking households (higher **risk-att-init**) may adopt herd expansion strategies during favorable conditions, leading to abrupt crashes during dry seasons, while risk-averse peers maintain stable but suboptimal herd sizes. Simultaneously, E-RO-SL1 agents, constrained by "fixed initial grazing areas" (start-on-homepatch?=True), may lag in adapting to pasture degradation, deepening inequality between adaptive and non-adaptive households.   
   
By the final time step (30), the Gini coefficient reaches its peak (2.64e-4), reflecting entrenched inequality driven by cumulative learning effects (behavioral heterogeneity), climatic shocks, and risk-tolerant strategies. This trajectory aligns with observed patterns in common-pool resource systems, where adaptive capacity and environmental stochasticity jointly determine distributional outcomes. Decision-makers should prioritize interventions that mitigate information gaps (e.g., enhancing social learning) and buffer climate shocks (e.g., coordinated pasture rest), while balancing risk tolerance incentives to stabilize outcomes.
