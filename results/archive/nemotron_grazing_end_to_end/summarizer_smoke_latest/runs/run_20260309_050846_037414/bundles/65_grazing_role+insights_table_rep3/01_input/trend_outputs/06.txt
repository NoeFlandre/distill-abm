The data reveals distinct phases in destocking behavior across the 30-year simulation. Initially, all households maintained herds at 6.0 livestock units through timestep 13, suggesting a system-wide adjustment to baseline resource availability. This stability shifted at timestep 14, where destocking rose to 7.18 units - a threshold marking the system's response to accumulating environmental pressures. Subsequent fluctuations between 5.1 and 7.55 units (timesteps 14-30) reflect dynamic interactions between climate variability (std=100 rainfall fluctuations) and adaptive agent strategies.   

E-RO agents (fixed rules) likely contributed to early stability, while E-LBD agents (learning-by-doing) and E-RO-SL1 agents (social learning) introduced variability as they modified herd sizes based on personal experience and local knowledge. The **risk-att-init=0.5** parameter indicates agents balanced risk-seeking and cautious decisions, resulting in moderate destocking during dry periods (coinciding with pasture biomass <0.1 threshold) followed by partial restocking during favorable conditions. This cyclical pattern aligns with the model's design to replicate real-world adaptive management.   

Implications suggest that decentralized, behaviorally adaptive grazing systems can mitigate overgrazing risks in variable climates. The observed self-regulation contrasts with static systems, demonstrating how learning strategies enable resilience through iterative destocking decisions rather than predetermined fixed thresholds.   

In advising pastoral managers, this simulation pattern emphasizes the value of integrating knowledge-sharing among smallholders and enabling flexible herd adjustments. The 5-7 unit destocking range highlights sustainable equilibrium zones that prevent ecosystem collapse while maintaining livelihoods, contingent on rapid behavioral adaptation to stochastic shocks.
