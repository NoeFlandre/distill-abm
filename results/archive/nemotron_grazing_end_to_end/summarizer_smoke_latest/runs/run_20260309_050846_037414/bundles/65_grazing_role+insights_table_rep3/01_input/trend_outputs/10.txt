The data indicates that the proportion of households following the E-RO (non-learning) behavioral strategy remains remarkably stable across most simulations and time steps. Key observations include:

1. **Baseline Stability**: The majority of simulations maintain consistent E-RO counts near the initial value of 20 households throughout all time steps. This reflects the homogeneous basal configuration where E-RO agents dominate through fixed rules and lack of adaptive learning mechanisms.

2. **Low Frequency Variability**: Minor fluctuations occur in individual simulation runs, with counts occasionally rising to 25-32 (early time steps) or dropping to 0-2 (late time steps) in specific replicates. These deviations appear statistically negligible compared to the dominant baseline.

3. **No Learning Dynamics**: Complete absence of social learning (social-susceptibility=0), satisficing (threshold=0), and intrinsic preference for learning (intrinsic-preference=0) prevents behavioral adaptation. This explains the lack of sustained strategy shifts despite stochastic environmental pressures from variable rainfall (global-rain?=True, rain-std=100).

4. **Risk Factor Inactivity**: Despite risk-aware decision-making being enabled (risk-mode?=True), the baseline risk tolerance (risk-att-init=0.5) appears insufficient to disrupt stable behavior, suggesting the parameter configuration maintains risk-level demand acceptance.

5. **Resource Stress Tolerance**: Initial high livestock weights (livestock-init=90) combined with conservative grass regeneration rates (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1) and grazing pressure (w=0.8) may create pasture stress, yet behavioral stability suggests households maintain E-RO despite divergence from sustainable pastoral threshold levels.

Implications for decision makers: E-RO systems exhibit resilience against environmental volatility but lack adaptive capacity to recover from grazing pressure. This supports prioritizing social learning (enabled via E-RO-SL1) and risk management training as transition pathways.
