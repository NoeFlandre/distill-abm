 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The mean reserve biomass across all 100 steps shows a general pattern of initial decline followed by stabilization and eventual recovery . In the first 10 steps, values range narrowly between 89,000 and 90,000, suggesting early resource depletion . By steps 20-30, variability increases, with some runs . Between steps 40-70, biomass stabilizes, with fluctuations tied to climate variability . From steps 70-100, biomass settles into an equilibrium range of 111,000-130,000 .  Initial values uniformly start at 90 units, representing the baseline livestock robustness before model dynamics engage . A rapid, steep downward trend dominates the first 30 steps, with values plunging below 70 in all households by time step 30 . This collapse reflects agents' initial stocking decisions under uniform behavioral baseline (E-RO) conditions .  The CSV data represents the average cumulative livestock destocked across 21 households over 100 model steps . By step 20, destocking totals reach up to 52.9% of initial values, with significant upward curvature in later steps . Trend aligns with model’s focus on agent strategies and climatic variability, as households reacting to environmental pressures and differing behavioral approaches may exacerbate inequities .  E-RO households are vulnerable to climatic shocks in the long term, with their persistence relying heavily on stable environmental conditions or the absence of stressors like grazing competition . -RO dominance persists when external pressures are mitigated by model design (e.g., no habitat fragmentation, homogeneous satisfaction thresholds)
