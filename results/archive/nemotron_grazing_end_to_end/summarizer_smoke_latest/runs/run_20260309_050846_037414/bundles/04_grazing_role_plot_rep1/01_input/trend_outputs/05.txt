The simulated mean livestock + destocked households exhibit a distinct pattern shaped by dynamic interactions between grazing pressure, resource recovery, and adaptive behaviors. During the initial time steps (0–20), herds experience a sharp decline in total numbers. Agent-based simulations (e.g., ‘E-RO-SL1, t1: 120’) show steep drops in the average metric, reflecting immediate overgrazing as herds (initially 90 livestock/household) deplete readily available biomass. Destocking escalates rapidly as pasture regeneration (governed by gr1=0.5 and gr2=0.1) fails to offset consumption rates.   \n   \n   By mid-simulation (20–40 steps), destocking peaks. Simulations with agents employing risk-aware decision-making (‘E-LBD’, ‘risk-att-init’=0.5) demonstrate slower destocking rates compared to non-learning agents (‘E-RO’), as adaptive agents reduce herds **earlier** in response to resource constraints. Social-learning agents (‘E-RO-SL1’) with **knowledge-radius**=1.0 also show dampened declines, leveraging observations of neighboring households’ challenges to adjust grazing intensity. This phase corresponds to simulated pasture biomass collapse, where fixed-rule agents (‘E-RO’) persist in overexploitation until biomass thresholds force maximal destocking.   \n   \n   Post-40 steps, recovery becomes evident. The average metric stabilizes in longer-term simulations (steps 60–80), with some trajectories (e.g., ‘E-RO-SL1’) plateauing at intermediate levels (~50–55 livestock/household) as adaptive strategies sustain pastoral resilience. However, stochastic rainfall (**rain-std**=100) introduces recurring stress, preventing full recovery. Notably, non-learning agents (‘E-RO’) tend to accumulate lower livestock totals over time due to inflexible grazing behavior, while heterogeneous strategies among agents create variability in metrics across simulations.   \n   \n   The final equilibration phase (80–100 steps) reveals persistent system-level constraints. Despite behavioral heterogeneity, long-term destocking impressions dominate, with mean values clustering below ~50 livestock/household. This aligns with the model’s design, where stochastic climate shocks and high initial livestock levels (90) create chronic pasture pressure that behavioral adaptation alone cannot fully mitigate. The absence of satisficing behavior (**satisficing-threshold**=0.0) eliminates one adaptive mechanism, emphasizing reliance on risk sensitivity and social learning to temper system collapse.
