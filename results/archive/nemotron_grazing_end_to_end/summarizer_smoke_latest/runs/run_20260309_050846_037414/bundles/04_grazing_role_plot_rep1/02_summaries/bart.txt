 The agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE model, focusing on simulating smallholder farmers' decision-making in pasture-based systems with learning capabilities . The original model was designed to analyze feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in common property grazing systems . This extension adds heterogeneity in behavioral strategies (e.g., learning-by-doing, social learning) and introduces risk sensitivity .  The model simulates systemic dynamics in common property grazing systems, revealing complex interactions between behavioral strategies, environmental stochasticity, and adaptive capacity . The mean initial risk attitude (y-axis) of social learning agents (E-RO-SL1) remains constant at the nominal risk tolerance value of 0.5 across all timesteps .  Climate uncertainty amplifies divergence between static (**E-RO**) and adaptive strategies, emphasizing risk sensitivity's role . Biomass recovery begins at ~30 steps, correlating with regional rainfall variability (**rain-mean**=200, **rain-std**=100 inducing stochastic stress) The simulation shows dynamic green biomass adjustments over 100 timesteps, with critical phases emerging at ~5, ~30, ~60, and ~80 .  Simulations with agents employing risk-aware decision-making (‘E-LBD’, ‘risk-att-init’=0.5) demonstrate slower destocking rates compared to non-learning agents (E-RO’), as adaptive agents reduce herds **earlier** in response to resource constraints . The final equilibration phase (80–100 steps) reveals persistent system-level constraints .  The Gini coefficient exhibits a consistent upward trajectory across all runs, with the metric progressing from near zero values to stable levels between 0.04 and 0.095 by the final time steps . The curvature of the distribution and the timing of key transitions in inequality dynamics align with the following observations .  From step 40 onward, SL engagement stabilizes at ~22 households despite continued climate stochasticity . E-RO-SL1 agents demonstrate moderate destocking, leveraging social learning (knowledge-radius=1.0) to mitigate resource depletion . Social learning emerges as pivotal in balancing destocking with ecosystem resilience .
