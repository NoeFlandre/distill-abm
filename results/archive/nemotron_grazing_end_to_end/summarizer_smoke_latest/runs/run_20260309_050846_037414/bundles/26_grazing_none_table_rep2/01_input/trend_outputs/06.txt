The dynamic patterns observed in destocking reflect strategic adaptations to climatic variability under homogeneous agent conditions. Initially, universal destocking at 6.0 livestock per household stabilizes across all timesteps (Year 0–1), establishing baseline resource demands. Timestep 2 introduces variability, with 10 households exceeding 6.0 (up to 7.4+), while 15 households decline below 6.0, marking the first bifurcation in decision-making trajectories.  By Year 5, directional selection emerges: 2 households maintain >7.0 destocking, whereas 25 households show sustained declines below 6.0, including three sub-4.0 cases, indicating resource-driven attrition in lower-performing groups.  The critical stabilization phase (Years 20–29) demonstrates a bimodal equilibrium: 15 households cluster near 0.4–0.6 destocking, two households achieve extinction (0.0), and 12 maintain stable ~1.0 destocking through repeated resource congruence.  This convergence pattern reveals that persistent climatic volatility (rain-mean: 200, rain-std: 100) functions as a selective pressure, favoring adaptive agents who optimize stocking rates through repeat exposure, while non-adaptive agents persist in unsustainable equilibria.  No behavioral inheritance or strategy switching occurs during this period, suggesting individual-level learning outweighs social learning mechanisms in homogeneous populations under these parameter settings.  The decay in destock values over the 29-year triad ultimately establishes a floor at 0.0 for resource-limited households and 0.8+ thresholds for environmentally resilient groups.
