 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The data table presents fluctuations in the average household risk attitude (household-risk-att-init) across 29 simulation runs of a fixed E-RO agent population . The data reveals persistent variability in household risk attitudes across simulations . The absence of learning mechanisms in this configuration amplifies reliance on environmental feedback rather than experiential or social adjustments .  The data reveals a consistent decline in average livestock and destocked livestock numbers across all households over time, beginning after the initial stable phase . From year 25 onward, a consistent downslope appears across most simulations, with biomass dropping 200-300 units by year 28 in many cases .  The data table tracks the number of households engaging in social learning (household-SL) over 100 timesteps in a heterogeneous agent-based model with climatic variability . The absence of social learning mechanisms (due to homog-behav-types?=True) limits strategy alignment, resulting in fragmented responses to resource scarcity .  Lower-count scenarios may reflect households relying solely on individual learning-by-doing (E-RO), which struggles under variable rainfall and risk-aversion . This could explain instances of abrupt declines (e.g., sharp drops to **4** or **6** households), as agents in risk-sensitive or low-resilience configurations fail to sustain E-RO strategies under stress .
