The initial population of E-RO agents converges rapidly after environmental stress begins affecting herding outcomes. A rapid spike then occurs at approximately 15% during the first several time steps, at which point the number of households remains relatively stable through the end of the simulation. This consistent presence of E-RO households indicates the persistent role of non-learning agents despite changing environmental conditions, which present risks only through stochastic resolution of edge nodes. The initial high herd sizes lead to excessive pressure in the pasture resources. Therefore, conversion to different agent classes persists. The difference in rare cases indicates this factor derives from the variability of climatic conditions influencing herd management styles rather than the consequences of the agents' behavioral approaches in fluctuating conditions.

These agents have persistent herds throughout, but at serious risk of failure due to low strategic capacity in responding to external shocks. This accounts for the reason why herds do not recover pasture resources, as market and forestry managers find success with such agents. The pattern shows the resilience of heterogeneity within the clusters and their vulnerability in the model setup.

For decision-makers, the findings suggest that policies encouraging adaptation among otherwise non-learners could significantly improve system sustainability. The stability of the E-RO population challenges the assumption that all agents will necessarily adopt learning strategies in uncertain environments, highlighting the need for tailored support systems for resistant groups.
