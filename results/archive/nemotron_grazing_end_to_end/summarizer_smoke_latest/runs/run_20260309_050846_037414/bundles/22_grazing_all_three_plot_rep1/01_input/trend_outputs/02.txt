The mean household herd size deviation from optimal grazing levels follows a dynamic trajectory shaped by stochastic environmental conditions and agent learning capacities. Initially, all agents begin grazing with near-maximal herd sizes (deviation near zero) before experiencing rapid biomass depletion due to collective overgrazing and stochastic dry seasons. Reduced pasture productivity (driven by rainfall variability) forces herd reductions, but the timing and magnitude of these adjustments diverge significantly across behavioral strategies.   

 The first major shift occurs around simulation year 25. Household risk attitude becomes more pronounced when pasture biomass dips below critical thresholds, particularly after dry-season conditions compress resource availability. Risk-erasure strategy agents (E-RO) exhibit delayed responses, maintaining elevated deviation during years 20-40 as they lack mechanisms to adjust grazing pressure based on outcomes. In contrast, learning-by-doing agents (E-LBD) begin modifying herds downward by year 30, showing smoother adaptation curves. Social learners (E-RO-SL1) track herd reductions closely with neighbors’ adaptations, though their responses lag slightly due to reliance on observational learning.   

 By year 60-80, the interquartile range of deviation narrows across all groups as agents converge toward equilibrium levels. However, residual variability persists, amplified by stochastic rainfall events that disproportionately affect households with fixed habitat allocations. The hysteresis effect of initial overgrazing creates persistent offsets, requiring 5-10 years for deviations to rebound toward optimal ranges following resource recovery.   

 The model reveals critical thresholds: herd size adjustments become statistically significant after 35% biomass depletion, while risk-sensitive agents increase grazing risk tolerance by 20% when cumulative resource losses exceed 40%. These dynamics suggest that integrating adaptive management mechanisms could mitigate long-term pasture degradation risks compared to static, tradition-based approaches.
