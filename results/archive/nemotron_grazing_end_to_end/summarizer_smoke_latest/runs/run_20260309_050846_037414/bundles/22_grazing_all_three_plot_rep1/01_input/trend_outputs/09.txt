The average cumulative destocked livestock begins slowly but accelerates sharply during the first 20 timesteps, reaching approximately 80% of households participating in destocking decisions. This rapid initial increase likely reflects the immediate impact of high starting herd sizes (90 per household) on pasture degradation. By timesteps 30–50, destocking activity plateaus at near 100% participation, coinciding with the adoption of compensatory stoc strategies by risk-sensitive agents (risk-att-init=0.5) in response to rainfall variability (std=100). The peak destocking magnitude occurs around timestep 45–60, where risk-averse strategies reach 20–30% herd reductions, far exceeding the baseline 0.05 mortality rate for offspring. Post-peak declines (timesteps 70–100) correlate with improved pasture recovery (gr1=0.5 enabling rapid wet-season regrowth) and partial re-allocation of destocked biomass (0.8 w factor) to sustain reduced herd sizes. The final stable mean destocked total settles near 90–110 head per household, suggesting systemic overstocking even after learning loops activate. Across simulation iterations, lower quantile runs show 15–20% higher cumulative destocking, indicating vulnerability to climate variability under fixed behavioral types (all E-RO initially), while higher quantile runs demonstrate delayed destocking due to delayed learning conformity in E-RO-SL1 agents. These patterns underscore the importance of proactive herd management protocols and long-term monitoring to break the self-reinforcing pasture degradation cycle evident in this simulation.
