 The agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE model, focusing on simulating smallholder farmers' decision-making in pasture-based systems with learning capabilities . The original model was designed to analyze feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in common property grazing systems . This extension adds heterogeneity in behavioral strategies (e.g., learning-by-doing, social learning) and introduces risk sensitivity .  The average initial risk attitude (r-parameter) of E-RO-SL1 social learning agents begins at 0.5 - mirroring the global risk tolerance threshold set by risk-att-init . This value represents moderate risk-seeking behavior in herd management decisions . During steps 40-70, the average steadily declines to 0.1 indicating increased risk aversion .  Household risk attitude becomes more pronounced when pasture biomass dips below critical thresholds, particularly after dry-season conditions compress resource availability . Risk-erasure strategy agents exhibit delayed responses, maintaining elevated deviation during years 20-40 . Learning-by-doing agents (E-LBD) begin modifying herds downward by year 30, showing smoother adaptation curves . The total seismic activity follows the same pattern, starting at 10,000 events and declining steadily .  Simulations show the interplay between adaptive decision-making and climate variability in long-term sustainability . For smallholder farmers, these trends underscore the importance of early risk mitigation and the value of learning strategies in maintaining pasture health under erratic environmental conditions . Decision-makers should prioritize initial herd limits and controlled grazing intensity to avoid sharp destocking declines, particularly in variable climates .  The proportion of households engaging in social learning begins at approximately 20.0% (20 households) at the simulation's onset . By step 100, the metric plateaus near 0.005, indicating stable inequality as behavioral strategies converge . This trajectory implies that integrating social learning frameworks reduces resource inequity, but climate variability temporarily amplifies disparities, necessitating adaptive risk management systems .  The initial population of E-RO agents converges rapidly after environmental stress begins affecting herding outcomes . A rapid spike then occurs at approximately 15% during the first several time steps, at which point the number of households remains relatively stable through the end of the simulation . These patterns underscore the importance of proactive herd management protocols .
