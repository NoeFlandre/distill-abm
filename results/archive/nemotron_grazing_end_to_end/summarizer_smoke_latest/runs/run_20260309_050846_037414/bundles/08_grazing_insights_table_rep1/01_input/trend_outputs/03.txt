The data reveals an initial phase of stable reserve biomass levels followed by sustained decline and eventual stabilization. In the early timesteps (1–5), reserve biomass remained constant at 90,000 units, indicating a steady-state equilibrium. A gradual decline began at timestep 6 (90,180), continuing through timesteps 7–10 (90,427.10–90,553.66), suggesting early signs of resource pressure. A more pronounced downward trend emerged at timestep 11 (90,105.97), reaching 89,800 units by timestep 12, with intermittent fluctuations between 88,500–90,100 units until timestep 23 (87,500.14). The sharpest drop occurred between timesteps 24–25 (86,015.98–85,570.52), followed by cyclical variability between 84,000 and 88,000 units. By timestep 30, biomass stabilized near 85,000 units, with minor oscillations persisting through the final timesteps (e.g., 84,000s at timesteps 40–50, 86,000s at timesteps 60–80).   Since the model employs homogeneous E-RO agents in a scenario with fixed climatic variability (global-rain? = True), the observed decline likely reflects overexploitation of pastures driven by intrinsic preferences for livestock stocking. The lack of strategy switching (SL2-strategy-switch? = False) and disabled social learning (E-RO-SL1 agents inactive due to homog-behav-types?) limited adaptive responses, leaving biomass vulnerable to climatic stressors. Stabilization suggests a degraded but sustained equilibrium, underscoring risks of non-adaptive strategies in heterogeneous socioecological systems. For decision makers, this highlights the importance of incorporating behavioral diversity and social learning mechanisms to enhance resilience against resource degradation and climatic variability.
