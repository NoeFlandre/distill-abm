This analysis examines trends in livestock stocking deviations from recommended levels across 100 simulated years, focusing on E-RO-type agents in homogeneous conditions under climatic variability. Initial timesteps (1–20) show minimal deviations (±0.1 livestock units) as agents establish baseline strategies. A gradual increase in variability emerges between timesteps 25–50, with deviations expanding to ±0.3–0.5 units, indicating early adaptation to climatic shocks (mean rainfall = 200, std = 100). Late-stage timesteps (75–100) reveal pronounced divergence, with some households exhibiting extreme deviations (up to ±0.6 livestock units), suggesting WERNing adjustments to environmental variability and individual risk preferences (risk-att-init = 0.5).   

Deviations correlate with pasture biomass thresholds, where satisficers (threshold = 0.0 livestock) occasionally reduce stocking during biomass stress, while risk-tolerant agents maintain higher thresholds. The absence of social learning mechanisms (homog-behav-types? = True) limits collaborative strategy alignment, exacerbating individual decisions that amplify ecological stress. Notably, risk aversion (guided by risk-att-init) drives some agents toward conservative stocking (-0.3–0.1), while others pursue aggressive thresholds (+0.2–0.5) despite degradation risks.   

Implications for policymakers include prioritizing risk-aware education and collective decision-making frameworks. By integrating localized climate monitoring with group learning incentives, communities could mitigate divergence and enhance adaptive capacity in property-based grazing systems.
