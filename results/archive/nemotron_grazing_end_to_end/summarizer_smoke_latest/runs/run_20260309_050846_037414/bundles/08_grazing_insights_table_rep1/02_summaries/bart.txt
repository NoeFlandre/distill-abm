 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  An analysis examines trends in livestock stocking deviations from recommended levels across 100 simulated years . The data reveals variability in household-level risk attitudes across simulations . Negative values (-0.04 to -0.11) appear in some simulations, indicating households highly inclined to take risks (low risk aversion) Negative risk attitudes in subsets of households amplify vulnerability to climatic shocks .  The data reveals a discernible pattern of biomass decline over time, reflecting the dynamics of the E-RO behavioral type under homogeneous conditions . Decline likely reflects overexploitation of pastures driven by intrinsic preferences for livestock stocking, authors say . Decision makers should note strategies lacking adaptive mechanisms risk unsustainable outcomes in variable environments .  The model simulates 100 years of data for 60 homogeneous E-RO households under climatic variability (200±100 annual rainfall) The Gini coefficient trends reveal three key patterns: early inequality acts as a baseline of moderate homogeneity . Mid-game variability shows sensitivity to climate-driven shocks, and late-game convergence emerges despite external variability .  The lack of social learning (social-susceptibility 0, SL2-strategy-switch? false) and absence of adaptive strategy switching prevent agents from mitigating depletion, aligning with the model’s focus on how homogeneous strategies lead to systemic collapse .
