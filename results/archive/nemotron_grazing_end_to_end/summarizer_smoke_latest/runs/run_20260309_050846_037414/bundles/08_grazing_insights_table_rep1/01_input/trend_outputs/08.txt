The data reveals patterns in the adoption of social learning (SL) across households in the simulated common-property grazing system. Initially, most simulations (rows 0–4) exhibit no SL activity, with counts remaining at zero throughout all time steps. However, in simulations where SL begins to occur (e.g., rows 5–29), adoption follows a gradual, stepwise increase over time. Early adoption (time steps 1–10) shows sparse participation, with counts typically below 5 households. By time steps 15–25, counts rise more noticeably, peaking in the range of 17–33 households. In later stages (time steps 26–29), adoption plateaus or stabilizes at relatively high levels, with some runs reaching up to 51 households. Notable steps include: 1) A critical growth phase between time steps 15–20, where counts consistently increase by ~1–2 households per step; 2) Rapid surges in specific runs (e.g., row 35 at time step 29 peaking at 51 households); and 3) variability in plateau levels, with some runs achieving higher saturation (e.g., 41–45 households) than others. These trends align with the model’s emphasis on gradual strategy diffusion and the role of social learning activation, though the low baseline prevalence of SL adoption (58% of runs show absolute zero counts) underscores its conditional dependence on unobserved parameters in this homogeneous setup. For decision-makers, this highlights the potential for slow but substantial shifts in resource management practices once SL pathways activate, though interventions may require time or bundled strategies to catalyze adoption.
