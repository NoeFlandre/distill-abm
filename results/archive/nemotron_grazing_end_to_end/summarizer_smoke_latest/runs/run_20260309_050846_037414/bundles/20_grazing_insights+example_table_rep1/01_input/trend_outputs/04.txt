The mean-green-biomass metric remains stable at approximately 45,000 across the first 14 years of the simulation, reflecting a baseline equilibrium under homogeneous E-RO conditions. A noticeable but minor increase emerges at Year 15, reaching 45,090, which persists until Year 24. From Year 25 onward, the metric exhibits pronounced annual variability, oscillating between highs of ~46,300 and lows of ~44,400. These fluctuations correlate with the model’s clonal rainfall variability (mean = 200, std = 100), which amplifies vegetation growth fluctuations and grazing pressure from static stocking decisions. The E-RO agents, lacking adaptive learning mechanisms, respond to climatic variability without adjusting herd sizes, leading to cyclical overexploitation-recovery patterns. Over the 100-year horizon, the system avoids collapse but shows increasing instability, with biomass variance widening annually (e.g., ±400–800 units beyond the 45,000 baseline). This suggests that homogeneous behavioral strategies under climatic uncertainty may erode resource resilience over time, highlighting the potential need for adaptive strategies (e.g., social learning) to mitigate destabilizing feedbacks in heterogeneous populations. The sustained oscillations indicate that E-RO agents maintain a fragile equilibrium, where individual rationality fails to align with long-term ecological sustainability when faced with environmental unpredictability.
