 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The model focuses on E-RO under homogeneous conditions with climatic variability . The average deviation from livestock numbers follows a three-phase pattern across time steps . Four households show transient spikes to ±0.2+ before reverting, possibly due to inter-temporal risk reassessment linked to rainfall variability (mean=200, std=100)  The average total livestock numbers across all households begin at 90.0 and undergo a gradual, sustained decline over time, with intermediate fluctuations . Over the 100-year horizon, the system avoids collapse but shows increasing instability, with biomass variance widening annually (e.g., ±400–800 units beyond the 45,000 baseline) The trend underscores the long-term consequences of unmitigated stocking demands in ecologically fragile settings .  The data reveals a consistent upward trend in cumulative livestock destocking over the 100-year simulation period, with average destocking values rising from near-zero at time step 0 to approximately 170-180 units by the final time step . These findings align with the model's hypothesis that social learning dynamics, even in homogeneous settings, eventually establish a population-dependent resilience through sustained knowledge sharing .
