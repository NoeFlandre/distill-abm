Trajectories of mean green biomass exhibit stochastic variability but share consistent phase transitions across simulations. Initial grazing pressure (Step 0-20) causes biomass to decline from baseline levels (~45,000) to minimum troughs below 40,000 for most agents (5th percentile), particularly in scenarios with risk-averse or non-learning behavioral types. Recovery begins ~Step 40-50 across all trajectories, with learning-capable agents (E-LBD, E-RO-SL1) demonstrating faster biomass rebound due to adaptive grazing management. By Step 85-100, biomass stabilizes between 58,000-63,000 for 90th percentile trajectories, while 10th percentile trajectories remain below ~55,000, revealing intrinsic system vulnerability to stochastic rainfall shocks (global-rain?=True, rain-std=100). The elevated initial herd size (90/capita) amplifies grazing pressure during biomass troughs, while rapid regeneration rates (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1) enable partial recovery through wet-dry cycles. Risk tolerance (risk-att-init=0.5) moderates herd adjustments during variability, but baseline E-RO agents without learning capacity show delayed responses to grazing impacts compared to risk-sensitive agents. This pattern underscores the tradeoff between short-term grazing pressure and long-term pasture resilience under climate variability.
