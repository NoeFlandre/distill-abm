The simulation shows a gradual increase in the number of households engaged in social learning (household-SL=1), starting at 20 and rising to ~35 by the final timestep (100 years). Early trends (steps 1–10) suggest slow adoption, likely due to initial learning curve challenges. Between steps 20–40, acceleration in adoption coincides with heightened herd management uncertainty driven by stochastic rainfall shocks (global-rain?=True). By steps 60–100, plateauing occurs as behavioral strategies stabilize, with risk-averse agents (E-RO-SL1) dominating adoption. The fixed initial conditions (start-on-homepatch?=True) constrained early learning diffusion, while homogeneous behavioral groups (homog-behav-types?=True) limited strategy diversity. The constant risk-att-init=0.5 and gr2=0.1 suggest that moderate risk tolerance and slow dry-season grass recovery reinforced reliance on social learning for inter-herd resource coordination. Decision-makers could interpret these trends as evidence that social learning becomes entrenched as a stable adaptive tool under long-term climate risk, but initial implementation barriers require incentives or network effects to overcome.
