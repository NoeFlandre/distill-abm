 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The model focuses on E-RO under homogeneous conditions with climatic variability, though parameters are ready for heterogeneous comparisons . The data reveals three distinct phases in mean reserve biomass across 100 model years . Decision-makers should prioritize enabling adaptive learning over rigid management to mitigate climate risks .  In a simulated grazing system with 60 homogeneous risk-taker (E-RO) households, the mean destock of livestock exhibits rapid initial reduction followed by stabilization . Destocking levels gradually decline from step 2 onward, stabilizing between 0.5-0.9 units removed per household by timestep 30 . The plateau at 48-50 units indicates resource-dependent equilibrium, where asymmetrical pasture recovery rates under low/high precipitation balance livestock survival needs .  Trend suggests social learning becomes entrenched in the population as households adapt to climatic variability (e.g., rainfall-driven vegetation changes) By maintenance step 100, social learning adoption stabilizes at 48 households, indicating a tipping point where learning-by-doing interactions reinforce collective knowledge sharing . The model suggests a threshold-driven behavior, where sustained low pasture biomass triggers a systemic shift toward aggressive destocking .
