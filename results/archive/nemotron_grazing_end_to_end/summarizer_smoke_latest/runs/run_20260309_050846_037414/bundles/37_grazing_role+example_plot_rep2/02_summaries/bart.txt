 The agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE model, focusing on simulating smallholder farmers' decision-making in pasture-based systems with learning capabilities . The original model was designed to analyze feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in common property grazing systems . This extension adds heterogeneity in behavioral strategies (e.g., learning-by-doing, social learning) and introduces risk sensitivity .  This plot illustrates the evolution of mean household initial risk attitudes among smallholder farmers participating in pasture-based systems . The vertical axis represents risk tolerance (ranging from risk-averse to risk-seeking), with values between 0.05 and 0.15 . The horizontal axis spans 100 timesteps, corresponding to model timestep in years .  The first measurable divergence occurs between steps 20-40 as stochastic rainfall introduces pasture productivity variability . This manifests as fluctuating risk attitudes clustered around 0.4-0.6, with learning agents demonstrating faster adaptation than static E-RO agents due to their behavioral strategies . Long-term stability (steps 70-100) shows converging trajectories toward equilibrium levels as learning mechanisms balance environmental constraints and social dynamics .  The agent-based simulation begins with all households starting at 90 livestock, reflecting the high initial herd size parameter (livestock-init=90) This triggers rapid destocking in the first 20 simulation steps as grazing pressure exceeds pasture carrying capacity . Social learning agents (E-RO-SL1) demonstrate partial recovery by step 60 through local knowledge adoption within the defined knowledge- radius=1.0 .  The Gini peaks at 0.07–0.09 by step 90, reflecting extreme inequality . This convergence reflects a systemic shift: adaptive agents stabilize herd health through improved pasture management . Non-learners (**E-RO**) experience compounding setbacks . The pattern aligns with models of adaptive grazing where fixed behavioral rules lead to herd collapse .  The absence of learning mechanisms prevents adaptation to long-term environmental stressors, resulting in cyclical overgrazing and recovery rather than strategic adjustment . Key trends include a lack of adaptive shifts in herd size during recurring degradation episodes and dependency on stochastic rainfall patterns . Low-density pasture regions likely act as ecological benchmarks, limiting herd viability in worse patches .
