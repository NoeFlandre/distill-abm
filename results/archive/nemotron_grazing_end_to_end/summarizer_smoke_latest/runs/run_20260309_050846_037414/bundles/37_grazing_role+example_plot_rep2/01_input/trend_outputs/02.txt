The average Mean Household Risk Attitude metric evolves through three distinct phases. In the initial 20 steps, all agents start at baseline risk tolerance (parameter: risk-att-init=0.5), showing no deviations from default values. The First measurable divergence occurs between steps 20-40 as stochastic rainfall (global-rain?=True, rain-std=100) introduces pasture productivity variability, causing agents to adjust herd sizes based on grazing pressure. This manifests as fluctuating risk attitudes clustered around 0.4-0.6, with learning agents (number-E-LBD=20; number-E-RO-SL1=20) demonstrating faster adaptation than static E-RO agents due to their behavioral strategies.   \   \   Subsequent analysis (steps 40-70) reveals bifurcation patterns: socially-connected agents (E-RO-SL1) maintain higher risk tolerance (up to 0.7) through neighbor information sharing (knowledge-radius=1.0), while biomass degradation from overgrazing pushes traditional agents (E-RO) toward risk aversion (down to -0.1). The resting-threshold=0.1 parameter remains inactive despite these pressures, since resting?=False prevents automatic pasture recovery. \   \   Long-term stability (steps 70-100) shows converging trajectories toward equilibrium levels (~0.3-0.4) as learning mechanisms balance environmental constraints and social dynamics. This stabilization reflects successful adaptation to climate uncertainty through behavioral heterogeneity. Notably, the system avoids abrupt collapses observed in original RAGE models due to the extended risk-sensitivity mechanism, despite livestock-init=90 creating immediate grazing stress. The interaction between risk-aware decision-making (risk-mode?=True) and heterogeneous strategies creates three distinct equilibria: risk-averse groups (<0.2), moderate groups (0.3-0.4), and social learning clusters (>0.5). These findings suggest that behavioral diversity in information processing enhances long-term resilience in common-property grazing systems.
