This report analyzes trends in the number of households adhering to non-learning behavioral strategies in a pasture-based grazing system simulation. The simulation begins with all 60 households starting as E-RO agents (non-learning), with high initial herd sizes (90 livestock) and stochastic rainfall variability (relative nsims variance from rainfall). The metric tracking non-learning households (E-RO) follows a pattern of rapid resource-driven shifts, reflecting the interplay between environmental conditions and fixed behavioral rules.   

In early time steps, E-RO households maintain relatively stable herd sizes, ranging between 24–28 livestock during optimal grazing periods. However, during resource stressful periods, such as when rainfall deviations from mean occur, pastures experience !, triggering biomass depletion. For example, by time step 40, E-RO households show a recurrence of intensity drops, with herd numbers declining to 20–22 due to pasture degradation. These declines correlate with increased variability in rainfall-triggered biomass recovery rates, as wet seasons (gr rate 0.5) allow slower recovery compared to dry seasons (gr2=0.1).   

Within years 80–100, the number of non-learning households stabilizes around 23–25 livestock, indicating persistent oscillatory grazing cycles. This equilibrium likely reflects a tension between risk tolerance (risk-att-init=0.5) and stochastic biomass recovery, enabling intermittent pasture recovery despite static grazing rules. The absence of learning mechanisms prevents adaptation to long-term environmental stressors, resulting in cyclical overgrazing and recovery rather than strategic adjustment.   

Key trends include a lack of adaptive shifts in herd size during recurring degradation episodes and a dependency on stochastic rainfall patterns to prevent catastrophic failure. Low-density pasture regions likely act as ecological benchmarks, limiting herd viability in worse patches. The extended model’s risk sensitivity parameter amplifies intra-population variability during recovery phase experiments, though all E-RO agents maintain fundamentally similar trajectories due to homogeneous behavioral rules.
