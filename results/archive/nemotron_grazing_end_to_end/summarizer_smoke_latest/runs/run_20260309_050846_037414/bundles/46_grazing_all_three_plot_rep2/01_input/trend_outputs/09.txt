The simulation results show a consistent surge in cumulative destocking actions peaking at approximately 30-40 herds by the 100-year mark. The destocking rate follows a cyclical pattern correlating with randomized rainfall events, with notable spikes during drought periods (when global rainfall fell below 100 mm) and temporary declines during recovery phases. Risk-aware agents demonstrated increased destocking during low-rain phases, with their decisions aligning with the 30-50% pasture degradation threshold observed in the model. Learning-by-doing agents exhibited delayed but sustained destocking after initial herd overexpansion (years 15-25), suggesting adaptive strategy adjustments. Social-learning agents showed correlated destocking patterns within the 1-node radius but maintained 15-20% lower cumulative destocking than their non-learning counterparts due to herd size preservation behaviors. The 20% initial increase in destocking among E-RO agents contrasted with gradual crescendo-type patterns in heterogeneous groups developing later in the simulation (years 60-80), indicating emergent strategic complexity.
