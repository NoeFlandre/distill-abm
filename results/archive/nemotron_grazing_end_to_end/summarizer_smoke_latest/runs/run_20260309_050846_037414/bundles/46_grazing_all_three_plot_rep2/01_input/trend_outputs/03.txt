The total reserve biomass decreases to a basal value around the 10'000 step. These values slightly increase after this point, reaching a peak at about 60 to 70 days before declining. The reservoir biomass stabilizes then for the remainder of the timesteps, between 114'000 and 128'000 units. Convergence of the individual lines in the graph at their lower values at the 10'000 mark indicates that, for most strategies, grazing pressure across the different households equalizes the limited forage available. The eventual peak at approximately 60 to 70 steps may signify partial pasture recovery in areas that were previously under heavy grazing pressure, hence reproducing the cyclic nature of managed pastures. Last but not least, the general leveling in trends after the peak implies long-term adaptive grazing strategies that maintain biomass by matching herd sizes with pasture growth, irreducible to otherwise INNIVUATE patterns of use. This describes a system where resource use was balanced and social learning of behavioural strategies likely impacted the way grazing landscapes would have shaped their landscapes over time.
