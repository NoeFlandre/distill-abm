 The agent-based model extends the original RAGE model to study how behavioral types and learning mechanisms influence social-ecological dynamics in a common property grazing system . The context involves 60 households managing pastures on private property, with agents deciding how many livestock to stock annually . The model evaluates long-term outcomes like pasture biomass, livestock survival, and household resilience .  The model focuses on E-RO under homogeneous conditions with climatic variability . Social learning (E-RO-SL1) is inactive due to homog-behav-types?. Strategy switching (SL2-strategy-switch?) is disabled, preventing agents from adopting other types . The data reveals a structured pattern in livestock stocking deviations across household groups .  The data demonstrates a consistent decline in average reserve biomass over the 29-year simulation period . Initial stability is observed from timesteps 0–2 (biomass: 90000–90427), indicating baseline equilibrium under homogeneous E-RO behavioral strategies . The absence of social learning mechanisms (E-RO-SL1) and strategy diversity (homog-behav-types?) accelerates resource exhaustion .  The data shows a consistent positive trend in the mean destocking values across households over the 100-year simulation period . E-RO's lack of social learning or behavioral flexibility limits adaptation to environmental cycles, leading to chronic underperformance . The findings highlight risks of collective overexploitation in competitive, individualized systems amid climatic volatility .  The trajectory suggests that social learning adoption, even in a homogeneous system dominated by E-RO agents (who lack formal social learning mechanisms per model parameters), accelerates over time . For decision makers, this highlights that homogeneous adaptive strategies (E-RO) can yield self-organized social learning patterns as a response to environmental uncertainty .
