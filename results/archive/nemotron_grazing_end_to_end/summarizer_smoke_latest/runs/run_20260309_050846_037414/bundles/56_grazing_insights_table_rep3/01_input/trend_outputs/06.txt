The data shows a consistent positive trend in the mean destocking values across households over the 100-year simulation period. Destocking begins at baseline levels (0.0) at timestep 0 and gradually increases, reaching a peak of 7.55 at timestep 29 before declining slightly to 0.6 by timestep 100. This pattern suggests that agents initially adopt conservative stocking strategies, likely due to risk aversion (risk-att-init = 0.5) and intrinsic preferences for resource conservation (intrinsic-preference = 0). Climatic variability, represented by rainfall standard deviation (rain-std = 100), appears to drive intermittent spikes in destocking, particularly noticeable during drought periods (e.g., timesteps 15–20 where values exceed 6.0), reflecting resource scarcity from unfavorable precipitation. The subsequent gradual decline after timestep 29 may indicate adaptive adjustments by agents, possibly through learning-by-doing mechanisms, though the absence of social learning (social-susceptibility = 0) and disabled strategy switching (SL2-strategy-switch? = False) limits system-wide behavioral shifts. The final stabilized values (~0.4–0.6) suggest a quasi-equilibrium state where destocking stabilizes despite persistent climatic stress, highlighting the resilience of risk-averse strategies under homogeneous conditions. Decision-makers should consider that E-RO agents alone may struggle to adapt to chronic resource unpredictability without complementary learning or behavioral diversification policies.
