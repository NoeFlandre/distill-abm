The data reveals a consistent decline in average livestock numbers across households over 100 timesteps, with fluctuations reflecting climatic variability. Initially, all households begin with 90 livestock units. By timestep 1-5, values drop to ~84-85, indicating early resource stress. Gradual declines persist: by timesteps 10-15 (~78-80), 20-25 (~73-75), and 30-35 (~70-71), highlighting sustained grazing pressure despite rainfall variability. By timesteps 50-55 (~60-61), livestock levels halt their decline, suggesting partial equilibrium under persistent environmental stress. The final timesteps (61+) show stabilization at ~40-41 livestock units, implying long-term degradation.  

This trajectory reflects E-RO agents' static decision-making under climate shocks. During high-rainfall periods (rainfall >200 units), households likely overstock due to optimistic assessments of vegetation capacity (as E-RO lacks learning mechanisms), followed by destocking when low rainfall reduces biomass. The absence of adaptive behaviors (e.g., resting, strategic restocking) exacerbates overgrazing during droughts. The stabilization at ~40-41 suggests resilience thresholds: households partially adjust by reducing herd sizes but cannot recover to baseline levels.  

For decision-makers, this underscores risks of rigid strategies in variable climates. E-RO's lack of social learning or behavioral flexibility limits adaptation to environmental cycles, leading to chronic underperformance. Implementing dynamic learning mechanisms (e.g., E-LBD or E-RO-SL1) could enhance resource management by enabling households to adjust stocking rates in response to peer behaviors or historical climate patterns.
