The average reserve biomass across all pasture patches begins the simulation at approximately 90,000 units and initially declines to around 82,500 units by the middle timesteps, indicating early-stage overgrazing and degradation due to high herd sizes (90 head/agent) and fixed grazing rules (E-RO behavioral-type dominance). Critical thresholds occur at approximately 10–15 timesteps, where biomass drops below 90,000, marking ecological stress from sustained livestock pressure. A second critical threshold emerges at 50–60 timesteps, where biomass fluctuations increase in variance—correlating with activated risk-aware decision-making (risk-parameter=0.5) and stochastic rainfall events disrupting recovery. Recovery patterns show nonlinearity: some runs rebound to ~96,000 biomass by 70–80 timesteps due to adaptive strategy shifts among E-LBD agents, while others degrade further below 88,000, likely from cumulative stochastic rainfall losses or delayed behavioral adjustments. By 90–100 timesteps, two divergent trajectories persist—stable levels near 100,000 in runs with successful learning-behavioral alignment and severe deficits below 92,000 in runs dominated by unadapted grazing persistence. The model’s stochastic rainfall (std=100) amplifies variability post-50 timesteps, confirming climatic uncertainty’s destabilizing role in early-warning systems. Critical agent-strategy interactions emerge: E-LBD agents with higher risk-tolerance (r=0.5) show moderate recovery via recalibrated herd sizing, whereas E-RO agents’ static approaching exacerbates legacy degradation in later timesteps.
