The total number of livestock, including both healthy and destocked animals across all households, remains constant at 90 for the first 10 timesteps, reflecting a stable initial equilibrium. After timestep 10, livestock numbers decline gradually but consistently, dropping to approximately 65 by timestep 20 and further declining to 40–45 livestock units by timestep 29. The trajectory exhibits fluctuations, particularly pronounced in the latter half of the simulation, which align with the stochastic climate shocks introduced by high rainfall variability (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100). These climatic events amplify pressures on pasture resources, exacerbating biomass depletion under sustained grazing pressure. Early in the simulation, the decline is slower, likely due to initial resource availability, but the rate of decline accelerates as pasture productivity diminishes over time. By the final timestep, the system stabilizes at a lower equilibrium, suggesting resource limitations and inadequate adaptive capacity among agents to counteract degradation. The presence of learning-capable agents (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1, totaling 40% of the population) does not alter the overarching trend, indicating that behavioral heterogeneity alone cannot offset the cumulative effects of overgrazing and environmental stochasticity. The extended simulation horizon highlights systemic trade-offs between pastoral livelihoods and ecological sustainability in the absence of restorative pasture management.
