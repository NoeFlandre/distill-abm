This decline is sharp and consistent, with all households participating in the destocking response. By timestep 100, mean destocking stabilizes at minimal levels (~0.1–0.2 livestock units per household), with negligible temporal fluctuations.

This trajectory aligns with the model’s stochastic climate shocks (**global-rain? = True**) and high initial herd sizes (**livestock-init = 90**), which likely drove immediate resource depletion. The rapid destocking suggests alarming vulnerability to climate stressors, exceeding the adaptive capacity of both climate-resilient (**risk-mode? = True**) and learning strategies. The subsequent stabilization reflects a transition to a quasi-stable equilibrium, mediated by risk-aware decision-making (**risk-att-init = 0.5**) and learning mechanisms (**number-E-LBD = 20**, **number-E-RO-SL1 = 20**). Despite the model’s disabled social learning (**social-susceptibility = 0**, **SL2-strategy-switch? = False**), individual learning and risk tolerance parameters sufficed to dampen grazing pressure over time. The absence of pasture rest mechanisms (**resting? = False**) and fixed initial assignment to private patches (**start-on-homepatch? = True**) likely constrained adaptive flexibility, yet the persistence of normalized grazing pressure by timestep 100 suggests partial recovery.

The simulated dynamics indicate that under these settings—combining stochastic climate stressors, high initial herds, and risk-sensitive learning—the system rapidly transitions to a low-destocking equilibrium. The extension model’s focus on adaptive management and strategy diffusion is supported by the observed stabilization, though the lack of social learning mechanisms may limit long-term resilience compared to more interconnected behavioral strategies.
