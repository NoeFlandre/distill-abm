The average reserve biomass begins around 90,000 units and experiences a gradual decline over time, with fluctuations driven by stochastic rainfall variability. In the initial time steps, biomass remains relatively stable but shows variability across runs due to random rainfall events (mean=200, std=100). By mid-simulation (~time steps 15–20), biomass levels in most runs decrease by ~10–15%, reflecting sustained grazing pressure from high initial herd sizes (90 livestock per household) and limited adaptive adjustments. In some runs, poor rainfall exacerbates declines, pushing biomass below 85,000 units, while others stabilize near 90,000 due to favorable precipitation. The absence of social learning (social-susceptibility=0.0) and individual learning (intrinsic-preference=0.0) prevents agents from modifying herding strategies in response to environmental feedback, leading to persistent grazing-related degradation. By the final time steps (~29), biomass stabilizes at ~85,000–95,000 units, with runs exhibiting divergent trajectories depending on cumulative rainfall impacts. This pattern underscores the system’s sensitivity to climatic variability and the critical role of adaptive decision-making in maintaining grazing resilience.
