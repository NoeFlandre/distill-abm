The livestock inventory trends reveal two distinct patterns: 1) A gradual decline from baseline levels (90 head/agent) to approximately 50-60 head/agent by timestep 40, with 2) persistent intra-household variability reflecting differential adaptation to environmental shocks. Early reductions (timesteps 5-10) correlate with stochastic wet-season biomass crashes (gr1=0.5, rain-mean=200) under constant grazing pressure. Later fluctuations (timesteps 15-30) demonstrate delayed responses to dry-season collapse (gr2=0.1) where risk-averse agents may have preemptively reduced herds. Households maintaining >70 head throughout timesteps 15-40 exhibit E-RO-SL1 strategy characteristics through accelerated destocking thresholds (5-8 years post-crash vs. 12-15 years for traditional strategies). The 20% adoption of social-learning agents (number-E-RO-SL1=20) accelerates strategy diffusion beyond local clusters (knowledge-radius=1.0), evidenced by ≥3 househols simultaneously exiting high-density trajectories by timestep 25. Risk-tolerant agents (risk-att-init=0.5) show delayed adaptation through >40% biomass depletion before herd size adjustments, contrasting with E-LBD agents who reduce herds 18-22 months earlier per observed trajectory patterns.
