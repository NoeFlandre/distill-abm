The data reveals a degradation of livestock-stocking levels over 100 timesteps, with critical thresholds emerging at specific timepoints. Initially (timesteps 1-20), all households maintain baseline stocking levels at 90 livestock units, reflecting equilibrium conditions before adverse dynamics set in.  At timestep 21 (year 21), stocking levels decline sharply to 84.0 units, marking the onset of systemic collapse. This initial drop exceeds 6% of initial capacity, exceeding environmental carrying capacity given the high livestock-init parameter (90 units) and permanently disabled restoration mechanisms (resting?=False). 

Clustered declines below 80 units occur in two distinct phases: 1) A rapid deterioration phase between timesteps 25-30 (78.83-80.42 units), driven by compounding biomass depletion from sustained grazing pressure under risk-sensitive decision-making (risk-mode?=True). 2) A protracted degradation phase from timesteps 40-60, where averages dip below 62 units, with failure points clustered between 55-58 units. The nadir of 51.7 units at timestep 53 coincides with highest cumulative rainfall variability (rain-std=100), demonstrating environment-behavior decoupling under stress.  During the terminal phase (timesteps 80-100), averages stabilize around 48-49 units, suggesting a carrying capacity trap below which adaptation mechanisms fail.

The lack of pasture rest mechanisms (resting?=False) critically enables this collapse, as passive waiting for natural recovery is impossible. The presence of social learners (E-RO-SL1) and experiential agents (E-LBD) provides insufficient resilience given the initial herd size-to-pasture ratio. This aligns with findings that learning strategies require supplementary institutional frameworks to counteract path-dependent ecosystem degradation in smallholder systems.
