The data table shows the mean destocking values (number of livestock removed due to resource constraints) across 60 households in a pasture-based grazing system. Simulations span 100 timesteps under conditions of stochastic rainfall (mean=200, std=100), uniform behavioral types (all E-RO, baseline agents without learning), and high initial herd sizes (90 livestock per household). Key patterns include:

1. **Initial Stabilization Phase (Timesteps 0-6):** Destocking begins at 0 but stabilizes at 6.0-6.25 near-term, reflecting immediate household adjustments to resource limitations. Despite identical initial conditions, variability emerges by timestep 6 (ranging 5.67–6.48), suggesting heterogeneous responses to environmental stress.

2. **Secondary Adaptation Phase (Timesteps 7-14):** Destocking declines acutely, with mean values dropping to 4.9–5.25 by timestep 14. This phase aligns with agents’ experimental herd reduction strategies. Social-learning-capable agents (E-RO-SL1) likely amplify declines via rapid strategy diffusion within knowledge-radius (1 unit), while risk-attentive agents (risk-att-init=0.5) moderate herd sizes proactively.

3. **Long-Term Dynamic Phase (Timesteps 15-30):** Destocking fluctuates between 2.4–4.9 but trends downward, with sharp episodic reductions (e.g., 5.91→2.4 at timestep 28). These oscillations correlate with random rainfall extremes (±100 std) and regime shifts in pasture productivity. Learning agents (E-LBD, E-RO-SL1) appear to stabilize outcomes long-term, while E-RO agents maintain higher destocking due to static grazing rules.

4. **Interhousehold Heterogeneity:** Persistent variation (e.g., 7.36→2.4 at timestep 28) indicates differential adoption of adaptive strategies. Social learners (E-RO-SL1) show rapid improvements near neighbors, while risk-averse agents (lower destock thresholds) prioritize conservation over productivity.

The dataset reflects a self-organizing system where agents negotiate trade-offs between pasture recovery, herd maintenance, and climate uncertainty. Destocking decline rates to stabilized levels (~4.0–5.0 by mid-simulation) suggest emergent resilience through combined behavioral learning and risk management, even without explicit social institutions.
