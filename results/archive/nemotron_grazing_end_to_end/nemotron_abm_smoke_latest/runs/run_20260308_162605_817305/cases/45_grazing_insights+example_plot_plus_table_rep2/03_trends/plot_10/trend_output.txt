The count of non-learning (E-RO) households exhibits significant variability in the initial timesteps, followed by stabilization. In most simulation runs, E-RO households hover around a steady 20–25 households after ~20 timesteps. Some runs show sharp fluctuations: early-phase increases to 30–40 households in certain simulations are followed by declines, while others drop as low as 0–10 households. These short-term deviations likely reflect interactions with climate variability (fixed rainfall with sd=100) and household decisions under risk-averse constraints (risk-att-init=0.5). Long-term trends reveal equilibrium dynamics, with no significant drift above ~25 households or below ~15 households by timestep 60. Notably, the absence of strategy switching (SL2-strategy-switch?=False) and fixed behavioral type homogeneity (homog-behav-types?=True) explain why E-RO remains dominant despite climate shocks. This stability suggests that under homogeneous conditions, E-RO strategies demonstrate adaptive resilience to environmental perturbations, maintaining a baseline number of households via decision-making thresholds (e.g., resting-threshold=0.1). The data also hints at potential tipping points, as some runs exhibit rapid declines before stabilizing, possibly due to resource depletion or constrained risk management. For decision-makers, this underscores the importance of equilibrium planning in grazing systems, where predictable outcomes emerge despite variable inputs.
