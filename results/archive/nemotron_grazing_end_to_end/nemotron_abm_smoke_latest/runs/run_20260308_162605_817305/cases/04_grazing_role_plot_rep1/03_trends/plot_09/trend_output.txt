Trends in the average cumulative destocked livestock households reveal distinct behavioral trajectories influenced by adaptive strategies, climate variability, and risk sensitivity. Initial destocking rates (t ≈ 10–20) are relatively low across all behavioral types, suggesting initial equilibrium as households adjust to baseline resource availability. By mid-simulations (t ≈ 30–40), destocking accelerates under global rainfall variability (std=100), with risk-tolerant E-RO agents (risk-att-init=0.5) experiencing sharper declines due to herd size adjustments during simulated droughts. E-RO-SL1 agents demonstrate moderate destocking, leveraging social learning (knowledge-radius=1.0) to mitigate resource depletion. E-LBD agents initially underperform compared to E-RO-SL1, reflecting slower adaptation through individual experience. By t ≈ 60–70, destocking trajectories diverge significantly: E-RO-SL1 stabilizes herd sizes through social strategy diffusion, while E-LBD exhibits gradual improvement as learning-by-doing accumulates. Risk-averse agents (not explicitly quantified here but inferred via variable risk attitudes) conclude with minimal destocking, reflecting conservative resource management. The extinction parameter (E-RO-SL1=20) interacts with pasture regeneration rates (gr1=0.5 for wet seasons, gr2=0.1 for dry), limiting long-term herd recovery during prolonged droughts. Structural parameters like starting herd size (livestock-init=90) and grazing pressure intensity (Rmax=0.5) constrain destocking responses, particularly for non-adaptive strategies. No evidence of satisficing behavior (threshold=0.0) or tradition-driven decisions (TRAD=0) is observed, consistent with model design prioritizing proactive learning and risk-aware adaptation. Social learning (E-RO-SL1) emerges as pivotal in balancing destocking with ecosystem resilience, aligning with the model’s focus on collaborative strategy propagation in heterogeneous populations under climate uncertainty.
