Summary of Trends:
In a simulated grazing system with 60 homogeneous risk-taker (E-RO) households, the mean destock of livestock exhibits rapid initial reduction followed by stabilization. Across all 30 simulation runs (columns 0-29), households consistently initiate mass destocking at timestep 0 and 1, heating to mean values around 6.0-7.0 livestock units removed. This immediate destocking reflects overwhelmingly unfavorable conditions at baseline (step 0: satisficing-threshold unset, resting? disabled, risk-att-init=0.5) where immediate overstocking triggers resource depletion. Destocking levels gradually decline from step 2 onward, stabilizing between 0.5-0.9 units removed per household by timestep 30.

The consistent stabilization pattern across simulations suggests agents converge to risk-averse equilibrium thresholds, where destocking reflects adaptive depletion to avoid pasture collapse. Notably, IDs 0-29 show negligible variability (0.01-0.2 units range), despite differing initial conditions - this implies strong deterministic drivers in the system (rainfall mean=200, std=100, livestock-init=90). Destocking amplitude aligns with risk-averse parameters (risk-att-init=0.5, resting-threshold=0.1), where households err on the side of caution.

For decision makers, this highlights two key insights: 1) Risk-taker strategies (E-RO) enable rapid resource adjustment to prevent overgrazing, but yield suboptimal herd sizes (0.5-0.8 removed post-stabilization) compared to more aggressive strategies in homogeneous settings. 2) Climatic variability (rain-std=100) and risk-sensitivity create uniform pathways to sustainable states - suggesting risk-oriented policies may buffer population-wide crisis events despite reduced productivity.
