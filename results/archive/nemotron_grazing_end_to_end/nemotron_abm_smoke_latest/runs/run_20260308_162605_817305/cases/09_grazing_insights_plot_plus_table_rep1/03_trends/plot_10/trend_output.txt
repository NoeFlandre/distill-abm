The data indicates that in nearly all simulation trials, the proportion of non-learning E-RO households remains stable at its initial value of 20 households for the first 29 steps. Starting at step 30 (year 30), a subset of trials showed gradual declines in E-RO households, with counts dropping to as low as 8-12 in certain runs. These declines suggest a shift toward learning-based strategies over time, though E-RO households never fully disappeared. The stability in most trials implies that homogeneous behavioral settings (forcing all agents to E-RO) prevent significant strategy diversification, while the declines in a minority of trials hint at stochastic effects or interactions with climatic variability (fixed rainfall with high variability). These trends underscore the challenge of sustaining non-learning strategies in dynamic environments, though most simulations show resilience due to the controlled homogeneity assumption. For policymakers, this implies that in tightly regulated systems, traditional practices may persist, but in more open or variable contexts, adaptive strategies could dominate long-term outcomes.
