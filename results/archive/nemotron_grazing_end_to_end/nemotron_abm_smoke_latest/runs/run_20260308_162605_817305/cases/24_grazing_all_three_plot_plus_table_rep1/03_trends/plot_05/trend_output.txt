The total livestock and destocked households metric begins at 90 across all 60 households and experiences a sharp decline over the first 20 timesteps, dropping to approximately 40–50 by step 30. This initial collapse aligns with high grazing pressure (livestock-init=90), overwhelming pasture regeneration capacity (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1). The rapid depletion likely stemmed from inefficient consumption rates (Rmax=0.5) and stochastic rainfall shocks (global-rain?=True, rain-std=100), which destabilized pasture biomass. From steps 30–100, the metric gradually rises to stabilize between 50–62 across households. This recovery phase reflects adaptive adjustments: risk-sensitive agents (risk-att-init=0.5) reduced herd sizes in response to declines, while pasture regeneration (matched by biomass recovery rates) offset grazing losses. The homogeneous trajectory across all simulations (homog-behav-types?=True) suggests that in this configuration, universal rules and consensus-driven adaptation drove system-level recovery. Long-term stabilization at ~50–62 indicates a new equilibrium where grazing pressure aligns with sustainable biomass thresholds, though below the initial peak.  

   Imputations: This pattern highlights the risks of overgrazing at extreme initial herd sizes and underscores the importance of adaptive herding practices. For decision-makers, the model demonstrates that preemptive destocking during biomass declines—even without explicit learning capabilities—can stabilize systems.
