The number of households consistently using the E-RO strategy (non-learning agents) declines steadily across timesteps, starting at approximately 20 agents in most runs and reaching near-zero values by the final timesteps. Initial variability in counts (ranging from 20 to 39 agents) reflects sensitivity to stochastic climatic shocks (fixed at average=200, std=100), which disproportionately reduce E-RO households due to their inability to adapt to changing pasture conditions. By the simulation’s end, only 1–4 E-RO households remain in each run, with most runs showing complete extinction of the strategy by timestep 100. This long-term decline (spanning 100 years) suggests that E-RO agents face persistent pressure to abandon inflexible grazing decisions under variable environmental conditions. Fluctuations in early declines (e.g., temporary increases to 39 agents in some runs) align with stochastic events that initially heighten stress on pastures, accelerating the transition to learning-based strategies. The near-zero persistence of E-RO households by the final timesteps underscores the strategic disadvantage of non-adaptive systems in environments with learning-capable agents and climate variability. These trends highlight the importance of incorporating adaptive decision-making (e.g., learning-by-doing or social learning) for long-term resilience in grazing systems under uncertainty.
