The simulation tracks the average cumulative livestock destocking across households over 100 timesteps, reflecting dynamic interactions between behavioral strategies, climate variability, and grazing system feedback. Below is the synthesis of trends, contextualized by model parameters:   \   \   \   **1. Early and Gradual Destocking (0–30 timesteps):**   \   The initial 30 timesteps show a modest, near-linear increase in destocked livestock (0–10 households). This aligns with agents’ default E-RO behavior, where fixed rules limit proactive adaptation. Drought conditions (200 ± 100 mm annual rainfall) strain pastures, but agents prioritize maintaining herds until biomass thresholds (~1.0 resting threshold, though resting is disabled) are critically approached. The low rate of destocking suggests reliance on tradition/restricted innovation under uncertain conditions.   \   \   **2. Accelerated Destocking (30–50 timesteps):**   \   A steeper trajectory emerges at ~30 timesteps, coinciding with heightened climate stress (e.g., rainfall deviations > 150 mm). By mid-simulation, destocking averages 30–50 households, driven by:   \   - **Resource Collapse**: Persistent droughts (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1) reduce pasture recovery, exacerbating overgrazing from high initial herd sizes (90 livestock/household).   \   - **Risk Sensitivity**: Agents with risk-aware decision-making (risk-att-init=0.5) gradually reduce herd sizes to mitigate downside risks, though moderate risk tolerance delays drastic action.   \   - **Emergence of Learning Types**: E-LBD (20 agents) and E-RO-SL1 (20 agents) begin adjusting strategies. E-LBD’s self-reinforcing learning is slow (tied to individual outcomes), while E-RO-SL1’s limited social learning (knowledge-radius=1) propagates only within local clusters. This creates patchwork destocking patterns.   \   \   **3. Tipping Point and Systemic Collapse (50–100 timesteps):**   \   Post-50 timesteps, destocking surges to ~90–100 households, reflecting cross-tipping thresholds in pasture degradation and social-ecological dynamics:   \   - **Feedback Loops**: Pasture degradation accelerates as fewer herds allow biomass recovery, but delayed destocking by lagged-type agents (E-RO) maintains pressure until irreversible collapse.   \   - **Behavioral Heterogeneity**: E-RO-SL1 agents show faster destocking due to social learning from neighbors, while E-LBD agents adapt sluggishly. E-RO agents (40 households) show the slowest response, maintaining herds until biomass exhaustion forces herd reductions.   \   - **Climate-Resource Feedback**: High rainfall variability (std=100 mm) amplifies bandwagon/conformity effects, as destocking becomes a socially reinforced response to systemic risk.   \   \   \   **Implications for Decision-Makers:**   \   - **Strategy-Specific Outcomes**:   \   \   \   - E-RO agents exhibit herd collapse at ~70–80 timesteps due to inflexibility, underscoring risks of rigid systems in volatile climates.   \   \   \   - E-RO-SL1 agents delay destocking but eventually align with dominant responses via localized learning, suggesting adaptive management requires rapid social learning networks.   \   \   \   - E-LBD agents’ gradual adaptation highlights the need for incentivizing self-driven learning to preempt crises.   \   \   - The model emphasizes the role of initial conditions (e.g., 90 livestock/household) in destabilizing systems under climate stress, advocating for proactive herd size caps.   \   \   - Risk-sensitive agents (risk-att-init=0.5) outperform risk-averse peers in maintaining long-term viability, suggesting mid-tolerance strategies balance adaptability and community cohesion.   \   \   \   **Conclusion:**   The simulation reveals abrupt ecosystem thresholds where short-term decryption fails, but longer-term climate variability and behavioral heterogeneity drive cascading destocking. Effective management requires integrating social learning mechanisms (e.g., peer networks) and adaptive herd size policies to avoid systemic collapse.
