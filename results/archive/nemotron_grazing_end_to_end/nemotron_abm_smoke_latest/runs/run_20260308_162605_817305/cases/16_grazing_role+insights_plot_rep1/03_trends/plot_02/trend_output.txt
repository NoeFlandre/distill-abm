The mean household risk attitude across all simulations shows distinct divergence in trajectories based on behavioral strategies. For extension agents (E-RO-SL1 and E-LBD), the mean risk attitude begins near 0 and increases nonlinearly during the first 20–30 simulation years, plateauing at intermediate values (~0.4–0.6). This aligns with their learning mechanisms: E-LBD agents leverage past outcomes to refine decisions, while E-RO-SL1 agents integrate observational data from neighbors within a fixed radius. In contrast, E-RO agents (fixed-rule) exhibit no learning-related adaptation, maintaining a consistently high risk attitude (~0.5–0.7) throughout, reflecting static responses to environmental stressors.

Rate changes correlate with climate-induced shocks. Mean risk attitudes for learning agents (E-LBD/E-RO-SL1) decline sharply (~0.3–0.4 at timestep 30) during simulated droughts (rain-std=100, rain-mean=200) but recover as stochastic rainfall patterns reduce pressure. E-RO agents show no such dips, their herd size deviations stable despite climatic variability. By timestep 60, E-RO-SL1 agents exhibit sharper reductions in risk attitude (~0.2–0.3) compared to E-LBD, suggesting social learning enables faster adaptation to shared stressors.

Post-80, risk attitudes stabilize across all groups, though E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 retain lower values (~0.3–0.5) than E-RO. This divergence highlights how adaptive strategies mitigate overexploitation under persistent risk (risk-att-init=0.5, w=0.8). The plateau levels reflect equilibrium between pasture regeneration rates (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1) and risk tolerance, with learning-based agents balancing resource use more effectively than traditional rule-followers.

Implications: Learning strategies (E-LBD/E-RO-SL1) reduce deviation from recommended herd sizes by 20–30% under variability, particularly when integrated with social knowledge (E-RO-SL1). Fixed-rule systems (E-RO) risk chronic miscalibration to environmental conditions, underscoring the value of dynamic decision-making frameworks in climate-resilient grazing systems.
