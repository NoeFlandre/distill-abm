The Gini coefficients for healthy livestock distribution follow a distinct pattern across 100 timesteps. Early dynamics show remarkable homogeneity in the first 15-20 time steps, with Gini values clustered around 2 × 10⁻⁴, suggesting uniform resource access among all households. This initial equal distribution aligns with the model's starting conditions where households begin with identical herd sizes (livestock-init = 90) and controlled grazing levels (w = 0.8).

At approximately timestep 20, a divergence emerges as the Gini coefficient rises sharply through 40-50 steps, reaching intermediate levels (0.002–0.005) by timestep 60. This phase likely reflects the accumulation of feedbacks from risk-sensitive decision-making (risk-att-init = 0.5, enabling uncertainty management) combined with active learning strategies among E-LBD (learning-by-doing) and E-RO-SL1 agents. The absence of social learning (social-susceptibility = 0) may limit knowledge sharing between behavioral types, exacerbating performance disparities.

The most pronounced inequality appears in the final timesteps (e.g., 70–100), where Gini coefficients reach 0.009–0.015, indicative of entrenched disparity. By this stage, households employing learning strategies (particularly E-LBD agents, who adjust herd sizes based on historical outcomes) likely outperform fixed-rule agents (E-RO), amplifying resource disparities. Simultaneously, global rainfall variability (rain-std = 100) and absent pasture rest (resting? = False) would intensify competition for limited biomass regeneration capacity, further concentrating resources among adaptable agents.

The late-time presence of Gini values exceeding 0.01 suggests persistent marginalization of non-learning households, consistent with the model's emphasis on long-term social-ecological feedbacks. However, the absence of critical thresholds in this metric makes it challenging to distinguish between temporal stochastic fluctuations and structural inequality drivers. The interplay between risk tolerance, learning mechanisms, and environmental stochasticity appears to drive cumulative inequality rather than transient market-like equilibria.
