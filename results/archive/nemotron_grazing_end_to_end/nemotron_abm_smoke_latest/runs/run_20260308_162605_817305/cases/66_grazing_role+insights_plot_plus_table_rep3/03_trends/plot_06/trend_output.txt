### Trends in Destocking Dynamics  

The simulation data reveals a clear pattern of initial instability followed by gradual stabilization in destocking behavior across households. Key observations include:

1. **Initial High Destocking (Steps 0-30):**
   - All households began with high destocking levels (5.7-7.5 livestock units), reflecting the strain of large initial herds (90 head per household) under variable climate conditions (rain-std=100). This aligns with the model's design to stress-test grazing systems through stochastic rainfall shocks.
   - Sharp declines occurred in the first 10-15 steps, as risk-sensitive agents (risk-parameter=0.5) reduced herd sizes in response to early drought events during the wet season.

2. **Learning-Driven Stabilization (Steps 30-60):**
   - Destocking rates decelerated as learning agents (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1) adjusted herd sizes based on outcomes. E-LBD agents showed faster individual adaptation to biomass recovery rates (gr1=0.5 for wet seasons, gr2=0.1 for dry seasons)
   - Social-learning agents (E-RO-SL1) exhibited slower but more persistent adjustments, likely due to constrained knowledge-radius=1.0 limiting observational learning opportunities.

3. **Equilibrium Phase (Steps 60-100):**
   - By step 100, average destocking stabilized near 0.5 livestock units per household across all behavioral types. This suggests the emergence of adaptive equilibrium grazing regimes.
   - Heterogeneous strategies converged on similar herd sizes, indicating risk sensitivity and learning capabilities mitigate long-term destocking variance despite climate shocks.


### Implications for Decision-Makers

- **Adaptive Herd Management:** The 75% reduction in destocking from step 0 to step 100 underscores the need for flexible herd sizing policies that reward dynamic adjustments to environmental feedback.
- **Learning Incentives:** While all agent types achieved stability, E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 strategies demonstrated superior resilience. Programs promoting experiential learning or peer mentoring could enhance real-world adaptation.
- **Risk Buffering:** The pronounced impact of rainfall variability (rain-std=100) highlights the importance of risk management systems (e.g., insurance) to complement behavioral adaptations.
- **Pasture Monitoring:** The model's disabling of resting mechanisms (resting?=False) suggests that without formal rest periods, monitoring systems to detect overuse thresholds are critical.

The simulated convergence across behavioral types implies that even in homogeneous systems with risk aversion (risk-parameter=0.5), intrinsic learning capacities enable sustainable outcomes. However, the delayed stabilization (peak destocking at step 15, resolution time ~30 steps) indicates short-term vulnerabilities that necessitate transitional support mechanisms for smallholders.
