The analysis of the data table and plot reveals the following key insights into the dynamics of risk attitudes among social learning agents in the extended RAGE model:

1. **Oscillatory Pattern of Risk Attitudes**:
   - The initial risk attitudes (measured as `r-parameter` in the range [0.0, 1.0]) exhibit no clear directional trend. Instead, values oscillate between negative (risk-averse) and positive (risk-seeking) magnitudes across the simulation steps (e.g., -0.11 to +0.18). This suggests agents repeatedly adjust their risk tolerance in response to environmental and social feedbacks.
   - Near-zero values (e.g., ~0.0) appear sporadically, potentially reflecting transitional phases where agents' decision-making stabilizes or aligns with environmental constraints.

2. **Influence of Climate Variability**:
   - The high rainfall variability (`rain-std` = 100) and stochastic climate shocks (`global-rain?` = `True`) likely drive fluctuating risk attitudes. For instance, drought-triggered biomass degradation (via `gr2` = 0.1 and `gr1` = 0.5) may amplify risk-aversion in certain steps (e.g., -0.11 values), while favorable conditions could temporarily increase risk-seeking behavior (+0.13 to +0.18).
   - These oscillations align with the model’s focus on uncertainty management, as agents balance herd size (**livestock-init** = 90) against unpredictable biomass regeneration (`gr1` = 0.5, `gr2` = 0.1).

3. **Behavioral Strategy Heterogeneity**:
   - Despite the `homog-behav-types?` parameter enabling homogeneous groups, the data show variability in initial risk attitudes, even among social learners (`E-RO-SL1` agents). This implies that while agents share a baseline learning strategy (social or experience-driven), their individual risk sensitivity (**risk-att-init**) diverges, possibly due to:
     - **Learning-by-doing (E-LBD)**: Agents with positive initial risk attitudes (+0.075 to +0.18) may reinforce risk-seeking herd size decisions after short-term gains.
     - **Social learning dissonance**: Even with `social-susceptibility` = 0 (disabling direct copying), neighborhood-level observations (`knowledge-radius` = 1.0) might indirectly influence decisions in low-risk tolerance subgroups (negative values).

4. **Structural Constraints and Implications**:
   - The high initial herd size (`livestock-init` = 90) and conservative pasture rest mechanisms (disabled: `resting?` = `False`) create a high-pressure environment. Agents with positive risk attitudes may exacerbate pasture degradation, while risk-averse agents might reduce grazing intensity prematurely, risking undernutrition.
   - Early convergence to moderate values (e.g., 0.05–0.04) in later steps (e.g., Step 25) suggests partial adaptation to system constraints, with agents balancing risk tolerance against resource availability.

5. **Decision-Maker Implications**:
   - The lack of convergence to stable risk attitudes indicates that herding strategies in variable climates are inherently unstable, with agents oscillating between exploitation and caution. Decision-makers should:
     - Promote adaptive governance (e.g., flexible rest periods) to accommodate heterogeneous risk metrics.
     - Incentivize risk-sharing mechanisms (e.g., collective insurance) to mitigate the impacts of climate variability on smallholders’ decisions.   

**Key Metrics Observed**: Initial `r-parameter` = 0.5 (baseline risk tolerance), `livestock-init` = 90 (high grazing pressure), `rain-std` = 100 (high uncertainty). These parameters create a feedback loop where environmental stochasticity and agents’ adaptive strategies interact to drive erratic risk behaviors.
