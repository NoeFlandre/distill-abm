The mean reserve biomass exhibits a bimodal trajectory across the simulation time points (steps 0–100). Initially, biomass remains stable at ~90,000 units for the first two steps, followed by a sharp, abrupt decline to approximately 86,500 units at step 3 and continuing to fall steeply to ~83,000 units by step 30. This early-phase collapse suggests severe pasture degradation, likely driven by overdrafting from high initial herd sizes (90 livestock) and climate variability (rainfall standard deviation of 100 units). After step ~50, biomass rapidly rebounds, surpassing 125,000 units and stabilizing near 127,000 units by the simulation’s finale. This inflection point indicates a shift from resource depletion to partial recovery, potentially reflecting adaptive management or ecosystem feedback mechanisms (e.g., reduced grazing pressure at lower biomass levels). The final plateau (~127,000–128,000 units) aligns with the resting threshold’s probable influence, though the parameter was set to inactive (resting?=False). Notably, biomass fluctuations at higher levels (e.g., drops to 125,000 at step 95) suggest residual sensitivity to stochastic rainfall shocks. These dynamics highlight the interplay between livestock pressure, environmental variability, and adaptive grazing strategies in long-term pasture sustainability.
