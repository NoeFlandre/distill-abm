The average deviation from the recommended number of livestock begins near zero at the start of the simulation, indicating alignment with the initial target herd size. Over the first 20-30 time steps, deviation increases steadily across all behavioral types, reaching a peak deviation of approximately -0.15 to -0.25 (indicating overgrazing relative to recommendations) during moderate-to-strong rainfall variability periods. This initial peak corresponds to agents responding primarily to short-term environmental shocks rather than learning dynamics.  

After the peak, deviation begins to decline for learning-influenced types (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1), with E-RO-SL1 showing the most rapid adaptation, decreasing deviation to -0.05 by ~step 50. This aligns with their social-learning behavior, likely reflecting adoption of strategies that reduce overgrazing after observing neighbor successes. E-LBD agents exhibit slower, more gradual decline (reaching -0.10 by step 80), consistent with trial-and-error learning requiring repeated climate feedback cycles. Meanwhile, E-RO agents (fixed-rule behavior) show persistent deviation plateauing at -0.15, lacking adaptive adjustments.  

A notable decline occurs ~step 70, where deviation drops below -0.05 as pasture biomass recovery improves during favorable rainfall phases. This suggests model agents’ sensitivity to environmental feedback loops, though E-RO’s inability to fully converge to recommendations highlights the importance of learning capacity in sustainability outcomes.  

The dispersion of trajectories among E-RO-SL1 agents widens after step 40, reflecting heterogeneous learning outcomes from social learning interactions. These findings underscore the differential performance of behavioral strategies under stochastic climate conditions, with social learning accelerating adaptation and risk sensitivity moderating overgrazing pressures. For decision makers, this implies that integrating learning mechanisms into management frameworks could enhance resilience without compromising productivity when facing climate uncertainty.
