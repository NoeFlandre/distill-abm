The data reveals persistent variability in household risk attitudes across simulations, with values fluctuating between positive, neutral, and negative ranges (-0.35 to +0.65). In early time steps (first ~5 rows), risk attitudes predominantly cluster near neutral to mild positive values (0.0125–0.18), suggesting conservative stocking decisions that align with baseline recommendations. By mid-simulation (rows 10–20), deviations intensify, with mid-center households (households 10–18) shifting toward moderate negative values (-0.11 to -0.05), indicating herding tendencies or reactive overstocking. Later time steps (rows 25–30) exhibit extreme negative values (-0.35 to -0.16) in lower-numbered households, correlating with heightened risk-seeking behavior and potential overexploitation under climatic stress. Households 20–29 consistently maintain lower magnitudes of deviation (-0.05 to -0.12), reflecting stabilized or adaptive strategies. Spatial clustering of negative deviations in households 1–15 across runs suggests localized herding or learning dynamics, while households 16–29 exhibit greater individual variability. The absence of extreme positive deviations (understocking) implies agents prioritize growth over conservation despite parameterized risk preferences, aligning with the model's emphasis on resilience trade-offs under rainfall variability (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100).
