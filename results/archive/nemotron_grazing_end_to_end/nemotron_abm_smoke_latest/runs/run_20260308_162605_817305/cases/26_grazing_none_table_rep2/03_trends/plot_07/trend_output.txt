The data reveals a trend of increasing inequality in healthy livestock distribution across households over time, with notable shifts around the 50-year mark. Initially, Gini coefficients remain near-zero (e.g., ~1.37e-4 to 3.717e-4) for the first ~30 years, suggesting stable resource distribution under homogeneous E-RO conditions. This stability persists despite climatic variability, as agents maintain uniform grazing thresholds (satisficing-threshold=0, resting-threshold=0.1) and lack social learning mechanisms.  After approximately 30–50 timesteps, Gini coefficients rise sharply (e.g., reaching 0.096 by timestep 30), indicating growing disparities in livestock health. This aligns with the model's climate-driven shocks (rain-mean=200 mm, rain-std=100 mm), where localized droughts or overgrazing amplify stress on pastures. E-RO agents, constrained by conservative resting behaviors and individual decision-making without social adaptation, fail to mitigate resource depletion collectively. The trend underscores how static behavioral strategies exacerbate resource inequity under prolonged environmental stress, highlighting the need for adaptive learning mechanisms (e.g., social learning or strategy diversification) in heterogeneous populations to buffer against systemic risks.
