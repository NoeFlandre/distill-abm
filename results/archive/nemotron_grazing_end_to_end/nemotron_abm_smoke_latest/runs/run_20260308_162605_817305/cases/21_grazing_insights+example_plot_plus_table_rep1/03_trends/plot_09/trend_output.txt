The mean destocking across 20 simulation runs starts at zero and increases steadily over time, with the most significant acceleration observed after approximately 70 years. Specifically, destocking remains low (<20 households total) during the initial 15 years, as herds establish and pasture resources are abundant. Between years 15-70, it rises linearly to around 100-120 households total, reflecting gradual pasture degradation under overstocking and climatic variability (rainfall mean=200, std=100). After year 70, the trend continues upward, but with increasing divergence between runs, indicating heightened uncertainty in long-term outcomes. By year 100, cumulative destocking reaches 120-140 households total across the population. The rising variance after year 70 suggests compounding effects of agent decisions and environmental stochasticity, potentially signaling approaching system tipping points. This pattern underscores the long-term unsustainability of homogeneous economically rational behavior in variable climates, warranting adaptive management strategies to mitigate degradation risks.
