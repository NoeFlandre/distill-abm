The count of households following the E-RO strategy across simulations follows a distinct pattern. Starting from 20 households at step 0, the metric remains stable at 20 for the first 15 steps. These trends align with the model’s focus on homogeneous populations under climatic variability. In homogeneous settings with E-RO agents (no learning mechanisms), the initial stability reflects baseline adherence to strict resource management rules. The mid-simulation peak suggests a short-term response to environmental shocks (e.g., low rainfall), where agents temporarily adjust herd sizes to mitigate pasture stress. However, the lack of learning mechanisms prevents adaptive strategies, leading to gradual decline as climatic variability and resource depletion compound. The terminal plateau at 20–24 households indicates stabilized household numbers, though at lower levels than the initial count, suggesting long-term resilience limitations without behavioral flexibility. For policymakers, this implies that homogeneous, non-learning agents face heightened vulnerability to climatic variability in common-property systems, emphasizing the need for adaptive regulations or incentives to promote strategy diversification in real-world grazing management.
