The average number of households destocking livestock begins at a high baseline (6.0 households) across all simulations and declines sharply over the first 40 steps. This initial rapid decrease likely reflects immediate adjustments to overstocking practices, as all households start with 90 livestock per household under a fixed total pasture area. By step 40, the destocking rate stabilizes near zero for most households, suggesting a convergence toward a sustainable equilibrium where livestock numbers align with carrying capacity. The model shows minimal variance among runs after this equilibrium is reached, indicating that the population self-regulates effectively under homogeneous conditions.  

The sustained low destocking rate after step 40 suggests that households adaptively reduce stocking levels in response to resource scarcity, consistent with the E-RO strategy’s intrinsic focus on individual resource use. The absence of social learning mechanisms (E-RO-SL1 is inactive) and constraints like resting-threshold or satisficing-threshold imply that this adaptation occurs primarily through direct experience of resource depletion rather than collective strategies. Climate variability (mean rainfall = 200, std = 100) introduces minor fluctuations in vegetation growth, but the agent-level adaptation to rainfall shocks prevents long-term destocking crises.  

For decision-makers, this trend highlights the effectiveness of household-level satisficing behavior in achieving sustainability without requiring formal institutional mechanisms. The initial destocking crisis underscores the importance of rapid, adaptive responses to overstocking risks in common-property systems. While the homogeneous setup may not capture complex social dynamics, the results suggest that self-regulated grazing policies can stabilize pasture biomass and reduce demand shocks in the long term.
