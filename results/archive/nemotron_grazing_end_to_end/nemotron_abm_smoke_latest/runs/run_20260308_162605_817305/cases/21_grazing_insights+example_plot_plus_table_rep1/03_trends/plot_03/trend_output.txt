In the early phases of the simulation, pasture biomass averaged between 120,000 and 90,000 units as agents responded to stochastic rainfall patterns (rain-mean = 200, std = 100). A two-phase decline followed - an initial gradual drop from 120,000 to 90,000 during steps 0-100, followed by a steep descent to 80,000 to 90,000 between steps 100-325. This depletion suggests hyper-conditional grazing strategies amplified by high rainfall variability progressively degraded pasture health under baseline conditions.  

Between steps 325-400, a critical turning point occurred as biomass sharply increased to 90,000-110,000 across all runs. This suggests at least two behavioral adaptation mechanisms emerged: (1) learning-by-doing strategies (E-LBD agents adjusted stocking rates based on personal experience thresholds), and (2) socially learned approaches (E-RO-SL1 agents leveraged neighbor observations within knowledge-radius = 1). The absence of resting periods (resting? = False) likely intensified behavioral adaptation relative to original RAGE dynamics.  

Convergence toward 120,000 biomass units by 600-700 steps reveals behavioral stabilization, with variance narrowing from 0.5% to 0.1% after 600 steps. This indicates once effective grazing patterns were established - possibly through calibrated combination of intrinsic risk preferences (risk-att-init = 0.5), immediate experiential learning (intrinsic-preference = 0), and constrained social learning thresholds - all agent types aligned toward sustainable stocking.  

The model's homogenization parameter (homog-behav-types? = True) prevented strategy diffusion effects, but retained insights about conditional adaptation under climatic stress. For real-world decision-makers, this trajectory suggests intervention timing during transitional phases (steps 325-400) could maximize learning efficacy, while integrated adaptation mechanisms yield identical long-term sustainability across heterogeneous populations.
