The count of households adhering to the non-learning behavioral strategy (E-RO) begins with variability across simulation runs, ranging from 20 to 32 agents initially. In most cases, this number stabilizes temporarily before diverging significantly due to the combined pressures of stochastic climate variability and resource constraints imposed by high initial herd sizes (livestock-init=90). The presence of risk-sensitive agents (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1), who dynamically adjust herd sizes based on environmental feedback and observational learning (despite social learning being disabled by social-susceptibility=0.0), drives gradual shifts away from the E-RO population. These learning-capable agents adapt their decisions—such as reducing grazing intensity during dry spells (rain-std=100) or responding to biomass regeneration rates (gr1=0.5 during wet seasons, gr2=0.1 during droughts)—which indirectly reduces grazing pressure. Over time, the data reveals a bimodal trend: some simulation runs retain stable E-RO populations (e.g., consistently 20 over timesteps), while others experience a sharp decline to near-zero counts by timestep 15–20, reflecting catastrophic pasture collapse in those scenarios. The late-stage persistence of E-RO households (e.g., 15–25 agents in intermediate steps) suggests residual robustness in non-adaptive strategies under moderate climate stress. These trends align with the model’s focus on illustrating how learning strategies influence resource sustainability, as well as the destabilizing effect of uncertainty on homogeneous risk aversion (risk-att-init=0.5). Each simulation’s divergence highlights the interplay between environmental stochasticity, managed grazing, and adaptive decision-making within the context of smallholder livelihood resilience.
