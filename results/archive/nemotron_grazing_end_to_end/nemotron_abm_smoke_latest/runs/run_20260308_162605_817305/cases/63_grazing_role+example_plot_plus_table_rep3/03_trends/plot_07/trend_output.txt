The Gini coefficient quantifying livestock health inequality begins near zero at the simulation's outset, reflecting uniform resource distribution among households. A gradual increase emerges within the first 10–20 years, with some runs showing accelerated growth after environmental perturbations (e.g., droughts tied to global rainfall variability). By step 50–60, abrupt rises occur, driven by agent responses to pasture collapse and herd size adjustments under risk-sensitive decision-making. Over time, the metric stabilizes near 0.7–0.8, indicating entrenched inequality. Agent behavioral heterogeneity—particularly among learning-capable types (E-LBD, E-RO-SL1)—amplifies divergence in outcomes due to asymmetric access to information and adaptive heuristics. Climate shocks exacerbate these disparities by disproportionately impacting lower-resource households, whose recovery strategies are constrained by initial resource deficits. The data reveals that runs emphasizing risk tolerance (**risk-att-init=0.5**) and social learning (e.g., within **knowledge-radius=1.0**) exhibit more pronounced inequality trajectories, suggesting feedbacks between behavioral adaptation and resource distribution amplify systemic imbalance.
