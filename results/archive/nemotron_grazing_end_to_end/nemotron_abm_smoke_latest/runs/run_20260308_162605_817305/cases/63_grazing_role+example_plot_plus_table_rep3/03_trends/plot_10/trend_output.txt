The mean count of E-RO households across simulation runs remains remarkably stable, fluctuating between 20 and 28 households throughout all timesteps. At initialization (step 0), all runs begin with exactly 20 E-RO households, consistent with the fixed \*number-E-RO\*=20 parameter value. A minor upward drift emerges early in the simulation, reaching mean values of 24-25 households by step 20-25, attributed to climate-induced herd stress from stochastic rainfall patterns (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100) reducing pasture biomass and forcing basal fitness thresholds. However, this early variation dissipates after step 40, reverting to the baseline mean of 20±2 households in all subsequent runs.   \n   \nThe absence of sustained behavioral shifts aligns with the E-RO strategy's lack of learning mechanisms: non-herd dynamics persist because agents never adjust grazing intensity or timing despite changing environmental conditions. The stable equilibrium reflects the original RAGE model's assumptions of stochastic pastoralist decisions fixed to preconceived grazing rules, despite representation of adaptive learning capabilities in the extended framework. While minor stochastic fluctuations occur, no systematic trends emerge between homogeneous behavioral groups, confirming the system remains in pre-equilibrium states throughout the 100-step temporal horizon. This constancy provides a counterfactual baseline against which learning-capable behavioral types (E-LBD, E-RO-SL1) would subsequently differ through emergent adaptability.
