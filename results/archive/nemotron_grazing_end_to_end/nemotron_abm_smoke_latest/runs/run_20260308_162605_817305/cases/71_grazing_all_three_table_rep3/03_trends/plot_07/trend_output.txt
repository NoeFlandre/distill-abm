The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock exhibits a dynamic pattern of increasing inequality over the 100-timestep simulation. Starting near zero (essentially perfect equity), the metric rises steadily through early to mid-timesteps, reaching peak values of approximately 0.009–0.012 between timesteps 30–70. Some runs show extended inequality patterns with values exceeding 0.017 in later timesteps. Key drivers include the absence of social learning (disabled via **social-susceptibility**=0.0) and satisficing behavior, combined with uniform starting conditions where all agents default to E-RO (fixed-rule) strategies. The high initial herd size (90 head per household) amplifies pasture degradation dynamics under variable rainfall conditions (mean=200, std=100), disproportionately disadvantaging risk-averse agents managing fragile herds. Risk sensitivity (risk-parameter=0.5) further exacerbates divergence, as agents with resilient strategies benefit from opportunistic herd adjustments, while others face chronic productivity losses. Late-stage deviations in Gini values across runs reflect amplifying climate shocks and non-linear herding pressure effects. These trends underscore how inflexible behavioral strategies and environmental stochasticity together drive wealth stratification, implying that adaptive management frameworks—particularly social learning mechanisms—could mitigate emerging inequalities by redistributing information about herd resilience and climate stress thresholds.
