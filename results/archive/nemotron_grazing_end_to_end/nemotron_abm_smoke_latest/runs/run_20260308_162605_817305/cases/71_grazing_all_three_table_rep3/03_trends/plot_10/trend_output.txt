A notable peak of 41 E-RO agents occurs in one run at step 8, followed by gradual declines to 10–14 agents by step 29, often interrupted by recoveries (e.g., 28 agents at step 10).

The persistence of E-RO households through resource-stressed periods suggests their reliance on fixed routines may clash with environmental unpredictability, potentially leading to unsustainable grazing practices. However, the absence of sustained directional trends implies that the system does not exhibit catastrophic collapse under the tested conditions, possibly due to biomass regeneration rules (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1) or household mortality (mr=0.05) moderating resource depletion.   

For decision makers, these findings underscore the vulnerability of homogeneous, non-adaptive strategies in climatically variable systems. While abrupt shifts are limited, cumulative stress from environmental shocks could accelerate if E-RO populations grow unchecked. This aligns with the model’s emphasis on risk sensitivity, as even baseline agents may intensify long-term unsustainability without institutional or behavioral interventions. The simple stochasticity of fluctuations suggests prioritizing early warning systems for pasture degradation over structural reforms, given the relatively stable baseline performance of E-RO strategies.
