The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households, measured by the Gini coefficient, shows a clear upward trend over time, with intermittent peaks and stabilization phases. Initially, during the first few dozen time steps, Gini coefficients remain low (<0.002), reflecting relatively equal livestock health across households. However, by time step 40, a gradual increase emerges, with values rising to ~0.002–0.003 across many replicates. This rise correlates with periods of heightened climatic variability (global rainfall with mean 200, std 100), where droughts likely exacerbate resource stress and uneven adaptations among households. 

Peaks in Gini coefficients become pronounced around time steps 60–80, where values exceed 0.007 in several replicates. These peaks align with extended periods of cumulative stress, as repeated resource scarcity prevents households from recovering to earlier equilibrium levels. Notably, the final time steps exhibit the widest variation, with some replicates reaching Gini coefficients as high as 0.0082, suggesting persistent structural inequities. 

The trajectory reflects the model’s emphasis on climatic shocks amplifying disparities over time. Without social learning mechanisms (E-RO agents lack SL1/2 capabilities) or strategy-switching (SL2 disabled), households relying solely on satisficing thresholds and intrinsic preferences cannot collectively stabilize resource use. This leads to irreversible divergence: some households maintain viable stocking levels through early risk mitigation, while others suffer irreversible biomass depletion from overgrazing during low-rainfall years. The absence of AL practices prolongs recovery from ecosystem degradation, entrenching inequality. 

Despite stochastic rainfall driving short-term fluctuations, the overall trend underscores how behavioral homogeneity under climatic stress accelerates social-ecological inequity. The data confirms that even baseline strategies fail to prevent long-term inequality accumulation, highlighting the fragility of governing common resources in heterogeneous environments.
