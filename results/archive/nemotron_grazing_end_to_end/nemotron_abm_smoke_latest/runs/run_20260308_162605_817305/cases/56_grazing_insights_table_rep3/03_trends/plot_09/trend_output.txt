The data reveals a consistent upward trend in the average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households over repeated simulations. Destocking begins at 0 in the initial time steps and gradually increases, with values ranging from 6.0 to 102.25 by the final recorded period. The rate of increase appears relatively linear in early phases, with larger cumulative increases emerging in later periods, particularly after 100.0 timesteps, suggesting accelerating destocking as simulations progress.   Methods: The analysis reflects the behavior of E-RO agents under homogeneous conditions, who rely on intrinsic preferences and risk sensitivity (risk-att-init=0.5) to manage livestock stocking. Climatic variability (global-rain?=True, rain-mean=200, rain-std=100) likely exacerbates resource degradation, driving sustained destocking as pasture biomass declines. The absence of social learning (homog-behav-types?=True, social-susceptibility=0) and strategy switching (SL2-strategy-switch?=False) prevents adaptive responses to resource scarcity, amplifying persistent declines in available forage.   Implications for decision makers: The steady accumulation of destocked livestock highlights the risk of irreversible ecosystem destabilization under static behavioral strategies. To mitigate this, proactive management frameworks that integrate dynamic monitoring (e.g., regular biomass assessments) and adaptive thresholds (e.g., adjust stocking rates to satisficing-threshold levels during droughts) could reduce long-term pasture damage. Introducing social learning mechanisms in heterogeneous settings may also enhance resilience by fostering knowledge exchange and strategy adaptation.
