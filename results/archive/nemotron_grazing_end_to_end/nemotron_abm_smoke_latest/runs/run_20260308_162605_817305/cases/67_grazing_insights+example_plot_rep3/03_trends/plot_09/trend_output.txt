The mean destock total exhibited distinct phases across the simulation period. Initially, during the first 30 steps, all lines showed a gradual rise, with no significant divergence between strategies. By step 100, lines plateaued, with grey (~160 households), burgundy (~150 households), and teal (~140 households) dominating. This stabilization suggests adaptive equilibrium, where destocking balances vegetation recovery under fixed rainfall regimes (global-rain?=True). Variability between runs increased after step 100, distinguishing runs by ~5 households, possibly due to non-linear interactions between risk attitudes (risk-att-init=0.5) and social learning (E-RO-SL1 agents), even under homogeneous defaults. The results highlight the importance of temporal scaling in destocking decisions—initial conservative responses degenerated into systemic stabilization, aligning with risk-aware strategies under climatic uncertainty. For decision-makers, these trends underscore the necessity of dynamic resource thresholds and leveraging behavioral type differentiation (e.g., introducing E-LBD or E-RO-SL1 agents via homog-behav-types?=False) to mitigate long-term pasture collapse risks.
