The data shows a pattern where social learning adoption follows a sigmoidal increase: (1) No adoption in initial steps, (2) Gradual rise reaching 60%+ households by step 40, (3) Temporary fluctuation in middle steps, (4) Stabilization at 20-35% adoption through final steps. This suggests social learning acts as a threshold-dependent behavior where network effects sharpen adoption over time. The stabilization at ~25% adoption indicates a bio-social equilibrium where agents balance social learning benefits against individual decision-making incentives. The limited adoption despite risk-aware decision-making (risk-att-init 0.5) implies social learning acts as a bounded rationality amplifier rather than a full replacement. For governments, this highlights the importance of social norms diffusion rather than long-term enforcement of learning behaviors once critical mass is achieved. The plateau suggests policy interventions should focus on dynamic monitoring systems to maintain adaptive capacities within this equilibrium range.
