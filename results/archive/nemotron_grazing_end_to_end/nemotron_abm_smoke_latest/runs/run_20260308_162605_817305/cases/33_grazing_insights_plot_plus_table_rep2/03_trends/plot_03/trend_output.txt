The data reveals a multistage pattern in reserve biomass dynamics across replicates involving 60 homogeneous E-RO households under fixed rainfall (mean=200, std=100) and continuous grazing. Initial biomass levels stabilize at ~90,000 units. A sudden decline occurs at step 20 (values dropping to 83k-84k across replicates), coinciding with the model's safisficing threshold triggering conservation strategies. Recovery begins at step 30, with biomass reaching 123k-128k by step 100, demonstrating long-term adaptive biomass management through risk-sensitive decision-making. Variability between replicates suggests stochastic elements in climate-vegetation interactions amplify management challenges, yet sustained grazing under risk-modeling parameters enables eventual resource renewal. The transition from depletion to recovery underscores the importance of considering behavioral adaptability in common property systems, particularly when no resting periods are enforced. This pattern implies that even without institutional controls, strategic learning mechanisms embedded in agent decision rules prevent terminal biomass collapse in variable climates.
