The analysis of the simulated agent-based model (ABM) reveals distinct trends in household risk attitudes and herd size deviations from recommendations, shaped by environmental variability and adaptive behaviors. Below is a synthesis of the observed patterns, contextualized within the model's design parameters:

1. **Initial Overgrazing and Steady Decline in Herd Sizes**
- Most households begin with herd sizes exceeding recommendations (mean deviations >0.0 in early steps), reflecting the high initial herd size parameter (livestock-init=90). This aligns with the model's setup of common property resource stress and risk-seeking behavior (risk-att-init=0.5).
- Over time, deviations shift toward negative values (undergrazing), particularly in agents with behavioral strategies emphasizing risk aversion (E-LBD, E-RO-SL1). For example, deviations drop from +0.18 to -0.19 in later steps for some agents, indicating adaptive redistribution of herds in response to pasture collapse.
- The decline accelerates in steps 40–60, coinciding with simulated climate shocks (global-rain?=True, rain-std=100). Droughts reduce pasture biomass growth (gr2=0.1), forcing agents to reduce herd sizes below recommendations to avoid starvation.

2. **Behavioral Stratification and Risk Sensitivity**
- Agents with learning capabilities (E-LBD, E-RO-SL1) diverge from risk-neutral TRAD agents. E-LBD agents reduce herd sizes earlier (step ~30) due to negative experience feedback (number-E-LBD=20), while E-RO-SL1 agents rely on local social learning (knowledge-radius=1.0). However, hardcoded parameters disable social learning (social-susceptibility=0.0), limiting cross-agent coordination.
- Risk-aware agents consistently show smaller deviations (-0.08 to -0.03 in late steps) compared to risk-neutral peers, reflecting risk-mode=True and the r-parameter (0.5) balancing exploration and exploitation in dynamic resource conditions.

3. **Persistence of Deviations**
- Some households stabilize near zero deviations (e.g., -0.01 to 0.01) by step 100, suggesting partial adaptation to environmental carrying capacity. Others exhibit persistent under/overgrazing: low-risk-tolerance agents (-0.15 to -0.20) maintain conservative herds long-term, while high-risk-tolerance outliers (>0.5) overshoot during rainfall variability (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100).
- The final data table highlights bimodal outcomes: ~60% of agents cluster within -0.1 to 0.1, while 40% show >0.2 deviations, indicating heterogeneous strategy efficacy.

4. **Model-Specific Dynamics**
- Absence of satisficing (satisficing-threshold=0.0) and disabled pasture rest (resting?=False) amplify resource depletion during dry spells, accelerating herd size adjustments.
- The dominance of E-RO agents (baseline non-learning type) creates inertia in herd management, as fixed rules suppress rapid adaptation. Learning strategies (15%) introduce slower but more responsive transitions.

**Interpretation**: The trends reflect trade-offs between risk tolerance, learning mechanisms, and environmental shocks. Initial overdependence on pasture resources under fixed herd sizes leads to overshooting, followed by crashes. Behavioral heterogeneity drives divergent outcomes, with risk-averse and learning-capable agents achieving more equilibrium over time. The model captures emergent feedbacks between pasture degradation, risk perception, and adaptive decision-making, critical for evaluating management strategies under uncertainty.
