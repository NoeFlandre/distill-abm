By timestep 50, the mean risk attitude declines to 0.45, coinciding with intensified drought conditions (rain-mean=150–190 in simulated storms) that strain pasture recovery (gr2=0.1). This decline suggests herds are increasingly undersized relative to post-storm biomass regeneration capacity, as agents with moderate risk tolerance reduce grazing pressure to avoid overexploitation. This stabilization suggests a shift toward cautious pasture management, prioritizing long-term biomass recovery over short-term herd growth. The absence of social learning adoption (**social-susceptibility=0.0**) means these changes arise from individual risk-aware decision-making (**risk-mode=True**) rather than strategy diffusion. For smallholder farmers, these trends underscore the importance of adaptive herd management in common property systems, particularly under variable climate conditions. Maintaining a risk attitude below 0.5 may improve resilience to stochastic shocks, but overly conservative strategies could limit productivity gains during favorable rainfall periods.
