The trajectory of mean household risk attitude deviations shows distinct patterns aligned with agent types and environmental stressors. For E-RO agents (orange line), deviations begin at 0.15 in early steps, declining steeply to -0.05 by step 20, then stabilizing near 0.0. This reflects initial herd reductions due to stochastic rainfall shocks (average 200mm, std 100mm), aligning with risk-averse adjustments to pasture strain from high initial herd size (90 animals). E-RO-SL1 agents (purple) exhibit sharper, faster declines—starting at 0.2, dropping to 0.05 by step 30, stabilizing near 0.0. Their social learning radius (1.0) enables rapid knowledge transfer about viable herd sizes amid climate variability, resulting in earlier adaptation than E-RO. E-LBD agents (green), starting at 0.0, show minimal early deviation but plunge to -0.3 by step 50, recovering partially to -0.1 by step 80. This delayed response matches their learning-by-experience mechanism, which only triggers after observing pasture recovery patterns over longer feedback loops. The absence of TRAD/MAX/SAT agents (disabled via parameter settings) eliminates positional bias from fixed strategies, isolating behavioral heterogeneity effects. Notably, all trajectories flatten before 80 steps, suggesting equilibrium adaptation to stochastic rainfall cycles. Decision-makers might prioritize social learning networks for risk-sensitive agents and adjustable grazing quotas to align E-LBD’s delayed adaptation with faster-responding groups, given pasture dynamics governed by biweekly rain variability and fixed biomass regeneration rates (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1).
