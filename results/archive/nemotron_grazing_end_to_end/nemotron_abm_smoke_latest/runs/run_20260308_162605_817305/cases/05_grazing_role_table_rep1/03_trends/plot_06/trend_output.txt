The data reveals a distinct two-phase pattern in destocking trends over the 100-year simulation period. Initially, destocking remains remarkably stable between 5.8–6.2 across all households for the first 25 time steps, suggesting a systemic equilibrium under the baseline E-RO behavioral rules combined with moderate risk aversion (risk-att-init=0.5). This plateau likely reflects agents' consistent application of fixed decision rules to manage herd sizes amid climate volatility (rain-std=100) and high initial grazing pressure (livestock-init=90).

Around time step 25, a pronounced and sustained decline begins, with mean destocking dropping from ~6.1 to ~3.4 by the simulation's end. This represents a 43% reduction in destocking rates, indicating a systemic shift toward improved resource management. The decline accelerates after time step 50, with values falling below 3.0 and oscillating between 1.8–2.5 in the final decades. Notably, individual household variability persists, with some households (e.g., households 24–29) showing destocking rates below 1.5 by the end phase, while others maintain higher rates (e.g., household 0 remains at ~4.0 throughout).

The absence of satisficing (satisficing-threshold=0) and social learning (social-susceptibility=0) suggests this decline stems from agents' intrinsic risk sensitivity and behavioral inertia rather than collaborative adaptation or exploratory learning. The extension-model's implementation of risk-aware decision-making may have enabled agents to anticipate future shocks through conservative herd management, reducing reactive destocking in later years. However, the gradual decline implies agents did not adopt predefined strategies like grazing rotation or herd size caps, as proposed by the SIMON (IM1, IM-E-RO-SL2) frameworks. Instead, the pattern suggests tacit learning emerging from long-term exposure to climate uncertainty, even without explicit learning mechanisms.

This trend aligns with the original model's focus on social-ecological feedbacks, demonstrating how livestock management intensity responds to environmental variability in common property systems when agents operate under fixed but risk-aware rules. The persistent destocking in some households also highlights persistent variability in individual resource stress levels despite system-wide improvement.
