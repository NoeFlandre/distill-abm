The data reveals a progressive increase in cumulative livestock destocking across all households over the simulation period. Initially, destocking remains negligible for the first ~14 timesteps, after which it begins to rise gradually, accelerating notably around timestep 15–20 (values increasing from ~6 to ~17.9). By timestep 29, the mean destocking rate grows to ~20.6, and continues to climb steadily, reaching ~31.2 by timestep 33 and exceeding ~170 by the final timestep (~54). This pattern suggests a delayed but sustained response to environmental pressures.  

The trend reflects adaptive management under stress: households begin destocking cautiously in response to pasture degradation triggered by initial overgrazing (livestock-init=90) and stochastic rainfall shocks (global-rain?=True, rain-std=100). Cumulative destocking accelerates as resource scarcity exacerbates due to slow biomass regeneration in dry seasons (gr2=0.1) and climate volatility. The absence of social learning (social-susceptibility=0.0, homog-behav-types?=True with uniformly E-RO agents) implies decisions stem from individual risk-aware adjustment (risk-att-init=0.5) rather than collective strategy diffusion. While all households exhibit increasing destocking, variability in per-household rates persists, indicating heterogeneous responses to compounded environmental stress.
