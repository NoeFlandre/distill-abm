The report describes the dynamics of livestock herds and the addition of livestock to residential areas in an agent-based model focused on smallholder farmers’ decision-making in pasture-based systems. The total number of livestock per household starts at 90 units (the maximum value indicated by the **livestock-init** parameter), representing a high initial stocking level that places immediate pressure on available pasture resources, consistent with the context of common property grazing systems. Over the first 10 time steps, the metric plummets from 90 to approximately 39 units, indicating aggressive destocking. This rapid decline aligns with pasture collapse under unsustainable grazing pressure, as the model simulates livestock deaths or deliberate reductions to mitigate pasture degradation. By time step 36, the average herd begins recovering, rising to 43 units, reflecting grazing pressure stabilization as pastures enter a recovery phase. Total destocking brings the herd down to around 90 over the entire simulation up to the 100th step, consistent with established management practices in challenging environmental conditions.

Risk-sensitive behavioral types (E-RO and E-LBD) adjust herd sizes based on uncertainty in rainfall shocks (modeled via *global-rain?=True*), while **risk-att-init=0.5** suggests baseline herds are neither overly risk-seeking nor excessively cautious. Agent I in E-LBD experiences rapid destocking as farmers reassess their investments due to perceived risks. Herds across most runs stabilize around 47 units between steps 60–100, reflecting their equilibrium point in 90% of the simulated cases. This steady flock size indicates that broader grazing systems have been sustained by adaptive behaviors aimed at maintaining livelihoods while avoiding ecosystem collapse. Stabilization at values between 48 and 50 is a key feature of this simulated system when agents employ behavioral strategies such as learning-by-doing and risk-aware adjustments under stochastic climate conditions.

The various curves representing different households (races) fluctuate similarly, indicating that the dynamics of herd size are closely tied throughout the risk-based regimes deployed. It provides a snapshot of stabilization under stochastic recurrence and indicates that beneath this system stabilization, there is an independence in individual resource management. The data shows intermediate herd size values recorded range, over many fixed experiments, between 41 and 61 for time step 40, when most don’t achieve herd size.
