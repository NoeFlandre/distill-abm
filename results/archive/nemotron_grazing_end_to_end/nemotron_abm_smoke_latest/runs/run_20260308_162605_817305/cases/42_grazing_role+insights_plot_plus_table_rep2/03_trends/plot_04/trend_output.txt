The simulated mean green biomass exhibits a progressive decline over time, beginning at approximately 45,000 units across all initial time steps and stabilizing around lower values by the final simulated years. Early in the simulation (first 15–20 steps), biomass remains stable, reflecting initial equilibrium conditions under fixed grazing intensity (high initial herd size = 90) and parametric settings (e.g., gr1 = 0.5, Rmax = 0.5). After step 20, biomass begins to decline steadily, with sharper reductions observed after step 50, consistent with the combined effects of high grazing pressure and stochastic rainfall variability (rain-std = 100). The extinction of marginal patches (lowest values = ~40,000) and the emergence of highly productive outliers (>60,000) after step 75 suggest spatial heterogeneity in pasture resilience. The absence of learning strategies (all agents default to E-RO) and disabled social learning (social-susceptibility = 0) likely amplify grazing impacts, as agents lack behavioral adaptation to resource depletion. Risk-sensitive decision-making (risk-att-init = 0.5) does not prevent sustained biomass reduction, implying the interaction of grazing pressure and climate shocks overwhelms risk mitigation. By step 100, the average biomass stabilizes near 58,000–60,000, indicating a new equilibrium with heightened climate variability limiting recovery. Decision-makers must prioritize herd size reduction (livestock-init = 90) and drought resilience, as natural recovery is constrained by model parameters.
