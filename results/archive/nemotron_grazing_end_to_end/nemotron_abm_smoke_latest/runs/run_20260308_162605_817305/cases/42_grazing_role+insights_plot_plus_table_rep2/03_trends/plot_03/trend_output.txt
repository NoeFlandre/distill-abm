The analysis of mean reserve biomass trends reveals a systematic decline in pasture conditions across the simulation's 100-year time frame (timesteps). Initial biomass levels (~90,000) gradually decrease due to persistent grazing pressure from fixed herd sizes (90 livestock units/household) and uninterrupted consumption patterns. The absence of adaptive behavioral strategies—particularly the lack of social learning (knowledge-radius=1.0 but social-susceptibility=0.0) and disabled satisficing behavior—prevents agents from dynamically adjusting grazing intensity despite worsening pasture conditions. Climate stochasticity (rain-std=100) introduces episodic stress events that exacerbate degradation, yet risk-averse decision-making (risk-att-init=0.5) fails to mitigate cumulative impacts. Behavioral urgency patterns are staggered, with early-stage declines reflecting initial grazing shocks and later declines representing irreversible resource depletion. The data suggests that without explicit mechanisms for strategy diffusion or institutional memory (e.g., social learning thresholds or rest protocols), grazing systems remain vulnerable to overshoot, even with heterogeneous risk preferences. Decision-makers should prioritize adaptive herd size limits and decentralized learning platforms to align individual behavior with ecological carrying capacity.
