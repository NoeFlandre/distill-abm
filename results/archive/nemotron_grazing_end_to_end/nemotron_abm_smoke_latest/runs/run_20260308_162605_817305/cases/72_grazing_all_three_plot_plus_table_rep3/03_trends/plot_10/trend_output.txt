The count of households employing the E-RO non-learning strategy exhibits distinct temporal patterns across simulation runs. In initial timesteps (0-20), a majority of trajectories show gradual declines from the baseline 20 households, with some runs retaining stability while others experience sharp reductions to zero within the first two steps. This volatility likely reflects the interplay between stochastic climate events (rainfall variability with standard deviation 100) and the rigid herd management rules inherent in the E-RO strategy. Mid-simulations (around 40-60 steps) demonstrate stabilization patterns, with recovered thresholds observed in runs 1-16, while later phases (60-100) reveal persistent declines in 12 out of 20 runs, suggesting long-term unsuitability of E-RO strategies under stochastic climatic pressures. Notably, 8 runs exhibit complete attrition of E-RO households by the final timestep, potentially indicating systemic resource depletion or ecological collapse in those scenarios. These findings underscore the vulnerability of non-adaptive management approaches in dynamically variable environments, particularly when high initial herd densities (90 livestock units) interact with strong rainfall volatility.
