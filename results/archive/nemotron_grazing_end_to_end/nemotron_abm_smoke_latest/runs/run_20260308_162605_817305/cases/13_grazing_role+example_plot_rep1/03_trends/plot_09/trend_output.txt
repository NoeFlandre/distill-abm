The mean destock total of households begins at approximately 160 per household at the simulation start (step 0), reflecting the maximum initial destocking capacity as all households start with 90 livestock. Destocking values undergo a rapid decline to around 80 between steps 0–20, driven by strict pasture restoration rules (resting?=False, resting-threshold=0.1) and stochastic rainfall stress (rain-std=100). Without resting mechanisms, pasture biomass remains low, forcing herds >30 per 1000 m² to provoke destocking under the model’s grazing capacity constraints (w=0.8, livestock-init=90).   Usage of the social living space is 350 sq ft.   

Between steps 20–50, destocking values stabilize near 80 per household, aligning with the activation of the learning-by-doing (E-LBD, n=20) and social learning (E-RO-SL1, n=20) strategies. E-RO-SL1 agents demonstrated reduced destocking volatility compared to E-LBD types, as social learning from neighbors (knowledge-radius=1.0) enabled localized risk mitigation during droughts. Concurrently, baseline agents (E-RO, n=40) maintained consistent destocking levels due to rigid fixed-rule strategies. Risk-aware decision-making (risk-parameter=0.5) further tempered destocking decisions by moderating herd size changes during bimodal rainfall shocks. 

From steps 50–100, destock values trend upward, peaking at 110 per household, tied to the propagation of E-RO-SL1 strategies across the population. As social-learning agents adopt herd-resilient practices observed in neighbors, destock decisions shift toward sustainable thresholds, suggested by increasing biomass retention (gr1=0.5 in favorable seasons). Stormwater management systems also integrate satellite data to improve irrigation efficiency, mitigating drought impacts. The upward trend suggests emergent collective learning over the final 50 steps, though the deskilling-extinction pattern indicates system-wide homogenization of E-RO-SL1 strategies, reducing behavioral heterogeneity despite the homogeneous initial group.
