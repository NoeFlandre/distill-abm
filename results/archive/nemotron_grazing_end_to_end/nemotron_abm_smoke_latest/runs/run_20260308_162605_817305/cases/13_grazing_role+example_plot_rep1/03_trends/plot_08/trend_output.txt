The count of households engaging in social learning (household-SL) begins at approximately 20 agents during the initial phase of the simulation. This baseline reflects the predefined allocation of 20 agents to the E-RO-SL1 behavioral type, which inherently incorporates social learning through observation of neighbors within the configured *knowledge-radius* of 1.0. Over the first 20–30 steps, the metric rises to 30–35 households, driven by gradual adoption among initially non-social-learning agents. This increase coincides with the emergence of recurring droughts triggered by high rainfall variability (*rain-std* = 100), which stress pastures and reduce grazing productivity. Agents with social learning capabilities (E-RO-SL1) appear to adapt more effectively to these shocks by observing neighboring strategies, such as reduced herd sizes, which aligns with observed resilience in social-learning dynamics. From steps 30–60, the metric plateaus at ~30 households, suggesting stabilization in the proportion of agents benefiting from social learning despite persistent climatic variability. A secondary decline begins around step 60, dropping to 15–20 households, likely reflecting overshoot-impact dynamics. As pasture biomass declines under prolonged grazing pressure (amplified by high initial herd sizes of 90 and *w* = 0.8), the sustainability of social learning may diminish for agents forced to prioritize herd adjustments over knowledge acquisition. By the final steps (80–100), the metric stabilizes near 15–20 households, consistent with a population equilibrium where learning strategies are outweighed by resource limitations. Risk-sensitivity (*risk-att-init* = 0.5) likely moderates abrupt strategy shifts, as agents balance exploration of new methods against the uncertainty of climate variability.
