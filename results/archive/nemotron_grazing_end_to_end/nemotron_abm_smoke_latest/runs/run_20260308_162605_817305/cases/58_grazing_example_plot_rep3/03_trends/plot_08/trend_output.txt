The analysis of the simulated data reveals trends in social learning adoption across households. Initially, the count of households engaging in social learning starts low, with minimal adoption in the first 20–30 steps. This phase likely reflects baseline behavior, where agents prioritize resource management without social learning mechanisms. By approximately step 30–40, adoption accelerates, with most simulations experiencing a sharp increase in proactive knowledge-sharing. This surge aligns with the model’s emphasis on climatic variability (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100), which may drive agents to adopt learning strategies during periods of resource scarcity or unpredictability. By step 50–60, adoption stabilizes within a range of 30–50 households, with some simulations plateauing near the upper bound of 50. This plateau suggests a saturation point where household strategies align with optimal resource management practices, minimizing further need for adaptive learning. Notably, simulations with higher risk attitudes (risk-att-init=0.5) or those responding to prolonged low pasture biomass (e.g., resting-threshold=0.1 scenarios) exhibit earlier and more sustained social learning adoption. The variability in timing and magnitude across runs underscores the influence of stochastic elements, such as random initial resource conditions and climatic shocks, which intermittently disrupt herd management norms. The data collectively supports the model’s goal of demonstrating how social learning emerges as a response to environmental stress, with its adoption peaking during critical transitional periods before stabilizing as ecosystems reach equilibrium.
