**Analysis of Reserve Biomass Dynamics in a Common Property Grazing System**

The simulation of 60 E-RO agents (homogeneous behavioral types) reveals three distinct phases in reserve biomass evolution: rapid depletion (Steps 0–30), steep recovery (Steps 30–70), and stabilized growth plateaus (Step 100). These patterns emerge from interactions between climatic variability, individualized risk-aware decisions, and continuous grazing under fixed resting conditions.

**Phase 1: Initial Depletion (Steps 0–30)**
Biomass consistently declines from ~100,000 to ~75,000 units across all runs. Agents begin with maximal livestock (90/head) and maintain continuous grazing (resting? = False) despite low satisficing-threshold-level depletion. The uniform decision-making strategy leads to synchronized overgrazing, particularly under low-rainfall conditions. The lower bound of depletion (~78,000) persists due to stochastic rainfall extremes impairing regrowth.

**Phase 2: Recovery Acceleration (Steps 30–70)**
A universal exponential increase occurs as agents reduce livestock stocking to ~65/head (risk-adverse adjustments in risk-mode? = True). Recovery rates vary spatially: patch-specific grass growth rates (gr1=0.5, gr2=0.1) and rainfall-dependent mortality create divergent rebound trajectories. The median trajectory reaches equilibrium at ~125,000 by Step 60, with high variance in later phases reflecting individual risk tolerance and patch-specific ecosystem responses.

**Phase 3: Stabilized Growth Plateaus (Step 100)**
All trajectories asymptotically approach equilibrium between 118,000–128,000 units, constrained by climatic stability (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100) and adaptive carrying capacity.
