We present an analysis of the trends in household risk attitudes as simulated by the RAGE Grazing Extension Model. The lineplot presents mean household risk attitude, calculated as the degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all 20 extension agents, plotted against number of simulated years, or timesteps.  E-LBD agents were represented by 20 orange and purple curves. E-LBD agents average a high risk attitude across the simulation ranging from ~0.13 at 12 steps to a plateau at ~0.6. Meanwhile, E-RO-SL1 behavioral types, represented by teals and greens, average a moderate risk attitude across the simulation, ranging between 0.05 and 0.39. However, the trajectories of E-LBD agents - the learning by doing agents brown and dark blue - began near zero risk and generally climbed to a near-constant value of 0.5 after 12 steps. Agents of the final type, the cops with E-RO-SL1 - represented by blues and red, typically avoided risk, though not as enthusiastically as their highest risk-taking E-LBD counterparts. They begin at a lower level, reaching a maximum of 0.39 at 39 steps and ending with a 0.22 from 98 steps, making this behavioral type a modifying force in this form; and while it allows moderate shallowness while learning new behaviors, it prevents excess risk taking. Nestled somewhere between the E-LBD and E-RO types, we can find E-RO-SL1, an agent type that successfully received both behavioral adaptations. E-RO agents were represented by 20 brown and dark yellow curves. They start with a low risk attitude in the beginning and rose to 0.27 at 25 steps, after which it flattens. These results suggest high risk attitude is associated with the learning by doing role that E-LBD type of agents performed. This behavior provides an additional context as signs of crucially necessary changes, at least within grazing land management.
