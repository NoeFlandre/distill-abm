The average reserve biomass starts at an exceptionally high level of approximately 90,000 units in the first time steps, suggesting an artificially idealized initial state in the simulated grazing system. This initial abundance gradually declines to around 90,400 units by mid-simulation (~timestep 15-20) due to agents' cumulative stocking decisions, which appear to reflect risk-averse adaptation to variable conditions. The biomass then exhibits pronounced fluctuations, dropping to ~87,000 units by timestep 50—a critical threshold indicating ecosystem stress during wet-dry cycles. Mid-simulation trends show sustained volatility (±3,000 units) between 85,000–93,000 units, correlating with climatic variability (rain-mean ±100) and herd management responses. Post-timestep 70, biomass rebounds to 102,000 units by final steps, suggesting partial recovery through reduced overgrazing pressures as agents adjust stocking rates following biomass depletion events. The pattern demonstrates E-RO's satisficing behavior leading to cyclical degradation-recovery dynamics under homogeneous risk attitudes (0.5) without social learning mechanisms, highlighting climate's central role in shaping herd management outcomes despite absent behavioral innovation or strategy switching.
