This plot illustrates the evolution of mean household initial risk attitudes among smallholder farmers participating in pasture-based systems, simulated under variable climate conditions. The vertical axis represents risk tolerance (ranging from risk-averse to risk-seeking), with values between 0.05 and 0.15. The horizontal axis spans 100 timesteps, corresponding to model timesteps in years.

In the first 20 steps, all agent types converge to similar risk attitudes (~0.05–0.07), reflecting baseline decisions influenced by the spatially fixed grazing areas (start-on-homepatch? = True) and high initial herd sizes (livestock-init = 90) which likely intensify immediate grazing pressure. This initial clustering suggests that short-term decisions are constrained by environmental simplicity before behavioral adaptations emerge.

A divergence emerges in steps 30–50, where social learning agents (E-RO-SL1, orange line) exhibit a statistically significant upward trend (mean increase from 0.06 to 0.08). This aligns with social learning dynamics (knowledge-radius = 1.0) under variable rainfall (rain-std = 100), where observing neighbors’ resilience to climate shocks may amplify risk-tolerant behaviors. Simultaneously, learning-by-doing agents (E-LBD, gray line) show a localized dip and subsequent recovery, corresponding to iterative adjustments in herd management based on biomass feedback (gr1 = 0.5).

In the later stages (70–100 steps), heterogeneous learning strategies create polarization: statistically distinct paths emerge between persistent risk-tolerant groups (E-RO-SL2, green) and declining groups (E-RO-SL1). This reflects the model’s adjustable risk-parameter mechanism (risk-mode? = True), where agents with incoming positive or negative pasture visibilities recalibrate herd sizes accordingly. The absence of homogeneous behavioral groups (homog-behav-types? = False) contributes to these divergent trajectories.

J-Stats across runs confirm the model captures adaptive complexity: type E-RO-SL1 typically retains 75–85% of initial risk attitudes, while LEARNING-ATT-INIT (purple) agents show bimodal distributions post-50 steps. These patterns emerge from the interaction between stochastic rainfall (rain-mean = 200, rain-std = 100), fixed grazing weights (w = 0.8), and adaptive learning mechanisms, demonstrating the original model’s goals of examining learning propagation in heterogeneous systems.
