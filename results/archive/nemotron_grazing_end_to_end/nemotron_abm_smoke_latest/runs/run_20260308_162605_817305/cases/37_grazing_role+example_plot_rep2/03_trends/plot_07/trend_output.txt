The Gini Coefficient of total livestock health starts near zero at step 0, indicating near-perfect equity in livestock health outcomes among all 60 households at the simulation's onset. This initial homogeneity is likely driven by three factors: all agents begin with identical herd sizes (90 livestock), baseline **behavioral-type**=E-RO (no prior knowledge), and the **fixed initial grazing** on private patches (**start-on-homepatch?=True**). The first 20–40 steps are dominated by convergence as agents respond to stochastic rainfall (std=100) and fixed **resting?=False** pasture dynamics. By step 30, the Gini increases to ~0.015–0.025 as risk-sensitive strategies (**risk-att-init=0.5**) cause divergent herd management choices: some households reduce grazing due to climate shocks, while others take risks to maintain herd size.

Between steps 40–60, social learning agents (**E-RO-SL1, n=20**) and learning-by-doing agents (**E-LBD, n=20**) outperform others. The Gini peaks at ~0.07–0.09 by step 90, reflecting extreme inequality. This spike coincides with heterogeneous rainfall impacts and the mitigation strategies of adaptive agents. For example, **E-RO-SL1** agents likely adopt successful neighbors' grazing schedules (within knowledge-radius=1.0), reducing health losses during droughts. Meanwhile, **E-LBD** agents adjust their own herd sizes based on past rainfall outcomes, creating wider gaps in health outcomes. The brown and green lines (risk-tolerant types) diverge sharply from the gray baselines (**E-RO**), driving higher variance in outcomes.

After step 90, the Gini declines to ~0.05–0.07 across nearly all runs. This convergence reflects a systemic shift: adaptive agents stabilize herd health through improved pasture management (e.g., timed resting after biomass drops due to **gr1=0.5** wet-season recovery), while non-learners (**E-RO**) experience compounding setbacks. The final plateau near 0.05–0.06 suggests a new equilibrium where behavioral heterogeneity persists but climate resilience stabilizes outcomes.

Time series variance is low before step 50 but increases by step 70 due to compounding effects of stochastic shocks and learning accumulation. The temporal stratification—early homogeneity, mid-simulation divergence due to adaptation strategies, and late convergence around a new equilibrium—aligns with models of social-ecological systems where learning and risk sensitivity reshape resource allocation over time. The absence of satisficing (threshold=0) and global strategy switching (**SL2-strategy-switch?=False**) limits behavioral homogenization, but climate-driven selection pressure ensures persistent performance differences.
