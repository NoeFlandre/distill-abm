The average cumulative destocked livestock begins at 0 herds per household and follows a gradual, nonlinear ascent over the simulation period. A notable acceleration occurs after approximately 20 timesteps, coinciding with the first stochastic rainfall-induced biomass degradation event. This spike reflects risk-sensitive adjustments by agents as pasture productivity declines. The curve exhibits cyclical fluctuations, with pronounced troughs at ~40 and ~70 timesteps correlating with stochastic rainfall recoveries that allow temporary herd recovery. By the final timestep, the mean cumulative destocking approaches 120 herds per household, suggesting persistent, cumulative management pressure in response to recurring environmental stressors. Strategies such as E-RO-SL1 demonstrate lagged responses, with destocking increases following social observation windows, while E-LBD agents show more immediate adjustments due to their learning-by-doing mechanism. The distribution widens after ~60 timesteps due to compounding uncertainty from variable rainfall, with high-variance simulations showing complete herd depletion in extreme cases. The pattern aligns with models of adaptive grazing where fixed behavioral rules (E-RO) lead to herd collapse under fluctuating conditions, while modified strategies incorporate dynamic thresholds for restocking and herd size reduction.
