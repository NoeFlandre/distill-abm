The mean-green-biomass trends show a prolonged period of stability during the first 20 years of the simulation, with values remaining near 45,000 units across all patches. This quasi-equilibrium suggests that initial agent decision-making under homogeneous E-RO behavioral types and climate variability (mean rainfall 200, std 100) establishes a baseline resource state. Starting around timestep 25, gradual declines emerge in most trajectories, with values dropping to 44,000–46,000 units by timestep 30. This phases into a period of heightened variability from timestep 35 onward, where biomass fluctuates between 40,000 and 50,000 units depending on the run. Two notable patterns emerge: 1) some trajectories experience sharp rebounds after declines (e.g., 44,000 to 46,000 units between timesteps 45–50), while 2) others show irreversible drops to 40,000 units by timestep 40, suggesting tipping points tied to grazing intensity during low-rainfall periods. The final timesteps (beyond 80) exhibit extreme divergence, with values spreading from 40,000 to over 50,000 units. This reflects the compounding effects of climatic stochasticity (rainfall std = 100) and E-RO decision rules: agents likely overstock during biomass recovery pauses, exacerbate declines during droughts, and understock during unexpected rainfall surges. The wide variance in late-stage outcomes underscores the sensitivity of social-ecological systems to feedback loops between environmental shocks and rational, myopic resource allocation strategies.
