The count of E-RO households remains remarkably stable at 20 across most simulations, mirroring their fixed behavioral type enforced by homogeneous conditions. In typical runs, this invariant population suggests a resilient equilibrium where baseline strategies resist fluctuations under climatic variability (rainfall mean=200, std=100). However, some simulations exhibit transient volatility: initial counts dip to 15–16 households by step 2 before recovering, followed by sustained stability. These drops may reflect acute stress from rainfall extremes triggering short-term adjustments (e.g., reduced grazing pressure) even among non-learning agents. Conversely, a subset of runs shows higher initial counts (up to 32 households) followed by sharp declines, suggesting localized tipping points where temporary resource overexploitation under extreme dryness forces further ecosystem constraints. Long-term stability across all simulations (with counts converging to 20 post-fluctuations) underscores E-RO’s consistency as a conservative strategy, unaffected by social or behavioral complexity. Risk-averse decision-making (risk-att-init=0.5) likely buffers against catastrophic herd collapses but does not prevent mild population dips during climatic stress. These dynamics imply that while homogeneous E-RO agents provide predictable stability, abrupt environmental shocks can induce fleeting destabilization, highlighting their vulnerability to climate variability despite strict fixed strategies.
