In **early-mid simulation years (approximately steps 1–25)**, deviations from recommended livestock stocking levels remain relatively small and stable, averaging near zero with minimal fluctuations. By **mid-simulation (approximately steps 26–50)**, deviations begin to widen across scenarios, with some households showing larger negative (understocking) or positive (overstocking) deviations. This period may indicate heightened sensitivity to climatic stressors (e.g., rainfall variability affecting vegetation growth), prompting divergent strategies to mitigate perceived risks. In **later years (approximately steps 51–100)**, deviations escalate further, reaching magnitudes exceeding ±0.3 in multiple runs. For instance, some agents exhibit extreme overstocking (+0.7) or understocking (~−0.35) deviations, reflecting amplified responses to climatic shocks or inertia in behavioral patterns. These trends highlight growing divergence over time, as households increasingly prioritize short-term survival or exploit favorable conditions, compounding resource pressures. Notably, the absence of social learning mechanisms (e.g., disabled strategy-switching) may limit cross-agent coordination, exacerbating individual deviations. For decision-makers, these patterns underscore the need to address environmental volatility and behavioral rigidity in promoting sustainable stocking practices.
