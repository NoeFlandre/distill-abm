The initial mean household risk attitudes across all households exhibit significant variability, with values ranging from positive (risk-seeking) to negative (risk-averse) levels before stabilizing. This diversity reflects agents' varied responses to uncertainty under homologous behavioral settings. Over the first 30 time steps, most households reduce their absolute risk attitudes, transitioning toward zero, suggesting adaptive adjustments to resource management dynamics. By 50–60 steps, deviations stabilize near neutral values, indicating equilibration of risk perceptions in response to persistent climatic variability.   A subset of households demonstrates transient oscillations in risk attitudes, marked by sharp dips or rises between steps 40–60. These fluctuations likely correspond to short-term stress responses (e.g., droughts or biomass thresholds) followed by recalibration to baseline decision-making patterns. Despite early divergence, long-term clustering around zero by step 100 implies convergence in strategic alignment with system stability. The observed decline in mean absolute deviation across time steps further underscores the system’s tendency to mitigate extreme risk-taking or overly conservative behavior in favor of balanced management.   The behavior of individual households reveals distinct adjustment trajectories. For instance, some agents exhibit rapid convergence within the initial 20 steps, while others oscillate before stabilizing. This heterogeneity in adaptation rates aligns with varying sensitivity to environmental feedback. Notably, the average magnitude of risk attitudes decreases monotonically after step 50, reinforcing the model’s capacity to stabilize communal decision-making despite inherent stochasticity in climate-induced resource availability.
