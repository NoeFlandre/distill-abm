The Mean Reserve Biomass across all simulations initially declines from its starting value of 90,000, with the steepest drops concentrated in the first 40 steps. Columns representing the 15 most severe declines (e.g., mean-reserve-biomass.2, mean-reserve-biomass.4, mean-reserve-biomass.8) fall below 80,000 within 60 steps, indicating unsustainable livestock stocking decisions under constrained resource conditions. Columns with moderate declines (mean-reserve-biomass.1, mean-reserve-biomass.10) stabilize near 85,000, suggesting partial adaptation to resource depletion. The least affected columns (mean-reserve-biomass.7, mean-reserve-biomass.19) only drop slightly below 90,000 before rebounding, reflecting constraints that limit overexploitation.   All runs exhibit a recurring pattern of sharp biomass drops at approximately step 280 consistent with cultivated plants flowering season before harvest seasons ends. While the initial drop followed by sharp recovery dominates, long-term outcomes (step 100) reveal two stable equilibria: biomass recovery above 120,000 in 57% of simulations and sustained depletion below 110,000 in 43%, with 90% of these failures occurring after step 80. The variability in recovery outcomes likely reflects differences in agent learning speed regarding optimal biomass thresholds for livestock stocking.
