The simulated count of households engaging in social learning exhibits a stepwise ascent until stabilization at approximately two-thirds of the total population. During the initial phase (steps 0–25), gradual adoption occurs as agents with social-learning capabilities (initially 20% of the population) implement their strategies. The accelerating phase (steps 25–50) reveals heightened interaction within the defined knowledge-radius (1.0), correlating with environmental stochasticity driven by global rainfall variability (std=100). Risk-sensitive agents (r=0.5) demonstrate adaptive trajectory shifts during drought events, with temporary spikes in social-learning adoption corresponding to periods of abrupt pasture degradation. Final stabilization at ~40 households (67%) aligns with the coexistence of risk mitigation through social strategies and constraints from the finite pasture capacity (Rmax=0.5). The emergence of observable heterogeneity in behavioral outcomes despite the homogeneous behavioral-type initialization suggests self-organized strategy dissemination through localized knowledge exchange. This pattern implies that social learning dynamics can emerge from a minimal initial pool when combined with adaptive feedbacks between climatic shocks, risk perception, and resource dependent behaviors.
