The Gini coefficient for total livestock health across households evolves from near-zero values at early time steps to reach its maximum asymptotic levels by 100 time steps. Initial periods (0–20 steps) show minimal divergence among simulation runs, with all distributions clustering tightly around 0.000. From steps 20–60, the coefficient increases exponentially in magnitude for most behavioral types, though E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 exhibit slower growth rates compared to risk-seeking variants (TRAD-max and TRAD-elevated). At steps 60–100, the curves flatten into asymptotic trajectories, with final values ranging between 0.045–0.095 depending on the strategy. Key observations: 1) Homogeneous E-RO populations stabilize at Gini ≈0.030, reflecting equilibrium under systematic rules. 2) Socially learning agents (E-RO-SL1) reach intermediate inequality levels (~0.045–0.060) due to network-dependent knowledge diffusion. 3) Risk-sensitive agents (high TRAD-max variants) achieve maximum inequality (0.090–0.095) through asymmetric herd-size adjustments. 4) Global rainfall variability amplifies divergence at steps 40–60, with Gini accelerations correlating with sudden biomass collapses. The final equilibrium values suggest that strategy propagation creates self-reinforcing disparity patterns compatible with the model's risk-learning semantics.
