The data reveals that mean-reserve-biomass trends under homogeneous E-RO agents exhibit significant variability across runs, driven by environmental stochasticity and risk-related decision-making. All agents follow the same baseline strategy (E-RO) but display divergent trajectories due to interactions with climatic variability (rain-mean=200, rain-std=100) and risk attitudes (risk-att-init=0.5). Key observations include:

1. Initial decline followed by divergent recovery: Most runs show an initial dip to ~85,000-90,000 biomass, likely from overstocking under high-livestock initial conditions (livestock-init=90). However, some simulations recover to >127,000 reserves, demonstrating potential for resilience despite identical behavioral programming.

2. Stochastic environmental impacts: The high rainfall variability (std=100) acts as a critical filter, with final biomass values clustering around 115,000-127,000 in successful runs versus <95,000 in unsuccessful ones. This reflects the model's sensitivity to climatic extremes, where extreme precipitation events significantly influence long-term sustainability.

3. Risk modulation effects: The risk-att-init=0.5 threshold appears to moderate agent responses to environmental uncertainty. Runs with prolonged low-biomass periods (<85,000) suggest instances where risk-averse adjustments failed to prevent depletion, while resilient runs maintained adaptive stocking levels that avoided overshoot.

4. No homogeneous equilibrium: Despite homog-behav-types=1 (all agents E-RO) and social learning disabled, no stable universal outcome emerges. Final biomass values span 89,000-128,000, indicating solution multiplicity under identical behavioral settings - a key insight for understanding state-dependent dynamics in grazing systems.

The absence of SL2-strategy-switch (False) and fixed intrinsic-preference=0 further confirms that observed variability stems solely from environmental shocks and risk-adjusted behavioral responses, rather than strategy-form learning or coordination mechanisms.
