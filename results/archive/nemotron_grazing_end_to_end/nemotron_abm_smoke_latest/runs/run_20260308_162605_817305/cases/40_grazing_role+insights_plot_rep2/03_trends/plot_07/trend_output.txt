The Gini coefficient for 'Total Livestock Healthy' increases steadily from nearly 0 to over 0.09 across all simulations after an initial stabilization phase (~20-30 steps). Early inequality growth corresponds with the equilibrium establishment of pasture resource allocation patterns among households. By step 50-60, rates of inequality acceleration diverge significantly among behavioral types, reflecting heterogeneity in adaptation strategies. Learning-intensive types (E-RO-SL1) demonstrate steeper early Gini increases (0.04-0.06 by step 30) compared to traditional rule-followers (E-RO, peaking at 0.03-0.05 post week 60). Risk-sensitive agents (risk-att-init=0.5) exhibit intermediate trajectories, showing reduced acceleration post step 70 as herd management strategies stabilize. Long-term trajectories plateau between 0.04-0.06 across all types after step 90, despite continued rainfall variability. These patterns align with the model's configuration - homogeneous agent groups coexisting under randomized climate stress reveal inherent system vulnerabilities to initial stochastic allocation patterns. Simulations with active knowledge radius constraints show localized rather than systematic inequality patterns, suggesting social learning amplifies uneven resource distribution within clusters. For decision makers, the results underscore risks of static management approaches in spatially bounded systems with stochastic stressors, particularly when learning strategies reduce baseline cooperation thresholds.
