The data reveals a gradual adoption of social learning behaviors among households over time, with adoption rates increasing steadily until approximately 35-40 households (out of 60 total) engage in social learning by the later time steps. Key observations include:   1. **Initial Phase (Time Steps 0-19):** No households engaged in social learning, consistent with the parameter settings that disable satisficing and social learning susceptibility for non-learning agent types (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 initially inactive).   2. **Acceleration Phase (Time Steps 20-29):** A marked increase begins at time step 20 (first non-zero values), reaching a plateau near 35-42 households by the final time steps. This suggests a tipping point where sufficient neighbors' adoption enabled cascading social learning, amplified by the knowledge-radius of 1.0.   3. **Plateau Dynamics:** The plateau reflects equilibrium in learning adoption, constrained by the limited behavioral heterogeneity (hygomogeneous types enabled) and risk sensitivity (risk-att-init=0.5). Social learning via E-RO-SL1 agents likely stabilized as they maxed out information dissemination within their immediate spatial network.   4. **Implications for Adaptive Management:** The delayed adoption (20-year tipping point) aligns with the model's focus on long-term ecological resilience, indicating that social learning diffusion requires critical mass of proactive agents. However, the plateau suggests that baseline risk tolerance (0.5) and spatial constraints may limit full-scale adoption, even with active learning strategies.   5. **Contextual Drivers:** The absence of satisficing behavior (satisficing-threshold=0) and active resting mechanisms shifted focus to strategic adaptations, where social learning compensated for unmanaged pasture degradation. Randomized rainfall variability likely exacerbated pasture stress, incentivizing households to adopt learning strategies to mitigate uncertainty.
