The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear trend over the simulation period. Initially, the count starts at zero and begins to increase steadily. The early phase of the simulation, up to around 1,200 ticks, shows a rapid rise in the number of females killed, indicating an initial period of intense hunting activity. This phase is characterized by several inflection points, suggesting changes in the rate of increase. The most notable inflection points occur around ticks 245, 262, and 285, indicating shifts in the hunting dynamics. 

Following this initial phase, the rate of increase in the number of females killed begins to stabilize. Between ticks 1,200 and 2,000, the trend continues to rise but at a slower and more consistent rate. This period is marked by fewer inflection points, suggesting a more stable hunting pattern. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms this trend, showing significant increasing trends in multiple windows, with the slope of the increase gradually decreasing over time. 

Towards the latter part of the simulation, around tick 1,856, there is a notable change point where the trend shifts from increasing to decreasing. This indicates a potential reduction in hunting activity or a stabilization in the number of females killed. The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of 63 ticks, suggesting a cyclical pattern in the hunting activity towards the end of the simulation. The peaks and valleys identified in the data further support this cyclical behavior, with minor fluctuations around the mean value of approximately 119 females killed. 

Overall, the simulation shows an initial rapid increase in the number of female prey animals hunted, followed by a stabilization period, and eventually a slight decrease or stabilization towards the end. The trends and patterns observed provide insights into the dynamics of hunting pressure and its impact on the female prey population within the model.
