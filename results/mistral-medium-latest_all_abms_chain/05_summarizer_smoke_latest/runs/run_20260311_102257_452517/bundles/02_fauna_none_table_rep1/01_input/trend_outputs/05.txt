The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a general increasing trend over the simulation period. Initially, the count starts at zero and gradually rises, reflecting the accumulation of hunted postpartum females over time. The early phase of the simulation, up to around tick 475, shows a steady increase in the count. This phase is characterized by a consistent upward trend, indicating a relatively stable rate of hunting. Following this, there are notable change points around ticks 625 and 850, where the rate of increase begins to slow down, suggesting a reduction in the hunting rate or a stabilization in the number of postpartum females being hunted. As the simulation progresses beyond these change points, the cumulative count continues to rise but at a more gradual pace, indicating a slower rate of accumulation. The latter part of the simulation, particularly after tick 2310, shows minor oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, suggesting periodic fluctuations in the hunting rate. The peaks and valleys identified in the data indicate minor variations around the overall trend, but these do not significantly deviate from the general increasing pattern. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms these observations, with significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, and a notable shift to a decreasing trend towards the end, indicating a potential reduction in hunting activity or a stabilization in the population dynamics. Overall, the data suggests a cumulative increase in the number of postpartum female prey animals hunted over time, with periods of stabilization and minor fluctuations reflecting the complex interactions within the model.
