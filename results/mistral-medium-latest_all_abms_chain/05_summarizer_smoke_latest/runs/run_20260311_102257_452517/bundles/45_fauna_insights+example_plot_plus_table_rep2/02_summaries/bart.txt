 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbivores . The model simulates a simplified grassland environment with human hunters and male and female ungulates . The landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, consisting of a 211x211 patch matrix .  The model allows users to manipulate these parameters to test various hypotheses related to extinction events . The temporal units used in the model are 'ticks,' which can be scaled to any temporal granularity . The number of human hunter-gatherer bands in the simulation remains constant at 20 throughout the entire duration . The model includes summary statistics that are shown as continuous line graphs, reflecting data from each tick .  The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at zero and increases rapidly in the first 500 time steps . The rate of increase in deaths slows down significantly after this initial period, indicating a potential stabilization in the population dynamics . For decision makers, these trends suggest that initial hunting efforts are highly effective, but over time, the system reaches a point of diminishing returns . Understanding these dynamics can help design interventions to manage wildlife populations .  The number of female animals in the simulation starts at 125 and exhibits a rapid decline within the first 500 time steps . This sharp decrease suggests a high initial predation or mortality rate, possibly due to the combined effects of human hunting pressure and environmental factors . By around 2000 time steps, the number of male animals approaches zero, indicating near-complete extinction within the simulated environment . Mann-Kendall analysis confirms a consistent trend in the overall decline .  The total energy of all animals in the simulation starts at approximately 16,667 units . This high initial value indicates a large and healthy population of prey animals with substantial energy reserves .
