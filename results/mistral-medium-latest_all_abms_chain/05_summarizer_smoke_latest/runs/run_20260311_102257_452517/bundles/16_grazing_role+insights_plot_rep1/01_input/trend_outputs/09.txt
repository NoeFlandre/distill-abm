The trends in the data from the repeated simulations of the agent-based model show distinct patterns in the mean destock total of households over 100 steps. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the mean destock total within the first 20 steps, where the values rise steeply from near zero to approximately 60. This sharp increase indicates a significant initial adjustment period where households are actively managing their livestock numbers, likely in response to initial ecological and climatic conditions. 

Following this initial phase, the rate of increase in the mean destock total begins to slow down. Between steps 20 and 40, the growth continues but at a more moderate pace, reaching around 80. This suggests that households are stabilizing their livestock numbers as they adapt to the conditions and possibly learning from their experiences and interactions. 

From step 40 onwards, the mean destock total continues to rise but at an even slower rate, indicating a phase of gradual adjustment. By step 100, the values range between approximately 80 and 160, showing a wide variability in the final destock totals among different simulations. This variability implies that while there is a general trend of increasing destocking over time, the specific outcomes can vary significantly based on the interactions of various parameters and stochastic elements in the model. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial management strategies and the potential benefits of adaptive learning in stabilizing livestock numbers over time.
