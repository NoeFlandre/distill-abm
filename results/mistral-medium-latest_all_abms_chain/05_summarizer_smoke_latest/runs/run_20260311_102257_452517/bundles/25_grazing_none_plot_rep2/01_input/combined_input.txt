The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system. The extension adds learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, to study adaptive management strategies. The model's context is rooted in understanding the long-term social-ecological consequences of different household behavioral types and their decision-making processes under various ecological and climatic conditions. The goal is to evaluate how different behavioral types and learning strategies impact the resilience and sustainability of pastoral systems. The model allows for the adjustment of a range of parameters to observe their effects on social, ecological, and economic outcomes. The model is used to compare the performance of different behavioral types and learning strategies, as well as to study the diffusion of learning behaviors in a heterogeneous population of agents. The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making, which are linked by a yearly sequence of processes. The model parameters are divided into extension model parameters and original model parameters used by the extension model. The extension model parameters include: extension-model?, risk-mode?, number-households, timesteps, b, knowledge-radius, homog-behav-types?, number-E-RO, number-E-LBD, number-E-RO-SL1, rain, SL2-strategy-switch?, and behavioral-type. The original model parameters used by the extension model include: w, gr1, gr2, rue, mg, mr, R0part, lambda, Rmax, and intake. The model is used to study the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of various learning strategies, the impact of social learning double-loop (strategy switching), and the diffusion of learning behaviors. The model is based on the following references: Dressler et al. (2018), Dressler et al. (2012), Martin et al. (2014), Müller et al. (2007), and Schulze (2011).
