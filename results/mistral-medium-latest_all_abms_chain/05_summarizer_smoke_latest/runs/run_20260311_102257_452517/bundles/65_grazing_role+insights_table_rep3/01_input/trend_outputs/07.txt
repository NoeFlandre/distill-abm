The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all households, as measured by the Gini coefficient, exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period. Initially, the Gini coefficient starts at a value of approximately 0, indicating perfect equality in the distribution of healthy livestock. Over time, the inequality gradually increases, reaching a value of approximately 0.0726 by the end of the simulation at time step 100. The mean value of the Gini coefficient over the entire period is 0.0302, with a median value of 0.0229, suggesting that the distribution is slightly skewed towards higher inequality values. The standard deviation of 0.0239 indicates some variability in the inequality levels over time. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a significant increasing trend in inequality across all windows, with a consistent positive slope. Several inflection points are detected, suggesting changes in the rate of increase in inequality at various time steps. Additionally, there are multiple oscillation indices towards the latter part of the simulation, indicating some periodic fluctuations in the rate of change of inequality. For decision makers, these trends suggest that over time, the distribution of healthy livestock becomes increasingly unequal among households. This could imply that certain households are accumulating more livestock while others are falling behind, which may have implications for the overall sustainability and equity of the grazing system.
