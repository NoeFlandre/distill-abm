 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households . The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems .  Simulation of agent-based model shows distinct trends in the average total number of livestock across all households over the 100 timesteps . For decision makers, these trends highlight the resilience and recovery capacity of the grazing system over time . The initial decrease in biomass underscores the immediate impact of grazing, while the subsequent recovery and growth suggest effective adaptive management and ecological resilience .  Trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a significant increasing trend across all windows, with high tau values and significant p-values . For decision makers, this trend suggests that social learning behaviors can diffuse significantly within a population of households over time, even in the absence of specific interventions .
