 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices . The model includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where influence is a function of interaction probability and agent susceptibility .  The statistical analysis indicates several key inflection points and periods of oscillation, which highlight the dynamic nature of consumer adoption behaviors . For decision makers, this trend implies a steady increase in the adoption of whole milk choices, which could be influenced by the agents' habits, social influence, and perceived characteristics of milk types .  The trend of decreasing whole milk consumption is statistically significant in several time windows, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall test . The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents starts at approximately 5.29 units . By around the 10th time step, the mean quantity reaches approximately 1265.47 units, indicating a significant shift in consumer behavior .  The trend analysis shows a consistent increasing trend across all rolling Mann-Kendall windows, with significant increases in deviation throughout the simulation period . For decision makers, this implies that interventions or campaigns promoting skimmed and semi-skimmed milk are likely to see increasing effectiveness over time .
