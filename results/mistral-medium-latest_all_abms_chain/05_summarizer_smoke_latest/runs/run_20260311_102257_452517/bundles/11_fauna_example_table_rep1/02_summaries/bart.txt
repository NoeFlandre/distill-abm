 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbivores . The model simulates a simplified grassland environment with human hunters and male and female ungulates . The landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, consisting of a 211x211 patch matrix .  The model allows users to manipulate these parameters to test various hypotheses related to extinction events . The temporal units used in the model are 'ticks,' which can be scaled to any temporal granularity . The model includes summary statistics that are shown as continuous line graphs, reflecting data from each tick . These statistics include the number of prey items taken per 10 ticks, the proportion of grazed to ungrazed grass, forager energy, and the number animals in the environment .  The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands begins at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the early time steps . By around 245 ticks, the rate of increase starts to show signs of slowing down, marking the first inflection point . The oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation suggest some periodic variability in the hunting rate .  The number of female prey animals in the simulation begins at 125 and exhibits a rapid decline in the initial phase . By 600 ticks, the population has significantly reduced, marking a critical change point in the trend . The oscillation indices indicate periodic fluctuations, but these are minor compared to the overall declining trend . By the final time step, the prey population reaches zero, indicating the complete extinction .  Total energy available in prey animals within the simulation begins at approximately 16,667 units . The energy levels eventually approach zero by the end of the simulation at tick 2311, indicating a complete depletion of energy resources among the female prey population . The overall trend is a consistent decrease in energy levels, with minor oscillations, reflecting a steady decline in the energy available to female prey animals .
