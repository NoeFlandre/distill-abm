The number of male animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) simulations exhibits a clear and consistent declining trend over time. Initially, the number of male animals starts at 125 and shows a rapid decrease in the early stages of the simulation. By around 250 steps, the population has significantly reduced, continuing this downward trajectory sharply until approximately 500 steps, where the number of male animals drops to lower values. 

Throughout the mid-stages of the simulation, from around 500 to 1500 steps, the decline in the number of male animals becomes more gradual but remains consistent. The population continues to decrease steadily, with occasional minor fluctuations that do not alter the overall downward trend. These fluctuations are relatively small and short-lived, indicating temporary stabilization points rather than any significant recovery in the population. 

In the later stages of the simulation, beyond 1500 steps, the number of male animals approaches zero. The population continues to decline until it eventually reaches extinction, with no male animals remaining by the end of the simulation period. The statistical analysis confirms this trend, showing significant decreasing trends in multiple rolling windows and identifying several change points that mark the stages of rapid decline. 

For decision-makers, these trends suggest that under the given parameters and conditions of the MHPM, the male animal population is highly susceptible to extinction due to the combined pressures of hunting and environmental factors. The rapid initial decline indicates a critical period where intervention could be most impactful. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing conservation strategies that address the key factors driving the decline, such as adjusting hunting pressures or enhancing habitat conditions to support the population.
