 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households . The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems .  The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households, starts at 90 . The average livestock count across the simulation period is about 48.79, with a standard deviation of 9.57 . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial adjustments in managing livestock under varying environmental conditions .  The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households begins at zero and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period . The trend is characterized by a rapid initial increase, with the first significant rise occurring within the first 10 time steps, where the slope of the increase is quite steep . After time step 33, the trend shows a very gradual increase, eventually reaching a peak of approximately 32 households around time step 63 .
