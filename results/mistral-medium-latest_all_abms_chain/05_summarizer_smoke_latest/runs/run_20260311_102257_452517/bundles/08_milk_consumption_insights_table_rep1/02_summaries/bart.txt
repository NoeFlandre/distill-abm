 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where influence is a function of interaction probability and agent susceptibility . The simulation shows distinct trends in the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent . Understanding these dynamics can help in strategizing interventions or policies aimed at influencing milk consumption patterns .  Simulation results show a consistent and significant increasing trend in the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk by agents over the observed time period . For decision makers, these trends imply that interventions or policies aimed at promoting healthier milk choices can leverage the observed social influence and habit formation dynamics to accelerate adoption rates .  The mean factor applied to the whole milk choice function due to habit shows a consistent increasing trend over the simulation period from 1974 to 2005 . For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of early interventions and consistent reinforcement of health messages to drive significant and lasting changes in consumer behavior .  The mean deviation between the agents' held environmental values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.0000 and exhibits a rapid increase in the early time steps, reaching a peak value of approximately 13.4756 by the 13th time step . The rolling Mann-Kendall test results show an initial increasing trend that is statistically significant, followed by periods with no significant trend .
