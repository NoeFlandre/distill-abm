The simulation of the agent-based model depicting the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 shows distinct trends in the mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents. Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of 5.29 units. This is followed by a rapid and significant increasing trend in the early time steps, reaching up to approximately 1265.47 units around the first peak. This initial surge indicates a swift shift in consumer preferences, likely driven by early adoption behaviors influenced by perceived health benefits and social norms. Following this peak, the consumption exhibits minor fluctuations with several smaller peaks and valleys, suggesting periods of stabilization and slight adjustments in consumer habits. Key peaks are observed at values around 1240.35 units and 1226.86 units, while notable valleys are seen at approximately 1233.62 units and 1209.70 units. These oscillations imply intermittent periods where the consumption slightly dips, potentially due to varying social influences or temporary shifts in consumer perceptions. Towards the latter part of the simulation, there is a resurgence in the increasing trend, culminating in the highest consumption value of 1456.60 units. This final increase suggests a strong and sustained adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, reinforcing the long-term impact of health perceptions and social conformity on milk consumption habits. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of early interventions and consistent reinforcement of health messages to drive significant and lasting changes in consumer behavior.
