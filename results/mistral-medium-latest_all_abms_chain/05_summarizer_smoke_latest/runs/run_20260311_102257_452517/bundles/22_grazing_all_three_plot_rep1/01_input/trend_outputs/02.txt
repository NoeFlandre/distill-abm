The mean household risk attitude starts with a value of 0.5 across all simulations. In the initial 20 time steps, there is a rapid divergence in risk attitudes among households. Some households increase their risk attitude sharply to values around 0.8, while others decrease to values as low as -0.2. This divergence indicates that households quickly adapt their risk attitudes based on initial experiences and learning mechanisms. By around 20 time steps, the risk attitudes begin to stabilize, with distinct groups forming based on their risk tolerance levels. Households with higher risk attitudes tend to stabilize around 0.7 to 0.8, while those with lower risk attitudes stabilize around 0.2 to 0.4. A small group of households exhibits negative risk attitudes, stabilizing around -0.2. From time step 20 to 100, the risk attitudes remain relatively stable within these formed groups, indicating that households have settled into their respective risk attitude behaviors. The stabilization of risk attitudes suggests that households have reached a steady state in their decision-making processes, influenced by their learning experiences and interactions within the grazing system. For decision makers, the rapid initial divergence highlights the importance of early interventions to guide household risk attitudes towards desirable outcomes. The stabilization of risk attitudes into distinct groups suggests that tailored strategies may be necessary to address the varying risk tolerance levels among households. Understanding these trends can help in designing policies that promote sustainable grazing practices and resilient household livelihoods.
