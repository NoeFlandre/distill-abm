The mean destock total of households starts at 0 and increases rapidly within the first 20 steps, reaching approximately 60. This initial rapid increase suggests that households are quickly reducing their livestock numbers, possibly due to immediate environmental pressures or initial adjustments to the grazing system. Following this rapid increase, the rate of destocking slows down significantly, indicating a period of stabilization where households may be adapting to the new conditions and finding a balance in their livestock management practices. Between steps 20 and 100, the mean destock total continues to increase but at a much slower and more steady rate, reaching values between 80 and 160 by the end of the simulation. This gradual increase suggests ongoing but less drastic adjustments in livestock numbers, possibly due to long-term adaptive strategies and learning behaviors among households. The variability among different simulation runs increases over time, indicating that while initial destocking actions are relatively uniform, long-term strategies diverge based on household-specific factors and learning experiences. For decision makers, the initial rapid destocking highlights the need for immediate support and interventions to help households manage initial shocks. The subsequent stabilization and gradual increase in destocking suggest the importance of long-term adaptive strategies and the potential benefits of learning and knowledge-sharing among households. Understanding these trends can help in designing policies that support both immediate adjustments and long-term sustainability in grazing systems.
