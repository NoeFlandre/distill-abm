The simulations of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) show a consistent trend across all runs. Initially, the total number of animals starts at 250, which is the set value for the initial number of prey. In the early stages, up to around 500 steps, there is a gradual decline in the number of animals. This decline accelerates significantly between 500 and 1500 steps, where the number of animals drops sharply from approximately 200 to near zero. By around 2000 steps, the number of animals in most simulations reaches zero, indicating a complete extinction event. 

The rapid decline in the number of animals suggests that the combined effects of hunting pressure, environmental conditions, and internal mortality rates are substantial enough to drive the population to extinction within a relatively short time frame. The initial gradual decline could be attributed to the natural mortality rates and the initial hunting pressure. The sharp decline phase likely represents a tipping point where the population can no longer sustain itself due to the cumulative effects of these pressures. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight the critical need to consider the interplay between hunting pressures and environmental factors in conservation strategies. The rapid extinction observed in the simulations underscores the vulnerability of megaherbivore populations under sustained hunting pressure and environmental stress. This information can be crucial for developing policies aimed at sustainable hunting practices and habitat conservation to prevent the extinction of vulnerable species.
