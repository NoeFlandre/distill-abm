 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households . The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems .  Simulation of agent-based model reveals key trends in the average total number of livestock across all households over the 100 timesteps . These trends highlight the initial stress on biomass likely due to grazing activities, followed by a recovery phase that could be attributed to adaptive management practices . Understanding these trends can inform policies aimed at sustainable grazing practices and resilient household livelihoods .  Understanding the inflection and change points can help in identifying critical periods where policy interventions might be most effective . For decision makers, this trend suggests a growing adoption of social learning behaviors among households over time, which could imply increasing collaboration and knowledge sharing within the community .
