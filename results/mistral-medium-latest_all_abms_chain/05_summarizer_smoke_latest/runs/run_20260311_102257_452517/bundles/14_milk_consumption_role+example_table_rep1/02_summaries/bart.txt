 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent begins at a high value of 2654.81 units . The consumption then exhibits a rapid decline in the early time steps, reflecting a significant shift in consumption patterns . This decline could be attributed to agents becoming more disposed to consider alternative milk types due to changing perceptions, social influences, or other factors modeled in the simulation .  The total number of milk choices made by agents combined starts at 0 and increases steadily over the entire simulation period, reaching a final value of 32,000 . There are no detected peaks or valleys, indicating a smooth and continuous increase without significant fluctuations . The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this increasing trend across multiple windows, with significant positive slopes and p-values .  The overall trend shows a significant upward trajectory in the consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, reflecting a successful shift in consumer preferences over the simulated period . The overall mean of the habit factor across all time steps is 1.0359, with a median value of 1.2578 . The rolling Mann-Kendall test results confirm this increasing trend across multiple windows .  The mean deviation between the agents' held environmental values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.0000 . It increases rapidly in the initial time steps, reaching a value of approximately 13.4756 by the 13th time step . After this sharp rise, the deviation stabilizes around a mean value of 12.6015 .
