 The Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbiivores . Model considers various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, sexual dimorphism, and life history of animals . The model allows users to manipulate various parameters to establish the socio-natural dynamics of the experiment matrix .  The model's landscape consists of an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, existing on a 211x211 patch matrix with a relative spatial scale . Ungulate herds are sexually dimorphic, with distinct food values and processing costs for males and females . Hunters are embedded with logic approximating dynamic optimal foraging theory, making foraging decisions based on environmental feedback .  The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population starts at zero and remains constant until around 800 time steps . By around 1000 time steps, the number of postpartum females killed stabilizes, fluctuating slightly around 120 . This stabilization suggests that a balance has been reached where the remaining post partum females are either less accessible to hunters or have adapted behaviors to reduce their vulnerability . The number of male animals in the simulation begins at approximately 120 individuals .  The total number of animals starts at 250, which is the initial number of prey set in the model . This number declines rapidly over the first 500 time steps, with a steep drop observed . The decline continues more gradually, with the number approaching zero around 2000 time steps . There is low variance across simulation runs initially, but deviations become more noticeable as the number of steps increases . The variability among simulation runs increases slightly after the initial decline .  The percentage of green patches then stabilizes again, maintaining a steady state until around the 900th time step . At this point, another sharp decline occurs, with the percentage dropping to approximately 28% . This second decline may indicate another surge in grazing activity or a reduction in grass regrowth rates .
