 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents begins at a low value of 0.0074 . For decision makers, these trends imply that while initial household behaviors may lead to significant deviations from recommended practices, there is a tendency towards stabilization over time .  The total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, begins at 90 and exhibits a rapid decline in the initial phase of the simulation . The overall trend indicates a significant recovery and growth in biomass after the initial decline . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial resource management and the potential for periodic adjustments in livestock numbers .  The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 10 time steps, reaching approximately 14 households . The trend continues to rise more gradually, reaching around 32 households by time step 63 . The gradual increase in inequality suggests that interventions may be needed to support households that are falling behind in livestock accumulation .
