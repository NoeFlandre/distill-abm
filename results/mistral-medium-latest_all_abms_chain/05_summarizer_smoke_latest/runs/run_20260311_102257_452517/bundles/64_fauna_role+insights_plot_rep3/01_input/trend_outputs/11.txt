The total energy of male animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at a high value of approximately 12,000 units. This initial high energy level indicates a healthy and robust population of male prey animals at the beginning of the simulation. 

In the early stages, up to around 500 steps, there is a steep decline in the total energy, dropping to about 2,000 units. This rapid decrease suggests a significant reduction in the male population's energy reserves, potentially due to high hunting pressure, environmental factors, or a combination of both. Decision makers should note that this period is critical, as it indicates a rapid depletion of energy resources among male animals, which could lead to increased vulnerability and potential population decline. 

Following this steep decline, from approximately 500 to 1,500 steps, the total energy continues to decrease but at a slower rate, stabilizing around 500 units. This phase indicates a period of adjustment where the remaining male animals may have adapted to the pressures, but their energy levels are still being depleted. This trend suggests that while the initial shock has passed, the population is still under significant stress. 

Beyond 1,500 steps, the total energy of male animals approaches near-zero levels and remains there for the rest of the simulation. This indicates that the male population has been nearly or completely depleted, with very few, if any, male animals remaining in the simulation. For decision makers, this implies that the conditions set in the model lead to a near-total extinction of male animals over time, highlighting the severe impact of the simulated pressures on the male population.
