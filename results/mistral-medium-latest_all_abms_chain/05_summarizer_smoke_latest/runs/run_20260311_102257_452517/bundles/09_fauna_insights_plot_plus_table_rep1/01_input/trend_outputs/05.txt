The cumulative number of postpartum females killed in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a rapid increase initially, followed by a gradual stabilization. Initially, the number of postpartum females killed rises sharply from 0 to approximately 110 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase indicates a high hunting rate of postpartum females by foragers during the early stages of the simulation. Following this initial surge, the rate of increase slows down significantly, and the cumulative number of postpartum females killed stabilizes around 117. This stabilization suggests that the hunting pressure reaches a point of equilibrium where the number of postpartum females killed does not change drastically over time. The data shows minor fluctuations around the mean value of 102.44, with a standard deviation of 30.00, indicating some variability but overall stability in the long-term trend. The median value of 116.86 further supports the observation that the number of postpartum females killed tends to stabilize at higher values after the initial rapid increase. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points suggests periods where the rate of increase shifts, but these changes are less pronounced as the simulation progresses. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results indicate significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with a shift to no significant trend and even a slight decreasing trend towards the end. This analysis implies that while initial hunting pressure is high and impactful, over time, the system reaches a form of equilibrium where the rate of postpartum females killed plateaus, providing insights for decision-makers on the dynamics of hunting pressure and its long-term effects on the prey population.
