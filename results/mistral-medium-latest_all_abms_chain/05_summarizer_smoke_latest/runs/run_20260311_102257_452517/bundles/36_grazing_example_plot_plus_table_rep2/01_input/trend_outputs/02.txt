The average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents starts at approximately 0.0074. It then increases rapidly, reaching a peak value of around 0.3814 at around 33 time steps. This initial rise indicates a growing deviation from the recommended livestock numbers as households adapt their strategies and learn from their experiences and neighbors. Following this peak, the deviation slightly fluctuates but generally trends downward, stabilizing around a value of 0.1911 by the end of the simulation at 100 time steps. The mean deviation across the entire simulation period is 0.2333, with a median of 0.2013, suggesting that while there are periods of higher deviation, the central tendency remains moderate. The standard deviation of 0.0936 indicates some variability in the deviation levels across different time steps. The initial increase in deviation is statistically significant, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall test, which shows a strong increasing trend in the early time steps. This trend shifts to a significant decreasing trend after reaching the peak, suggesting a correction phase where households adjust their livestock numbers closer to the recommended values. The presence of multiple inflection points and oscillation indices indicates periods of adjustment and stabilization in household risk attitudes. The change points at 50 and 100 time steps further highlight significant shifts in the trend, marking key phases in the adaptive management process.
