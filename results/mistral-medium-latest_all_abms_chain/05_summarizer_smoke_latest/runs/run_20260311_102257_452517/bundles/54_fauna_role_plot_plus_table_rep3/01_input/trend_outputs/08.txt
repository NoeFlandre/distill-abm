The number of male animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) simulations exhibits a clear and consistent declining trend over time. Initially, the number of male animals starts at 125 and shows a rapid decrease in the early stages of the simulation. This decline continues steadily, with the population of male animals decreasing significantly within the first 1000 steps. By around 2300 steps, the number of male animals approaches zero, indicating a near-complete extinction within the simulated timeframe. The statistical analysis reveals several key points and inflections, but the overall trend remains a consistent decrease. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this decreasing trend across multiple windows, with significant negative slopes indicating a steady decline in the population of male animals. The presence of multiple inflection points suggests periods where the rate of decline changes, but the overall trajectory remains downward. The model's parameters, such as high initial prey numbers, specific energy costs and gains for hunters, and environmental factors, contribute to the observed extinction dynamics. The simulation effectively captures the impact of hunting pressure and environmental conditions on the male animal population, leading to their eventual extinction within the given timeframe.
