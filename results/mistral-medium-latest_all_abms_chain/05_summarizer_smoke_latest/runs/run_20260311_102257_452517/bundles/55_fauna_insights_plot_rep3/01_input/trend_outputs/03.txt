The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands exhibits a rapid increase initially, followed by a gradual plateau. In the early stages, up to approximately 500 steps, the number of males killed rises steeply from 0 to around 80. This rapid increase indicates a high initial hunting success rate or a high initial population of male prey. Between 500 and 1000 steps, the rate of increase begins to slow, with the number of males killed reaching around 100 to 110. This suggests that either the male prey population is being depleted, or the foragers are encountering limitations in their hunting efficiency. Beyond 1000 steps, the curve starts to plateau, indicating that the number of males killed is stabilizing. By around 1500 steps, the cumulative number of males killed reaches approximately 120 and remains relatively constant thereafter. This plateau implies that a balance has been reached where the rate of males being killed is roughly equal to the rate at which new males are entering the population or becoming available for hunting. For decision makers, this trend suggests that initial hunting efforts are highly effective but that sustained hunting pressure leads to a stabilization in the number of males killed, potentially indicating a balance between hunting pressure and prey availability.
