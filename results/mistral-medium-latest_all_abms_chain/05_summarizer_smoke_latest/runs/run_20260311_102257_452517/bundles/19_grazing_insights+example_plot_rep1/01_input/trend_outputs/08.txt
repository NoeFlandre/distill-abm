The count of households engaging in social learning begins at zero and exhibits a rapid increase within the first 20 time steps. This initial surge indicates a quick adoption phase where households start to learn from each other almost immediately. By around 20 time steps, the number of households involved in social learning stabilizes across different simulation runs, with values ranging between approximately 12 and 54 households. This wide range suggests variability in the adoption rates of social learning behaviors among households. The stabilization at different levels implies that certain groups of households may reach a saturation point in adopting social learning practices earlier than others. For decision makers, this trend highlights the importance of initial conditions and early interventions in promoting social learning. Understanding the factors that contribute to the rapid adoption phase and the subsequent stabilization can help in designing policies that encourage widespread and sustained engagement in social learning practices. The variability in the number of households adopting social learning also underscores the need for tailored approaches that consider the diverse characteristics and behaviors of different household groups.
