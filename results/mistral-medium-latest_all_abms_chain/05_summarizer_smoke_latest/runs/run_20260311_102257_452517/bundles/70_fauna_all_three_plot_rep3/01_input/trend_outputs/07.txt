The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 200 deaths. The steep slope suggests that the prey population is highly vulnerable initially, possibly due to the sudden introduction of predators and the natural mortality factors. 

Following this rapid increase, the rate of deaths slows down significantly, and the cumulative number of deaths gradually approaches an asymptote around 250 deaths by approximately 1500 time steps. This plateau suggests that the prey population has stabilized, with the remaining individuals being those that have either adapted to the predation pressure or are less susceptible to the mortality factors. The stabilization could also indicate that the prey population has reached a balance with the carrying capacity of the environment and the predation pressure. 

The trends observed in the cumulative number of deaths provide insights for decision-makers regarding the dynamics of prey population under predation and natural mortality. The eventual stabilization of deaths suggests that the system reaches a new equilibrium, which could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices and habitat management to ensure the long-term survival of the prey species.
