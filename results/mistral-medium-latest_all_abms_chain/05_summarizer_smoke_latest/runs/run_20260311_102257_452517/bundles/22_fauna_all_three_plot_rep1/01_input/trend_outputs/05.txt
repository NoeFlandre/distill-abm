The cumulative number of postpartum females killed starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 100. This rapid initial increase indicates a high vulnerability of postpartum females to hunting pressure immediately after giving birth, likely due to reduced mobility and increased visibility. The rate of increase then slows down significantly, with the cumulative number stabilizing around 120 postpartum females killed by approximately 1500 time steps. This stabilization suggests that the initial susceptible population of postpartum females has been largely depleted, and the remaining population may have adapted or become less accessible to hunters. The low variance among simulation runs indicates a consistent pattern in the hunting dynamics across different scenarios. For decision-makers, this trend highlights the critical period immediately postpartum where females are most vulnerable, suggesting that conservation efforts should focus on protecting females during this time to mitigate the impact of hunting pressure.
