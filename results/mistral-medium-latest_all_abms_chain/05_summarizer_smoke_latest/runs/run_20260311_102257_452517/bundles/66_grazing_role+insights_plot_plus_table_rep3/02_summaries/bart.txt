 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households . The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems .  The overall mean biomass across the simulation period is around 51,529 units, with a median value of approximately 51,197 units . From step 22 onwards, there is a consistent increasing trend in the mean green biomass . This recovery phase can be divided into several sub-phases where the rate of increase varies . Understanding these dynamics can inform policies aimed at sustainable grazing practices .  Trend shows a consistent increase in social learning adoption, with minor oscillations observed around certain steps, particularly between steps 22 to 33 and steps 51 to 59 . Maximum number of households engaging in sociallearning is reached around the 63rd step, after which number stabilizes and remains relatively constant through to the end of the simulation at the 100th step .
