 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households .  The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock starts at 0 for all simulations, indicating an initial state of perfect equality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households . Initially, from step 0 to around step 20, there is a steep rise in the mean destock total, indicating a significant amount of livestock destocking early in the simulation . After step 40, the metric begins to rise gradually, showing a steady increase until it reaches approximately 60 by step 100 .  By around the 20th time step, a noticeable stratification begins to emerge . Some trajectories show a significant decline in the number of non-learning households . Conversely, other trajectories stabilize at higher counts . This suggests that different simulation runs lead to varying degrees of adoption of learning strategies among households .
