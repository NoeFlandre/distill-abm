The total energy of foragers in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a consistent and steady decline throughout the simulation period. Initially, the total energy starts at a value of 2000 units. This value gradually decreases over time, with the energy levels dropping more steeply after certain inflection points. Key time steps where notable changes in the rate of decline occur include around steps 358, 1238, 1329, 1397, 1402, 1422, 1452, 1477, 1597, 1713, 1828, 2040, 2156, and 2167. These inflection points indicate moments where the rate of energy decline either accelerates or decelerates slightly, reflecting changes in the dynamics of hunting success, movement costs, or other factors influencing the foragers' energy status. 

The statistical analysis reveals several significant trends and change points. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a consistent decreasing trend in energy levels across all windows, with significant negative slopes ranging from -11.070 to -20.000. This suggests a persistent and significant decline in the total energy of foragers throughout the simulation. Change points identified at steps 650, 1075, 1500, 1925, and 2310 mark significant shifts in the trend, possibly due to changes in the environmental conditions or the behavior of the foragers and prey. 

The peaks and valleys in the energy levels provide additional insights. The initial peak at step 0 with a value of 2000 units is followed by a series of decreasing peaks and increasing valleys, indicating a continuous decline in energy levels. The most prominent valleys occur towards the end of the simulation, with the lowest point at step 2310 reaching -38233 units. This significant drop in energy levels suggests a critical phase where the foragers' energy status is severely depleted. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the factors contributing to the decline in foragers' energy levels. The consistent decrease in energy suggests that the current parameters and conditions of the model may not be sustainable for the foragers in the long term. Identifying the specific causes of these trends, such as hunting success rates, movement costs, or environmental changes, can provide valuable insights for developing strategies to mitigate the decline in energy levels and potentially prevent the extinction of megaherbivores.
