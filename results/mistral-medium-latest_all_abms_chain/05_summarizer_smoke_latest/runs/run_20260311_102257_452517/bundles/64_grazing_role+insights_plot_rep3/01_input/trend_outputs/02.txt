The mean household risk attitude shows a variety of trends across different simulations. Initially, most households start with a risk attitude around 0.5. In the early steps, up to around step 20, there is a rapid divergence in risk attitudes. Some households increase their risk attitude significantly, reaching values as high as 0.8, while others decrease their risk attitude, with values dropping to as low as -0.2. This divergence indicates a period of rapid adaptation and learning as households respond to initial conditions and early experiences in the grazing system. 

Between steps 20 and 40, the risk attitudes tend to stabilize somewhat, but there is still considerable variability. Some households maintain high risk attitudes, while others stabilize at lower levels. This period suggests that households are settling into their strategies based on their initial learning and experiences. 

From step 40 onwards, the risk attitudes generally stabilize, with most households maintaining their risk attitudes within a narrower range. However, there are still some fluctuations, indicating ongoing adaptation and response to changing conditions in the grazing system. The stabilization suggests that households have largely adapted to the system and are making decisions based on their established strategies. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of the initial period in shaping household risk attitudes. The early steps are crucial for households to adapt and learn, and interventions during this period could have significant long-term impacts. The stabilization of risk attitudes in the later steps suggests that once households have adapted, their decision-making becomes more predictable, which could aid in long-term planning and management of the grazing system.
