 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying interactions in grazing systems . The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households .  The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, starts at a high value of approximately 90 at the initial time step . The average livestock number remains relatively stable, fluctuating slightly around the 40 to 50 range from approximately step 20 to step 60 .  The distinct clusters of household counts engaging in social learning highlight the potential for different segments within the community to respond differently to social learning initiatives . Understanding these segments could help tailor interventions to specific groups, potentially increasing overall adoption rates . The steady state observed from step 40 onwards suggests that long-term planning should account for sustained engagement strategies to maintain the benefits of social learning over time .
