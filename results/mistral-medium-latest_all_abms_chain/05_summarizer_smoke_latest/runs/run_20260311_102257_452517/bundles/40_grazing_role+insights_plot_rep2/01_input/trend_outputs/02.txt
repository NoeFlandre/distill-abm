The mean household risk attitude shows a variety of trends across different simulations, reflecting the diverse adaptive behaviors and decision-making processes of households in a grazing system. Initially, the risk attitudes are relatively close to each other, starting around a neutral risk attitude of 0.5. However, as the simulation progresses, the risk attitudes begin to diverge significantly. 

By around 20 steps, several distinct groups emerge. One group of households exhibits a sharp increase in risk attitude, reaching values as high as 0.8. This suggests that these households become more risk-averse over time, possibly due to experiencing adverse conditions or learning from negative outcomes. Another group shows a moderate increase, stabilizing around 0.5 to 0.6, indicating a balanced approach to risk. A third group demonstrates a decrease in risk attitude, with values dropping to around 0.2 or even lower, suggesting these households become more risk-tolerant. 

Around the 40-step mark, the trends stabilize for most households, indicating that they have settled into their respective risk attitudes based on their experiences and learning. The stabilization of risk attitudes implies that households have adapted to the conditions and are making consistent decisions aligned with their learned behaviors. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the diverse adaptive behaviors of households in grazing systems. The significant divergence in risk attitudes underscores the need for tailored interventions that consider the varying levels of risk tolerance and aversion among households. This can inform policies aimed at promoting sustainable grazing practices and improving household livelihoods by addressing the specific needs and behaviors of different household groups.
