The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock starts at 0 for all simulations, indicating an initially equal distribution of livestock among households. As the simulation progresses, the Gini coefficient increases steadily, reflecting growing inequality in livestock distribution. By around 20 steps, the Gini coefficient values range between approximately 0.015 and 0.045, indicating a noticeable divergence in livestock distribution among households. This trend continues, and by around 40 steps, the values range from approximately 0.03 to 0.075, showing further increased inequality. As the simulation approaches 60 steps, the Gini coefficient values spread more widely, ranging from about 0.03 to 0.09, signifying substantial variability in livestock distribution. By the end of the simulation at 100 steps, the Gini coefficient values stabilize somewhat, with most values clustering between 0.045 and 0.09. This indicates that while there is significant inequality in livestock distribution, the rate of increase in inequality has slowed down. For decision-makers, these trends suggest that over time, certain households accumulate more livestock, leading to greater disparities. This insight can inform policies aimed at promoting more equitable distribution of resources and supporting households that may be at a disadvantage.
