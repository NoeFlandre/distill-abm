The number of non-learning households starts at 20 and exhibits a slight increase in the early stages, peaking at around 21.1 by the 8th time step. This initial rise could be attributed to the initial conditions of the model where households are still adapting to the environment and have not yet started to adopt learning strategies. Following this peak, there is a gradual decline in the number of non-learning households, reaching approximately 14.7 by the end of the simulation at the 100th time step. This overall decreasing trend suggests that over time, households are increasingly adopting learning strategies, likely due to the benefits these strategies provide in adapting to the ecological and climatic conditions. The decline is not steady but shows periods of stabilization and minor fluctuations, indicating phases where the rate of adoption of learning strategies varies. The inflection points and change points identified in the data further highlight these periods of varying rates of decline. For decision-makers, this trend implies that interventions promoting learning strategies among households could be beneficial in the long term, as they seem to be naturally adopted over time. The variability in the rate of decline also suggests that there might be critical periods where additional support or incentives could accelerate the adoption of beneficial strategies.
