The mean household risk attitude begins at a low value of approximately 0.0074, indicating an initial conservative approach towards risk. Over the first 20 steps, there is a rapid increase in the risk attitude, reaching a peak value of around 0.3814 at step 33. This sharp rise suggests that households quickly adapt and become more willing to take risks, potentially due to initial learning and adaptation processes within the model. Following this peak, the risk attitude shows a gradual decline, stabilizing around a value of 0.1911 by the end of the simulation at step 100. This decline and stabilization indicate a period of adjustment where households possibly balance their risk-taking behaviors based on accumulated experiences and outcomes. The presence of multiple inflection points, particularly around steps 7, 12, 13, 39, 46, 51, 67, 70, 74, 75, 76, 81, 83, 85, 86, and 90, suggests several phases of adjustment and stabilization in household risk attitudes. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in risk attitude during the initial phases (steps 0-32) and significant decreasing trends during the middle phases (steps 33-65), followed by periods of no significant trend towards the end of the simulation (steps 66-98). This pattern implies that while initial risk attitudes are highly dynamic and responsive to early experiences, they tend to stabilize as households gather more information and adapt their strategies over time. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial conditions and early interventions in shaping long-term risk attitudes among households. The stabilization of risk attitudes in the later stages suggests that policies aimed at influencing risk behavior should be implemented early to have a lasting impact.
