 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent starts at a very low value of approximately 5.29 units . Initially, there is a rapid increase in consumption, reaching around 1220.21 units by the 11th step . This period of rapid growth is characterized by a strong upward trend, indicating a significant shift in consumer behavior towards adopting skimmed milk . The statistical analysis confirms significant decreasing trends in multiple time windows .  The simulation of the agent-based model shows a clear and consistent downward trend in the mean quantity of whole milk consumed by agents over time . In the early steps, there is a rapid decline in whole milk consumption, dropping sharply to around 1000 units by step 10 . By step 32, the mean whole milk quantity reaches its minimum value of approximately 253.10 units . Overall trend indicates an initial rapid adoption phase, followed by a stabilization period with minor fluctuations, and ending with a slight increase in consumption . This final increase suggests a renewed or intensified adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk by the agents .  The overall mean deviation across all time steps is 0.4965, with a median of 0.5584, indicating that the central tendency of the data is slightly higher than the mean . The trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows a consistent increasing trend across all windows . Inflection points are noted at several time steps (4, 6, 16, 19, 22, 28, and 31) suggesting changes in the rate of increase at these points .
