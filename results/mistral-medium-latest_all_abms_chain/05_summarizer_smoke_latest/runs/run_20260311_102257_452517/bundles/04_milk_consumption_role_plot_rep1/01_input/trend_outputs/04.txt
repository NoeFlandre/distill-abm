The data from the repeated simulations of the agent-based model show a clear and consistent trend in the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices over time. Initially, at step 0, the total number of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices is very low, close to zero. This indicates that at the beginning of the simulation, agents predominantly choose whole milk, reflecting strong initial habits and low initial perceptions of the health and environmental benefits of alternative milk types. As the simulation progresses, there is a gradual increase in the number of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices. By step 5, the total number of these choices begins to rise more noticeably, reaching approximately 1,000. This suggests that agents are starting to consider and adopt alternative milk types, possibly due to the influence of social interactions and changing perceptions. Around step 10, the trend becomes more pronounced, with the total number of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices increasing to around 5,000. This indicates a significant shift in consumer behavior, as more agents are influenced by social norms, health perceptions, and environmental considerations. By step 15, the total number of choices for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk reaches approximately 10,000, showing a steady and substantial adoption rate. This trend continues linearly, and by step 20, the total number of choices reaches around 15,000. The linear growth pattern suggests a consistent and sustained change in consumer preferences over time. Finally, by step 30, the total number of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices approaches 25,000. This indicates that, over the simulated period, a significant majority of agents have shifted their preferences towards skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, reflecting the cumulative effects of social influence, changing perceptions, and habit formation.
