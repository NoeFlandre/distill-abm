The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a general increasing trend over time, starting from an initial value of 0. The trend shows a rapid increase in the early time steps, with significant growth observed up to around the 927th tick, where the slope of increase starts to diminish. The most substantial increases occur within the first 1159 ticks, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, which shows strong increasing trends with high tau values and significant p-values. 

After this period, the rate of increase slows down considerably. Between the 1392nd and 1623rd ticks, there is no significant trend, suggesting a period of stabilization in the number of postpartum females hunted. Following this stable period, there is a slight resurgence in the increasing trend up to the 1855th tick. Subsequently, a decreasing trend is observed from the 1856th tick onwards, indicating a reduction in the rate of postpartum females being hunted. 

Key inflection points are noted around the 245th, 262nd, and 285th ticks, suggesting early dynamic changes in the hunting patterns. Further inflection points are observed at later stages, notably around the 1180th, 1311th, and 1394th ticks, indicating periods where the trend undergoes notable changes. The data also exhibits periodic oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, particularly evident towards the end of the time series. 

The peaks and valleys identified in the data suggest minor fluctuations around a relatively stable high value, with the maximum value reaching 119 postpartum females hunted. The overall mean of approximately 102.44 and a median of 116.86 further support the observation that after initial rapid increases, the values stabilize at a high level with minor oscillations. 

For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial conditions and early dynamics significantly influence the hunting pressure on postpartum female prey. The stabilization at higher values suggests an equilibrium in hunting pressure after the initial rapid increases, which could be critical for understanding long-term socio-natural dynamics and potential extinction risks in the modeled environment.
