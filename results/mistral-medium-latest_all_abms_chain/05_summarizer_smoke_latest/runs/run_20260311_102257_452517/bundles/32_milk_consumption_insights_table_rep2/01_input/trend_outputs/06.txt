The simulation results indicate a consistent and significant decline in the mean quantity of whole milk consumed by agents over the observed period from 1974 to 2005. Initially, the average consumption starts at a high value of approximately 2654.81 units per week. This value experiences a sharp decline in the early years, dropping steadily across the timeline. By the end of the simulation period, the mean consumption reduces drastically to about 253.10 units per week. The trend analysis reveals several windows of significant decreasing trends, particularly notable in the early and middle phases of the simulation. There are periods where the decline slows, showing minor fluctuations and temporary stabilizations, but the overall trajectory remains downward. Key inflection points and peaks suggest moments of slight recovery or stabilization in consumption, but these are followed by continued declines. The data suggests that the adoption of alternative milk types, influenced by changing perceptions, habits, and social norms, has led to a substantial reduction in whole milk consumption. For decision makers, this trend underscores the impact of health and environmental perceptions, social influence, and habit formation on consumer behavior. It highlights the importance of understanding these factors in predicting and influencing market trends for dairy products.
