The simulations of the agent-based model reveal several distinct trends in the mean household risk attitude over the course of 100 steps. Initially, the risk attitudes of households exhibit a wide range of values, indicating diverse starting points for different households. In the early stages, up to around step 20, there is a noticeable divergence in risk attitudes. Some households show a significant increase in risk attitude, while others demonstrate a decrease or maintain a relatively stable attitude. This divergence suggests that households are responding differently to initial conditions and early experiences within the model. 

Between steps 20 and 40, the risk attitudes of most households tend to stabilize, although at different levels. This period of stabilization indicates that households may have adapted to the initial conditions and are settling into their respective risk management strategies. The variation in stabilized values implies that different households have adopted different long-term strategies based on their learning and experiences. 

After step 40, the trends in risk attitude become more consistent, with households maintaining their risk attitudes at relatively steady levels until the end of the simulation. This consistency suggests that households have reached a state of equilibrium where their risk attitudes are less influenced by new information or experiences. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial conditions and early interventions in shaping long-term risk attitudes. The stabilization of risk attitudes after an initial period of divergence suggests that early experiences and learning play a crucial role in determining household strategies. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing policies that support adaptive management and resilience in pastoral systems.
