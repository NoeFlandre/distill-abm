The combined total energy available in the population of male prey animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the energy level starts at 12500 units. This high energy level experiences a sharp decline in the early stages, dropping significantly by the 231st tick. The decline continues steadily, with notable inflection points indicating changes in the rate of decrease around ticks 316, 453, 516, and several others. Despite minor fluctuations and brief periods of stabilization, the overall trajectory remains downward. By the 2284th tick, the energy level reaches zero, indicating the complete depletion of energy in the male prey population. The statistical analysis confirms this trend, with multiple rolling Mann-Kendall windows showing significant decreasing trends. The presence of several change points and inflection points suggests periods where the rate of energy decline shifts, potentially due to varying interactions between hunting pressure, environmental factors, and herd dynamics. For decision-makers, this trend underscores the critical impact of sustained hunting pressure and environmental conditions on the energy reserves of male prey animals, highlighting the need for careful management of hunting practices and habitat conservation to prevent the depletion of energy reserves in prey populations.
