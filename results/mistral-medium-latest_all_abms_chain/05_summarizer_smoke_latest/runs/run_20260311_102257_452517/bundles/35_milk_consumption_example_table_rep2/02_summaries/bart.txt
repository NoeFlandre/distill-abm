 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices . The model includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where influence is a function of interaction probability and agent susceptibility .  The overall trend demonstrates a significant shift from whole milk to lower-fat alternatives, driven by the complex interplay of individual perceptions, social influences, and habitual behaviors . The total number of milk choices made by all agents combined starts at 0 and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period . The number of choices reaches its peak at 32,000 by the end of the simulation .  The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at 0.0000 . At the start of the simulation, there is no deviation among agents' choice functions, suggesting uniform decision-making criteria . The data shows significant increasing trends in multiple rolling Mann-Kendall windows, reinforcing the steady rise in the habit factor over time .  At the beginning of the simulation, there is no deviation, suggesting that agents' perceptions of health values are perfectly aligned with their milk choices . The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent increasing trend in deviation across multiple time windows . The oscillation analysis reveals dominant periodic behavior with a period of 15.00 time steps .
