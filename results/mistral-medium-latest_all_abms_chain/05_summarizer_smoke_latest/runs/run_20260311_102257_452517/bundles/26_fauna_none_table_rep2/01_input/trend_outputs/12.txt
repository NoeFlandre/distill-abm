The combined total energy available in the population of female prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear overall decreasing trend throughout the simulation. Initially, the energy level starts at 12,500 units. This high initial value rapidly declines in the early stages of the simulation. By the 2311th tick, the energy level drops to zero, indicating the complete depletion of energy reserves among female prey animals. The mean energy level across the simulation is approximately 1,569 units, with a median of 100 units, highlighting the skewed distribution towards lower energy values as the simulation progresses. The standard deviation of 3,083 units and a variance of 9,505,022 units further emphasize the significant variability and overall decline in energy levels. Several peaks and valleys are observed around the 375th to 410th ticks, but these fluctuations are minor compared to the overall downward trend. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this decreasing trend across multiple windows, with significant negative slopes indicating consistent declines in energy levels. There are brief periods of stabilization and slight increases, particularly around the 1160th to 1391th ticks and 1856th to 2087th ticks, but these are followed by continued declines. The dominant oscillation period of 63 ticks suggests some cyclical patterns within the overall decline, but these oscillations do not alter the primary trend of decreasing energy levels. The simulation ultimately shows a continuous and significant reduction in the energy reserves of female prey animals, leading to complete depletion by the end.
