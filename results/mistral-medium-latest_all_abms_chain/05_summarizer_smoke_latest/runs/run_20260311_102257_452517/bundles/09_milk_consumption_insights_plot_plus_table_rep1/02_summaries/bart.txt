 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices . The model includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where influence is a function of interaction probability and agent susceptibility .  The overall trend highlights the dynamic nature of consumer behavior in response to perceived benefits and social influences . Understanding these dynamics can help in strategizing interventions or policies aimed at influencing milk consumption behaviors . For decision makers, these trends imply a growing preference or adoption of whole milk choices among agents .  The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents in the simulation shows distinct trends over time . For decision makers, these trends imply a substantial shift in milk consumption patterns, likely influenced by changing perceptions, habits, and social influences among the agents . The mean habit factor for whole milk shows a consistent increasing trend over the simulation period .  Trend analysis shows a consistent increasing pattern across all rolling Mann-Kendall windows, reinforcing the steady rise in choice function deviation over time . For decision makers, these trends imply that interventions aimed at influencing milk consumption behaviors should consider the initial rapid change phase and the subsequent stabilization period .
