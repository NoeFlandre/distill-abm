 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . For decision-makers, this implies that under the given model, households maintain a steady initial risk attitude, which could suggest stability in decision-making processes related to livestock management . Initially, the mean livestock count starts at 90.0000. There is a sharp decline in the early stages, reaching a minimum value of approximately 41.0089 around time step 37. This period of rapid decrease is followed by a gradual recovery phase . This trend suggests a growing inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households over time .  The initial increase in non-learning households suggests a short-term rise in households adhering to the E-RO strategy, possibly due to initial conditions or immediate responses to the simulation setup . The subsequent decrease indicates a shift away from the strategy . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing more effective and sustainable grazing management practices .
