The percentage of green patches in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulated time steps. Initially, the percentage of green patches is around 32%. This value remains relatively stable for approximately the first 500 steps. Following this period, there is a sharp decline in the percentage of green patches, dropping to around 24% by step 600. This decline suggests a significant reduction in grass availability, likely due to increased grazing pressure from the prey animals. After this drop, the percentage of green patches quickly recovers to around 32% by step 700, indicating a period of grass regrowth or reduced grazing pressure. This recovery is followed by another sharp decline to approximately 24% by step 1000, mirroring the previous pattern. Subsequently, the percentage of green patches recovers again to around 32% and stabilizes, maintaining this level with minor fluctuations for the remainder of the simulation. These fluctuations suggest cyclical patterns of grass consumption and regrowth, which could be influenced by seasonal variations or dynamic interactions between the prey animals and their environment. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the cyclical nature of resource availability and the potential impacts of grazing pressure on the landscape. This information can be crucial for developing strategies to manage wildlife populations and their habitats effectively.
