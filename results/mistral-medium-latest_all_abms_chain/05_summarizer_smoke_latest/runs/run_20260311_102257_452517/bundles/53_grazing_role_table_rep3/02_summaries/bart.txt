 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households .  The simulation of the agent-based model shows distinct trends in the average total number of livestock across all households over 100 timesteps . A decreasing trend is observed in the early stages of the simulation, with significant declines noted in the first three rolling Mann-Kendall windows . However, from timestep 44 onwards, there is a notable shift to an increasing trend in livestock numbers .  The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households exhibits a consistent increasing trend over the simulation period . Inflection points are observed at various timesteps, notably around timestep 8, 13, 15, 20, and 20, 25, 37, and 57, which may indicate subtle shifts in the rate of increase . The absence of detected peaks and valleys suggests a smooth and continuous rise in inequality .
