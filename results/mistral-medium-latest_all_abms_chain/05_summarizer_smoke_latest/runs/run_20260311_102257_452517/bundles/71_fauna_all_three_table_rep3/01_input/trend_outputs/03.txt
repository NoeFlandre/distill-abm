The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands begins at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the initial time steps. This early phase of the simulation shows a steep rise, indicating a high rate of hunting success or a high initial population of male prey. By around 250 time steps, the rate of increase starts to slow down, suggesting a potential decrease in the male prey population or a reduction in hunting efficiency. The cumulative count continues to rise but at a more gradual pace, reaching approximately 117.5 by the end of the simulation. The trend analysis reveals several inflection points, notably around time steps 362, 1241, and 1330, indicating periods where the rate of change in hunting success shifts. These inflection points could correspond to changes in the dynamics of the prey population or hunter behavior. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with periods of no significant trend and even a decreasing trend towards the end. This suggests that the initial hunting pressure is high but stabilizes over time, possibly due to a balance being reached between prey availability and hunting efforts. The presence of multiple peaks and valleys in the data, although with low prominence, indicates minor fluctuations in the hunting success rate. For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressure can significantly impact male prey populations, but over time, the system may reach a more stable state. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing conservation strategies or managing hunting practices to ensure sustainable prey populations.
