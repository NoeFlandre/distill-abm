The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, the number of males killed starts at zero and increases rapidly. This rapid increase continues until around 500 steps, where the rate of increase begins to slow down. By approximately 1000 steps, the cumulative number of males killed starts to plateau, indicating a significant reduction in the rate of increase. The trend stabilizes around 120 males killed, with minor fluctuations observed thereafter. The mean number of males killed across the simulations is approximately 100, with a median of 116.5, suggesting that in most simulations, the number of males killed tends to stabilize around this value. The standard deviation of 32.5 indicates some variability in the outcomes of different simulations. The initial rapid increase in the number of males killed suggests that early in the simulation, hunting pressure is high, and male prey are relatively easy to hunt. As time progresses, the rate of increase slows, potentially indicating a reduction in the male prey population or increased difficulty in hunting due to various factors such as prey adaptation or environmental changes. The plateauing trend after 1000 steps suggests a possible equilibrium where the hunting rate balances the remaining male prey population. For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressures can significantly impact male prey populations, but over time, the system may reach a state of equilibrium. Understanding these dynamics can help in formulating strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts.
