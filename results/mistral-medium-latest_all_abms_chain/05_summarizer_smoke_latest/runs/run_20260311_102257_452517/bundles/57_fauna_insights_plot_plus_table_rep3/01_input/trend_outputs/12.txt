The total energy of female animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at a high value of 12500 and exhibits a rapid and consistent decline over time. Initially, the energy levels drop sharply, indicating a significant reduction in the energy available within the female animal population. This steep decline continues until around 500 steps, where the rate of decrease starts to slow down. Throughout the simulation, there are several inflection points and minor peaks and valleys, suggesting periods of slight stabilization or minor fluctuations in the energy levels. However, the overall trend remains a steady decline. By the end of the simulation at 2311 steps, the total energy of female animals reaches zero, indicating complete depletion. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this decreasing trend across various windows, with significant negative slopes. The presence of multiple change points and oscillations suggests intermittent periods of slight recovery or stabilization, but these are not sufficient to reverse the overall declining trend. For decision-makers, this trend implies that under the given parameters, the female animal population is unable to sustain its energy levels over time, leading to a complete depletion. This could indicate potential issues with the sustainability of the prey population under the current model settings, suggesting a need for further investigation into the factors contributing to this decline, such as hunting pressure, environmental conditions, or animal life history traits.
