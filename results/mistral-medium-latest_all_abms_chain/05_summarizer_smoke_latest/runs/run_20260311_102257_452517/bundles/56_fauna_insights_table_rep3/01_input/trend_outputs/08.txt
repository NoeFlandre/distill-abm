The simulation of male prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the number of male prey animals starts at 125 and exhibits a sharp decline in the early stages of the simulation. This decline continues steadily, with significant drops observed at various key time steps, notably around ticks 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 875. These change points indicate periods where the rate of decline accelerates or shifts. By the end of the simulation, the number of male prey animals reaches zero, indicating a complete extinction within the modeled time frame. Throughout the simulation, there are minor fluctuations and small peaks, particularly around ticks 730, 709, 803, 820, and 893, but these do not significantly alter the overall downward trend. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this decreasing trend across multiple windows, with significant negative slopes and tau values, reinforcing the consistent decline. The presence of oscillations with a dominant period of 63 ticks suggests some cyclical patterns, but these are overshadowed by the overall downward trajectory. For decision-makers, this trend implies that under the given parameters and conditions, the male prey population is unable to sustain itself, leading to eventual extinction. This insight could be crucial for understanding the dynamics of hunting pressure and environmental factors on prey populations and for designing interventions or conservation strategies.
