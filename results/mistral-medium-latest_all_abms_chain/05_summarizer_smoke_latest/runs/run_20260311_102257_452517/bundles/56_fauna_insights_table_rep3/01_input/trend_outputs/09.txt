The simulation of female prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the number of female prey animals starts at 125 and exhibits a sharp decline in the early stages of the simulation. This decline continues steadily throughout the simulation, with occasional minor fluctuations. By the end of the simulation, the number of female prey animals reaches zero, indicating a complete extinction event. Key time steps highlight significant drops, particularly around the early and middle phases of the simulation, where the rate of decline is more pronounced. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend across multiple time windows, with significant negative slopes and tau values. The presence of several inflection points and change points suggests periods where the rate of decline varies, but the overall trajectory remains downward. The oscillation indices indicate periodic fluctuations, but these are minor compared to the overall trend. For decision-makers, this trend suggests that under the given parameters, the female prey population is not sustainable and heads towards extinction. This insight could be crucial for understanding the dynamics of hunting pressure and environmental conditions on prey populations, particularly in scenarios aiming to model extinction events or evaluate conservation strategies.
