 The Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbiivores . Model considers various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, sexual dimorphism, and life history of animals . The model allows users to manipulate various parameters to establish the socio-natural dynamics of the experiment matrix .  The model allows users to conduct experiments to test how environmental conditions, human hunting, and animal life history interact to influence extinction dynamics . Hunters are embedded with logic approximating dynamic optimal foraging theory, making foraging decisions based on environmental feedback . The model's landscape consists of an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges .  The cumulative number of deaths among the prey population begins at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps . At around 700 time steps, the number of births begins to increase, reaching approximately 4 births . This increase suggests that the population has started to adapt and reproduce within the environment . The trend suggests that without intervention, the population of male hunters could decline to near-complete extinction .  Simulation results show low variance across different runs, suggesting that the decline in the female population is a consistent outcome under the given parameters . The rapid initial decline followed by stabilization at zero highlights the vulnerability of the megaherbivore population . Understanding the specific pressures leading to this rapid decrease can inform conservation strategies and policy decisions aimed at preserving the population .  Percentage of green patches starts at approximately 33%, reflecting the initial proportion of the landscape covered in grass . Initial stability, followed by periods of sharp decline and recovery, underscores the importance of understanding the factors that influence grass availability . For decision makers, these trends highlight the dynamic interplay between grazing pressure and grass regrowth .
