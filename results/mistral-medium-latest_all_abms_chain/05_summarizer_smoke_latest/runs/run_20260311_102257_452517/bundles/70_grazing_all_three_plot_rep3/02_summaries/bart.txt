 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The mean household initial risk attitude remains constant throughout the simulation, indicating that the initial disposition to deviate from the recommended number of livestock does not change over time for social learning agents .  The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, starts at approximately 90% . This insight can guide policies aimed at maintaining long-term ecological balance and household livelihoods in grazing systems . For decision makers, the initial decline in green biomass highlights the importance of managing initial grazing pressure to avoid overconsumption .  Without intervention, inequalities in livestock distribution tend to increase over time and then stabilize at a higher level of disparity . Understanding the factors contributing to these disparities can inform targeted strategies to promote more equitable outcomes in grazing systems . For decision-makers, this highlights the importance of early interventions to manage and mitigate inequality .
