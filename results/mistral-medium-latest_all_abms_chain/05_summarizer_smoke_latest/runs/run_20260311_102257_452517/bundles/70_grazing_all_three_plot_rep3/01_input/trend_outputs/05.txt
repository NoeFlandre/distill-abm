The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, starts at approximately 90. This high initial value indicates that households begin with a substantial herd size, which is crucial for their livelihood and economic stability. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid decline in the total livestock, dropping to around 40. This sharp decrease suggests an initial period of high stress on the grazing system, potentially due to limited pasture resources or high initial destocking as households adjust their herds to sustainable levels. 

Between time steps 20 and 40, the number of livestock stabilizes, fluctuating slightly around the 40 mark. This stabilization period indicates that households may have reached a temporary equilibrium where the available pasture resources can support the current livestock population without significant further destocking. 

After time step 40, there is a gradual increase in the total number of livestock, reaching approximately 60 by time step 100. This upward trend suggests that households are gradually able to increase their herd sizes as they adapt their management practices, possibly through learning and improved decision-making over time. The increase could also imply that pasture resources are being managed more effectively, allowing for a higher carrying capacity of livestock. 

For decision makers, the initial rapid decline highlights the importance of preparing for significant early adjustments in livestock numbers. The subsequent stabilization and gradual increase suggest that long-term planning and adaptive management practices can lead to sustainable livestock populations. This insight underscores the need for policies that support adaptive co-management and learning strategies among households to enhance resilience and sustainability in grazing systems.
