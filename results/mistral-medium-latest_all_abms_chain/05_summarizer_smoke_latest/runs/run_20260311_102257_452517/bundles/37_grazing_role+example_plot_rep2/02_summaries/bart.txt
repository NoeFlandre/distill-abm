 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . It allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households .  The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase within the first 20 time steps . The Gini coefficient for livestock health starts at 0 across all simulations, indicating an initial state of perfect equality in the distribution of healthy livestock among households . Over time, disparities in livestock health emerge and grow, influenced by the complex interactions of ecological, climatic, and household decision-making factors .  The count of non-learning households starts with a high variability, with values ranging from approximately 10 to 40 households . This initial variability suggests a diverse distribution of household strategies at the beginning of the simulation . The overall pattern indicates a shift towards more adaptive and potentially more effective household behaviors as the simulation progresses .
