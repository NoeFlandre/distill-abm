 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  The total number of whole milk choices starts at zero and increases steadily over the 33 time steps . The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per person starts at approximately 5.29 units . The consumption then increases rapidly, reaching around 1265.47 units by the 11th time step . The trend analysis shows significant decreasing trends in multiple time windows, confirming the overall decline in whole milk consumption throughout the simulation .  The total number of milk choices made by all agents combined starts at 0 and increases steadily over the 33 time steps, reaching a final value of 32,000 . The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent increasing trend across all windows, with a slope of 1000 and significant p-values, indicating a strong and steady upward trajectory in the number of choices made . The overall trend from start to finish is a significant decrease in whole milk consumption .  The mean habit factor for whole milk consumption starts at a baseline value of 1.0000 . By the 33rd time step, the mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed reaches approximately 1456.60 units . The overall trend indicates a substantial transition from whole milk to alternative milk types .  The mean value environmental deviation starts at 0.0000 and increases rapidly in the first few steps, reaching approximately 13.4756 by step 13 . This rapid increase suggests that agents quickly adjust their environmental perceptions to align with their milk choices . After step 25, the deviation begins to increase again, reaching a peak of approximately 0.725 by the final step . This final increase suggests a renewed divergence between health perceptions and choices .
