The total number of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk choices starts at zero and increases gradually over the first 5 time steps. This initial slow growth could be attributed to the initial low perception of health and environmental benefits of alternative milk types, as well as strong initial habits for whole milk consumption. As agents begin to interact and influence each other, the number of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk choices starts to rise more rapidly. 

Around the 10th time step, there is a noticeable increase in the rate of adoption. This acceleration could be due to the cumulative effect of social influence and the gradual weakening of initial habits for whole milk. As more agents become disposed to consider alternative milk types, the social susceptibility and conformity parameters play a more significant role in the decision-making process. 

Between the 15th and 25th time steps, the growth in the number of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk choices continues at a steady pace. This period of sustained growth indicates that agents are increasingly adopting alternative milk types as their perceptions of health and environmental benefits improve, and as social norms shift towards these choices. The habit threshold and cognitive dissonance threshold parameters may also contribute to this trend, as agents become more open to change and less resistant to new information. 

After the 25th time step, the growth rate starts to slow down, approaching a saturation point near the 30th time step. This saturation suggests that the majority of agents who are likely to switch to skimmed/semi-skimmed milk have already done so. The remaining agents may be those with strong initial habits for whole milk or those who are less susceptible to social influence and conformity. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of social influence and the gradual shift in perceptions and habits in driving the adoption of healthier and more environmentally friendly milk choices. The initial slow growth underscores the need for targeted interventions to raise awareness and improve perceptions of alternative milk types. The rapid acceleration phase suggests that once a critical mass of adopters is reached, social influence can drive widespread change. Finally, the saturation phase indicates that there may be a limit to the adoption of alternative milk types, and additional strategies may be needed to reach the remaining resistant consumers.
