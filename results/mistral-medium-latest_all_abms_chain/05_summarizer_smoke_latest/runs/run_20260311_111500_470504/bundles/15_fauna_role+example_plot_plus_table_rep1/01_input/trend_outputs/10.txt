This initial decline can be attributed to the combined effects of hunting pressure, internal and external mortality factors, and possibly limited food resources. The steep decline suggests a high initial predation rate or significant environmental pressures leading to a rapid reduction in the prey population. Around the 730 time step mark, there is a noticeable but minor peak where the number of animals briefly increases to about 11 individuals. This could be due to a temporary reduction in hunting pressure or a brief increase in food availability, allowing for a slight recovery in the population. Following this peak, the population continues to decline, albeit at a slower rate, indicating ongoing but less intense pressures on the prey population. By around 2000 time steps, the number of prey animals stabilizes near zero, indicating that the population has been nearly or completely extirpated. The stabilization at zero suggests that the remaining individuals were insufficient to sustain the population, leading to complete extinction. Throughout the simulation, there are several inflection points and minor oscillations, indicating periods where the rate of decline changes or small temporary recoveries occur. However, these are not sufficient to alter the overall trend of decline and eventual extinction. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results indicate a consistent decreasing trend in the population across different time windows, reinforcing the observation of a steady decline in the number of prey animals over time.
