The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at zero and increases rapidly in the initial phase, reaching approximately 200 deaths by around 500 time steps. This rapid increase can be attributed to the initial conditions of the simulation, where both the prey population and human hunters are introduced, leading to a high rate of predation and natural mortality. The steep rise indicates a period of significant stress on the prey population as they adapt to the presence of predators and environmental conditions. 

Following this initial surge, the rate of increase in the number of deaths slows down significantly. By around 1000 time steps, the cumulative deaths reach approximately 230, and the curve begins to plateau. This plateauing suggests that the prey population and the predators may have reached a form of equilibrium, where the rate of deaths stabilizes due to a balance between predation pressure and the prey's ability to sustain their population through reproduction and survival strategies. 

The simulation shows minor fluctuations in the cumulative number of deaths after reaching the plateau, with small peaks and valleys observed around the 235 mark. These minor variations could be due to seasonal changes, stochastic events, or slight imbalances in the predator-prey dynamics. The overall trend remains relatively stable, indicating that the system has reached a steady state where the number of deaths does not change drastically over time. 

The statistical analysis reveals several inflection points and change points, particularly around the initial 500 time steps, which correspond to the periods of rapid increase in deaths. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in the early phases of the simulation, which later shift to no significant trend, reflecting the stabilization of the system. The oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation suggest minor periodic variations, but these do not substantially alter the overall stable trend observed in the cumulative number of deaths.
