The simulation of male prey animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the population starts at 125 male prey animals. This number steadily declines, with significant drops observed at various inflection points, notably around ticks 390, 755, and 817. The most substantial decreases occur early in the simulation, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, which shows strong decreasing trends in the initial windows. For instance, from ticks 0 to 231, the population decreases with a slope of -0.149, and from ticks 232 to 463, the slope is -0.244. These early and sharp declines suggest a high initial hunting pressure or other environmental factors significantly impacting the male prey population. As the simulation progresses, the rate of decline slows down, with some windows even showing no significant trend, such as from ticks 1856 to 2087. The population eventually reaches zero around tick 2284, indicating the extinction of male prey animals in the simulation. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points highlights periods where the rate of decline shifts, possibly due to changes in the dynamics of hunting pressure, environmental conditions, or prey demographics. The oscillation indices suggest periodic fluctuations in the population, but these are overshadowed by the overall decreasing trend. For decision-makers, these trends underscore the vulnerability of male prey populations under sustained hunting pressure and environmental changes. The early sharp declines and eventual extinction point to the need for careful management of hunting practices and conservation efforts to prevent such outcomes in real-world scenarios.
