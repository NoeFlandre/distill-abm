The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 200 time steps, reaching approximately 100. This rapid initial increase indicates a high initial hunting success rate, likely due to the abundance of prey and the relatively low processing cost for female prey. The rate of increase slows down significantly after this initial phase, suggesting a depletion in the easily accessible female prey population or an adaptation in prey behavior to avoid hunters.

The trend continues to rise gradually, reaching around 120 by the 1000th time step. This slower increase could be attributed to the hunters' continued efforts to hunt females despite the reduced availability or increased wariness of the prey. The number of females killed stabilizes around 120 after approximately 1500 time steps, indicating a possible equilibrium where the hunting pressure is balanced by the remaining females' ability to evade or reproduce. The slight oscillations observed towards the end of the simulation suggest minor fluctuations in hunting success, potentially due to environmental variability or stochastic events in the model.

The statistical analysis reveals several inflection points and change points, indicating shifts in the dynamics of hunting success. The initial inflection points around 245 to 285 time steps highlight the rapid increase phase, while later inflection points around 1204 to 2040 time steps mark the transition to a more stable phase. The Mann-Kendall test results show significant increasing trends in the early phases, followed by a significant decreasing trend in the later phase, reinforcing the observation of an initial rapid increase followed by stabilization.

For decision-makers, these trends suggest that initial hunting efforts are highly effective but that the effectiveness diminishes over time as prey populations adapt or are depleted. This insight could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices or conservation efforts to maintain prey populations.
