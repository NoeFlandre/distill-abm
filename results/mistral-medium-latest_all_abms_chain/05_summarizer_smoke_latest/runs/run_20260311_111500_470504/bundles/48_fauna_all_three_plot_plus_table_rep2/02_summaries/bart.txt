 The Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbiivores . Model considers various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, sexual dimorphism, and life history of animals . The model allows users to manipulate various parameters to establish the socio-natural dynamics of the experiment matrix .  The model's landscape consists of an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, existing on a 211x211 patch matrix with a relative spatial scale . Hunters are embedded with logic approximating dynamic optimal foraging theory, making foraging decisions based on environmental feedback . Ungulate herds are sexually dimorphic, with distinct food values and processing costs for males and females .  The Mann-Kendall test results show significant trends of increasing births in the early and middle phases, followed by a significant decreasing trend in the later phase . For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of monitoring and managing environmental conditions and predation pressures to sustain prey animal populations .  The number of male animals in the simulation starts at 125 and exhibits a rapid decline within the first 500 time steps, reaching a value close to zero . The decline continues steadily, with minor fluctuations, until around 750 time steps where the rate of decline slows down . The stabilization of deaths suggests that the system reaches a form of equilibrium, which could be important for conservation strategies .  The total energy of male animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at 12,500 units and exhibits a rapid decline within the first 125 time steps . The energy levels continue to decline steadily, reaching near-zero values by the end of the simulation . Understanding these dynamics can inform strategies to mitigate factors contributing to energy loss among male animals, potentially adjusting hunting pressures or environmental conditions to sustain energy levels .  The percentage of green patches in the landscape starts at approximately 33% and remains relatively stable throughout the simulation . Understanding these dynamics can inform conservation strategies and hunting regulations to prevent the collapse of vulnerable species . The overall trend shows minor fluctuations but remains largely stable, indicating a resilient vegetation regrowth mechanism or balanced grazing pressure .
