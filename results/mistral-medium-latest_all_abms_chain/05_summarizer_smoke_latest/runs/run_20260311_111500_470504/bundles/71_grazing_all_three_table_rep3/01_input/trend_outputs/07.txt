The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all households, as measured by the Gini coefficient, starts at a value of 0.0, indicating perfect equality. Over the course of the simulation, the Gini coefficient exhibits a consistent increasing trend, reaching a final value of approximately 0.0726. This trend suggests a gradual increase in inequality among households in terms of healthy livestock distribution. The mean value of the Gini coefficient across the simulation is 0.0302, with a median of 0.0229, indicating that while the inequality is increasing, it remains relatively low on average. The standard deviation of 0.0239 and variance of 0.0006 indicate low variability in the inequality measures over time. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a significant increasing trend in inequality across all windows, with high Kendall tau values and significant p-values, reinforcing the consistent upward trajectory of the Gini coefficient. Several inflection points are detected, suggesting changes in the rate of increase in inequality at specific time steps. The presence of multiple oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation suggests some periodic fluctuations in the rate of change of inequality. For decision makers, the increasing trend in inequality implies that over time, disparities in the distribution of healthy livestock among households are widening. This could have implications for the equitable management of grazing resources and the need for interventions to ensure fair access to livestock-related benefits among households.
