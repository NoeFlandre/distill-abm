 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households . The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems .  The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households, begins at 90.0 . The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly . This initial phase indicates a quick adoption of social learning behaviors due to the homogeneous behavioral types in the model .  The number of livestock destocked across all households starts at zero and increases steadily throughout the simulation period . For decision makers, this trend suggests that under the given parameters, households are continuously adapting their livestock management practices . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions to promote beneficial behaviors more effectively .
