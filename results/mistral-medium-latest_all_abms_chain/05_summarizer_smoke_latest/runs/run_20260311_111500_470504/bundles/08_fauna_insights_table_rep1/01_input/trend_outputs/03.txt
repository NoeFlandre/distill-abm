The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a general increasing trend over time, with notable dynamics and fluctuations. Initially, the number of males killed starts at zero and gradually increases, reflecting the early stages of the simulation where hunting activities begin to impact the male prey population. The trend shows a rapid increase in the early time steps, with significant inflection points around ticks 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850, indicating periods of accelerated hunting activity. The most substantial increases occur within the first 2310 ticks, after which the rate of increase stabilizes. The mean number of males killed is approximately 100, with a median of 116.5, suggesting that while there are periods of high hunting activity, the central tendency remains around these values. The maximum value reached is 120.1667 at tick 1876, indicating a peak in hunting activity at this point. The data also shows several peaks and valleys, suggesting periodic fluctuations in hunting success. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with periods of no significant trend and a decreasing trend towards the end. The oscillation indices suggest a dominant periodicity of 63 ticks, indicating regular fluctuations in the hunting patterns. For decision makers, these trends imply that hunting pressure on male prey is initially high and increases rapidly, but eventually stabilizes with periodic fluctuations. This information can be crucial for understanding the dynamics of hunting pressure and its potential impact on the male prey population over time.
