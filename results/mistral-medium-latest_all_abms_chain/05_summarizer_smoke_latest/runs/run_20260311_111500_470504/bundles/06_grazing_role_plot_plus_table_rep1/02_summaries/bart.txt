 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The mean household initial risk attitude remains constant throughout the simulation period . The statistical summary indicates that the mean, median, standard deviation, and variance are all consistent with a value of approximately 0.0074 .  The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households starts at a high value of 90.0000 units . The data shows a clear inflection point around time step 49, marking the transition from a decreasing trend to an increasing trend . By the end of the simulation at step 100, the average livestock number reaches approximately 47.5472 units .  The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households in the grazing system model shows a consistent increasing trend over the 100 time steps . The number of households engaging in social learning continues to rise, albeit at a slower pace, reaching a peak around time step 63 with approximately 32 households . The average mean number of non-learning households across the simulation is 16.37, with a standard deviation of approximately 1.92 .
