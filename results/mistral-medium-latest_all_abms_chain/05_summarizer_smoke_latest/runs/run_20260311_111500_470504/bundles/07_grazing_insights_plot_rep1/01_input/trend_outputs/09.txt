The simulations of the agent-based model show a general upward trend in the mean destock total of households over time. Initially, the mean destock total starts near zero and increases rapidly within the first 20 steps, reaching approximately 60. This rapid increase suggests that households are quickly adapting to initial conditions by reducing their livestock numbers. Following this initial phase, the rate of increase in destocking slows down, and the curves begin to level off, indicating a stabilization in the decision-making process of households. By around 40 steps, the mean destock total ranges between 80 and 100, showing a more gradual increase. Beyond 60 steps, the curves continue to rise but at a much slower rate, eventually approaching values between 100 and 140 by the end of the simulation at 100 steps. The highest curve reaches close to 170, indicating some households destock significantly more than others. This trend implies that over time, households are continuously adjusting their livestock numbers, but the rate of adjustment decreases as they approach a new equilibrium. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial adaptive strategies and the eventual stabilization of household responses to environmental and economic conditions. The insights suggest that while initial interventions may lead to significant changes, the long-term impact tends to stabilize, requiring ongoing but less intensive management strategies.
