 The Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbiivores . Model considers various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, sexual dimorphism, and life history of animals . The model allows users to manipulate various parameters to establish the socio-natural dynamics of the experiment matrix .  The model's landscape consists of an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, existing on a 211x211 patch matrix with a relative spatial scale . Hunters are embedded with logic approximating dynamic optimal foraging theory, making foraging decisions based on environmental feedback . Animals can reproduce, and users can control sex ratios and reproduction probability .  The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the early time steps . For decision makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressure is intense but stabilizes over time, which could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices .  The number of male prey animals in the simulation begins at 125 and exhibits a rapid decline in the initial phase, dropping sharply within the first 500 time steps . The population experiences several smaller peaks and valleys, notably around time steps 709, 803, and 820 . The dominant oscillation period of 63 time steps suggests cyclical patterns in the death rate, which could be linked to seasonal variations or other periodic factors in the model .  After time step 2310, energy levels stabilize at a very low value, approaching zero . This stabilization at near-zero energy levels suggests a critical decline in the male prey population, potentially indicating a trend towards extinction . The rolling Mann-Kendall test results confirm a consistent decreasing trend in energy levels across multiple time windows .  The mean vegetation cover across the simulation is 32.6558%, with a standard deviation of 0.9503%, suggesting relatively low variability . The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in vegetation cover during the initial time windows . The dominant oscillation period is 63 time steps, indicating cyclic patterns in vegetation regrowth and consumption .
