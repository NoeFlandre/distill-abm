The trend in the average number of destocked livestock across all households shows a series of fluctuations and a general decline over the simulation period. Initially, the average number of destocked livestock starts at 0 and rapidly increases to a peak of approximately 6.61 at time step 3. This sharp increase is followed by a steep decline to around 3.33 by time step 8. The trend continues with smaller peaks and valleys, indicating periodic fluctuations in destocking rates. Corresponding valleys are observed at time steps 8, 11, 16, and 18, with values around 3.33, 2.47, 1.45, and 0.90, respectively. After time step 30, the destocking rate stabilizes and exhibits a gradual decline with minor fluctuations, eventually reaching a value of approximately 0.31 at the final time step. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant decreasing trends in the initial windows, followed by periods of no significant trend and a slight increasing trend towards the end. The dominant oscillation period of 49 time steps suggests a cyclical pattern in the destocking rates. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the periodic nature of destocking and the potential for stabilization over time, which could inform strategies for managing livestock and resources more effectively.
