 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent begins at approximately 2654.81 units . The overall trend indicates a significant shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk over the simulation period . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the factors driving the initial rapid decline and the subsequent stabilization .  The total number of milk choices made by all agents combined starts at 0 and increases steadily over the 33 time steps . The mean number of choices is approximately 11,157, with a median of around 10,679, suggesting a relatively symmetric distribution of choices over time . For decision-makers, these trends imply that interventions aimed at promoting healthier milk choices can lead to significant long-term adoption .  10 time steps, there is a rapid increase in the consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, reaching around 1,200 units . This sharp rise suggests a significant shift in consumer preferences, likely driven by initial perceptions of health and environmental benefits associated with alternative milk types . The mean habit factor for whole milk starts at a baseline value of 1.0000 .  The initial phase is crucial as it sets the stage for how perceptions and habits start to form and differentiate among the population . The mean value environmental deviation starts at 0.0, indicating that initially, the agents' health perceptions are perfectly aligned with their milk choices .
