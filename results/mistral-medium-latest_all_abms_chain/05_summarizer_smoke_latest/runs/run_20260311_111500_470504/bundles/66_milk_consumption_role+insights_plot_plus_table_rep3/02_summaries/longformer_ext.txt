An agent-based model (ABM) is a computational framework used to simulate the actions and interactions of autonomous agents, with the goal of assessing their effects on the system as a whole. In this case, the ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005, focusing on the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The simulation results indicate a significant decline in the average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent over the observed period. Initially, the mean whole milk consumption starts at approximately 2654.81 units. This high consumption level rapidly decreases within the first few steps, showing a sharp downward trend. By around the fifth step, the consumption has already dropped to about 1500 units. This steep decline continues, and by the tenth step, the consumption further reduces to approximately 1000 units. The trend of decreasing consumption persists, albeit at a slower rate, reaching around 773.96 units by the 19th step. Throughout the simulation, there are minor fluctuations, but the overall trajectory remains downward. Notable points include slight increases around the 19th, 27th, and 30th steps, where consumption temporarily rises to approximately 773.96, 620.98, and 335. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial perceptions and social influences in driving significant changes in consumption behaviors. The steep initial decline suggests that interventions targeting health and environmental perceptions, as well as leveraging social networks, can be highly effective in promoting the adoption of alternative milk types.
