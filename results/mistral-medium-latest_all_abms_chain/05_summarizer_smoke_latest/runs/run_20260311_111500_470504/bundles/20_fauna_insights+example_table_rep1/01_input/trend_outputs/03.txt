The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands begins at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the initial phase, reaching approximately 120 by around 2000 ticks. This initial rise indicates a high hunting success rate early in the simulation, likely due to the abundance of prey and the initial energy levels of the foragers. The trend then stabilizes, fluctuating slightly around the maximum value of 120, suggesting an equilibrium where the hunting rate matches the prey availability and forager energy constraints. The peaks and valleys identified in the data are minimal, indicating a steady-state with little variation once the initial rapid increase is completed. The inflection points and change points further support this interpretation, showing significant changes in the trend early on, which then taper off as the system stabilizes. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results indicate a strong increasing trend initially, which gradually shifts to no significant trend, reinforcing the idea of an early dynamic phase followed by stabilization. For decision makers, this suggests that initial conditions and early interactions are critical in determining the long-term dynamics of the system. The equilibrium reached implies that sustainable hunting practices could be modeled under these parameters, providing insights into managing hunter-prey ecosystems.
