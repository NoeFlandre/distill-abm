The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands exhibits a rapid increase initially, followed by a gradual leveling off. In the early stages, up to approximately 500 steps, the number of females killed rises sharply from 0 to around 100. This rapid increase indicates a high initial hunting success rate or a high initial population of female prey. Following this initial surge, the rate of increase begins to slow down significantly. Between 500 and 1500 steps, the cumulative number of females killed continues to rise but at a much slower pace, eventually stabilizing around 120 females killed. This stabilization suggests that the hunting pressure and the prey population have reached a form of equilibrium, where the rate of hunting matches the rate at which new females are either born or become available for hunting. The overall trend indicates that while initial hunting efforts are highly successful, over time, the availability of female prey decreases, leading to a plateau in the cumulative number of females killed.
