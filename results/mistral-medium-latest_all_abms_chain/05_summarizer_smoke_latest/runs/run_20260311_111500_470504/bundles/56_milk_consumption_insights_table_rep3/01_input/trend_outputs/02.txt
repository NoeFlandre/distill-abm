The simulation of the agent-based model shows distinct trends in the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent from 1974 to 2005. Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of approximately 5.29 units. There is a rapid increase in consumption during the early years, with significant growth observed up to around the mid-1980s. This period is marked by a strong upward trend, indicating a swift adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk among the agents. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this increasing trend with high significance during the initial windows. 

Following this rapid increase, the consumption stabilizes and enters a phase of minor fluctuations around a higher mean value. The peaks and valleys identified during this period suggest small oscillations in consumption, but overall, the values remain relatively high and stable. The median consumption value during this stable phase is around 1220.21 units, indicating that a majority of agents have adopted skimmed and semi-skimmed milk as their primary choice. 

Towards the end of the simulation period, there is a slight resurgence in the increasing trend, as indicated by the significant upward trend in the final rolling Mann-Kendall window. The consumption reaches its peak at approximately 1456.60 units by the end of the simulation in 2005. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the initial rapid adoption phase followed by a stabilization period, suggesting that interventions or campaigns promoting skimmed and semi-skimmed milk were particularly effective in the early years. The minor fluctuations during the stable phase indicate that while the adoption rate plateaued, there were still periodic variations in consumption patterns. The final increase suggests a potential renewed interest or additional factors influencing the adoption of healthier milk options towards the end of the observed period.
