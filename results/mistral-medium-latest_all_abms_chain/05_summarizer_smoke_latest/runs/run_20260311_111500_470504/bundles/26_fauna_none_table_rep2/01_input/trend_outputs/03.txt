The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a general increasing trend over the simulation period. Initially, the count starts at zero and gradually rises, reflecting the accumulation of hunted males over time. The trend shows a rapid increase in the early stages, particularly noticeable within the first few hundred ticks, indicating a high initial hunting rate. This is followed by a more gradual increase as the simulation progresses. The data reaches a peak value of approximately 120.17 around tick 1876, after which it stabilizes with minor fluctuations. These fluctuations suggest periodic variations in hunting success or prey availability. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with periods of no significant trend and even a slight decreasing trend towards the end. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points further highlights the dynamic nature of the hunting pressure and prey population interactions. Overall, the trend suggests that the hunting pressure on male prey is initially high and then stabilizes, reflecting a possible equilibrium between hunting efforts and prey availability in the later stages of the simulation.
