The combined total energy available in the population of male prey animals begins at 12,500 units. This value experiences a rapid decline within the first 125 time steps, dropping significantly. This initial sharp decrease could be attributed to the immediate impact of hunting pressure and environmental factors on the male prey population, leading to a swift reduction in their energy reserves. Following this initial decline, there is a brief period of stabilization and minor fluctuations around the 377th time step, where the energy levels hover around 4,000 units. This period of relative stability might indicate a temporary balance between the energy intake from food and the energy expenditure due to movement and other metabolic costs. However, this stability is short-lived, and the energy levels continue to decline steadily afterward. The decline becomes more gradual and consistent, reflecting a systematic reduction in the energy reserves of the male prey population. The energy levels eventually approach zero around the 2,284th time step, indicating the near-complete depletion of energy reserves in the male prey population. This trend suggests a continuous and sustained pressure on the male prey, likely due to a combination of hunting, environmental stress, and internal mortality factors. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms these observations, showing significant decreasing trends across multiple windows, with only brief periods of non-significant trends or slight increases. The oscillation indices and change points further highlight the periods of minor fluctuations and temporary stabilizations within the overall declining trend.
