 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  The average weekly consumption of whole milk per person starts at approximately 2654.81 units . This high initial value indicates a strong preference and habit for whole milk consumption among the British public at the beginning of the simulation period in 1974 . The consumption then declines rapidly over the first few time steps, reaching around 773.96 units by step 19 .  The total number of milk choices made by all agents combined starts at 0 and increases linearly over the 33 time steps, reaching a final value of 32,000 . As time progresses, the number of choices for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk rises, reflecting a shift in consumer behavior influenced by the model's parameters such as social influence, health perceptions, and habit thresholds .  The mean quantity consumed stabilizes and oscillates around a median value of 1220.21 units, with minor fluctuations . These fluctuations are relatively small, indicating a period of equilibrium where the adoption rate of alternative milk types has plateaued . By the end of the simulation, around the 27th time step, the mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed begins to increase again, reaching a maximum value of approximately 1456.60 units .  The mean value health deviation starts at 0, indicating that initially, the agents' perceived health values align perfectly with the health values implied by their milk choices . From step 12 to step 28, the deviation continues to increase but at a more gradual pace, reflecting a steady diversification in agent choices over time . The overall trend indicates that as agents interact and influence each other, their choices become more varied .
