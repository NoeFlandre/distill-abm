The mean choice function deviation in the agent-based model simulating milk consumption behaviors shows a clear increasing trend over the time steps analyzed. Initially, the deviation starts at 0.0000 and exhibits a rapid increase in the early stages, reaching approximately 0.6 by the 7th step. This early phase indicates a significant initial divergence in agent choices as they begin to evaluate and adjust their milk consumption habits. Following this rapid increase, the deviation continues to rise but at a slower rate, indicating a stabilization phase where agents' choices become more consistent relative to each other. By the 12th step, the deviation is around 0.67, and it continues to gradually increase, reaching a final value of approximately 0.75 by the 32nd step. The inflection points at steps 7, 12, and 28 suggest notable changes in the rate of deviation increase, reflecting periods where the agents' decision-making processes undergo significant adjustments. The consistent increasing trend across all rolling Mann-Kendall windows further confirms the steady rise in deviation throughout the simulation period. For decision makers, this trend implies that as agents become more accustomed to evaluating their choices based on health, environmental factors, and social influences, their decisions diverge more significantly from one another initially but then stabilize into a more predictable pattern. This insight can help in understanding how public perception and social norms evolve over time and influence consumption behaviors.
