 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices, specifically the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk .  The total number of whole milk choices starts at zero and increases steadily over the 30 time steps . The linear trend with minimal variance across simulation runs indicates a predictable and uniform adoption pattern among the agents . The plateaus indicate periods where additional strategies might be needed to sustain growth or address market saturation .  The mean habit factor for whole milk starts at a value of 1.00 and rapidly increases to approximately 1.02 within the first few steps . Following this sharp increase, the mean quantity stabilizes and fluctuates slightly around the 1200 to 1300 units range from step 10 to step 25 . After step 25, there is a noticeable increase again, reaching around 1400 units by step 30 . This second rise could imply a renewed influence or a secondary wave of adoption, possibly due to reinforced social norms  Social influence and habit formation might be balancing out, leading to a more consistent deviation . For decision makers, understanding these trends can provide insights into how public perceptions and behaviors regarding milk consumption evolve over time . The initial rapid increase in deviation highlights the importance of early interventions to align health perceptions with actual choices .
