The consumption then increases rapidly, indicating a significant shift in consumer behavior towards skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. This rapid increase could be attributed to the influence of health and environmental perceptions, social interactions, and the breaking of old habits associated with whole milk consumption. By the end of the simulation, the average weekly consumption reaches a peak of approximately 1456.60 units, showing a substantial adoption of alternative milk types. The mean consumption over the entire period is around 1051.59 units, with a median of 1220.21 units, indicating that the majority of the time steps have high consumption values. The standard deviation of 405.15 units suggests some variability in the consumption patterns, but the overall trend is strongly upwards. The presence of multiple peaks and valleys suggests periods of fluctuation in consumption, potentially due to varying influences of social norms, habits, and individual evaluations. The inflection points indicate significant changes in the trend at various time steps, reflecting shifts in the underlying dynamics of the model. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results show that the initial trends are significantly increasing, followed by periods of no significant trend, and then a return to increasing trends. This pattern suggests that the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk is not uniform but occurs in waves, influenced by the complex interplay of the model's parameters. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial perceptions and social influences in driving the adoption of healthier milk alternatives. The rapid increase in consumption suggests that once the shift begins, it can quickly gain momentum, leading to widespread adoption.
