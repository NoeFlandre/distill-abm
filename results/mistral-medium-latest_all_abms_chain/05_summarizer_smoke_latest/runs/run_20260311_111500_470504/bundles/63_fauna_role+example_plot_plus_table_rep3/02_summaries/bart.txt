 The Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbiivores . Model considers various factors such as environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, movement, sexual dimorphism, and life history of animals . The model allows users to manipulate various parameters to establish the socio-natural dynamics of the experiment matrix .  The model's landscape consists of an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, existing on a 211x211 patch matrix with a relative spatial scale . Hunters are embedded with logic approximating dynamic optimal foraging theory . Ungulate herds are sexually dimorphic, with distinct food values and processing costs for males and females . Animals can reproduce, and users can control sex ratios and reproduction probability .  The system shows a clear trend towards stabilization after an initial period of rapid increase . The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at zero and remains at this level until around 725 time steps . The number of births begins to decline steadily, indicating a potential shift in the environmental or population dynamics that reduces reproductive success .  The total number of animals in the simulation starts at approximately 167 individuals . The total energy of male animals starts at 12,500 units and exhibits a rapid decline within the first 125 time steps, reaching approximately 11,608 units . After time step 500, the decline resumes more steadily, with the energy levels dropping to near zero by time step 2310 . This prolonged decline suggests a consistent reduction in the prey population due to sustained hunting pressure .  The percentage of green patches in the landscape starts at approximately 33% and remains relatively stable throughout the simulation . The maximum value remains at the initial 33.0002%, indicating that the landscape never exceeds its starting vegetation cover . The dominant oscillation period of 63 time steps may correspond to seasonal cycles or other periodic environmental factors affecting the prey population .
