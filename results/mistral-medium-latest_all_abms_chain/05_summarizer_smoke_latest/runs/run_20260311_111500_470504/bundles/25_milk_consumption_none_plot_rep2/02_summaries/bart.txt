 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices . The model includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where influence is a function of interaction probability and agent susceptibility .  Data from the repeated simulations of the agent-based model show a clear and consistent trend in the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk over time . Initially, the total number of milk choices is very low, starting close to zero . By around the 5th step, the number of these choices begins to rise more noticeably, reaching approximately 2,500 . The growth trend remains consistent and accelerates as the steps progress .  The habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consistently increases throughout the entire period . The mean choice function deviation starts at a low value close to zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the early steps . The trend then shows a steady increase with minor fluctuations, reaching a value of around 0.55 by step 10 . The overall trend suggests that initially, there is a significant change in the deviation, which then stabilizes as the simulation progresses, reflecting the evolving perceptions and choices
