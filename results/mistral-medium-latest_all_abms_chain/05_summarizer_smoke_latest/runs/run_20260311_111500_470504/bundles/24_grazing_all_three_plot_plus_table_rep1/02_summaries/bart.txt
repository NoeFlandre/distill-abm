 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors .  The mean biomass across the simulation period is around 51,529 units, with a standard deviation of approximately 8,000 units, indicating some variability but a general trend towards recovery and growth . Initial decline could be attributed to the grazing pressure exerted by livestock, which consumes the available biomass faster than it can regenerate in the early stages of the simulation . After reaching this low point, the biomass begins a steady recovery . This recovery phase is marked by a consistent increase in biomass .  The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households starts at 0 and increases rapidly in the early stages, reaching approximately 60 by step 25 . By step 60, the cumulative destocking reaches around 80, and this trend continues at a steady but slower pace until the end of the simulation . For decision-makers, these trends imply that interventions may be necessary to address growing inequalities in livestock health .  The stabilization phase indicates that after the initial adjustments, the system reaches a more predictable state. This insight can help in designing interventions that consider the early dynamics of household behaviors and the eventual stabilization in the long term.    household behaviors .
