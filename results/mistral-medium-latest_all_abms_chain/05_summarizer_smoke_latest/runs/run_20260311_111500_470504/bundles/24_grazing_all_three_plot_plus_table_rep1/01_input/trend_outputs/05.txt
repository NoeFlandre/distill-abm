The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households starts at 90. This high initial value reflects the starting conditions of the simulation where each household begins with a substantial herd size. The number of livestock declines rapidly over the first 20 time steps, reaching a minimum value of approximately 41 at time step 37. This sharp decline could be attributed to the initial adjustment phase where households are adapting to the environmental conditions and possibly destocking livestock to align with the carrying capacity of the pastures. 

Following this initial decline, the number of livestock begins to increase gradually from time step 44 onwards. This trend continues steadily until the end of the simulation, reaching a value of approximately 47.55 at time step 100. The increase suggests that households are able to manage their livestock more effectively over time, possibly due to learning and adaptation to the environmental conditions. The steady rise indicates a stabilization phase where the livestock numbers are more in balance with the available resources. 

The statistical analysis reveals significant trends in different time windows. Initially, there is a strong decreasing trend in the number of livestock, which is significant across the first three windows (0-32 time steps). This is followed by a period of no significant trend (33-43 time steps), indicating a stabilization phase. Subsequently, there is a significant increasing trend observed from time step 44 onwards, which continues until the end of the simulation. This increasing trend, although modest in slope, indicates a gradual improvement in livestock management and possibly an adaptation to the ecological conditions. 

For decision makers, the initial rapid decline in livestock numbers highlights the importance of careful management during the early stages of grazing systems to avoid overstocking and subsequent destocking. The subsequent gradual increase suggests that adaptive management strategies can lead to more sustainable livestock numbers over time. This underscores the potential benefits of adaptive co-management practices and the importance of allowing households to learn and adapt to their environment.
