The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households exhibits distinct trends over the 100 steps of the simulation. Initially, the mean livestock count starts at approximately 90. This high initial value sharply declines within the first 20 steps, reaching a low point of around 40. This rapid decrease suggests a significant initial adjustment period where households may be adapting to the ecological and climatic conditions, leading to a substantial destocking of livestock. 

Following this sharp decline, the mean livestock count stabilizes and remains relatively constant between steps 20 and 60, fluctuating around the 40 to 50 range. This period of stability indicates that households have adapted to the conditions and have found a balance in their livestock management practices. 

After step 60, there is a gradual increase in the mean livestock count, which continues until the end of the simulation at step 100. The livestock count rises to approximately 60, indicating a recovery phase where households are possibly benefiting from accumulated knowledge and adaptive strategies, leading to an increase in livestock numbers. 

For decision makers, the initial sharp decline highlights the importance of preparing for significant adjustments in livestock management during the early stages of adaptive co-management. The subsequent stabilization period suggests that households can achieve a balance, but it may take time and resources to reach this state. The final recovery phase indicates the potential long-term benefits of adaptive strategies, emphasizing the value of investing in learning and adaptive practices for sustainable livestock management.
