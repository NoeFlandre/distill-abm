The data from the repeated simulations of the agent-based model reveal several distinct trends in the number of households engaging in social learning over time. Initially, at step 0, no households have engaged in social learning. By step 10, a significant increase is observed, with the count of households engaging in social learning rising sharply to various levels between approximately 12 and 30 households. This rapid initial adoption phase indicates a quick uptake of social learning behaviors among the households. 

Between steps 10 and 20, the number of households engaging in social learning continues to increase, albeit at a slower rate, reaching values between approximately 24 and 42 households. This phase suggests a period of continued but gradual adoption of social learning. By step 30, the count of households engaging in social learning stabilizes for most simulations, with values ranging from approximately 18 to 54 households. This stabilization indicates that a significant portion of the population has adopted social learning behaviors, and the rate of new adoptions has slowed down. 

From step 30 onwards, the number of households engaging in social learning remains relatively constant, with minor fluctuations. This long-term stability suggests that the households have reached a steady state in terms of social learning adoption. The final counts of households engaging in social learning vary widely, indicating different levels of social learning adoption across simulations. 

For decision-makers, these trends imply that social learning behaviors can be quickly adopted initially but may take time to reach a steady state. The wide range of final adoption levels suggests that different initial conditions or parameters can significantly influence the extent of social learning in the population. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions that promote adaptive management strategies and enhance the resilience and sustainability of pastoral systems.
