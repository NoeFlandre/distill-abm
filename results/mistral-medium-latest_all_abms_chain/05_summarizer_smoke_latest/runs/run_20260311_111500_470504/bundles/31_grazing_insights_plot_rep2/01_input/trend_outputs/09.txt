The simulations of the agent-based model show a consistent upward trend in the mean destock total of households over time. Initially, the mean destock total starts near zero and increases rapidly within the first 20 steps, reaching approximately 40 to 60. This rapid increase suggests that households are quickly adapting to initial conditions and making significant adjustments to their livestock numbers early on. Following this initial phase, the rate of increase in destocking slows down, but the total continues to rise steadily. By around 40 steps, the mean destock total ranges between 70 and 100, indicating a period of continued but more gradual adjustments. As the simulation progresses to 100 steps, the mean destock total stabilizes somewhat, with values ranging from approximately 80 to 140. The highest values observed towards the end of the simulation period suggest that some households continue to make substantial adjustments even after the initial rapid changes. For decision makers, these trends imply that initial conditions and early interventions are critical in shaping long-term outcomes. The rapid early increase in destocking highlights the importance of early adaptive strategies, while the continued gradual increase suggests the need for ongoing management and support to sustain household livelihoods.
