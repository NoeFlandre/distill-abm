The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, there is a rapid increase in the number of males killed, starting from 0 and rising steeply. This phase of rapid increase continues until around 500 steps, where the rate of increase begins to slow down. By approximately 1000 steps, the cumulative number of males killed reaches a plateau, stabilizing around 120 males killed. This plateau indicates that the rate at which males are being killed has significantly reduced, and the cumulative count remains relatively constant for the remainder of the simulation period. The mean number of males killed across the simulations is approximately 100, with a median of 116.5, suggesting that in most simulations, the number of males killed tends to be high. The standard deviation of 32.5 indicates some variability in the outcomes, but the overall trend is consistent across different runs. The analysis of trends using the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows significant increasing trends in the early phases of the simulation, which transition to no significant trend and eventually to a slight decreasing trend towards the end. This suggests that the initial conditions and early dynamics are crucial in determining the overall outcome of the number of males killed. For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressure and early environmental conditions significantly impact the cumulative number of males killed. The plateau phase indicates a potential equilibrium point where the hunting pressure balances with other factors such as prey availability and environmental conditions. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions or policies aimed at managing hunting pressures and conserving megaherbivore populations.
