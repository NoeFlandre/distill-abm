The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 150 deaths. This initial rapid increase suggests a high initial mortality rate, which could be attributed to a combination of predation pressure from human hunters and natural causes. Following this steep rise, the rate of increase in the number of deaths begins to slow down significantly. By around 1000 time steps, the cumulative number of deaths stabilizes and approaches an asymptote near 250 deaths. This stabilization indicates that the population has reached a state where the rate of new deaths is balanced by the remaining population size, suggesting a potential equilibrium between births and deaths. The variability among different simulation runs is relatively low, indicating consistent dynamics across multiple simulations. This consistency suggests that the model parameters and initial conditions lead to predictable outcomes in terms of prey mortality over time.
