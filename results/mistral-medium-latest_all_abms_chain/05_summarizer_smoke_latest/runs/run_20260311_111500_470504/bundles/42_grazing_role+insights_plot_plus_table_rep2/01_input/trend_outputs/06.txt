The analysis of the simulation results reveals several key trends and insights regarding the destocking of livestock across households over time. Initially, there is a sharp increase in the mean destocking of households, peaking at approximately 6.61 livestock at step 3. This peak is followed by a rapid decline, with significant reductions observed by step 8, where the destocking value drops to around 3.33 livestock. This initial spike and subsequent decline suggest a period of acute resource scarcity or environmental stress that prompts households to reduce their livestock numbers drastically. 

Following this initial phase, the destocking values continue to decrease, albeit with some fluctuations. Notable peaks and valleys are observed at various steps, such as step 9 (3.37 livestock), step 12 (2.74 livestock), and step 17 (1.47 livestock). These fluctuations indicate periodic stresses or adjustments in household strategies. By step 27, the destocking values stabilize around 0.51 livestock, suggesting a period of relative stability where households have adapted to the environmental conditions or resource availability. 

The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates significant decreasing trends in destocking values during the initial phases (steps 0-32), with tau values ranging from -0.491 to -0.855 and significant p-values. This decreasing trend suggests a consistent reduction in the need for destocking over time. However, from steps 33 to 65, the trend stabilizes, showing no significant trend, indicating a period of equilibrium. Interestingly, a slight increasing trend is observed from steps 66 to 76, with a tau value of 0.509 and a significant p-value, suggesting a minor resurgence in destocking needs. 

The change points identified at steps 30 and 100 further emphasize the shifts in destocking dynamics. The initial change point at step 30 marks the transition from a high destocking phase to a more stable period, while the change point at step 100 indicates the end of significant fluctuations and the onset of a stable phase. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial adaptive strategies in response to environmental stresses. The rapid reduction in destocking values suggests that households can effectively adapt to resource scarcity over time. However, the periodic fluctuations indicate the need for continuous monitoring and potential intervention to manage resource availability and household livelihoods. The stabilization of destocking values in the later steps suggests that long-term strategies can lead to sustainable grazing practices, but the slight increasing trend towards the end underscores the need for ongoing vigilance and adaptive management practices.
