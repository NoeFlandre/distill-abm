The cumulative number of postpartum females killed starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 100. This rapid initial increase indicates a high initial hunting pressure on postpartum females, likely due to their vulnerability and the high food value they represent to hunters. Around the 500 to 1000 time steps mark, the rate of increase slows down, and the cumulative number stabilizes around 120. This stabilization suggests that the population of postpartum females has either been significantly reduced or that the remaining females have adapted to avoid predation more effectively. The variance among different simulation runs is relatively low initially but increases slightly as time progresses, indicating some variability in hunting success or prey availability across different simulations. For decision makers, these trends highlight the critical early period where hunting pressure is most intense and suggest that interventions aimed at protecting postpartum females should be prioritized during this initial phase to prevent significant population decline.
