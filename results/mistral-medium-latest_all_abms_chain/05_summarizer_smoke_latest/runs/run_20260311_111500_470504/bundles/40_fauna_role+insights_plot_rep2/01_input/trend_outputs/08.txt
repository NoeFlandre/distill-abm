The number of male animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) starts at approximately 120 individuals. This initial population size is consistent with the set value of 250 total prey, assuming a roughly balanced sex ratio. Over the first 500 steps, there is a noticeable decline in the number of male animals, dropping to around 60 individuals. This rapid decrease suggests a high initial hunting pressure or mortality rate. Between 500 and 1000 steps, the decline continues but at a slower rate, with the population reducing to about 20 individuals. This phase indicates a period of sustained pressure on the male population, potentially due to continued hunting and environmental factors. Beyond 1000 steps, the number of male animals approaches zero, with complete extinction occurring around 1500 steps. This trend highlights the vulnerability of the male population under the given conditions of the model. For decision makers, these trends underscore the critical impact of initial hunting pressure and environmental conditions on the survival of male megaherbivores. The rapid decline and eventual extinction suggest that even with a balanced initial population, sustained pressure can lead to significant population reductions and potential extinction. This information is crucial for understanding the dynamics of megaherbivore populations and the factors contributing to their decline.
