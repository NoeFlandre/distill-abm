An agent-based model (ABM) is a computational framework used to simulate the actions and interactions of autonomous agents, with the goal of assessing their effects on the system as a whole. In this case, the ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005, focusing on the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice. The main outcome of the ABM is the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, with a median of approximately 2654.81 units. This initial value indicates a strong preference or habit for whole milk consumption among the agents at the start of the simulation. The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation is informed by both theoretical grounding and empirical data, with calibration performed against observed macro-level data. The model compares two variants: one using a threshold-based approach and the other using a probability-based approach for how agents become disposed to consider their choices. Agents in the model represent adult consumers who make milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics , habit formation, and positive perceptions of the health and environmental benefits of alternative milk types.
