The simulation results indicate that the mean household risk attitude in the agent-based model exhibits distinct trends over the 100 time steps. Initially, the mean household risk attitude starts at a low value of approximately 0.0074. During the early stages, there is a rapid increase in the risk attitude, reaching a peak value of around 0.3814 at time step 33. This upward trend is statistically significant, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall test, which shows a strong increasing trend with high tau values and significant p-values in the initial windows. 

Following this peak, the risk attitude experiences a gradual decline. The decline is statistically significant in the subsequent windows, as evidenced by negative tau values and significant p-values in the rolling Mann-Kendall test. The risk attitude stabilizes around time step 50, with no significant trends observed in the later windows. The final value of the mean household risk attitude at time step 100 is approximately 0.1911. 

The data also shows several inflection points and oscillations, indicating periods of change in the trend. The dominant oscillation period is around 49 time steps, suggesting cyclical patterns in the risk attitude over the simulation period. 

For decision-makers, these trends suggest that household risk attitudes initially increase significantly as households possibly adapt to new information or conditions. However, over time, the risk attitudes tend to stabilize, indicating a potential adaptation or learning effect among the households. The cyclical patterns and inflection points highlight periods of adjustment and potential strategy switching among households.
