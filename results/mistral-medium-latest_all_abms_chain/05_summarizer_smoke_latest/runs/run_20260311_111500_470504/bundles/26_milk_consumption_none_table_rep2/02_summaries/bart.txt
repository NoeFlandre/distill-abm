 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices . The model includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where influence is a function of interaction probability and agent susceptibility .  Simulation of milk consumption choices by agents from 1974 to 2005 shows a consistent and steady increase in the total number of milk choices made over time . Initially, the number of times agents chose skimmed or semi-skimmed milk was zero . Over time, this number steadily increased, reaching a peak of approximately 25,643.83 at the end of the simulation period . No peaks, valleys, or inflection points were detected, underscoring the simplicity and uniformity of the trend .  The mean factor applied to the whole milk choice function due to habit shows a consistent increasing trend over the simulation period from 1974 to 2005 . The early 1970s and late 1990s to early 2000s show strong increasing trends, while the mid-1980s to mid-1990s exhibit no significant trend . The dominant oscillation period of 15 time steps suggests cyclical patterns in the adoption behavior .  The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates an initial increasing trend in the early time steps, which transitions into periods of no significant trend as the simulation progresses . Overall the mean deviation rapidly increases initially and then stabilizes with minor oscillations around a high value for the remainder of the simulation period .
