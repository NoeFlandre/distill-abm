The total energy of all animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent downward trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of approximately 16,666.67 units. This high initial energy level rapidly declines within the first few hundred time steps, indicating a sharp decrease in the overall energy of the prey population. By around 500 time steps, the total energy has dropped significantly to approximately 4,000 units. This rapid decline continues, albeit at a slower rate, with the total energy further decreasing to around 1,000 units by 1,000 time steps. Beyond this point, the decline becomes more gradual, with the total energy approaching zero as the simulation progresses towards 2,312 time steps. The statistical analysis reveals multiple inflection points and change points, indicating periods where the rate of decline changes. Notably, the rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms significant decreasing trends across various windows, reinforcing the overall downward trajectory of the total energy. The presence of peaks and valleys suggests minor fluctuations within the general decline, but these do not alter the primary trend. For decision-makers, this trend implies that under the given parameters and conditions of the MHPM, the prey population's energy reserves are consistently depleted over time, leading to a potential extinction scenario. This insight could be crucial for understanding the dynamics of megaherbivore extinction and the impact of various factors such as hunting pressure and environmental conditions.
