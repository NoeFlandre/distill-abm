 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent in the simulation exhibits a clear and consistent downward trend over the observed time period . For decision makers, this trend underscores a significant shift away from whole milk consumption over time . Understanding these dynamics can help in strategizing interventions or marketing efforts aimed at influencing milk consumption patterns .  Simulation results indicate a consistent and significant increasing trend in the total number of times agents chose skimmed or semi-skimmed milk over the observed time steps . For decision-makers, these trends imply a growing preference or habit formation towards whole milk among agents over time .  Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of 5.29 units . This is followed by a rapid increase in the early stages of the simulation, with significant growth observed up to around the 9th time step . This initial phase of rapid adoption suggests that agents quickly become disposed to consider alternative milk types .  The mean deviation between the agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.0000 and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation . The consistent increase in the mean percentage difference implies that over time, agents in the model are increasingly diverging in their choice function scores . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions to better align health perceptions with consumption behaviors .
