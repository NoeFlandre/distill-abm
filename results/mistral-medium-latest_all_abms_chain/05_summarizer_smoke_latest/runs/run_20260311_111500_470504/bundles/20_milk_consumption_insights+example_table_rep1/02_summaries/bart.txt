 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices, specifically the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk .  The total number of milk choices made by all agents starts at 0 and increases steadily over the entire simulation period . The steady increase over time suggests a growing adoption or preference for whole milk among the agents . Valleys are observed at time steps 12, 20, 23, and 18, with values around 1233.62, 1214.46, 1209.70, and 1183.39 units respectively .  The mean quantity of whole milk consumed by agents begins at approximately 2654.81 units and exhibits a general declining trend throughout the simulation period . For decision makers, this trend implies a steady and predictable pattern of milk choices over time, which could be useful for planning and resource allocation .  The mean factor applied to the skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choice function due to habit starts at a value of 1.0000 . For decision makers, the steady increase in the habit factor implies a growing influence of habit on the choice of whole milk over time . This trend suggests that as consumers become more accustomed to their choices, the likelihood of continuing to choose whole milk increases .  This rapid increase indicates that agents' initial choices do not align well with their environmental values, suggesting a period of adjustment or exploration in their decision-making process . By around the 13th time step, the deviation reaches a peak value of approximately 13.4756 . This peak suggests that agents are experimenting with different milk types, possibly influenced by social norms, habits, or perceived health benefits .
