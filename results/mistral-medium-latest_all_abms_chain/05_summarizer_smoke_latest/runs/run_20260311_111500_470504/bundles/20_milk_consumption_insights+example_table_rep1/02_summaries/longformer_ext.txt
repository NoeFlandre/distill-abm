The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumers. The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices, specifically the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The model uses both theoretical grounding and empirical data, with calibration performed against observed macro-level data. The main outcome is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The model compares two variants: a threshold-based approach and a probability-based approach for how agents become disposed to consider their choices. Agents represent adult consumers who make milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics, modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates increasing trends in the early and later stages of the simulation, with significant increases noted in windows 1, 2, and 6. This suggests that initial adoption was rapid, followed by a period of stabilization, and then another increase towards the end. The dominant oscillation period of 15 time steps indicates cyclical patterns in consumption behavior, possibly due to periodic social or marketing influences. The overall increasing trend is consistent with the overall increasing trend. For decision makers, this trend implies a steady and predictable pattern of milk choices over time, which could be useful for planning and resource allocation in the context of milk consumption By around the 13th time step, the deviation reaches a peak value of approximately 13.4756, indicating a significant misalignment between agents' environmental values and their milk choices. This peak suggests that agents are experimenting with different milk types, possibly influenced by social norms, habits, or perceived health benefits, leading to choices that deviate from their environmental values. After reaching this peak, the deviation stabilizes and maintains a high level with minor fluctuations around the mean value of 12.6015. The minor fluctuations suggest that while the overall misalignment remains high, there are periodic adjustments in agents' choices, possibly due to ongoing social influence or habit formation. The stabilization of the deviation at a high level implies that agents' milk choices have reached a new equilibrium, where the influence of social norms, habits, and perceived characteristics of milk types outweighs their environmental values.
