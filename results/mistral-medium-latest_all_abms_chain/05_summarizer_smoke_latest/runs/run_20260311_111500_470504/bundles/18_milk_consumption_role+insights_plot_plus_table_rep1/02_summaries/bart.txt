 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  Simulation results indicate a clear and consistent downward trend in the average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent over the observed time steps . Initially, the mean whole milk consumption starts at approximately 2654.81 units . This high initial value suggests a strong preference or habit for whole milk among the agents at the beginning of the simulation . In the early stages, there is a steep decline in consumption, dropping to around 800 units by step 18 .  The trend in the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices over the simulation period shows a consistent and significant increase . For decision-makers, the linear increase in total milk choices suggests a steady engagement of agents in the decision-making process over time .  The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents in the simulation exhibits distinct trends over time . Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of approximately 5.29 units . This is followed by a rapid increase in consumption, reaching around 1220 units by the 11th step . Following this initial surge, consumption levels enter a phase of relative stability with minor fluctuations .  The mean choice function deviation in the agent-based model shows a clear trend of rapid initial increase followed by a gradual stabilization period . The inflection points at steps 7, 12, and 28 highlight key transitions in the rate of change . Understanding these dynamics can help in designing timely and effective strategies to guide consumer behavior towards desired outcomes .
