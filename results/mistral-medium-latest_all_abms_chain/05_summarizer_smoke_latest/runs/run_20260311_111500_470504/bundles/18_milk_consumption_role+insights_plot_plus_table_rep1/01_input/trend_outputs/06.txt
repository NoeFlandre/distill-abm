The simulation results indicate a clear and consistent downward trend in the mean quantity of whole milk consumed by agents over the time steps analyzed. Initially, the mean whole milk quantity starts at approximately 2654.81 units and exhibits a sharp decline within the first few steps, dropping significantly to around 800 units by step 18. This rapid decrease suggests a substantial shift in consumption patterns early in the simulation. Following this initial sharp decline, the rate of decrease slows, and the mean quantity stabilizes somewhat, fluctuating around a median value of approximately 816.85 units. Notable inflection points occur at steps 5, 13, 16, 19, 24, 25, 27, and 30, indicating periods where the rate of change in consumption patterns shifts. The trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms significant decreasing trends in several windows, particularly in the early and middle stages of the simulation. The mean whole milk quantity continues to decline gradually, reaching a minimum value of approximately 253.10 units by the final step. The presence of peaks and valleys, although not highly prominent, suggests minor fluctuations in consumption patterns but does not alter the overall downward trajectory. For decision-makers, these trends imply that interventions or factors influencing milk choice early in the simulation period have a substantial impact on reducing whole milk consumption. The stabilization periods suggest that after initial changes, consumption patterns become more resistant to further reductions, indicating the possible entrenchment of new habits or norms. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing policies or campaigns aimed at further reducing whole milk consumption or promoting alternative milk types.
