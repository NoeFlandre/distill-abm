The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, the number of males killed starts at zero and increases rapidly. This rapid increase continues until around 500 steps, where the rate of increase begins to slow down. By approximately 1000 steps, the number of males killed starts to plateau, indicating a significant reduction in the rate of increase. The cumulative number of males killed stabilizes around 120, with minor fluctuations observed thereafter. The mean number of males killed across the simulations is approximately 100, with a median of 116.5, suggesting that most simulations resulted in a high number of males killed. The standard deviation of 32.5 indicates some variability among the simulations. The maximum value of 120.17, reached at around 1876 steps, represents the peak number of males killed in the simulations. The analysis of trends using the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulations, followed by periods of no significant trend and a slight decreasing trend towards the end. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points further highlights the dynamic nature of the hunting pressure over time. Overall, the data suggests a rapid initial increase in the number of males killed, followed by a plateau phase with minor fluctuations, reflecting the complex interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting behavior, and animal life history in the model.
