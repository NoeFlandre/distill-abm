The cumulative number of postpartum females killed in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a rapid increase initially, followed by a gradual stabilization. Initially, the number of postpartum females killed rises sharply from 0 to around 100 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase indicates a high hunting rate of postpartum females by foragers during the early stages of the simulation. Following this initial surge, the rate of increase slows down significantly, and the cumulative number of postpartum females killed stabilizes around 117. This stabilization suggests that the hunting pressure reaches a point of equilibrium where the number of postpartum females killed plateaus, indicating a balance between the hunting activities and the remaining population of postpartum females. The statistical analysis reveals that the mean number of postpartum females killed is approximately 102.44, with a median of 116.86, indicating that the data is slightly skewed towards the lower end. The standard deviation of 30.00 and variance of 900.13 suggest some variability in the number of postpartum females killed across different simulations, but the overall trend remains consistent. The analysis of trends using the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows that there is a significant increasing trend in the early stages, which gradually diminishes and stabilizes, reflecting the initial rapid hunting followed by a plateau. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points further supports the observation of an initial rapid increase followed by stabilization. The dominant oscillation period and oscillation indices suggest minor periodic fluctuations around the stabilized value, but these do not significantly alter the overall trend.
