The combined total energy available in the population of male prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear overall decreasing trend over time. Initially, the energy level starts at a high value of 12500 units. This high energy level experiences a rapid decline in the early stages of the simulation, dropping significantly within the first few hundred ticks. By around tick 377, the energy level stabilizes temporarily around 4002 units, showing minor fluctuations and small peaks and valleys, indicating some periodic oscillations in the energy levels. These oscillations have a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, suggesting a cyclical pattern in the energy dynamics of the male prey population. Despite these oscillations, the overall trend continues to be a decrease in energy levels. After tick 1624, the rate of decrease slows down considerably, with the energy levels approaching zero around tick 2284 and remaining there for the rest of the simulation. The inflection points and change points identified in the data indicate several shifts in the rate of energy decline, reflecting changes in the dynamics of the male prey population. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms these trends, showing significant decreasing trends in most windows, with a brief period of increasing trend around ticks 1392 to 1623, followed by a return to a decreasing trend. This analysis suggests that the male prey population undergoes significant stress and decline in energy levels over the course of the simulation, with periodic fluctuations but an overall downward trajectory.
