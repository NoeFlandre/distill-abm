This decline likely reflects the onset of grazing activities by livestock, which reduces the available green biomass. After this decline, the mean green biomass remains relatively stable, fluctuating slightly around 42,000 units until about the 60th time step. This period of stability suggests a balance between vegetation regrowth and grazing pressure. Beyond the 60th time step, the mean green biomass begins to increase steadily, reaching approximately 62,000 units by the 100th time step. This significant rise indicates a recovery phase where vegetation growth outpaces grazing pressure, possibly due to adaptive management strategies or favorable ecological conditions. The trends suggest that while initial grazing pressure reduces green biomass, adaptive strategies and ecological resilience can lead to recovery and increased biomass over time. For decision makers, this implies the importance of sustainable grazing practices and adaptive management to ensure long-term ecological balance and livestock sustainability.
