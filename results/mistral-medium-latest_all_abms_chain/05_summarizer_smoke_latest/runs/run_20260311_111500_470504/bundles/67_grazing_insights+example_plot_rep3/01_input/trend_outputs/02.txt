The mean household risk attitude starts with a value of approximately 0.5 across all simulations. In the initial 20 time steps, there is a noticeable divergence in the trends. Some households exhibit a sharp increase in risk attitude, reaching values as high as 0.8, while others show a significant decrease, dropping to values as low as -0.2. This divergence suggests that households are rapidly adapting their risk attitudes based on initial experiences and learning. Between time steps 20 and 40, the trends stabilize somewhat, with households maintaining their new risk attitudes. This period of stabilization indicates a phase where households have settled into their adaptive strategies. After time step 40, the trends remain relatively constant with minor fluctuations, indicating that households have established their risk attitudes and are making fewer adjustments. The stabilization of risk attitudes implies that households have likely found a balance in their adaptive management strategies, which could be crucial for long-term sustainability and resilience in pastoral systems. For decision-makers, understanding these trends can provide insights into how different households adapt to ecological and climatic conditions over time, which can inform policies aimed at enhancing the resilience and sustainability of pastoral systems.
