The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a general increasing trend over time, with specific dynamics and periods of stability. Initially, the count starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase during the early time steps, indicating a high rate of hunting success. This rapid increase continues until around the 850th tick, where the rate of increase starts to slow down significantly. 

Following this initial phase, the trend transitions into a period of much slower growth, with the cumulative count approaching a plateau. This plateau phase is characterized by minor fluctuations and a near-constant value, suggesting that the rate at which postpartum females are hunted stabilizes and becomes relatively low. The maximum value reached is around 119, with the mean value of the series being approximately 102.44 and the median around 116.86. These values indicate that while there are periods of slight increases and decreases, the overall trend stabilizes around these central values. 

The analysis of the rolling Mann-Kendall trend indicates significant increasing trends in the early phases, which gradually shift to periods with no significant trend and eventually to a slight decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation. This suggests that the hunting pressure on postpartum females initially increases but eventually stabilizes, reflecting a possible equilibrium in the predator-prey dynamics within the model. 

For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressures can be quite high, but over time, the system may reach a state of equilibrium where the rate of hunting stabilizes. This could inform strategies related to conservation efforts and the management of hunting practices to prevent the over-exploitation of prey populations.
