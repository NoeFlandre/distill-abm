The combined total energy available in the population of male prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear overall decreasing trend over time. Initially, the energy level starts at 12500 units. This high initial value is followed by a rapid decline, with significant drops observed early in the simulation. By the 65th tick, the energy level decreases to approximately 11608 units, marking the first notable peak. This downward trend continues, with several smaller peaks and valleys indicating periods of slight recovery followed by further declines. For instance, around the 377th tick, there is a peak at 4002 units, followed by minor fluctuations. The energy levels continue to decrease, with occasional small increases, but the overall trajectory remains downward. By the 2284th tick, the energy level reaches zero, indicating the complete depletion of energy in the male prey population. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this decreasing trend across multiple windows, with significant negative slopes and tau values. The presence of several inflection points and change points suggests periods where the rate of decline changes, but the overall trend remains consistent. The oscillation indices indicate periodic fluctuations, but these are minor compared to the overall decline. For decision makers, this trend suggests that under the given parameters, the male prey population's energy resources are consistently depleted over time, leading to potential extinction events. This insight can inform strategies related to conservation efforts, hunting regulations, and environmental management to mitigate such outcomes.
