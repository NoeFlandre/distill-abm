The data from the repeated simulations of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) show a clear trend in the cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals killed over time. Initially, the number of postpartum females killed starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 steps. By around 500 steps, the cumulative number of postpartum females killed reaches approximately 80. This rapid increase continues, and by around 1000 steps, the cumulative number stabilizes, approaching a plateau near 120 postpartum females killed. Beyond 1000 steps, the rate of increase significantly slows down, indicating that the majority of the hunting events occur within the first 1000 steps. The cumulative number of postpartum females killed remains relatively constant after this point, with minor fluctuations. For decision makers, this trend suggests that the initial period is critical for understanding the dynamics of hunting pressure on postpartum females. The rapid increase followed by stabilization indicates that most of the hunting impact occurs early in the simulation, which could inform strategies for managing hunting pressure and conservation efforts to protect vulnerable prey populations during these critical early periods.
