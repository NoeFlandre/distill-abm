The average amount of reserve biomass available across all patches begins at 90,000 units. Initially, there is a slight increase, reaching approximately 90,523 units by the third time step. Following this, the reserve biomass experiences a gradual decline until around the 26th time step, where it reaches its minimum value of about 84,114 units. This initial decline could be attributed to the initial grazing pressure exerted by the livestock as they begin to utilize the available biomass. After this low point, the reserve biomass starts to recover, showing a consistent increasing trend. This recovery phase can be divided into several stages, with notable inflection points around the 8th and 48th time steps. The biomass increases steadily, with significant growth observed between the 33rd and 87th time steps, where the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates strong increasing trends. By the final time step, the reserve biomass reaches approximately 121,073 units, indicating a substantial recovery and growth in the biomass levels. The overall trend suggests an initial period of biomass depletion followed by a robust recovery phase, potentially reflecting adaptive management strategies and the resilience of the grazing system over time.
