The simulation of the agent-based model tracking the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent from 1974 to 2005 reveals several notable trends and dynamics. Initially, the average consumption starts at a very low value of 5.29. This value rapidly increases in the early stages of the simulation, indicating a swift adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk among the agents. By the early 1980s, the consumption rate experiences a significant rise, reaching a peak of approximately 1265.47 around 1984. This period marks the first major inflection point where the growth rate starts to stabilize. Following this peak, the consumption values exhibit minor fluctuations with several smaller peaks and valleys, suggesting periods of slight increases and decreases in consumption. Notable peaks occur around 1986, 1991, and 1994 with values approximately 1240.35, 1226.86, and 1221.57 respectively. Corresponding valleys are observed around 1987, 1993, and 1995 with values approximately 1233.62, 1214.46, and 1209.70 respectively. The trend analysis indicates significant increasing trends in the early 1980s and late 1990s, with periods of no significant trend in between. The late 1990s show another notable inflection point where the consumption rate begins to rise again, culminating in the highest recorded average weekly consumption of 1456.60 by the end of the simulation period in 2005. The overall trend demonstrates a substantial shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk over the three decades, reflecting changing consumer preferences and behaviors influenced by perceived health benefits, social norms, and other modeled factors.
