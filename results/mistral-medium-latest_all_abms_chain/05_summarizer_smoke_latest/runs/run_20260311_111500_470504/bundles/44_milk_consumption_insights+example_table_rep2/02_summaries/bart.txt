 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices, specifically the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk .  Several peaks and valleys are observed throughout the simulation, indicating periods of accelerated adoption followed by slight declines or plateaus . The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in the early and later stages of the simulation . For decision makers, these trends underscore the importance of understanding the factors driving the shift in milk consumption habits .  The overall trend of decreasing whole milk consumption implies a significant shift towards other types of milk, such as skimmed or semi-skimmed milk, over the simulated period . The data shows several peaks and valleys, indicating fluctuations in the adoption rates . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the underlying factors driving consumer behavior changes .  The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at 0.0000 and exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period . For decision makers, this trend implies a growing influence of habit on the choice of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk over time .  The change point at the 32nd time step marks the end of the initial increasing trend and the beginning of the stable phase . For decision makers, these trends imply that interventions aimed at aligning milk choices with environmental values should focus on the initial stages of the adoption process .
