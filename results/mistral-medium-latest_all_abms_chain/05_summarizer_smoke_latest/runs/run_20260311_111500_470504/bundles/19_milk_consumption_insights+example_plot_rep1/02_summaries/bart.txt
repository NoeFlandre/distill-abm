 The agent-based model aims to simulate the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choices . The model includes 1,000 agents, each part of a social network where influence is a function of interaction probability and agent susceptibility .  The total number of milk choices made by all agents combined starts at 0 and increases steadily over the 30 time steps of the simulation . The low variance among the simulation runs suggests a high degree of consistency in the model's outcomes . For decision-makers, this trend highlights the importance of early interventions and the potential for social influence to drive significant changes in consumer behavior over time .  The mean habit factor for whole milk starts at a value of 1.00 and rapidly increases within the first few steps, reaching approximately 1.02 . Following this, the habit factor begins to rise more noticeably from the 10th step onwards, indicating a growing preference or habit formation towards skimmed/semi-skimmed milk . Understanding this trend can help in predicting long-term consumption patterns .  The mean value environmental deviation starts at 0, indicating that initially, there is no discrepancy between the agents' environmental values and their milk choices . After step 20, the rate of increase slows down, and the deviation stabilizes around a value of 0.5 . The trend highlights the complexity of influencing health perceptions and behaviors, suggesting that interventions may need to be sustained and adaptive to achieve long-term alignment .
