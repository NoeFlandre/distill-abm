The data from the repeated simulations of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) show a clear trend in the cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals killed over time. Initially, the number of postpartum females killed starts at zero. As the simulation progresses, there is a rapid increase in the number of postpartum females killed, with a steep rise observed up to approximately 500 steps. During this period, the cumulative number of postpartum females killed increases from zero to around 100. Following this rapid increase, the rate of increase begins to slow down, and the curve starts to plateau. By around 1000 steps, the cumulative number of postpartum females killed stabilizes, reaching a value close to 120. Beyond this point, the number of postpartum females killed remains relatively constant, indicating that the rate of hunting and killing of postpartum females has reached an equilibrium. This trend suggests that after an initial period of rapid hunting, the system stabilizes, and the number of postpartum females killed per unit time decreases significantly, leading to a plateau in the cumulative count.
