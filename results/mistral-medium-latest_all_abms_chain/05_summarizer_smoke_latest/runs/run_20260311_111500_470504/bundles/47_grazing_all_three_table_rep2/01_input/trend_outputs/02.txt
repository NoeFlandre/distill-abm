The average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents begins at a low value of 0.0074. This initial value suggests that households start with a livestock count very close to the recommended number. Over the first few time steps, there is a rapid increase in deviation, reaching a peak value of approximately 0.3814 around time step 33. This peak indicates a significant divergence from the recommended livestock numbers, suggesting that households are either increasing or decreasing their livestock beyond the suggested thresholds. Following this peak, the deviation shows a general decreasing trend, with notable fluctuations. The deviation stabilizes somewhat towards the end of the simulation, concluding at a value of 0.1911. The overall mean deviation across the simulation is 0.2333, with a median of 0.2013, indicating that while there are periods of high deviation, the central tendency remains moderate. The standard deviation of 0.0936 and variance of 0.0088 highlight the variability in household adherence to recommended livestock numbers throughout the simulation. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in deviation during the initial phases of the simulation, followed by significant decreasing trends. Towards the latter part of the simulation, no significant trends are detected, suggesting a stabilization in household behavior regarding livestock management. The presence of multiple inflection points and oscillation indices further underscores the dynamic and fluctuating nature of household decisions over time. For decision makers, these trends imply that while initial household behaviors may lead to significant deviations from recommended practices, there is a tendency towards stabilization over time. This stabilization could be leveraged to introduce interventions aimed at reducing deviations and promoting sustainable livestock management practices.
