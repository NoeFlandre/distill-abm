The percentage of green patches, representing vegetated areas in the landscape, exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the percentage of green patches starts at approximately 33% and remains relatively stable for a short period. Around the 150th tick, a noticeable decline begins, reaching a minimum of about 25% at the 365th tick. This decline indicates a significant reduction in vegetated areas, potentially due to grazing pressure from the prey animals. Following this decline, there is a recovery phase where the percentage of green patches increases sharply, returning to around 33% by approximately the 725th tick. This recovery suggests a period of regrowth and reduced grazing pressure or other factors allowing vegetation to recover. After this recovery, the percentage of green patches stabilizes and exhibits minor fluctuations around the 33% mark for the remainder of the simulation. The minor fluctuations indicate periodic variations in vegetation cover, possibly due to seasonal effects or dynamic interactions between grazing and regrowth. The overall trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows significant increasing trends in the initial and middle phases, followed by significant decreasing trends in the later phases, indicating periods of both vegetation recovery and decline. The dominant oscillation period of 63 ticks suggests regular cyclic patterns in vegetation cover. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the dynamic balance between grazing pressure and vegetation regrowth, emphasizing the importance of managing grazing intensity to maintain vegetation cover and ensure the sustainability of the ecosystem.
