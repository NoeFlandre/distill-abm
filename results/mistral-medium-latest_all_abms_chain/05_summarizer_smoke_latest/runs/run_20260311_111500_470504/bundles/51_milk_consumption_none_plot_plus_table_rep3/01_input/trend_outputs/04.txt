The model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005, focusing on the shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The trend in the data shows a consistent increase in the total number of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk choices over time. Initially, the number of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk choices starts at zero. As time progresses, the adoption rate increases gradually at first and then more rapidly. By the end of the simulation period, the total number of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk choices reaches approximately 25,643.83. The mean number of choices over the entire period is 11,157.35, with a median of 10,679.43, indicating a steady upward trend. The standard deviation of 8,241.32 highlights the variability in adoption rates over time. The rolling Mann-Kendall test across various windows consistently shows an increasing trend, with significant positive slopes, indicating a steady rise in the adoption of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk. The inflection point at index 31 suggests a notable change in the rate of adoption around that time. The oscillation analysis indicates periodic fluctuations, but the dominant trend remains an overall increase in skimmed/semi-skimmed milk choices.
