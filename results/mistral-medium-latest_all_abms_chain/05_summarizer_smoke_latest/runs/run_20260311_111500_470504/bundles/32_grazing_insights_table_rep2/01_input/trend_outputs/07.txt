The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all households, as measured by the Gini coefficient, exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period. Initially, the Gini coefficient starts at a value of approximately 0, indicating near-perfect equality in the distribution of healthy livestock. Over time, the inequality gradually increases, reaching a value of 0.0726 by the end of the simulation. The mean value of the Gini coefficient across the simulation period is 0.0302, with a median value of 0.0229, suggesting that while the inequality is generally low, it is steadily rising. The standard deviation of 0.0239 indicates some variability in the inequality levels over time. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a significant increasing trend in inequality across all windows, with a consistent slope of approximately 0.001. This trend suggests that over time, the distribution of healthy livestock becomes increasingly unequal among households. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points indicates periods where the rate of increase in inequality shifts, potentially reflecting changes in the underlying dynamics of the system. For decision makers, this trend implies that interventions may be needed to address the growing disparity in livestock distribution to ensure more equitable outcomes among households.
