 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the original RAGE (Rangeland Use and Adaptation) model, which simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The goal is to evaluate how different behavioral types and learning strategies impact the resilience and sustainability of pastoral systems . The model allows for the adjustment of a range of parameters to observe their effects on social, ecological, and economic outcomes .  Simulation of agent-based model shows distinct trends in the average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents over the course of 100 time steps . The model exhibits several inflection points and oscillations, particularly between time steps 20 to 35, suggesting periods of adjustment and stabilization in household decision-making . Subsequently, the destocking rate continues to decrease, albeit with smaller fluctuations and peaks . The initial sharp rise and fall suggest a significant early adjustment period where households are rapidly adapting to initial conditions, possibly due to a sudden environmental change or resource limitation . For decision makers, this trend suggests that non-learning strategies may become less prevalent over time in the context of this model .
