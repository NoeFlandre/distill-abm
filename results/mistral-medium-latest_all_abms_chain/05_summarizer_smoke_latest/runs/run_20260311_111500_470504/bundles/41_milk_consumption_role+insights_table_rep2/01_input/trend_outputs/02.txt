The simulation of the agent-based model reveals several key trends in the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent over the observed period. Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of 5.29. This low initial value suggests that at the beginning of the simulation, the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk is minimal, likely due to strong initial habits favoring whole milk and lower initial perceptions of the health and environmental benefits of alternative milk types.  \n\nThe consumption then exhibits a rapid and significant increasing trend in the early stages of the simulation. This is evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall test, which shows a strong increasing trend with high significance in the initial windows. For instance, from the start to the fourth time step, the trend is increasing with a tau of 0.900 and a slope of 140.433, indicating a steep rise in consumption. This rapid increase can be attributed to the agents becoming more disposed to consider alternative milk types, influenced by parameters such as social susceptibility and social conformity. \n\nFollowing this initial surge, the consumption levels enter a phase of stabilization. From the tenth to the fourteenth time step, the rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates no significant trend, with a tau of 0.000 and a slope of 1.085. This stabilization suggests that the agents have reached a temporary equilibrium in their consumption patterns, possibly due to the balancing effects of habit thresholds and cognitive dissonance. \n\nThe consumption values then exhibit minor oscillations around a high mean value, with several peaks and valleys identified. For example, peaks are observed at time steps 11, 13, 21, and 19, with values around 1265.47, 1240.35, 1226.86, and 1221.57, respectively. These oscillations indicate periodic fluctuations in consumption, potentially due to the dynamic interactions between agents and their varying susceptibilities to social influence and norms. \n\nTowards the end of the simulation, there is a slight increasing trend again, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall test from the twenty-fifth to the twenty-ninth time step, with a tau of 1.000 and a slope of 48.544. This late-stage increase suggests a renewed disposition among agents to adopt skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, possibly due to cumulative social influences and reinforced positive perceptions of alternative milk types. \n\nThe simulation concludes with a maximum consumption value of 1456.60, indicating a substantial shift towards the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk over the observed period. This final value highlights the significant impact of social influence, habit formation, and perceptual changes on the agents' milk consumption choices. \n\nFor decision-makers, these trends underscore the importance of social influence and habit formation in driving the adoption of healthier and more environmentally friendly products. The initial rapid increase suggests that targeted interventions and marketing strategies can effectively shift consumer behavior. The subsequent stabilization and oscillations highlight the need for continuous engagement and reinforcement to maintain consumer interest and adoption. The final increasing trend indicates the potential for long-term behavioral change, emphasizing the value of sustained efforts in promoting alternative milk types.
