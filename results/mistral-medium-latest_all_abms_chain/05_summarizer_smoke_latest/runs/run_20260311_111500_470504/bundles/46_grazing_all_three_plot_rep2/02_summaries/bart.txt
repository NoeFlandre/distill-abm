 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies and the diffusion of learning behaviors among households .  The mean livestock plus destock of households begins with a high value of approximately 7 livestock per household . The initial high destocking rate suggests that households start with a significant number of livestock that cannot be sustained given the initial conditions of the pasture and climate . The low variance across simulation runs suggests consistent vegetation growth patterns .  Social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly in the first 20 time steps of the model . The rapid initial increase suggests a quick adoption of social learning behaviors among households . The number of households with social learning stabilizes across different simulation runs . For decision makers, this suggests that interventions aimed at promoting social learning could see quick initial uptake but the long-term adoption rates may vary widely .
