The simulation results indicate a consistent and steady increase in the total number of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices made by agents over time. Initially, the adoption rate is low, with the total number of choices starting at zero. However, as the simulation progresses, the rate of adoption accelerates, showing a rapid increase in the number of choices for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. By the end of the simulation, the total number of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices reaches approximately 25,643.83. The mean number of choices over the entire simulation period is around 11,157.35, with a median of 10,679.43, indicating a skewed distribution towards higher values as time progresses. The standard deviation of 8,241.32 highlights significant variability in the adoption rates over time. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results consistently show increasing trends across all windows, with significant p-values, indicating a strong and consistent upward trend in the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. This trend suggests that the parameters governing agent behavior, such as social influence, habit formation, and perception of health benefits, are effectively driving the adoption process. For decision-makers, these insights imply that interventions aimed at promoting the health and environmental benefits of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, along with leveraging social influence and habit formation, could be effective strategies to encourage the adoption of healthier milk choices.
