 The ABM is designed to replicate and understand the adoption behaviors of milk consumption by the British public from 1974 to 2005 . The model aims to explore how perception, habits, and social influence affect an individual's decision-making process regarding milk choice . The main outcome of interest is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person .  Simulation results indicate a clear and consistent downward trend in the average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent over the observed time steps . The mean whole milk consumption starts at approximately 2654.81 units . The steep decline suggests that agents are quickly adopting alternative milk types as they become more aware of health and environmental benefits .  Simulation results indicate a consistent and steady increase in the total number of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices made by agents over time . The mean number of choices over the entire simulation period is around 11,157.35, with a median of 10,679.43, indicating a skewed distribution towards higher values as time progresses . No peaks, valleys, or inflection points were detected, reinforcing the linear nature of the trend .  The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents in the simulation exhibits distinct trends over the observed time steps . For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial habits and the impact of social influence and perceptions on consumption behaviors . Understanding these dynamics can inform strategies aimed at promoting healthier or more environmentally friendly consumption choices .  The mean value environmental deviation metric starts at 0.0 and exhibits a rapid increase in the early steps . This initial phase indicates a significant divergence between the agents' perceived health values and their milk choices . The inflection points at steps 7, 12, and 28 highlight key moments where the rate of change in deviation shifts, suggesting significant adjustments in agents' decision-making processes . Understanding these phases can help in designing targeted strategies to influence milk consumption patterns effectively .
