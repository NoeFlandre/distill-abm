 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The mean household initial risk attitude remains constant at approximately 0.0074 throughout the entire simulation period of 100 steps . The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies .  The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households starts at 90.8 . This high initial value reflects the starting conditions of the simulation where each household begins with a set livestock . The trend analysis shows a decreasing trend in the initial phases, which stabilizes over time, reflecting the households' adaptation and stabilization in their livestock management practices .  The number of non-learning households starts at 20 and exhibits a slight increase in the first 10 steps, reaching a peak of 21.1 at step 8 . The inflection points identified at various time steps (45, 47, 53, 56, 57, 62, 67, 72, 74, and 75) indicate periods where the rate of change in destocking behavior shifts .
