The simulation of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decline in the number of male prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at 125 males and exhibits a sharp decrease in the early stages of the simulation. This downward trend continues steadily, with the population eventually reaching zero by the end of the simulation period. The mean number of male prey animals across the simulation is approximately 21, with a median value of about 1.35, indicating that for the majority of the simulation, the population of male prey is quite low. The standard deviation of 36.04 suggests significant variability in the population numbers at different time steps. Key inflection points and change points indicate periods where the rate of decline shifts, but the overall trend remains consistently downward. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this decreasing trend across multiple windows, with significant decreases noted in the early and middle phases of the simulation. Towards the later stages, the trend stabilizes with no significant changes, indicating that the population has nearly diminished. The oscillation indices suggest periodic fluctuations, but these are minor compared to the overall declining trend. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the vulnerability of male prey populations under the given model parameters, emphasizing the need for careful consideration of hunting pressures and environmental factors to prevent rapid declines in prey populations.
