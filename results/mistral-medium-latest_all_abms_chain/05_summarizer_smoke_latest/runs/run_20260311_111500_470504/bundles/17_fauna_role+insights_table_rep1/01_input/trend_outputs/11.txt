The combined total energy available in the population of male prey animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear overall decreasing trend over time. Initially, the energy level starts at 12500 units. There is a rapid decline in the early stages, with the energy level dropping to around 11608 units by the 65th tick. This initial sharp decline is followed by a series of smaller fluctuations and peaks, indicating periods of temporary stabilization or slight recovery in the energy levels. Notable peaks occur at ticks 377, 380, and 388, with energy values around 4002, 3986, and 3975 units respectively. These peaks suggest brief periods where the male prey population's energy levels experience minor resurgences. However, the overall trend remains a steady decline. By the 2284th tick, the energy level reaches zero, indicating the complete depletion of energy in the male prey population. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this decreasing trend across multiple windows, with significant negative slopes and tau values. The presence of several inflection points and change points further highlights the dynamic nature of the energy levels, with notable changes in the trend occurring at ticks such as 316, 453, and 516. The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of 63 ticks, suggesting a cyclical pattern in the energy levels, albeit within the broader context of decline. For decision makers, these trends imply that under the given model parameters, the male prey population faces a consistent decline in energy levels, leading to eventual depletion. This insight could be crucial for understanding the sustainability of prey populations under varying hunting pressures and environmental conditions, and for designing interventions aimed at conservation or management of megaherbivore populations.
