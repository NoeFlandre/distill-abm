The combined total energy available in the population of female prey animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear overall decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12500 units. This high energy level rapidly declines in the early stages of the simulation, with significant drops observed within the first few hundred ticks. By around tick 375, the energy levels have decreased substantially, marking the first notable change point. The decline continues, albeit at varying rates, with additional change points identified at ticks 500, 625, and 875. These change points indicate shifts in the rate of energy decrease, suggesting periods where the decline accelerates or decelerates. Throughout the simulation, the energy levels exhibit minor oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, indicating periodic fluctuations in the total energy. Despite these oscillations, the overall trend remains a steady decline. By the end of the simulation at tick 2311, the total energy available in the population of female prey animals reaches zero, indicating complete depletion. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms the significant decreasing trend across multiple windows, with only brief periods of slight increase or stability. For decision makers, this trend suggests that under the given parameters, the population of female prey animals faces a continuous and significant reduction in energy levels, ultimately leading to extinction. This insight could inform strategies aimed at conservation or understanding the impacts of hunting pressure and environmental factors on megaherbivore populations.
