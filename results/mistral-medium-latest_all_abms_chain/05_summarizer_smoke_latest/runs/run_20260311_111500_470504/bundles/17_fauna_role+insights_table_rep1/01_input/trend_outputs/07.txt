The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the death count starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase, reaching significant values early in the simulation. This early phase is marked by a steep upward trend, indicating a high rate of prey mortality. The first notable change point occurs around tick 125, followed by subsequent change points at regular intervals, suggesting periodic shifts in the dynamics of prey mortality. 

The most substantial increases in the death count are observed in the early to mid phases of the simulation, with the mean death count reaching approximately 200.79 and the median at 233. The maximum value of 240.33 is observed around tick 1876, indicating a peak in mortality rates at this point. The data also shows several peaks and valleys, with prominent peaks around ticks 1876, 2040, and 1672, suggesting fluctuations in mortality rates over time. 

The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis reveals significant trends in different windows of the simulation. Early windows show a strong increasing trend in mortality, with high tau values and significant p-values, indicating a consistent rise in deaths. However, as the simulation progresses, some windows exhibit no significant trend, while others show a decreasing trend, particularly towards the later stages of the simulation. This suggests that the rate of increase in mortality slows down and eventually stabilizes or slightly decreases. 

The oscillation analysis indicates a dominant period of 63 ticks, with multiple oscillation indices identified towards the end of the simulation period. This suggests periodic fluctuations in the death count, possibly due to cyclical environmental factors or predator-prey dynamics. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of early intervention to mitigate high mortality rates among prey populations. The periodic fluctuations and eventual stabilization of mortality rates suggest that long-term strategies should consider the cyclical nature of these dynamics. Understanding the factors contributing to these trends can inform conservation efforts and hunting regulations to ensure the sustainability of both prey and predator populations.
