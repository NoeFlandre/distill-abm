 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to test hypotheses related to the extinction of megaherbivores . The model simulates a simplified grassland environment with human hunters and male and female ungulates . The landscape is an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges, consisting of a 211x211 patch matrix .  The model allows users to manipulate these parameters to test various hypotheses related to extinction events . The temporal units used in the model are 'ticks,' which can be scaled to any temporal granularity . The model includes summary statistics that are shown as continuous line graphs, reflecting data from each tick .  The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model begins at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the initial phase of the simulation . Initial trend indicates an initial period of high hunting activity followed by a stabilization phase, suggesting that the forager bands quickly exploit the available postpartum female prey and then maintain a steady state as prey population dynamics and forager behavior reach a balance .  The number of female prey animals in the simulation begins at 125 and exhibits a rapid decline in the initial phase . This sharp decrease continues until around 700 time steps, where the population drops to approximately 5.4 . The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms a consistent decreasing trend across most windows, with brief periods of increase around time steps 1160 and 1856 . The simulation ultimately concludes with the number of male prey animals reaching zero, indicating the complete extinction of the male population .  The total energy available in prey animals within the simulation begins at approximately 16,667 units . This value experiences a rapid decline within the first 350 time steps, dropping significantly . The energy levels continue to decrease overall, despite the periodic oscillations . Towards the end of the simulation, the energy levels approach zero, indicating a near-complete depletion of energy in the female prey population .
