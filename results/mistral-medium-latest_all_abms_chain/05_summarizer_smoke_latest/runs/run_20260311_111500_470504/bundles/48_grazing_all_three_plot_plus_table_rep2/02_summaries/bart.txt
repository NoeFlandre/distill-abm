 The agent-based model in question is an extension of the RAGE (Rangeland Grazing Ecology) model . It simulates the interactions between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in a common property grazing system . The extension introduces learning capabilities to the agents, specifically learning-by-doing and social learning, which are central to adaptive co-management . The model consists of two main sub-models: Vegetation and Household decision-making .  The model is grounded in social theory and reflects empirical observations, providing a robust framework for studying the complex interactions in grazing systems . The model allows for the evaluation of various scenarios, such as the influence of demographic settings and herd size, the impact of different learning strategies, and the diffusion of learning behaviors .  The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates various trends over different time windows, with significant decreasing trends early on and significant increasing trends in the later periods . The overall trend shows a significant recovery and growth in green biomass after an initial decline, suggesting that the system can rebound effectively under the given conditions .  Trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows a consistent increasing trend in the adoption of social learning across all windows . The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 20 time steps, reaching around 30 households . The rate of increase slows down significantly after step 25, indicating a shift in the dynamics of destocking . For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of monitoring and addressing growing inequalities in livestock health among households .
