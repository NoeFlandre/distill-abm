The mean quantity of whole milk consumed by agents shows a clear and consistent downward trend over the simulation period. Initially, the mean whole milk quantity starts at approximately 2500 units. This high initial value indicates a strong initial habit and preference for whole milk among the agents, likely influenced by the high incumbent-initial-habit parameter set to 10.0. 

In the early steps, from 0 to around 5, there is a sharp decline in the mean whole milk quantity, dropping to about 1000 units. This rapid decrease suggests that agents are quickly adopting alternative milk types, possibly due to the influence of initial perceptions of health and environmental benefits of alternative milk types, as indicated by the parameters alt-health-mean-initial and alt-env-mean-initial. 

From steps 5 to around 20, the decline continues but at a slower and more gradual pace, reaching approximately 750 units. This phase indicates a stabilization period where the rate of adoption of alternative milk types slows down. Agents may be adjusting to new habits and social influences, with parameters like social-susceptibility and social-conformity playing significant roles. 

After step 20, the mean whole milk quantity continues to decrease but at an even slower rate, eventually leveling off around 500 units by step 30. This final phase suggests that the adoption of alternative milk types has reached a saturation point, where most agents who are inclined to switch have already done so. The remaining agents may be those with strong habits or those less influenced by social and health perceptions. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial perceptions and habits in driving the adoption of new products. The sharp initial decline underscores the impact of health and environmental awareness campaigns. The gradual stabilization and eventual leveling off suggest that while social influence and habits are crucial, there is a limit to how much behavior can be changed through these factors alone. This insight can guide the development of targeted interventions to encourage further adoption of healthier or more environmentally friendly products.
