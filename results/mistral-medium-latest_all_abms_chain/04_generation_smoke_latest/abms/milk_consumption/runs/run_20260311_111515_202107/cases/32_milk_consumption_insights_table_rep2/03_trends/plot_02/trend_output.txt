The simulation of the agent-based model shows distinct trends in the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent from 1974 to 2005. Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of approximately 5.29 units. This is followed by a rapid and significant increase in the early years, reaching around 1265.47 units by the mid-1980s. This period of sharp growth indicates a substantial shift in consumer behavior towards adopting skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The trend stabilizes around the mid-1980s, with consumption values oscillating around a high mean of approximately 1220.21 units. These oscillations suggest periodic fluctuations in consumption patterns, potentially reflecting varying influences of social norms, habits, and perceptions over time. Towards the end of the simulation period, the consumption reaches its peak at about 1456.60 units, indicating a strong and sustained preference for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk among the agents. The overall trend highlights a significant transition from whole milk to healthier alternatives, driven by the modeled factors of health perception, social influence, and habit formation. For decision-makers, these trends underscore the importance of health campaigns and social norms in shaping long-term consumer behavior towards healthier dietary choices.
