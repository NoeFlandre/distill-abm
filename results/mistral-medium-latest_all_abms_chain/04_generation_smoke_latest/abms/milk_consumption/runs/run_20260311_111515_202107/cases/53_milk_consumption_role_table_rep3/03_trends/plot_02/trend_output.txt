The simulation of the agent-based model shows distinct trends in the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent over time. Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of approximately 5.29 units. This low consumption level is maintained for a brief period before a significant and rapid increase begins. The most notable rise occurs early in the simulation, where the consumption jumps sharply, indicating a rapid adoption phase. This increasing trend is statistically significant in the early stages, as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall test results for the initial windows, which show a strong increasing trend with high tau values and significant p-values.  \n\nFollowing this rapid increase, the consumption levels enter a phase of stabilization where the growth rate slows down considerably. During this middle period, the consumption values oscillate around a higher mean, with several peaks and valleys identified. The peaks reach values around 1265.47, 1240.35, and 1226.86 units, while the valleys dip to approximately 1233.62, 1214.46, and 1209.70 units. These oscillations suggest periodic fluctuations in consumption without a clear upward or downward trend, supported by the rolling Mann-Kendall test results indicating no significant trend during these middle windows. \n\nTowards the end of the simulation, there is a resurgence in the increasing trend of milk consumption. The final window of the rolling Mann-Kendall test again shows a significant increasing trend, indicating that the consumption levels rise notably, reaching a maximum value of approximately 1456.60 units by the end of the simulation. This final increase suggests a renewed phase of adoption or a shift in consumer behavior towards higher consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. \n\nOverall, the simulation demonstrates an initial rapid adoption phase, followed by a period of stabilization with oscillations, and concluding with another phase of increasing consumption. The inflection points and change points identified further support these transitions between different phases of consumption behavior.
