The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents starts at a very low value of 5.29 units. This initial value suggests that at the beginning of the simulation, the consumption of alternative milk types is minimal, likely due to strong initial habits favoring whole milk and low initial perceptions of the health and environmental benefits of alternative milk types. The consumption increases rapidly in the early time steps, reaching around 1265.47 units by the 11th time step. This rapid increase can be attributed to agents starting to consider and adopt alternative milk types as they become more disposed to evaluating their choices, influenced by factors such as health perceptions, social interactions, and cognitive dissonance. Between the 11th and 13th time steps, the consumption slightly fluctuates around 1233.62 and 1240.35 units, indicating a period of stabilization where the adoption rate of alternative milk types balances out. This stabilization could be due to agents reaching a temporary equilibrium in their decision-making processes, influenced by their habits, social norms, and justifications for their choices. After the 13th time step, the consumption continues to increase gradually, with minor fluctuations, reaching a peak of 1456.60 units by the end of the simulation at the 32nd time step. The overall trend shows a significant upward trajectory in the consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, reflecting a growing preference and adoption of these milk types over time. The oscillations and inflection points observed throughout the simulation suggest dynamic interactions among agents, influenced by their memory, social susceptibility, and conformity to social norms. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in the early and later stages of the simulation, with periods of no significant trend in the middle stages, suggesting phases of rapid adoption followed by stabilization and then continued growth.
