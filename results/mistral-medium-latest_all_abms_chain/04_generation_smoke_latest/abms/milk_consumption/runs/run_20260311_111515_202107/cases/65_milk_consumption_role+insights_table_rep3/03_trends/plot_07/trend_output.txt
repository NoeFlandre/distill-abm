The simulation of the agent-based model reveals several key trends in the mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents over time. Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of 5.29 units. This is followed by a rapid increase in the early stages of the simulation, with significant growth observed up to around the 9th time step, reaching approximately 1265.47 units. This initial phase of rapid adoption suggests that agents quickly become disposed to consider alternative milk types, likely influenced by initial perceptions of health and environmental benefits. 

Following this rapid increase, the consumption levels enter a phase of stabilization with minor fluctuations. Between the 10th and 24th time steps, the consumption values oscillate around a mean of approximately 1220 units, indicating a period of equilibrium where the adoption rate stabilizes. This period is characterized by several peaks and valleys, such as peaks at 1265.47, 1240.35, and 1226.86 units, and valleys at 1233.62, 1214.46, and 1209.70 units. These fluctuations suggest periodic variations in agent behavior, potentially due to interactions between social influence, habit formation, and cognitive dissonance. 

After the 25th time step, there is a renewed increasing trend in consumption, culminating in a peak value of 1456.60 units at the final time step. This late-stage increase indicates a resurgence in the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, possibly driven by sustained social influence and habit reinforcement over time. 

The identified inflection points at time steps 4, 12, 13, 16, 19, 22, 27, and 30 mark significant changes in the trend dynamics, reflecting shifts in the underlying factors influencing agent decisions. The dominant oscillation period of 15 time steps suggests a cyclical pattern in the adoption behavior, which could be linked to periodic social interactions or habit reevaluation. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial perceptions and social influence in driving the adoption of healthier milk alternatives. The rapid initial increase underscores the impact of early interventions, while the stabilization phase suggests the need for continuous engagement to maintain adoption levels. The late-stage resurgence indicates that long-term strategies focusing on habit reinforcement and social norms can sustain behavioral changes over extended periods.
