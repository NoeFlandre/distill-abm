The simulation of the agent-based model shows distinct trends in the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent from 1974 to 2005. Initially, the consumption starts at a very low value of 5.29 units. There is a rapid increase in consumption during the early years, with significant growth observed up to around the mid-1980s. This period is marked by a strong upward trend, as indicated by the significant increasing trends in the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis during the early windows. By the mid-1980s, the consumption reaches a peak around 1265.47 units. Following this peak, the consumption stabilizes and exhibits minor fluctuations around a high average value, approximately between 1209.70 and 1265.47 units. These fluctuations indicate periods of slight increases and decreases, but overall, the consumption remains relatively stable with no significant trends during the middle windows of the analysis. Towards the end of the simulation period, there is a slight resurgence in the increasing trend, leading to the highest recorded consumption value of 1456.60 units by 2005. The dominant oscillation period of 15 years suggests cyclical patterns in consumption behavior, possibly reflecting periodic social or market influences. For decision-makers, the initial rapid adoption phase highlights the critical period where interventions or marketing strategies could significantly impact consumption patterns. The subsequent stabilization phase suggests a mature market where changes are gradual, and strategies might need to focus on maintaining consumer preferences and addressing minor fluctuations.
