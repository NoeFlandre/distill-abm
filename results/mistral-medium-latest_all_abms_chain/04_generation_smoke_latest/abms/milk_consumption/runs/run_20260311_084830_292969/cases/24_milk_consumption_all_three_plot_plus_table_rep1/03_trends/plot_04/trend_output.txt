The total number of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk choices starts at 0 and increases steadily over the 33 time steps. The initial increase is gradual, reflecting a slow adoption rate as agents begin to consider and switch to alternative milk types. This slow start could be attributed to strong initial habits for whole milk and low initial perceptions of the health and environmental benefits of alternative milk types. As time progresses, the rate of increase accelerates, indicating a growing acceptance and adoption of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk. This trend suggests that social influence, habit formation, and positive perceptions of alternative milk types are reinforcing each other, leading to a faster adoption rate. By the final time step, the total number of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk choices reaches approximately 25,643.83, indicating a significant shift in consumer behavior towards these milk types. The mean number of choices over the time steps is 11,157.35, with a median of 10,679.43, showing that the majority of the adoption occurs in the latter half of the time steps. The standard deviation of 8,241.32 highlights the variability in adoption rates across different time steps, with a more rapid increase in the later stages. The consistent increasing trend across all rolling Mann-Kendall windows, with significant p-values, underscores the robustness of the adoption trend. For decision makers, these trends imply that interventions aimed at promoting the health and environmental benefits of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk can be effective in the long run. However, initial efforts may see slow progress due to entrenched habits and perceptions. Persistent and targeted campaigns that leverage social influence and habit formation could accelerate the adoption process.
