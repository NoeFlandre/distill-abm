The average number of livestock destocked across households in the agent-based model shows a distinct trend over the simulated time steps. Initially, the destocking rate starts at zero and rapidly increases, reaching a peak of approximately 6.61 livestock at the third time step. This sharp rise is followed by a steep decline, with the destocking rate dropping to around 3.33 livestock by the eighth time step. The trend continues to decrease, albeit with some fluctuations, reaching a value of approximately 0.31 livestock by the final time step. Throughout the simulation, several smaller peaks and valleys are observed, indicating periodic increases and decreases in the destocking rate. Notable peaks occur at time steps 9, 12, 17, and 19, with values of approximately 3.37, 2.74, 1.47, and 1.38 livestock, respectively. Corresponding valleys are observed at time steps 8, 11, 16, and 18, with values around 3.33, 2.47, 1.45, and 0.90 livestock, respectively. The overall trend indicates a significant reduction in the destocking rate over time, with the most substantial changes occurring in the initial time steps. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis reveals significant decreasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with some periods showing no significant trend and a slight increasing trend towards the end. The oscillation analysis suggests periodic fluctuations in the destocking rate, with a dominant period of 49 time steps. The model's behavior reflects the adaptive management strategies of households in response to ecological and climatic conditions, with initial high destocking rates stabilizing as households adjust their livestock management practices.
