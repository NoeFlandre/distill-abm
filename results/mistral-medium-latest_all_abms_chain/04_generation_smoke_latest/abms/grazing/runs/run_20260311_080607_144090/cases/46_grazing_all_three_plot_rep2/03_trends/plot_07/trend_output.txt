The Gini coefficient for total livestock healthy starts at 0 across all simulations, indicating an initially equal distribution of healthy livestock among households. Over the first 20 time steps, the Gini coefficient increases gradually, suggesting a slow divergence in livestock health distribution among households. By around 40 time steps, the Gini coefficient reaches approximately 0.015, indicating a slight increase in inequality. This trend continues with a more noticeable rise in inequality up to around 0.045 by 60 time steps, reflecting a period where some households begin to experience significantly better livestock health outcomes than others. Beyond 60 time steps, the rate of increase in the Gini coefficient accelerates, reaching values between 0.06 and 0.09 by the end of the simulation at 100 time steps. This indicates a substantial increase in inequality in livestock health distribution among households over time. The variability among different simulation runs also increases, particularly noticeable after 60 time steps, suggesting that different initial conditions or stochastic events lead to diverse outcomes in livestock health distribution. For decision makers, this trend highlights the importance of monitoring and potentially intervening in the early stages of livestock management to prevent significant disparities in livestock health among households. Understanding the factors contributing to this inequality could help in designing policies that promote more equitable distribution of healthy livestock, thereby enhancing the overall resilience and sustainability of the grazing system.
