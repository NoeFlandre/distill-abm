The average amount of green biomass across all patches starts at approximately 45,000 units. It initially exhibits a slight increase, reaching around 45,261 units by the fourth time step. Following this, there is a notable decline, with the biomass dropping to its lowest point of approximately 42,057 units at the 27th time step. This decline suggests an initial period of high grazing activity or environmental stress that reduces the available green biomass. After reaching this low point, the green biomass begins a steady recovery. This recovery phase is marked by a consistent increase in biomass, indicating a period of regrowth and reduced grazing pressure or improved environmental conditions. By the 100th time step, the green biomass reaches its peak at approximately 60,535 units. The overall trend shows a significant recovery and growth in green biomass after an initial decline, suggesting that the system can rebound effectively under the given conditions. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates various trends over different time windows, with significant decreasing trends early on and significant increasing trends in the later periods, reinforcing the observation of initial decline followed by recovery and growth. The presence of change points at the 50th and 100th time steps further supports the identification of distinct phases in the biomass dynamics. For decision makers, this trend implies that while initial grazing or environmental pressures can significantly reduce green biomass, the system has the capacity for substantial recovery and growth over time. This resilience suggests that with appropriate management, the grazing system can sustain and even enhance green biomass levels, supporting long-term ecological balance and livestock sustainability.
