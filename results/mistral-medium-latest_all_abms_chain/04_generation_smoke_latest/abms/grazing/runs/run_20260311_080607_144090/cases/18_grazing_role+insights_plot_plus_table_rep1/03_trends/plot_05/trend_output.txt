The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households shows a distinct trend over the 100 time steps. Initially, the mean livestock count starts at 90. This high starting value rapidly decreases in the early stages, reaching a minimum value of approximately 41 at around time step 37. This sharp decline indicates a significant reduction in livestock numbers early in the simulation. 

Following this initial drop, the trend stabilizes and begins to gradually increase. The inflection points at time steps 3 and 46 mark the transitions in the trend. From time step 44 onwards, there is a consistent, albeit slow, increase in the livestock numbers. By the end of the simulation at time step 100, the mean livestock count is approximately 47.55. 

The statistical analysis reveals several key insights. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a significant decreasing trend in the early windows (time steps 0-43), followed by a shift to a significant increasing trend in the later windows (time steps 44-98). The change points at time steps 20, 55, and 100 further highlight the shifts in the trend dynamics. The oscillation analysis suggests periodic fluctuations with a dominant period of 49 time steps, indicating cyclical patterns in the livestock numbers. 

For decision makers, these trends suggest an initial period of rapid livestock reduction followed by stabilization and gradual recovery. This pattern may reflect the adaptive strategies of households in response to ecological and climatic conditions, emphasizing the importance of resilience and adaptive management in grazing systems.
