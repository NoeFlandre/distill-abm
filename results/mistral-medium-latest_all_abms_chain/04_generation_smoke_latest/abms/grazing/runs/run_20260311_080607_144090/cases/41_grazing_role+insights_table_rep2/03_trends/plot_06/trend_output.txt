The average number of destocked livestock across all households exhibits a notable trend over the simulation period. Initially, the destocking rate starts at zero and experiences a sharp increase, peaking significantly at time step 3 with a value of approximately 6.61. This peak is followed by a rapid decline to around 3.33 by time step 8. The trend continues with a series of decreasing peaks and troughs, indicating a general downward trajectory in the destocking rate. By time step 21, the destocking rate stabilizes at lower values, fluctuating mildly around an average of 0.31. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms significant decreasing trends in the initial phases, with periods of no significant trend and a slight increasing trend towards the latter part of the simulation. The presence of multiple inflection points and oscillations suggests variability in the destocking behavior, potentially influenced by the adaptive strategies of households and ecological conditions. For decision makers, the initial spike in destocking highlights a critical period where resource management might be particularly challenging, necessitating robust strategies to mitigate high destocking rates. The subsequent stabilization suggests that households may adapt over time, but continuous monitoring and adaptive management practices are essential to maintain stability in livestock numbers.
