The mean household risk attitude starts with a value of approximately 0.5 across all simulations. During the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid divergence in the risk attitudes among households. Some households exhibit an increase in risk attitude, reaching values as high as 0.8, while others show a decrease, dropping to values as low as -0.2. This divergence suggests that households are quickly adapting their risk attitudes based on initial experiences and interactions within the grazing system. By around 20 time steps, the risk attitudes begin to stabilize, with distinct groups of households settling into different risk attitude ranges. Households with higher risk attitudes tend to stabilize around 0.6 to 0.8, while those with lower risk attitudes stabilize around -0.2 to 0.2. This stabilization indicates that households have likely found a balance in their risk attitudes that aligns with their experiences and the ecological conditions they face. Throughout the remaining time steps, from 20 to 100, the risk attitudes remain relatively stable within these established ranges. This long-term stability suggests that the adaptive processes, such as learning-by-doing and social learning, have led households to maintain consistent risk attitudes over time. For decision makers, the rapid initial divergence in risk attitudes highlights the importance of early interventions and support to guide households towards sustainable risk management practices. The long-term stability in risk attitudes suggests that once households adapt to their environment, their risk management strategies become entrenched, underscoring the need for policies that promote adaptive and resilient behaviors from the outset.
