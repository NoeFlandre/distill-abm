The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all households, as measured by the Gini coefficient, starts at a value of approximately 0. This indicates an initially equal distribution of healthy livestock among households. Over the course of the simulation, the Gini coefficient exhibits a consistent increasing trend, reaching a value of approximately 0.0726 by the end of the 100 time steps. This trend suggests a gradual increase in inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock over time. The increase is steady and significant across all rolling Mann-Kendall windows, indicating a robust trend throughout the simulation period. Several inflection points are observed, suggesting subtle changes in the rate of increase in inequality at specific time steps. The presence of multiple oscillation indices towards the latter part of the simulation suggests some periodic fluctuations in the rate of change of inequality. For decision makers, this trend implies that over time, certain households may be accumulating more healthy livestock than others, potentially leading to disparities in household livelihoods and economic outcomes. This information could be crucial for developing interventions aimed at promoting more equitable distribution of resources and improving the resilience of the pastoral system.
