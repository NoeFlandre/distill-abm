The simulation of the agent-based model shows distinct trends in the average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents over the course of 100 time steps. Initially, the deviation starts at a low value of 0.0074. There is a rapid increase in deviation during the early time steps, reaching a peak value of approximately 0.3814 around time step 33. This increasing trend is statistically significant and indicates that households are initially deviating more from the recommended livestock numbers as they adapt their strategies. Following this peak, there is a gradual decline in the deviation, indicating a correction phase where households adjust their livestock numbers closer to the recommended values. This decreasing trend continues and becomes stable towards the later time steps, ending with a value of 0.1911 at time step 100. The model exhibits several inflection points and oscillations, particularly between time steps 20 to 35, suggesting periods of adjustment and stabilization in household decision-making. The dominant oscillation period of 49 time steps indicates cyclical patterns in the deviation, possibly reflecting periodic adjustments in household strategies. For decision makers, these trends suggest that while initial adaptations may lead to significant deviations from recommended practices, over time, households tend to correct their strategies, leading to more sustainable livestock management practices. The cyclical patterns highlight the importance of periodic reviews and adjustments in pastoral management strategies.
