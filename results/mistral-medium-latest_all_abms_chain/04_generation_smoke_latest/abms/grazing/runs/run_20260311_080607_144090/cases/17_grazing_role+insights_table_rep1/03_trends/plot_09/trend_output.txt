The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households exhibits a consistent increasing trend over the entire simulation period of 100 timesteps. Starting from an initial value of 0, the destocking metric rises steadily, reaching a final value of approximately 96.78. The trend is characterized by a series of inflection points, indicating periods where the rate of increase changes subtly. These inflection points occur at timesteps 45, 47, 53, 56, 57, 62, 67, 72, 74, and 75. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a significant increasing trend across all windows, with the slope of the trend decreasing over time, indicating a rapid initial increase in destocking followed by a more gradual rise. Change points are detected at timesteps 25, 60, and 100, suggesting notable shifts in the rate of destocking at these points. The oscillation analysis reveals periodic fluctuations with a dominant period of 49 timesteps, indicating cyclical patterns in the destocking behavior. For decision makers, these trends suggest that destocking is a persistent and increasing phenomenon under the given model parameters. The initial rapid increase followed by a more gradual rise may indicate an initial adjustment period followed by a stabilization in household behaviors. The periodic oscillations suggest that there may be underlying cyclical factors influencing destocking decisions, which could be related to environmental or socio-economic conditions.
