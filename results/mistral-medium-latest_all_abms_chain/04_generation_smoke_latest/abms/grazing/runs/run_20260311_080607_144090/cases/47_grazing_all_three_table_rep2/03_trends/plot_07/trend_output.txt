The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all households, as measured by the Gini coefficient, starts at a value of 0. This indicates perfect equality in the initial distribution of healthy livestock among households. Over the course of the simulation, the Gini coefficient exhibits a consistent increasing trend, ending at a value of approximately 0.0726. This suggests a gradual shift towards inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock. The mean Gini coefficient across the simulation is 0.0302, with a median of 0.0229, indicating that while there is some inequality, it remains relatively low throughout the simulation. The standard deviation of 0.0239 and variance of 0.0006 suggest minor fluctuations around the mean. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a significant increasing trend in inequality across all windows, with consistent positive tau values and significant p-values. This persistent increase in the Gini coefficient implies that over time, certain households are accumulating more healthy livestock than others. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points suggests that the rate of increase in inequality is not constant but experiences periods of acceleration and deceleration. The dominant oscillation period of 49 time steps indicates cyclical patterns in the rate of change of inequality. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of monitoring and addressing disparities in livestock distribution to ensure equitable access to resources among households. The gradual increase in inequality suggests that interventions may be needed to support households that are falling behind in livestock accumulation.
