The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system starts at approximately 90,000 units. Initially, there is a slight increase in biomass within the first few time steps, followed by a gradual decline reaching a minimum around 85,000 units at approximately 20 time steps. This decline could be attributed to the initial grazing pressure exerted by the livestock as they begin to utilize the available biomass. Following this decline, there is a significant and steady increase in mean reserve biomass starting around 20 time steps and continuing until about 60 time steps, where the biomass levels off and stabilizes around 120,000 units. This increase and subsequent stabilization suggest that the grazing system reaches a new equilibrium where the biomass production matches the consumption by livestock. The stabilization at a higher biomass level indicates a sustainable balance in the grazing system, potentially influenced by adaptive management strategies and learning behaviors of the households. There is some variability among the simulation runs, particularly noticeable during the initial decline and subsequent increase phases, but the overall trend remains consistent across the simulations.
