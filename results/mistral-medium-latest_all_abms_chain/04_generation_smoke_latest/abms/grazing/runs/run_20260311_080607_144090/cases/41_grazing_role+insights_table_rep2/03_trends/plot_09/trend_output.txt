The average cumulative number of destocked livestock across all households exhibits a consistent increasing trend over the 100 timesteps, starting from 0 and reaching approximately 96.78. The trend is characterized by a rapid initial increase, followed by a more gradual rise. This is followed by a noticeable change point at timestep 25, after which the rate of increase slows down but remains positive and significant throughout the remaining timesteps. Additional change points are observed at timestep 60 and timestep 100, indicating shifts in the rate of destocking. The data also shows several inflection points, suggesting periods where the rate of increase experiences minor fluctuations. The consistent upward trend, as confirmed by the rolling Mann-Kendall test across various windows, indicates a steady and significant increase in destocking over time. For decision makers, this trend suggests a progressive reduction in livestock numbers, which could have implications for household livelihoods and pasture management strategies. The steady increase in destocking highlights the need for adaptive management practices to sustain both ecological and household systems.
