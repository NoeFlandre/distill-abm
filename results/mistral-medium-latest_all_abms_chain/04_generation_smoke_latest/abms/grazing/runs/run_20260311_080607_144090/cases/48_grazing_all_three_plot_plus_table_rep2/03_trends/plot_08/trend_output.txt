The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero and increases rapidly within the first 20 time steps, reaching around 30 households. This initial rapid increase indicates a quick adoption of social learning behaviors among households, likely due to the initial conditions and interactions set in the model. The number of households engaging in social learning then stabilizes, fluctuating slightly around 30 to 32 households for the remainder of the simulation. This stabilization suggests that after the initial adoption phase, the rate of new households adopting social learning behaviors slows down, reaching a steady state where most households that are likely to adopt social learning have already done so. The mean number of households engaging in social learning over the entire simulation period is approximately 27, with a standard deviation of about 9, indicating some variability but generally consistent behavior across different simulation runs. The trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows a consistent increasing trend in the adoption of social learning across all windows, although the rate of increase slows down over time. The presence of multiple inflection points suggests periods where the rate of adoption changes, potentially due to underlying dynamics in household interactions or ecological factors. For decision makers, these trends imply that initial conditions and early interactions are crucial for the adoption of social learning behaviors. Once a critical mass of households adopts these behaviors, the system stabilizes, indicating the importance of early interventions and support for facilitating social learning in grazing systems.
