Following this peak, there is a noticeable decline in mean green biomass, dropping to around 43,000 units by the 20th time step. This decline may indicate the onset of grazing activities by livestock, which begins to consume the available biomass. From the 20th to the 40th time step, the mean green biomass continues to decrease gradually, reaching a low point of approximately 42,000 units. This period represents a phase where grazing pressure is high, and the biomass is being consumed faster than it can regenerate. After the 40th time step, the mean green biomass starts to recover, showing a steady increase. By the 60th time step, it reaches around 50,000 units, indicating a period of significant regrowth. This recovery phase suggests that the grazing pressure has reduced or that the vegetation has adapted to the grazing patterns, allowing for regrowth. From the 60th time step onwards, the mean green biomass continues to increase steadily, reaching a plateau of approximately 63,000 units by the 100th time step. This final phase indicates a stable equilibrium where the biomass growth rate matches the grazing rate, resulting in a sustained level of green biomass. The trends in mean green biomass provide insights into the dynamics of vegetation growth and grazing pressure over time. The initial decline followed by a recovery phase highlights the resilience of the vegetation to grazing activities. For decision-makers, understanding these trends is crucial for managing grazing practices to ensure sustainable biomass levels. The final stabilization of biomass suggests that with appropriate management, a balance can be achieved between livestock grazing and vegetation regrowth, ensuring long-term sustainability of the pastoral system.
