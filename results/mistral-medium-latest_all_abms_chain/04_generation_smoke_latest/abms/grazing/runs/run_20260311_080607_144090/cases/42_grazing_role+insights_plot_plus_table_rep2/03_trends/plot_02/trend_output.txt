The average deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents in the simulation shows distinct trends over the 100 time steps. Initially, the deviation starts at a low value of approximately 0.0074. During the early stages, from time step 0 to around time step 20, there is a rapid increase in the deviation, reaching a peak value of about 0.3814 at time step 33. This period is characterized by a significant upward trend, indicating that households are increasingly deviating from the recommended livestock numbers. 

Following this peak, the deviation enters a phase of gradual decline. From time step 33 to around time step 50, the deviation decreases steadily, reflecting a correction phase where households adjust their livestock numbers closer to the recommended values. This downward trend continues until the deviation stabilizes around a value of approximately 0.1911 by the end of the simulation at time step 100. 

The statistical analysis reveals several inflection points and change points, notably around time steps 50 and 100, indicating shifts in the trend dynamics. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms significant increasing trends in the early phases and significant decreasing trends in the middle phases, with no significant trends observed in the later phases. 

For decision-makers, these trends suggest that initial household decisions may lead to significant deviations from recommended livestock numbers, but over time, adjustments are made that bring the deviations closer to the recommended values. This implies that while there may be initial challenges in adhering to recommendations, adaptive behaviors and learning mechanisms eventually contribute to better alignment with suggested practices.
