The number of households engaging in social learning, as indicated by the variable household-SL being true, exhibits a clear increasing trend over the simulation period. Initially, no households are engaged in social learning at the start of the simulation. The adoption of social learning begins gradually and accelerates over time, with significant inflection points observed at various stages, notably around time steps 7, 28, 30, 33, 38, 57, and 60. These inflection points suggest periods where the rate of adoption experiences notable changes. The trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent increasing trend across multiple windows, with high statistical significance in each case. The slope of the increase varies, being steeper in the early stages and gradually flattening out as the number of households engaging in social learning approaches a saturation point. The maximum number of households engaging in social learning is observed around time step 63, reaching approximately 32 households. The oscillation indices suggest some periodic fluctuations in the adoption rate, but the dominant trend remains one of steady increase. For decision makers, these trends imply that social learning behaviors diffuse gradually through the population of households, with critical periods where the rate of adoption accelerates. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions or policies that facilitate or leverage social learning to enhance adaptive management strategies in pastoral systems.
