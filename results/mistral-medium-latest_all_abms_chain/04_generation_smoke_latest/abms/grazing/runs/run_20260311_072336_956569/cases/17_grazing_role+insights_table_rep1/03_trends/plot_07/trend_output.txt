The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all households, as measured by the Gini coefficient, exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period. Initially, the Gini coefficient starts at a value of approximately 0, indicating a nearly equal distribution of healthy livestock among households. Over time, the inequality gradually increases, reaching a value of approximately 0.0726 by the end of the simulation at time step 100. The mean Gini coefficient across the entire simulation period is 0.0302, with a median value of 0.0229, suggesting that the distribution of inequality values is slightly skewed towards higher inequality. The standard deviation of 0.0239 indicates some variability in the inequality values over time. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a significant increasing trend in inequality across all windows, with high Kendall tau values and significant p-values, indicating a robust and consistent upward trend. Several inflection points are detected, suggesting changes in the rate of increase in inequality at specific time steps. The presence of multiple oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation period suggests some periodic fluctuations in the rate of increase in inequality. For decision makers, these trends imply that over time, the distribution of healthy livestock becomes increasingly unequal among households. This could have significant implications for the sustainability and equity of the grazing system, potentially requiring interventions to address the growing disparity in livestock distribution.
