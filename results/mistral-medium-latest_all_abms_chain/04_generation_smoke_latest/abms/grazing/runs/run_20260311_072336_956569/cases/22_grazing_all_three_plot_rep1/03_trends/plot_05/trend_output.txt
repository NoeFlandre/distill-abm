The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, starts at approximately 90 across all households. This initial value reflects the starting condition set by the livestock-init parameter. Over the first 20 time steps, there is a rapid decline in the number of livestock, dropping to around 40. This sharp decrease could be attributed to initial adjustments in the grazing system as households adapt to the environmental and climatic conditions set by parameters such as rain-mean and rain-std. 

Following this initial decline, the number of livestock stabilizes and remains relatively constant between 40 and 50 livestock per household from time step 20 to around time step 60. This period of stability suggests that households have reached a temporary equilibrium in managing their livestock under the given conditions. The stability in livestock numbers during this phase indicates that households might be effectively balancing their livestock management practices with the available resources. 

After time step 60, there is a gradual increase in the number of livestock, reaching up to approximately 60 by the end of the simulation at time step 100. This upward trend could be due to several factors, including improved grazing management practices, favorable climatic conditions, or the effects of learning and adaptation by households over time. The increase suggests that households are potentially benefiting from adaptive strategies, possibly influenced by the learning capabilities introduced in the model. 

For decision makers, the initial rapid decline in livestock numbers highlights the importance of preparing for significant adjustments in the early stages of grazing management. The subsequent stabilization period indicates a phase where management practices are relatively effective, but the gradual increase towards the end of the simulation suggests that long-term strategies, potentially involving adaptive learning and improved grazing practices, can lead to better outcomes in livestock management.
