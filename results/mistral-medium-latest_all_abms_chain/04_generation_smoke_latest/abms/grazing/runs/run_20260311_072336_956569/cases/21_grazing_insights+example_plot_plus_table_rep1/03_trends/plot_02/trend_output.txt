The average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock for all extension agents begins at approximately 0.0074. This metric experiences a rapid increase during the initial time steps, reaching a peak value of around 0.3814 at time step 33. This sharp rise indicates that households are initially adjusting their livestock numbers significantly, possibly due to initial learning and adaptation processes in response to environmental and economic conditions. Following this peak, the deviation metric exhibits a gradual decline, stabilizing around a value of 0.1911 by the end of the simulation at time step 100. This stabilization suggests that households are converging towards a more consistent and possibly optimized livestock management strategy over time. The presence of multiple inflection points and oscillations, particularly between time steps 20 to 35, indicates periods of adjustment and fine-tuning in household decision-making processes. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results show significant increasing trends in the early phases (time steps 0 to 32) and significant decreasing trends in the middle phases (time steps 33 to 65), followed by periods with no significant trend. This pattern underscores the dynamic nature of household risk attitudes as they adapt to changing conditions and learn from their experiences. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial adaptive strategies and the eventual stabilization of household behaviors, which can inform policies aimed at supporting sustainable livestock management practices.
