The mean reserve biomass in the grazing system starts at approximately 90,000 units. Initially, there is a slight increase in biomass, peaking around 92,000 units within the first few time steps. This initial rise could be attributed to the initial growth phase of vegetation under favorable conditions. Following this peak, the mean reserve biomass experiences a notable decline, reaching a low point of around 85,000 units by the 20th time step. This decline may be due to the initial grazing pressure exerted by the livestock as they begin to consume the available vegetation. After this decline, the biomass begins to recover and increases steadily. By the 40th time step, the mean reserve biomass surpasses its initial value and continues to rise. This recovery phase indicates that the vegetation growth rate surpasses the grazing pressure, allowing the biomass to accumulate. The upward trend continues, and by the 60th time step, the mean reserve biomass reaches approximately 120,000 units. Beyond this point, the biomass levels off and stabilizes, maintaining a steady state around 120,000 to 130,000 units for the remainder of the simulation. This stabilization suggests a balance between vegetation growth and grazing pressure has been achieved, reflecting a sustainable equilibrium in the grazing system. There is some variability among the different simulation runs, particularly noticeable during the initial decline and recovery phases, but the overall trend remains consistent across all simulations.
