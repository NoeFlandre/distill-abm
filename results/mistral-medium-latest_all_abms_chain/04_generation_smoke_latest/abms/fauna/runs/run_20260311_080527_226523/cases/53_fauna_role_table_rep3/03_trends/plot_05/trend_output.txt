The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the count starts at zero and begins to increase rapidly. This rapid increase is evident in the early stages, with significant upward trends observed in the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis during the first six windows, indicating a consistent rise in the number of postpartum females hunted. The slope of this increase is steep initially, with a slope of 0.213 in the first window, and gradually decreases to 0.001 by the sixth window, suggesting a slowing rate of increase over time. Key inflection points occur early in the simulation, notably around time steps 245, 262, and 285, indicating periods where the rate of change in the trend shifts. Change points at time steps 475, 625, and 850 further mark significant shifts in the trend dynamics. As the simulation progresses, the trend stabilizes, and by the seventh window of the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, there is no significant trend detected, indicating a plateau in the cumulative count. This stabilization is characterized by minor fluctuations around a mean value of approximately 117 postpartum females hunted, with peaks and valleys showing minimal prominence. The maximum value reached is 119, occurring around time step 1826. Towards the end of the simulation, a slight decreasing trend is observed in the ninth window of the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, although this change is relatively minor. The dominant oscillation period of 63 time steps suggests regular minor fluctuations around the stabilized mean value. Overall, the trend shows an initial rapid increase in the hunting of postpartum females, followed by a stabilization phase with minor oscillations, reflecting the dynamic interactions between hunters and prey within the model's parameters.
