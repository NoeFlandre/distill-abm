The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the count starts at zero and shows a rapid increase, reaching significant values early in the simulation. This early phase is characterized by a steep upward trend, indicating a high rate of hunting success. By around the 245th tick, the first inflection point is observed, suggesting a slight change in the rate of increase. The trend continues to rise, albeit at varying rates, with notable inflection points at ticks 262, 285, and subsequent intervals, indicating periods of acceleration and deceleration in the hunting rate. The most substantial increases occur within the first 2000 ticks, where the cumulative count approaches its peak values. The maximum value of 119 is reached around the 1826th tick, after which the trend stabilizes with minor fluctuations. These fluctuations are evident in the identified peaks and valleys, which show small variations around the mean value of approximately 117. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with a notable shift to no significant trend around the mid-point, followed by a brief period of decreasing trend towards the end. The dominant oscillation period of 63 ticks suggests regular fluctuations in the latter part of the simulation. For decision-makers, these trends imply that hunting pressure on postpartum females is initially high and increases rapidly, stabilizing as the simulation progresses. The minor fluctuations near the end suggest a balance between hunting success and potential limiting factors such as prey availability or hunter saturation. Understanding these dynamics can help in assessing the impact of hunting pressure on the prey population and in designing conservation strategies or hunting regulations.
