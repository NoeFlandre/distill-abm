The simulation of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear trend of decreasing total number of prey animals over time. Initially, the number of animals starts at approximately 166.67. This number rapidly declines within the first few hundred time steps. By around 500 time steps, the number of animals has significantly decreased, and this downward trend continues, albeit at a slower rate, as time progresses. The decline is steep initially and then gradually tapers off, indicating a rapid initial impact followed by a prolonged period of lower but persistent decline. The mean number of animals across the simulation is approximately 25.01, with a median of 1.48, suggesting that for the majority of the simulation time, the number of animals is quite low. The standard deviation of 44.90 indicates a high variability in the number of animals, particularly in the early stages of the simulation. The minimum value of zero animals is reached at the final time step, indicating complete extinction by the end of the simulation. The statistical analysis reveals multiple inflection points and change points, suggesting periods where the rate of decline changes. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant decreasing trends in the early windows and some periods of slight increase in later windows, though the overall trend remains a decrease. The presence of peaks and valleys in the data suggests some oscillations in the animal population, but these are minor compared to the overall declining trend. The dominant period of oscillation is 63 time steps, indicating some cyclical patterns within the general decline. The simulation ultimately shows a consistent and significant reduction in the prey animal population, leading to complete extinction by the end of the observed time steps.
