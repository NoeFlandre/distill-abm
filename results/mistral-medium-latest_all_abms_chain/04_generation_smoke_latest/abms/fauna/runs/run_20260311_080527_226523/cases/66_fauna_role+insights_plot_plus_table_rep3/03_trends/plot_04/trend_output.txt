The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a rapid initial increase followed by a gradual stabilization. Initially, the number of females killed rises sharply from 0 to approximately 120 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase is followed by a more gradual rise, eventually stabilizing around 120 to 123 females killed. The trend analysis indicates a consistent increasing trend in the early stages, which gradually slows down and stabilizes, with minor fluctuations in the later stages. Key inflection points occur around steps 245, 262, 285, and several others, indicating significant changes in the rate of increase. The model reaches a plateau with minimal variation, suggesting a balance between hunting pressure and the remaining female prey population. The dominant oscillation period of 63 steps suggests some cyclical behavior in the later stages, although the amplitude of these oscillations is relatively small. For decision makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressure has a significant impact on the female prey population, but over time, the system reaches a dynamic equilibrium where the rate of hunting stabilizes. This stabilization could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices or conservation efforts to maintain prey populations.
