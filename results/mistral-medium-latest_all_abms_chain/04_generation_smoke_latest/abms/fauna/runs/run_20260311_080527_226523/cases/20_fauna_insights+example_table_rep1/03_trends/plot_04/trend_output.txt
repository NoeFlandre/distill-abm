The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands begins at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the early time steps. This initial rise is steep, indicating a high rate of hunting success or a high initial population of female prey. The trend continues to increase steadily, though at a gradually diminishing rate, suggesting that the hunting pressure remains consistent but the availability of female prey may be decreasing over time. By around the 1851st tick, the cumulative number of hunted females reaches its peak at approximately 123.67, indicating a saturation point where the rate of successful hunts significantly slows down. This peak is followed by minor fluctuations, with slight decreases and increases, but the overall trend stabilizes around this maximum value. The minor oscillations observed towards the end of the simulation suggest periodic variations in hunting success, potentially due to seasonal changes or other cyclic factors affecting prey availability or hunter efficiency. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a consistent increasing trend in the early and middle phases of the simulation, which shifts to a decreasing trend towards the end, reflecting the stabilization and minor fluctuations in the cumulative count of hunted females. For decision makers, these trends imply that initial hunting efforts are highly effective but may lead to a saturation point where further increases in hunting success are minimal. This could inform strategies related to sustainable hunting practices and the management of prey populations to avoid depletion.
