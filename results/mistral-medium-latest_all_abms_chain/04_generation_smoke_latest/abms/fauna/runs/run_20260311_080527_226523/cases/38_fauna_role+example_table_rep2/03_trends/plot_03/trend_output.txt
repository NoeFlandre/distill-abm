The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the initial time steps. This rapid increase can be attributed to the high initial availability of male prey and the active hunting behavior of the foragers. By around 250 time steps, the rate of increase begins to slow down, indicating a possible reduction in the male prey population or a shift in hunting dynamics. The cumulative count continues to rise steadily, reaching approximately 117.5 by the end of the simulation. Throughout the simulation, there are several inflection points, notably around time steps 362, 1241, and 1330, suggesting periods where the rate of hunting changes. These changes could be due to variations in prey availability, environmental conditions, or hunter behavior. The data shows a dominant oscillation period of 63 time steps, indicating a cyclical pattern in the hunting success rate. This cyclical pattern could be influenced by seasonal changes or periodic movements of the prey. The rolling Mann-Kendall test reveals significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with a notable shift to no significant trend around time step 1160, followed by a decreasing trend towards the end. This suggests that while hunting pressure is initially high and effective, it stabilizes and eventually decreases, possibly due to a significant reduction in the male prey population or changes in the ecosystem dynamics.
