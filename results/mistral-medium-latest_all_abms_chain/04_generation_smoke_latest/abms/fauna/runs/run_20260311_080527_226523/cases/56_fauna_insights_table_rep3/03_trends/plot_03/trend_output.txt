The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a general increasing trend over the simulation period. Initially, the count starts at zero and gradually rises, indicating a steady increase in hunting activity. The trend shows significant growth in the early stages, with notable inflection points around ticks 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850, suggesting periods of accelerated hunting activity. The most substantial increases occur within the first 2310 ticks, after which the rate of increase stabilizes. The data reaches a peak value of approximately 120.17 around tick 1876, followed by minor fluctuations and a slight decrease towards the end of the simulation. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms these observations, showing significant increasing trends in the early windows and a shift to no significant trend or a slight decrease in later windows. The oscillation analysis indicates a dominant period of 63 ticks, suggesting periodic fluctuations in hunting activity. For decision makers, these trends imply that hunting pressure on male prey increases over time, with periods of rapid growth followed by stabilization. This information can be crucial for understanding the dynamics of predator-prey interactions and the potential impacts on the prey population over extended periods.
