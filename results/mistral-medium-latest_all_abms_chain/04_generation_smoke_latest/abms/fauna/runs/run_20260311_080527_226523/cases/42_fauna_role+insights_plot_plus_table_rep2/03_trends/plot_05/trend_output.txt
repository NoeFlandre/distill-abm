The cumulative number of postpartum females killed in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) simulations exhibits a rapid increase in the early stages, followed by a gradual stabilization. Initially, the number of postpartum females killed rises sharply from 0 to approximately 115 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase indicates a high initial hunting pressure on postpartum females. 

Following this initial surge, the rate of increase slows down significantly, and the cumulative number of postpartum females killed stabilizes around 117. This stabilization suggests that the hunting pressure reaches a plateau, where the number of postpartum females killed per step becomes relatively constant. The data shows minor fluctuations around this stabilized value, indicating some variability in hunting success or prey availability over time. 

Key inflection points occur around steps 245, 262, and 285, marking the transition from rapid increase to stabilization. Additionally, change points at steps 475, 625, and 850 further highlight shifts in the trend dynamics. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results indicate significant increasing trends in the early stages, followed by periods of no significant trend and slight decreases, reflecting the stabilization phase. 

For decision-makers, these trends suggest that initial hunting pressure is intense but eventually stabilizes, which could inform strategies related to hunting regulations and conservation efforts. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing interventions to manage hunting pressure and protect vulnerable populations of megaherbivores.
