The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a general increasing trend over time, with notable patterns and fluctuations. Initially, the number of females killed starts at zero and gradually increases. Early inflection points around ticks 245, 262, and 285 indicate initial phases of rapid increase in hunting activity. This trend continues with varying rates of increase, as evidenced by subsequent inflection points and change points, suggesting periods of accelerated and decelerated hunting activity. The most significant change points occur around ticks 125, 375, 475, 625, 850, 1600, and 2310, indicating shifts in the dynamics of hunting pressure. The mean number of females killed is approximately 104.56, with a median of 119.06, suggesting a skewed distribution. The maximum value of approximately 123.67 is reached around tick 1826, after which the trend stabilizes with minor fluctuations. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates significant increasing trends in most windows, with a notable shift to a decreasing trend in the later window from ticks 1856 to 2087. The oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation suggest periodic fluctuations in the hunting activity. For decision makers, these trends highlight the dynamic interaction between hunting pressure and prey population, emphasizing the need to consider temporal variations and potential stabilization points in hunting activity when evaluating extinction risks or equilibrium conditions.
