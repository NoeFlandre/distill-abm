The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear trend over time. Initially, the number of males killed starts at zero and increases rapidly. This rapid increase continues until around 500 steps, where the rate of increase starts to slow down. By approximately 1000 steps, the cumulative number of males killed reaches a plateau, stabilizing around 120 males killed. This plateau indicates that the rate at which males are being killed has significantly decreased, and the system has reached a near-equilibrium state where the number of males killed remains relatively constant over time.

Key time steps and values highlight the dynamics of the system. At the beginning (0 steps), no males have been killed. By 500 steps, the number of males killed has increased substantially, reflecting the initial rapid hunting rate. Around 1000 steps, the cumulative number of males killed starts to level off, indicating a reduction in the hunting rate or a balance between hunting and other factors affecting the male population. The maximum value observed is approximately 120 males killed, which is maintained from around 1000 steps onwards.

The implications for decision-makers are significant. The initial rapid increase in the number of males killed suggests that hunting pressure is high at the beginning of the simulation. The subsequent plateau indicates that either the hunting pressure has decreased, or the male population has adapted or been reduced to a level where further hunting does not significantly increase the cumulative count. This could imply that the initial hunting pressure was unsustainable and that the system has reached a new equilibrium. Decision-makers might consider these dynamics when setting hunting quotas or designing conservation strategies to ensure the sustainability of both prey and predator populations.
