The total energy of female animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12,500 units. This high initial energy level rapidly declines within the first few hundred time steps. By around 500 time steps, the energy level has dropped significantly to approximately 2,000 units. This rapid decline continues, and by 1,000 time steps, the energy level further reduces to around 500 units. The trend of decreasing energy persists, albeit at a slower rate, and by 2,000 time steps, the energy level approaches zero, indicating the near extinction or severe depletion of the female animal population. Throughout the simulation, there are minor fluctuations and small peaks, but the overall trend remains consistently downward. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend with significant Mann-Kendall test results across various windows, indicating a strong and consistent decline in the total energy of female animals. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points suggests periods where the rate of decline changes, but the overall direction remains the same. For decision makers, this trend implies that under the given model parameters and conditions, the female animal population is under significant stress, leading to a rapid depletion of their total energy and potentially their extinction. This insight could be crucial for understanding the dynamics of megaherbivore populations under hunting pressure and environmental conditions, guiding conservation efforts and policy decisions.
