The total energy of male animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12500 units. This high energy level rapidly declines within the first few hundred ticks, indicating a sharp reduction in the energy available within the male animal population. By around 500 ticks, the total energy has decreased significantly, reaching values closer to 4000 units. This rapid decline continues, albeit at a slower rate, and by approximately 1000 ticks, the total energy levels off to lower values, fluctuating around a few hundred units. The trend continues to decrease gradually, eventually approaching zero by around 2300 ticks and remaining there for the rest of the simulation. The statistical analysis confirms this decreasing trend with multiple significant windows identified by the rolling Mann-Kendall test, all showing a significant decrease in total energy over time. The presence of several inflection points and change points further supports the observation of a consistently declining energy level. The implications for decision-makers suggest that under the given parameters, the male animal population experiences a rapid and sustained reduction in total energy, which could be indicative of high hunting pressure or other environmental factors leading to a decline in the population's overall energy reserves.
