The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a general increasing trend over time, with notable dynamics and fluctuations. Initially, the number of males killed starts at zero and gradually increases, reflecting the initial hunting activities of the foragers. The early phase of the simulation, up to around 1,200 ticks, shows a steady rise in the number of males killed, indicating consistent hunting pressure. 

Key time steps reveal significant changes in the trend. For instance, around the 1,876th tick, the cumulative number of males killed reaches its peak at approximately 120.17, suggesting a period of intense hunting activity. Following this peak, there are several smaller peaks and valleys, indicating fluctuations in hunting success and possibly changes in prey availability or forager behavior. The inflection points identified around ticks 362, 1241, and several others suggest periods where the rate of increase in males killed changes, potentially due to shifts in the balance between prey population dynamics and hunting pressure. 

The rolling Mann-Kendall test results indicate various trends over different time windows. Initially, there is a strong increasing trend in the number of males killed, which gradually weakens over time. Notably, between ticks 1160 and 1391, there is no significant trend, suggesting a period of stability in hunting activities. This is followed by another increasing trend and then a decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation, indicating a possible decline in hunting success or a reduction in prey availability. 

The oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation suggest periodic fluctuations in the number of males killed, which could be attributed to seasonal variations or cyclic patterns in prey behavior and availability. The dominant oscillation period of 63 ticks may reflect underlying periodic dynamics in the model, such as seasonal changes in grass regrowth or prey movement patterns. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the complex interactions between hunting pressure and prey population dynamics. The initial increase in males killed suggests that hunting pressure can significantly impact prey populations, but the subsequent fluctuations and periods of stability indicate that other factors, such as prey reproduction rates and environmental conditions, also play crucial roles. Understanding these dynamics can inform strategies for managing wildlife populations and hunting practices to achieve sustainable outcomes.
