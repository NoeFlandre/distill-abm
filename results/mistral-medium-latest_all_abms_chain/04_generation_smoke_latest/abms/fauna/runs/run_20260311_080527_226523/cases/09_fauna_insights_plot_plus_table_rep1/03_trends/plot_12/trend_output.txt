The total energy of female animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent downward trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12,500 units. This high energy level rapidly declines within the first few hundred steps, indicating a sharp decrease in the energy available within the female animal population. By around 500 steps, the energy level has significantly reduced, continuing to decrease more gradually thereafter. Throughout the simulation, the energy levels experience minor fluctuations, but the overall trend remains a steady decline. By the end of the simulation at 2,312 steps, the total energy of female animals reaches zero, indicating the complete depletion of energy within this population. Key inflection points and change points highlight periods where the rate of decline varies, but the consistent downward trend is the dominant characteristic. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this decreasing trend across various windows, with significant negative slopes. The implications for decision-makers suggest that under the given parameters, the female animal population faces a continuous and eventual total depletion of energy, which could be indicative of increasing stress or unsustainable conditions within the simulated environment.
