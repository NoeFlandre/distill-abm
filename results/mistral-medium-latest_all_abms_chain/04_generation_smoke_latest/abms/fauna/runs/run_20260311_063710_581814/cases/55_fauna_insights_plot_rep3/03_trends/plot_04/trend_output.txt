The data from the repeated simulations of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) show a clear trend in the cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands over time. Initially, the number of females killed increases rapidly from 0 to around 100 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase suggests a high initial hunting pressure on the female prey population. After this initial surge, the rate of increase slows down significantly, and the number of females killed stabilizes around 120 by approximately 1000 steps. This stabilization indicates that the hunting pressure reaches a point of equilibrium where the rate of hunting matches the rate at which new females are either born or become available for hunting. For decision makers, this trend implies that initial hunting activities have a significant impact on the female prey population, but over time, the system reaches a balance. This could inform strategies related to sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts, highlighting the importance of monitoring and managing hunting pressures to prevent rapid declines in prey populations.
