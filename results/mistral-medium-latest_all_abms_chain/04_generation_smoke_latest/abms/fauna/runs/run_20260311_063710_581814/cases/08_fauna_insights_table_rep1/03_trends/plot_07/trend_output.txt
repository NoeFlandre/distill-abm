The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a general increasing trend over time, indicating a rise in mortality due to both natural causes and predation by foragers. Initially, the death count starts at zero and gradually increases, reflecting the early stages of the simulation where interactions between predators and prey are establishing. The most significant increases occur within the first 2000 ticks, where the death count rises steeply. This period is marked by several inflection points, suggesting notable changes in the rate of prey mortality. Key time steps such as 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850 ticks are identified as change points, indicating shifts in the dynamics of the system. After approximately 2000 ticks, the rate of increase in the death count slows down, and the trend stabilizes, oscillating around a mean value of approximately 200 deaths. The peaks and valleys identified in the data suggest minor fluctuations in the death count, but these do not significantly deviate from the overall trend. The dominant oscillation period of 63 ticks indicates a cyclical pattern in the death count, possibly related to seasonal or periodic changes in the model. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results show significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, followed by periods of no trend and a slight decreasing trend towards the end. This suggests that while the initial phases are characterized by rising mortality, the system eventually reaches a more stable state with minor fluctuations. For decision makers, these trends imply that initial conditions and early interactions are critical in determining the mortality dynamics of the prey population. Understanding these patterns can help in assessing the impact of hunting pressure and environmental factors on the survival of megafaunal species.
