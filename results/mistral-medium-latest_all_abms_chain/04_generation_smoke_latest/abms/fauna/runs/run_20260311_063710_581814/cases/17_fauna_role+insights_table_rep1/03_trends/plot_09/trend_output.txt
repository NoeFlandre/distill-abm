The simulation of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decline in the number of female prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at 125 and exhibits a sharp decrease in the early stages of the simulation. This downward trend continues throughout the simulation, with the population eventually reaching zero by the end of the observed period. The mean number of female prey animals across the simulation is approximately 16.48, with a median value of 1, indicating that for the majority of the simulation, the population is quite low. The standard deviation of 31.55 highlights significant variability in the population size at different time steps. Key inflection points and change points suggest periods where the rate of decline varies, but the overall trend remains consistently downward. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this decreasing trend across multiple windows, with significant p-values indicating strong statistical evidence of decline. The presence of several peaks and valleys suggests minor fluctuations in the population, but these are overshadowed by the overall downward trajectory. The dominant oscillation period of 63 time steps indicates cyclical patterns within the broader decline. For decision-makers, these trends suggest that under the given parameters, the female prey population is under significant pressure, leading to eventual extinction. This insight could inform conservation strategies and highlight the need for interventions to mitigate factors contributing to the decline.
