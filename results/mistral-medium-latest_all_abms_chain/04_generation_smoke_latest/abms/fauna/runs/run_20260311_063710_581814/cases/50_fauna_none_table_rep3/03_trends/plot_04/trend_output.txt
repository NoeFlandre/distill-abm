The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a general increasing trend over the simulation period. Initially, the count starts at zero and gradually rises, indicating a steady increase in the hunting and killing of female prey. The trend is marked by several inflection points and change points, suggesting variations in the rate of increase at different time steps. Early in the simulation, the rate of increase is relatively rapid, as evidenced by the significant slopes in the initial rolling Mann-Kendall windows. For instance, the slope is 0.213 in the first window and 0.226 in the second window, indicating a sharp rise in the early stages. As the simulation progresses, the rate of increase slows down, with slopes decreasing to 0.006 and even lower in subsequent windows. This deceleration suggests a potential stabilization in the hunting rate or a balance being reached in the ecosystem dynamics. The data series reaches several peaks, with the highest peak value of approximately 123.6667 around the time step 1826, followed by a slight decrease and subsequent smaller peaks. The presence of multiple peaks and valleys indicates periodic fluctuations in the hunting rate, possibly due to seasonal variations or other cyclic factors in the model. The oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation period further support the presence of periodic patterns. Overall, the trend demonstrates an initial rapid increase in the hunting of female prey, followed by a gradual stabilization and periodic fluctuations, reflecting the complex interactions between human hunters and prey animals in the simulated environment.
