The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population begins at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 200 deaths. This sharp rise in deaths can be attributed to the initial interactions between the prey population and human hunters, as well as natural mortality factors. As the simulation progresses, the rate of increase in the number of deaths slows down significantly. By around 1000 time steps, the cumulative number of deaths stabilizes and plateaus at approximately 250 deaths. This stabilization suggests that the prey population and the human hunters have reached a dynamic equilibrium, where the rate of deaths balances with the birth rate and other survival factors. The low variance among simulation runs indicates a consistent pattern in the prey population dynamics under the given parameters. For decision makers, this trend implies that initial hunting pressure and natural mortality have a significant impact on the prey population, but over time, the system stabilizes, suggesting that sustainable hunting practices could be developed to maintain the prey population at a stable level.
