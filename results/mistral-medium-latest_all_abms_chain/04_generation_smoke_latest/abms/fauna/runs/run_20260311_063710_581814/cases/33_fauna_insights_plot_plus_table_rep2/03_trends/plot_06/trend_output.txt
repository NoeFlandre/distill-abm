The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits several distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the number of births remains at zero until around 725 ticks, indicating no births during this early phase. Following this, there is a significant increase in the number of births, reaching a peak of approximately 5.6 births around 1673 ticks. This upward trend is marked by several inflection points and is statistically significant, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall test, which shows increasing trends with high tau values and significant p-values in multiple windows. After reaching the peak, the number of births begins to decline, with notable change points at 1325 and 1850 ticks. The decline continues, showing significant decreasing trends in the rolling Mann-Kendall test windows. The number of births stabilizes at a lower level towards the end of the simulation, with minor oscillations detected around specific indices. The dominant period of these oscillations is approximately 50 ticks. The implications for decision-makers include understanding the dynamics of prey population growth and decline under given environmental and hunting pressure conditions, which can inform strategies for conservation and management of wildlife populations.
