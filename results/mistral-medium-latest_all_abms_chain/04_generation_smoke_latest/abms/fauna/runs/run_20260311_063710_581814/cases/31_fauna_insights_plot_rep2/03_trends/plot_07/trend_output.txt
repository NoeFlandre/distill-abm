The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population shows a rapid increase initially, followed by a gradual leveling off. Initially, the number of deaths rises steeply from 0 to approximately 200 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase indicates a high mortality rate early in the simulation, which could be due to a combination of predation by human hunters and natural causes. Following this initial surge, the rate of increase in the number of deaths slows down significantly. Between 500 and 2000 steps, the cumulative number of deaths stabilizes, reaching a plateau around 250 deaths. This stabilization suggests that the prey population may have reached a more stable state, where the remaining animals are either less susceptible to predation or have adapted to the environmental conditions. For decision makers, this trend implies that initial conditions and early interventions are critical in influencing the survival rates of the prey population. Understanding the factors contributing to the high early mortality could be key to developing strategies to mitigate extinction risks.
