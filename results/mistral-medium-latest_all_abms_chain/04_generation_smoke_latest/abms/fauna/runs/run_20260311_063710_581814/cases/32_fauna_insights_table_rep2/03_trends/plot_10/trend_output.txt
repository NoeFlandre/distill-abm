The simulation of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent trend of decreasing prey animal population over time. Initially, the population starts at approximately 166.67 animals. This number steadily declines throughout the simulation, with the population eventually reaching zero by the final tick. The decline is marked by several inflection points and change points, indicating periods where the rate of decline changes. Key inflection points occur at ticks 357, 747, 825, and several others, suggesting shifts in the dynamics of the population decline. Change points at ticks 125, 475, 625, 875, and 2310 further highlight significant moments where the trend of the population changes notably. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent decreasing trend across multiple windows, with significant negative slopes and tau values, reinforcing the overall decline in prey numbers. Peaks and valleys in the data indicate minor fluctuations, but these do not alter the overall downward trajectory. The dominant oscillation period of 63 ticks suggests some cyclical patterns within the decline, but these oscillations are not sufficient to reverse the overall trend. For decision-makers, this simulation suggests that under the given parameters, the prey population is on a path to extinction without intervention. The consistent decline highlights the potential impact of hunting pressure and environmental conditions on the survival of megaherbivores, emphasizing the need for careful management and conservation strategies to mitigate such outcomes.
