The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands begins at zero and increases rapidly within the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 80 males killed. This rapid initial increase indicates a high initial hunting success rate, likely due to the abundance of prey and the high energy value gained from hunting males. Between 500 and 1000 time steps, the rate of increase slows down, with the number of males killed reaching around 110. This deceleration suggests that the initial surge in hunting success is followed by a period of stabilization, where the rate of successful hunts decreases as the prey population potentially becomes more wary or scarce. Beyond 1000 time steps, the number of males killed plateaus, fluctuating slightly around 120 males killed. This plateau indicates that the system may have reached a dynamic equilibrium where the rate of successful hunts balances the rate of prey reproduction and other mortality factors. The insights from these trends suggest that initial hunting efforts are highly successful, but over time, the efficiency of hunting decreases and stabilizes, which could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts.
