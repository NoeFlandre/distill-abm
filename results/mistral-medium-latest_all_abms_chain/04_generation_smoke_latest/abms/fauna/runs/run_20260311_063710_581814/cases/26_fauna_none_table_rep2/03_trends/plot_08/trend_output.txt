The simulation of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear trend in the population dynamics of male prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at 125 male prey animals. From the beginning, there is a consistent and significant decreasing trend in the number of male prey animals. This decline is steep initially, with the population dropping rapidly in the early time steps. The rate of decline slows down as time progresses, but the overall trend remains downward. By the end of the simulation, the population of male prey animals reaches zero, indicating a complete extinction event. Throughout the simulation, there are several inflection points and minor oscillations, but these do not alter the overall decreasing trend. The most notable inflection points occur around time steps 390, 755, and 817, where the rate of decline changes slightly. The population experiences minor peaks and valleys, particularly around time steps 730, 803, and 820, but these fluctuations are relatively small and do not significantly impact the overall trend. The dominant period of oscillation is approximately 63 time steps, indicating some regularity in the minor fluctuations observed. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms the significant decreasing trend in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with the trend becoming less significant and eventually stabilizing in the later phases. The change points identified at time steps 125, 250, and 375 mark significant shifts in the rate of decline. Overall, the simulation demonstrates a steady and significant decline in the male prey population, leading to complete extinction by the end of the observed period.
