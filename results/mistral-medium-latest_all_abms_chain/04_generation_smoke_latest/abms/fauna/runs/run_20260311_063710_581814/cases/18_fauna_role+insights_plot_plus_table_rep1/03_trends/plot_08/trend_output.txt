The number of male animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a consistent and significant decreasing trend from an initial population of 125. This decline is steep initially, with the population dropping sharply within the first few hundred time steps. By around 250 time steps, the population has decreased substantially, and this downward trend continues, albeit at a slower rate, through subsequent time steps. The rate of decline varies, with notable inflection points indicating changes in the rate of decrease. For instance, there are significant changes in the trend around time steps 390, 755, and 817, among others. These inflection points suggest periods where the rate of population decline either accelerates or decelerates. The population continues to decrease steadily, with minor fluctuations and small peaks and valleys, but the overall trend remains downward. By around 2310 time steps, the population of male animals approaches zero, indicating near or complete extinction. The statistical analysis confirms this trend, with significant decreasing trends identified in multiple rolling Mann-Kendall windows, reinforcing the consistent decline in the male animal population throughout the simulation. The implications for decision-makers suggest that under the given parameters, the male animal population is highly susceptible to extinction due to the combined pressures of hunting and environmental factors. This trend highlights the need for careful management of hunting practices and environmental conditions to prevent the extinction of male megaherbivores in similar ecological settings.
