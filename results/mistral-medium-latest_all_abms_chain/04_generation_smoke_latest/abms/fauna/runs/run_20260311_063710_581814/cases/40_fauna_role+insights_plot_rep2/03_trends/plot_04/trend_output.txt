Following this steep rise, the rate of increase slows down significantly, and the cumulative number of females killed stabilizes around 120. This plateau suggests that the hunting pressure on female prey reaches a balance, possibly due to a reduction in the female prey population or changes in the dynamics of the ecosystem, such as the availability of grass and the movement patterns of the remaining prey. 

For decision makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressure can significantly impact the female prey population quickly. However, over time, the system reaches a new equilibrium where the rate of hunting stabilizes. This information can be crucial for understanding the sustainability of hunting practices and the potential long-term impacts on prey populations.
