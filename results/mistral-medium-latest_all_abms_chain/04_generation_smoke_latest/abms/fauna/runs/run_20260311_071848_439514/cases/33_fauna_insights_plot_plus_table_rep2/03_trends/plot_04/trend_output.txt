The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands exhibits a rapid increase initially, followed by a gradual stabilization. The number of females killed starts at 0 and rises sharply within the first 500 steps, reaching approximately 100. This rapid increase continues more gradually until around 1000 steps, where it starts to plateau. By around 1500 steps, the number of females killed stabilizes around 120, with minor fluctuations. The mean number of females killed is approximately 104.56, with a median of 119.06, indicating that the data tends to cluster around the higher end of the range. The standard deviation of 31.10 suggests some variability in the number of females killed across different simulations. The maximum value recorded is 123.67, indicating the upper limit of females killed in the simulations. The trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows a consistent increasing trend in the early phases, which gradually diminishes and eventually shows a slight decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation period. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points suggests shifts in the rate of increase at various stages. The dominant oscillation period of 63 steps indicates periodic fluctuations in the latter part of the simulation. For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial hunting pressure is intense but stabilizes over time, suggesting that hunting efforts may become less effective or that the prey population reaches a point where further hunting does not significantly increase the cumulative count of females killed.
