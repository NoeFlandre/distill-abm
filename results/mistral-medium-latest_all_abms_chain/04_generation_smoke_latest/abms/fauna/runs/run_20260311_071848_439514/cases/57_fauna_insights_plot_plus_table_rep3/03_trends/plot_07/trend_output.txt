The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulated time steps. Initially, the number of deaths starts at zero and increases rapidly. This rapid increase continues until around 500 steps, where the rate of increase begins to slow down. By approximately 1000 steps, the cumulative number of deaths stabilizes, indicating a plateau. This plateau persists with minor fluctuations around a mean value of approximately 235 deaths, suggesting a steady-state or equilibrium condition in the population dynamics. The minor fluctuations around the plateau indicate some variability in the death rate, but the overall trend remains stable. The initial rapid increase in deaths can be attributed to the initial interactions between the prey and hunters, as well as natural mortality factors. As the simulation progresses, the system reaches a balance where the death rate stabilizes, reflecting an equilibrium between births, natural deaths, and predation. For decision makers, this trend suggests that initial conditions and early interactions significantly impact the prey population. However, over time, the system tends to stabilize, indicating that long-term management strategies should focus on maintaining this equilibrium to prevent drastic fluctuations in the prey population.
