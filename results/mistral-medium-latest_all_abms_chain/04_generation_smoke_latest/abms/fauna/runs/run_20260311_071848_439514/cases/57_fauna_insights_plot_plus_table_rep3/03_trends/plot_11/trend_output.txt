The total energy of male animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12,500 units. This high energy level rapidly declines within the first few hundred ticks, indicating a sharp reduction in the energy available within the male animal population. By around 500 ticks, the energy level drops significantly to approximately 4,000 units, marking a critical point in the decline. 

Throughout the subsequent ticks, the energy continues to decrease, albeit at a slower rate, with several minor peaks and valleys observed around the 377 to 408 tick range. These fluctuations suggest brief periods of stabilization or slight recovery in energy levels, but the overall trend remains downward. The inflection points identified at various intervals (e.g., 316, 453, 516 ticks) further emphasize the continuous decline in energy, with each inflection point marking a shift in the rate of decrease. 

The rolling Mann-Kendall test results confirm this decreasing trend across multiple windows, with significant negative slopes indicating a steady reduction in energy levels. The presence of change points at specific ticks (e.g., 125, 350, 500 ticks) highlights key moments where the rate of energy decline undergoes notable changes. 

The oscillation indices suggest periodic fluctuations within the overall decreasing trend, but these oscillations do not alter the primary downward trajectory. By the end of the simulation period, the total energy of male animals approaches zero, indicating a near-complete depletion of energy within the population. 

For decision-makers, these trends suggest that under the given model parameters, the male animal population experiences a rapid and sustained decline in energy, which could have significant implications for their survival and the overall ecosystem dynamics. Understanding these trends can help in evaluating the impact of hunting pressure and environmental conditions on the energy levels of male animals and in designing interventions to mitigate potential extinctions.
