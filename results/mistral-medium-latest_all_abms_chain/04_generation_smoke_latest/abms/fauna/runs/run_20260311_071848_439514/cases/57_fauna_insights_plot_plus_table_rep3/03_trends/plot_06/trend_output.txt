The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulated time steps. Initially, the number of births remains at zero until around 725 ticks, indicating a period with no births. Following this, there is a gradual increase in the number of births, reaching a peak of approximately 5.6 births around 1673 ticks. This increasing trend is statistically significant, as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, which shows increasing trends with high Kendall's tau values and significant p-values in multiple windows. After reaching the peak, the number of births begins to decline, with a notable decrease observed around 1850 ticks. This decline continues, with some fluctuations, until the end of the simulation. The analysis also identifies several change points and inflection points, indicating shifts in the trend of births over time. The oscillation analysis suggests periodic fluctuations in the number of births, with a dominant period of 50 ticks. For decision makers, these trends highlight the dynamic nature of prey population births under the given environmental and hunting conditions, suggesting periods of population growth followed by decline, which could inform strategies for managing wildlife populations and understanding extinction risks.
