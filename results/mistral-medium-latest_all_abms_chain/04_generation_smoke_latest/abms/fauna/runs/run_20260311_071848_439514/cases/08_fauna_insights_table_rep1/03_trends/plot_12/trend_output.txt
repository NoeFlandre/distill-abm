The combined total energy available in the population of female prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the energy level starts at 12,500 units. This high initial value rapidly declines, indicating a significant reduction in the energy available within the female prey population early in the simulation. The trend continues to decrease steadily throughout the simulation, eventually reaching zero by the final tick. This suggests a complete depletion of energy resources among female prey animals over the modeled period. The statistical analysis reveals multiple inflection points and change points, indicating phases where the rate of energy decline varies. Notably, there are periods of slight oscillations, particularly around the mid-range of the simulation, but these do not alter the overall downward trajectory. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms the significant decreasing trend across various windows, reinforcing the consistent decline in energy levels. For decision makers, this trend implies that under the given parameters, the female prey population faces a continuous and eventual total depletion of energy, which could lead to potential extinction or severe population decline. This insight is crucial for understanding the dynamics of prey populations under specific environmental and hunting pressures, and for making informed decisions regarding conservation strategies or further research directions.
