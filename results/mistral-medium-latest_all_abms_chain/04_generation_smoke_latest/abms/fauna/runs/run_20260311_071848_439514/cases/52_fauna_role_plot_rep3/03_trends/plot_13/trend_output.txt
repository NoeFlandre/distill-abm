The total energy of all animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent trend across the simulations. Initially, the total energy of all animals starts at a high value of approximately 25,000 units. This high energy level is maintained for a brief period, indicating a stable and abundant energy state among the prey population at the beginning of the simulation. 

As the simulation progresses, there is a noticeable and rapid decline in the total energy of all animals. By around 500 steps, the total energy drops significantly to about 5,000 units. This sharp decrease suggests a substantial reduction in the energy reserves of the prey population, potentially due to increased hunting pressure, environmental changes, or a combination of both factors. 

Following this steep decline, the total energy of all animals continues to decrease but at a slower rate. Between 500 and 1,500 steps, the energy levels gradually taper off, eventually approaching zero. This indicates that the prey population is experiencing a continuous decline in energy reserves, leading to a potential collapse or extinction of the population. 

Beyond 1,500 steps, the total energy of all animals stabilizes at a very low value, close to zero. This suggests that the prey population has reached a critical point where their energy reserves are nearly depleted, and the population is no longer sustainable under the given conditions of the simulation. The trends observed in the total energy of all animals highlight the dynamic interactions between hunting pressure, environmental factors, and the life history of the prey population within the MHPM.
