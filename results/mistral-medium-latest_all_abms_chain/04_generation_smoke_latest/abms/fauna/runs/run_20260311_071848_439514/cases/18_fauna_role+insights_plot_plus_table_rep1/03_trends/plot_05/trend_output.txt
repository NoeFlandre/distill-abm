The cumulative number of postpartum females killed in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a rapid initial increase followed by a stabilization phase. Initially, the number of postpartum females killed rises sharply from 0 to approximately 120 within the first 1000 steps. This rapid increase indicates a high hunting pressure on postpartum females at the beginning of the simulation. Following this initial surge, the rate of increase slows down significantly, and the cumulative number of postpartum females killed stabilizes around 120, with minor fluctuations. The stabilization phase suggests that the hunting pressure reaches a balance, potentially due to a reduction in the available postpartum female population or changes in the dynamics of the predator-prey interactions. Key inflection points and change points in the data indicate shifts in the trend, with notable changes occurring around steps 245, 262, 285, and further points up to 2310, reflecting periods where the rate of increase in hunting changes. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results show significant increasing trends in the early phases, transitioning to periods with no significant trend and eventually to a decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation. This indicates varying phases of hunting pressure and potential shifts in the dynamics of the ecosystem or hunting strategies. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial conditions and early interventions in managing hunting pressures to prevent rapid declines in specific subpopulations, such as postpartum females, which are crucial for the sustainability of the prey population.
