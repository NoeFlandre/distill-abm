The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, the number of males killed starts at zero and increases rapidly. This rapid increase continues until around 500 steps, where the rate of increase begins to slow down. By approximately 1000 steps, the cumulative number of males killed reaches a plateau, stabilizing around 120 males killed. This plateau indicates that the rate at which males are being killed has significantly decreased, and the cumulative count remains relatively constant with minor fluctuations. The mean number of males killed across the simulations is approximately 100, with a median of 116.5, suggesting that in most simulations, the number of males killed tends to be high. The standard deviation of 32.5 indicates some variability in the outcomes of different simulations. The maximum value observed is 120.1667 males killed, which occurs around the 1876th step, while the minimum value remains at zero, as expected at the start of the simulations. The analysis of trends using the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulations, with a shift to no significant trend and even a slight decreasing trend towards the end. This suggests that the initial phases are characterized by a rapid increase in hunting success, which stabilizes over time. The identified change points and inflection indices further support the observation of distinct phases in the hunting dynamics, with notable changes around the 125th, 250th, 375th, 500th, 625th, and 850th steps, followed by a significant change point at the 2310th step. The oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation period indicate minor periodic fluctuations in the number of males killed, reflecting small variations in hunting success rates during the plateau phase.
