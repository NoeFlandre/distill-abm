The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands shows a rapid increase in the early stages of the simulation, reaching approximately 100 males killed by around 500 steps. This initial phase is characterized by a steep upward trend, indicating a high rate of hunting success or a high initial population of male prey. Following this rapid increase, the rate of males killed begins to plateau, with the cumulative number stabilizing around 120 males killed. This plateau phase starts around 1000 steps and continues for the remainder of the simulation, suggesting a balance between the hunting rate and the availability of male prey. The data indicates several inflection points and change points, which highlight shifts in the dynamics of the hunting process. Notably, the rolling Mann-Kendall test reveals significant increasing trends in the early phases, followed by periods of no significant trend and a slight decreasing trend towards the end. This suggests that the initial hunting pressure is high but eventually stabilizes, potentially due to a reduction in the male prey population or changes in hunting behavior. The oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation indicate minor periodic fluctuations in the number of males killed, but these oscillations are relatively small in magnitude. For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial hunting efforts are highly effective but may lead to a stabilization in the number of males killed over time. This could inform strategies related to sustainable hunting practices and the management of prey populations to avoid potential extinctions or significant disruptions in the ecosystem.
