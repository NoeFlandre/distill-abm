The number of male animals in the simulation starts at 125 and declines rapidly over the first 500 time steps. This initial decline could be attributed to the high initial number of hunters and the relatively high energy value and low processing cost of male prey, making them a prime target for hunters. The number of male animals continues to decrease steadily, reaching near zero around 2000 time steps. This steady decline reflects the continuous hunting pressure and the lack of sufficient reproduction to sustain the population. There are minor fluctuations and small peaks observed around 730, 803, and 820 time steps, which could be due to temporary reductions in hunting pressure or slight increases in birth rates. However, these fluctuations are not significant enough to reverse the overall declining trend. The simulation ends with the number of male animals at zero, indicating a complete depletion of the male population under the given conditions. The rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a consistent decreasing trend in the number of male animals across multiple time windows, reinforcing the observation of a steady decline. The presence of several change points suggests shifts in the rate of decline at different stages of the simulation. For decision makers, these trends highlight the vulnerability of male animals under sustained hunting pressure and the need for potential interventions to balance hunting rates with reproduction rates to prevent extinction.
