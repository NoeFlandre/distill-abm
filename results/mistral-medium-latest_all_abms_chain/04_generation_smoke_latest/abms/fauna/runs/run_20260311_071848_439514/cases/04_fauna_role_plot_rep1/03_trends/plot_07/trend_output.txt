The simulations of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibit distinct trends in the cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population over time. Initially, the number of deaths starts at zero and remains relatively low for the first few hundred steps. Around the 500-step mark, there is a noticeable increase in the death rate, which continues to rise steeply until approximately 1000 steps. During this period, the cumulative number of deaths reaches around 200. Following this rapid increase, the rate of deaths begins to plateau, indicating a stabilization in the number of deaths per time step. By around 1500 steps, the cumulative number of deaths stabilizes at approximately 250, with minimal increases thereafter. This trend suggests an initial phase of high mortality, potentially due to a combination of environmental factors, hunting pressure, and internal dynamics of the prey population, followed by a stabilization phase where the population dynamics reach a more steady state. The plateauing effect could indicate that the remaining population has adapted to the pressures or that the pressures themselves have lessened due to a reduced prey population.
