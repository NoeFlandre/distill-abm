The simulation of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear overall decreasing trend in the combined total energy available in all prey animals over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of approximately 16,666.67 units. This high initial value suggests a robust and healthy prey population at the beginning of the simulation. However, the energy levels begin to decline rapidly from the outset. The most significant drops in energy levels occur within the first few hundred time steps, indicating a sharp decline in the prey population's overall energy. By the end of the simulation, the total energy available in prey animals drops to zero, suggesting the complete extinction of the prey population. Throughout the simulation, there are several notable peaks and valleys in the energy levels, indicating periods of temporary stabilization or slight recovery followed by continued decline. For instance, around time steps 385 to 440, there are fluctuations where the energy levels briefly stabilize before continuing to decrease. These fluctuations could be due to various factors such as temporary reductions in hunting pressure, changes in environmental conditions, or demographic changes within the prey population. The simulation also identifies several inflection points and change points, indicating significant shifts in the trend of energy levels. For example, change points at time steps 350, 500, 625, and 875 mark periods where the rate of decline in energy levels changes noticeably. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results further confirm the overall decreasing trend in energy levels across different time windows, with significant decreasing trends observed in the initial phases of the simulation. There are also periods where the trend temporarily shifts to increasing, such as around time steps 1392 to 1623 and 1856 to 2087, suggesting brief periods of recovery or stabilization in the prey population's energy levels. The dominant oscillation period of 63 time steps indicates a cyclical pattern in the energy levels, possibly corresponding to seasonal or other periodic environmental factors. In summary, the simulation demonstrates a clear and significant decline in the total energy available in prey animals over time, leading to their eventual extinction. The trends and patterns observed in the data suggest complex interactions between hunting pressure, environmental conditions, and prey population dynamics.
