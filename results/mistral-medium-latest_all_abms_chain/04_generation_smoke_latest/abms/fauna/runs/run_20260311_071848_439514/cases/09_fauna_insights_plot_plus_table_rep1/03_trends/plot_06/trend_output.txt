The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulated time steps. Initially, there are no births, as indicated by the starting value of 0.0. The number of births begins to increase gradually, with notable peaks and valleys indicating fluctuations in birth rates. Key time steps and values include a peak at around 1,673 steps with a value of 5.6 births, followed by other significant peaks at approximately 1,392 steps (4.3 births), 1,214 steps (3.45 births), and 1,073 steps (2.875 births). The data also shows valleys at around 1,422 steps (3.8889 births), 1,281 steps (3.0588 births), and 1,097 steps (2.871 births). The mean number of births is 2.1751, with a median of 2.4865, indicating a generally increasing trend in births over time. The rolling Mann-Kendall test reveals significant increasing trends in birth rates during the initial and middle phases of the simulation, followed by decreasing trends towards the later phases. Change points are identified at approximately 725, 1,325, 1,850, 2,050, and 2,310 steps, marking shifts in the birth rate dynamics. The oscillation analysis suggests periodic fluctuations in the birth rates with a dominant period of 50 steps. These trends imply that the prey population experiences phases of growth and decline, which could be influenced by various factors such as environmental conditions, hunting pressure, and internal dynamics of the animal population. For decision-makers, understanding these trends can provide insights into the resilience and sustainability of the prey population under different scenarios, aiding in the development of conservation strategies and management practices.
