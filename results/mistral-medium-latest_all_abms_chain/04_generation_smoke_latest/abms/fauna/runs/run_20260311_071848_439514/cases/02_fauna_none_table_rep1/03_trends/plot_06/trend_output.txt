The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits several distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the number of births starts at zero and gradually increases. The early phase of the simulation, up to around the 725th tick, shows a steady rise in the birth count. This increasing trend continues more sharply through subsequent periods, notably between the 928th and 1159th ticks, where the rate of increase becomes more pronounced. The most significant peaks in the birth count occur around the 1673rd tick, reaching a maximum value of 5.6 births. Following this peak, there is a noticeable decline in the birth rate starting around the 1850th tick, indicating a shift in the population dynamics. The simulation also reveals several inflection points and oscillations, suggesting periodic fluctuations in the birth rate. These oscillations have a dominant period of approximately 50 ticks, indicating regular intervals of increase and decrease in the birth count. The overall trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms significant increasing trends in the early and middle phases of the simulation, followed by significant decreasing trends in the later phases. The change points identified at various ticks further highlight the shifts in the birth rate dynamics, marking transitions between different phases of population growth and decline.
