The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the initial time steps, reaching approximately 120 by around 2000 ticks. This initial rapid increase suggests an early phase of intense hunting activity, possibly due to the high initial availability of prey and the active foraging behavior of hunters. Following this rapid increase, the rate of increase slows down significantly, indicating a potential stabilization in the hunting dynamics. The cumulative number of females killed plateaus around 123, suggesting that the hunting pressure reaches a steady state where the number of prey killed balances with the remaining prey population and other ecological factors. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points throughout the time series indicates shifts in the hunting dynamics, possibly due to changes in prey availability, hunter behavior, or environmental conditions. The rolling Mann-Kendall test results show a consistent increasing trend in the early phases, which transitions to a decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation. This shift could imply that as the prey population decreases, hunting becomes less effective or that hunters reach a saturation point in their hunting capacity. The oscillation indices towards the end of the series suggest minor periodic variations in the hunting success rate, potentially due to seasonal effects or other cyclic environmental factors. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of considering the long-term sustainability of hunting practices and the potential for prey population stabilization under constant hunting pressure.
