The simulation of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent decline in the number of female prey animals over time. Initially, the population starts at 125 female prey animals. The most rapid decline occurs early in the simulation, with a steep decrease in the first few hundred ticks. This trend continues, albeit at a slower rate, throughout the simulation. By the end of the simulation at tick 2311, the population of female prey animals reaches zero, indicating complete extinction. Key inflection points and change points in the data suggest periods where the rate of decline changes, but the overall trend remains consistently downward. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this decreasing trend across multiple windows, with significant negative slopes and tau values. The presence of peaks and valleys indicates some fluctuations in the population, but these are minor compared to the overall downward trajectory. The dominant oscillation period of 63 ticks suggests some cyclical patterns, potentially related to seasonal or reproductive cycles, but these do not alter the long-term trend of decline. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the severe impact of the modeled conditions on the female prey population, leading to eventual extinction. This underscores the need for careful consideration of hunting pressures and environmental factors in conservation strategies.
