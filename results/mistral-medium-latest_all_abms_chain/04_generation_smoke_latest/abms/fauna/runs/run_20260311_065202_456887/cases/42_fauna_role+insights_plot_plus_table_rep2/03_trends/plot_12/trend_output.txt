The total energy of female animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of 12,500 units. This high energy level rapidly declines within the first few hundred time steps, indicating a sharp drop in the energy reserves of the female prey population. By around 500 time steps, the total energy has significantly decreased, reflecting a substantial reduction in the population's energy levels. 

Throughout the simulation, the energy levels continue to decline, albeit at a slower rate compared to the initial drop. The trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this consistent decrease, with significant negative trends observed in multiple time windows. The inflection points and change points identified in the data further support the notion of a continuous decline in energy levels, with notable changes in the rate of decline occurring at specific time steps. 

The presence of minor peaks and valleys suggests some fluctuations in the energy levels, but these are relatively small and do not alter the overall downward trend. The dominant oscillation period of 63 time steps indicates some regularity in these minor fluctuations, but their impact is minimal compared to the overall decline. 

For decision-makers, these trends imply that under the given model parameters, the female prey population experiences a significant and sustained reduction in energy reserves over time. This could be indicative of increasing stress on the population, potentially leading to higher mortality rates and a decline in overall population health. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for developing strategies to mitigate the impact of hunting pressure and environmental factors on megaherbivore populations.
