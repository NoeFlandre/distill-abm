The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a general increasing trend over time, with notable patterns and fluctuations. Initially, the number of hunted females starts at zero and gradually increases. The early phase of the simulation, up to around 231 ticks, shows a rapid increase in the cumulative count, with a slope of approximately 0.213. This trend continues with varying rates of increase across different time windows, as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis. For instance, between ticks 232 and 463, the trend remains strongly increasing with a slope of 0.226. This rate of increase slows down in subsequent windows but remains positive and significant. Around the midpoint of the simulation, particularly after 1160 ticks, the rate of increase becomes more gradual, with slopes approaching zero, indicating a stabilization in the cumulative count of hunted females. A notable change occurs after 1856 ticks, where the trend shifts to a decreasing pattern, although the decrease is minimal. The cumulative count reaches its maximum value of approximately 123.6667 around tick 1826 and remains relatively stable thereafter, with minor fluctuations. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points suggests periods of varying rates of increase, reflecting dynamic interactions within the model. The oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation indicate minor periodic fluctuations in the cumulative count. For decision makers, these trends highlight the initial rapid impact of hunting pressure on female prey, followed by a stabilization phase, and ultimately a slight decline. This suggests that while hunting pressure significantly affects the female prey population initially, other factors such as prey availability, environmental conditions, and hunter behavior may contribute to the observed stabilization and minor fluctuations in the long term.
