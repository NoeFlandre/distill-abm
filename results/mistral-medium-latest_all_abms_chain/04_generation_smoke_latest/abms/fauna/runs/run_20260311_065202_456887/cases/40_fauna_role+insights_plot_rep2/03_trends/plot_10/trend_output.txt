The simulations of the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibit a consistent trend across all runs. Initially, the total number of animals starts at approximately 250, which aligns with the set value for the initial number of prey. In the early stages, up to around 500 steps, there is a gradual decline in the total number of animals. This decline accelerates significantly between 500 and 1500 steps, where the number of animals drops sharply from around 200 to near zero. By approximately 2000 steps, the total number of animals in most simulations reaches zero, indicating a complete extinction event. 

The rapid decline in the number of animals suggests that the combined effects of hunting pressure, environmental conditions, and internal dynamics of the animal population lead to a swift extinction. The initial gradual decline could be attributed to the natural mortality rates and the initial hunting pressure. The accelerated decline phase likely reflects a tipping point where the population can no longer sustain itself due to increased mortality from hunting and possibly reduced reproductive success. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight the critical thresholds and tipping points in the dynamics of megaherbivore populations under hunting pressure. Understanding these patterns can inform conservation strategies and policies aimed at preventing the extinction of vulnerable species. It underscores the importance of monitoring population sizes and implementing timely interventions to mitigate factors leading to rapid declines.
