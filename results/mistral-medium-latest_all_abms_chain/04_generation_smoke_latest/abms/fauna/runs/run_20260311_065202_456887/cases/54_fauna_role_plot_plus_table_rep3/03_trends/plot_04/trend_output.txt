The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the number of females killed starts at zero and rapidly increases within the first 500 steps, reaching approximately 100. This rapid increase is followed by a more gradual rise, eventually stabilizing around 120 females killed. The stabilization occurs around the 1000-step mark, with minor fluctuations observed thereafter. The data shows that the maximum value reached is approximately 123.67 females killed, with this peak occurring around the 1826-step mark. The mean number of females killed across the simulation is about 104.56, with a median of approximately 119.06. The standard deviation is around 31.10, indicating some variability in the number of females killed across different simulation runs. The trend analysis using the rolling Mann-Kendall test indicates a consistent increasing trend in the early phases of the simulation, which transitions to a decreasing trend towards the end. Key inflection points are noted at various steps, such as 245, 262, and 285, indicating significant changes in the rate of increase. The data also shows several change points, notably at steps 125, 375, and 475, which mark shifts in the dynamics of the cumulative number of females killed. Overall, the simulation results suggest a rapid initial increase in hunting success, followed by a stabilization phase with minor oscillations, reflecting the complex interactions between hunters and prey within the model's parameters.
