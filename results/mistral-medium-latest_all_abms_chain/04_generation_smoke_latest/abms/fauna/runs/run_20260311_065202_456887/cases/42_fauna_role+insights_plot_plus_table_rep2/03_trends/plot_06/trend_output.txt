The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulated time steps. Initially, the number of births remains at zero until around time step 725, indicating no births in the early stages of the simulation. Following this, there is a significant increase in the number of births, peaking at approximately 5.6 births around time step 1673. This upward trend is supported by the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, which shows significant increasing trends in the number of births during the windows from time steps 696 to 1623. 

After reaching the peak, the number of births begins to decline, with notable change points at time steps 1850 and 2050. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates significant decreasing trends in the number of births during the windows from time steps 1624 to 2087. The decline continues until the end of the simulation, where the number of births stabilizes around 1.0. 

The analysis also identifies several inflection points and oscillation indices, suggesting periods of fluctuation in the birth rates. The dominant oscillation period is 50 time steps, indicating regular intervals of variation in the number of births. 

For decision makers, these trends highlight critical periods where interventions could be targeted to either support or mitigate the birth rates of the prey population. The initial period of no births followed by a rapid increase suggests a potential lag in the population's reproductive response to environmental conditions or hunting pressures. The subsequent decline and stabilization indicate possible long-term effects of sustained hunting pressure or environmental changes on the prey population's reproductive success.
