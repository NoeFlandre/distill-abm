The total energy available in all prey animals within the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) simulation shows a clear and consistent decreasing trend over time. Initially, the total energy starts at a high value of approximately 16,666.67 units. This high initial value indicates a robust and healthy prey population at the beginning of the simulation. 

The energy levels experience a sharp decline in the early stages, with significant drops observed around the initial time steps. For instance, by time step 385, the energy level decreases to about 4,102.18 units. This rapid decline continues, and by time step 730, the energy level further reduces to approximately 1,083.15 units. The trend of decreasing energy levels persists throughout the simulation, eventually reaching zero by the final time step of 2,311. 

The statistical analysis reveals multiple inflection points and change points, indicating periods where the rate of decline varies. Notable inflection points occur around time steps 309, 460, 513, and several others, suggesting shifts in the dynamics of energy depletion. Change points at 350, 500, 625, 875, and 2310 further highlight significant transitions in the trend. 

The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms the overall decreasing trend across various windows, with significant negative slopes and tau values, reinforcing the consistent decline in total energy. The presence of peaks and valleys, although minor, indicates temporary fluctuations within the overall downward trajectory. 

For decision-makers, these trends suggest that under the given parameters and conditions, the prey population's energy reserves are consistently depleted over time. This could imply that the current hunting pressure and environmental conditions are unsustainable for the prey population, leading to a potential collapse if no interventions are made. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for developing strategies to mitigate the decline and promote the sustainability of megaherbivore populations.
