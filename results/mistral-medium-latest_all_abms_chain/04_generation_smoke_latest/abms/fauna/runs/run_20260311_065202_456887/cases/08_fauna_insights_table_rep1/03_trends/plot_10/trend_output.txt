The simulation of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a clear and consistent trend of decreasing prey animal population over time. Initially, the population starts at approximately 166.67 animals. This number steadily declines throughout the simulation, reaching zero by the final tick. The decline is marked by several inflection points and change points, indicating shifts in the rate of decrease. Notably, the most significant drops occur early in the simulation, with the rate of decline slowing as the population decreases. There are minor oscillations in the population, with a dominant period of 63 ticks, suggesting periodic fluctuations likely tied to seasonal or reproductive cycles. Despite these oscillations, the overall trend remains a steady decrease. The rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms this decreasing trend across multiple windows, with significant decreases noted in the early phases of the simulation. For decision makers, this trend suggests that under the given parameters, the prey population is not sustainable and heads towards extinction. This insight could be crucial for understanding the dynamics of hunting pressure and environmental conditions on megaherbivore populations.
