The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits several distinct trends over the simulated time steps. Initially, there are no births, as indicated by the start value of 0.0000. The number of births begins to increase gradually, with a notable rise starting around the 613th time step. This upward trend continues, reaching a peak of 5.6000 births at the 1673rd time step. Following this peak, there is a slight decline and subsequent fluctuations in the number of births. The mean number of births across the simulation is 2.1751, with a median of 2.4865, indicating that the distribution of births is somewhat skewed. The standard deviation of 1.8327 suggests variability in the number of births over time. Key inflection points occur at various time steps, such as 613, 733, and 903, indicating significant changes in the trend of births. The rolling Mann-Kendall test reveals periods of increasing trends in births, particularly between time steps 696 to 1623, followed by decreasing trends from time steps 1624 to 2087. Change points at 725, 1325, 1850, 2050, and 2310 mark significant shifts in the birth trends. The oscillation analysis indicates a dominant period of 50 time steps, suggesting regular fluctuations in the number of births. For decision makers, these trends highlight the dynamic nature of prey population births under varying environmental and hunting pressures. Understanding these patterns can inform strategies for managing wildlife populations and assessing the impact of human activities on megaherbivore extinction risks.
