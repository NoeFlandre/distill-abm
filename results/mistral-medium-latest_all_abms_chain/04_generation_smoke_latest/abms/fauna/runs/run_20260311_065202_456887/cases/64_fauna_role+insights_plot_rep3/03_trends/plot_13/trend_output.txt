The total energy of all animals in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits a consistent declining trend across all simulations. Initially, the total energy of all animals starts at approximately 25,000 units. This high energy level is maintained for a brief period, indicating a stable and abundant prey population at the beginning of the simulation. 

Around the 200th step, a noticeable decline in total energy begins, marking the onset of a significant reduction in the energy levels of the prey population. By the 500th step, the total energy drops sharply to around 5,000 units, suggesting a critical phase where the prey population experiences substantial stress or depletion. This rapid decline continues until approximately the 1,000th step, where the total energy stabilizes at a much lower level, close to zero. 

The stabilization at near-zero energy levels indicates that the prey population has been nearly exhausted or has reached a state of minimal energy reserves. This trend implies that the combination of hunting pressure, environmental conditions, and other model parameters leads to a significant reduction in the prey population's energy over time. 

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the potential impacts of sustained hunting pressure and environmental factors on megaherbivore populations. The rapid decline in total energy suggests that without intervention or changes in hunting practices and environmental management, prey populations could face severe depletion or extinction. This insight underscores the importance of sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts to maintain the energy balance and viability of prey populations in the long term.
