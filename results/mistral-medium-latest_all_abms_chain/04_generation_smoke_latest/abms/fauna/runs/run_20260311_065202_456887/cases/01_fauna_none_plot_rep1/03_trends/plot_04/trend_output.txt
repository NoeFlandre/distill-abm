The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulated time steps. Initially, the number of females killed starts at zero. In the early stages, up to around 500 steps, there is a rapid increase in the number of females killed, rising steeply from zero to approximately 100. This phase indicates a high rate of hunting success or a high encounter rate between hunters and female prey. Following this initial surge, the rate of increase begins to slow down. Between 500 and 1000 steps, the number of females killed continues to rise but at a more gradual pace, reaching around 120. Beyond 1000 steps, the trend stabilizes, and the number of females killed plateaus, indicating that the rate of hunting success has balanced out with other factors in the model, such as prey availability or hunter saturation. This plateau continues for the remainder of the simulation, suggesting a dynamic equilibrium where the number of females killed remains relatively constant around 120. This trend reflects the interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting behavior, and animal life history as modeled in the MHPM.
