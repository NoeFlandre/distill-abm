The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands starts at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the initial time steps, reaching approximately 100 by around 500 time steps. This initial phase of rapid increase suggests an early period of intense hunting activity, possibly due to the high initial availability of prey and the immediate energy needs of the foragers. Following this rapid increase, the rate of accumulation slows down significantly, indicating a potential stabilization in the hunting dynamics. The cumulative count continues to rise but at a much slower pace, eventually plateauing around 117.5 by the end of the simulation. This plateau suggests that the hunting pressure has reached a balance with the prey population's ability to sustain itself or that the foragers have met their energy requirements. The data shows several inflection points, notably around time steps 362, 1241, and 1330, which may indicate shifts in the hunting behavior or changes in the prey population dynamics. The presence of multiple peaks and valleys in the latter half of the simulation, although with minimal prominence, suggests minor fluctuations in hunting success or prey availability. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates a strong increasing trend in the early phases, which transitions to periods of no significant trend and eventually to a slight decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation. This shift could reflect the depletion of male prey or a change in the foragers' strategy as they adapt to the changing environment. The oscillation indices towards the end of the simulation suggest periodic variations in the hunting success, possibly due to seasonal effects or cyclic patterns in prey behavior.
