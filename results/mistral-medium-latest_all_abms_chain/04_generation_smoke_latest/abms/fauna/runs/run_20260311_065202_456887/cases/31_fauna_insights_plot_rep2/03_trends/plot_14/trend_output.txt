The percentage of green patches in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over the simulated time steps. Initially, the percentage of green patches is around 32%. This value remains relatively stable for approximately the first 300 steps. Following this period, there is a sharp increase in the percentage of green patches, reaching a peak of around 34% at approximately 500 steps. This increase suggests a rapid regrowth or expansion of grass patches in the landscape. After reaching this peak, the percentage of green patches stabilizes again, maintaining a consistent level around 34% until roughly 1000 steps. At this point, there is another sharp decline, bringing the percentage back down to around 32%. This decline indicates a significant reduction in grass coverage, possibly due to increased grazing or environmental changes. Following this drop, the percentage of green patches stabilizes once more, fluctuating slightly but generally maintaining a level close to 32% for the remainder of the simulation. The trends observed in the percentage of green patches provide insights into the dynamics of grass regrowth and grazing pressure within the model. The initial stability, followed by rapid increases and decreases, suggests periods of balance and disruption in the ecosystem. For decision-makers, understanding these patterns can be crucial for managing grazing lands and ensuring the sustainability of both prey animals and their food resources. The fluctuations in green patch percentages highlight the importance of monitoring and potentially intervening in grazing practices to maintain ecological balance.
