The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population, from both natural causes and predation by foragers, exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, the number of deaths increases rapidly, with a steep rise observed from the start up to around 500 time steps. During this period, the death count rises from 0 to approximately 200, indicating a phase of high mortality. This rapid increase is followed by a more gradual rise, which continues until around 1000 time steps, where the death count stabilizes around 230 to 240 deaths. 

Key time steps and values highlight significant points in the trend. For instance, at around 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850 time steps, change points are identified, indicating shifts in the rate of increase in deaths. The inflection points around 362, 1241, 1330, 1397, 1402, 1419, 1455, 1474, 1595, 1713, 1828, and 2040 time steps further emphasize periods where the trend changes its curvature, suggesting variations in the underlying dynamics of the system. 

The statistical analysis reveals that the initial phase of the simulation shows a strong increasing trend in the number of deaths, as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall test results for the first five windows, all indicating significant increasing trends with high tau values and slopes. However, as the simulation progresses, the trend stabilizes, with some windows showing no significant trend and others indicating a slight decrease. 

The peaks and valleys in the data, although not prominent, occur around time steps 1876, 2040, 1672, 1732, 1208, 1395, 1516, and 1166, with values hovering around 237 to 240 deaths. These minor fluctuations suggest periods of slight increases and decreases in the death count, but overall, the system reaches a relatively stable state. 

For decision-makers, these trends imply that initial conditions and early-time dynamics are crucial in determining the mortality rates within the prey population. The rapid increase in deaths early on suggests that initial hunting pressure and environmental conditions significantly impact the prey population. As the system stabilizes, the death rate plateaus, indicating a potential balance between predation pressure and the prey population's ability to sustain itself under given conditions. Understanding these dynamics can help in designing conservation strategies and managing hunting practices to ensure the sustainability of the prey population.
