The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits distinct trends over time. Initially, the number of deaths increases rapidly from 0 to approximately 200 within the first 500 time steps. This rapid increase is followed by a gradual rise, stabilizing around 235 deaths by the 1000th time step. The trend then plateaus, maintaining a relatively constant number of deaths with minor fluctuations around the mean value of approximately 201 deaths. The median number of deaths is 233, indicating that in most simulations, the number of deaths stabilizes around this value. The standard deviation of 65.03 suggests some variability in the number of deaths across different simulations, but the overall pattern remains consistent. The maximum number of deaths recorded is 240.33, observed around the 1876th time step. The analysis of trends using the rolling Mann-Kendall test shows significant increasing trends in the early time steps, transitioning to no significant trend in the middle time steps, and eventually showing a significant decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation period. The presence of multiple inflection points and change points indicates periods of shifting dynamics in the death count, reflecting changes in the underlying processes influencing mortality. The dominant oscillation period of 63 time steps suggests cyclical patterns in the death count, possibly linked to seasonal or periodic environmental factors. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the initial vulnerability of the prey population to hunting pressure and environmental factors, followed by a stabilization phase where the population adapts to the prevailing conditions. The minor fluctuations and cyclical patterns suggest periodic influences that could be targeted for further investigation to understand their impact on the prey population dynamics.
