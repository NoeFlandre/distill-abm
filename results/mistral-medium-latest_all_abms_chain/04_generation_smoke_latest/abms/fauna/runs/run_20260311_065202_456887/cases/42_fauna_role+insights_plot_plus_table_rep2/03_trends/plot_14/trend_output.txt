The percentage of green patches in the landscape, representing the availability of fodder for prey animals, exhibits distinct trends over the simulation period. Initially, the percentage of green patches starts at approximately 33% and remains relatively stable. Around time step 150, there is a noticeable decline in the percentage of green patches, reaching a minimum of about 25% at time step 365. This decline indicates a significant reduction in available fodder, which could be due to increased grazing pressure or environmental changes affecting grass regrowth. Following this decline, the percentage of green patches gradually recovers, showing a series of increasing trends as indicated by the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis. These increasing trends are significant and suggest periods of recovery in grass coverage. The recovery phases are interspersed with periods of stability and minor fluctuations. Around time step 725, the percentage of green patches stabilizes near the initial value of 33%, indicating a balance between grazing pressure and grass regrowth. Subsequent trends show minor oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 time steps, reflecting regular seasonal or cyclical patterns in grass availability. The final phases of the simulation exhibit slight decreases in the percentage of green patches, though these changes are less pronounced compared to the initial decline. Overall, the trends suggest that while there are periods of significant reduction in fodder availability, the system tends to recover and stabilize, indicating resilience in the grassland environment. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of managing grazing pressure and ensuring conditions that support grass regrowth to maintain fodder availability for prey animals.
