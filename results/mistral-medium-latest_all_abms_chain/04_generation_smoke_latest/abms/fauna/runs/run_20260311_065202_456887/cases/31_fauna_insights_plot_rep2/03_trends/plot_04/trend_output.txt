The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands exhibits a rapid increase initially, followed by a gradual plateau. Initially, the number of females killed rises steeply from 0 to approximately 100 within the first 500 steps. This rapid increase indicates a high rate of hunting success or a high initial population of female prey. Following this initial phase, the rate of increase slows down significantly. Between 500 and 1500 steps, the number of females killed continues to rise but at a much slower pace, eventually stabilizing around 120 females killed. This plateau suggests that the hunting pressure stabilizes, possibly due to a balance being reached between the remaining female prey population and the hunting efforts of the foragers. For decision makers, this trend implies that initial hunting efforts are highly effective but that sustained hunting leads to a stabilization in the number of females killed, potentially indicating a need for adaptive management strategies to maintain prey populations or hunting efficiency over time.
