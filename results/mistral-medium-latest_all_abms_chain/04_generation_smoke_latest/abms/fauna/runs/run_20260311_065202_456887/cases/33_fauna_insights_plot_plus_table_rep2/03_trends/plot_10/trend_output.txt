The total number of animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a consistent and significant decreasing trend over time. Initially, the number of animals starts at approximately 166.67 and steadily declines to zero by the end of the simulation period. The mean number of animals across the simulation is 25.01, with a median of 1.48, indicating that for most of the simulation duration, the number of animals is relatively low. The standard deviation of 44.90 and variance of 2015.71 suggest substantial variability in the number of animals over time. The statistical analysis reveals multiple inflection points and change points, indicating periods where the rate of decline changes. Notably, the rolling Mann-Kendall test confirms a consistent decreasing trend across various windows, with significant negative slopes. There are minor oscillations with a dominant period of 63 ticks, but these do not substantially alter the overall declining trend. The implications for decision-makers suggest that under the given parameters, the animal population is not sustainable and tends towards extinction. This trend could inform strategies related to conservation efforts, hunting regulations, and environmental management to potentially alter the outcome and promote equilibrium conditions.
