The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model begins at zero and exhibits a rapid increase in the initial time steps. This early rise in deaths can be attributed to the initial interactions between the human hunters and the prey population, where the prey are not yet adapted to the hunting pressure. The death count continues to rise steeply until around 500 ticks, reaching approximately 200 deaths. This phase likely represents the initial impact of hunting and natural mortality on a naive prey population.

Following this rapid increase, the rate of deaths starts to slow down, indicating a potential stabilization in the dynamics between predators and prey. The death count reaches a plateau around 235 deaths, with minor fluctuations observed around this value. These fluctuations suggest periodic variations in the prey population dynamics, possibly due to seasonal changes or other cyclic environmental factors. The oscillations in the death count, with a dominant period of 63 ticks, indicate regular intervals of increased and decreased mortality, which could be linked to the regrowth patterns of grass and the corresponding availability of food resources for the prey.

The inflection points identified at various time steps suggest changes in the trend of the death count, possibly reflecting shifts in the balance between hunting pressure and prey population resilience. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis indicates significant increasing trends in the early phases, followed by periods of no significant trend, and eventually a decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation. This suggests that while the initial phases are marked by a rapid increase in deaths, the system eventually reaches a more stable state with periodic fluctuations.

For decision-makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial conditions and adaptive behaviors in predator-prey dynamics. The rapid initial increase in deaths underscores the vulnerability of prey populations to sudden changes in hunting pressure. The subsequent stabilization and periodic fluctuations suggest that prey populations can adapt to some extent, but are still subject to regular variations in mortality due to environmental factors. Understanding these dynamics can inform strategies for conservation and management of wildlife populations under hunting pressure.
