The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) shows a general increasing trend over time, with some periods of stabilization and slight decreases. Initially, the number of males killed starts at 0 and increases rapidly. The first significant change point occurs at tick 125, where the rate of increase starts to slow down slightly. This trend continues with additional change points at ticks 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850, indicating periods where the rate of increase shifts. The most notable peaks in the number of males killed occur around ticks 1876, 2040, and 1672, with values reaching approximately 120.17, 119.75, and 119.42, respectively. These peaks suggest periods of intense hunting activity. The inflection points identified at various ticks (e.g., 362, 1241, 1330) indicate changes in the rate of increase, reflecting shifts in hunting dynamics or prey availability. The rolling Mann-Kendall test reveals several windows of increasing trends, particularly in the early and middle phases of the simulation, with significant increases in the cumulative number of males killed. However, there are also periods with no significant trend and even a decreasing trend towards the end of the simulation, suggesting a potential stabilization or reduction in hunting activity. The oscillation analysis indicates a dominant period of 63 ticks, suggesting cyclic patterns in the hunting dynamics. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of understanding the temporal dynamics of hunting pressure and its potential impact on prey populations. The initial rapid increase in hunting followed by periods of stabilization and slight decreases could inform strategies for sustainable hunting practices and conservation efforts.
