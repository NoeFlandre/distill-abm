The cumulative number of births among the prey animal population in the Megaherbivore Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) exhibits several distinct trends and patterns over the simulation period. Initially, the number of births starts at zero and gradually increases. The mean number of births across the simulation is approximately 2.18, with a median of 2.49, indicating a slightly skewed distribution. The standard deviation of 1.83 suggests moderate variability in the birth rates over time. The birth count reaches its maximum value of 5.6 at around the midpoint of the simulation, after which it generally trends downward. Key peaks in the birth count occur at approximately one-quarter, one-third, and halfway through the simulation, with values of 4.3, 3.45, and 2.875 respectively. These peaks are followed by notable valleys, indicating periods of decline in birth rates. The simulation data shows significant increasing trends in birth counts during the early and middle phases, as evidenced by the rolling Mann-Kendall test, with strong positive slopes and significant p-values. However, towards the latter part of the simulation, there is a notable shift to decreasing trends in birth counts, marked by negative slopes and significant p-values. Change points identified around one-tenth, halfway, and near the end of the simulation suggest shifts in the underlying dynamics of the birth rates. The presence of oscillations with a dominant period of 50 time steps indicates regular fluctuations in the birth counts. For decision-makers, these trends highlight the dynamic nature of prey population births under varying environmental and hunting pressures. The initial increase and subsequent decline in birth rates could inform strategies related to conservation efforts and the management of hunting pressures to sustain prey populations.
