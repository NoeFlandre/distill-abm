The cumulative mortality of prey animals initiates at zero and rises sharply throughout the first 400 to 600 time steps, with most simulation runs accumulating between 200 and 240 deaths. This rapid accumulation reflects the immediate impact of hunting pressure from 20 foragers combined with baseline natural mortality (internal rate 0.025, external rate 0.0025) on the initial population of 250 individuals.

Between 600 and 1000 time steps, the rate of mortality increase slows considerably across all replicates. The cumulative death count eventually plateaus at terminal values ranging from approximately 225 to 250, with trajectories stabilizing completely after 1200 to 1500 steps and showing no further accumulation through the remainder of the observation period.

The convergence of final mortality totals near the initial population size indicates severe depletion of the prey population. The cessation of additional deaths suggests that surviving prey persist at densities below the threshold where pursuit remains energetically profitable for foragers operating under optimal foraging constraints, effectively creating a spatial or numerical refuge that buffers the final individuals from predation pressure despite the presence of immortal hunters.

Substantial variation exists between simulation runs during the accumulation phase, particularly between 300 and 800 steps, where some trajectories exhibit delayed mortality pulses compared to others. One replicate shows markedly slower mortality accumulation, reaching only 200 deaths by step 1000 before eventually converging toward the typical final range. This variability likely stems from stochastic differences in hunter-prey encounter rates and the spatial distribution of prey relative to forager starting positions.

**Insights for decision makers:** The model consistently drives prey populations to near-extinction levels (survival of fewer than 25 individuals) within the first 1000 to 1500 time steps under the current configuration. This suggests that a forager population of 20 individuals represents unsustainable hunting pressure for prey populations of 250, given the specified grassland productivity (33% coverage), seasonality constraints (0.8), and prey life history parameters. To promote sustainable coexistence or slower decline rates, decision makers should consider reducing forager density, increasing prey fecundity by shortening the 730-tick interbirth interval, or modifying forager pursuit costs to reduce hunting efficiency at low prey densities. The emergence of small refuge populations indicates that complete extinction is avoidable under optimal foraging theory, but the resulting demographic bottleneck is severe enough to threaten long-term population viability.
