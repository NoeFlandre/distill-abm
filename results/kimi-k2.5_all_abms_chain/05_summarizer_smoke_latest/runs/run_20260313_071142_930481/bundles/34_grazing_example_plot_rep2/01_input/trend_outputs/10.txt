The population of E-RO households initiates at 20 across all simulation runs. During the initial 20 time steps, rapid divergence occurs as the count spreads across a broad spectrum, reaching maxima of approximately 40-42 households in several runs while declining to fewer than 5 in others. This early volatility indicates intense competition and diffusion dynamics among behavioral strategies, with some runs experiencing rapid proliferation of non-learning agents and others witnessing swift adoption of learning-based alternatives.

Between time steps 20 and 40, trajectories that peaked at high levels generally maintain elevated counts, fluctuating between 35 and 40, before undergoing declines. From step 40 to step 60, several high-count trajectories decrease substantially, with one falling from approximately 40 to the 23-25 range. Concurrently, low-count trajectories largely stabilize at values between 0 and 10.

During the final 40 time steps, most runs achieve relative stability, exhibiting minor fluctuations around equilibrium values. By step 100, the terminal count of E-RO households spans a wide range from 0 to approximately 26, with outcomes clustering around distinct levels: near-extinction (0-5 households), low prevalence (8-10), intermediate levels (13-18), and sustained majority (23-26).

This persistent heterogeneity across simulation runs suggests that the competition between E-RO and learning-enabled strategies is sensitive to initial conditions and stochastic events in the social learning and decision-making processes. The maintenance of substantial E-RO populations in some runs, despite the presence of learning alternatives, indicates that the non-learning strategy can remain viable under certain configurations of the private property grazing system. Conversely, the elimination or near-elimination of E-RO households in other runs demonstrates that learning capabilities can dominate when households effectively leverage learning-by-doing or social learning to improve their grazing decisions. The wide variance in outcomes reflects the complex feedback between household behavioral adaptation and pasture conditions in the adaptive co-management system.
