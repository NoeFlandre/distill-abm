During the initial 20 to 30 steps, it undergoes a pronounced decline, reaching minimum values ranging between 80,000 and 88,000 depending on the simulation trajectory. This initial reduction likely reflects the immediate impact of livestock grazing pressure on vegetation reserves before adaptive management strategies begin to influence pasture conditions.

Following this minimum, the biomass enters a phase of rapid recovery between approximately steps 30 and 60, with values increasing steeply toward levels between 110,000 and 130,000. The rate of increase varies across simulation runs, with some trajectories showing accelerated ascents beginning around step 40, while others demonstrate more gradual recovery starting closer to step 50, potentially reflecting differences in how quickly households adjust their risk parameters and grazing decisions through learning-by-doing and social learning processes.

After approximately step 60, the biomass stabilizes into distinct equilibrium states that persist through step 100. Final values at the end of the simulation span a range from roughly 111,000 to 128,000, indicating substantial heterogeneity in long-term pasture outcomes. The majority of trajectories settle at values exceeding the initial 90,000 units, suggesting that the interplay between vegetation regrowth dynamics and adaptive household behaviors generally leads to improved reserve biomass conditions relative to the starting state, despite the initial degradation period.

The variability across simulation runs is pronounced, particularly regarding the final equilibrium levels and the depth of the initial trough, reflecting the influence of stochastic rainfall patterns (mean 200, standard deviation 100) and heterogeneous agent decision-making. The system exhibits relatively tight clustering during the initial decline while constraints are similar across runs, but diverges significantly during the recovery phase as different combinations of learning behaviors and ecological feedbacks generate distinct stable states that persist throughout the latter portion of the simulation.
