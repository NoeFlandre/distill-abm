The number of female animals begins at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation runs. Over the initial 500 steps, the population undergoes a steep decline, with most trajectories falling to between 20 and 40 individuals, though some runs exhibit faster collapse reaching near-zero levels by step 300. Between steps 500 and 1000, the decline continues but at a reduced rate, with populations typically falling below 20 individuals as the trajectories converge toward extinction. By step 1500, the majority of simulations have reached zero female animals, while a minority of runs maintain small remnant populations of fewer than 5 individuals until approximately step 2000, after which all populations have effectively ceased to exist.

This consistent downward trajectory indicates that under the current parameter configuration, the mortality pressure from human hunting combined with natural mortality and environmental constraints overwhelms the species' reproductive capacity. The birth spacing interval of 730 steps (equivalent to two years) appears insufficient to offset the combined effects of the 20 active foragers and the internal mortality rate of 0.025. The variation in extinction timing across runs—ranging from approximately 300 to 2200 steps—suggests that stochastic factors such as localized hunter encounters, spatial distribution of grass resources, and demographic randomness influence the precise timing of population collapse, even while the ultimate outcome remains consistent.

For decision makers examining megafaunal conservation or management strategies, these results demonstrate that the current hunting pressure represents an unsustainable exploitation rate that inevitably drives the population to extinction. The lack of stable equilibrium states or recovery trajectories indicates that parameter adjustments are necessary to achieve coexistence. Potential interventions could include reducing hunter density, decreasing hunting efficiency through increased processing costs or reduced food values, or enhancing population resilience through reduced birth spacing intervals or increased maximum lifespans. The persistence of some populations for extended periods (beyond 2000 steps) suggests that under certain stochastic conditions, small refuge populations may temporarily persist, but these do not represent viable long-term survival strategies under current pressures.
