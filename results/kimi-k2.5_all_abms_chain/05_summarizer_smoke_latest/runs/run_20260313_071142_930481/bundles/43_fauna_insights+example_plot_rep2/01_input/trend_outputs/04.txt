The cumulative number of females killed begins at zero across all simulation runs and increases monotonically throughout the observation period. During the initial phase, approximately the first 400 to 600 time steps, the count rises steeply from 0 to between 80 and 120 individuals across most trajectories, indicating intensive hunting pressure on female prey during this interval. By around step 800 to 1000, the rate of increase diminishes substantially for the majority of runs, with the cumulative count approaching asymptotic values ranging from approximately 118 to 125.

Certain trajectories exhibit delayed dynamics, showing minimal accumulation during the first 300 to 400 steps (reaching fewer than 20 kills) and maintaining slower rates of increase until approximately step 600, eventually converging with other runs between steps 1500 and 1700 at comparable terminal values. By step 2000, nearly all simulations plateau at levels between 118 and 123 females killed, with only marginal incremental increases observed in isolated runs extending toward step 2200.

The consistent terminal plateau across diverse trajectories suggests that hunting pressure systematically depletes the available female population, effectively removing the entire cohort of approximately 120 individuals from the system. The initial rapid accumulation phase corresponds to periods of high hunter efficiency and prey accessibility when population densities remain high, while the subsequent saturation reflects demographic collapse or functional extinction of the female segment. For decision makers, these dynamics indicate that under current parameter configurations—specifically with female processing costs of 3 and food values of 27—hunting pressure invariably extirpates the reproductive portion of the megaherbivore population regardless of temporal variability in kill rates, driving the system toward extinction within 2000 time steps.
