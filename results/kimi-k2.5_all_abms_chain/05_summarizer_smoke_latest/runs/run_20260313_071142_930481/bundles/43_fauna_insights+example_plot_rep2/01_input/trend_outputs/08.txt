The number of male animals begins at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation runs. The population undergoes a deterministic decline toward extinction throughout the experimental period. During the initial phase from 0 to 500 steps, the number of males falls rapidly from roughly 125 to fewer than 20 individuals in most runs, with several trajectories reaching zero within this window. Between steps 500 and 1000, the remaining populations continue to decrease at a slower rate, with most runs approaching or reaching zero by step 1000. A subset of simulations maintains small residual populations of fewer than 5 individuals for an extended duration, with final extinction occurring at various points between steps 1000 and 2200.

The variability in extinction timing—ranging from as early as step 400 to as late as step 2200—suggests high sensitivity to stochastic events, such as the specific spatial distribution of hunter encounters, herd movement patterns, and demographic stochasticity within small populations. However, the consistent endpoint of zero males across all runs indicates that under the current parameter configuration, extinction is inevitable. This trajectory reflects the combined pressures of immortal foragers (providing constant predation mortality), high seasonality (0.8), limited grass availability (0.33 coverage), and the specific life history and energetic constraints programmed into the model.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
These results demonstrate that the current hunting pressure and environmental conditions create an unsustainable system where male megaherbivore populations face certain extinction, though the timeline varies probabilistically. For managers and conservation planners, this indicates that maintaining immortal hunter populations at this density (20 foragers) within constrained environments inevitably leads to local extinction of the male cohort. Interventions such as reducing hunter numbers, limiting foraging radius, decreasing seasonality severity, or increasing resource availability would be necessary to shift the system from deterministic extinction to a sustainable equilibrium or slower decline.
