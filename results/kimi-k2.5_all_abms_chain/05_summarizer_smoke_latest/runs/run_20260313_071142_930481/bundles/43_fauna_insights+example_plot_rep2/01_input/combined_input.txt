The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The spatial scale is unitless but can be conceptualized relative to daily movement distances of megaherbivores. The model integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Agents include human hunters and sexually dimorphic ungulate herds. Hunters operate under logic approximating dynamic optimal foraging theory, making pursuit decisions based on their current energy status, prey food value, prey density in the landscape, and the costs of pursuit, hunting, and processing. Hunters do not reproduce, expend energy when moving, can die from energy depletion, and can store energy surpluses. Ungulates move as herds with environmental feedback, age over time, reproduce with user-controlled birth spacing intervals, and exhibit increased mortality risk after reaching senescence. Nursing offspring add to the food value of females. The environment consists of grass patches that can be static or dynamic, with optional seasonality limiting regrowth to specific seasonal cycles.

The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. It seeks to determine whether these interactions accelerate extinction, buffer against extinction, or create equilibrium conditions that prevent extinction events. The model allows users to isolate and test specific hypotheses by manipulating variables related to fodder availability, seasonality, relative animal food value, animal fecundity, lifespans, relative hunting danger, and movement energy costs. Experimental protocols involve establishing baselines without hunting or environmental change, then iteratively adding variables such as environmental degradation, hunter population density, sexual dimorphism ( differential food values and processing costs), and life history constraints (interbirth intervals, lifespan) to observe their individual and combined effects on extinction probability, herd demography, herd movement patterns, and hunting selectivity.

The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the simulation period from initialization through approximately step 2200, exhibiting no deviation from this initialization value across any observed time steps or simulation repetitions. This static behavior reflects the parameter configuration where forager agents are immortal and do not reproduce, preventing both mortality from energy depletion and recruitment through population growth. Consequently, the hunting pressure remains temporally invariant and deterministic, providing a fixed predation intensity of 20 active hunters against the megaherbivore populations regardless of environmental conditions or prey availability.

For decision makers, this indicates that any observed extinction dynamics or demographic shifts in the prey populations arise solely from the interactions between environmental variables, prey life history traits, and the constant hunting pressure, rather than from density-dependent feedback in the hunter population. This configuration serves as a critical baseline for isolating the specific impacts of prey biology and environmental degradation on extinction risk, though it should be noted that real-world conservation scenarios typically involve dynamic human populations where hunting pressure varies with prey scarcity, technological change, or resource competition.

The total energy available to forager bands begins at approximately 2,000 energy units across simulation runs, exhibiting initial variation between 1,500 and 2,500. During the first 500 time steps, this energy declines rapidly to values between -4,000 and -6,000, with considerable divergence across individual simulation runs reflecting stochastic differences in early hunting success and spatial exploration patterns.  

After approximately step 800, the rate of energy decline stabilizes into a consistent linear trajectory. Between steps 800 and 2,000, the total energy decreases steadily at a rate of roughly 15 to 20 energy units per time step, with simulation runs converging tightly around a shared downward path. By step 2,000, total forager energy reaches approximately -32,000 to -35,000 units, with at least one simulation trajectory continuing to step 2,200, reaching nearly -38,000.  

The sustained negative trend indicates that forager energy expenditure consistently exceeds energy gains from hunting throughout the simulation duration. The convergence of trajectories after the initial phase suggests that despite early variability in foraging outcomes, the system settles into a predictable regime where the fixed forager population of 20 accumulates energy debt at a constant rate. The linear decline rate of approximately 20 energy units per step closely corresponds to the configured movement cost of 1 unit per forager multiplied by the initial population of 20 hunters, implying that successful hunts become rare enough in later stages that energy dynamics are dominated by basal movement expenditures.  

The consistent linear decline in forager energy suggests that under these parameter settings, hunting returns are insufficient to offset movement costs, leading to progressive energy depletion. This indicates that either prey availability is too low to support the hunter density, or that the combined effects of processing costs, sexual dimorphism in food values, and senescence-related mortality in prey have reduced hunting success to levels that cannot sustain energetic needs. For decision makers, this configuration implies that the current hunting pressure and prey population dynamics create unsustainable foraging conditions that would result in hunter starvation and population collapse if the immortal-foragers setting were disabled, potentially accelerating extinction dynamics through maintained predation pressure even as forager energy reserves deteriorate.

The cumulative mortality of male ungulates begins at zero and increases rapidly through discrete hunting events. During the initial 500 time steps, representing approximately the first 1.4 years of simulation time, kills accumulate steeply from 0 to values ranging between 80 and 115 individuals. This corresponds to an average extraction rate of roughly 0.16 to 0.23 males per step, reflecting intensive hunting pressure by the 20 forager agents targeting males with their higher food value of 30 and moderate processing cost of 6.

Between steps 500 and 1000 (years 1.4 to 2.7), the accumulation rate decelerates markedly as cumulative kills approach 100 to 120 individuals. This slowdown indicates declining encounter rates as the male population, initially comprising roughly 125 individuals assuming equal sex distribution within the starting cohort of 250 prey, becomes depleted. Hunters must expend greater search effort or wait longer between successful pursuits, reducing the per-step probability of male kills.

After approximately 1000 to 1500 steps, cumulative mortality stabilizes into plateaus across most simulation runs, with final values converging between 110 and 125 males killed. Some trajectories reach saturation as early as step 1000, while others continue accumulating sporadic kills until approximately step 2000. The final extraction totals approach the theoretical maximum initial male population size, indicating effective extirpation of the male cohort within 3 to 5.5 years.

The substantial variation in saturation timing—spanning roughly 1000 steps between the earliest and latest plateaus—suggests that stochastic elements in herd movement patterns, grass distribution (set to 33% coverage with seasonality of 0.8), and forager search behavior introduce significant uncertainty in the extinction trajectory. However, the deterministic convergence toward the maximum possible extraction indicates that male population collapse is inevitable under the specified parameter configuration.

Implications for decision makers: The data demonstrates that male megaherbivore populations face extremely rapid collapse under hunting pressure, with effective extinction occurring within 2–5 years. Given the sexual dimorphism in food value (males valued at 30 versus females at 27) and the higher processing cost for males (6 versus 3 for females), hunters exhibit clear preference for males, driving their population to exhaustion while the broader herd may persist. This creates a functional extinction scenario where breeding capacity is eliminated even if female survivors remain. Management strategies must implement sex-specific protections for males or reduce forager density below the critical threshold of 20 agents to prevent the reproductive collapse that precedes total population extinction.
