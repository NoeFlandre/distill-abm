The mean reserve biomass initiates at approximately 90,000 units and declines during the first 20 steps, reaching minimum values between 80,000 and 88,000. This initial depletion likely corresponds to the immediate utilization of reserve pastures by livestock as households establish baseline grazing strategies, overwhelming the initial vegetation regeneration capacity.

Following this trough, the biomass undergoes a rapid recovery phase between steps 20 and 60, ascending steeply to values ranging from 110,000 to 125,000. This upward trajectory suggests that learning processes—whether through learning-by-doing or social learning—enable households to adapt their livestock placement decisions, and resting mechanisms allow vegetation to recover from initial grazing pressure.

After step 60, the rate of increase diminishes substantially as the system approaches equilibrium. By step 100, the biomass stabilizes at values between approximately 111,000 and 128,000, with individual simulation runs maintaining distinct plateau levels. This persistent divergence across runs indicates that while the system consistently recovers from initial degradation, stochastic elements such as rainfall variability and heterogeneous risk attitudes among households generate substantially different long-term ecological outcomes.

The variance across simulations remains constrained during the initial decline but expands considerably during the recovery and stabilization phases. This pattern suggests that short-term pasture degradation is highly deterministic given initial stocking densities, whereas long-term resilience depends critically on the specific trajectories of adaptive co-management and climatic stochasticity encountered during the recovery period.

Decision makers should anticipate an inevitable initial decline in reserve biomass lasting approximately 20 steps before adaptive management practices yield measurable improvements. The wide range in final biomass equilibria suggests that policy interventions should prioritize supporting effective learning mechanisms and uncertainty management strategies, as these factors determine whether the system achieves higher or lower long-term pasture productivity.
