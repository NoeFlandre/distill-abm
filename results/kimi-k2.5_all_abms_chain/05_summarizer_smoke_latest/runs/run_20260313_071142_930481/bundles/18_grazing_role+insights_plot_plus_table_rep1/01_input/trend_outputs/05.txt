The agent-based simulations commence with all households maintaining 90 animals, representing initial stocking levels on private pastures. During the first 37 steps, the system undergoes a precipitous decline characterized by sequentially slowing rates of destocking. The most severe reduction occurs within the first 20 steps, where change-point analysis detects the first major transition, with initial slope coefficients reaching -3.47 per step in the earliest decade and moderating to -0.368 by steps 22-32. This degradation phase culminates at step 37, when livestock levels reach a minimum of approximately 41 animals.

Following this nadir, the system enters a brief stagnation period between steps 33 and 43 where no statistically significant trend is detected. A recovery phase initiates around step 44, marked by a second inflection point at step 46 and confirmed by statistically significant increasing trends in rolling Mann-Kendall tests. The recovery progresses through three distinct phases delineated by change points at steps 55 and 100: an initial rapid increase through step 65 (slope = 0.313), followed by moderating growth through step 87 (slope declining to 0.005), and concluding with very gradual accumulation (slope = 0.002) during the final decades. By step 100, the aggregate mean stabilizes at approximately 47.5 animals, representing a persistent 47% reduction from initial conditions despite six decades of recovery effort.

Substantial heterogeneity characterizes long-term outcomes across the 30 simulation series. While the aggregated mean approaches 47.5 animals, individual trajectories diverge significantly following the minimum at step 37. The majority of populations stabilize between 40 and 50 animals, yet a distinct subset achieves equilibria of 58 to 62 animals, indicating that specific learning strategies or risk dispositions enable maintenance of herds 30-50% larger than alternative approaches under identical ecological conditions.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The initial 20-step window represents a critical period of acute destocking where intervention to support household resilience may be most urgent. The prolonged recovery duration—approximately 60 steps from minimum to stabilization—demonstrates that rebuilding livestock assets after shocks constitutes a multi-decadal process, suggesting that short-term restocking programs may prove insufficient. The persistent gap between initial (90) and final (47.5) stocking levels implies that either initial configurations exceeded sustainable carrying capacity or that adaptive learning converges on risk-adjusted, conservative stocking rates that prioritize pasture health over maximization.

Most significantly, the divergence in outcomes between households maintaining 40-45 versus 60+ animals demonstrates that learning strategy selection profoundly affects long-term livelihood security. Extension services and policy mechanisms that facilitate effective experiential learning or social information-sharing may substantially enhance pastoral system resilience, enabling households to maintain viable herd sizes despite significant rainfall variability and ecological uncertainty.
