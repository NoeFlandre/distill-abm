This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes. Individual households or simulation runs display heterogeneous initial risk attitudes distributed across a range from approximately -0.11 to +0.18. This indicates that heterogeneous risk tolerances constitute a fixed structural constraint in grazing system management. The presence of both negative (risk-averse) and positive (risk seeking) values in the population, centered near zero, indicates that uniform recommendations will face divergent responses. An initial reduction reflects the immediate impact of livestock grazing as households deploy their initial herds across private pasture patches, outpacing vegetation regrowth capacity. The consistency across simulation runs during this phase indicates predictable transient transient variations in household decision-making. The final stabilization above initial conditions indicates that adaptive co-management strategies can outperform static approaches. The mean destock of households initiates at 0.0, indicating no forced livestock removals at the simulation outset. Within the first three steps, the metric escalates sharply to a maximum value of 6.61. This spike reflects the immediate ecological feedback when initial herd sizes encounter limited pasture biomass, requiring rapid liquidation of approximately 7.3% of initial holdings per household. The finding that inequality accelerates over time rather than stabilizing suggests that without external intervention or institutional mechanisms to redistribute resources, the gap between resource-rich and resource-poor households will continue to widen. Policy approaches should consider whether the observed stratification represents efficient specialization or problematic marginalization. Stakeholders should anticipate an initial 25-step period of high destocking activity as households adjust to private property boundaries and initial climatic shocks. Intervention policies implemented during this window may have amplified effects on long-term herd trajectories. The sustained linear accumulation throughout the simulation indicates that continuous destocking remains necessary even after learning equilibrium is achieved.
