Model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making. The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. The baseline disposition toward deviating from recommended livestock levels is fixed for social learning agents. The mean household risk attitude begins at a minimal level of approximately 0.007 during the initial time steps. This metric undergoes a rapid and statistically significant increase throughout the first 33 time steps, reaching a maximum value of 0.3814 around time step 33. Available green biomass across grazing patches begins at approximately 45,000 units. Exhibiting a brief fluctuation with a minor peak around the fourth time step at roughly 45,261 units before initiating a pronounced decline. Biomass enters a sustained recovery phase characterized by increasing trends beginning around the thirty-third time step. Initial stocking rates of 90 units per household significantly exceed sustainable limits under the specified climatic variability. The incomplete recovery within the 100-step timeframe suggests that conservative initial stocksing rates around 45–50 units would prevent costly boom-and-bust cycles. Inequality is structurally embedded in the heterogeneity of risk attitudes and learning processes. The number of households engaged in social learning initiates at 0 and exhibits a monotonic increase over the 100-step simulation, converging toward approximately 32 households by the conclusion. Continuous destocking is a core adaptive strategy in this system rather than an emergency measure. Cyclical patterns suggest that destockers rhythmically track environmental or economic cycles, likely corresponding to inter-annual rainfall variability driving periodic forage scarcity.
