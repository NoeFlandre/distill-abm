The simulations reveal a consistent four-phase trajectory in reserve biomass over the 100-year period, though with substantial variation in the magnitude and timing of recovery across runs.

Initially, mean reserve biomass begins at approximately 90,000 units, with values peaking slightly at approximately 90,500 within the first 2 to 5 time steps. During the subsequent period from approximately time step 5 to 30, the system experiences a persistent decline, with biomass decreasing to minimum values ranging between 80,000 and 88,000 units. This reduction represents the initial grazing pressure exerted by the starting herd of 90 animals per household across the landscape.

Between approximately time step 20 and 45, the system enters a variable phase during which biomass conditions diverge across simulations. Some configurations maintain levels near the minimum of approximately 80,000 to 85,000 units through time step 40, while others exhibit early recovery, reaching values between 90,000 and 95,000 units by time step 40.

Around time step 40 to 50, a rapid recovery phase commences across all simulations, characterized by steep increases in biomass lasting until approximately time step 60 to 70. During this period, values rise from trough levels to between approximately 110,000 and 125,000 units, with different simulation runs exhibiting markedly different rates of increase.

After time step 70, the system stabilizes into distinct equilibrium states that persist through the remainder of the simulation. Final biomass values at time step 100 vary considerably across runs, ranging from approximately 111,000 to 128,000 units. This variation in terminal values suggests that the different learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—produce divergent long-term pasture conditions when interacting with the substantial rainfall variability (coefficient of variation of 50%).

The inflection point around time step 40 to 50 likely corresponds to the period when farmers' adaptive learning strategies, operating within their assigned behavioral types and initial risk dispositions of 0.5, successfully adjust stocking rates in response to observed pasture degradation, thereby allowing vegetation recovery in the reserve biomass component despite the absence of formal resting protocols.
