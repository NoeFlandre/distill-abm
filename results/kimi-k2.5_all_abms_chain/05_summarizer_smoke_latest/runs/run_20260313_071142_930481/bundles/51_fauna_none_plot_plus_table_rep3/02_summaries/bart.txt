The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The population of human hunter-gatherer bands remained fixed at exactly 20 agents throughout the observed simulation period spanning 2,312 time steps. The metric exhibited zero variance, maintaining the initial value of 20 from the simulation start at step 0 through the final recorded step. The cumulative number of male prey killed exhibits a characteristic saturation curve across the simulation ensemble. The cumulative mortality of female prey initiates at zero and follows a markedly sigmoidal trajectory over the course of the simulations, ultimately plateauing at approximately 123 individuals. The trajectory indicates that forager hunting pressure rapidly eliminates the female prey cohort until the population is functionally exhausted, with the terminal plateau representing the effective extinction or severe depletion of females. The number of male prey animals begins at 125 individuals and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction, reaching zero by step 2284. The population exhibits fluctuations with a dominant periodicity of 63 steps, manifesting as minor resurgences including local maxima around steps 709–730 (8–730 individuals) and step 803 (approximately 7 individuals) The median abundance of 1.35 individuals is lower than the mean of 21.03. The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model shows the interaction between human hunting pressure exerted by 20 immortal foragers and ungulate life history parameters. The population dynamics initiate at approximately 250 individuals and follow a monotonically decreasing trend toward zero. During this period, the aggregated mean population across simulations drops precipitously from the initial 250 individuals to approximately 50 or fewer. Total energy of male animals begins at approximately 12,500 units and follows a predominantly decreasing trajectory over the course of the simulation, ultimately reaching zero at step 2,284. The extinction outcome demonstrates that the interaction between human hunting efficiency, prey life history characteristics, and environmental constraints creates a non-equilibrium system where prey populations cannot sustain themselves. The depletion trajectory was characterized by distinct phases of decay. The most rapid decline occurred during the initial periods, particularly between steps 232 and 463, where the rate of decrease reached its maximum intensity with a slope of -36.027 units per step. During this transitional phase, local maxima appeared around steps 385 through 440, where total energy temporarily stabilized.
