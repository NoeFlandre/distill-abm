This divergence demonstrates how stochastic environmental conditions interact with the three distinct learning strategies—baseline rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—to produce heterogeneous long-term outcomes. The low variance observed during the initial 40 steps indicates consistent initial conditions and uniform stocking rates across all replicates, while the increasing divergence after step 70 reveals that the combination of risk dispositions (initially set at 0.5) and adaptive responses leads to divergent landscape-level productivity outcomes over time.

The initial decline suggests that static management approaches without active learning lead to pasture degradation, while the subsequent recovery demonstrates that adaptive decision-making can restore vegetation productivity. However, the persistent variance in final biomass levels across simulation runs indicates that environmental uncertainty significantly affects long-term sustainability outcomes. Decision makers should note that early intervention around step 40, when trajectories begin to diverge, may be critical to ensure recovery toward higher biomass equilibria rather than lower productive states, and that the choice among learning strategies substantially influences long-term land health under variable climatic conditions.
