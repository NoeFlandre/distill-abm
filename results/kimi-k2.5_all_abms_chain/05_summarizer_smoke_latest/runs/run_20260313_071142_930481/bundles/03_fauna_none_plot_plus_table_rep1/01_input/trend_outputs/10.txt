The simulation initiates with a mean prey population of approximately 167 individuals. Over the course of 2,311 time steps, the population undergoes a continuous decline to extinction, reaching zero individuals by the terminal step.

The trajectory is characterized by a steep initial decrease, with the most rapid losses occurring within the first 125 steps. The rate of decline decelerates at successive change points located near steps 475, 625, and 875, transitioning from rapid collapse to slower attrition. Superimposed on this downward trend are minor oscillations exhibiting a dominant period of approximately 63 steps.

While the mean population across all simulations decreases steadily, substantial heterogeneity exists in persistence times among individual runs. The median population size across the temporal series falls to approximately 1.5 individuals, indicating that for the majority of the simulation duration, populations consist of very few animals. Some populations collapse within the first 500 steps, whereas others persist for over 2,000 steps before eventual extinction. In the final phase (after approximately step 1,400), when remnant populations are extremely small, brief intermittent periods of slight population increase occur between steps 1,392–1,623 and 1,856–2,087, though these minor recoveries do not prevent ultimate extinction.

Within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, this pattern reflects the impact of continuous hunting pressure from 20 immortal foragers operating under optimal foraging constraints. The initial rapid decline corresponds to efficient predation on abundant prey, while the progressive deceleration and extended persistence of some populations suggest density-dependent effects wherein scarce prey become increasingly difficult to encounter. The 63-step oscillatory component likely reflects seasonal environmental cycles or demographic feedback loops interacting with hunting pressure. The variability in extinction timing across simulations illustrates the stochastic nature of herd movement, reproduction, and hunter-prey encounters, while the deterministic endpoint of zero population indicates that under these parameter settings (including the specified birth spacing of 730 ticks, processing costs, and grass regeneration dynamics), hunting pressure overwhelms the megaherbivore population's capacity for demographic recovery.
