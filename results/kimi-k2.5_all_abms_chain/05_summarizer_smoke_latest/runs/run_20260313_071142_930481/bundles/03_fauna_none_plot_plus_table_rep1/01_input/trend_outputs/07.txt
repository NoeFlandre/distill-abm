The cumulative mortality trajectories exhibit a characteristic saturation pattern, beginning at zero deaths and rising steeply during initial steps before asymptotically approaching maximum values consistent with prey depletion. Across the 40 simulation replicates, the aggregate mean starts at 0 and terminates at 235 deaths, with individual runs converging on values between approximately 225 and 250 deaths—aligning with the initial prey population of 250 agents.

The most rapid accumulation occurs during the early phase, with a major inflection point at step 362 marking the transition from accelerating to decelerating mortality rates. Through approximately step 1159, the death count increases monotonically and significantly, with trend analysis confirming statistically significant positive slopes ranging from 0.17 to 0.44 deaths per step across consecutive rolling windows. During this period, some trajectories accumulate over 200 deaths, while others lag considerably, with the slowest runs reaching only 150–175 deaths by this stage.

Following the initial surge, the rate of mortality accumulation diminishes substantially. Between steps 1160 and 1391, the aggregate series exhibits no statistically significant trend, indicating a plateau phase where deaths accumulate sporadically. A secondary period of slight increase occurs around steps 1392 to 1623, though at a greatly reduced rate (0.003 deaths per step) compared to the initial phase.

Individual simulation runs display considerable heterogeneity in the timing of mortality events. While certain trajectories reach 230 deaths by step 500, others exhibit gradual, stepwise accumulation extending beyond step 2000. Despite these temporal differences, most runs stabilize between 230 and 245 deaths, with the aggregate mean reaching its maximum value of 240.33 at step 1876 before settling to 235 at termination. After step 1856, the aggregate series shows no significant increasing trend, suggesting effective cessation of mortality as populations become depleted.

This pattern reflects the dynamics of a closed prey population subject to continuous hunting pressure from immortal foragers. The inevitable saturation near 250 deaths represents the exhaustion of the initial prey cohort, while the variation in accumulation rates across runs illustrates stochastic differences in the efficiency of hunter-prey encounters and demographic stochasticity within the herd structures.
