The cumulative count of postpartum females killed initiates at zero across all simulation realizations and undergoes a steep, rapid ascent during the initial 500 to 800 time steps, with most trajectories accumulating between 80 and 110 kills by this time. This early surge reflects intensive hunting pressure on reproductive females, who represent high-value targets due to the additional food value provided by their nursing offspring, consistent with the dynamic optimal foraging logic implemented by agent hunters.

Subsequent to this initial period, accumulation rates decelerate markedly as individual runs approach stable maximum values. The cessation of killing activity occurs at variable time points: approximately half of the simulations plateau between steps 600 and 1,000 at levels ranging from 100 to 120 kills, while others demonstrate extended accumulation phases continuing through step 1,500 to 2,000, ultimately reaching comparable terminal values between 110 and 125. This temporal heterogeneity indicates stochastic variation in spatial encounter rates between hunter agents and reproductive females across the wrapped landscape matrix, influenced by random initial positioning and movement patterns.

One atypical trajectory exhibits a pronounced lag phase, maintaining cumulative kills below 10 until approximately step 150, followed by a compensatory rapid increase that converges with the main cluster of runs by step 400. This pattern suggests initial spatial segregation between forager bands and breeding herds in that particular realization, delaying the onset of intensive hunting on the reproductive cohort.

Terminal cumulative values converge within a narrow band of approximately 105 to 125 kills across all runs, implying a consistent demographic ceiling for postpartum female mortality under the specified initial prey population of 250 and birth spacing interval of 730 steps (equivalent to two years). The plateauing dynamics indicate that hunting pressure effectively exhausts the available reproductive female cohort faster than replacement through natality can sustain. As the density of postpartum females declines due to this front-loaded mortality, encounter rates drop sufficiently to stabilize the cumulative kill count, despite continued forager activity and energy storage capabilities. The consistency of final values across runs suggests that the interaction between the initial prey population, reproductive life history constraints, and forager efficiency establishes a predictable upper limit on total reproductive female mortality during the simulated time horizon.
