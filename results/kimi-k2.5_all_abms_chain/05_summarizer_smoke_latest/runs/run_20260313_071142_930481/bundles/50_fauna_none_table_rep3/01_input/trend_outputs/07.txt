The cumulative mortality of prey populations exhibits a complex, non-monotonic trajectory characterized by four distinct phases of accumulation interspersed with periodic oscillations. Initially, the system manifests zero mortality at commencement, followed by rapid accumulation of deaths through approximately the first 625 time steps, with pronounced structural shifts at intervals of 125, 250, 375, 500, and 625 ticks indicating escalating mortality pressure. This early phase reflects intensive predation as the initial cohort of twenty hunters encounters the starting prey population, resulting in steep increases in cumulative fatalities. 

Between ticks 625 and 1159, the rate of mortality accumulation decelerates markedly, transitioning from aggressive linear increases to incremental gains approaching zero slope. This deceleration coincides with an inflection point near tick 362, suggesting the onset of density-dependent feedback mechanisms or prey behavioral adaptations that moderate per-capita hunting success. Subsequently, the system enters intermittent plateau phases between ticks 1160–1391 and 1624–1855, during which cumulative mortality stabilizes temporarily, indicating quasi-equilibrium conditions where recruitment and natural mortality balance predation losses.

The trajectory reaches its apex at approximately 240 cumulative deaths near tick 1876, representing the maximum ensemble mortality across the forty simulation series. Following this peak, the metric exhibits an anomalous decline to approximately 235 deaths by tick 2312, with a significant decreasing trend emerging after tick 1856. This terminal-phase reduction in mean cumulative mortality likely reflects extinction events within individual simulation runs, wherein depleted prey populations cease contributing new fatalities, thereby altering the statistical composition of the active ensemble as runs conclude.

Superimposed upon these secular trends are regular oscillations with a dominant period of 63 time steps, particularly prominent after tick 2170. These high-frequency fluctuations correspond to seasonal environmental cycles, consistent with the model's seasonality parameters, wherein periodic constraints on grass regrowth and prey vulnerability generate pulsed mortality patterns. Multiple inflection points distributed throughout the series— notably at ticks 1241, 1330, and 1828—demarcate transitions between exploitation phases, indicating shifting balances between hunter energetic demands, prey demographic recovery, and environmental carrying capacity. The overall pattern suggests an initial overexploitation phase yielding to transient demographic buffering, ultimately culminating in population exhaustion across the simulation ensemble.
