 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model . It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes .  The mean household risk attitude initializes at approximately 0.5 across all simulation runs . During the first 20 steps, the metric undergoes rapid divergence, with trajectories spreading across the range of 0.0 to 0.8 . For decision makers, this suggests that risk interventions must account for persistent heterogeneity in baseline risk dispositions .  The metric begins at 90 units at step 0, representing the initial livestock endowment across households . A rapid decline occurs over the first 20 steps, with values falling sharply to approximately 45 units . From step 40 to step 60, values rise from their minimum to range between 42 and 56 units . Some runs achieve resilient herd sizes through successful adaptation, while others remain trapped in low-livestock states .  The average cumulative number of destocked livestock begins at zero across all simulations and increases rapidly during the initial 20 time steps . During the first 20 steps, the count undergoes a rapid increase, rising to values ranging between approximately 12 and 48 households . This indicates that ultimately between 30% and 90% of the 60 households engage in social learning behaviors, suggesting the system settles into multiple distinct equilibria regarding the prevalence of adaptive co-management practices .  The count of non-learning households begins at 20 across all simulation runs . The high variability in destocking outcomes underscores the inherent unpredictability in social-ecological grazing systems . The existence of divergent trajectory groups suggests that policies should be designed to accommodate diverse household responses and foster adaptive capacities .
