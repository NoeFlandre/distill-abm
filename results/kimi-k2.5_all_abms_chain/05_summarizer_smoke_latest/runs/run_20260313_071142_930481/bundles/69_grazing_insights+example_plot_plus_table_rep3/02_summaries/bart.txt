This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes. The mean reserve biomass follows a distinct U-shaped trajectory characterized by an initial depletion phase, a critical minimum, and a sustained recovery culminating in high-level equilibrium. By step 100, biomass approaches an equilibrium value near 121,073 units, representing a 34% net increase from initial conditions. The mean green biomass begins at 45,000 units, exhibiting minimal variation during the initial 9 steps with a minor peak of 45,261 units at step 4. Between steps 11 and 27, the biomass declines significantly, indicating early vegetation depletion from initial grazing pressures. By step 100, average livestock levels stabilize across a wide range of outcomes, with some households or behavioral groups maintaining approximately 40–45 animals. The acceleration of inequality after time step 50 suggests that learning effects and environmental feedbacks become more pronounced as households accumulate experience and as pasture conditions diverge across the landscape. Strengthening social learning mechanisms or facilitating knowledge transfer between households may help moderate inequality growth, though the current model suggests learning alone is insufficient to prevent concentration. The count of households adhering to the E-RO behavioral strategy, representing non-learning agents, declined to approximately 14.7 households, representing a net reduction of roughly 26.5% from the initial population. The attrition rate is most pronounced during the first 20 steps, marked by a change point at step 20, followed by a continued decline until a continued but moderating decline.
