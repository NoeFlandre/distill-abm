**Trend Analysis of Mean Green Biomass Dynamics**

The aggregate vegetation biomass across all pasture patches begins at approximately 45,000 units. During the initial phase from year 0 to year 4, biomass remains relatively stable with a minor increase to approximately 45,260 units. Following this brief plateau, a significant declining trend emerges and continues until year 27, when biomass reaches its minimum value of approximately 42,057 units. This represents a degradation of roughly 6.6% from the initial state.

From year 27 onward, the trajectory reverses decisively into a sustained recovery and growth phase. Between years 33 and 54, biomass increases significantly with steep positive slopes, rising from the minimum through approximately 55,000 units. This rapid recovery continues through year 100, though the rate of increase progressively diminishes over time—from slopes exceeding 950 units per year in the mid-phase (years 44-54) to gradual increments of roughly 3 units per year in the final decade (years 88-98). By the conclusion of the simulation at year 100, biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units, representing a 34% increase above initial levels and a 44% increase from the year 27 nadir.

The oscillation patterns indicate cyclic dynamics with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, with notable oscillatory activity concentrated between years 10-20 and years 41-49. This suggests that the system experiences transitional fluctuations as households adjust their stocking decisions in response to observed pasture conditions.

**Contextual Interpretation**

The initial decline from years 0-27 reflects a period of pasture degradation occurring as households, starting with 90 livestock each and applying diverse learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning), initially overstock their private parcels relative to carrying capacity. The minimum at year 27 marks a critical threshold where vegetation stress peaks across the landscape.

The subsequent recovery demonstrates that the three learning mechanisms eventually enable farmers to reduce grazing pressure sufficiently to allow vegetation regeneration. The progressive slowing of growth rates after year 54 suggests the system is approaching a new equilibrium state, with biomass stabilizing at levels substantially higher than the initial condition. The absence of livestock reproduction (b=0) means these dynamics reflect pure stocking rate adjustments rather than herd growth pressures.

The divergence in individual simulation runs during the recovery phase (years 27-50) followed by convergence toward higher values indicates that while different learning strategies may follow distinct adjustment pathways initially, they ultimately achieve improved pasture conditions compared to the baseline.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The 27-year degradation period before recovery suggests that initial stocking rates of 90 animals per household exceed sustainable carrying capacity under the variable rainfall conditions (mean 200, standard deviation 100), but adaptive learning enables farmers to eventually correct these imbalances. Policymakers should anticipate that transitions to sustainable grazing management may involve significant time lags and initial resource degradation before improvement manifests. The eventual biomass levels exceeding initial conditions indicate that learning-based adaptation can create win-win outcomes where both vegetation health and potentially livestock productivity improve over the long term, though patience is required during the adjustment period. The high inter-run variability during the recovery phase suggests that the specific learning strategy employed matters less than the presence of some form of adaptive capacity, as all approaches eventually converge toward improved outcomes.
