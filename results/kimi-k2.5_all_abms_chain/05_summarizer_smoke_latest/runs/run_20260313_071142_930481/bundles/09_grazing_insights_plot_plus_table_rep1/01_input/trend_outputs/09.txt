**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The persistent accumulation of destocked livestock across all simulation runs indicates that households face continuous pressure to reduce herd sizes, regardless of their learning capabilities or decision-making strategies. This suggests that external factors such as climatic variability, pasture productivity, or initial stocking densities may exert stronger influence on destocking outcomes than behavioral adaptations.

The decelerating rate of destocking accumulation offers some reassurance that initial crises stabilize over time, but the continued linear growth throughout the simulation indicates that destocking remains a chronic feature of system dynamics rather than a transient shock.

For policy interventions, these patterns suggest that supporting households during the critical first 25 steps—when destocking rates are highest—may be most effective for preserving livelihoods. Additionally, the comparable performance across behavioral types implies that promoting specific learning strategies may have limited impact relative to addressing underlying ecological constraints or providing direct resource support during high-pressure periods.
