 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model . It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes .  RAGE extension suggests that, on average, households begin simulations with a neutral disposition toward risk . Individual agents maintain heterogeneous baseline attitudes that do not converge or diverge over time . Policy interventions targeting risk-taking behaviors must account for this baseline heterogeneity .  The simulation of the heterogeneous population with E-RO, E-LBD, and  behavioral types reveals a characteristic pattern of initial decline followed by sustained recovery and growth in reserve biomass over the 100-step period . The results suggest that encouraging learning-based behavioral adaptation among smallholder livestock keepers can lead to improved pasture conditions over time .  The trajectory reveals a critical vulnerability window between steps 20 and 40 when biomass falls below 43,000 units . The oscillatory patterns detected around steps 10-20 and 41-49 suggest cyclic adjustments between vegetation regeneration and grazing pressure as households optimize livestock placement decisions . The combination of learning-enabled behaviors and private property arrangements facilitates sustainable pasture management without external intervention .  The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock begins at effectively zero, indicating perfect equality in livestock distribution across the 60 stationary households at initial point . The final phase (steps 60–100) demonstrates accelerated inequality growth, with the aggregate metric more than doubling from approximately 0.035 to 0.073 . Policymakers should consider supplemental equity-focused interventions, particularly monitoring for divergence after step 60 when inequality accelerates most rapidly .  The average cumulative number of destocked livestock exhibits a monotonic increase from 0 at initialization to approximately 97 by step 100 . The accumulation follows a decelerating trajectory, characterized by rapid initial growth that progressively slows over time . The diffusion process demonstrates strong path dependency, with individual runs diverging significantly from the mean and from each other .  The findings suggest blanket adoption of learning-based interventions may be unnecessary or ineffective for all households . Instead, targeted support recognizing the coexistence of multiple stable strategies may better serve diverse farming communities . The stabilization after approximately 75 time steps suggests that strategy adoption reaches equilibrium within the simulation timeframe .
