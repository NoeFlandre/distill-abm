The simulation reveals a consistent long-term trend toward increasing inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock among the 60 households, beginning from a state of near-perfect equality and reaching a Gini coefficient of approximately 0.073 by year 100. This trajectory indicates that despite identical starting conditions—with each household initially holding 90 animals and equivalent risk dispositions—the combination of distinct learning strategies and environmental variability drives households toward divergent outcomes over the century-long period.

The accumulation of inequality proceeds through distinct phases marked by changing rates of divergence. During the initial 50 years, inequality grows gradually from effectively zero, with the pace of divergence characterized by multiple inflection points occurring at years 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, and 37. These adjustments suggest periods where the rate of differentiation between households temporarily shifts as distinct learning approaches—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning with single-loop adaptation—begin processing feedback from the variable rainfall conditions (mean 200 units, standard deviation 100) differently.

A transition occurs at year 51, marking the first significant change point where the dynamics of inequality accumulation shift. Following this transition, the rate of divergence accelerates noticeably through year 71, where a second change point indicates another modulation in the system's behavior. Throughout this latter period (years 51–100), inequality increases more steadily compared to the earlier phase, though the magnitude remains relatively modest with the Gini coefficient averaging approximately 0.030 across the full simulation.

Superimposed on this long-term increasing trend is a cyclical pattern with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, becoming particularly evident in the final decades of the simulation between years 78 and 93. This oscillatory behavior suggests that divergence in livestock holdings is not strictly monotonic but involves periodic partial convergences or slowdowns in differentiation as households' adaptive strategies interact with the stochastic rainfall regime.

By the conclusion of the simulation at year 100, the distribution of healthy livestock remains relatively equitable despite the statistically significant upward trend, with the final inequality level of 0.073 indicating that no single learning strategy achieves complete dominance or drives widespread household failure under the modeled conditions of private pasture management and restricted social learning.
