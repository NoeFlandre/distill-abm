The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The number of active foragers remained constant at 20 agents throughout the entire simulation duration of 2,312 steps. With both mortality and recruitment eliminated, the original cohort of 20 foragers persisted indefinitely, ensuring that hunting pressure on prey populations remained fixed regardless of environmental conditions, prey density, hunting success, or elapsed time. The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model suggests that male prey populations experience heavy initial exploitation, followed by a prolonged period of sustained but diminishing hunting success. The data indicates that hunting pressure on males is front-loaded in the interaction timeline, with the majority of male mortality occurring within the first 500 steps. Decisions makers should recognize that the consistent terminal plateau across diverse simulation trajectories indicates inevitable reproductive collapse of megaherbivore populations under current hunting parameters. The critical intervention window occurs within the first 500 steps, before cumulative kills exceed 100, as subsequent deceleration phases represent irreversible demographic damage. The male prey population consistently exhibited an extinction trajectory across all 40 simulation replicates. Under conditions of constant predation and environmental seasonality, megaherbivore populations lack the demographic capacity (given the 2-year interbirth interval) to withstand sustained hunting pressure, underscoring the vulnerability of slow-reproducing megafauna to persistent predation. Within the first 125 time steps, the population experiences its most rapid decline, falling sharply as indicated by strongly negative trend slopes exceeding -0.25 individuals per step. From step 500 to roughly step 875, the rate of decline attenuates significantly, transitioning from steep linear loss to gradual attrition. The total energetic reserves of female ungulate populations initiate at 12,500 units and follow a deterministic downward trajectory toward complete depletion. By tick 2,284, all simulation runs converged on zero male energy, though individual runs reached depletion at varying times. Management interventions would need to reduce hunter density or increase processing costs/energy expenditure for hunters to shift the system away from rapid depletion regime observed in the initial 500 ticks. The total prey energy exhibits a deterministic collapse from an initial high of approximately 16,667 units toward complete extinction at 0 units by step 2,311. A precipitous decline dominates the first 350-500 time steps, during which aggregate energy falls by over 75% to roughly 4,000 units.
