The cumulative mortality of female prey due to hunting exhibits a saturating growth pattern characterized by three distinct phases. Initially, from the start of the simulation through approximately tick 500, the cumulative count increases rapidly with slopes averaging 0.21 to 0.23 per tick, reaching values between 60 and 80 individuals. This rapid accumulation phase shows significant inflection activity between ticks 245 and 285, indicating dynamic adjustments in hunting pressure or prey availability during early establishment.

During the intermediate phase spanning roughly ticks 500 to 1500, the rate of increase decelerates substantially. Rolling trend analyses reveal slopes declining from 0.059 to 0.006, with change points occurring at ticks 850 and 1600 marking transitions in accumulation dynamics. By tick 1200, the cumulative count approaches 100 individuals, and the growth trajectory begins flattening as the system approaches saturation.

The late phase, from approximately tick 1500 through the conclusion at tick 2312, demonstrates marked stabilization. The cumulative count reaches a maximum mean value of 123.7 around tick 1826, then maintains a plateau with minimal variation (standard deviation ~31 across all observations). A slight decreasing tendency appears in the final window (ticks 1856-2087), though the terminal value remains at 123, indicating near-complete cessation of new female kills.

The distribution of terminal outcomes shows left skew: the median final count (119.1) exceeds the mean (104.6), indicating that while most simulation runs converge toward 120-124 cumulative kills, a subset terminates with substantially lower counts (minimum observed plateau around 108). This variability suggests stochastic elements in herd demographics or hunter foraging success create divergent extinction or equilibrium trajectories.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The front-loaded nature of hunting mortality indicates that conservation or management interventions must be implemented early in the interaction period, as the majority of female harvest occurs within the first 500-1000 ticks (approximately 1.4 to 2.7 years). The rapid approach to saturation suggests that hunting pressure quickly reaches an asymptote, potentially reflecting prey population collapse or hunter behavioral adaptation. The variability in terminal outcomes implies that identical initial conditions can produce divergent results, necessitating robust monitoring protocols rather than deterministic predictions. The plateau at approximately 120 kills relative to an initial prey population of 250 suggests high hunting impact on females, which may critically affect herd viability given the two-year birth spacing interval and long lifespan parameters (20 years to senescence) configured in the model.
