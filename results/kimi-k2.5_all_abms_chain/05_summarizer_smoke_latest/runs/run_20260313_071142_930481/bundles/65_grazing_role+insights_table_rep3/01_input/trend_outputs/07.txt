The distribution of healthy livestock among the 60 households initiates from a state of perfect equality, beginning at effectively zero (indicating identical holdings of 90 animals per household). Over the century-long simulation, inequality exhibits a steady monotonic increase, culminating in a Gini coefficient of approximately 0.073 by year 100. The average level of inequality across the full period remains modest at roughly 0.030, suggesting that while disparities emerge, they never reach extreme levels of concentration.

The trajectory of divergence proceeds through several distinct phases. During the initial two decades, the system experiences rapid differentiation relative to its starting point, with notable inflections occurring around years 8, 13, 15, 20, and 25. These early transitions likely reflect the initial differentiation of outcomes as households apply their respective learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—to stochastic rainfall variability. By year 37, the rate of divergence shifts again, followed by another transition around year 57, indicating evolving dynamics in how households' livestock holdings separate over time.

Structural change points at years 51 and 71 mark significant shifts in the inequality generation process, potentially corresponding to periods where cumulative differences in pasture management decisions and risk disposition interactions begin to compound. During the final phase, from approximately year 78 through year 93, the system exhibits oscillatory behavior with a dominant periodicity of roughly 49 years, suggesting cyclical variations in the rate of divergence during the latter decades before reaching the terminal value of 0.0726.

Despite the heterogeneity of learning strategies and significant environmental stochasticity (rainfall standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200), the maximum inequality remains below 0.1, indicating that private pasture tenure with autonomous decision-making prevents extreme asset polarization. However, the persistent upward trend across all analysis windows demonstrates that even with identical starting conditions and shared climatic signals, the interaction between individual learning approaches and risk dispositions generates inexorable differentiation in household outcomes over multi-decadal timescales.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The gradual emergence of inequality (reaching only ~7% after 100 years) suggests that heterogeneous learning strategies within private tenure systems maintain relative equity while permitting adaptive specialization to local conditions. Development interventions should recognize that disparities begin solidifying significantly after the 50-year mark; therefore, early support mechanisms for struggling households may prevent long-term stratification. The modest magnitude of inequality indicates that policies promoting diverse adaptive approaches—rather than enforcing uniform management rules—may effectively balance equity with ecological resilience in stochastic environments.
