**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The oscillatory decline pattern demonstrates that exploited megaherbivore populations may exhibit temporary demographic rebounds or apparent stabilization phases that do not indicate long-term viability. These transient increases in biomass, occurring even under sustained hunting pressure, could misleadingly suggest sustainable harvest rates when they actually represent delayed extinction dynamics. Conservation and management protocols must account for such nonlinear trajectories, recognizing that temporary population growth during exploitation regimes often precedes rather than prevents ultimate collapse. Monitoring strategies should prioritize detecting long-term trend directions rather than responding to short-term oscillations or intermediate equilibria that obscure the trajectory toward extinction.
