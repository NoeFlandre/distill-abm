 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction . It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges . The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics .  The population of active human hunter-gatherer bands maintains a constant value of 20 across all 2,312 observed time steps . The total energy available to forager bands begins at 2000 units and declines continuously throughout the simulation, reaching approximately -38229 units by the conclusion .  The cumulative mortality of female prey initiates at zero and accumulates to approximately 123 individuals by the conclusion of the observation period at step 2312 . The non-linear accumulation pattern indicates that hunting pressure on female megaherbivores is not constant but subject to complex feedbacks involving hunter energy budgets, prey spatial avoidance, and reproductive phenology .  The rate of death per person is 0.346 per year, but the rate of decline continues to slow through the first 100 years . The decline is statistically robust, with significant change points at steps 1,624 through 1,856 and 1,312 . The study suggests that the decline is consistent with a steady decline in mortality .  The metric reaches its maximum value of approximately 240 cumulative deaths around year 162, after which it declines slightly to 235 by the conclusion of the 200-year simulation period . The trajectory suggests an approximate six-year window to extinction under current parameters, indicating that interventions must be implemented during the early decline phase to prevent irreversible collapse .  The prey population commences at approximately 167 individuals and undergoes a precipitous decline to extinction over 2,311 time steps . The trajectory reveals a classic overharvesting dynamic: rapid initial depletion driven by established hunting pressure, followed by an extended terminal phase where reduced encounter rates between hunters and prey temporarily slow the decline but cannot prevent ultimate demographic collapse due to insufficient recruitment relative to ongoing mortality .  The decline occurs in distinct phases characterized by varying rates of attrition . The oscillatory decline pattern demonstrates that exploited megaherbivore populations may exhibit temporary demographic rebounds or apparent stabilization phases that do not indicate long-term viability . Conservation and management protocols must account for such nonlinear trajectories, recognizing temporary population growth during exploitation regimes often precedes rather than prevents ultimate collapse .  The percentage of vegetated landscape patches begins at approximately 33.0%, consistent with the initial grass proportion, then declines sharply over the first 365 time steps to reach a minimum of roughly 24.9% at the one-year mark . The model demonstrates that grassland environments with rapid regrowth capacities can maintain stable long-term fodder availability (near 33% coverage)**
