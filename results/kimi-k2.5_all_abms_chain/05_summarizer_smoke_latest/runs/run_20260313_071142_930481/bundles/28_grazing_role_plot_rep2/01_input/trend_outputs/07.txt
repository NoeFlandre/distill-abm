The Gini coefficient measuring inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all 60 households begins at 0.000 at time step 0, reflecting the initial condition where every household possesses exactly 90 animals. Over the 100-year simulation period, inequality increases monotonically in all runs, with final Gini coefficients at step 100 ranging from approximately 0.030 to 0.095.

The trajectories exhibit substantial heterogeneity across simulation runs. Approximately one-third of runs show rapid divergence, reaching Gini values of 0.090 or higher by year 100. Another third display moderate inequality growth, ending between 0.060 and 0.085. The remaining third show relatively constrained inequality, with final Gini values between 0.030 and 0.060.

A notable pattern emerges around time steps 40-60, where many trajectories exhibit accelerated growth in inequality. Prior to step 40, most Gini values remain below 0.030, with gradual increases. Between steps 40 and 60, the rate of increase intensifies for the high-inequality trajectories, while low-inequality trajectories continue modest growth. After step 60, the divergence between high and low inequality runs widens substantially, with some trajectories maintaining steep increases while others plateau or slow.

This pattern indicates that despite identical initial endowments and uniform exposure to the same rainfall regime within each simulation, the interaction of heterogeneous learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning), stochastic rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100), and individual risk dispositions generates divergent accumulation dynamics. The acceleration phase around steps 40-60 suggests the emergence of positive feedback mechanisms where households with growing healthy herds can better buffer environmental shocks and maintain advantages, while those with declining herds struggle to recover, amplifying initial stochastic differences into persistent structural inequality.
