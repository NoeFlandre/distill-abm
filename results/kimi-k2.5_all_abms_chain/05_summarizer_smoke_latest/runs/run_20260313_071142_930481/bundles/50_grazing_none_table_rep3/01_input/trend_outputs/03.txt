Reserve biomass initiates at approximately 90,000 units and undergoes a pronounced decline during the first 26 time steps, reaching a minimum value of roughly 84,114 units. This early reduction occurs with a notably steep negative trajectory evidenced by significant decreasing trends in the initial two decades of the simulation, with an inflection in the rate of decline appearing around year 8. Following this nadir at year 26, the system enters a transitional phase spanning approximately years 22 through 32 where no statistically significant directional trend manifests, indicating a temporary stabilization before directional change.

Subsequent to this plateau, a sustained recovery phase commences around year 33 and continues through the conclusion of the simulation at year 100. This ascending trajectory exhibits markedly variable rates of increase across distinct sub-periods. Between years 33 and 43, biomass increases with a moderate positive slope, followed by a period of rapid accumulation from years 44 to 54 characterized by the steepest growth rate observed in the time series. After this mid-simulation acceleration, the rate of increase progressively decelerates through years 55 to 100, with increments becoming gradually smaller though remaining consistently positive, culminating in a final value of approximately 121,073 units.

Superimposed upon these directional trends, the system exhibits oscillatory dynamics with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 years, with notable oscillatory activity concentrated around years 10 through 20 and again near years 41 through 49. These cyclical fluctuations likely reflect the influence of high rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 relative to a mean of 200) interacting with vegetation growth and grazing pressure.

In the context of this extension to the RAGE model—featuring heterogeneous populations of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types managing private pastures with stationary households—the initial decline corresponds to immediate pressure from the substantial initial livestock endowment (90 animals per household) prior to the activation of learning mechanisms. The subsequent recovery and net increase above initial conditions suggest that the combination of learning-by-doing processes and social learning through r-parameter updating enables adaptive management that progressively improves pasture biomass conditions despite climatic variability. The saturation pattern observed in later time steps implies convergence toward a system carrying capacity as household decision-making stabilizes under accumulated learning.
