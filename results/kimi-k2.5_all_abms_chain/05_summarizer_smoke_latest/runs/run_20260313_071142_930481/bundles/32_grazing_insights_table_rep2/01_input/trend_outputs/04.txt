The simulation reveals a U-shaped trajectory in available green biomass over the 100-year period, beginning at approximately 45,000 units, declining to a minimum of roughly 42,057 units by timestep 27, and subsequently recovering to reach approximately 60,535 units by timestep 100. This pattern indicates an initial period of ecological pressure followed by sustained recovery and growth exceeding initial conditions by over 34%.

During the first decade, biomass remains relatively stable near 45,000 units with slight downward pressure, reflecting the initial grazing impact from the stationary household population managing private pasture patches. Between timesteps 11 and 27, the system undergoes a significant decreasing trend, with the steepest declines occurring between timesteps 11 and 21, culminating in the minimum value at timestep 27. This decline phase represents the lag period during which the mixed population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types has not yet fully activated their learning mechanisms to optimize grazing strategies on their fixed patches.

Following this nadir, a transition phase occurs between timesteps 22 and 32 where biomass stabilizes and begins its recovery. From timestep 33 onward, the system enters a prolonged period of significant increase. The recovery initially proceeds at a moderate pace, then accelerates notably between timesteps 44 and 54, before the rate of increase gradually decelerates from timestep 55 through the remainder of the simulation. Despite this slowing growth rate in later decades, biomass continues rising steadily through timestep 100, with an inflection in the growth curve visible around timestep 49 and a notable change in trajectory near timestep 50.

In the context of this private-property grazing system with heterogeneous learning behaviors, the initial 27-year decline suggests that learning-by-doing and social learning processes require substantial time to overcome initial stocking pressures from the starting herd sizes of 90 animals per household. The subsequent recovery and overshoot beyond initial levels indicate that the combination of individual experience accumulation (E-LBD agents) and socially-informed risk parameter adjustments (E-RO-SL1 agents) eventually enables more sustainable pasture management than baseline strategies alone. The stationary nature of households on their private patches, combined with global rainfall variability, creates conditions where localized learning can effectively rebuild biomass stocks once initial overgrazing pressures are mitigated through adaptive decision-making.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

Stakeholders should anticipate an initial vulnerability window of approximately 27 years where available biomass declines despite the presence of learning capabilities, suggesting that early intervention may be necessary to prevent crossing irreversible degradation thresholds before adaptive management benefits materialize. The long-term trajectory demonstrates that supporting diverse learning strategies—both individual experiential learning and social learning from neighboring households—enhances ecological resilience and can achieve pasture conditions superior to initial states, though this requires patience and stability in institutional arrangements to allow the full 50+ year recovery cycle to unfold. The sustained growth despite high rainfall variability indicates that these mixed learning populations provide robust ecological outcomes in uncertain climatic conditions once the initial adaptation period is complete.
