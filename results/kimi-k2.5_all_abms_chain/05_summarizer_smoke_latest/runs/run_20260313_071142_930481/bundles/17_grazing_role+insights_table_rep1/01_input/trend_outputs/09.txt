Cumulative destocking begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year period, reaching approximately 97 animals per household by the final year. The accumulation follows a decelerating trajectory characterized by distinct phases of varying intensity. During the initial decade, destocking proceeds rapidly at rates exceeding four animals per year, followed by a sharp reduction in pace through year 25. Between years 25 and 60, the rate of accumulation declines further to approximately 0.3 to 1.5 animals per year, with a notable change in dynamics around year 60 where the system enters a prolonged phase of steady, low-rate accumulation at roughly 0.3 animals per year for the remainder of the simulation.

Superimposed on this long-term increasing trend are oscillatory dynamics with an approximate 50-year periodicity, evident in localized fluctuations concentrated around years 22–26 and again during years 49–61. These cycles indicate that destocking pressure intensifies and relaxes in multi-decadal waves rather than following a smooth adjustment path. The temporal distribution of cumulative values reveals that the system maintains an average accumulation of approximately 75 animals destocked per household across the full period, with a median value of 81 animals, indicating that the trajectory spends roughly half the simulation below this mid-range threshold before accelerating toward the terminal value.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The accumulation of nearly 100 animals destocked per household over the century—exceeding the initial herd size—indicates substantial livestock turnover as households actively manage stocking levels in response to highly variable rainfall conditions. The pronounced initial liquidation during the first 25 years suggests a necessary adaptation phase where farmers reduce herds to align private pasture carrying capacity with actual resource availability. The continued, albeit slower, accumulation throughout subsequent decades, coupled with multi-decadal oscillation cycles, demonstrates that stationary households operating under uncertainty and incomplete information do not converge on stable equilibrium herd sizes. Instead, they maintain flexibility through persistent destocking activity, indicating that long-term grazing management requires institutional and financial mechanisms capable of supporting ongoing livestock marketing rather than expecting a one-time adjustment to optimal stocking rates.
