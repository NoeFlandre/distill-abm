Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change. Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances. Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores. Foragers-movement-cost ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1.0, and distance hunters can perceive and pursue prey is determined by foraging-radius. The number of active human forager bands remains constant at the initial value of 20 agents throughout the entire observed simulation sequence, from the initial time step through approximately 2,200 steps. The cumulative number of female ungulates killed begins at zero and exhibits discrete, stepwise increases as individual hunting events occur. The model suggests that without intervention, anthropogenic hunting pressure alone can demographically eliminate male megaherbivores within a few years. Conservation strategies should prioritize reducing hunter density below the critical threshold of 20 individuals per comparable landscape area. The cumulative mortality count of postpartum females begins at zero across all simulation runs. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, the number of killed individuals increases rapidly. This represents the period of highest hunting pressure on nursing females, driven by high encounter rates. The number of male animals begins at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation runs, ultimately approaching zero in most runs. During the first 500 time steps, the population undergoes its most rapid reduction, typically falling from 125 to between 20 and 60 individuals. Populations typically decrease from the 20-60 range down to fewer than 10 individuals. The high variability in extinction timing suggests that small population management must account for stochastic extinction risks. The data reveal a consistent pattern of population collapse across all runs, with no instances of demographic recovery or stabilization at sustainable population levels. The pronounced variability in the timing of collapse suggests that stochastic elements in hunter-prey interactions and environmental conditions substantially influence the velocity of decline without altering the terminal outcome. The total energy stored in the female ungulate population begins at approximately 12,500 energy units across all simulation replicates. During the initial 400 time steps, energy declines precipitously to values ranging between 2,000 and 8,000 units. From step 500 onward, most replicates enter a terminal decline phase where female energy reserves approach zero. The percentage of vegetated landscape patches initiates at approximately 33%, consistent with the initial grass proportion parameter setting. Within the first 50 time steps, this metric undergoes a modest decline to approximately 32%, followed by a gradual recovery to 32.5–33% by step 300. At approximately step 350, the system experiences a catastrophic vegetation crash across simulation runs, with the percentage of green patches plummeting to between 23% and 30%.
