All simulations begin with approximately 12,500 units of total energy stored in the male ungulate population. During the initial 500 time steps, representing approximately 1.37 years, a rapid and steep decline occurs across all replicate runs. By step 500, total male energy falls to between 2,000 and 5,000 units, representing a depletion of 60 to 85 percent of the initial energy stores. This precipitous drop indicates intense initial pressure on male animals, consistent with the higher caloric value assigned to males (30 units) compared to females (27 units), which drives preferential targeting by human foragers.

During the subsequent 500 steps (steps 500 to 1000, covering approximately 1.37 to 2.74 years), the rate of energy depletion slows but remains consistent. Total male energy declines from the 2,000-5,000 range to near-zero values for the majority of simulation runs by approximately step 1000. Some replicates exhibit minor temporary fluctuations—small plateaus or slight increases in energy—suggesting intermittent inputs from maturing juvenile males or temporary reductions in hunting success, but these minor recoveries prove insufficient to reverse the overall trajectory of depletion.

Beyond step 1000 (approximately 2.74 years), the majority of simulation runs reach effectively zero total male energy by step 1200. A subset of replicates persists with trace energy levels (less than 500 units) extending to approximately step 1500 (4.1 years), and isolated runs maintain marginal energy stores until beyond step 2000 (5.5 years) before ultimately collapsing to zero.

Significant heterogeneity exists across the 20 replicate simulations. The fastest declining trajectories approach near-zero energy by step 500-600, while the most persistent trajectories maintain over 5,000 units until after step 500 and do not fully deplete until after step 1500. This variability suggests that while the eventual collapse of male energy stores is inevitable under the specified parameters—20 human foragers hunting an initial population of 250 ungulates—the precise timing of collapse is subject to demographic stochasticity, random variation in hunter encounter rates, and individual animal movement patterns.

For decision makers, the near-universal collapse of male energy stores within 1000 to 1500 steps (approximately 2.7 to 4.1 years) indicates that the current configuration of 20 foragers hunting a population of 250 ungulates is not sustainable for male megaherbivores. Management interventions must either reduce forager numbers, implement restrictions on hunting high-value male targets, or substantially increase initial ungulate population sizes to prevent rapid collapse of the male cohort. The high variability in collapse timing further indicates that early warning indicators may be unreliable—populations may appear stable for extended periods before sudden collapse, complicating monitoring and intervention efforts.
