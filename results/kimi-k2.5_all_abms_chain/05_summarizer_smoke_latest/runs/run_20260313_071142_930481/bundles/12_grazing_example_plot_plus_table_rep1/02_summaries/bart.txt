This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes. The mean reserve biomass across all patches initiates at 90,000 units and undergoes distinct phases of decline, recovery, and stabilization over the 100-step simulation horizon. The system exhibits an oscillation cycle of approximately 49 steps throughout the simulation, indicating periodic adjustment patterns in household decision-making. The aggregate livestock holdings inclusive of destocked animals commence at 90 units per household at step 0. The mean number of livestock destocked per household initiates at zero and immediately surges to a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals by step 3. This peak represents the most severe destocking event across the simulation timeframe. After step 37, the trend reverses, entering a recovery phase that persists through the remainder of the simulation. The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock commences at effectively zero, reflecting perfect equality in livestock health distribution across all 60 households. The trajectory suggests stationary households managing private pastures initially destock aggressively to align livestock with carrying capacity, then gradually refine their strategies through adaptive management. The count of non-learning households (E-RO behavioral type) initiated at 20, consistent with the initial parameterization distributing households equally among the three behavioral strategies. During the first eight time steps, the population exhibited a marginal aggregate increase, reaching a maximum mean value of approximately 21.1 households. The descent was particularly steep between steps 11 and 21, with the aggregate mean dropping sharply at a rate of roughly 0.22 households.
