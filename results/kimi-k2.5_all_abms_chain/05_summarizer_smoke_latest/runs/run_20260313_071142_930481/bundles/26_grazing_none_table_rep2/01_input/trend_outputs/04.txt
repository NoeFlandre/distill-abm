During the first ten timesteps, the system exhibits relative stability with no significant directional trend, though a slight downward trajectory emerges. Between timesteps 11 and 27, the biomass undergoes a statistically significant decline with a steep negative slope, reaching a minimum value of approximately 42,057 units at timestep 27. This represents the point of maximum vegetation depletion under initial grazing pressure from the 60 households managing livestock herds.

Following this minimum, the system enters a brief stabilization period between timesteps 22 and 32 where no significant trend is detected, as biomass levels hover near the minimum threshold. At approximately timestep 33, a marked regime shift occurs, initiating a sustained recovery phase. From timesteps 33 to 54, the biomass increases significantly with an accelerating growth rate, peaking at a slope of approximately 954 units per timestep between timesteps 44 and 54.

An inflection point occurs around timestep 49, followed by a confirmed change point at timestep 50, after which the growth rate begins to decelerate while remaining positive. Between timesteps 55 and 100, the biomass continues to increase significantly but at progressively slower rates (declining from slopes of approximately 379 units per timestep to 3 units per timestep in the final window). The system concludes at timestep 100 with approximately 60,535 units of available green biomass, representing a 34% increase over initial conditions and the maximum value observed across the simulation period.

This trajectory reflects the dynamics of a private-property grazing system where initial livestock pressure depletes vegetation, followed by adaptive recovery facilitated by learning mechanisms. The three behavioral types present—E-RO (baseline), E-LBD (learning-by-doing), and E-RO-SL1 (social learning)—appear to adjust grazing strategies through updating of risk parameters and experiential learning, allowing pasture recovery while maintaining household operations. The sustained growth beyond initial levels suggests that the combination of stationary households on private patches with adaptive learning behaviors eventually yields improved pasture conditions compared to the starting state, despite the absence of strategy switching between behavioral types.
