The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods. At timestep 0, each simulation run initializes with a distinct constant value that persists unchanged through timesteps 20, 40, 60, 80, and 100. The distribution shows clustering around small positive values (0.05–0.12), near-zero values (-0.02) and small negative values (−0.04 to −0.08) The distribution reveals that social learning agents’ long-term grazing outcomes are highly sensitive to initial risk disposition. At the start of a 100-year simulation horizon, mean green biomass exhibits a consistent U-shaped trajectory marked by initial degradation followed by recovery. The most successful outcomes achieve approximately 14% higher reserve biomass than the least successful ones. This suggests that adaptation mechanisms require approximately 40 time steps to effectively adjust stocking practices to sustainable levels. The time series trajectories of mean household destock exhibit a pronounced three-phase dynamic over the 100-step simulation horizon. After initial overstocking, the metric undergoes an immediate transient shock characterized by rapid escalation from zero to peak values ranging between 6 and 7.5 animals destocked per household within the first 5 time steps. By step 40, the system transitions to a steady-state regime characterized by persistent low-level destocking and trajectory divergence. The convex growth pattern suggests positive feedback mechanisms where early disparities in pasture condition or livestock health compound over time, leading to persistent inequality in livestock holdings across households. The wide outcome range (0.030–0.095) indicates high sensitivity to environmental stochasticity and adaptive decision-making processes. The wide variability in cumulative destocking outcomes suggests that learning strategy implementations can produce fundamentally different asset management trajectories. The plateaued patterns indicate that certain configurations enable early achievement of sustainable stocking levels, preserving household livestock assets. The prevalence of declining trajectories suggests systemic pressures against the baseline E-RO strategy over century-scale time horizons.
