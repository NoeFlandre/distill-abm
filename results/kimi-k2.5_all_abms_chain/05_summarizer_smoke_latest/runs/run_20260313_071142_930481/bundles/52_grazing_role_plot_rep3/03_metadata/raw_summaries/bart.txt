 The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system . Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors .  The mean reserve biomass begins at approximately 90,000 units . During the initial phase of the simulation, households begin with deviation values tightly clustered near zero . Some households stabilize at high positive deviations (0.70–0.80), others at moderate levels . However, during steps 40–60, a distinct subset of the population experiences dramatic negative transitions .  An initial slight rise, followed by a protracted decline to a minimum, then a steep recovery, and finally stabilization at varying equilibrium levels . The data reveal a four-phase temporal pattern characterized by an initial crisis, rapid adjustment, volatile transition, and divergent stabilization .  The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock distribution exhibits a consistent monotonic increase across all simulation runs, beginning at approximately zero (indicating perfect equality at initialization) and rising to values between 0.030 and 0.095 by the final timestep of 100 . The data demonstrate that inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households increases continuously over time in this system .  The analysis reveals substantial path dependence and divergence in the count of households employing the E-RO (non-learning) strategy across simulation replicates . By the conclusion of the simulations at step 100, mean cumulative destocking across the ensemble spans more than a threefold range, from 58 animals in the most successful stabilization scenarios to over 170 .
