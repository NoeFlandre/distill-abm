The population of households employing the non-learning baseline strategy initiates at 20 families. During the first eight years, this count undergoes a modest expansion, reaching a maximum of approximately 21 households near year 8. This brief increase suggests an initial period of demographic stability before the full impact of environmental variability manifests.

Following this peak, the system enters a sustained decline commencing around year 12. The reduction accelerates significantly through year 20, dropping from roughly 20 to below 19 households, indicating the onset of household exits as fixed rule-of-thumb decision-making encounters the realities of high rainfall variability (annual standard deviation of 100 units) and dynamic pasture feedbacks. Between years 22 and 45, the attrition continues at a moderated but steady rate, falling from approximately 17.5 to 15.5 households, reflecting ongoing vulnerability of non-adaptive agents to ecological stochasticity and competitive pressure from learning-capable neighbors.

After approximately year 60, the rate of exit diminishes substantially, and the series stabilizes near 15 households. From years 66 through 100, no significant directional trend persists, with values fluctuating narrowly between approximately 14.7 and 15.0 households, reaching a minimum of roughly 14.7 near year 92 before concluding at 14.7. This late-stage plateau suggests that the surviving E-RO households have adjusted their risk dispositions through single-loop learning to viable levels despite lacking access to experiential or social learning mechanisms, achieving a persistent equilibrium with their local ecological conditions.

The overall trajectory reveals a net decline of approximately 26.5% over the century, demonstrating that in environments characterized by substantial rainfall uncertainty and vegetation dynamics, stationary households relying solely on static decision rules face elevated attrition rates, though a subset achieves long-term persistence through within-strategy risk adjustment.
