The average degree of deviation from recommended livestock levels begins at a near-optimal state of approximately 0.007 units at year 3, indicating that households initially maintain stocking rates very close to theoretical recommendations. This minimal deviation is short-lived, as the metric rises sharply throughout the early simulation period, reaching a maximum value of 0.3814 around year 33. This substantial increase suggests that as farmers apply their respective learning strategies—whether baseline rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning—they initially diverge significantly from optimal stocking densities, potentially due to overestimation of carrying capacity during favorable rainfall years or delayed responses to pasture degradation.

Following this peak, the deviation enters a sustained decline from year 33 through approximately year 50, decreasing to values around 0.27 by year 48 before continuing to fall. This reduction likely reflects the effectiveness of experiential and social learning mechanisms in correcting earlier misjudgments, as households adjust their risk perceptions and stocking decisions based on accumulated feedback from pasture conditions and neighbor outcomes. The decline is particularly pronounced between years 33 and 50, corresponding to the period when agents refine their single-loop adaptations within their fixed behavioral strategies.

After year 50, the deviation stabilizes considerably, fluctuating without significant directional trend around a mean of approximately 0.23, ultimately settling at 0.1911 by the end of the 100-year simulation. While this represents a substantial improvement from the peak deviation observed at year 33, the final value remains significantly elevated above the initial near-zero baseline. This persistence suggests that despite continuous learning and adaptation, the combination of high rainfall variability and restricted knowledge radius (limited to neighboring patches) prevents households from fully converging on recommended stocking rates.

The system exhibits a dominant oscillatory pattern with a period of approximately 49 years, indicating cyclical adjustments in stocking decisions that likely correspond to multi-decadal vegetation recovery cycles or recurrent patterns of drought and recovery. These oscillations reflect the ongoing tension between livestock accumulation pressures and pasture regeneration rates, mediated by farmers' evolving risk attitudes under uncertainty. The high variance in deviation during the middle years (approximately years 20-40) compared to the final decades suggests that the learning process introduces significant variability in outcomes before settling into more consistent, albeit imperfect, management patterns.
