Inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households begins at essentially zero, reflecting the initial condition where all 60 households start with identical herd sizes of 90 animals. Over the 100-year simulation period, this inequality rises monotonically to a mean value of approximately 0.073, with a median of 0.023, indicating that the distribution becomes increasingly skewed over time. The standard deviation of 0.024 across the time series suggests moderate variability in the rate at which inequality develops.

The trajectory of inequality exhibits distinct phases. During the first approximately 40 steps, the increase is gradual, with values remaining below 0.015 as households maintain relatively similar outcomes. This initial period of low inequality corresponds to the early stages of adaptation where the three learning strategies—baseline experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—have not yet produced substantially divergent results. Inflection points occur at steps 8, 13, 15, 20, and 25, indicating subtle adjustments in the rate of divergence as agents begin processing environmental feedback differently based on their respective learning mechanisms.

A notable acceleration occurs around step 51, marked as the first significant change point, where the slope of inequality increase steepens considerably. Between steps 51 and 71, the Gini coefficient rises from approximately 0.03 to 0.05, reflecting growing differentiation in household outcomes. This acceleration suggests that cumulative advantages or disadvantages in stocking decisions begin to compound, with households employing different learning strategies experiencing divergent success in maintaining healthy livestock under variable rainfall conditions. The second change point at step 71 marks a further intensification of divergence, with inequality increasing most rapidly in the final 30 steps of the simulation.

Across the 30 simulation runs, final inequality values at step 100 range from approximately 0.03 to over 0.09, demonstrating substantial path dependency in system outcomes. This wide range indicates that stochastic elements, particularly the high annual rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200), interact with learning strategies to produce heterogeneous trajectories. The oscillation indices clustered between steps 78 and 93 suggest cyclical fluctuations in the late-phase divergence rate, likely corresponding to feedback loops between pasture condition and stocking decisions as the system approaches its terminal state.

Statistical tests confirm a significantly increasing trend throughout all segments of the simulation, with Kendall's tau values of 1.0 in nearly all rolling windows, indicating a consistent monotonic increase in inequality. The slope of increase accelerates from near zero in early windows to approximately 0.001 per step in later windows, confirming that inequality generation is not linear but rather exhibits increasing returns over time.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that even with identical starting conditions and equal representation of learning strategies, smallholder grazing systems naturally tend toward increasing inequality in productive assets. The acceleration of inequality after the 50-year mark suggests that early intervention periods are critical—policies aimed at preventing asset polarization must be implemented well before the mid-century point of system development. The substantial variation in final inequality levels across simulation runs implies that rainfall variability acts as an amplifier of differential learning outcomes, meaning that safety net mechanisms may need to be more robust in environments with high climatic uncertainty. The consistent upward trend across all runs, regardless of stochastic variation, suggests that the combination of private property regimes, heterogeneous learning capabilities, and environmental variability structurally produces winner-take-most dynamics in livestock wealth distribution.
