Across the 100-year simulation period, the mean green biomass exhibits a characteristic pattern of initial decline followed by substantial recovery, though with considerable heterogeneity in long-term outcomes. At initialization, the system begins with approximately 45,000 biomass units. During the first 5 timesteps, values remain relatively stable or show a marginal increase to roughly 45,500. Between timesteps 5 and 40, a pronounced decline occurs, with biomass dropping to minimum values ranging between 40,000 and 42,000. The nadir typically occurs around timesteps 30-40, representing a reduction of approximately 8-11% from initial conditions. Following this trough, a rapid recovery phase spans timesteps 40 to 65, during which biomass increases sharply from approximately 42,000 to values between 55,000 and 62,000. After timestep 65, the system enters a stabilization phase where growth rates decelerate considerably, though values continue to rise gradually through timestep 100. Final biomass levels at timestep 100 vary substantially across simulation runs, ranging from approximately 56,000 to 64,000. This divergence becomes apparent from timestep 60 onwards, with some trajectories plateauing around 58,000-60,000 while others achieve higher equilibria near 63,000-64,000. The coefficient of variation appears highest during the recovery phase (timesteps 50-70) and remains elevated in the final outcomes, indicating that stochastic climatic factors (rain mean: 200, std: 100) and heterogeneous learning dynamics among the three behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) generate divergent ecological trajectories even within identical parameter configurations. **Implications for decision makers:** The consistent pattern of initial degradation followed by recovery suggests that private-property grazing systems with learning-capable agents possess inherent resilience mechanisms, but the 30-40 timestep lag before recovery indicates a vulnerability window where intervention might be necessary to prevent irreversible damage. The wide dispersion of final biomass outcomes (approximately 8,000 units difference between best and worst cases) highlights that adaptive capacity varies significantly across households, suggesting that policies promoting knowledge sharing and uncertainty management (particularly supporting social learning and learning-by-doing processes) could help shift more households toward the higher-productivity equilibria observed in the upper range of outcomes. The high variability introduced by stochastic rainfall patterns necessitates risk management strategies that account for ecological uncertainty in grazing decisions.
