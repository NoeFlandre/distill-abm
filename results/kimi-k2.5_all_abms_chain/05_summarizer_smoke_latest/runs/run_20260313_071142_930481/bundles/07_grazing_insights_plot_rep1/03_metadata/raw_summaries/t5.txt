positive values indicating risk-seeking behavior . by step 25, values spread across nearly the full eventual range, with approximately 30% of households reaching high positive deviations . approximately one-quarter of households stabilize at high deviation levels . biomass declines progressively to minimum values ranging between 80,000 and 88,000 units . this represents a reduction of 2% to 11% from initial levels . rapid recovery phase dominates the period from step 40 to step 70 . the rate of recovery substantially exceeds preceding rate of decline . the high variability introduced by stochastic rainfall patterns necessitates risk management strategies that account for ecological uncertainty in grazing decisions . the agent-based simulations consistently initialize with an average livestock holding of 90 animals per household . following this period of minimum herd sizes, the trajectories diverge significantly across simulation runs . over time, learning-by-doing and social learning processes enable households to better align stocking rates with carrying capacity, reducing destocking needs . however, the variability in long-term outcomes across simulation runs indicates that stochastic rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100) and heterogeneous risk attitudes continue to produce divergent household outcomes even after adaptation. trajectories display step-like increments, indicating discrete adoption events where individual households engage in social learning . rapid initial uptake suggests that social learning mechanisms spread quickly through grazing communities once initiated . high variability in final adoption rates indicates that achieving widespread behavioral change is not guaranteed . non-learning households in many scenarios suggest traditional behaviors remain resilient . the strong path dependency observed indicates that timely support during initial implementation phases may be critical for influencing long-term behavioral distribution .
