Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change. Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances. Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores. Foragers-movement-cost ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1.0, and distance hunters can perceive and pursue prey is determined by foraging-radius. The number of active human forager bands remains constant at 20 throughout the simulation, from the initial time step through 2,200 ticks. This initial phase represents a period of high hunting efficiency where foragers encounter and target male prey due to their higher food value relative to processing costs. After approximately 1,000 time steps, the majority of runs exhibit a plateau, with the cumulative kill count stabilizing between 110 and 125 individuals and remaining constant until the simulation terminates. The cumulative number of births across the prey population remains at zero for the first approximately 750 time steps. Around step 750 to 800, a synchronized pulse of births occurs across multiple simulation runs. Following this initial reproductive pulse, the cumulative birth counts increase in a stepwise manner, with subsequent birth events occurring at irregular intervals between approximately steps 800 and 2000. By the end at step 2000, the total cumulative births vary substantially across simulation runs, ranging from approximately 1 to 10 births. The number of male animals commences at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation runs. By step 500, some populations have already crashed to zero, while others retain 20–30 males. The higher nutritional value assigned to males (30 vs. 27 for females) likely contributes to their elevated vulnerability. The total prey population begins at 250 individuals across all simulation runs. A rapid decline commences immediately, with most populations falling below 150 individuals within the first 250 time steps and dropping to fewer than 50 individuals by approximately step 500. By step 1000, the majority of runs have reached extinction or near-extinction states. The total energy contained within the megaherbivore population begins at approximately 25,000 energy units across all simulation runs. Over the first 500 time steps, this metric undergoes a precipitous decline. Some simulations lose over 90% of initial energy by step 400, falling below 1,000 units. By step 800, the majority of runs have converged near zero, indicating functional extinction of the male population. The timing of collapse is sensitive to initial conditions and stochastic events By step 2,000, all simulation trajectories converge toward zero total energy, indicating complete or near-complete depletion of the megaherbivore population. The model demonstrates that grassland ecosystems with high seasonal variability (seasonality = 0.8) and rapid regrowth capacity can sustain herbivore populations, but are vulnerable to severe overgrazing events.
