The model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape. Each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for the herbivores. The model specifically incorporates sexual dimorphism. Animals-gain-from-food ranges from 0 to 100, set at 1.5, representing energy obtained from grazing. Threshold for starvation mortality, animals-starvation-threshold, ranges between 0 and 100. Movement costs energy, with animals-movement-cost ranging from 1 to 10. Cumulative number of male ungulates harvested by human foragers begins at zero and increases rapidly during the initial phase of the simulations. The trajectories begin to diverge during this middle phase, with the cumulative kill count reaching approximately 100 to 125 males killed. A consistent maximum cumulative harvest regardless of stochastic encounter patterns. Female mortality within the first 500 to 700 time steps demonstrates that the reproductive capacity of the megaherbivore population collapses quickly under current hunting pressures. The existing forager population of 20 agents, combined with the specified energetic returns and processing costs, generates unsustainable harvest rates. Management strategies must prioritize immediate reduction of hunting pressure or enhancement of female refugia within the initial 400 to 500 time steps to prevent irreversible loss of reproductive potential. The 2-year interbirth interval creates severe demographic vulnerability. Population recovery is slow and highly sensitive to stochastic factors. Management strategies should prioritize reducing hunting pressure below the current threshold of 20 hunters. A small number of runs exhibit extended persistence, maintaining low numbers of males (1–5 individuals) until approximately 1500 to 2200 steps, but ultimately these populations also succumb. As populations approach critical thresholds below 10 individuals, variance decreases as most runs reach the absorbing state of extinction. The total prey population begins at 250 individuals across all simulation runs. A rapid decline begins immediately, with most populations falling below 150 individuals within the first 250 time steps and dropping to fewer than 50 individuals by approximately step 500. The rate varies substantially across simulation runs during this phase, with populations fluctuating between 0 and 40 individuals. From step 800 onward, the remaining populations continue eroding toward zero. By step 1,500, nearly all simulations converge at or near zero energy. A few outliers persist with trace reserves until approximately step 2,000 before complete collapse. Each successive decline becomes less severe: the second crash reaches approximately 29-30%, the third drops to 31.5-32%, and the fourth to around 32.5%. Variance among simulation runs is highest during the first vegetation crash (steps 300-350), where some runs experience catastrophic declines to 23% while others only fall to 29%.
