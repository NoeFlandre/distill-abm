The total energy available to forager bands initiates at values between 1,000 and 2,000 units. Over the first 500 time steps, energy declines precipitously to approximately -4,000 to -6,000, exhibiting substantial heterogeneity across simulation runs. This initial variance suggests that stochastic factors governing early hunter-prey encounters create divergent energetic outcomes during the establishment phase, with some forager bands securing early harvests while others experience immediate deficits.

Between time steps 500 and 1,000, the trajectories undergo convergence, with between-run variation diminishing significantly. By time step 1,000, energy values cluster tightly between -12,000 and -14,000. This narrowing of outcomes indicates that initial stochastic advantages in hunting success are temporary and that the system transitions toward deterministic dynamics as prey availability stabilizes or declines.

From time step 1,000 through approximately 2,100 steps, all trajectories follow a synchronized linear decline at a constant rate of approximately 18 to 20 units per tick, terminating at values between -30,000 and -38,000. The tight clustering during this phase, with minimal variance across runs, demonstrates that forager bands enter a steady state of consistent energy loss. Given the model configuration where 20 foragers each incur movement costs of 1 unit per tick, the observed decline rate of approximately 20 units per tick indicates that hunting returns are negligible during the latter portion of the simulation. The linearity and synchronization suggest that prey populations have been depleted below sustainable hunting thresholds, leaving foragers to expend energy through landscape traversal without compensatory gains from predation.

For decision makers: The inevitable progression toward deep energy deficits across all simulation runs reveals that the current parameter combination—20 hunters, 250 initial prey, high seasonality (0.8), and a foraging radius of 6—produces an energetically unsustainable hunting system. The convergence of trajectories indicates that stochastic variation in early hunting success cannot overcome the structural deficit created by movement costs relative to prey availability. To establish viable forager populations, interventions should focus on increasing prey initial density, expanding foraging radius, or reducing movement costs to achieve positive energy balance. The consistent linear decline phase suggests that once prey depletion occurs, the system exhibits limited resilience or recovery capacity, highlighting the critical importance of maintaining prey populations above thresholds that can sustain hunter energetic demands.
