The number of female prey animals begins at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation runs. The population undergoes a rapid and sustained decline throughout the simulation period, ultimately approaching zero in all instances.

During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 400 to 500 time steps, the female population decreases sharply from the initial 125 to fewer than 20 individuals in most simulation runs. This represents a decline of over 80% within the first 1.4 years of the simulation. The rate of decline is steepest during this period, suggesting intense pressure on the reproductive core of the population.

Between time steps 500 and 1000, the majority of simulation runs show the female population continuing to decline from residual values of 10–20 individuals down to near-zero levels (0–5 individuals). However, several trajectories exhibit prolonged persistence during this interval, with populations fluctuating between 5 and 15 individuals for extended periods before resuming decline.

From approximately time step 1000 onward, most runs have reached effective extinction (0–2 females), though a minority of trajectories maintain small populations of 1–10 individuals until as late as time step 2000. By the conclusion of the simulation period, all runs have converged to zero or near-zero female counts.

The high variability in the timing of final extinction—ranging from approximately 600 to 2000+ time steps—indicates strong stochastic effects in the system's trajectory once populations fall below critical thresholds. This suggests demographic stochasticity and potential Allee effects operating in the terminal phase of population decline, where small population sizes introduce randomness in survival and reproduction outcomes.

Interpreted within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, the rapid collapse of the female population from 125 to near-extinction within 500–1000 time steps indicates that the configuration of 20 human foragers, combined with sexual dimorphism in hunting value (males valued at 30 energy units versus females at 27), and high environmental seasonality (0.8), creates unsustainable hunting pressure. The slightly lower food value of females (27 versus 30 for males) does not appear to provide sufficient protection for the reproductive segment of the population, as the female cohort still experiences rapid depletion. The prolonged tail of small populations in some runs suggests that even with intense pressure, demographic stochasticity can occasionally sustain tiny populations temporarily through random fluctuations, though eventual extinction appears inevitable under these parameter settings.

**Insights for Decision Makers:** The consistent trajectory toward extinction across all simulation runs suggests that the current configuration represents a regime of certain population collapse rather than sustainable coexistence. Management interventions would need to either reduce hunter numbers significantly below the threshold of 20 foragers or implement strong hunting selectivity patterns that protect reproductive females to prevent the rapid erosion of the female base that drives the observed collapse dynamics. The high variability in extinction timing (ranging from approximately 1.6 to 5.5 years) indicates that while collapse is certain, the exact timing is subject to stochastic factors, suggesting that monitoring programs should not interpret temporary population stability as evidence of recovery when populations fall below approximately 20 individuals.
