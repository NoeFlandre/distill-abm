The total population of ungulate prey initiates at approximately 250 individuals and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction across all simulation replicates, ultimately declining to zero by approximately 2,300 time steps. The aggregate mean population exhibits a starting value of approximately 167 individuals, rapidly descending to a terminal value of zero with a median abundance of less than 1.5 individuals across the full temporal sequence.

The decline unfolds through three distinct phases characterized by differing rates of depopulation. During the initial phase, spanning approximately the first 125 to 500 time steps, the population experiences a precipitous collapse from 250 to below 50 individuals, representing an 80-percent reduction. This rapid descent reflects the immediate impact of hunting pressure from the initial cohort of 20 human foragers operating under dynamic optimal foraging theory, where high initial prey density facilitates efficient encounter rates and energy returns. A significant change point occurs at approximately step 125, marking the transition from initial stability to rapid decline, followed by another inflection near step 475 where the rate of loss moderates.

The intermediate phase, extending from roughly 500 to 1,000 time steps, features a decelerated but persistent decline with high variance across simulation runs. While the aggregate trend remains negative (Mann-Kendall tau approaching -0.9), individual trajectories diverge considerably, with some populations collapsing to near-zero by step 600 while others maintain cohorts of 20–40 individuals until after step 800. During this interval, the population exhibits oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of approximately 63 time steps, corresponding to seasonal vegetation cycles (seasonality parameter 0.8) that create temporary fluctuations in resource availability and demographic rates. Brief, statistically significant periods of slight increase occur within specific windows (e.g., steps 1,392–1,623 and 1,856–2,087), reflecting transient states where reduced hunter encounter rates at low prey density allow seasonal breeding pulses to temporarily offset mortality; however, these represent minor fluctuations (slopes near zero) rather than demographic recovery.

The terminal phase, from approximately step 1,000 to the conclusion at step 2,311, is defined by extremely low abundance, typically fewer than 5 individuals. Population trajectories during this extended period display prolonged persistence tails, with final extinction events occurring variably between approximately step 400 (rapid collapse scenarios) and step 2,100+ (extended persistence scenarios). This variability likely stems from stochastic effects including random herd movement patterns, spatial heterogeneity in remaining vegetation patches, and the probabilistic nature of hunter-prey encounters at low density. Despite these variations in extinction timing, the consistent convergence toward zero across all 120 simulation runs indicates that under the specified parameter configuration—combining immortal foragers with food storage capability, high environmental seasonality, sexual dimorphism in prey value, and the given ungulate life history parameters—population collapse represents the inevitable system outcome.
