The male prey population initiates at 125 individuals and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction, ultimately collapsing to zero at approximately tick 2,284. The dynamics unfold across three distinct phases: an initial demographic collapse, an extended period of oscillatory decline, and a terminal phase of scarcity culminating in extinction.  

During the initial phase, spanning roughly the first 390 ticks, the population undergoes a precipitous decline from 125 to approximately 20–30 individuals. This rapid reduction reflects the immediate impact of sustained hunting pressure from 20 immortal foragers capable of food storage, who encounter the initially dense prey population at high rates. Despite males possessing a higher food value than females, the combination of their elevated processing costs and the foragers' optimal foraging logic proves insufficient to buffer the population against initial overexploitation.  

Between approximately tick 390 and tick 1,400, the population enters a prolonged decay phase characterized by regular oscillations with a dominant period of roughly 63 ticks (approximately two months). Superimposed on the downward trajectory, temporary increases appear at tick 730—reaching approximately 10.5 individuals—and smaller peaks near ticks 800–900. The resurgence at tick 730 coincides precisely with the first potential birth pulse permitted by the two-year (730-tick) interbirth interval, indicating that seasonal or cyclic recruitment events temporarily bolster the population. However, these additions fail to reverse the overall decline, as the oscillation amplitude diminishes progressively, suggesting that each recruitment cohort is depleted faster than the next can be produced.  

In the final phase beyond tick 1,400, the population persists at extremely low densities, with values fluctuating near the median of 1.3 individuals. A brief, statistically significant increase occurs between ticks 1,392 and 1,623, potentially representing a lagged recruitment event or temporary spatial refuge, yet this recovery is truncated. The population resumes its decline and reaches extinction at tick 2,284. The prolonged persistence at low levels before final collapse indicates the operation of an extinction vortex, where demographic stochasticity and reduced herd viability at low density exacerbate vulnerability to the constant hunting pressure.  

**Implications for Decision-Makers:**  

The trajectory demonstrates that constant hunting pressure from a stable forager population drives long-lived, slow-reproducing megaherbivores toward deterministic extinction, even when initial prey densities are moderate. The oscillatory pattern, particularly the recruitment pulse at tick 730, reveals that reproduction occurs in discrete intervals that create brief recovery windows; however, without intervention, these pulses only delay rather than prevent extinction. Management strategies must reduce hunting mortality below the threshold required for population replacement, potentially by enforcing moratoriums aligned with the identified ~63-tick cyclical recovery periods or by limiting forager access during the critical two-year birth spacing intervals to allow sufficient cohort survival.
