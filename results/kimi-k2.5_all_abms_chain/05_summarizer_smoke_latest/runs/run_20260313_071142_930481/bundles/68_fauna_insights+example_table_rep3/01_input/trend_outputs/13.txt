The combined energy available across all prey animals initiates at 16,666.67 units, representing the total biomass of the megaherbivore herds at the start of the simulation. During the first 350 time steps, this value undergoes a rapid and substantial decline to approximately 4,100 units, indicating immediate heavy exploitation by hunters before the prey population can implement demographic adjustments through reproduction or movement.

Between time steps 350 and roughly 875, the system exhibits a pattern of oscillating decline characterized by regular fluctuations with a periodicity of approximately 63 steps. Within this interval, the energy temporarily rebounds to localized peaks near 4,100 units around steps 385 through 440, followed by a progressive diminishment to values oscillating between approximately 800 and 1,100 units by step 730. These regular fluctuations likely correspond to seasonal pulses in grassland resource availability and synchronized reproductive cycles, which prove insufficient to offset sustained hunting mortality.

From step 875 through 2,310, the population enters a prolonged terminal phase marked by high volatility within a general downward trajectory. Despite intermittent demographic recoveries—specifically significant increasing trends between steps 1,392–1,623 and again between 1,856–2,087 where available energy temporarily rises—the system cannot establish a stable equilibrium. These brief reprieves suggest continued reproductive attempts and resource utilization by surviving herds, but the magnitude of recovery diminishes over time.

Complete extinction occurs at step 2,311, with available prey energy diminishing to zero. The variability in population outcomes increases substantially throughout the simulation, transitioning from predictable initial decline to increasingly stochastic fluctuations as herd sizes drop below critical demographic thresholds.

For decision makers: The deterministic extinction within approximately 6.3 years (2,311 steps) demonstrates that the current configuration creates unsustainable predation pressure. Despite high initial prey biomass, seasonal resource availability, and temporary population recoveries, the combination of two-year birth spacing intervals and constant hunting pressure from twenty immortal, efficient foragers inevitably drives the megaherbivore population to collapse. Preventing extinction would require reducing hunter numbers or processing efficiency, or alternatively, significantly increasing prey fecundity or reducing interbirth intervals to allow demographic recovery to outpace hunting mortality.
