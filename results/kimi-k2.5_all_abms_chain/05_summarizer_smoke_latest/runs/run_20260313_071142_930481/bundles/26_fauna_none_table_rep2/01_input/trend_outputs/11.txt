The combined energy stored within the male prey population initiated at 12,500 units and underwent a precipitous decline to complete depletion by tick 2,284. 

In the initial phase spanning approximately the first 125 ticks, the system exhibited a steep downward trajectory from the starting maximum, superimposed with regular oscillations occurring at intervals of roughly 63 ticks. Within this early decline, a transient recovery peaked at approximately 11,608 units around tick 65 before the downward trend resumed.

Between approximately ticks 350 and 875, the rate of decline moderated considerably, with aggregate male energy stabilizing in a plateau regime. During this interval, localized fluctuations produced secondary peaks clustered between ticks 377 and 408, where values oscillated narrowly around 3,950 to 4,000 units, temporarily decelerating the approach toward extinction.

Following tick 875, the trajectory resumed its decline, though proceeding through a series of variable rates marked by inflection points at ticks 1,262, 1,337, 1,467, and subsequent intervals. Notably, the system exhibited transient demographic recoveries between ticks 1,392 and 1,623, and again near tick 1,856, where aggregate male energy briefly increased before resuming negative trends. These interruptions suggest temporary alignments of low hunting pressure with favorable seasonal resource availability or successful recruitment pulses.

After tick 2,156, the decline accelerated terminally, reaching zero energy units by tick 2,284 and maintaining that state thereafter, indicating complete extirpation of the male cohort. 

Throughout the 2,312-tick observation window, the distribution of values remained heavily right-skewed, with a mean of 1,984 units substantially exceeding the median of 135 units, reflecting the dominance of high initial energy states followed by prolonged low-energy conditions prior to extinction. The persistent oscillatory dynamics embedded within the overall collapse likely reflect interactions between seasonal grassland productivity (operating under high seasonality), pulsed foraging pressure from energy-storing hunters, and the demographic constraints of ungulate herd movement and senescence acting upon the sexually dimorphic population structure.
