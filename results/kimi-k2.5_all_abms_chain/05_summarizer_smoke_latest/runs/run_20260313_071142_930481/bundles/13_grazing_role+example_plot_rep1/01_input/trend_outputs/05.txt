The metric begins at approximately 90 animals per household across all simulations, consistent with the initial livestock endowment. Over the first 30 steps, values decline rapidly and synchronously to a minimum ranging between 40 and 45, indicating a system-wide crisis of overstocking where initial pasture conditions cannot support the endowed herds, forcing substantial destocking and/or livestock losses.

Between steps 30 and 40, the decline slows as the metric reaches a trough, stabilizing temporarily at approximately 40 to 44 animals. This floor represents the bottom of the degradation cycle, where vegetation conditions and herd sizes have contracted to a stressed equilibrium.

After step 40, simulations diverge into three distinct recovery trajectories. One cluster of approximately 10–12 simulation runs remains essentially flat, stabilizing between 41 and 45 animals through step 100, suggesting households maintaining conservative stocking rates without active rebuilding. A second group exhibits moderate growth, reaching steady-state values between 46 and 52 animals by approximately step 80. A third group, comprising two distinct trajectories, demonstrates robust recovery: one rises steadily to approximately 58–59 animals by step 100, while the other reaches approximately 61–62 animals, indicating that certain learning strategies successfully facilitate herd rebuilding through restocking or reduced mortality as pasture conditions permit.

Variance remains negligible during the initial 30 steps, increases substantially between steps 40 and 80 as behavioral strategies differentially affect outcomes, then stabilizes as trajectories plateau. The final values at step 100 range from approximately 41 to 62 animals, demonstrating that while no strategy recovers the initial unsustainable stocking level of 90, learning mechanisms significantly influence long-term livelihood security, with the most effective approaches achieving herd sizes roughly 50% larger than the most conservative outcomes.
