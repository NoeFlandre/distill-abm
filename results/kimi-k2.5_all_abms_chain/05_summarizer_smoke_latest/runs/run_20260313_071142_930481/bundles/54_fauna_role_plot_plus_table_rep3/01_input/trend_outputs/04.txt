The cumulative mortality trajectory of female ungulates exhibits a pronounced saturating pattern across the 40 simulation replicates, evolving through three distinct dynamical phases over the 2,312-tick observation window. The series initiates at zero and undergoes rapid accumulation during the first approximately 500 ticks, reaching values near 100 individuals. This initial phase is characterized by steep, statistically significant increasing trends (Mann-Kendall tau = 1.000, slope ≈ 0.21–0.23, p < 0.001) and marked by early inflection points at ticks 245, 262, and 285, indicating an acceleration in hunting success as foragers establish efficient targeting strategies.

During the intermediate phase spanning roughly ticks 500 to 1,800, the rate of accumulation decelerates substantially despite continued monotonic increase. The slope diminishes progressively from 0.059 to 0.002, with trend strength attenuating (tau declining from 0.999 to 0.124) but remaining statistically significant (p < 0.01). Multiple inflection points punctuate this interval at ticks 1,204, 1,336, 1,487, 1,631, 1,725, and 1,800, reflecting the gradual exhaustion of harvestable females. The trajectory reaches its maximum value of 123.67 at tick 1,826, having achieved plateaus clustering around 119–120 individuals from tick 1,164 onward.

In the final phase from tick 1,856 to 2,312, the series stabilizes with a slight declining tendency, falling from the peak of 123.67 to terminate at 123.00. This late-period decrease is statistically significant (tau = -0.453, slope ≈ 0.000, p < 0.001) and coincides with concentrated oscillatory activity at 63-tick periodicity evident between ticks 2,170 and 2,265. The overall mean cumulative mortality of 104.56 (median: 119.06, SD: 31.10) indicates right-skewed distribution across replicates, with substantial early variability converging toward the saturation level of approximately 120–124 females killed per simulation.

Interpreted within the MHPM framework, the rapid early accumulation reflects efficient forager exploitation of female ungulates, likely driven by their favorable processing cost (3 energy units) relative to males (6 units), despite marginally lower food value (27 vs. 30). The pronounced deceleration and eventual plateau suggest demographic depletion of the female cohort or adaptive behavioral switching by foragers as female availability declines. The 63-tick oscillatory pattern superimposed on the late-phase trajectory corresponds to seasonal hunting cycles, consistent with the strong environmental seasonality parameter (0.8) driving resource availability fluctuations. The stabilization at approximately 123 females killed—representing roughly 49% of the initial ungulate population (250 individuals)—indicates that hunting pressure effectively suppresses female recruitment below replacement levels within approximately five years (1,826 daily ticks), after which hunting mortality becomes negligible or intermittent.
