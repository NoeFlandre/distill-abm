The cumulative number of males killed begins at zero and increases rapidly during the initial 200 to 400 steps. This initial surge reflects the immediate availability of naive male prey populations and the high encounter rates between forager agents and male ungulates before significant depletion occurs. During this phase, the kill count rises steeply from 0 to approximately 60 to 80 males across all simulation runs, with tight clustering indicating consistent initial hunting success despite stochastic variation.

Between approximately 400 and 800 steps, the rate of increase moderates considerably as the cumulative count approaches 100 to 110 males killed. This deceleration corresponds to the depletion of accessible male prey from the initial population, forcing foragers to travel farther or wait longer between successful hunts. During this middle phase, divergence between simulation runs becomes pronounced, with some trajectories maintaining higher kill rates while others slow markedly, reflecting variation in herd movement patterns and forager search efficiency across the stochastic landscape.

After approximately 800 to 1000 steps, the curves flatten into plateaus with final cumulative counts ranging between approximately 108 and 125 males killed. This saturation indicates that the male subpopulation has been hunted to the point where further kills become energetically unprofitable or demographically limited, with remaining males either scarce, difficult to access, or protected by the spatial distribution of herds. Some simulation runs terminate earlier around 1500 steps while others continue to 2000 steps or beyond, but all maintain stable final kill counts, suggesting that the system has reached a quasi-equilibrium where male mortality from hunting is balanced by population dynamics or forager abandonment of male targeting.
