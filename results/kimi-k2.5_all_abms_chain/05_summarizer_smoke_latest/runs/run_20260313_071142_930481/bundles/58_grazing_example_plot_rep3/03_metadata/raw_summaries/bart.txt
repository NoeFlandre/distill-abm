 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model . It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes .  The mean household risk attitude starts at step 0 with values distributed approximately between -0.1 and 0.2 across different simulation runs . Several runs converge to high risk attitudes of approximately 0.75 to 0.8, indicating strong uncertainty aversion . Some populations converging to highly risk-averse postures while others adopt risk-seeking behaviors .  The average total livestock inventory, inclusive of destocked animals, commences at 90 units per household at the initiation of the simulation . The mean number of livestock destocked per household initiates at zero, then rises sharply within the first 5 time steps to peak values between 6 and 7.5 . By step 80, all trajectories stabilize, indicating the attainment of distinct long-term equilibria .  The count of households with social learning initiates at zero across all simulation runs . During the first 20 steps, trajectories diverge rapidly, exhibiting substantial variability in outcomes . The pattern demonstrates that even with initially equivalent conditions, the combination of private property boundaries, learning processes, and climate variability inevitably generates increasing concentration of healthy livestock among subsets of households .
