During the initial three years, the aggregate undergoes a precipitous decline as farmers immediately adjust away from the uniform initial stocking rate upon confronting the actual productivity constraints of their private pastures under variable rainfall conditions. This rapid downward correction reflects the initial shock of reality-adjustment, where stationary households encounter the limited carrying capacity of their fixed land parcels.\par
\par
The decline persists through approximately year 20, though the rate of reduction moderates considerably after the initial drop. Between years 11 and 32, the destocking continues at a decelerating pace, representing a period of active calibration where households employing learning-by-doing and social learning strategies progressively refine their stocking decisions based on accumulated experiential feedback and observation of neighbors within the knowledge radius.\par
\par
From year 33 to year 43, the trend stabilizes into a plateau with no significant directional movement, reaching a minimum value of approximately 41 animals per household around year 37. This trough represents a system-wide conservative equilibrium where the aggregate stocking level temporarily aligns with the most risk-averse interpretations of pasture conditions, as agents with differing behavioral types converge toward survival-minimum herd sizes under uncertainty.\par
\par
Subsequent to year 44, the trajectory reverses into a sustained recovery phase lasting through the remainder of the simulation. Herd sizes increase steadily from the minimum, with the rate of growth moderating significantly after year 55. By year 100, the average reaches approximately 47.5 animals per household, stabilizing at roughly 53 percent below the initial endowment. This prolonged, decelerating recovery suggests that while single-loop learning enables gradual restocking and adaptation to environmental feedback, the absence of double-loop strategy switching and the persistence of rainfall variability constrain the system's ability to return to initial stocking levels, resulting in a long-term equilibrium that reflects cautious pasture management under incomplete information.}
