The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods. The average deviation from recommended livestock levels begins at nearly zero (0.007), indicating that households initially maintain stocking rates very close to the optimal recommendation. The system exhibits pronounced oscillatory behavior with multiple local peaks and valleys ranging from approximately 0.27 to 0.38. The average livestock holdings per household, encompassing both actively grazed animals and those that have been destocked, commences at 90 animals. During the initial three years, the aggregate undergoes a precipitous decline as farmers adjust away from the uniform initial stocking rate upon confronting the actual productivity constraints of their private pastures. The number of households engaging in social learning begins at zero and undergoes a rapid initial ascent over the first 40 years.
