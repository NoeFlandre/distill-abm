**Context and Goals**

This agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Unlike the original RAGE model which examined common property scenarios where herders move livestock across shared rangelands, this extension focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods.

The simulation tracks the interaction between vegetation condition and household decisions about livestock numbers over time. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors.

The central purpose is understanding how these different ways of adapting to changing conditions influence pasture health, herd sizes, and the persistence of households. The model particularly examines how farmers handle uncertainty and incomplete information when making stocking decisions, represented through individual risk dispositions that affect how they perceive and react to environmental feedback.

**Key Parameters**

*Population and Spatial Setup:*
The **number-households** parameter establishes how many farming families occupy the landscape, which can range from 1 to 100 families, with this study using 60 households. Each household begins with **livestock-init** set at 90 animals. The simulation runs for **timesteps** set at 100 years. Farmers begin the simulation positioned on their designated land parcels, with **start-on-homepatch?** set to True. Each farmer can observe conditions within a **knowledge-radius** that ranges from 0 to 5 patches, set here at 1 patch distance.

*Behavioral Type Configuration:*
The study examines three specific learning strategies with equal representation: **number-E-RO** (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb), **number-E-LBD** (learning-by-doing), and **number-E-RO-SL1** (social learning with single-loop adaptation), each set to 20 households. The **homog-behav-types?** parameter set to True ensures each type is evaluated in uniform groups. The original RAGE model behavioral types are inactive in this extension: **number-SAT**, **number-TRAD**, **number-MAX**, and **number-random** are all set to 0. The default reference **behavioral-type** is set to E-RO.

*Grazing Management Practices:*
Formal pasture resting is disabled with **resting?** set to False. The threshold for initiating rest periods, **resting-threshold**, ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0.1. The **intrinsic-preference** for livestock relative to other values ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0. Similarly, **social-susceptibility** to neighbor influence ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0, indicating decisions rely on other factors.

*Original Model Parameters:*
Several parameters from the foundational RAGE model are retained. The **satisficing-threshold** for satisfied decision-making ranges from 0 to 100 and is set at 0. The **satisficing-trials** parameter, ranging from 0 to 100, is set at 21. These control how the original satisficer type would search for acceptable solutions, though this type is not active in the current setup.

*Ecological Conditions:*
Rainfall is applied uniformly across the landscape with **global-rain?** set to True. The average rainfall **rain-mean** is set at 200 units annually. Year-to-year variation in precipitation is controlled by **rain-std** set at 100, indicating significant variability in annual rainfall totals. Vegetation growth responds through two rates: **gr1** set at 0.5 and **gr2** set at 0.1. The parameter **w** from the original vegetation model is set at 0.8, influencing how pastures recover from grazing. Livestock reproduction is not active in this extension, with **b** set to 0.

*Learning and Risk Mechanisms:*
The extension module is activated with **extension-model?** set to True. Uncertainty handling is engaged with **risk-mode?** set to True. Each agent begins with an initial risk disposition **risk-att-init** set at 0.5, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance. The ability to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False, confining agents to single-loop learning within their assigned type.

The aggregate mean green biomass initiates at 45,000 units and maintains relative stability during the initial phase, with only minor fluctuations through step 10, briefly reaching a local maximum of approximately 45,261 units around step 4. Following this nadir, the biomass enters a stagnation phase lasting from approximately step 22 to step 40, where values hover between 40,000 and 45,000 units without sustained directional movement. This plateau represents a period of system adjustment where degradation pressures and recovery forces remain in temporary balance.

Starting around step 33 and accelerating markedly after step 40, the biomass enters a robust recovery trajectory. The most rapid expansion occurs between steps 44 and 54, during which the growth rate reaches its maximum slope of approximately 954 units per step. This swift recovery drives the metric upward by more than 15,000 units over a span of 20 steps as management adaptations take effect.

After step 55, while the upward trend persists through step 100, the rate of increase progressively attenuates, declining from several hundred units per step to merely 3 units per step in the final window. By the simulation's conclusion, the biomass asymptotically approaches 60,535 units, representing a net gain of over 15,000 units from the starting value and a recovery of nearly 18,500 units from the minimum.

Notable divergence emerges across the 30 simulation runs during the latter half of the time series. While trajectories remain tightly clustered with low variance through step 40, they subsequently spread across a range of approximately 9,000 units by step 100, with final equilibrium values spanning from roughly 55,000 to 64,000 units. This divergence indicates that stochastic environmental factors and emergent household decision-making patterns generate heterogeneous long-term pasture outcomes despite uniform initial conditions. The dominant oscillatory cycle of 49 steps aligns with the full degradation-recovery trajectory, while a structural change point at step 50 marks the transition from rapid recovery to gradual equilibration.

The aggregate livestock holdings across all households begin at 90 animals per household, then undergo a steep decline over the first 37 time steps, reaching a minimum value of approximately 41 animals. This initial reduction occurs in three distinct phases of decreasing intensity: a rapid contraction with a slope of -3.47 during steps 0-10, followed by a continued sharp decline with a slope of -0.96 through step 21, and a slower decrease with a slope of -0.37 until step 32. By step 37, the system reaches its lowest point at 41.01 animals per household, representing a 54% reduction from initial stocking levels. This early collapse reflects the immediate pressure of high initial stocking rates combined with significant rainfall variability, forcing households to destock aggressively as they confront the ecological limits of their private pastures.

Following the trough at step 37, the trend reverses direction and enters a sustained recovery phase lasting through the remainder of the simulation. From steps 44-54, livestock numbers increase significantly with a slope of 0.31, after which the growth rate gradually diminishes across subsequent decades: 0.15 (steps 55-65), 0.02 (steps 66-76), 0.005 (steps 77-87), and finally 0.002 (steps 88-98). By the final timestep, the average stabilizes at approximately 47.5 animals per household, though individual simulation runs diverge considerably, with final values ranging between roughly 41 and 62 animals. This divergence reflects the differential effectiveness of the three learning strategies—baseline experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—with some households successfully rebuilding herds toward moderate levels while others remain constrained at minimal stocking rates. The oscillation patterns detected between steps 5-20 and steps 39-42 suggest periodic adjustment behaviors as households react to annual rainfall fluctuations and neighbor observations within their knowledge radius of one patch. The system ultimately stabilizes well below initial capacities, indicating that sustainable stocking levels in this variable environment are substantially lower than the starting endowment of 90 animals, with behavioral differences determining whether households settle at the lower bound of 40-45 animals or recover to 55-65 animals by the century's end.

The average number of livestock destocked across all households begins at zero and immediately surges to a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals by the third year. This initial spike reflects the immediate pressure on pasture resources as households begin with livestock levels that exceed the sustainable carrying capacity of their private parcels, forcing rapid adjustments through destocking in the critical opening phase of the simulation.

Following this peak, destocking rates decline sharply and significantly throughout the first decade, dropping by roughly 50% by year 9 and continuing to decrease through year 30. This rapid reduction suggests that farmers across all learning strategies quickly adjust their stocking decisions in response to observed pasture degradation, with the experiential and social learning mechanisms enabling households to calibrate their herd sizes to avoid future resource shortfalls. The pronounced oscillations during this period, with secondary peaks occurring around years 9 and 12 before diminishing, indicate ongoing adaptation to the high inter-annual rainfall variability, where dry years trigger temporary destocking events as households respond to stochastic environmental conditions.

After approximately year 30, the system undergoes a structural shift toward stabilization. Destocking rates plateau at low levels, with the series mean of 0.96 masking the convergence toward near-zero values by year 100, where the average destocking settles around 0.31 animals per household. This long-term equilibrium suggests that persistent households have successfully aligned their livestock holdings with the expected productivity of their private pastures, given the stochastic rainfall regime.

Throughout the simulation, substantial variance exists across the population of 60 households, reflecting the differential effectiveness of the three learning strategies. While some trajectories stabilize at minimal destocking levels by year 40, others maintain low but persistent destocking rates around one animal per year, indicating that households employing certain learning approaches or facing particular spatial configurations of pasture quality continue to experience occasional resource constraints even after the initial adjustment period. The oscillation pattern with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, despite the 100-year timeframe, suggests that long-term climatic cycles continue to influence destocking decisions even as overall rates diminish.

The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock holdings initiates at effectively zero, reflecting the uniform initial allocation of 90 animals per household across the 60 farming families. Throughout the 100-year simulation period, inequality exhibits a sustained monotonic increase across all scenarios, with aggregate mean values rising to approximately 0.073 by the final timestep, though individual simulation outcomes diverge to range between roughly 0.030 and 0.095.  

During the initial establishment phase (approximately years 0-25), inequality accumulates at a gradual pace, reaching values of roughly 0.010 to 0.015 by year 20. This early differentiation emerges as households begin applying their distinct learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—to manage private pastures under variable rainfall conditions. The rate of inequality growth undergoes multiple inflections during this period, particularly around years 8, 13, 15, and 20-25, indicating episodic accelerations in the divergence of herd health outcomes as management effectiveness begins to separate households into performance tiers.  

The middle phase (approximately years 25-60) witnesses a moderate acceleration in inequality generation, with the Gini coefficient typically reaching 0.030 to 0.045 by year 60. A notable change point occurs around year 51, marking a transition where vegetation degradation dynamics and differential stocking decisions begin to compound disparities more rapidly. The inflection around year 37 and the structural break at year 57 further segment this phase into periods of varying inequality accumulation rates, reflecting the complex interaction between ecological feedback loops and adaptive learning behaviors.  

In the final phase (years 60-100), particularly following change points at years 71 and 100, the trajectories exhibit substantial divergence across simulation runs. While all scenarios maintain increasing inequality (as confirmed by significant positive Mann-Kendall trends across all rolling windows with tau values of 1.000), the rate of increase varies dramatically. Some trajectories accelerate steeply to reach Gini coefficients exceeding 0.090 by year 100, while others plateau at more modest levels of approximately 0.045 to 0.060. This divergence suggests that stochastic rainfall variability (operating with a standard deviation of 100 units around a 200-unit mean) and the spatial arrangement of learning types generate path-dependent outcomes where initial small differences in pasture condition compound into substantial wealth disparities.  

The absence of detected peaks or valleys throughout the time series indicates that inequality, once established, is not corrected by market forces or ecological feedback within the observed timeframe. By year 100, the system exhibits high variance across runs, with oscillation patterns in the final decades (indices 78-93) overlaying the dominant increasing trend, suggesting that while cyclical environmental fluctuations continue to influence short-term outcomes, the long-term trajectory toward resource concentration among successful learners remains consistent across all scenarios.

Some simulation runs achieve near-universal adoption where 54 households engage in social learning, while others stabilize with limited diffusion of only 18 households despite identical initial parameters.

The temporal dynamics exhibit discrete stepwise increases concentrated around steps 22-32 and 51-59, reflecting the episodic nature of strategy revision and adoption events within the discrete-time framework.
