The Gini coefficient quantifying inequality in healthy livestock holdings across the 60 households initiates at 0.0000, reflecting perfect equality at timestep 0 when all households begin with identical livestock endowments of 90 animals. Over the subsequent 100 timesteps, inequality increases monotonically and significantly, achieving a mean value of 0.0726 by timestep 100, with individual simulation runs exhibiting terminal values ranging approximately from 0.030 to 0.095.

The trajectory of inequality emergence exhibits distinct phases. During the initial 20 timesteps, the Gini coefficient rises gradually from 0 to approximately 0.015, representing the initial differentiation period. Between timesteps 20 and 50, the rate of divergence intensifies, with inflection points detected at timesteps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, and 37 indicating shifts in the velocity of household differentiation. At timestep 51, a structural change point marks the transition to an accelerated phase of inequality growth, with the coefficient increasing more steeply thereafter.

From timestep 60 to 100, the divergence between households becomes pronounced, with values progressing from approximately 0.030 to terminal values between 0.060 and 0.095 across different simulation runs. Statistical validation confirms significant monotonic increases across all examined decades of the simulation, with tau coefficients of 1.000 throughout, indicating virtually no reversals in the inequality trend. A dominant oscillatory period of 49 years manifests in the later timesteps, particularly between steps 78 and 93, suggesting cyclical fluctuations in relative livestock health superimposed on the secular upward trend.

The persistent increase in the Gini coefficient demonstrates that despite uniform initial conditions and shared exposure to variable rainfall (mean 200, standard deviation 100), the three distinct learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—produce markedly divergent outcomes in livestock management success. By timestep 100, some households maintain substantially larger populations of healthy livestock relative to others, indicating that adaptive learning approaches differentially buffer against environmental variability or capitalize on favorable conditions.

**Implications for decision makers:** The emergence of significant inequality even among homogeneous populations with equal resource access suggests that learning strategy heterogeneity inherently generates wealth disparities in stochastic grazing systems. Policy interventions may need to address equity considerations, as certain decision-making approaches confer persistent advantages under variable rainfall regimes. The acceleration of inequality after timestep 50 indicates that early interventions may be critical to prevent entrenched divergence in household welfare.
