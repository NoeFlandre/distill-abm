Across 120 simulation runs, the aggregate total energy of prey populations initiates at approximately 16,667 units (with individual trajectories ranging up to 25,000 units) and follows a deterministic extinction trajectory toward zero by step 2,312. The decline occurs through three distinct phases characterized by different rates of depletion and underlying demographic dynamics.

**Phase 1: Rapid Depletion (Steps 0–500)**
The initial phase exhibits the steepest rate of decline, with Mann-Kendall trend analysis confirming a consistent downward trajectory (τ = -1.0, slope = -24.95 to -36.03 units per step, p < 0.001). By step 350, the aggregate mean has declined substantially, marked by a significant change point. Temporary stabilization attempts appear around steps 385–440, where minor oscillations create localized peaks (values near 4,100 units) and valleys, suggesting brief moments of reduced predation pressure or seasonal resource pulses. However, these prove insufficient to reverse the trend, and by step 500—the location of the second change point—the population has collapsed to approximately 20-25% of initial biomass.

**Phase 2: Moderated Decline (Steps 500–875)**
Following the initial crash, the rate of depletion moderates though remains consistently negative (τ = -0.894 to -0.999, slopes ranging from -10.96 to -3.14). Inflection points at steps 513, 751, and 830 demarcate shifts in the curvature of decline, indicating transitions between different ecological regimes—likely corresponding to the sequential depletion of adult males (higher food value, 30 units) followed by females and juveniles (combined food value of 27-30 units including calves). Oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 steps persist throughout this phase, reflecting seasonal environmental variation (seasonality = 0.8) or demographic cycles, superimposed upon the overall downward trend. By step 875 (change point 4), aggregate energy levels typically fall below 1,000 units.

**Phase 3: Terminal Fluctuation and Extinction (Steps 875–2312)**
In the final phase, populations persist at critically low energy levels (median = 146.375, mean = 2,368.77 across full series, but below 500 after step 875). Brief, statistically significant increasing trends appear in rolling windows 7 (steps 1,392–1,623, τ = 0.206) and 9 (steps 1,856–2,087, τ = 0.445), representing minor demographic recoveries or hunting saturation effects. However, these insufficient rebounds cannot overcome the sustained predation pressure from the immortal hunter population (init-foragers = 20) equipped with food storage capabilities. The final change point at step 2,310 precedes the extinction endpoint (value = 0.0000 at step 2,311), though individual simulation runs reach zero at varying times between approximately step 500 and step 2,200, reflecting stochastic variation in extinction dynamics.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The invariant outcome of complete prey extinction across all 120 runs indicates that the current parameterization—combining immortal hunters, food storage capacity, and high hunting pressure (20 foragers)—creates an ecological trap with zero probability of sustainable coexistence. The steep initial decline (steps 0–500) represents a critical intervention window; once populations drop below the senescence threshold (age 7,300 ticks) and birth-spacing constraints (730 ticks) become binding due to reduced population density, recovery becomes mathematically impossible despite temporary fluctuations. Management strategies must either reduce hunter density, eliminate immortality and energy storage advantages for predators, or enhance prey fecundity/birth spacing intervals to prevent the deterministic collapse observed in this scenario.
