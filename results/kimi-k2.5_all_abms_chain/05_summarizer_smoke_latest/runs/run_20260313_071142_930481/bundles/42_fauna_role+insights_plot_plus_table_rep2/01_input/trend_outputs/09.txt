The female megaherbivore population undergoes deterministic collapse from an initial cohort of 125 individuals to complete extinction (0 individuals) across all 40 simulation replicates. The decline initiates immediately at tick 0 and follows a steep negative trajectory through approximately tick 600, during which the mean population plummets from 125 to below 10 individuals. Statistical analysis confirms this initial phase represents a significant monotonic decrease (Mann-Kendall tau = -1.0, p < 0.001) with a slope of -0.227 individuals per tick in the first 231 ticks, moderating to -0.061 per tick between ticks 464-695.

Following the rapid initial decline, the population enters a prolonged attrition phase characterized by gradual decay superimposed with low-amplitude oscillations of approximately 63 ticks periodicity. Throughout this phase (approximately ticks 600-2000), population levels fluctuate seasonally around a declining mean, with minor peaks observed at ticks 710 (5.08 individuals), 730 (6.51 individuals), and 893 (2.69 individuals). Despite brief intervals of slight increase detected in windows 1160-1391 and 1856-2087, the overall trajectory remains negative through tick 2311.

The timing of final extinction varies substantially across replicates, ranging from approximately tick 400 in the fastest collapsing runs to beyond tick 2000 in the most persistent, with the aggregate mean reaching zero at tick 2311. By tick 1000, most populations have declined to fewer than 5 individuals, and by tick 1500, the majority of runs have reached extinction. The median population size across the full simulation duration is 1 individual, indicating that populations spend the majority of their existence at critically low levels, while the mean of 16.48 reflects the weighting of initial high-density conditions.

Key inflection points in the rate of decline occur at ticks 305, 740, 833, and 1309, marking transitions between phases of rapid depopulation and slower attrition. Change points at ticks 475, 600, and 875 delineate shifts in the velocity of population loss, with the period between ticks 475-600 showing accelerated decline and post-tick 875 showing the slowest rates of decrease prior to final extinction.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The consistent extinction outcome under these parameter configurations indicates that a hunting pressure of 20 foragers with immortal status and food storage capabilities creates unsustainable predation on a starting population of 250 ungulates. The rapid descent to median levels near 1 individual within approximately 730 ticks (2 years) suggests minimal window for intervention or natural recovery. The 1600-tick variability in extinction timing between fastest and slowest collapsing runs implies that stochastic demographic and environmental factors significantly influence the precise moment of extinction without altering the deterministic outcome. Management interventions would require substantial reduction in hunter numbers or efficiency, or enhancement of vegetation regrowth parameters below the current 3-tick recovery rate, to shift the system from deterministic extinction toward stable coexistence. The 63-tick seasonal oscillation component suggests that resource management strategies should account for seasonal bottlenecks where predation pressure on depleted populations intensifies during low-resource periods.
