 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model . It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management .  The mean household risk attitude exhibits complex non-monotonic dynamics over the 100-step simulation period, characterized by rapid initial growth, subsequent divergence into distinct trajectory clusters, and eventual stabilization at heterogeneous equilibrium values . This pattern reflects the model's learning dynamics where households initially increase their risk tolerance as they accumulate experience, but subsequently diverge based on performance feedback, local interactions, and behavioral type differences .  The average available green biomass across all patches begins at approximately 45,000 units . The minimum at step 26 indicates a critical transition where learning-by-doing and social learning processes begin to significantly alter household decision-making . After step 65, the rate of increase gradually moderates but remains consistently positive through step 100, suggesting a slowing but continuing improvement in pasture conditions .  The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock begins at effectively zero at initialization, indicating perfect equality in livestock holdings across all 60 households . By the final time step 100, the average destocking reaches 0.31 livestock units, with most individual simulation trajectories clustering between 0 and 1.0 .  The metric exhibits monotonic growth throughout the 100-step simulation, ultimately stabilizing at an average value of approximately 32 households (53 percent of the 60-household population), with substantial cross-run heterogeneity producing a range of final values from roughly 18 to 54 . Some populations maintain relatively egalitarian distributions while others develop highly stratified livestock holdings .  Learning capabilities become dominant in the adaptive landscape of the private property grazing system . The stabilization of the non-learning population after step 65 indicates that the system reaches a quasi-equilibrium where approximately 73%  retain their initial strategy, while the remainder have successfully adopted alternative learning-based approaches .
