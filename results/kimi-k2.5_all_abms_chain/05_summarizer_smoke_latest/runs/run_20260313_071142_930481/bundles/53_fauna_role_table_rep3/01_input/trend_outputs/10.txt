The megaherbivore population undergoes deterministic collapse from an initial mean abundance of approximately 167 individuals to complete extinction within 2,312 time steps (approximately 6.3 years). The demographic trajectory follows a non-linear decline characterized by distinct phases of rapid attrition, decelerating mortality, transient recoveries, and persistent seasonal oscillations superimposed upon an overarching downward trend.

The collapse initiates swiftly during the first 125 ticks (roughly four months), when the population experiences precipitous decline from 166.7 individuals. This rapid attrition continues through tick 475 (1.3 years), representing the phase of maximal population loss. Between ticks 475 and 875 (1.3 to 2.4 years), the rate of decline attenuates substantially as the population enters a prolonged period of low-density persistence, though remaining in negative growth.

Seasonal demographic rhythms emerge prominently around tick 730 (year two), manifesting as regular oscillations with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 days (two months). These cycles generate recurrent sub-population peaks of diminishing magnitude—initially reaching 11.4 individuals, subsequently declining to 9.3, then 7.4, and progressively lower values at successive intervals. This pattern suggests synchronized reproductive pulses corresponding to seasonal resource availability, yet each cohort yields diminishing returns as hunting pressure continues.

During the extended terminal phase spanning ticks 875 to 2311 (years 2.4 to 6.3), the population exhibits high volatility while trending toward zero. Brief interludes of positive growth occur between ticks 1392–1623 (years 3.8–4.4) and 1856–2087 (years 5.1–5.7), during which mean abundance increases slightly before resuming decline. These transient recoveries likely reflect temporary relaxation of hunting pressure at critically low densities, permitting short-term reproductive success. However, with median abundance falling to 1.5 individuals and constrained by biennial interbirth intervals, stochastic processes dominate. The final individual expires by tick 2311, yielding a terminal abundance of zero.

The trajectory indicates that constant hunting pressure from energy-storing, immortal foragers, combined with strong environmental seasonality, creates conditions where predation mortality chronically exceeds the population's reproductive recruitment capacity. Despite seasonal resource pulses and brief episodes of demographic rebound at low densities, the biennial birth spacing proves insufficient to offset continuous harvest, resulting in rapid deterministic extinction within approximately six years.
