**Trend Description:**

All simulation runs initiate with a total prey energy of approximately 25,000 units. In the early phase (steps 0–400), there occurs a steep, monotonic decline across all replicates, with total energy dropping sharply to values ranging between 2,000 and 10,000 units by step 400. The rate of decline varies substantially among runs: some trajectories descend rapidly to near-zero within 400–500 steps, while others maintain energy levels of 5,000–8,000 units through step 500–600.

During the middle phase (steps 400–1,000), the decline continues but with pronounced heterogeneity in progression. Energy levels in surviving populations typically range from 500 to 5,000 units, with intermittent, minor fluctuations or temporary plateaus visible in subset trajectories. However, the overall directional trend remains negative across all replicates.

By step 1,000, the majority of simulations have converged to near-zero energy values (0–1,000 units). A minority subset exhibits extended persistence, maintaining trace energy levels (0–2,000 units) through steps 1,200–2,200 before ultimately declining to zero or negligible values.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The consistent trajectory toward zero energy across all simulation replicates indicates that the current parameter configuration—specifically the combination of 20 immortal hunters, high seasonality (0.8), and baseline prey demography—generates unsustainable predation pressure that leads to deterministic megaherbivore extinction. The temporal variability in extinction timing (ranging from ~400 to ~2,200 steps) suggests stochastic elements in hunting efficiency or spatial encounter rates, but the eventual outcome remains invariant. This implies that without intervention to reduce hunter density, modify forager mortality rules, or enhance prey reproductive capacity or environmental resource availability, population collapse is inevitable under these conditions.
