The male ungulate population initiates at 125 individuals and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction, reaching zero approximately 2,284 ticks (6.25 years) into the simulation. The population exhibits a strongly right-skewed distribution over time, with a median abundance of 1.3 individuals compared to a mean of 21.0, indicating that the majority of the simulation duration is characterized by sparse, remnant populations following an initial crash.

The decline occurs in distinct phases marked by shifting rates of decrease. During the first 625 ticks (1.7 years), the population undergoes a steep monotonic collapse from 125 to approximately 20 individuals, reflecting intense initial hunting pressure from the 20 human foragers. This rapid reduction aligns with the higher nutritional value assigned to males (30 energy units versus 27 for females), which renders them preferred targets despite marginally higher processing costs. The decline rate moderates between ticks 625 and 1,391, though remaining significantly negative, as density-dependent encounter rates decrease and the population enters a transient low-density phase.

A notable deviation occurs between ticks 1,392 and 1,623 (years 3.8–4.4), where the trend reverses to a statistically significant increase. This temporary recovery likely represents the first recruitment pulse from offspring born to the initial female cohort, given the 730-tick (2-year) interbirth interval specified in the life history parameters. However, this rebound proves insufficient to prevent subsequent collapse. Following tick 1,624, the population enters a terminal phase characterized by high volatility and no significant directional trend until final extinction, with sporadic oscillations occurring at a dominant period of 63 ticks (approximately bimonthly cycles) that reflect strong seasonal forcing on resource availability and demographic processes.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The rapid male-biased extinction within six years, despite initial population viability, indicates that sexual dimorphism in hunter preference creates an anthropogenic Allee effect that compromises long-term sustainability. The brief recovery window observed during year four suggests that management interventions targeting male protection during breeding intervals or reducing initial hunting quotas could theoretically exploit natural reproductive pulses to prevent collapse. However, the inevitable extinction outcome under current parameters implies that without active density-dependent refuge creation or behavioral adaptation mechanisms, standard optimal foraging behavior by human agents inevitably drives megafaunal populations toward elimination rather than stable coexistence.
