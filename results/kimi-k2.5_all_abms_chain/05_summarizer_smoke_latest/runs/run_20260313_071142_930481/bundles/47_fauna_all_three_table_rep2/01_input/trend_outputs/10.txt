Seasonal dynamics generate rhythmic fluctuations superimposed upon the declining trend, manifesting as temporary plateaus and minor resurgences. A second, less pronounced increasing period emerges between time steps 1,856 and 2,087. However, these respites prove insufficient to alter the terminal outcome. Following time step 2,087, the population enters a final phase of terminal decline, with inflection points at time steps 2,041 and 2,156 marking the ultimate adjustments toward complete depletion. **Implications for Decision Makers:**

The consistent trajectory toward extinction across all simulation iterations suggests that the configured hunting pressure—exerted by 20 foragers with energy storage capabilities and immortal status—exceeds the threshold for sustainable megaherbivore population maintenance within this high-seasonality environment. The seasonal oscillations provide brief windows of population stability at approximately 63-tick intervals that do not translate into recovery, indicating that temporary resource abundance alone cannot counteract continuous predation pressure. The achievement of extinction within approximately 6.3 years suggests that intervention timeframes are extremely compressed; conservation strategies would require either reducing initial hunter density below critical thresholds or implementing strict temporal hunting moratoriums synchronized with the 63-tick seasonal periodicity to interrupt the deterministic decline pattern. The brief increasing trends observed mid-simulation suggest that population recovery is theoretically possible if predation pressure can be sufficiently alleviated during critical demographic bottlenecks.
