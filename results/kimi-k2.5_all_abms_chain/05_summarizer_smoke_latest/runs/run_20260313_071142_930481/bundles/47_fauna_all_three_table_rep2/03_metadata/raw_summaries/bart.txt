 Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change . Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances . Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores .  The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation duration of 2312 time steps . There is no variance in the time series, with standard deviation and variance equal to zero . The aggregate forager energy commences at 2000 units and declines monotonically to approximately -38229 units by tick 2312 .  The cumulative count of female prey harvested begins at zero and follows a sigmoidal trajectory that ultimately saturates near 123 individuals . The trajectory reaches its maximum value of approximately 120 individuals near tick 1876 . The eventual plateau and slight decline in cumulative male mortality around year five (tick 1876) signals a potential tipping point where the male segment of the population becomes functionally depleted or hunting effort is redirected .  Postpartum females face disproportionate mortality risk during the first 18 months of simulation time . Population viability limited to approximately five to six years before reproductive collapse . Conservation strategies must reduce hunter density, expand grassland regeneration capacity to prevent megaherbivore extinction .  The male ungulate population initiates at 125 individuals and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction . During the first 625 ticks (1.7 years), the population undergoes a steep monotonic collapse from 125 to approximately 20 individuals . The population exhibits a strongly right-skewed distribution over time, with a median abundance of 1.3 individuals compared to a mean of 21.0, indicating that the majority of the simulation duration is characterized by sparse, remnant populations .  The population spends the majority of the simulation below 5 individuals, with extinction finalized at tick 2312 (approximately 6.3 years) The extinction trajectory demonstrates that even moderate initial hunting pressure (20 foragers versus 250 prey) can drive female megaherbivore populations to extinction within 6 to 7 years .  The combined energy stored in female ungulate populations initiates at 12,500 units, representing a robust starting population with substantial energetic reserves . This value undergoes a precipitous decline during the first 375 time steps, plummeting to approximately 2,232 units . The collapse resumes unabated after tick 2,087, passing through final inflection points at 2,115, 2,156, and 2,167 .  Moderate hunter densities can drive megaherbivore populations to extinction within a decade . The bulk of energetic reserves depletes within the first year of exposure to hunting pressure . The population cannot sustain itself beyond approximately 6.3 years into the simulation .  The stability of vegetation cover around 33% despite strong seasonal forcing and continuous consumption indicates that grassland systems with rapid regrowth capacities (3-day recovery cycles) can sustainably support megaherbivore populations without catastrophic degradation . The predictable first-year minimum at tick 365 identifies a critical vulnerability window during initial population establishment .
