The cumulative destock total of households begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the entire simulation period, reaching a mean value of approximately 97 units by the final time step. However, substantial heterogeneity characterizes the system, with final values across individual simulation runs ranging from roughly 58 to 173 units and a standard deviation of 20.3 around the overall mean of 75.5.

During the initial phase spanning the first 25 time steps, the metric exhibits the most rapid accumulation, rising steeply with decreasing but significant rates from approximately 4.96 units per step down to 1.47 units per step. By step 25, households have typically accumulated between 70 and 80 units of destocking, reflecting immediate responses to initial ecological conditions and the activation of risk management strategies among the heterogeneous population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types.

A distinct change point occurs at step 25, after which the rate of increase moderates considerably through step 60. During this middle phase, slopes decline from approximately 0.69 to 0.36 units per step as the pace of destocking decelerates. This inflection suggests that learning processes—particularly learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms—enable households to adapt their livestock management strategies to local pasture conditions and neighbor behaviors, thereby reducing the immediate pressure to destock.

From step 60 to the conclusion at step 100, the system enters a phase of gradual, nearly linear increase maintaining a consistent rate of approximately 0.31 units per step. Despite this slowing, the metric shows no indication of plateauing within the simulation timeframe, indicating that destocking pressures persist throughout the century-long sequence without reaching equilibrium.

The trajectory exhibits multiple inflection points between steps 45 and 75, reflecting subtle adjustments in the destocking rate as the complex feedbacks between vegetation dynamics, household decision-making, and stochastic rainfall continue to evolve. The persistent positive trend across all rolling Mann-Kendall windows confirms the absence of any reduction in cumulative destocking.

For decision makers, the concentration of destocking activity in the early simulation period suggests that initial policy interventions and early warning systems are critical for supporting household resilience in grazing systems. The substantial and persistent variability across runs, combined with the absence of stabilization by step 100, indicates that uniform policy approaches may prove inadequate; instead, flexible, long-term support mechanisms that account for heterogeneous household learning behaviors, risk attitudes, and private property contexts may better address the diverse destocking needs across the farming population. The continued upward trajectory through the final time step further implies that destocking pressures in private property grazing systems may not self-resolve without sustained external support or alternative livelihood strategies.
