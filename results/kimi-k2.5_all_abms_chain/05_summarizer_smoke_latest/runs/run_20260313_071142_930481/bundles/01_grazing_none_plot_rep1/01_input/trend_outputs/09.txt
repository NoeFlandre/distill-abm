The data reveals substantial heterogeneity in cumulative destocking outcomes across the 100 time steps. During the initial phase (approximately steps 0-20), all simulation runs exhibit a rapid accumulation of destocked livestock, with the mean cumulative destock count rising sharply from 0 to values ranging between approximately 60 and 80.

Following this initial period, the trajectories diverge considerably. A subset of simulations continues to accumulate destocked animals at a relatively constant or slightly accelerating rate throughout the entire simulation period, reaching cumulative totals between approximately 140 and 180 by step 100. Another group shows moderate continued accumulation, ending with values between roughly 100 and 130.

Notably, several trajectories plateau at relatively low levels, stabilizing around step 30-40 at cumulative values of approximately 60-90 and remaining nearly constant through step 100. The lowest observed trajectory stabilizes at approximately 58 from step 40 onward.

This divergence results in a widening gap between simulation outcomes over time, with the range between the minimum and maximum cumulative destock totals expanding from near zero at step 0 to approximately 115 units by step 100 (spanning roughly 58 to 173).

In the context of the grazing system model, these patterns indicate that household destocking behavior—representing adaptive responses to pasture conditions, livestock management needs, and climatic variability—follows markedly different long-term trajectories depending on stochastic rainfall realizations and the interaction of heterogeneous risk attitudes with the three behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1). The plateauing trajectories suggest that in some simulation runs, households reach a stable equilibrium where minimal further destocking is required, while the continuously rising trajectories indicate persistent pressure for livestock reduction throughout the 100-year period in other runs.
