The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to the combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change. Unlike traditional approaches that look at populations as uniform groups, this model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances. The model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for the herbivores. The latest extinctions occurred around step 2100, representing approximately 5.7 years of daily time steps, while the earliest collapses completed within the first year. The model results demonstrate that the configuration of 20 immortal human foragers with food storage capabilities, operating against an initial population of 250 ungulates under high environmental seasonality (0.8), generates immediate predation pressure that exceeds the population's replacement rate. The inter-simulation variability in collapse timing—spanning roughly 300 to over 2000 steps—likely emerges from stochastic elements including spatial heterogeneity in grass regrowth, probabilistic reproductive events governed by 730-tick birth-spacing intervals, and variability in encounter rates between mobile herds and hunters operating within 6-patch foraging radii. The observed heterogeneity in collapse timing, while substantial, indicates that under these parameter settings, which include sexual dimorphism in prey value (males 30, females 27) and differential processing costs, the megafauna population cannot achieve stable coexistence with persistent human hunting pressure.
