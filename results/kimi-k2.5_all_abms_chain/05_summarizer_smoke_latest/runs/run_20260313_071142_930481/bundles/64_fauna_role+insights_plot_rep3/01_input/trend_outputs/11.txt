The repeated simulations reveal a consistent pattern of rapid decline in total male energy from an initial aggregated value of approximately 12,000–12,500 energy units. Within the first 500 time steps (approximately 1.4 years), total male energy decreases by 60–100% across all simulation runs, with the majority of trajectories falling below 2,000 energy units by this point and several reaching near-zero levels.

Between steps 500 and 1,000, the decline continues but exhibits substantial heterogeneity across runs. While some simulations effectively exhaust male energy by step 600–700, others maintain residual energy between 500–3,000 units until approximately step 900–1,100, with isolated runs retaining 1,000–2,000 units until around step 1,200.

Beyond step 1,000, most simulations converge to zero or minimal residual male energy (under 500 units). A small subset of runs displays protracted persistence of trace male energy extending to 1,500–2,200 steps (approximately 4–6 years), though these trajectories also ultimately collapse to zero or near-zero values.

The trajectories demonstrate considerable inter-run variability in the rate of depletion during the intermediate phase (steps 300–800), suggesting sensitivity to stochastic elements such as hunter-prey encounter rates, herd movement patterns, or seasonal vegetation fluctuations. Despite this variability, the terminal outcome is consistent across all runs: total collapse of male energy stores.

**Implications for Decision Makers**: Under the specified parameter configuration (20 hunters, 250 initial prey, high seasonality of 0.8, immortal hunters with food storage capability), male megaherbivore populations experience rapid energetic depletion and functional extinction within 500–1,000 days (1.4–2.7 years). The consistent collapse pattern indicates that hunting pressure exceeds the population's regenerative capacity, even with active vegetation regrowth every 3 ticks. Because males have slightly higher nutritional value (30 vs. 27) and potentially different herd movement patterns, they face disproportionate depletion risk. This suggests that targeted hunting of males can eliminate reproductive capacity before total population extinction, creating an "extinction vortex" where remaining females cannot find mates. Conservation strategies must prioritize immediate reduction of hunting pressure on males, as the current rate leads to assured population collapse within a few years. The parameter set with immortal hunters and food storage creates a particularly severe scenario where hunting pressure never abates due to hunter mortality or energy constraints.
