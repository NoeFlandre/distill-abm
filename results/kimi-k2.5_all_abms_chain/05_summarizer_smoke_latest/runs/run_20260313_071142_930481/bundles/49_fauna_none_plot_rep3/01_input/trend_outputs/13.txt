The total energy of all prey animals initiates at approximately 25,000 units across all simulation replicates. This metric exhibits a universal decline toward zero, though the temporal dynamics of depletion vary stochastically across runs.

During the initial 500 time steps, the majority of simulations demonstrate a precipitous drop from 25,000 to near-zero levels. Approximately half of the replicates fall below 10,000 total energy within the first 200-300 steps, while others maintain higher values, sustaining energy above 15,000 until approximately 300-400 steps. One replicate exhibits delayed depletion, maintaining energy above 20,000 until roughly 400-500 steps before undergoing rapid decline.

By 500 steps, most simulations have reached energy levels below 2,000, with many at or near zero. Between 500 and 1,000 steps, the remaining populations with higher persistence complete their decline to minimal levels, typically below 1,000 units. A small subset of runs exhibits prolonged persistence, maintaining residual energy between 1,000 and 5,000 until approximately 800-900 steps.

After 1,000 steps, measurable prey energy remains in only a few replicates, generally registering below 1,000. One simulation exhibits exceptional persistence, maintaining low but detectable energy levels until approximately 2,000-2,200 steps before ultimately declining to zero.

Within the MHPM framework, these dynamics indicate that hunting pressure from 20 foragers operating under the specified parameters systematically overwhelms the prey population. With initial prey set to 250 herds, birth-spacing at 730 ticks (two years), and the defined food values and processing costs, the prey populations cannot achieve demographic replacement sufficient to offset hunting mortality. The variation in the timing of energy collapse reflects stochastic elements in hunter movement, encounter rates, and herd demographic processes, but the deterministic outcome of prey elimination to functional extinction is consistent across all replicates. The rapid trajectory from 25,000 to near-zero energy within approximately 500-1,000 steps for most runs suggests that forager efficiency and prey life history constraints create conditions where predation-driven mortality exceeds the system's capacity for population maintenance.
