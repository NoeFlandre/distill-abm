The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the simulation period from step 0 through approximately step 2200. Since hunters do not reproduce and cannot die under these parameters, the hunting pressure remains stable and unchanging over time. The total energy available to all forager bands exhibits a consistent downward trajectory. Pation of nearly all female prey units from the system within the first 1000 to 1500 steps. Credential mortality of postpartum females commences at zero and undergoes a phase of rapid accumulation during the initial 300 to 600 steps. Some runs show no reproductive activity throughout the observation period, remaining at zero through step 1500. The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model suggests that under the specified parameters, hunting pressure generates unsustainable mortality rates that consistently drive megaherbivore populations toward extinction. The decline occurs in discrete steps reflecting the stochastic nature of hunting mortality and herd demography, rather than a smooth continuous decrease. The total energy of all prey animals initiates at approximately 25,000 units across all simulation replicates. By step 1,500, most trajectories have reached or nearly reached zero energy. The majority of simulation runs reach effectively zero total male energy between steps 1000 and 1500, indicating functional elimination of the male prey demographic. The trajectory from 25,000 to near-zero energy within approximately 500-1,000 steps for most runs suggests that forager efficiency and prey life history constraints create conditions where predation-driven mortality exceeds system's capacity for population maintenance. The timing of energy collapse reflects stochastic elements in hunter movement, encounter rates, and herd demographic processes.
