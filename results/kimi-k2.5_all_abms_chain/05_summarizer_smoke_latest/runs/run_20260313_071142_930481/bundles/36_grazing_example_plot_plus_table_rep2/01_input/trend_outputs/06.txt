The average quantity of livestock destocked per household initiates at zero and undergoes an immediate, sharp escalation, attaining a maximum value of approximately 6.6 by the third yearly time step. This early peak reflects acute resource pressure as initial livestock populations encounter pasture biomass constraints, prompting forced removals before household decision-making strategies can effectively adapt to private property conditions and initial herd sizes of 90 animals.

Subsequently, the metric exhibits a pronounced decreasing trajectory through approximately the first 30 time steps, with statistically significant declines detected across consecutive intervals. The rate of decrease is particularly steep between time steps 11 and 21, during which destocking frequencies fall from values above 3.0 to below 1.0, followed by continued reduction through time step 32. This decline coincides with the activation of learning processes—particularly learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms among the heterogeneous behavioral types—as households refine their uncertainty management (characterized by the r-parameter) and grazing placement decisions based on observed biomass states and neighbor performance.

Around time step 30, the system undergoes a regime transition from rapid decline to low-magnitude fluctuation. Between approximately time steps 33 and 65, destocking rates stabilize at minimal levels, exhibiting no statistically significant directional trend as the mixed population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 agents converges toward sustainable stocking strategies. During this middle phase, outcomes diverge substantially across the simulation ensemble, with some households achieving near-zero destocking while others maintain persistent removal rates ranging between 0.5 and 1.0 animals.

In the latter portion of the simulation, from approximately time step 66 through 76, a modest but statistically significant increasing trend emerges, though values remain well below 1.0. By the conclusion of the 100 time steps, the average destocking settles at approximately 0.31, closely aligned with the median value of 0.31 across all observations. The substantial reduction from the initial peak, combined with low terminal values, indicates that the combination of private property rights, heterogeneous risk attitudes, and adaptive learning behaviors enables grazing systems to largely avoid severe destocking events once initial adjustment periods have passed, despite the high variability in rainfall conditions (mean 200, standard deviation 100) that characterize the ecological setting.
