The total energy available to forager bands begins at approximately 1,000 to 2,000 units across simulation runs. During the initial 500 time steps, this metric declines rapidly to values ranging between -4,000 and -6,000, exhibiting substantial variance across replicates. This early divergence reflects stochastic differences in initial hunting success, prey encounter rates, and the spatial distribution of foragers relative to ungulate herds as exploitation of the landscape commences.

After approximately step 500, the trajectories converge and enter a phase of steady, linear decline. From step 500 to step 2,000, total forager energy decreases at a relatively constant rate of roughly 16 to 20 units per step, with the majority of runs reaching approximately -32,000 by step 2,000. One replicate continues this decline to approximately -38,000 by step 2,200. The progressive narrowing of variance after step 1,000 indicates that despite initial stochastic differences, the system stabilizes into a consistent dynamical regime where energy losses occur predictably.

The sustained negative trend indicates that collective forager energy expenditure, driven by movement costs and processing efforts, consistently exceeds energy acquisition from hunting megafauna under these parameter configurations. Because foragers are immortal in this experimental setup, the increasingly negative values represent an accumulating energetic deficit rather than mortality events. This suggests that the initial population of 20 foragers cannot achieve energetic sustainability on the available prey base of 250 ungulates, resulting in a continuous drawdown of energy reserves that would, in systems lacking immortality, lead to forager population collapse.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The model indicates that the current combination of forager density, prey availability, and foraging efficiency produces unsustainable energetic returns, with foragers operating at a systematic deficit of approximately 16-20 energy units per time step after the initial transient phase. Decision makers should recognize that reducing forager movement costs, increasing hunting success probabilities, or decreasing initial forager numbers may be necessary to achieve energetic equilibrium. The strong convergence of trajectories after step 1,000 suggests that system outcomes become highly predictable once initial stochastic effects diminish, allowing reliable early forecasting of long-term forager energetic stress based on dynamics observed in the first 500 steps.
