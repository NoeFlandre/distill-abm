The cumulative mortality of female prey initiates at zero and accumulates rapidly during the first 500 time steps, reaching values between 60 and 120 individuals. This steep initial trajectory reflects efficient harvest rates during the early exploitation phase, likely driven by the favorable cost-benefit ratio of targeting females, who offer high nutritional return relative to processing costs compared to male prey.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the accumulation rate decelerates markedly across all runs, with most trajectories adding fewer than 20 additional kills during this interval. This inflection point corresponds to the progressive depletion of accessible females, forcing hunter agents to expend greater search effort or switch to alternative prey classes.

Final mortality totals stabilize between approximately 115 and 125 females, representing near-complete extirpation of the initial female cohort (estimated at roughly 125 individuals given the initial population of 250). The plateau is reached at variable times, with some runs stabilizing by step 800 while others show intermittent low-level mortality extending to step 2200 and beyond.

This temporal variability suggests that while the deterministic outcome is functional extinction of the female subpopulation, stochastic elements including herd movement patterns, spatial distribution of nursery groups, and vegetation patchiness create uncertainty in the precise timing of extinction. The extended tail observed in some trajectories indicates that residual females may persist in low-density landscape refugia before eventual detection and harvest, though the ultimate demographic outcome remains unchanged.

For decision makers: The consistent ceiling of approximately 120 female deaths across all simulation runs indicates that under current hunting pressure and demographic parameters, the female segment of the population faces deterministic extinction. The rapid accumulation during the first 500 steps represents a critical intervention window; once mortality exceeds approximately 100 females, population viability is irreversibly compromised. The high variability in extinction timing (spanning 800–2200 steps) demonstrates that landscape heterogeneity can delay but not prevent collapse, implying that spatial management strategies alone are insufficient without concurrent reduction in hunting mortality rates.
