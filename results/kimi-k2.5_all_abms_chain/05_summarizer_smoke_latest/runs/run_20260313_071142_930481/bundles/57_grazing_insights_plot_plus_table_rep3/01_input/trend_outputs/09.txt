The average cumulative destocking across households initiates at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation period, reaching approximately 97 animals per household by the final timestep. The accumulation follows a distinct decelerating pattern characterized by three primary phases. During the initial phase (years 0-25), destocking occurs at the highest rates, with early windows showing slopes near 5.0 animals per year moderating to approximately 1.5 and then 0.7 animals per year as this phase progresses. By year 25, a structural shift occurs marked by a significant reduction in accumulation rate. The middle phase (years 25-60) exhibits continued but slower increases with slopes stabilizing around 0.3-0.4 animals per year. A second adjustment occurs around year 60, after which the late phase (years 60-100) maintains a steady but diminished rate of approximately 0.31 animals per year through the simulation conclusion.

Throughout the trajectory, the metric exhibits substantial cross-sectional variability, with a standard deviation of approximately 20 animals and individual household outcomes diverging considerably by year 100, ranging from roughly 58 to 173 cumulative destocked animals. The time series displays oscillatory characteristics with periodic behavior detected around year 25 and year 49 cycles, suggesting responsiveness to underlying environmental variability (rainfall mean 200, standard deviation 100). Multiple inflection points between years 45 and 75 indicate nuanced adjustments in destocking rates as the heterogeneous agent population—comprised of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types—adapts to private property grazing constraints.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The trajectory reveals that destocking pressure is front-loaded, with households removing over half of the total century-long destocking volume within the first 25 years, suggesting an initial adjustment crisis to stationary private property regimes. The fact that cumulative destocking (97 animals) exceeds initial herd sizes (90 animals) by year 100 indicates chronic herd depletion requiring external replenishment or restocking mechanisms to maintain viability. The wide variance in outcomes implies that uniform policy interventions may be ineffective; instead, targeted support for high-destocking households and investigation of the factors driving successful low-destocking trajectories (potentially associated with specific learning behaviors) would be warranted. The persistent accumulation even in late years suggests that learning mechanisms and social learning (E-RO-SL1) or learning-by-doing (E-LBD) do not fully eliminate destocking pressure under the modeled climatic variability, indicating need for complementary risk management strategies beyond behavioral adaptation alone.
