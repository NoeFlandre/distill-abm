The inequality in healthy livestock distribution originates from a state of perfect equality (Gini coefficient approximately 0.000) at initialization, reflecting the homogeneous starting conditions where all 60 households begin with 90 livestock units. Over the subsequent 100 timesteps, the Gini coefficient follows a monotonically increasing trajectory, reaching a mean value of 0.0726 by the conclusion of the simulation, with individual runs exhibiting final values ranging between approximately 0.030 and 0.095.

The accumulation of inequality progresses through distinct phases characterized by accelerating divergence. During the initial approximately 50 timesteps, inequality accumulates gradually. A structural shift in the rate of divergence occurs around timestep 51, followed by a second inflection near timestep 71, after which the pace of inequality generation intensifies further. Statistical analysis confirms significant positive trends (p<0.001) throughout all observation windows, with successive inflection points detected at timesteps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, 37, and 57 indicating periodic adjustments in the rate of wealth concentration.

Notably, this inequality emerges despite all households facing identical climatic conditions due to global rainfall synchronization and beginning with uniform endowments. The divergence stems from performance differentiation among the three behavioral types—E-RO (baseline), E-LBD (learning-by-doing), and E-RO-SL1 (social learning)—as they interact with local pasture dynamics. During the final phase of the simulation, specifically between timesteps 78 and 93, the system exhibits oscillatory dynamics with a dominant period of 49 timesteps, superimposed on the overall upward trend, suggesting cyclical fluctuations in the pace of wealth concentration superimposed on the long-term divergence.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

These results indicate that private property grazing systems inherently trend toward increasing livestock wealth inequality even when households have access to adaptive learning mechanisms and face identical climatic conditions. Decision makers should anticipate that initial equality in herd sizes will erode systematically over time, with the rate of divergence accelerating after approximately 50 timesteps. The emergence of Gini coefficients exceeding 0.07 by the centennial mark, despite social learning and learning-by-doing capabilities, suggests that information-sharing and individual adaptation alone are insufficient to prevent wealth concentration. Policy interventions such as targeted safety nets, restocking assistance for disadvantaged households, or pasture management support may be necessary to maintain livelihood resilience across the population and prevent excessive inequality that could destabilize the social-ecological system.
