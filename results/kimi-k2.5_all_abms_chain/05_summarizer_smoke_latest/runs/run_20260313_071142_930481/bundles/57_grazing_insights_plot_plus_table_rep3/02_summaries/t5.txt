Model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making. The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. The aggregate population mean of 0.0074 indicates a near-neutral initial configuration where risk-seeking and risk-averse tendencies balance at the population level. The absence of peaks, valleys, or inflection points in the aggregate series confirms that the initial risk distribution persists without drift. From step 33 onward, a pronounced recovery begins, with biomass increasing significantly through step 54. The growth phase exhibits steep upward trajectories, particularly between steps 44 and 54 where the increase is most rapid. Growth rates slow considerably but continuing a slight upward trend through the final step at 100, where biomass reaches approximately 121,073 units. Bifurcation indicates that the interaction between stochastic rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100) and learning-by-doing processes generates divergent resilience pathways. The predictable collapse phase (0-37 timesteps) suggests that initial stocking rates of 90 animals substantially exceed the long-term carrying capacity under the modeled climatic variability. Inequality progresses through distinct phases characterized by accelerating divergence. A structural shift occurs around timestep 51, followed by a second inflection near timestep 71. This inequality emerges despite all households facing identical climatic conditions. The average cumulative destocking across households initiates at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation period. The accumulation follows a distinct decelerating pattern characterized by three primary phases. By year 25, a structural shift occurs marked by a significant reduction in accumulation rate. Minority of households will continue to employ static, non-adaptive strategies regardless of system dynamics. Policy designs should account for this persistent behavioral heterogeneity rather than expecting universal adoption of learning-based approaches.
