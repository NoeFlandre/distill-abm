 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model . It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes .  The mean household risk attitude (r-parameter) begins at approximately 0.0074 and follows a distinct three-phase trajectory over the 100-timestep period . Policymakers should recognize that while the average population may appear risk-neutral, significant heterogeneity exists at the individual level that remains constant throughout the simulation period .  The simulation series reveals a consistent pattern of initial livestock decline followed by divergent recovery trajectories across the 100-timestep period . Despite initial grazing pressure from substantial starting herd sizes (90 livestock per household), the system exhibits strong resilience and recovery capacity under private property arrangements with heterogeneous learning behaviors .  The average number of livestock destocked across all households begins at 0, increases sharply to a maximum value of approximately 6.61 by timestep 3 . The overall mean destocking rate across the entire 101-timestep series is 0.96, while the median of 0.31 indicates that the distribution remains right-skewed due to high early values .  The simulation of social learning adoption among 60 households begins with zero households engaged in social learning and exhibits a characteristic S-curve diffusion pattern over 100 time steps . The number of households practicing social learning increases to an average of approximately 32 households (53% of the population) by the conclusion of the simulation .  The trajectory of destocking pressure is front-loaded, with households removing over half of the total century-long destocking volume within the first 25 years . The fact that cumulative destocking (97 animals) exceeds initial herd sizes (90 animals) by year 100 indicates chronic herd depletion requiring external replenishment or restocking mechanisms .
