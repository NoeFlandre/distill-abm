**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The model configuration demonstrates robust ecosystem resilience, indicating that grassland environments with rapid vegetation regrowth capacities can sustain baseline fodder availability indefinitely under sustained megaherbivore grazing and hunting pressure without directional degradation. However, decision makers must account for predictable annual resource bottlenecks where vegetated coverage drops approximately 25% below baseline during specific seasonal windows, creating critical constraints on herbivore energy budgets during these intervals. The presence of regular bi-monthly oscillatory cycles superimposed on annual patterns suggests that grazing pressure generates dynamic, short-term depletion zones that recover rapidly; consequently, monitoring strategies should prioritize tracking seasonal minimums rather than long-term trends when assessing population carrying capacity or extinction risk thresholds. The stability of the 33% coverage baseline across two centuries of simulation suggests that current parameter settings maintain an equilibrium that buffers against extinction through environmental stochasticity, though the severity of seasonal downturns may still exert significant demographic pressure on herbivore populations during lean periods.
