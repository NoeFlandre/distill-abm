The cumulative mortality of postpartum female ungulates exhibits a saturating growth pattern over the simulation period, characterized by three distinct phases: an initial period of rapid accumulation, followed by progressive deceleration, and ultimately stabilization around an asymptotic maximum of approximately 117 to 119 individuals.

During the initial interval spanning roughly the first 475 ticks (approximately 1.3 years), the cumulative kill count increases precipitously from zero, establishing a steep upward trajectory. This phase corresponds to the establishment of hunting pressure on the landscape, where forager bands encounter and target postpartum females—representing high-return prey due to their combined nutritional value and lower processing costs relative to adult males. The accumulation rate is highest during this period, indicating immediate and intensive exploitation of available reproductive females.

Following the initial surge, the rate of increase decelerates significantly from tick 475 through approximately tick 1800 (covering roughly 3.6 years). The cumulative count rises from values near 40–50 toward the upper 110s, but the slope diminishes progressively. This deceleration suggests diminishing encounter rates as the population of postpartum females declines under sustained hunting pressure, or reflects the demographic constraint imposed by the two-year birth spacing interval limiting the replenishment of this vulnerable cohort. Inflection points scattered throughout this interval—particularly around ticks 1180, 1311, and 1394—indicate transient adjustments in the rate of accumulation as seasonal cycles and herd reproduction create periodic pulses of vulnerability.

In the final interval from tick 1800 to 2312 (roughly 1.4 years), the cumulative mortality stabilizes, oscillating narrowly between approximately 117 and 119 individuals with minor fluctuations. Notable peaks reach 119 around ticks 1826 and 1933, while valleys dip only slightly to 117. This plateau indicates that hunting mortality of postpartum females has effectively ceased to accumulate, either because the standing population of this demographic has been depleted to levels below sustainable encounter rates, or because hunting pressure has shifted to other prey categories as optimal foraging theory predicts reduced returns from scarce, high-value targets. The absence of continued growth suggests the system has reached a quasi-equilibrium where births of new cohorts balance or fall below losses to hunting, or where hunters have exhausted the initial supply of postpartum females present at simulation onset.

Superimposed on the saturating trend is a regular oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks (roughly 5–6 weeks), evident particularly in the plateau phase. These oscillations likely reflect seasonal environmental constraints affecting grass availability and herd movement patterns, or synchronized calving seasons that periodically concentrate postpartum females and thus hunting opportunities. Rolling trend analysis confirms the transition from strong monotonic increase in early windows to negligible or slightly negative trends in the final observation periods, with a notable shift to decreasing trends in the terminal segment (ticks 1856–2087), suggesting possible adaptive responses or stochastic failures in late-stage encounters.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The rapid saturation of cumulative mortality within approximately five years suggests that postpartum females face extreme vulnerability to hunting pressure due to their enhanced nutritional value, posing acute risks to long-term population viability through the selective removal of reproductive individuals. The two-year birth spacing appears insufficient to replenish losses under continuous forager presence, indicating that even moderate hunter densities can deplete the reproductive female cohort faster than demographic recovery permits. The pronounced 63-tick seasonal cycle concentrates hunting risk during specific vulnerable windows, necessitating temporally structured management strategies such as calving-season moratoriums or spatial refugia. The early plateau in mortality accumulation suggests that conservation strategies relying on non-reproductive prey availability may fail to prevent reproductive collapse, requiring explicit protection measures targeting females with dependent offspring to maintain herd demography and prevent Allee effects in small populations.
