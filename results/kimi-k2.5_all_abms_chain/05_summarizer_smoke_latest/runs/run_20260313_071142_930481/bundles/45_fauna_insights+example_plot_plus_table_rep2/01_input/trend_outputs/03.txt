The cumulative mortality of male ungulates exhibits a characteristic saturation curve across simulation runs, beginning at zero and rising rapidly during the initial phase before decelerating toward an asymptotic maximum. During the first approximately 500 time steps, the aggregate increases steeply, reaching values between 100 and 120 individuals as foragers exploit the initially abundant male prey population. This early phase is marked by high hunting efficiency, with statistical change points detected at 125, 250, 375, and 500 steps indicating progressive shifts in the accumulation rate as the prey base begins to contract.

Between steps 500 and 1200, the rate of increase diminishes significantly, with an inflection point observed around step 362 marking the transition from exponential to logarithmic growth. During this middle phase, rolling trend analysis shows steadily decreasing slope magnitudes (from 0.219 to 0.030), reflecting increasing foraging costs as male prey density declines. By step 850, the system undergoes another structural change, after which the mortality count advances only incrementally.

After approximately 1200 steps, the series stabilizes into a plateau phase with minimal net accumulation. The aggregate mean converges toward a terminal value of 117.5, with individual simulation runs settling between 110 and 125 total males killed. Late-stage fluctuations appear after step 2000, including a detected decrease in one rolling window, though these represent minor oscillations around the saturation level rather than systematic trends. The maximum observed value of 120.17 occurs at step 1876, after which the cumulative count remains effectively constant.

This pattern indicates that forager populations rapidly deplete the available male prey to a critical threshold within the first 500–850 steps, after which hunting pressure on males effectively ceases—either due to local extirpation of males, prohibitive search costs, or strategic switching to females and calves as optimal foraging theory would predict given the higher processing costs associated with males (6 energy units versus 3 for females). The consistent upper bound near 120 males across runs suggests a hard demographic limit imposed by the initial prey population of 250 animals and the specific life history parameters governing male survival and recruitment.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The front-loaded mortality profile demonstrates that hunting pressure achieves maximum impact on male populations within the first 500 simulation steps, creating a narrow window for intervention if conservation of breeding males is a priority. The rapid approach to saturation indicates that once the cumulative threshold of approximately 110–120 males is reached, the male segment of the prey population is effectively removed from the ecosystem, potentially destabilizing herd social structures and long-term breeding capacity. Managers should note that the high variance in the timing of saturation (ranging from roughly step 900 to step 2100 across runs) implies that stochastic factors significantly influence exactly when male populations collapse, even when final mortality totals remain consistent.
