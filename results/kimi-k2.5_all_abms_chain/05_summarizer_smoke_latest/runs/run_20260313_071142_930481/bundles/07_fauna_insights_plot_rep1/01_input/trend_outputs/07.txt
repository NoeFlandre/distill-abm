Across all simulation runs, the cumulative number of prey deaths increases from zero to between 220 and 250 individuals over the course of 2000 or more steps. The mortality trajectory follows a characteristic rapid-rise-and-plateau pattern: during the initial 500 steps, deaths accumulate swiftly, reaching 200–230 individuals (representing 80–92% of the initial population of 250). Subsequently, the rate of mortality decelerates substantially, with counts increasing more gradually to final plateaus of 230–250 deaths by steps 800–1500 in most runs, after which no further deaths occur.

Significant stochastic variation exists in the speed of mortality accumulation between individual simulations. While some runs achieve maximum death counts exceeding 240 individuals by step 600, others exhibit markedly slower trajectories, requiring 1500 to 2000 steps to approach comparable cumulative totals. One run demonstrates a particularly protracted accumulation, reaching approximately 230 deaths only by step 2000. The final cumulative death counts consistently represent 88–100% of the initial prey population across all runs, indicating near-complete population depletion regardless of the specific temporal trajectory.

Implications for decision makers: The consistent progression toward near-total mortality suggests that the modeled parameter configuration—including human hunting pressure, environmental seasonality, and herbivore life history constraints—creates a system with extremely high extinction risk for prey populations. The rapid accumulation of deaths during the first 500 steps indicates that population decline occurs swiftly once hunting pressure is applied, leaving a narrow window for potential intervention. The substantial stochastic variation in extinction timing between runs, despite consistent final outcomes, implies that predicting the specific moment of population collapse is unreliable; effective conservation strategies would require precautionary measures implemented well before the rapid decline phase begins.
