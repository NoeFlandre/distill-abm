The aggregate total energy of ungulate herds across 120 simulation runs exhibits a pronounced declining trajectory from initial conditions to complete depletion. At initialization, the mean total prey energy begins at approximately 16,667 units, reflecting the starting population of 250 animals with varying individual energy reserves. This value represents the maximum observed across the entire simulation span.

The most precipitous decline occurs during the initial 500 ticks, where the mean energy drops by over 75% of its initial value. Statistical segmentation reveals particularly steep negative slopes in the first 463 ticks, with rates of decline reaching approximately 24 to 36 energy units per tick during intervals 0-231 and 232-463 respectively. By tick 500, a critical change point, the aggregate energy has diminished to approximately 20% of initial levels, with many individual simulation runs having already approached or reached near-zero energy states.

Superimposed on this overall decline are regular oscillatory fluctuations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, persisting throughout the first 150 ticks of the simulation. These oscillations manifest as alternating peaks and troughs, with local maxima occurring around ticks 385-440 maintaining values between 3,700 and 4,100 energy units, followed by secondary peaks around ticks 709-730 at substantially reduced levels of 880-1,080 units.

Between ticks 500 and 875, the rate of decline moderates considerably, with slopes decreasing to approximately 3 units per tick (ticks 696-927) and eventually less than 1 unit per tick (ticks 928-1159). Despite this slowing, the directional trend remains consistently negative through tick 1391. Notably, brief periods of slight energy increase appear between ticks 1392-1623 and 1856-2087, though these intermittent upticks prove insufficient to reverse the overall trajectory.

By tick 2311, the mean total prey energy reaches exactly zero, indicating complete energetic depletion of the megaherbivore population across all simulation runs. The median value of 146.375 energy units—substantially lower than the mean of 2,368.77—indicates that for the majority of the simulation duration, populations maintained only minimal energy reserves, with the higher mean being driven by the early-phase abundance.

In the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, this pattern indicates that the configuration of 20 human foragers with immortal status and food storage capabilities, operating under high seasonality (0.8) with the specified energetic and reproductive parameters, generates sufficient hunting pressure to drive prey populations to deterministic extinction. The rapid initial decline suggests that hunter populations quickly establish sustainable predation rates that exceed prey population replacement capacity, even accounting for seasonal vegetation fluctuations and the sexual dimorphism parameters favoring slightly higher hunting value for males (30) versus females (27).

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The consistent collapse across all 120 simulation replicates indicates that this parameter configuration leads to inevitable megaherbivore extinction, suggesting that sustainable coexistence would require reducing initial hunter numbers below 20, reducing foraging efficiency (currently radius = 6 with low processing costs of 3-6 units), or increasing prey reproductive rates or survival parameters. The rapid decline within the first 500 ticks (approximately 1.4 years given 365 ticks per year) suggests that management interventions, if any were possible in this system, would need to occur extremely early to prevent irreversible population collapse. The immortal hunter assumption with food storage creates a predation pressure that does not diminish as prey become scarce, representing a "predator pit" dynamic where conventional density-dependent reduction in hunting pressure is absent.
