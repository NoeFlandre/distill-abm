**Trends in Female Megaherbivore Population Dynamics**

The metric initiates at approximately 120–125 female animals across all simulation replicates, consistent with the initial prey parameter of 250 total animals and sexual dimorphism. From the initial time step, every simulation trajectory exhibits a monotonic decline toward zero population. The rate of depletion varies substantially across runs: rapid extinction pathways show populations collapsing below 20 individuals by step 300–400 and reaching zero by step 500; intermediate trajectories maintain 20–40 females through step 400–600 before declining to extinction by step 800–1000; and prolonged persistence pathways sustain 10–30 individuals through step 600–800, with remnant populations of 1–5 individuals persisting in isolated runs until step 1500–2000.

By step 500, approximately 75% of simulation runs have declined to fewer than 10 females. By step 1000, nearly all populations have reached zero or are maintained at 1–2 individuals. The decline exhibits discrete stepwise reductions consistent with discrete hunting events and demographic processes rather than smooth exponential decay. Temporary stabilization phases occur in some runs where populations maintain 5–15 individuals for 100–200 consecutive steps before resuming decline, suggesting brief intervals where recruitment temporarily balances mortality.

**Interpretation Based on Model Context**

The consistent extinction outcome across all simulation runs indicates that the parameter configuration produces deterministic population collapse. With 20 immortal hunters operating under optimal foraging logic and prey exhibiting 2-year birth spacing (730 ticks), 28-year maximum lifespans (10,220 ticks), and senescence at 20 years (7,300 ticks), the per-capita mortality from hunting exceeds the population's reproductive replacement rate. The differential processing costs (females: 3, males: 6) and food values (females: 27, males: 30) render females relatively efficient prey targets, while high environmental seasonality (0.8) and moderate grass cover (0.33) constrain resource availability that could otherwise support population resilience. The variation in extinction timing (400–2,000 steps) reflects demographic stochasticity and encounter probabilities, but the zero-survival outcome indicates that stochastic factors delay rather than prevent extinction under these hunting pressure and life history conditions.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The parameter configuration demonstrates that current hunter density (20 foragers) is incompatible with long-term megaherbivore persistence given the species' life history constraints. To achieve sustainable populations, interventions must reduce hunter numbers substantially below the current threshold, decrease birth spacing intervals below 730 ticks to increase fecundity, improve habitat carrying capacity by increasing grass proportion above 0.33, or modify hunter foraging logic to reduce encounter rates with reproductive females. The rapid collapse observed in most runs (extinction within 1,000 steps, approximately 2.7 years in model time) suggests that without immediate intervention, population viability cannot be maintained. The differential vulnerability of females due to lower processing costs exacerbates extinction risk by targeting the reproductive segment of the population, indicating that hunting selectivity patterns may require regulation even if overall hunter numbers remain constant.
