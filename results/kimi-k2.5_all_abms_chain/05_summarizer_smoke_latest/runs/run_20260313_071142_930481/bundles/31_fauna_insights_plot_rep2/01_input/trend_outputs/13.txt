Across all simulation runs, the combined total energy of all prey animals begins at approximately 25,000 energy units. This metric undergoes a pronounced decline throughout the simulation period, ultimately converging toward zero in all cases, though the rate and trajectory of decline varies across individual runs.

The depletion occurs in two primary phases. During the initial phase (approximately steps 0–500), total energy drops sharply from the initial 25,000 to roughly 5,000 or below, representing a decline of 80% or more. This rapid reduction is characterized by high variability between runs; while some simulations experience nearly linear descent, others exhibit stepped declines with brief periods of slower depletion or temporary stabilization before resuming their downward trajectory.

In the secondary phase (approximately steps 500–1500), the remaining energy continues to decline but at a decelerated rate, with values typically falling from approximately 5,000 to near-zero levels. Most simulation runs reach effectively zero energy by step 1000, though a subset of runs maintain small residual energy pools longer, with the final extinctions occurring between steps 1500 and 2200.

The variation in extinction timing suggests that stochastic factors in hunter-prey interactions, herd movement patterns, or demographic processes create different depletion trajectories despite identical initial parameters. The step-like patterns visible in the decline curves indicate discrete hunting events or seasonal pulses of mortality rather than continuous exponential decay.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The consistent trajectory toward extinction across all simulation runs indicates that the parameter configuration of 20 hunters with 250 initial prey, combined with the specified life history traits (birth spacing of 730 ticks, senescence at 7300 ticks) and processing costs, creates an unsustainable hunting pressure that inevitably drives prey populations to collapse. Decision makers should recognize that under these conditions, no equilibrium state is achieved; the system lacks the demographic resilience (through reproduction or reduced mortality) to offset hunting losses. To prevent extinction, interventions would need to substantially reduce hunter numbers, decrease hunting efficiency (by increasing processing costs or reducing food values), or enhance prey reproductive capacity through shortened birth intervals or reduced external mortality.
