The cumulative destock totals exhibit a monotonic increasing pattern throughout the simulation horizon. Beginning from zero, households accumulate destocked animals rapidly during the initial approximately 25 steps, with the aggregate mean increasing at rates exceeding 4.9 animals per step in the earliest period (steps 0-10) and 1.5 animals per step in the subsequent window (steps 11-21). This early phase represents an intense adjustment period from the initial stocking level of 90 animals.

After the first change point at step 25, the rate of destocking moderates considerably to approximately 0.36 animals per step in the middle period (steps 33-43), and continues decelerating through subsequent intervals. The second change point at step 60 marks a transition to a sustained, slow accumulation phase where the mean increases at roughly 0.31 animals per step through the remainder of the simulation. By step 100, the aggregate mean reaches approximately 96.8 animals destocked per household, though substantial heterogeneity exists across simulation runs.

Terminal cumulative destock values span a wide range, from approximately 58 to 173 animals across the 30 simulation series. Some trajectories saturate relatively early, flattening to near-zero slopes by steps 30-40, while others maintain linear or accelerating accumulation throughout the full 100-step period. The median cumulative destock of 81.2 animals indicates a distribution where the majority of runs cluster below the mean, while a minority of trajectories with persistently high destocking rates pull the average upward.

Oscillatory patterns emerge around steps 22-26 and steps 49-61, coinciding with detected inflection points between steps 45-75, suggesting periodic destocking responses that likely relate to rainfall variability. Statistical tests confirm significant monotonic increases across all observation windows, with the slope declining by an order of magnitude from the initial adjustment phase to the long-term equilibrium phase. The persistence of destocking activity across the full simulation horizon indicates that households continue reducing livestock numbers periodically throughout the 100-year period, though the frequency and magnitude of these reductions diminish over time for most behavioral configurations.
