The mean green biomass commenced at 45,000 units. Over the first 27 years, biomass declined steadily, reaching a minimum value of approximately 42,057 units. This initial degradation phase indicates that the initial livestock endowment of 90 animals per household created grazing pressure exceeding the immediate carrying capacity of private parcels, depleting available vegetation despite uniform rainfall distribution across the landscape.

Between approximately years 27 and 33, the system stabilized near this minimum threshold before entering a sustained recovery phase from year 33 onward. During this recovery, biomass increased continuously, with particularly rapid accumulation occurring between years 44 and 54, corresponding to a marked shift in system dynamics around year 50. Growth continued through year 100, though at progressively decelerating rates, ultimately reaching approximately 60,535 units—substantially exceeding initial conditions.

This U-shaped trajectory reflects the interplay between initial overstocking and subsequent adaptive management. The early decline (years 0–27) occurred as stationary households, operating under uncertainty with initial risk dispositions of 0.5 and observing conditions within a one-patch knowledge radius, maintained herds that suppressed vegetation growth. The subsequent recovery (years 33–100) demonstrates the effectiveness of heterogeneous learning strategies—combining baseline experiential rules-of-thumb, individual learning-by-doing, and single-loop social learning from neighbors—in adjusting stocking decisions to allow pasture restoration. The final biomass levels exceeding initial conditions suggest that this particular combination of adaptive behaviors, operating under significant rainfall variability without formal resting periods, ultimately established a more productive equilibrium than the initial state, as reduced grazing pressure permitted vegetation to recover beyond baseline levels.

Cyclical dynamics with a periodicity of approximately 49 years are evident throughout the series, manifesting as oscillatory patterns particularly during the decline phase (years 10–20) and early recovery (years 41–49), indicating feedback loops between sequential vegetation responses and lagged adjustments in farmer decision-making as agents updated their strategies based on environmental feedback and observed neighbor outcomes.
