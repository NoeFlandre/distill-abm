The cumulative mortality trajectory of the megaherbivore population initiates at zero and exhibits a sigmoidal accumulation pattern over the 2,312-tick observation window (approximately 6.3 years), ultimately terminating at 235 total deaths—a value slightly below the maximum observed cumulative mortality of 240.3 individuals registered at tick 1,876 (~5.1 years).

The dynamics unfold across three distinct temporal phases. During the initial accumulation phase (ticks 0–1,159), mortality increases with high statistical significance and steep monotonicity, progressing through a series of discrete stepwise increments marked by change points at ticks 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850. Early in this interval, the accumulation rate averages 0.35 deaths per tick, gradually decelerating to 0.017 deaths per tick by tick 1,159 as the system approaches demographic saturation. This phase reflects the combined impact of baseline physiological mortality (2.5% per tick), external predation, and the onset of human hunting pressure from the initial forager cohort of 20 agents operating under optimal foraging constraints.

The middle interval (ticks 1,160–1,855) transitions to a stationary regime characterized by high variability and prominent oscillatory behavior with a dominant periodicity of 63 ticks (~2.1 months). Cumulative mortality fluctuates between approximately 236 and 240 individuals, with local maxima clustering around ticks 1,208, 1,395, 1,516, 1,672, and 1,876. These oscillations likely manifest from the strong environmental seasonality parameter (0.8), which drives pulsed vegetation availability and subsequent fluctuation in starvation-mediated and hunting-mediated mortality. Notably, sexual dimorphism in hunter payoff (males: 30 energy units, females: 27) and differential processing costs may contribute to selective harvesting patterns that interact with seasonal resource bottlenecks to produce these rhythmic mortality pulses.

The final phase (ticks 1,856–2,312) exhibits a statistically significant decreasing trend, with cumulative mortality declining from the peak of 240.3 to the terminal value of 235. This counter-intuitive decline in an ensemble-averaged cumulative metric indicates differential simulation attrition: stochastic realizations experiencing higher mortality rates likely drove prey populations to extinction prior to tick 2,312, thereby exiting the ensemble and leaving only low-mortality realizations to contribute to the final temporal averages. This divergence suggests bistability in the system, wherein identical initial conditions (250 prey, 20 foragers) produce divergent outcomes—rapid collapse versus sustained persistence—with the median cumulative mortality (233) exceeding the temporal mean (200.8), indicating a left-skewed distribution dominated by early, high-mortality extinctions.

For decision makers, these trends imply that megaherbivore populations under synchronized hunting and environmental pressures face critical transition windows between years 2 and 3 (ticks 730–1,095) where mortality acceleration is highest. The 63-tick oscillation period offers a predictable temporal window for intervention, such as seasonal hunting moratoria or supplemental feeding during predicted high-mortality months. Furthermore, the ensemble divergence observed after year 5 underscores the importance of early monitoring; populations surviving the initial high-pressure phase may stabilize, but stochastic extinction remains a persistent risk throughout the simulation horizon, necessitating adaptive management strategies that account for non-linear threshold effects in cumulative mortality trajectories.
