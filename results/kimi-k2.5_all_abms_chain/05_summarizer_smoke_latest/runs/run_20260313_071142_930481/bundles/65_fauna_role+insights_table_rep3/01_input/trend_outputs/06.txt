Over the 6.3-year simulation period (2,312 daily ticks), the cumulative birth metric exhibits a pronounced boom-and-bust trajectory characteristic of density-dependent collapse under sustained harvesting pressure. The metric initiates at zero, reflecting the absence of immediate reproduction in the founding population of 250 individuals. Following an establishment phase of approximately 1.9 years (725 ticks), the system enters a prolonged growth phase lasting through year 4.4 (1,623 ticks), during which the metric demonstrates statistically significant monotonic increases (Mann-Kendall tau > 0.78, p < 0.001) with rising slopes ranging from 0.001 to 0.007 births per tick. This acceleration aligns with the model's 730-tick (2-year) birth spacing interval, as the initial cohorts reach reproductive maturity and establish overlapping reproductive events.

The trajectory reaches its apex at approximately year 4.66 (tick 1,702), achieving a maximum value of 5.6 births accumulated in the temporal window, likely representing peak calving activity during favorable seasonal conditions. Secondary peaks occur at years 3.33 (3.45) and 3.81 (4.3), reflecting the oscillatory breeding pulses with a detected periodicity of approximately 50 ticks (roughly 50-day cycles) superimposed on annual seasonality driven by the 0.8 seasonality parameter and 3-tick grass regrowth dynamics.

A critical transition occurs at year 5.07 (tick 1,850), marking a structural break from growth to decline. From this point through year 5.7 (tick 2,087), the metric exhibits significant decreasing trends (Mann-Kendall tau = -0.495 to -0.453, p < 0.001) with negative slopes, falling from the peak of 5.6 to approximately 3.9 by year 5.4 and continuing downward. This contraction coincides with change points at years 5.6 and 6.3, indicating accelerating reproductive failure. By the simulation terminus, the metric collapses to approximately 1.0, representing a 94% decline from peak reproductive output and near-cessation of successful breeding events.

The inflection points detected at years 1.7, 2.0, 2.5, 3.1, 3.4, 3.6, 3.9, and 4.1 reveal rapid adjustments in reproductive rates as the population responds to dynamic vegetation availability and hunting pressure from the 20 forager agents operating under optimal foraging rules with 6-patch foraging radii.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The four-to-five year lag between simulation initiation and reproductive collapse suggests that monitoring programs must extend beyond short-term population stability assessments to detect cryptic decline phases in long-lived megaherbivores with multi-year birth intervals. The 50-day oscillatory pattern superimposed on annual cycles indicates that breeding is highly synchronized with ephemeral resource pulses, suggesting that seasonal hunting moratoria during peak conception or calving windows (detected here as quarterly cycles) could disproportionately benefit population persistence. However, the terminal decline to near-zero reproduction despite immortal hunters and food storage capabilities demonstrates that an initial forager density of 20 agents exceeds the sustainable harvest threshold for this life-history configuration (10,220-tick lifespan, 2-year interbirth interval), implying that hunter densities must be maintained below this threshold to prevent Allee effects and demographic stochasticity from driving populations into extinction vortices within decadal timeframes.
