The average initial risk attitude of social learning agents (E-RO-SL1) remains constant across all 100 time steps in every simulation run. The metric exhibits fixed values ranging from approximately -0.11 to 0.18, with each discrete horizontal line representing a distinct simulation scenario where the initial risk disposition was set to a specific constant level. No temporal variation occurs; values measured at time step 0 are identical to those at time step 100 for all trajectories. This stability reflects that the metric captures a fixed initial condition—the baseline disposition to deviate from recommended livestock numbers—that does not change during the simulation period, even as agents engage in social learning and update their current risk parameters.

The constant nature of these values indicates that the simulations are treating initial risk attitude as a static experimental parameter to test how different baseline risk dispositions affect social-ecological system performance. For decision makers, this suggests the model is evaluating the long-term impacts of heterogeneous initial risk profiles—spanning from risk-averse (negative values) to risk-seeking (positive values) starting points—on the resilience of private property grazing systems, rather than modeling the adaptation of risk preferences themselves. The range of values tested enables assessment of which initial risk attitudes promote sustainable outcomes under stationary household conditions.
