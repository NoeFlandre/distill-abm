The data reveal a pronounced three-phase dynamic in livestock destocking behavior across the 100-step simulation horizon. At initialization (step 0), no destocking occurs, reflecting the starting condition where households possess initial livestock endowments without immediate resource constraints. By step 3, the system experiences an acute stress event, with the aggregate mean destocking reaching 6.6144 (with individual simulation peaks approaching 7.5), indicating that nearly 7-8% of initial household herds were liquidated due to insufficient pasture resources during this early crisis period.

Following this peak, a sustained decline in destocking rates occurs through approximately step 30, characterized by significant negative trends (statistically significant decreasing trends with Kendall tau values of -0.491, -0.855, and -0.527 across sequential windows). This reduction reflects adaptive responses as heterogeneous agents employing E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral strategies adjust livestock placement and consumption decisions based on observed biomass conditions and neighbor performance. The rate of decline attenuates around step 30, marking a transition from crisis destocking to chronic stress management.

From step 30 to step 100, destocking stabilizes at substantially lower levels, converging to a terminal mean value of 0.3111 by step 100. However, this aggregate stability masks persistent heterogeneity among household populations. While some agent sub-populations achieve near-zero destocking rates (approaching 0.0), indicating successful adaptation to private property grazing constraints, others maintain steady-state destocking between 0.5 and 1.0, and outliers persist above 1.0 throughout the simulation. This divergence suggests that learning capabilities—particularly the contrast between baseline E-RO behavior, learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1)—generate differentiated long-term resilience outcomes, with certain risk-attitude parameter configurations failing to fully mitigate resource scarcity despite the absence of population mobility.
