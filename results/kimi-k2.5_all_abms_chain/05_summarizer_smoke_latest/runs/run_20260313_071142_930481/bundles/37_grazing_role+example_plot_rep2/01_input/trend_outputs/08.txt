The number of households engaging in social learning initiates at zero across all simulation runs. During the first approximately 20 to 40 time steps, this metric increases rapidly through a series of discrete stepwise increments, indicating that individual households activate social learning behaviors at specific decision points rather than gradually. By approximately step 40, the majority of trajectories stabilize and maintain constant values through step 100, suggesting that once households adopt social learning under these conditions, they persist in this behavioral mode indefinitely.

The final count of households with social learning exhibits substantial variability across simulation runs, ranging from approximately 18 to 54 households. This range represents between roughly 30 percent and 90 percent of the total population of 60 farming households. Such divergence indicates that stochastic environmental factors, particularly the significant annual rainfall variability (with a standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200), generate divergent pathways for the diffusion or activation of social learning within the community.

The timing of adoption also varies considerably across runs. Some trajectories demonstrate rapid early increases, reaching counts of 30 or more households within the first 10 to 15 steps, while others show delayed adoption with household counts rising slowly until as late as step 30 or 35. This heterogeneity suggests that local pasture conditions and the specific sequence of environmental shocks experienced during the initial years critically influence when households transition to utilizing social information from neighbors within the knowledge radius. The stepwise nature of the trajectories reflects the discrete, annual decision-making cycle of the farmers, where stocking decisions made at specific time steps trigger the activation of social learning mechanisms based on observed outcomes and neighbor behaviors.
