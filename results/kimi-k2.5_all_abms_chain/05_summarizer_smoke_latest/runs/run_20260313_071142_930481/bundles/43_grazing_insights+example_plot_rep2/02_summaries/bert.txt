This agent - based model is an extension of the rage (resilience of agent - based grazing systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. These operate through a yearly sequence where green biomass updates, households with e - ro - sl1 types update their r - parameters, households update behavioral strategies (if switching is enabled), make livestock placement decisions, livestock feeds on biomass, households observe neighbor performance, reserve biomass updates, and households observe and remember reserve biomass states. For decision makers, the absolute stability of initial risk attitudes demonstrates that without explicit learning mechanisms or external interventions targeting risk perception, household dispositions toward grazing deviations remain entrenched throughout the simulation. This persistence underscores the critical importance of initial condition calibration in determining long - term system outcomes, suggesting that early - stage policies — such as initial risk socialization or targeted onboarding of households into grazing cooperatives — may exert lasting influence on pasture management behaviors. During the first 20 time steps, the trajectories diverge rapidly as agents with e - ro - sl1 (social learning) and e - lbd (learning - by - doing) capabilities adjust their uncertainty management parameters in response to initial ecological conditions and neighbor observations. Throughout this latter period, minor fluctuations occur within each cluster, but the boundaries between high - risk, moderate - risk, and low - risk management regimes remain distinct, reflecting the irreversible nature of early learning pathways in the adaptive co - management process. During the first two to five time steps, the metric exhibits a minor increase, peaking briefly at roughly 90, 500, before commencing a sustained decline that continues through approximately time step 35. After approximately time step 60, the trajectory transitions into a stabilization phase where reserve biomass continues to increase gradually before plateauing at values ranging between approximately 111, 000 and 128, 000 for the remainder of the simulation through time step 100. The convergence of trajectories during the recovery phase suggests robust system dynamics in the medium term, while the divergence in final outcomes highlights the importance of early intervention and management strategies to ensure favorable long - term biomass conditions. This recovery and stabilization above initial levels suggests that the combination of vegetation regrowth dynamics and adaptive learning - based management strategies — implemented through the e - ro, e - lbd, and e - ro - sl1 behavioral types — enables the system to achieve a higher sustainable biomass equilibrium than the initial state. The variation in final biomass levels across simulation runs reflects the influence of stochastic rainfall variability with a mean of 200 and standard deviation of 100, as well as potentially divergent outcomes from the heterogeneous learning behaviors and risk attitudes distributed across the household agents. Furthermore, the wide divergence in final outcomes emphasizes that environmental stochasticity significantly impacts pasture productivity, reinforcing the importance of maintaining adaptive capacity and flexible management strategies to manage variable climatic conditions in private property grazing systems. The divergence after step 40 reflects the interaction between stochastic environmental variability (such as rainfall fluctuations) and heterogeneous household behaviors, including different learning strategies and risk attitudes. For decision - makers, the results suggest that while initial destocking phases are highly predictable, long - term recovery outcomes are uncertain and highly sensitive to both environmental conditions and the specific behavioral adaptations employed by households. The high variance across simulation runs, particularly pronounced in the first 20 steps and again in the final 40 steps, suggests that stochastic elements such as rainfall variability and initial household conditions significantly influence the ability of households to achieve resilient grazing management. For decision makers, these trends highlight that early intervention may be critical to prevent the system from locking into high - destock trajectories, and that the potential for achieving low - destock equilibrium states exists but depends heavily on initial conditions and the specific realization of environmental variability. After step 60, the trajectory steepens considerably in most runs, with inequality accelerating rapidly through positive feedback mechanisms where households with healthier herds leverage superior pasture management knowledge to maintain livestock advantages. The sensitivity of final inequality levels to stochastic factors suggests that early intervention strategies — such as targeted support for lagging households or modulation of social learning networks — should be implemented before positive feedback loops consolidate livestock advantages, as redistribution becomes progressively more difficult once learning - based inequalities have entrenched. The step - like increments observed during this phase indicate discrete adoption events rather than gradual continuous uptake, consistent with the social learning mechanism where households update their behavioral strategies based on observed neighbor performance within the knowledge radius. This wide variance across runs suggests that stochastic elements in rainfall patterns, initial household configurations, and neighborhood interaction dynamics create divergent pathways for social learning diffusion, with some populations achieving near - universal adoption while others remain at low levels. Approximately one - third of simulation runs stabilize completely by steps 30 – 40, maintaining cumulative destocking totals between 58 and 70 for the remaining duration, suggesting these configurations rapidly identified sustainable stocking rates and required no further destocking interventions. By the conclusion of the simulations at step 100, the cumulative destocking metric clusters into three quasi - stable equilibria : a low - destocking range (58 – 75) representing successful early adaptation and stable herd management, a mid - range cluster (80 – 110) indicating ongoing adjustment pressure, and a high - destocking cluster (120 – 173) reflecting either highly conservative stocking strategies or continued ecological degradation forcing repeated destocking events across the century - scale simulation. Decision makers should prioritize identifying which behavioral configurations enable the rapid stabilization observed in the low - destocking cluster (58 – 70), as these represent sustainable equilibria that minimize transaction costs and destocking burdens over time. Additionally, the existence of high - destocking trajectories (exceeding 130 by step 100) indicates that without effective learning mechanisms, households may face continuous pressure to reduce herds, suggesting that policies supporting social learning or learning - by - doing processes could prevent the accumulation of excessive destocking requirements in smallholder grazing systems. Several runs exhibit sharp increases in this period, peaking between 35 and 42 households around time steps 15 to 35, indicating that in these scenarios, households originally using learning - based strategies switched to the baseline non - learning approach. Adaptive learning should prioritize early engagement with households, as delays may result in system lock - in to non - learning equilibria that prove resistant to later change. The coexistence of trajectories with high and low e - ro counts also suggests that uniform policy approaches may be ineffective, and interventions might need to account for local heterogeneity in social learning networks and initial risk attitudes.
