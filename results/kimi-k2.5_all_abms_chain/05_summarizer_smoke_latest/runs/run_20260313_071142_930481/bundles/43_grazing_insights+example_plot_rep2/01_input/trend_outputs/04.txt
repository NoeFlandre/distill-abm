The mean green biomass, representing the spatial average of actual available green biomass across all patches accounting for grazing reductions, starts at approximately 45,000 units at step zero. It declines rapidly over the first ten to twenty steps, reaching a minimum of approximately 40,000 to 42,000 units between steps twenty and thirty. This initial decline corresponds to the immediate grazing pressure exerted by the initial livestock stocking of ninety units per household on the available vegetation.

Following the trough, the biomass increases steadily from step thirty through step seventy, recovering and eventually exceeding the initial starting value. By step seventy, the metric stabilizes into a plateau phase that persists through step one hundred, with final values ranging between approximately 55,000 and 65,000 units across different simulation realizations. This recovery and stabilization above initial levels suggests that the combination of vegetation regrowth dynamics and adaptive learning-based management strategies—implemented through the E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types—enables the system to achieve a higher sustainable biomass equilibrium than the initial state.

The variation in final biomass levels across simulation runs reflects the influence of stochastic rainfall variability with a mean of 200 and standard deviation of 100, as well as potentially divergent outcomes from the heterogeneous learning behaviors and risk attitudes distributed across the household agents.

For decision makers, the data indicate that while the system demonstrates strong resilience and capacity for recovery to levels exceeding initial conditions, the initial period presents a critical vulnerability where biomass drops by roughly ten to twelve percent. This suggests the need for careful attention to initial stocking rates or supplemental feeding during system establishment phases to prevent overgrazing. Furthermore, the wide divergence in final outcomes emphasizes that environmental stochasticity significantly impacts pasture productivity, reinforcing the importance of maintaining adaptive capacity and flexible management strategies to manage variable climatic conditions in private property grazing systems.
