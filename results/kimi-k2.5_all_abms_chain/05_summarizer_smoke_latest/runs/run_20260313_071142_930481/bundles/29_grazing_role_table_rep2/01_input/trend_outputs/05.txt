The aggregate livestock holdings exhibit a pronounced non-monotonic trajectory characterized by an initial sharp decline followed by a gradual recovery toward a stable equilibrium. The system initiates with 90 animals per household. Over the first 37 years, holdings undergo a steep reduction, reaching a minimum value of 41.0089 animals per household at year 37. This contraction phase features an inflection around year 3, where the rate of decrease moderates from an initial steep slope, and a change in dynamics near year 20 as the decline continues but approaches its lower bound.

Following the minimum at year 37, the system enters a transitional period through approximately year 43 where livestock numbers stabilize without significant directional trend. A second inflection occurs around year 46, marking the onset of a recovery phase that becomes pronounced after year 55. During this recovery period, animal numbers increase steadily but with continuously diminishing marginal gains: rising from the minimum with moderate speed initially (years 44-54), then slowing progressively through years 55-65, 66-76, and 77-87, ultimately approaching an asymptote. By year 100, holdings reach 47.5472 animals per household, converging toward a long-term mean of 48.7946 animals.

This trajectory reflects the adaptation of stocking decisions to ecological constraints under variable rainfall conditions. The initial decline from 90 to 41 animals represents destocking responses to pasture degradation caused by initially excessive grazing pressure. The trough at year 37 indicates the point where reduced stocking allows vegetation recovery, while the subsequent slow recovery toward approximately 48 animals demonstrates how learning—whether through individual experience or observation of neighbors—enables households to cautiously rebuild herds toward a sustainable carrying capacity that accommodates environmental risk and uncertainty. The asymptotic approach to the final value suggests that farmers increasingly stabilize their decisions as they converge on risk-adjusted equilibrium stocking rates.
