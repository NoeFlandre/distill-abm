Model focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making. The average initial risk attitude of social learning households remained constant at 0.0074 across the entire 100-year simulation period. Starting at timestep 0 with this value and persisting unchanged through timestep 100, the metric exhibited zero variance and no directional movement throughout all observed intervals. The mean green biomass initiates at 45,000 units and undergoes a marked decline during the first three decades, reaching a nadir of approximately 42,057 units at year 27. This initial deterioration reflects the impact of initial stocking densities (90 animals per household across 60 stationary farms) on vegetation resources. Destocking rates fell significantly through year 32. In the final quarter, a modest increasing trend emerges between years 66 and 76. This trajectory suggests an initial adjustment phase corresponding to the collision between starting herd sizes of 90 animals per household and private pastures. The trajectory enters a final phase characterized by minimal yet persistent annual destocking at rates near 0.3 animals per year. This prolonged tail suggests continued fine-tuning of herd sizes or ongoing mortality management under stationary conditions, with households approaching terminal herd configurations significantly below initial stocking levels.
