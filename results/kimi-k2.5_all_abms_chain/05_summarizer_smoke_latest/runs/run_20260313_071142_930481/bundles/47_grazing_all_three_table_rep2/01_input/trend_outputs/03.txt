The average reserve biomass across all patches begins at approximately 90,000 units and undergoes an initial decline over the first 26 years, reaching a minimum value of roughly 84,114 units. This early degradation suggests that farmers initially maintain stocking rates that exceed the pasture's regenerative capacity, drawing down vegetation reserves as they navigate uncertainty in the new system. Around year 26, the trajectory shifts dramatically as biomass enters a sustained recovery phase that continues through the remainder of the 100-year simulation. By year 100, reserve biomass climbs to approximately 121,073 units, exceeding initial conditions by over 34 percent. The recovery accelerates notably after year 33, with statistically significant positive trends emerging through successive decades as learning mechanisms enable farmers to better calibrate livestock numbers to pasture conditions. A change point around year 50 marks the transition from recovery to sustained accumulation, where biomass builds upon already improved foundations. For decision makers, these dynamics indicate that early intervention during the initial decades is critical to prevent excessive vegetation loss, as the system demonstrates strong capacity for recovery once appropriate learning strategies take hold. The long-term trajectory suggests that supporting adaptive learning mechanisms ultimately yields pasture conditions superior to original states, providing a foundation for sustainable livestock production.
