The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods. The average deviation from recommended livestock levels begins near zero at approximately 0.007 during the initial years, indicating that households initially maintain stocking rates very close to ecological recommendations. This alignment rapidly deteriorates over the first three decades, with deviation increasing significantly to reach a maximum of 0.38 around year 33. The system settles into a state where households consistently maintain livestock levels approximately 19% above or below optimal recommendations. The average number of livestock destocked per household commences at zero, reflecting initial allocations that precede environmental stress realization. The aggregate livestock inventory per household, inclusive of animals that have been destocked, declines sharply throughout the first two decades. By year 100, biomass stabilizes near 60,500 units, substantially exceeding initial levels despite significant annual rainfall variability. The distribution of healthy livestock among farming households begins in a state of near-perfect equality. Over the century-long simulation, inequality gradually emerges, culminating in a Gini coefficient of approximately 0.073 by year 100. The persistence of minor destocking throughout the century underscores that in environments with high rainfall coefficient of variation, perfect stabilization remains elusive. The number of destocked livestock begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation. This trajectory indicates a continuous process of animal removal from the system. The rate of destocking exhibits distinct temporal phases. This suggests that even after initial adaptation, destocking activities respond to multi-decadal environmental cycles. Adaptive learning strategies are critical for maintaining household persistence in stochastic grazing systems. For policy interventions, promoting adaptive learning strategies is critical, authors say. The authors say reliance on experiential rules without learning mechanisms leads to significant population decline.
