The simulation series reveals a distinct three-phase pattern in birth activity over the 2,312-tick observation period. From initialization through approximately tick 725, the system remains in a dormant reproductive phase with birth activity at or near zero. This quiescent period corresponds to the maturation time required before the initial population of 250 animals can begin reproducing, consistent with the interbirth interval parameter of 730 ticks.

Following the change point at tick 725, the system enters a prolonged growth phase characterized by monotonic increases in birth activity. This phase spans from tick 725 to approximately tick 1,673 and contains four distinct sub-periods of significant positive trend. The strongest growth occurs between ticks 1,392 and 1,623, with a slope of 0.007 births per tick and high significance (tau = 0.784). Earlier growth windows (ticks 696-927 and 928-1,159) show steep increases with tau values of 0.833 and 0.851 respectively, though at lower absolute rates. Throughout this expansion phase, the aggregate metric rises from near-zero levels to a maximum value of 5.6 births per tick at tick 1,673.

Superimposed on this long-term growth trajectory are regular oscillatory fluctuations with a dominant period of approximately 50 ticks. These oscillations are particularly pronounced between ticks 625-690 and 785-810, likely reflecting seasonal environmental forcing given the high seasonality parameter (0.8) creating pulses in resource availability that synchronize reproductive events.

After reaching the peak at tick 1,673, the system undergoes a significant transition into a decline phase marked by two consecutive windows of negative trend (ticks 1,624-1,855 and 1,856-2,087). The birth activity decreases with tau values of -0.495 and -0.453, falling from the maximum of 5.6 to an end value of 1.0 by tick 2,312. This decline suggests that hunting pressure from the 20 human foragers, combined with environmental constraints and energetic costs, has depleted the breeding population below replacement levels, causing the birth rate to collapse despite continued simulation runtime.

Across the 40 replicated simulation runs, substantial inter-run variability is evident, with individual trajectories achieving cumulative birth counts ranging from 0 to 10. The step-wise accumulation patterns visible in individual runs occur at heterogeneous timings, reflecting the stochastic nature of agent survival, movement, and reproduction, though all runs respect the initial delay of approximately 700-800 ticks before the first reproductive events occur.
