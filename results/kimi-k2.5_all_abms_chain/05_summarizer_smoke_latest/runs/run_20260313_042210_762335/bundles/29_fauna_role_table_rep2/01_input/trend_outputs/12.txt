The combined energy available in the female prey population initiates at 12500 units and undergoes a comprehensive collapse to extinction by tick 2311. The trajectory commences with a steep monotonic decline from the initial 12500 units, dropping rapidly to approximately 2200 units by tick 375. This initial phase exhibits the most pronounced rate of depletion in the entire sequence.

Following tick 375, the decline moderates considerably but maintains persistent downward pressure. Between ticks 375 and 875, the trajectory incorporates oscillatory fluctuations with a characteristic periodicity of approximately 63 ticks, producing local peaks around ticks 376–410 where energy levels temporarily stabilize between 2094 and 2232 units. These oscillations superimpose seasonal resource dynamics onto the underlying depletion trend.

The period between ticks 875 and 1400 contains a significant transient recovery: from tick 1160 to 1391, the previously declining trend reverses to exhibit increasing energy accumulation, representing a temporary demographic expansion. However, this recovery phase terminates around tick 1392, followed by renewed decline extending through tick 1855.

A second transient recovery emerges between ticks 1856 and 2087, again interrupting the dominant downward trajectory with significantly increasing energy levels. Despite these intermittent growth periods, the trend following tick 2087 resumes decline toward ultimate extinction. By tick 2310, the final transition to zero occurs, with energy diminishing from residual levels to complete absence.

This pattern—marked by high initial standing energy, rapid early depletion, attenuated decline with superimposed oscillatory dynamics, multiple transient demographic recoveries during intermediate phases, and eventual total collapse—suggests intensified hunting pressure overwhelming the reproductive capacity and energy accumulation of the female population. The oscillatory components likely reflect the strong seasonal forcing in vegetation availability interacting with the 730-tick interbirth interval and continuous forager predation pressure. The transient recoveries indicate brief periods where reproductive output temporarily exceeded harvest rates, though ultimately insufficient to prevent extinction under sustained foraging pressure from 20 hunters operating with food storage capabilities.
