Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change. Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances. Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores. Foragers-movement-cost ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1.0, and distance hunters can perceive and pursue prey is determined by foraging-radius. Two critical behavioral switches control hunter mortality and food storage: imortal-foragers is set to True, meaning hunters do not die from energy depletion, and food-storage allows hunters to accumulate energy surpluses. The number of active human forager bands remains constant throughout the simulation. The cumulative number of postpartum female prey killed begins at zero across all simulation runs. During the initial phase from approximately step 0 to step 500, the metric increases rapidly in a stepwise manner, with most runs accumulating between 60 and 120 kills by step 500. By step 1000, the majority of runs have stabilized at cumulative values ranging from approximately 110 to 125 females killed. The cumulative mortality of megaherbivores begins at zero and exhibits a rapid, concave-downward increase throughout the initial phase of the simulation. The high variance across simulation runs reflects stochastic variation in how individual animals encounter hunters, find forage patches, and survive the multiple mortality sources. Some populations reach zero females by step 400 to 500, while others maintain small populations until beyond step 2000. The rate of decline varies substantially across runs, with the fastest declines reducing populations to fewer than 10 individuals within approximately 400-500 ticks. The rapid initial decline likely reflects intensive hunting pressure on the initially dense population. The total energy contained within the prey population initiates at approximately 25,000 energy units. During the initial 500 time steps, aggregate energy undergoes rapid depletion. Some trajectories decline more gradually, with one notable simulation maintaining energy levels above 10,000 until approximately step 250 before experiencing a rapid collapse between steps 300 and 400.
