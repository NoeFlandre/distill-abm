The number of female animals begins at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation runs, representing roughly half of the initial total ungulate population. From this starting point, there is a consistent and pronounced decline throughout the simulation duration, with all trajectories trending toward extinction.

The decline occurs in three distinct phases. During the initial rapid decline phase (approximately steps 0 to 500), the population drops steeply from 125 to roughly 20 to 40 females, representing the most dramatic reduction in population size. This is followed by a gradual decline phase (approximately steps 500 to 1000), where the rate of decrease slows considerably as numbers fall from approximately 20 to 40 down to near-zero levels. Finally, in the extinction phase (beyond step 1000), most simulation runs reach zero or near-zero female counts between steps 1000 and 1500, though some runs show persistence of very small populations (one to five individuals) extending to approximately step 2200.

While all runs follow the same general declining trajectory, there is notable variation in the rate of decline across simulations. Some populations reach zero females by step 400 to 500, while others maintain small populations until beyond step 2000. This variability suggests stochastic elements in hunter-prey interactions or environmental conditions affecting population persistence.

These trends indicate that under the experimental conditions of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, the combination of human hunting pressure, environmental constraints, and life history parameters results in inevitable population collapse. The rapid initial decline likely reflects intensive hunting pressure on the initially dense population, while the slower subsequent decline represents the prolonged persistence of small, remnant populations. The variation between runs reflects the inherent stochasticity in agent-based interactions, where chance encounters between hunters and prey, along with individual variation in animal reproductive success and survival, create different extinction trajectories even under identical initial conditions.
