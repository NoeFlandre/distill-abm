The combined total energy of female megaherbivores begins at approximately 12,500 energy units across simulation runs, representing the initial population of 250 animals with baseline energy reserves. This metric undergoes a pronounced decline over the course of the simulations, ultimately approaching zero in all runs, indicating complete depletion of the female population segment through hunting mortality and potential starvation.

The trajectory of decline exhibits high variability across replicate simulations during the initial phase. Between steps 0 and 300, most simulations show a steep downward trend, with total female energy dropping to between 2,000 and 4,000 units. However, some trajectories decline more gradually, with one notable simulation maintaining energy levels above 10,000 until approximately step 250 before experiencing a rapid collapse between steps 300 and 400. This variation suggests stochastic differences in initial hunter-prey encounter rates and movement patterns during the early establishment of hunting pressure.

Following the initial rapid decline, a secondary phase occurs between steps 300 and 800, characterized by slower descent rates and intermittent plateaus where energy levels temporarily stabilize or even increase slightly before resuming their downward trend. During this interval, values typically fluctuate between 500 and 2,000 units. These plateaus likely represent periods where remaining females are more difficult for hunters to locate, or brief intervals where energy intake from the highly productive vegetation (regrowth time of 3 ticks) temporarily offsets losses, though the high hunting pressure from 20 immortal foragers with food storage capabilities ultimately prevails.

By step 1,000, the majority of simulations have reduced female energy to below 500 units, with many approaching zero. The final phase, extending from step 1,000 to 2,000, shows the remaining trajectories converging toward complete depletion. The persistence of some simulations until steps 1,500-2,000 indicates that in certain stochastic realizations, small numbers of females evade detection for extended periods, but the sustained predation pressure prevents population recovery.

The complete collapse of female energy reserves across all simulations, despite strong vegetation regrowth and seasonality-driven resource pulses, suggests that hunting mortality constitutes the primary demographic force overwhelming the population. The lack of sustained recovery phases indicates that the interbirth interval of 730 ticks (two years) and the energetic costs of reproduction cannot compensate for the removal rate imposed by the forager population, leading to irreversible population collapse.
