Cumulative mortality among prey populations initiates at zero and accumulates rapidly during the initial 850 time steps, with distinct transitions in accumulation rates occurring at approximately 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850 time steps. During this early phase, the rate of mortality increase is steepest immediately after initialization—exhibiting slopes exceeding 0.34 deaths per tick in the first 231 steps—before gradually moderating to less than 0.02 deaths per tick by the 1159th time step. This pattern suggests an initial population adjustment period where the founding cohort of 250 animals and early offspring experience heightened vulnerability to the combined effects of predation by 20 human foragers and baseline environmental mortality, leading to rapid demographic turnover before density-dependent regulatory mechanisms or behavioral adaptations can stabilize the system.

Between approximately 1160 and 1390 time steps, mortality accumulation enters a stationary phase characterized by no significant trend, indicating a temporary demographic equilibrium where herd recruitment balances losses from hunting and natural senescence. A brief resumption of mortality increase occurs between 1392 and 1623 time steps, though at a substantially reduced rate of approximately 0.003 deaths per tick, followed by a second plateau from roughly 1624 to 1855 time steps. These oscillations likely reflect the interaction of the 730-tick interbirth interval with high seasonality (0.8), creating intermittent cohort recruitment pulses that temporarily offset hunting pressure despite continuous forager activity.

The system reaches its maximum observed cumulative mortality of approximately 240 deaths at 1876 time steps, representing nearly complete turnover of the initial prey population. Subsequently, between 1856 and 2087 time steps, the cumulative death count exhibits a significant declining trend, falling to 235 by the conclusion of the observation period at 2312 time steps while displaying oscillatory behavior with a 63-tick periodicity. This terminal decline in the ensemble mean suggests that simulation runs experiencing catastrophic mortality events concluded earlier than those maintaining viable populations, resulting in a selection effect where only resilient parameter combinations with lower cumulative mortality profiles persist through the final phase.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The steep initial mortality trajectory indicates that prey populations face acute pressure immediately upon hunter introduction, necessitating intervention protocols within the first 500 time steps to prevent rapid demographic collapse. The plateau phases demonstrate that sustainable equilibrium is theoretically achievable under current hunting parameters, but the late-phase decline in cumulative mortality across the simulation ensemble reveals that extinction events are not randomly distributed—they concentrate in specific stochastic outcomes that eliminate high-mortality runs early. This suggests hunting quotas should incorporate conservative safety margins that account for the nonlinear extinction dynamics observed after 1800 time steps, rather than relying on early-phase stability as an indicator of long-term sustainability.
