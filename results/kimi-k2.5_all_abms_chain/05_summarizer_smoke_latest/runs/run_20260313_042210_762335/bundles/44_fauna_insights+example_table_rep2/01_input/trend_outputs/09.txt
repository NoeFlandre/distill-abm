The female megaherbivore population initiates at 125 individuals and undergoes a deterministic decline to extinction by tick 2312. The trajectory exhibits three distinct phases: an initial rapid collapse, a prolonged persistence at critically low densities, and final extinction.

During the initial phase from tick 0 to approximately 475, the population decreases precipitously with steep negative slopes averaging approximately −0.23 individuals per tick, falling rapidly from 125 to below 10 individuals. This sharp attrition likely reflects intense encounter rates with the 20 immortal foragers operating under conditions where lower processing costs for females (3 units versus 6 for males) create preferential hunting pressure on the female demographic.

Between ticks 600 and 875, the dynamics transition to a phase of extreme scarcity characterized by high variability and superimposed oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, consistent with strong environmental seasonality (parameter value 0.8). During this interval, the population oscillates around a median abundance of 1 individual, exhibiting intermittent brief increases to approximately 6.5 individuals around tick 730 and smaller secondary peaks near ticks 803 and 820. These fluctuations correspond to the two-year birth spacing interval (730 ticks), suggesting attempted demographic recovery coinciding with reproductive events, though insufficient to reverse the overall trend.

From tick 875 onward, the population enters a final phase of terminal decline punctuated by only sporadic, transient increases—such as the slight positive trend observed between ticks 1160–1391 and again at 1856–2087—before ultimately collapsing to zero at tick 2311. The change point at tick 2310 marks the final transition to extinction.

For decision makers, these dynamics demonstrate that the specific combination of constant forager pressure, differential processing costs favoring female harvest, and life-history constraints (two-year birth intervals) creates an extinction vortex. The system exhibits a brief window of approximately 475–600 ticks during which sufficient population momentum exists for potential recovery; however, once abundance falls below approximately 10 individuals, the population enters a prolonged but non-viable persistence phase where temporary increases represent demographic stochasticity rather than sustainable rebound. This indicates that conservation or management interventions must be implemented extremely early in the decline trajectory, as the extended low-density period creates a false impression of stability while masking inevitable extinction.
