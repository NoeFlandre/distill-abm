The total energy available to forager bands exhibits a consistent monotonic decline throughout the entire simulation period across all 40 replicated runs. At initialization, the aggregate forager energy begins at approximately 2000 units. During the initial phase spanning roughly the first 500 ticks, the metric undergoes a rapid descent, reaching approximately -5000 units, indicating an immediate and substantial energy deficit as foragers commence hunting activities. Following this initial drop, the decline continues in a roughly linear fashion but with gradually steepening rates. Statistical analysis reveals that the rate of energy loss accelerates over time, with early phases showing slopes around -8.8 to -11 units per tick, while later periods exhibit steeper declines approaching -20 units per tick. By the conclusion of the simulation at tick 2310, the total forager energy reaches approximately -38229 units, representing a substantial accumulated energy debt.

This consistent downward trajectory indicates that within the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model configuration, forager energy expenditure consistently exceeds energy acquisition from hunting returns. The model parameters, which include immortal foragers and enabled food storage, allow this accumulated deficit to persist and grow without forager mortality, effectively tracking the cumulative energy cost of sustained hunting pressure on the megaherbivore population. For decision makers analyzing megafauna extinction scenarios, this pattern suggests that human forager populations operating under these energetic constraints could exert sustained predation pressure independent of their own energy balance, potentially decoupling hunting intensity from immediate resource limitations.
