The simulation series reveals a characteristic pattern of initial vegetation abundance followed by a catastrophic decline and subsequent recovery with persistent oscillations. At initialization, the landscape maintains approximately 33 percent vegetated coverage, consistent with the initial grass proportion parameter. During the first 365 steps (representing one annual cycle), the system exhibits a gradual reduction in green patches, culminating in a severe collapse to approximately 24.9 percent at step 365—the minimum vegetation state recorded across all 40 simulation runs.

Following this nadir, the vegetation percentage demonstrates rapid recovery, returning to approximately 33 percent by step 700. This recovery phase is characterized by high variance between simulation runs, evidenced by the broad dispersion of trajectories during this interval. A secondary, less severe perturbation occurs between steps 700 and 800, where vegetation levels temporarily decline to roughly 29 percent before restabilizing.

Beyond step 1000, the system enters a relatively stable regime where vegetation coverage oscillates narrowly around 32.9–33 percent, with minor periodic downturns occurring approximately every 63 steps. This dominant oscillation period, combined with the strong seasonality parameter (0.8), indicates regular pulsed fluctuations in resource availability throughout the annual cycle. The mean vegetation coverage across the entire simulation period averages 32.66 percent, slightly below the initial allocation, while the median stabilizes at 32.97 percent.

Statistical analysis identifies multiple inflection points at steps 176, 273, 402, 640, 769, 1005, 1134, 1370, 1500, 1576, 1637, 1735, 1864, 2100, and 2217, indicating frequent regime transitions in vegetation change rates corresponding to grazing pressure dynamics and seasonal forcing. Rolling trend analysis reveals consistent increasing trends in vegetation coverage through step 1623, followed by slight decreasing trends in the final simulation phases, though terminal values remain within 0.1 percent of the initial state.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
These results indicate that while the vegetation system possesses strong resilience and recovery capacity due to rapid regrowth parameters (3 ticks), it operates near a critical threshold where herbivore grazing pressure can induce rapid resource depletion. The consistent recovery to baseline levels (>32.9 percent) suggests that the configuration of 250 initial prey and 20 foragers does not lead to irreversible landscape degradation, but managers should monitor for rapid declines indicative of approaching the critical transition point observed at the one-year mark. The 63-step oscillation cycle implies that herbivore populations must regularly adapt to pulsed resource shortages approximately six times per annual cycle, which may stress populations during extended seasonal bottlenecks.
