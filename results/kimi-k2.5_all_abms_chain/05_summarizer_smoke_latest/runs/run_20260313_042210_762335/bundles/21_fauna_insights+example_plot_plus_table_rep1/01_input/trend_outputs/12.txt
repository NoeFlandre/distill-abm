By this terminal point, all biological energy stored in female animals has been depleted, indicating complete demographic collapse.

Decision makers should recognize that under the specified parameter configuration of 20 hunters and 250 initial prey, megaherbivore populations face inevitable extinction despite exhibiting substantial stochastic variation in persistence times. The absence of any simulation achieving stable equilibrium suggests that current hunting pressure and life history constraints (including the two-year birth spacing interval) preclude population viability. Interventions would need to substantially reduce hunter density or enhance reproductive rates to shift the system from deterministic collapse toward sustainable coexistence. The pronounced variability in extinction timing—ranging from fewer than 1,000 steps to over 2,300 steps—also indicates that short-term observations of population stability may misleadingly suggest resilience while masking inevitable long-term decline.
