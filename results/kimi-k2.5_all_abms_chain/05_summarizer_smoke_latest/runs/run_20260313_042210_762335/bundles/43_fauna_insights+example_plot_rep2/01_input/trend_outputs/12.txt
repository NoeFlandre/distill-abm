The combined total energy of the female prey population begins at approximately 12,500 energy units across all simulation runs. An immediate and rapid decline commences from the initial time step, with most runs losing 50-70% of their initial energy within the first 200 time steps. This steep descent continues through approximately step 400-600, by which point the majority of simulation runs have declined to below 2,000 energy units, representing an 80-85% reduction from starting levels.

Substantial variability exists across simulation runs regarding the rate of decline. Some trajectories exhibit extremely rapid collapse, reaching near-zero energy levels by step 300-400, while others demonstrate more gradual reduction, maintaining 8,000-10,000 energy units until approximately step 400 before undergoing sharp decline. This variation suggests that stochastic factors in herd movement, hunter-prey encounter rates, and demographic stochasticity significantly influence extinction trajectories even under identical parameter settings.

A minority of runs exhibit extended persistence characteristics, with populations maintaining low but non-zero energy levels (ranging from 200 to 4,000 units) between steps 600 and 1,000 through intermittent fluctuations. These fluctuations indicate temporary periods where reproduction briefly offsets hunting mortality, or where spatial dynamics allowed herds to temporarily avoid hunter detection. One simulation run persists with minimal energy reserves until approximately step 2,000, while several others maintain small populations until steps 1,400-1,700. By step 2,000, all runs have converged to zero or near-zero energy states.

The universal trajectory toward extinction across all runs, despite starting with 250 prey individuals and reasonable fecundity parameters (birth spacing of 730 ticks), indicates that the combination of 20 immortal hunters, high seasonality (0.8), and the particular cost-benefit structure of hunting (females providing 27 food value with only 3 processing cost) creates an unsustainable predation pressure. The immortality of foragers removes the density-dependent feedback mechanism that might otherwise allow prey populations to recover when hunter energy stores deplete.

For decision makers studying extinction dynamics, these results demonstrate that under conditions of persistent hunting pressure with immortal predators, megaherbivore populations collapse predictably regardless of stochastic variation, though the timing of collapse can vary by a factor of 3-5x depending on random spatial and demographic factors. The brief persistence windows observed in some runs suggest that temporary refugia or demographic "lucky streaks" can delay but not prevent extinction when hunting pressure exceeds sustainable thresholds. Conservation strategies would need to focus on reducing hunter efficiency or increasing prey recruitment significantly above the levels modeled here to prevent guaranteed population collapse.
