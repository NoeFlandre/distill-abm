The cumulative mortality of male ungulates attributable to human hunting exhibits a characteristic saturating trajectory across all simulation replicates, commencing at zero and asymptotically approaching terminal values ranging between approximately 110 and 125 individuals. 

During the initial phase spanning roughly 0 to 400 time steps, the cumulative kill count increases rapidly and approximately linearly, with most simulations accumulating 60 to 80 male kills by step 400. This early period displays considerable heterogeneity in accumulation rates across replicates, reflecting stochastic variation in encounter probabilities between the 20 human foragers and the male ungulate population operating within the foraging radius of 6 patches.

Between approximately steps 400 and 800, the rate of increase decelerates markedly across all trajectories as cumulative values approach 100 to 115 kills. The discrete stepwise pattern becomes increasingly pronounced during this interval, with progressively longer periods elapsing between successive hunting events, indicating reduced encounter rates as male population density declines.

After approximately step 1000, the majority of trajectories flatten completely, indicating effective cessation of male-specific hunting mortality. Final cumulative values converge narrowly between 110 and 125 kills regardless of the specific temporal pathway, consistent with near-total depletion of the initial male cohort given the starting ungulate population of 250 animals and assuming roughly equal sex ratios.

Substantial variation exists in the timing of saturation: some simulations achieve maximum cumulative kills by step 600-800, while others sustain gradual accumulation through step 1500-2000. This temporal heterogeneity likely derives from stochastic differences in herd movement patterns, seasonal vegetation dynamics (seasonality parameter of 0.8), and spatial clustering of hunters and prey across the 211×211 patch landscape.

The stepwise, discrete increments observable throughout all trajectories reflect the agent-based decision-making of foragers operating under dynamic optimal foraging theory, where individual hunters evaluate the net energetic return of pursuing males (food value of 30 minus processing cost of 6) against alternative foraging options. The eventual plateauing at approximately 120 cumulative kills suggests that male ungulates become functionally unavailable for hunting, either through direct depletion or behavioral avoidance, well before the maximum simulation time of 73,000 ticks.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The data indicate that with 20 human foragers operating under the specified energetic and environmental parameters, male megaherbivore populations face deterministic functional extinction within approximately 2,000 daily time steps (5.5 years), with total male mortality approaching 100% of the initial cohort. While ecological stochasticity can delay the timing of collapse by a factor of two or three, the narrow convergence of terminal values across all replicates suggests that population persistence cannot be achieved without intervention to reduce hunter density, constrain foraging radii, or modify the sexual dimorphism parameters that make males preferred targets despite their higher processing costs. Conservation strategies must account for the rapid depletion potential inherent in the 20-hunter scenario, as the window for sustainable coexistence appears to close within the first 1,000 time steps (approximately 2.7 years).
