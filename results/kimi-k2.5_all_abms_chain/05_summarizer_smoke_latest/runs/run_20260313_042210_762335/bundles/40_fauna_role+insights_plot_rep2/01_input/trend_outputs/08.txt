Across all simulation replicates, the male prey population exhibits a consistent deterministic trajectory toward extinction under the current parameter configuration. The population initiates at approximately 125 male animals and undergoes a monotonic decline throughout the temporal domain, reaching zero individuals in all replicates by step 2,200.

The decline occurs in three distinguishable phases. During the initial rapid collapse phase (steps 0-500), the population drops precipitously from approximately 125 to values ranging between 0 and 40, with the majority of replicates clustering between 10 and 25 individuals by step 500. This phase demonstrates the highest variability across replicates, with some populations retaining 60-70 individuals at step 400 while others have already declined to fewer than 10.

The second phase (steps 500-1,000) exhibits a decelerating but continued decline, reducing the population to 0-10 individuals. By step 1,000, approximately half of the replicates have reached zero, while the remaining populations persist at very low densities (1-5 individuals).

The terminal phase (steps 1,000-2,200) involves the gradual extinction of the remaining replicates. Most populations reach zero between steps 1,000 and 1,500, though one replicate persists with low numbers (1-3 individuals) until approximately step 2,200 before final extinction.

This pattern reflects the interaction of several model mechanisms. The configuration of 20 immortal foragers with food storage capabilities maintains constant hunting pressure regardless of prey density or hunter energy state. Sexual dimorphism parameters assign males a higher nutritional value (30) relative to females (27), creating a positive selection pressure for male-targeted hunting despite their higher processing cost (6 versus 3). The strong seasonality parameter (0.8) likely exacerbates vulnerability during resource-constrained periods, while the three-day vegetation regrowth time limits the landscape's carrying capacity recovery rate.

**Implications for decision makers:** The results demonstrate that a hunting pressure of 20 foragers represents an unsustainable exploitation rate for the male demographic under these environmental and biological parameters. The lack of population stabilization at any intermediate density suggests that density-dependent refuge effects are insufficient to counteract hunting mortality. Management interventions would need to either reduce hunter numbers, adjust the sexual dimorphism-related hunting preferences through processing cost modifications, or implement seasonal hunting restrictions to decouple hunting pressure from periods of environmental stress. The immortal hunter configuration creates a worst-case scenario where hunting pressure never abates due to hunter mortality, indicating that realistic hunter mortality might be a critical stabilizing factor requiring investigation.
