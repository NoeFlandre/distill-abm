A steep decline characterizes the first 300 to 500 time steps, with energy decreasing from 25,000 to values between 5,000 and 8,000 in most trajectories. Some replicates exhibit more rapid collapse, reaching minimal energy levels (below 2,000) by step 500, while select trajectories maintain higher energy levels longer, with one sustaining approximately 22,000 energy units until step 300 before undergoing precipitous decline.

Between steps 500 and 1,000, remaining populations continue to decline from intermediate levels toward extirpation. By approximately step 1,000, the majority of simulations reach energy minima near zero. However, substantial stochastic variation exists in the timing of final energy depletion: some populations reach negligible energy by step 600, while residual energy pools of 1,000 to 3,000 units persist in other replicates until steps 1,500 to 2,200.

The decline trajectories exhibit stepwise patterns with intermittent plateaus, particularly noticeable in the 300-800 step range, suggesting temporary periods of population stabilization before renewed decline. Brief increases in energy occasionally interrupt the downward trend in some replicates during this middle phase, indicating possible reproductive pulses or temporary reductions in hunting success.

**Interpretation:** The data indicate that a hunting pressure of 20 immortal foragers with food storage capability systematically depletes an initial population of 250 megaherbivores under high seasonality (0.8) and moderate vegetation regrowth (3 ticks). The deterministic trend toward zero energy across all replicates suggests unsustainable predation pressure relative to population recruitment, particularly given that hunters do not experience mortality from energy depletion and can accumulate surpluses. The stepwise nature of decline likely reflects the interaction between seasonal resource fluctuations and hunting efficiency, with brief stabilizations corresponding to favorable seasonal windows or temporary spatial refuge effects.

**Implications for decision makers:** The universal population collapse across stochastic replicates indicates that current hunting parameters exceed sustainable thresholds, leading to deterministic extinction. Management interventions should prioritize reducing hunter density, limiting foraging radius, or implementing seasonal restrictions to prevent extirpation. The high variability in extinction timing (ranging from approximately step 600 to step 2,200) poses significant monitoring challenges, as short-term population persistence may erroneously suggest stability when collapse is inevitable. Decision makers should account for agent-based stochasticity creating uncertainty in collapse timing even when outcomes are certain, requiring precautionary buffers in harvest quotas and avoiding complacency based on temporary population plateaus observed in the 300-800 step interval.
