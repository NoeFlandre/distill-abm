The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The number of human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation period of 2,312 time steps. The total energy available to all forager bands initiates at 2000 and declines continuously throughout the simulation. This trajectory indicates a sustained energetic deficit where movement and hunting costs consistently exceed energy acquisition from prey. The cumulative number of postpartum females killed initiates at zero and undergoes a rapid accumulation phase during the first 475 steps. After approximately 850 steps, the accumulation stabilizes into an asymptotic plateau, converging toward a maximum value of 119 individuals. This pattern demonstrates that within approximately 1000-1200 steps, hunting pressure effectively removes the majority of female herds from the system. The model’s life history parameters, including the two-year birth spacing and maximum lifespan of approximately 28 years (10220 steps), are based on the parameters of the model's life history. During the initial phase, the population undergoes a rapid collapse from 125 individuals to fewer than 10 males across the majority of simulation runs. The male prey population initiates at 125 individuals and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction. The megaherbivore population initiates at a mean count of 167 individuals across simulation instances (with individual runs initializing with up to 250 individuals), and undergoes a deterministic decline to extinction by step 2,311. The median population across all time steps remains at 1 individual, despite a mean of approximately 16.5, indicating that while some simulation runs maintain small populations for extended periods, the majority of the temporal distribution is skewed toward near-extinction conditions. The combined energy available in the population of female prey animals begins at approximately 16,667 units, representing the initial energetic reserves of the founding female herds. Over the first approximately 375 time steps, this value undergoes a rapid and steep decline, falling to roughly 2,200 units. This initial collapse reflects intense hunting pressure from the forager population combined with the demographic vulnerability of the prey during the early establishment phase. The percentage of vegetated landscape patches begins at 33.0%, consistent with the parameterized initial grass coverage. Within the first annual cycle, the metric undergoes a precipitous decline, reaching a minimum value of 24.9% at tick 365. The system ultimately reaches complete extinction with zero energy at step 2,311.
