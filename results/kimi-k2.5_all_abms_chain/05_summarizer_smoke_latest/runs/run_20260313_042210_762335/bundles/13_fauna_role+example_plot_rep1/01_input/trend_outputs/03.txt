The cumulative count of male ungulates killed begins at zero across all simulation runs. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, the number of males killed increases rapidly, with most simulations accumulating between 70 and 115 kills by step 500. This initial steep accumulation reflects high encounter rates between human foragers and the initially abundant male prey population, compounded by the higher nutritional value assigned to males relative to females.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of increase decelerates substantially across all runs. During this transitional phase, cumulative kill counts rise from their step-500 values to ranges between approximately 100 and 125. The slowing accumulation rate suggests diminishing returns as the male population becomes depleted through sustained hunting pressure combined with natural mortality sources including starvation, senescence, and external predation.

After approximately step 1000, most trajectories stabilize into plateau phases where the cumulative number of males killed increases minimally or ceases entirely. Final cumulative totals vary across simulation runs, ranging from approximately 108 to 125 males killed. Some simulations reach their asymptotic values as early as step 800, while others continue accumulating kills until beyond step 1500, indicating substantial stochastic variation in the temporal dynamics of the hunting process.

The trajectories exhibit discrete stepwise increments rather than smooth curves, consistent with the episodic nature of hunting events in the agent-based framework where kills occur when foragers successfully encounter and subdue individual prey. The variability in final outcomes and the timing of saturation points across runs likely emerges from stochastic differences in spatial configuration of hunters and prey, individual foraging decisions based on dynamic energy optimization, and the interplay between hunting mortality and the demographic constraints imposed by the ungulate life history parameters including interbirth intervals and sexual dimorphism in processing costs.
