The cumulative mortality data reveal a three-phase pattern characterizing the extinction trajectory of the megaherbivore population under persistent hunting pressure. 

**Initial Rapid Mortality Phase (Steps 0–500):** The cumulative death count increases steeply from zero, accumulating approximately 200 to 230 deaths by step 500. This phase exhibits the highest mortality rates observed, with initial slopes exceeding 0.3 deaths per tick and gradually decelerating to roughly 0.17 deaths per tick. The data identify significant shifts in mortality dynamics at steps 125, 250, 375, and 500, marking successive decelerations within this high-mortality period.

**Deceleration Phase (Steps 500–1000):** The rate of mortality accumulation declines markedly after step 500. Between steps 500 and 850—a critical change point identified in the series—the mortality rate drops to approximately 0.06 deaths per tick and further diminishes to roughly 0.017 deaths per tick by step 1000. By this point, the cumulative death count approaches 230 to 235 individuals, representing over 90% of the initial population of 250 animals.

**Asymptotic Stabilization Phase (Steps 1000–2312):** After approximately step 1200, the cumulative mortality curve flattens, showing no significant increasing trend for extended intervals. The aggregate mean reaches a maximum value of approximately 240.3 deaths at step 1876 (equivalent to roughly 5.1 years), after which the series stabilizes with minor fluctuations. By the conclusion of the observation window at step 2312 (approximately 6.3 years), the mean cumulative mortality settles at 235, with a median value of 233 across the 40 simulation runs. The final phase exhibits high variability in the timing of individual deaths across runs (standard deviation of 65), though all trajectories converge toward asymptotic values between 233 and 240 total deaths.

**Interpretation:** The data indicate that the combined pressures of immortal forager agents (who do not die from energy depletion) and environmental stress drive the prey population toward near-extinction within the first five to six years. Given the initial cohort of 250 animals, the asymptotic approach to approximately 235–240 cumulative deaths suggests that the vast majority of the initial population is ultimately harvested or succumbs to natural mortality, with the population suppressed to densities where additional deaths become rare events. The strong seasonal forcing (seasonality = 0.8) and rapid vegetation regrowth (3 ticks) do not prevent this collapse under the energetic and behavioral constraints configured in the model.
