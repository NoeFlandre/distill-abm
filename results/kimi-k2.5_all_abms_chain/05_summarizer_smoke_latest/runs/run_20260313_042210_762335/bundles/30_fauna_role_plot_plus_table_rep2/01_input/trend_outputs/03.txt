Across 80 simulation replicates of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, the cumulative number of male prey killed by forager agents exhibits a characteristic three-phase trajectory. Initial conditions begin at zero males killed. During the first approximately 600 to 850 time steps (ticks), the system undergoes rapid accumulation, with the aggregate mean value increasing steeply as foragers exploit the initial population of 250 ungulates. This phase is marked by change points at regular intervals (125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850 ticks), indicating progressively slowing but still significant increases in hunting success.

Between approximately tick 850 and tick 1200, the rate of increase decelerates substantially as the system approaches saturation. The aggregate mean value reaches approximately 100 males killed by tick 1000, with individual replicates beginning to plateau as local male populations are depleted or become harder to locate. Statistical analysis confirms this transition, with rolling trend tests showing significant increasing trends through tick 1159, followed by a period of no significant trend between ticks 1160 and 1391.

The system reaches its maximum aggregate mean value of approximately 120 males killed at tick 1876. Notably, after this peak, the mean value exhibits a slight decline to approximately 117.5 by the final tick 2312, accompanied by low-amplitude oscillations with a characteristic period of approximately 63 ticks. These oscillations, visible in the latter portion of the time series, likely reflect seasonal hunting patterns synchronized with the environmental seasonality parameter (set at 0.8) or demographic cycles in prey availability.

Throughout the simulation, the final cumulative kill counts vary across replicates, with most simulations plateauing between 110 and 125 males killed. This variation reflects stochastic differences in forager movement, prey distribution, and the energetic constraints governing hunter behavior. The preferential hunting of males, driven by their higher food value (30 units versus 27 for females) despite higher processing costs (6 versus 3), contributes to the eventual depletion of the male segment of the population, leading to the observed plateau in cumulative kills as the available male prey pool becomes exhausted.
