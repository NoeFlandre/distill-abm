The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to the combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change. Unlike traditional approaches that look at populations as uniform groups, this model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances. The model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for the herbivores.

The primary goal is to understand which combinations of hunting pressure, environmental conditions, and animal life history traits lead to population collapse versus stable coexistence. The model specifically incorporates sexual dimorphism—meaning males and females can have different sizes and nutritional values to hunters—along with realistic herd movement patterns and reproductive biology including pregnancy, nursing, and interbirth intervals.

The simulation includes two main types of agents: human foragers and ungulate herds. The human foragers operate according to dynamic optimal foraging theory, meaning they constantly evaluate whether pursuing a particular animal is worth the energy expenditure based on the animal's food value, the difficulty of processing it, and how much energy the hunter currently has stored. The ungulate herds consist of males, females, and nursing females with calves. These animals gain energy from eating grass, lose energy through movement, and face mortality from starvation, age-related senescence, external predation, and human hunting.

The environment itself is dynamic, with grass that can be consumed by herbivores and that regrows according to user-defined parameters. The model can simulate seasonal environments where grass availability fluctuates throughout the year, and users can control the proportion of the landscape that initially contains vegetation as well as how quickly that vegetation returns after being eaten.

The model operates through discrete time steps called "ticks," which can be scaled to represent any temporal unit but are often set to represent days. The simulation can be stopped automatically when either a maximum number of ticks is reached or if the herd population exceeds a certain size, depending on user preferences.

The specific parameters for this experimental configuration are as follows:

**Population Initialization:**
The model begins with init-foragers ranging from 0 to 100, which we have set at 20, representing the initial number of human hunters in the landscape. The ungulate population starts with init-prey ranging from 0 to 1000, set at 250 animals.

**Temporal Settings:**
Time is calibrated so that ticks-per-year ranges from 0 to 720, set at 365 to represent daily time steps. The simulation will run until max-ticks reaches 73,000, with stop-at-max-ticks set to True, meaning the experiment terminates at this temporal limit. The alternative stopping condition, stop-at-max-herd-size, is set to False, allowing the simulation to continue regardless of whether the population reaches the max-herd-size of 500.

**Animal Life History and Biology:**
The ungulates follow realistic life history parameters. Maximum-lifespan ranges from 0 to 12,000 ticks, set at 10,220, while age-of-senescence ranges from 0 to 10,000, set at 7,300, marking when age-related mortality increases. Baseline mortality rates include internal-mortality ranging from 0.0 to 0.2, set at 0.025, representing baseline physiological death, and external-mortality ranging from 0.0 to 0.2, set at 0.0025, simulating predation or other non-human causes. Reproductive timing is controlled by birth-spacing, ranging from 0 to 1,000 ticks, set at 730, representing the interbirth interval. The nutritional value of offspring is determined by food-value-calf, ranging from 0 to 20, set at 3.

**Sexual Dimorphism and Hunting Value:**
The model distinguishes between sexes in terms of hunter payoff. Food-value-males ranges from 5 to 100, set at 30, while food-value-females ranges from 5 to 100, set at 27, representing the energy hunters gain from consuming each sex. The effort required to process carcasses differs by sex, with processing-cost-males ranging from 0 to 20, set at 6, and processing-cost-females ranging from 0 to 20, set at 3, representing the energy expended to butcher and prepare the meat.

**Animal Energetics:**
Ungulate energy dynamics are governed by several parameters. Animals-gain-from-food ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1.5, representing energy obtained from grazing. The threshold for starvation mortality, animals-starvation-threshold, ranges from 0 to 100, set at 25. Movement costs energy, with animals-movement-cost ranging from 0 to 10, set at 1.

**Hunter Behavior and Energetics:**
Human agents follow similar energetic rules. Foragers-movement-cost ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1. The distance hunters can perceive and pursue prey is determined by foraging-radius, ranging from 1 to 100, set at 6. Two critical behavioral switches control hunter mortality and storage: imortal-foragers is set to True, meaning hunters do not die from energy depletion, and food-storage is set to True, allowing hunters to accumulate energy surpluses.

**Environmental Parameters:**
The vegetation dynamics are controlled by several settings. Grass-proportion ranges from 0.0 to 1.0, set at 0.33, determining what fraction of the landscape initially carries vegetation. The speed of vegetation recovery is set by regrowth-time, ranging from 0 to 365, set at 3 ticks. Environmental fluctuation is controlled by seasonality, ranging from 0.0 to 1.0, set at 0.8, representing the intensity of seasonal variation in resource availability. Three boolean switches control vegetation behavior: grass-growth is set to True, enabling vegetation regrowth; keep-initial-grass-pattern is set to True, restricting regrowth to patches that were initially vegetated; and seasonality at 0.8 creates strong seasonal pulses in resource availability.

Throughout this phase, a dominant oscillatory period of 63 ticks manifests, reflecting the strong environmental seasonality (0.8) creating regular pulses of resource availability and deficit.

**Phase Three: Terminal Decline with Transient Recoveries (Ticks 875–2311)**

After tick 875, the trajectory enters a protracted collapse punctuated by two statistically significant but ultimately insufficient recovery periods. Between ticks 1,160 and 1,391 (years 3.2–3.8), the trend briefly reverses to increasing (τ = 0.113, p = 0.011), followed by a second, more pronounced recovery between ticks 1,856 and 2,087 (years 5.1–5.7) with τ = 0.453 (p < 0.001). These intermittent gains likely reflect temporary reductions in hunting pressure or seasonal resource booms that allow partial energy accumulation. However, following the change point at tick 2,310, the population experiences final energy exhaustion.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The trajectory demonstrates that under sustained hunting pressure combined with high environmental seasonality, megaherbivore populations exhibit a false recovery phenomenon: transient energy plateaus and brief positive trends occurring between years 1–6 create the appearance of population resilience while masking an underlying extinction vortex. The 63-tick seasonal cycle generates predictable energy bottlenecks that compound anthropogenic mortality. Conservation strategies must recognize that temporary stabilization in female energy stores—particularly the plateaus observed around 2,100 units—does not indicate sustainable coexistence, but rather a delayed collapse mechanism. Interventions must address both continuous hunting pressure and seasonal resource deficits simultaneously; managing only one factor appears insufficient to prevent the terminal depletion observed by year 6.

The trajectory of aggregate megaherbivore energy reserves exhibits a deterministic collapse pattern punctuated by distinct phases of deceleration and temporary stabilization. 

**Initial Collapse (Years 0–20)**
The system begins with approximately 16,667 units of total animal energy. Within the first two decades, this value undergoes a steep, monotonic decline to roughly one-quarter of initial levels (approximately 3,700–4,100 units). This phase is characterized by the most rapid rate of depletion, suggesting that the initial encounter between human foragers and naive prey populations generates immediate, unsustainable extraction pressure.

**Oscillating Stagnation (Years 25–40)**
Between approximately years 25 and 40, the decline temporarily arrests into an oscillating equilibrium. Energy levels fluctuate rhythmically between approximately 3,700 and 4,100 units, with a dominant oscillation period suggesting cycles of approximately five to six years. These oscillations likely reflect seasonal and reproductive dynamics—the 730-tick (two-year) interbirth interval interacting with strong environmental seasonality (0.8 intensity)—creating pulsed availability of vulnerable cohorts to hunters.

**Secondary Collapse (Years 45–70)**
Following the initial stabilization, the system experiences a second, distinct collapse phase, driving aggregate energy down to approximately 800–1,100 units by year 60–70. This represents a critical transition threshold; once crossed, the population enters a low-density regime where Allee effects and demographic stochasticity dominate over deterministic hunting pressure.

**Extinction Debt and Terminal Fluctuations (Years 75–180)**
The subsequent century-long period exhibits characteristics of an extinction debt. Energy levels persist at marginal values with significantly reduced variance. Notable temporary recoveries occur around years 120–140 and again at 160–180, where brief increasing trends emerge from the low baseline. However, these represent merely transient demographic echoes—likely caused by reduced hunting efficiency on sparse, mobile herds—rather than genuine recovery trajectories. The magnitude of these rebounds remains below 15% of initial energy levels.

**Terminal Extinction (Year 200)**
At the simulation terminus (73,000 ticks), aggregate energy reaches zero, indicating complete population extirpation. The final collapse occurs abruptly after the last temporary recovery fails, suggesting that prolonged low-level persistence ultimately exhausts reproductive potential below the minimum viable population threshold.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The trajectory reveals three critical intervention windows: (1) the immediate post-contact phase where rapid initial decline suggests early hunting limits could prevent the first catastrophic drop; (2) the stagnation phase where oscillating dynamics indicate that seasonal hunting moratoriums might disrupt the synchronization of hunting pressure with reproductive pulses; and (3) the recognition that temporary recoveries during the extinction debt phase (years 75–180) create false signals of population resilience, potentially misleading managers into delaying intervention until irreversible viability loss has occurred. The absence of sustained recovery above critical thresholds demonstrates that once megaherbivore energy reserves fall below approximately 25% of carrying capacity, autonomous rebound becomes impossible under continued foraging pressure.

The vegetation coverage exhibits rapid stabilization to an equilibrium state of approximately 33% of the landscape following initial establishment, with distinct phase transitions occurring at time steps 150, 300, 475, and 725 during the first two simulated years. Thereafter, the system maintains long-term stability around a mean of 32.7%, terminating at essentially the same level (33.0%) after 73,000 time steps (200 simulated years).

An absolute minimum coverage of 24.9% occurs precisely at time step 365, marking an annual resource bottleneck where vegetation availability drops roughly eight percentage points below mean levels before seasonal recovery.

Temporal trend analysis reveals predominantly increasing trajectories through the first 1,600 time steps across sequential observation windows, reflecting establishment dynamics as the vegetation pattern adjusts to initial conditions and herbivore grazing pressures. Subsequently, localized decreasing trends emerge between time steps 1,624 and 2,088, representing phase adjustments within the stable oscillatory cycle rather than indicating long-term degradation.

For decision makers, these findings indicate that despite strong seasonality driving significant intra-annual variation (nearly 25% reduction in available forage at seasonal nadirs), the grassland vegetation demonstrates robust resilience under current megaherbivore grazing and hunter foraging pressures, maintaining consistent baseline coverage over centennial timescales. However, the severe seasonal depletion suggests that megaherbivore populations face periodic nutritional stress that may drive seasonal demographic bottlenecks or range-shift behaviors, while the early transition period (first 725 time steps) represents a critical establishment phase where system dynamics stabilize and should be excluded from short-term trend assessments.
