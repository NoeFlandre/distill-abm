The trajectory of aggregate megaherbivore energy reserves exhibits a deterministic collapse pattern punctuated by distinct phases of deceleration and temporary stabilization. 

**Initial Collapse (Years 0–20)**
The system begins with approximately 16,667 units of total animal energy. Within the first two decades, this value undergoes a steep, monotonic decline to roughly one-quarter of initial levels (approximately 3,700–4,100 units). This phase is characterized by the most rapid rate of depletion, suggesting that the initial encounter between human foragers and naive prey populations generates immediate, unsustainable extraction pressure.

**Oscillating Stagnation (Years 25–40)**
Between approximately years 25 and 40, the decline temporarily arrests into an oscillating equilibrium. Energy levels fluctuate rhythmically between approximately 3,700 and 4,100 units, with a dominant oscillation period suggesting cycles of approximately five to six years. These oscillations likely reflect seasonal and reproductive dynamics—the 730-tick (two-year) interbirth interval interacting with strong environmental seasonality (0.8 intensity)—creating pulsed availability of vulnerable cohorts to hunters.

**Secondary Collapse (Years 45–70)**
Following the initial stabilization, the system experiences a second, distinct collapse phase, driving aggregate energy down to approximately 800–1,100 units by year 60–70. This represents a critical transition threshold; once crossed, the population enters a low-density regime where Allee effects and demographic stochasticity dominate over deterministic hunting pressure.

**Extinction Debt and Terminal Fluctuations (Years 75–180)**
The subsequent century-long period exhibits characteristics of an extinction debt. Energy levels persist at marginal values with significantly reduced variance. Notable temporary recoveries occur around years 120–140 and again at 160–180, where brief increasing trends emerge from the low baseline. However, these represent merely transient demographic echoes—likely caused by reduced hunting efficiency on sparse, mobile herds—rather than genuine recovery trajectories. The magnitude of these rebounds remains below 15% of initial energy levels.

**Terminal Extinction (Year 200)**
At the simulation terminus (73,000 ticks), aggregate energy reaches zero, indicating complete population extirpation. The final collapse occurs abruptly after the last temporary recovery fails, suggesting that prolonged low-level persistence ultimately exhausts reproductive potential below the minimum viable population threshold.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The trajectory reveals three critical intervention windows: (1) the immediate post-contact phase where rapid initial decline suggests early hunting limits could prevent the first catastrophic drop; (2) the stagnation phase where oscillating dynamics indicate that seasonal hunting moratoriums might disrupt the synchronization of hunting pressure with reproductive pulses; and (3) the recognition that temporary recoveries during the extinction debt phase (years 75–180) create false signals of population resilience, potentially misleading managers into delaying intervention until irreversible viability loss has occurred. The absence of sustained recovery above critical thresholds demonstrates that once megaherbivore energy reserves fall below approximately 25% of carrying capacity, autonomous rebound becomes impossible under continued foraging pressure.
