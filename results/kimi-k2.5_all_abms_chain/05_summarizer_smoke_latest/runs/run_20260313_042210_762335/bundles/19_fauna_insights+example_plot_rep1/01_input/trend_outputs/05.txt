Simulations begin with zero postpartum females killed. During the initial phase, spanning approximately the first 500 to 600 steps, the cumulative number of postpartum females killed increases rapidly in all runs, exhibiting a steep accumulation pattern. This phase is characterized by discrete stepwise increments, indicating individual hunting events, with cumulative totals reaching values between approximately 100 and 120 by step 600.

Following this initial rapid accumulation, the rate of increase slows considerably across all runs, entering a plateau phase that typically begins between steps 600 and 1000 and continues for the duration of the simulation. During this plateau phase, the cumulative count of killed postpartum females increases only marginally, with final values at the end of the simulation ranging between approximately 105 and 125 across different runs. Some runs reach their plateau earlier than others, with a few runs stabilizing as early as step 600 while others continue to show slow accumulation until approximately step 1200.

The stepwise pattern observed throughout both phases indicates that hunting events occur stochastically rather than continuously. Variability between simulation runs is low during the initial steps but increases significantly as the simulation progresses, suggesting that stochastic factors in hunting success, herd demographics, or spatial distribution substantially influence outcomes over time. The eventual convergence to relatively similar final values despite initial divergence indicates that the system tends toward comparable equilibrium states regardless of stochastic variation.

These patterns suggest that postpartum females represent a highly vulnerable demographic subject to intensive initial hunting pressure, likely due to their elevated food value from nursing offspring. The rapid transition to a plateau implies either the depletion of available postpartum females, a shift in hunter selectivity as this demographic becomes scarce, or density-dependent limitations on herd reproduction. For decision makers concerned with conservation or sustainable harvesting, these findings indicate that postpartum females face disproportionate risk during early stages of hunting pressure, necessitating protective measures or selective hunting restrictions to prevent rapid depletion of reproductively active females. The variability across runs further underscores the need for robust management strategies that account for uncertainty in population responses rather than relying on deterministic projections.
