Cumulative female mortality in the simulated ungulate population follows a saturating trajectory across all replicates, initiating at zero individuals and asymptotically approaching terminal values between approximately 118 and 125 females killed. The temporal progression proceeds through three distinct phases: an initial lag period spanning roughly the first 100 to 200 steps, during which cumulative mortality accumulates slowly to fewer than 20 individuals; a rapid acceleration phase between steps 200 and 600, characterized by steep increases from approximately 20 kills to between 90 and 115 individuals; and a terminal deceleration phase extending from step 600 through 2000, where the rate of additional female mortality diminishes substantially as values stabilize near plateau levels.

Variability among simulation runs manifests primarily in the velocity of accumulation rather than terminal magnitude. Most replicates reach median cumulative mortality values of 60 females between steps 300 and 450, with subsequent progression to 100 kills occurring by step 500 to 700. One replicate exhibits delayed dynamics, maintaining cumulative mortality below 40 individuals until after step 300 and reaching comparable terminal values only after step 600. The discrete, stepwise nature of these increases reflects episodic hunting events rather than continuous mortality processes. By step 1000, the majority of runs have effectively ceased accumulating additional female deaths, maintaining stable totals despite continued simulation runtime.

Within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these saturation patterns indicate that human foragers operating under dynamic optimal foraging theory exert predation pressure that rapidly eliminates the female cohort from the initial population of 250 ungulates. Given roughly equal sex ratios, terminal mortality values approaching 125 individuals represent near-complete extirpation of the reproductive female segment. The preferential targeting of females emerges despite their marginally lower nutritional value (27 energy units) compared to males (30 energy units), demonstrating that reduced processing costs (3 units versus 6 for males) dominate hunter decision-making, rendering females more efficient prey items on a net energy basis. The rapid saturation within 600 steps (approximately 1.6 simulated years at 365 steps per year) suggests that the combination of energy storage capabilities, immortal hunter populations, and a foraging radius of 6 patches creates predation rates that exceed the demographic replacement capacity of the ungulate population.

For decision makers, these results highlight that prey selectivity driven by processing efficiency rather than gross caloric value can lead to systematic removal of reproductive individuals, accelerating population collapse. Management strategies should prioritize protecting female aggregation zones during high-vulnerability periods (initial 500-600 steps of exposure), as the window for intervention is brief before reproductive capacity becomes critically compromised. Additionally, the consistency of terminal mortality maxima across runs suggests that once hunting pressure initiates, population outcomes are largely deterministic regardless of stochastic variation in early encounter rates, indicating that preventive measures are more effective than reactive conservation attempts in small, isolated megaherbivore populations.
