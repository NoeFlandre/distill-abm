The model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape. Each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for the herbivores. The model specifically incorporates sexual dimorphism. Animals-gain-from-food ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1.5, representing energy obtained from grazing. Threshold for starvation mortality, animals-starvation-threshold, ranges between 0 and 100, set at 1. The distance hunters can perceive and pursue prey is determined by foraging-radius, ranging from 1 to 100. Cumulative number of male animals killed by hunting exhibits a consistent two-phase pattern over the first approximately 2,000 time steps. Trajectories increase rapidly and steeply, accumulating between 100 and 125 male deaths within the first 500 to 1,000 steps, starting from zero mortality at initialization. Terminal mortality values approaching 125 individuals represent near-complete extirpation of the reproductive female segment. The rapid saturation within 600 steps suggests that prey selectivity driven by processing efficiency rather than gross caloric value can lead to systematic removal of reproductive individuals, accelerating population collapse. The data reveals distinct phases in cumulative birth accumulation across simulation trajectories. Around steps 750 to 800, a synchronized onset occurs, with cumulative counts increasing from zero to one across multiple trajectories. A consistent 750-800 step delay before reproductive activity suggests significant maturity or gestational delays. Consistent downward trends suggest that the current combination of 20 human foragers and the specified hunting efficiency creates unsustainable pressure on male megaherbivore populations. Conservation or management strategies should consider reducing hunter numbers, modifying hunting selectivity (given the higher food value placed on males) or implementing seasonal restrictions to prevent the rapid population collapses observed.
