The population of male animals initiated at 125 individuals across all simulation runs, declining to complete extinction (0 individuals) by step 2284 in the final run, with a mean population size of approximately 21 individuals (median: 1.35) across the 2,312 recorded time steps. The decline followed a non-linear trajectory characterized by distinct phases of varying rates of change and substantial stochastic variation in extinction timing between runs.

During the initial phase (approximately steps 0–500), the population experienced a steep monotonic decrease, with aggregate means falling below 25 individuals and many individual runs collapsing to fewer than 10 males. Mann-Kendall trend analysis confirms statistically significant decreasing trends throughout the first 1,391 steps (tau values ranging from -0.475 to -1.000, p<0.001), with the steepest declines occurring between steps 232–463 (slope = -0.244).

Between steps 1,392 and 1,623, the aggregate trend reversed temporarily to a weak but significant increase (tau = 0.435, p<0.001), corresponding to intermittent demographic recoveries where some runs exhibited brief population resurgences, including a peak of approximately 10.5 individuals around step 730. These fluctuations occurred with a periodicity of approximately 63 steps (roughly 0.17 years given the parameter of 365 ticks per year), likely reflecting the interaction between hunting mortality and the birth spacing interval of 730 ticks (2 years). However, these pulses proved insufficient to reverse the overall decline.

From step 1,624 through 2,087, the population stabilized near zero with no significant trend detected (p>0.60), oscillating at low levels (typically fewer than 5 individuals) with decreasing amplitude before final extinction. While the aggregate mean approached zero by step 755, substantial heterogeneity persisted across the 40 simulation runs: some subpopulations collapsed to effective extinction (fewer than 5 males) by step 400–500, whereas others maintained small surviving cohorts until steps 1,500–2,200, demonstrating a wide distribution of extinction times spanning approximately 1,800 steps between the earliest and latest collapses.

The trajectory indicates that under the specified hunting parameters—20 human foragers, male food value of 30, processing cost of 6, and birth spacing of 730 ticks—male megafauna face deterministic extinction despite demographic characteristics including maximum lifespans of 10,220 ticks and senescence beginning at 7,300 ticks. The oscillatory patterns superimposed on the declining trend reflect demographic stochasticity and breeding cycles, yet the population never sustained recovery above replacement levels after the initial decline phase.

For decision makers, these results indicate that current hunting pressure creates irreversible trajectories toward male subpopulation extinction, though the highly variable extinction timelines (ranging from approximately 500 to 2,300 steps) could obscure the severity of decline during early stages if monitoring relies on single-run observations. The temporary increases during steps 1,400–1,600 represent demographic noise rather than recovery capacity. Management interventions would need implementation before step 500—when populations typically decline below 25 individuals—to prevent the establishment of irreversible extinction dynamics, as the model demonstrates no capacity for recovery once populations enter the low-density phase characterized by fewer than 10 males.
