The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The spatial scale is unitless but can be conceptualized relative to daily movement distances of megaherbivores. The model integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Agents include human hunters and sexually dimorphic ungulate herds. Hunters operate under logic approximating dynamic optimal foraging theory, making pursuit decisions based on their current energy status, prey food value, prey density in the landscape, and the costs of pursuit, hunting, and processing. Hunters do not reproduce, expend energy when moving, can die from energy depletion, and can store energy surpluses. Ungulates move as herds with environmental feedback, age over time, reproduce with user-controlled birth spacing intervals, and exhibit increased mortality risk after reaching senescence. Nursing offspring add to the food value of females. The environment consists of grass patches that can be static or dynamic, with optional seasonality limiting regrowth to specific seasonal cycles.

The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. It seeks to determine whether these interactions accelerate extinction, buffer against extinction, or create equilibrium conditions that prevent extinction events. The model allows users to isolate and test specific hypotheses by manipulating variables related to fodder availability, seasonality, relative animal food value, animal fecundity, lifespans, relative hunting danger, and movement energy costs. Experimental protocols involve establishing baselines without hunting or environmental change, then iteratively adding variables such as environmental degradation, hunter population density, sexual dimorphism ( differential food values and processing costs), and life history constraints (interbirth intervals, lifespan) to observe their individual and combined effects on extinction probability, herd demography, herd movement patterns, and hunting selectivity.

However, this alleviation proved insufficient for recovery; rolling trend analyses confirmed statistically significant monotonic decreases throughout all temporal windows, with only negligible and non-sustained increases detected after step 1,392.

The terminal phase, extending from roughly step 875 to 2,310, manifested as an extended low-energy regime where total animal energy fluctuated below 1,000 units, occasionally spiking to local maxima near 4,000 units due to stochastic recruitment events or temporary reductions in hunting pressure, but ultimately trending toward extinction. The high variance observed across replicates (standard deviation 4,360) indicates substantial stochasticity in extinction timing, with some populations persisting longer than others despite identical initial parameters, likely due to random walk effects in herd movements and hunter-prey encounter probabilities.

For decision makers examining megafaunal conservation or management strategies, these results demonstrate that under conditions of moderate hunting pressure (20 foragers), high environmental seasonality, and the specified life history parameters (maximum lifespan 10,220 steps, senescence at 7,300 steps), megaherbivore populations exhibit deterministic extinction trajectories regardless of stochastic variation. The prolonged persistence in the low-energy state suggests that late-stage interventions would face diminishing returns, as populations below critical energy thresholds of approximately 10,000 units lose demographic resilience. The consistent oscillation patterns indicate that reproductive pulsing and environmental seasonality create temporary windows of higher population vigor, but these do not translate into sustainable equilibria when hunting pressure remains constant. Management approaches must therefore prioritize maintaining total population energy above the 10,000-unit threshold observed at steps 300-400, as recovery becomes progressively improbable once populations enter the prolonged decline phase characteristic of steps 500 through 2,000.

Implications for decision makers:
The model demonstrates that grassland systems under megaherbivore grazing pressure exhibit predictable annual vulnerability windows wherein fodder availability contracts by nearly one-quarter. Conservation protocols should prioritize protecting residual vegetation during these cyclic minima, as the rapid regrowth capacity suggests the system can sustain itself if absolute thresholds are maintained. The identification of a 63-tick oscillation pattern provides actionable sub-annual intervention points to buffer against periodic shortages. Nonetheless, the trend reversal toward declining coverage after year four indicates that long-term equilibrium conditions may be metastable; management strategies should incorporate decadal monitoring to detect whether the observed decreasing trajectory accelerates into a regime shift or stabilizes at a new, lower carrying capacity.
