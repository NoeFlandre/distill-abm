The total energy of male animals initiates at 12,500 units and declines to zero by the conclusion of the simulation period. The depletion follows a nonlinear trajectory characterized by distinct phases demarcated by change points at steps 125, 350, 500, 625, and 875.  

During the initial 125 steps, energy decreases from 12,500 to approximately 11,600 units, representing a relatively modest initial decline. This is followed by a rapid depletion phase between steps 125 and 350, where energy plummets below 4,000 units, with temporary stabilization and minor fluctuations around 3,900–4,000 units observed between steps 376 and 408. This steep decline reflects intensive hunting pressure on the male population, which offers higher nutritional value (30 units) to human foragers compared to females.  

Between steps 350 and 500, the rate of decline moderates considerably as energy falls from approximately 4,000 to 2,000 units. The progression from step 500 to 875 shows continued but decelerating depletion, with energy diminishing from roughly 2,000 to below 1,000 units. After step 875, the decline becomes increasingly gradual, approaching zero asymptotically.  

Considerable variability exists across the 40 simulation runs regarding the timing of complete energy depletion. While the aggregate mean reaches zero at step 2,284, individual trajectories terminate at disparate points spanning from approximately 1,000 to 2,300 steps. This heterogeneity suggests that stochastic factors, including the spatial distribution of vegetation (initial grass proportion of 0.33 with regrowth time of 3 ticks), hunter movement patterns, and demographic fluctuations, create divergent population collapse dynamics.  

Superimposed on the long-term declining trend are oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 steps, particularly pronounced during the first 400 steps. These periodic fluctuations likely correspond to seasonal vegetation cycles (seasonality parameter of 0.8 with 365 ticks per year) influencing animal energy acquisition or pulsed hunting patterns.  

The distribution of energy values exhibits high positive skew, with a mean of 1,984 and a median of 135. This substantial discrepancy indicates that for the majority of the simulation duration, male animal energy reserves persist at minimal levels once the initial depletion phase concludes, with only occasional temporary recoveries or slower decline periods in certain runs maintaining higher values for extended durations. The rolling trend analysis confirms predominantly decreasing dynamics throughout most windows, with brief periods of nonsignificant or slightly increasing trends in later stages corresponding to the final depletion of scattered remaining individuals.
