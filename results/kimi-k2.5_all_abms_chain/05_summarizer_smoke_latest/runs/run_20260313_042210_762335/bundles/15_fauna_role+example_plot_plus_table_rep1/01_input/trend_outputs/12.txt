The total energy of female animals initiates at 12,500 units and declines to zero by step 2,312, traversing through multiple phases of decelerating and accelerating decrease punctuated by temporary stabilization periods.

The initial phase spanning steps 0 to 375 exhibits the steepest descent, with energy levels plummeting from the maximum value to approximately 2,232 units. This rapid depletion corresponds to the initial encounter between human foragers and the founding prey population, where hunting success is highest and processing costs are readily offset by energetic gains.

Between steps 375 and 875, the decline continues but at attenuated rates, characterized by significant oscillations with a dominant period of roughly 63 steps. These fluctuations likely reflect seasonal pulsing in resource availability synchronized with the environmental seasonality parameter, creating periodic opportunities for energy maintenance that are insufficient to reverse the overall negative trend.

Two brief intervals of marginal energy increase appear between steps 1,160–1,391 and 1,856–2,087, where the population experiences temporary energetic gains despite the overarching collapse trajectory. These transient recoveries may correspond to reproductive events given the interbirth interval of 730 steps, allowing surviving females to contribute to cohort replacement, or to temporary reductions in hunting pressure as predator and prey spatial distributions fluctuate.

Following step 2,000, the energy approaches zero asymptotically, with the final decline to extinction occurring by step 2,312. The substantial difference between the mean value of 1,569 and median of 100 across the simulation duration indicates that the population spends the majority of the temporal trajectory at severely depleted energy levels, with the high mean driven exclusively by the initial 400 steps before hunting pressure drives the system toward collapse.

This pattern suggests that under the configured hunting pressure of 20 foragers with energy storage capabilities and immortal status, combined with high environmental seasonality and limited grass coverage (33% of the landscape), the megaherbivore population cannot achieve energetic equilibrium, leading to deterministic extinction through the progressive removal of reproductive females from the system.
