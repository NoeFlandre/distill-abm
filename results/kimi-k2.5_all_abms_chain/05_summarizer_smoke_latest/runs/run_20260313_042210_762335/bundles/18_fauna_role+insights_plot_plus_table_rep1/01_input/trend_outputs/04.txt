The cumulative mortality of female megaherbivores follows a saturating trajectory characterized by three distinct phases. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, mortality accumulates rapidly from zero, with the rate of increase being highest in this period. This rapid accumulation transitions into a deceleration phase between approximately 500 and 1200 time steps, where the rate of increase slows progressively as indicated by inflection points occurring between steps 1200 and 1800.

By approximately step 1200, the accumulation approaches saturation, reaching a maximum value of approximately 123.7 individuals at step 1826. In the final phase from step 1826 to 2312, the cumulative count exhibits minor fluctuations around a plateau of approximately 123 individuals, with a slight declining trend detectable in the terminal window.

Given the initial population of 250 prey animals and the final cumulative mortality of approximately 123 females, the trajectory indicates near-complete depletion of the female segment of the population. The saturating pattern suggests that hunting pressure remains sustained throughout the simulation, but the availability of female prey becomes limiting as the population is depleted. The oscillations observed in the terminal phase, with a dominant period of approximately 63 time steps, likely reflect seasonal dynamics in a depleted population where limited recruitment allows for intermittent hunting opportunities.

For decision makers, these results demonstrate that a forager population of 20 individuals, operating under the specified energetic and behavioral parameters, generates sufficient hunting pressure to effectively eliminate the female breeding population within approximately 1200-1800 time steps (roughly 3.3 to 4.9 years given the 365 ticks-per-year calibration). This level of mortality is unsustainable for population persistence and would result in functional extinction of the megaherbivore population. Management interventions should focus on reducing hunter density, implementing seasonal hunting restrictions aligned with the 63-tick periodicity, or establishing spatial refugia to protect reproductive females and prevent demographic collapse.
