A critical system shock occurs at time step 365, coinciding with the completion of the first annual cycle given the temporal calibration of 365 ticks per year. At this juncture, vegetation coverage plummets to its absolute minimum of 24.91%, representing a 25.7% reduction from baseline conditions. This collapse reflects the convergence of strong seasonality parameters (0.8 intensity) with consumption pressures from the ungulate population. Following this nadir, the system enters a recovery phase, with vegetation coverage rebounding toward baseline levels within approximately 100-200 time steps as regrowth mechanisms (operating on 3-tick cycles) restore vegetated patches.

Subsequent annual cycles generate repeating patterns of decline and recovery, though with attenuated magnitude compared to the initial year. The statistical analysis identifies change points at time steps 150, 300, 475, 725, and 2310, marking transitions between distinct vegetation dynamic regimes. Oscillation analysis reveals a dominant periodicity of 63 time steps, suggesting sub-annual cycles likely driven by the interaction between rapid vegetation regrowth (3-tick recovery) and continuous herbivore consumption.

This pattern suggests the system gradually approaches a dynamic steady state characterized by seasonal oscillations around the 33% vegetation coverage level.

Decision makers should recognize that the vegetation subsystem demonstrates strong resilience, maintaining long-term stability despite severe seasonal bottlenecks. The 63-tick sub-annual oscillation suggests management interventions targeting vegetation recovery rates (currently 3 ticks) could effectively buffer against extreme coverage fluctuations. The maintenance of vegetation near 33% coverage across 73,000 time steps indicates the grassland ecosystem possesses substantial buffering capacity against the combined pressures of megaherbivore grazing and anthropogenic hunting, provided regrowth mechanisms remain functional.
