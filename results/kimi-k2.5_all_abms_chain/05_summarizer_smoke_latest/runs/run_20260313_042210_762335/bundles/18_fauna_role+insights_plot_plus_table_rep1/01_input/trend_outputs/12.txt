The aggregate mean of 40 simulation series reveals a deterministic trajectory of energetic collapse in female megaherbivore populations under combined hunting and environmental pressure. The system initializes with high total energy reserves, approximately 12,500 units at tick 0, indicating a robust starting population with substantial accumulated biomass.

The decline follows a multi-phasic pattern characterized by initially rapid depletion followed by decelerating exhaustion. During the first approximately 375 ticks, the population experiences steep energetic loss, with the rate of decline moderating briefly around tick 299 before continuing downward. This primary phase transitions into an intermediate period (roughly ticks 375 to 875) marked by increased volatility, including minor oscillatory peaks between ticks 376 and 410 where residual energy temporarily fluctuates between approximately 2,100 and 2,200 units before resuming decline.

Change point analysis identifies critical transitions at ticks 375, 500, 625, and 875, with the final collapse phase extending from approximately tick 875 to the terminal point at tick 2,311. Throughout this final phase, energy approaches asymptotically toward zero with decreasing marginal rates of depletion. For decision makers, these findings indicate that female megaherbivore populations under the specified parameter configuration exhibit no stable equilibrium state; instead, they follow a predictable collapse trajectory from initial conditions to complete energetic exhaustion within approximately 2,300 time steps. The rapid initial decline phase (first 375 ticks) represents a critical window for potential intervention, after which population energy reserves enter irreversible depletion dynamics.
