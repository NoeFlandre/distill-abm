The cumulative number of male ungulates harvested by human foragers begins at zero and increases rapidly during the initial phase of the simulations. Across all runs, the kill count rises steeply through approximately the first 500 time steps, reaching values between 60 and 110 individuals by this point. This initial phase represents a period of high hunting efficiency where foragers encounter and target male prey due to their higher food value relative to processing costs.

Between 500 and 1,000 time steps, the rate of accumulation decreases substantially for most simulation runs, with the cumulative count reaching approximately 100 to 125 males killed. The trajectories begin to diverge during this middle phase, with some runs continuing to accumulate kills steadily while others approach saturation levels more rapidly, suggesting high stochasticity in hunter-prey encounter rates or spatial distribution patterns across replicates.

After approximately 1,000 time steps, the majority of runs exhibit a plateau, with the cumulative kill count stabilizing between 110 and 125 individuals and remaining constant until the simulation terminates. Individual runs cease accumulation at different times, with termination points ranging from roughly 1,000 steps to over 2,000 steps. This stabilization indicates the functional depletion of the male population from the harvestable pool, as the remaining males either become too sparse to encounter reliably or the population collapses below viable hunting thresholds.

The final cumulative values cluster narrowly around 120 individuals across most replicates, with limited variance in total harvest size despite variation in the time required to reach these totals. This convergence suggests that the initial male population size and the foragers' optimal foraging strategies lead to a consistent maximum cumulative harvest regardless of stochastic encounter patterns, effectively exhausting the male subpopulation within the first several years of the simulation period.

**Implications for decision makers:** The rapid saturation of male harvests within approximately 1,000 to 2,000 time steps (roughly 3 to 5.5 years given the daily time step calibration) indicates that sexual dimorphism in hunting value creates intense selective pressure on males, potentially leading to severe sex-ratio imbalances or functional male extinction within short time horizons. Conservation strategies should account for the vulnerability of higher-value prey categories, as optimal foraging behavior drives rapid depletion of preferred targets before alternative prey categories are substantially impacted. The consistency of total harvests across runs suggests that stochastic environmental factors provide limited buffer against deterministic outcomes when forager populations maintain consistent energetic requirements and hunting efficiencies.
