The female prey population initiated at 125 individuals and followed a deterministic trajectory toward extinction over the course of the simulation, reaching zero females by approximately tick 2311. The decline exhibited distinct phases rather than a uniform decrease.

An initial rapid collapse characterized the first phase, with the population falling sharply through approximately tick 475 to 600, where the first major behavioral shifts in the decline rate occurred. By tick 875, the population had diminished substantially from its initial abundance, entering a prolonged phase of fluctuation at low densities. Throughout the middle period, roughly between ticks 1160 and 1391, the population exhibited temporary stabilization and slight increases, though these recoveries remained orders of magnitude below starting levels. A similar modest resurgence occurred between ticks 1856 and 2087, representing oscillatory dynamics with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks that persisted throughout the simulation.

Despite these intermittent fluctuations, the overarching trend remained monotonically decreasing across all analysis windows, with strong negative trends in early periods gradually attenuating but never reversing. The oscillations at low population densities—ranging between peaks of approximately 6.5 individuals early in the decline to less than 2 individuals in later periods—proved insufficient to prevent ultimate extinction.

In the context of megafaunal hunting pressure, these dynamics suggest that the combination of 20 active foragers, processing costs favoring female harvest (lower cost of 3 versus 6 for males), and life history constraints including 730-tick birth spacing and senescence beginning at tick 7300, created a predation pressure that overwhelmed the prey population's reproductive capacity. The temporary recoveries observed at various intervals represent demographic echoes or reduced hunting efficiency at very low densities, but critically, these rebounds never approached viability thresholds necessary for population persistence.

For decision makers examining conservation or management strategies, the trajectory indicates that intervention must occur early—specifically within the first 475 to 600 time units—before the population enters the irreversible decline phase characterized by low-density oscillations. Once populations fall below approximately 6 individuals, recovery becomes structurally unlikely under continued hunting pressure, even when that pressure is inconsistent or sporadic. The 63-tick oscillation cycle suggests that population surveys conducted at intervals shorter than this period may capture misleading snapshots of either temporary abundance or scarcity, potentially obscuring the deterministic extinction trajectory.
