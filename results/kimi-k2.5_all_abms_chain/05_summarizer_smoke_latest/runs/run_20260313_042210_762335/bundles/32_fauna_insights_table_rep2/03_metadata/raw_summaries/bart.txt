 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction . It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges . The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics .  The number of human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation period, from the initial time step through all 2,312 recorded observations . This absolute stability reflects the parametric configuration where foragers are immortal (imortal-foragers = True) and do not reproduce .  The cumulative mortality of female prey exhibits a four-phase trajectory characterized by rapid initial accumulation, progressive deceleration, a brief terminal resurgence, and ultimate stabilization . The system exhibits a clear saturation threshold near 120 cumulative male kills, beyond which additional hunting effort yields diminishing returns . Decision makers should recognize that initial hunting efficiency is not sustainable long-term . Intervention strategies must be implemented during the initial rapid accumulation phase to prevent irreversible demographic collapse .  The system exhibited an initial establishment phase extending approximately to tick 725, characterized by accelerating accumulation punctuated by inflection points at ticks 613 and 733 that signaled shifting reproductive rates . Between ticks 696 and 1623, the population experienced sustained significant growth, with particularly steep monotonic increases occurring from ticks 928 to 1159 and 1392 to 1623 . Subsequently, the system entered a prolonged decline phase .  The male megaherbivore population initiated at 125 individuals and underwent a pronounced, sustained decline to extinction over the simulation period . The trajectory was characterized by three distinct phases: an initial steep decline, a brief period of modest recovery, and a final terminal decline to zero . Management strategies must prioritize reducing hunter numbers or encounter rates well before populations decline to critically low levels .  The collapse unfolds through distinct phases of the first 875 ticks, characterized by strong monotonic decreases across consecutive intervals . During this phase, the population plummets from its initial density to substantially lower levels, falling to approximately 11 individuals by tick 730 (year 2) and establishing a trajectory of severe depletion .  The combined energy available in female ungulate populations initiated at 12,500 units and followed a trajectory toward complete depletion by tick 2,311 . The dynamics exhibited marked non-linearity across four distinct temporal phases . Decision makers should prioritize reducing hunter density below the critical threshold of 20 agents or adjusting the processing cost/food value ratio to decrease hunting efficiency on males .  The trajectory indicates that moderate hunter densities with immortal foraging populations and high environmental seasonality (0.8) generate relentless predation pressure that drives megaherbivore populations through a predictable sequence of rapid initial collapse, followed by prolonged decline punctuated by temporary, non-sustainable demographic recoveries . The severity of the initial 75-percent biomass crash within the first year suggests that management strategies must prioritize the critical initial window .
