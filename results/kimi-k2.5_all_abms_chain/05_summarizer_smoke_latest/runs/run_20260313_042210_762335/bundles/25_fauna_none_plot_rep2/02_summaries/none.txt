The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The spatial scale is unitless but can be conceptualized relative to daily movement distances of megaherbivores. The model integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Agents include human hunters and sexually dimorphic ungulate herds. Hunters operate under logic approximating dynamic optimal foraging theory, making pursuit decisions based on their current energy status, prey food value, prey density in the landscape, and the costs of pursuit, hunting, and processing. Hunters do not reproduce, expend energy when moving, can die from energy depletion, and can store energy surpluses. Ungulates move as herds with environmental feedback, age over time, reproduce with user-controlled birth spacing intervals, and exhibit increased mortality risk after reaching senescence. Nursing offspring add to the food value of females. The environment consists of grass patches that can be static or dynamic, with optional seasonality limiting regrowth to specific seasonal cycles.

The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. It seeks to determine whether these interactions accelerate extinction, buffer against extinction, or create equilibrium conditions that prevent extinction events. The model allows users to isolate and test specific hypotheses by manipulating variables related to fodder availability, seasonality, relative animal food value, animal fecundity, lifespans, relative hunting danger, and movement energy costs. Experimental protocols involve establishing baselines without hunting or environmental change, then iteratively adding variables such as environmental degradation, hunter population density, sexual dimorphism ( differential food values and processing costs), and life history constraints (interbirth intervals, lifespan) to observe their individual and combined effects on extinction probability, herd demography, herd movement patterns, and hunting selectivity.

The number of active hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation period. From initialization at step 0 through approximately step 2200, there is no observable fluctuation, increase, or decrease in the hunter population. This stability reflects the model parameter configuration in which foragers are immortal (imortal-foragers set to True) and do not reproduce. With the initial forager count (init-foragers) set to 20 and no mechanisms for population change—neither mortality from energy depletion nor recruitment through reproduction—the hunter population maintains this baseline value continuously. Consequently, hunting pressure remains constant on a per-capita basis throughout the observed timeframe, eliminating demographic stochasticity or density-dependent hunter population dynamics as variables affecting the extinction dynamics under investigation.

Total energy of forager bands begins at approximately 2,000 units and exhibits a biphasic decline across all simulation replicates. During the first 500 steps, energy decreases rapidly to values ranging between -3,000 and -6,000, with substantial stochastic variation across runs indicating differential hunting success or energy acquisition rates. 

Following step 500, the decline becomes strictly linear and deterministic, with aggregate energy decreasing at a constant rate of approximately 20 units per step. By step 1,000, energy reaches approximately -12,500; by step 1,500, approximately -20,000; and by step 2,000, approximately -30,000. Most simulations terminate near step 2,100 with values around -32,000, though one replicate continues declining to approximately -38,000 by step 2,200.

This pattern indicates that after an initial period of approximately 500 steps, energy gains from hunting effectively cease, consistent with prey depletion or extinction events. Thereafter, the 20 immortal foragers expend energy solely through movement at the configured cost of 1 unit per individual per step, producing the observed deterministic linear decay with a slope of -20. The convergence of trajectories after step 500 demonstrates that regardless of initial hunting success variability, all replicates reach a uniform state where energy depletion is governed exclusively by fixed movement costs and the constant forager population size.

Across all simulation replicates, the cumulative number of male prey killed increases monotonically over time, beginning at zero and generally approaching asymptotic values between approximately 100 and 125 individuals. The data exhibit a consistent temporal pattern characterized by rapid initial accumulation followed by stabilization. During the initial phase, typically spanning from the start of the simulation through approximately 500 to 800 steps, the kill count increases rapidly, indicating intensive hunting activity targeting male prey. Following this period, the rate of increase diminishes substantially, with most replicates showing slowed accumulation between steps 1000 and 1500 as the cumulative kills approach their maximum values.

Considerable variation exists among individual replicates regarding both the timing of kills and final cumulative totals. Some replicates stabilize earlier, achieving maximum values around 105 to 110 males killed, while others continue accumulating kills until later steps, ultimately reaching totals exceeding 120. This variability suggests differential dynamics in hunting pressure, prey availability, or forager decision-making across replicates. The eventual cessation of accumulation implies that either the male prey population has been depleted to levels where further hunting is no longer energetically viable, or that hunters have shifted to alternative foraging strategies due to declining returns.

The asymptotic plateau at approximately 120-125 cumulative deaths corresponds to the near-complete depletion of the female segment of the prey population, consistent with an initial prey population of 250 individuals and approximately equal sex ratios. The saturation of the mortality counts indicates that foragers hunt females to effective extinction, after which continued accumulation of female kills ceases. The heterogeneity in the timing of saturation reflects stochastic variation in encounter rates and foraging decisions, while the convergence of all runs to a narrow range of final values suggests a deterministic limit imposed by the initial female population size.

The step-wise pattern of increases at irregular intervals thereafter reflects discrete reproductive episodes consistent with the specified birth-spacing constraints, while the divergence in cumulative totals indicates stochastic variation in herd survival and reproductive success under hunting pressure and environmental conditions.
