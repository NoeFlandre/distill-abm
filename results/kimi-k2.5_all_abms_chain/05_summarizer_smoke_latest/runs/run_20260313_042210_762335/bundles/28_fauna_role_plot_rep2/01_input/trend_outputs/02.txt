The total energy of forager bands exhibits a continuous decline from initial positive values approximately ranging between 1,500 and 2,500 units down to negative values exceeding -30,000 units over the course of 2,000 to 2,200 time steps. During the initial 500 steps, substantial variation emerges across simulation replicates, with some trajectories declining rapidly to approximately -3,000 to -4,000 units by step 300–400, while others maintain energies near zero or slightly positive through step 500. This divergence resolves by approximately step 500 to 750, after which all runs converge to follow a tightly clustered linear trajectory characterized by a consistent depletion rate of roughly 15 to 18 energy units per step.

By step 1,000, the total energy reaches approximately -12,000 to -13,000 units, declining further to between -30,000 and -32,000 by step 2,000. A subset of simulation runs extends to approximately step 2,200, where values approach -38,000 units.

Given the model configuration where foragers possess immortality against starvation yet operate under food storage constraints, these dynamics indicate a systematic energetic deficit wherein movement costs consistently exceed caloric returns from hunting. The initial variability likely reflects stochastic differences in early hunting success and prey encounter rates, while the subsequent linear convergence suggests the system stabilizes into a steady-state resource extraction inefficiency. The persistent negative slope implies that forager bands operate at a net energy loss of approximately 0.8 to 0.9 units per hunter per step, substantially below the break-even threshold needed to offset the baseline per-step movement cost of 1 unit per forager.
