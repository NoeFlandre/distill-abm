The simulations consistently initialize with exactly 250 prey animals. Across all independent runs, the total population exhibits a deterministic downward trajectory from this starting value, ultimately collapsing to zero or near-zero levels, though the temporal dynamics vary substantially between replicates.

During the initial interval from step 0 to approximately step 500, populations undergo rapid decline. Most trajectories descend steeply, with numerous simulations falling below 100 individuals within the first 200-300 steps and reaching fewer than 50 animals by the 400-500 step mark. However, substantial variation characterizes this phase: several runs maintain populations exceeding 150 individuals at step 500, while others have already collapsed to zero or minimal levels (below 10 individuals) by this point.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of decline decelerates for populations that persist. During this middle phase, surviving populations typically fluctuate at low densities, generally ranging between 0 and 25 individuals. By step 1000, the majority of simulations register fewer than 10 animals, with many having reached complete extinction.

In the extended temporal domain beyond step 1000, a minority of trajectories continue with small remnant populations, typically sustaining 1-5 individuals for prolonged periods. These rare persistent runs occasionally exhibit brief plateau phases or minor fluctuations before eventual elimination, with some populations surviving until approximately 1500-2200 steps before terminal collapse.

This extinction pattern reflects the interaction of constant anthropogenic pressure and environmental constraints within the model configuration. The presence of 20 immortal foragers employing optimal foraging strategies with food storage capabilities creates unrelenting predation pressure that does not diminish as prey populations decline. Combined with high environmental seasonality (0.8) restricting resource availability and modest initial vegetation coverage (33%), these parameters generate conditions where prey recruitment via reproduction (constrained by interbirth intervals of 730 ticks and energetic costs) cannot compensate for mortality from hunting, starvation, and baseline physiological death. The variation in extinction timing across runs demonstrates stochastic elements in agent movement and encounter rates, while the consistent long-term outcome of population collapse indicates deterministic unsustainability under this parameter regime.
