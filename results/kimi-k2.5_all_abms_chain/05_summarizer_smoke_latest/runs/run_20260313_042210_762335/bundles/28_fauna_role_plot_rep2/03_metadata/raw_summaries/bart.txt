 Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change . Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances . Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores .  The number of human forager agents remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation period . The total energy of forager bands exhibits a continuous decline from initial positive values approximately ranging between 1,500 and 2,500 units down to negative values exceeding -30,000 units over 2,000 to 2,200 time steps .  During the initial phase (approximately steps 0–500), trajectories manifest rapid, approximately linear accumulation with rates varying considerably between replicate simulations . Most records accumulate between 80 and 120 female kills by step 500, though individual simulations range from approximately 60 to 120 at this temporal benchmark .  Around step 800, multiple trajectories register their first birth events, with values jumping from 0 to 1 or 2 births . Subsequently, the trajectories diverge in their accumulation rates . Some paths demonstrate rapid successive increases, reaching plateaus at intermediate values such as 2, 4, or 5 births before experiencing further increments .  Terminal extinction or near-zero abundanceances (0–2 females) was achieved in all runs by 2,000 time steps . The presence of 20 immortal foragers employing optimal foraging strategies with food storage capabilities creates unrelenting predation pressure . High environmental seasonality (0.8) restricting resource availability and modest initial vegetation coverage (33%) generate conditions where prey recruitment via reproduction cannot compensate for mortality from hunting, starvation, and baseline physiological death .  The total energy contained within the female ungulate population exhibits a consistent trajectory of collapse from initial values of approximately 12,000-12,500 energy units down to effective extinction (values near zero) over the course of roughly 500 to 2,000 time steps . The terminal state across all replicates is the complete or near-complete depletion of total prey energy .  The rapidity of this decline, occurring over roughly 50-100 time steps, followed by an equally rapid recovery to 32.5-33% by approximately step 400, indicates intense but temporary grazing pressure . Between steps 400 and 700, vegetation cover gradually increases and stabilizes near the 33% maximum .
