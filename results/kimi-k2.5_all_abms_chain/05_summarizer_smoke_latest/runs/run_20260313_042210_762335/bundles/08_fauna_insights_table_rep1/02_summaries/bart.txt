The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remained constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation period. This stability reflects the model configuration wherein hunter agents are immortal and do not reproduce, resulting in a fixed population size regardless of environmental conditions, energy dynamics, or prey availability. The fixed hunter population isolates the effect of constant hunting pressure on megaherbivore extinction dynamics. The cumulative mortality of female megaherbivores exhibits a sigmoid accumulation pattern characterized by rapid initial growth, progressive deceleration, an extended stagnation period, a brief resurgence, and ultimate stabilization. The temporal dynamics reveal distinct phases of hunting pressure modulation across the simulation horizon. The cumulative mortality of postpartum female ungulates exhibits a saturating growth pattern over the simulation period, characterized by three distinct phases: an initial period of rapid accumulation, followed by progressive deceleration, and ultimately stabilization around an asymptotic maximum of approximately 117 to 119 individuals. The accumulation rate is highest during this period, indicating immediate and intensive exploitation of available reproductive females.
