The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the simulation, from step 0 through step 2200 and beyond. The total energy available to forager bands exhibits a consistent downward trajectory across all simulation runs, declining from an initial value of approximately 2000 energy units at step zero to values between -32,000 and -38,000 energy units by the conclusion of the simulations. The rate of accumulation varies across runs, with some showing rapid early increases and others exhibiting more gradual but steady accumulation. After step 500, the rate of increase diminishes substantially for all trajectories. The saturation at levels approaching 125 - which corresponds to roughly half the initial prey population of 250 - indicates hunters eventually kill most or all available females. The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model shows that hunting pressure by 20 foragers drives the prey population to extinction. Under the specified parameters, 20 human foragers target sexually dimorphic prey with males possessing higher food value (30) and higher processing cost (6) compared to females. The total energy of male animals across simulation runs initiates at approximately 12,000 to 12,500 units.
