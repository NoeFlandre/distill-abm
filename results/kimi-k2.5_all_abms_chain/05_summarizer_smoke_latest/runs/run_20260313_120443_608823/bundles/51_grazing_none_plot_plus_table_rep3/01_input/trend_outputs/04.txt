The average available green biomass across all patches begins at approximately 45,000 units. During the initial four time steps, the biomass undergoes a marginal increase to approximately 45,261 units before entering a prolonged decline phase. From step 4 through step 27, the biomass decreases steadily, reaching a minimum value of approximately 42,057 units at step 27, representing the lowest point of vegetation availability observed during the simulation period.

Following this minimum, the biomass enters a sustained recovery and growth phase that continues through the remainder of the simulation. By step 100, the average biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units, marking an increase of over 18,000 units from the initial value and approximately 18,478 units from the minimum observed at step 27.

The rate of increase varies throughout the recovery phase. Between approximately steps 33 and 65, the biomass exhibits the steepest growth trajectory, with significant positive trends detected in the statistical analysis. After step 65, the rate of increase gradually moderates but remains consistently positive through step 100, suggesting a slowing but continuing improvement in pasture conditions as the system approaches a potential equilibrium state.

Across the 30 simulation runs, the trajectories exhibit qualitative similarity in the overall pattern of initial decline followed by sustained recovery, though quantitative differences emerge in the final outcomes. Final biomass values across individual runs range approximately from 58,000 to 64,000 units, indicating variability in the long-term social-ecological outcomes despite identical initial parameter configurations. This divergence suggests that stochastic elements in the model, including rainfall variability (with mean 200 and standard deviation 100) and heterogeneous agent behaviors across the three behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), generate path-dependent trajectories in vegetation dynamics.

The observed pattern—initial overexploitation followed by gradual recovery—aligns with the model's representation of private property grazing systems where 60 households, each initially managing 90 livestock units, adapt their grazing strategies over time. The minimum biomass at step 27 likely corresponds to a period of maximum grazing pressure before learning mechanisms, particularly the learning-by-doing (E-LBD) and social learning (E-RO-SL1) processes that characterize two-thirds of the agent population, enable improved vegetation management. The subsequent sustained increase suggests that these adaptive learning processes facilitate a transition toward more sustainable grazing practices, allowing green biomass to recover and eventually exceed initial levels despite continued livestock presence.
