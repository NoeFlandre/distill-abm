The deviation between consumers' held health values and their implied values through milk choices begins at zero, indicating perfect alignment between health perceptions and consumption behavior in 1974 when whole milk dominated the market. Over the 31-year simulation period, this divergence increases monotonically to approximately 0.725, suggesting a growing disconnect between internal health valuations and actual purchasing decisions as the market transitions toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties.

The trajectory exhibits distinct phases of acceleration. During the initial period spanning roughly the first four to six time steps, the divergence increases rapidly with a steep slope of 0.063, reflecting the initial disruption of established consumption patterns as agents begin encountering alternative milk options. This rapid adjustment continues through approximately time step nine, though at a moderating rate of 0.041, as early adopters progressively shift away from whole milk while their health valuations lag behind behavioral changes.

A pronounced deceleration occurs between time steps 10 and 20, where the rate of divergence slows dramatically to 0.012 and subsequently to 0.003. This plateau suggests a temporary equilibrium where the gap between health beliefs and choices stabilizes, potentially corresponding to the period where market penetration of alternative milks reaches saturation among receptive consumers while resistant segments maintain habitual whole milk consumption despite accumulating health information.

Following this stagnation, the divergence renews its ascent around time step 20, with the rate accelerating to 0.019 by time step 25. This late-stage divergence likely captures the gradual erosion of habitual barriers among resistant consumer segments and the cumulative effect of social norm shifts, where remaining whole milk consumers increasingly deviate from their own health valuations as social pressure and environmental perceptions favor alternatives. The oscillation pattern with a dominant period of approximately 15 time steps suggests cyclical adjustments in this alignment, potentially reflecting generational turnover or periodic intensification of health awareness campaigns that temporarily accelerate or retard the divergence between values and behaviors.

The final value of 0.7251 at time step 32 indicates that by 2005, a substantial misalignment persists between consumers' health valuations and their actual milk choices, suggesting that the historical transition toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties was driven significantly by factors beyond internalized health beliefs, including social influence, habit disruption, and environmental perceptions, resulting in a behavioral shift that outpaced or diverged from purely health-centric value formation.
