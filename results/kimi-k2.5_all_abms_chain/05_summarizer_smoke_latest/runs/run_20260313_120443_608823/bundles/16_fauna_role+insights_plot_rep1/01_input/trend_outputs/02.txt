Initial state analysis reveals that the collective energy reserves across the 20 forager agents commence at approximately 1,500 to 2,000 units. During the first 500 time steps, the system undergoes rapid energy depletion characterized by moderate inter-simulation variability, with total forager energy declining to values ranging between -5,000 and approximately zero. Following this transient phase, the dynamics transition to a highly consistent linear decay from step 500 through the termination of observations at step 2,000 to 2,200, with collective forager energy decreasing at a steady rate of roughly 15 to 18 units per temporal step. Throughout this extended linear phase, individual simulation trajectories exhibit strong convergence, with minimal dispersion between runs. Terminal measurements indicate cumulative energy deficits ranging from approximately -32,000 to -38,000 units.

Interpretive analysis within the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model framework indicates that the sustained negative trajectory reflects chronic energetic insufficiency, wherein forager metabolic and mobility expenditures consistently exceed caloric returns from megaherbivore predation. The initial 500-step decline likely corresponds to the depletion of accessible prey within the 6-patch foraging radius and the energetic costs of establishing hunting patterns, while the subsequent linear phase suggests a regime of diminishing returns as prey populations become increasingly scarce. The accumulation of substantial negative energy values—enabled by the configuration of immortal foragers with food storage capacity—demonstrates that the initial ungulate population of 250 individuals proves inadequate to sustain the energetic demands of 20 human hunters under the specified grassland productivity parameters (33% initial vegetation cover, 3-tick regrowth time, high seasonality of 0.8).

For decision makers evaluating sustainable harvest protocols, these results indicate that even relatively small forager groups generate predation pressure sufficient to induce rapid megaherbivore depletion and consequent hunter energy bankruptcy within approximately five to six annual cycles (given the 365 ticks-per-year calibration). The absence of stabilization in the energy trajectory suggests that no equilibrium state emerges under these parameter configurations; rather, the system progresses inexorably toward resource exhaustion. Conservation strategies should therefore prioritize maintaining prey populations well above the thresholds explored here, as the energetic feedback mechanisms indicate that delayed intervention leads to irreversible deficits rather than recoverable fluctuations.
