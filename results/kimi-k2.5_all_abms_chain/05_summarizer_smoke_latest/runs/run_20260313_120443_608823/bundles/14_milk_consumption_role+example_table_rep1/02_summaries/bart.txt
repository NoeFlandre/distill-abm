The model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. It captures how individual decision-making factors collectively drive macro-level market shifts. Success is measured by the model's ability to reproduce the historical crossover point where skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption surpassed whole milk consumption. The trajectory exhibits distinct phases of deceleration punctuated by temporary plateaus. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at approximately 5.3 units, reflecting the minimal market presence of these alternatives in 1974 when whole milk maintained dominant habitual strength. Consumption of whole milk initiates at approximately 2,655 units in 1974, reflecting the dominant market position characteristic of 1974, reinforced by the high initial habit strength of 10.0. Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at zero and follows a strictly increasing trajectory throughout the simulation period, reaching approximately 25,644 total selections by the conclusion. The consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk initiates at a minimal baseline of approximately 5.3 units, reflecting the initial market conditions of 1974 where whole milk maintained dominant incumbent habits. The trajectory concludes with whole milk consumption reduced by approximately 90% from initial levels, capturing the historical market transition where alternative varieties achieved dominance. In the context of the British milk transition, this trend suggests that despite the historical shift toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties, the psychological weight of habitual whole milk consumption remained substantial and intensified slightly over the period. This likely reflects the strong initial habitual tendencies programmed into the consumer population and the persistent memory of consumption patterns. The deviation between consumers' held environmental values and the environmental implications of their milk selections initiates at zero. This early divergence reflects the onset of cognitive dissonance as the pre-1974 habit-driven equilibrium encounters initial pressures from evolving health perceptions (initial alternative health mean of 1.69) and nascent social influence mechanisms. The deviation stabilizes at approximately 13.42, representing a lasting state of value-choice misalignment. This sustained elevation throughout the latter two-thirds of the simulation indicates that the macro-level transition to alternative milk varieties was driven by health attribute perceptions and social network influences.
