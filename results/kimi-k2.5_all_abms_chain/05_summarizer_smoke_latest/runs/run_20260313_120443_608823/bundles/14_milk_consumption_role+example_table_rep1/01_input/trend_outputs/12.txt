Between time steps five and ten, the rate of increase attenuates significantly to a gentler slope of roughly 0.11 units per step, though the trend remains consistently positive. This deceleration suggests a period of network propagation where social influence mechanisms, operating at moderate interaction probabilities and susceptibility levels, facilitate the spread of new consumption patterns while habit strength gradually weakens for the incumbent whole milk choice. The cumulative deviation reaches its maximum value of approximately 13.48 at time step thirteen, coinciding with the critical historical juncture where skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties achieved dominance in the marketplace.

Subsequent to this peak, the system enters a prolonged plateau phase extending through the remainder of the simulation period. These high-frequency oscillations, evident in recurring cycles at intervals such as steps 16-20 and 26-30, indicate a dynamic equilibrium where psychological justification mechanisms and cognitive dissonance thresholds allow consumers to rationalize choices that maintain a persistent gap between their environmental values and actual consumption behavior. Minor fluctuations between local peaks near 13.45 and valleys near 13.41 suggest ongoing micro-adjustments as agents continuously negotiate the tension between social conformity pressures and personal value alignment.

By the terminal period corresponding to 2005, the deviation stabilizes at approximately 13.42, representing a lasting state of value-choice misalignment. This sustained elevation throughout the latter two-thirds of the simulation indicates that the macro-level transition to alternative milk varieties was driven primarily by health attribute perceptions and social network influences rather than environmental value consistency, resulting in a new market equilibrium characterized by persistent deviation between consumers' environmental principles and their realized consumption patterns.
