Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. The transition becomes self-reinforcing and inevitable, highlighting the importance of early investment in network-embedded information dissemination. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately zero units during the initial period (Steps 0-1) between Steps 1 and 11, consumption undergoes rapid growth, ascending steeply from near-zero values to reach approximately 1,200 to 1,280 units. The cumulative count of milk choices increased linearly at a constant rate of 1,000 choices per simulation step. Between steps 5 and 15, adoption accelerates markedly, exhibiting a concave upward curvature. The cumulative total reaches approximately 25,000–26,000 lower-fat milk choices across the agent population. The metric briefly declines to approximately 1,180 around Step 18. The second growth phase initiates near Step 25 and continues through Step 32. Consumption rises monotonically from approximately 1,240 to 1,440-1,470 units. This represents an increase of approximately 200 units, or 16-18%, over the plateau level. The early plateau (steps 0-5) represents a window where interventions may face resistance due to lack of habitual reinforcement. The acceleration phase (step 5-10) constitutes a critical transition period where initial adopters establish behavioral patterns. Long-term adoption creates substantial intrinsic motivation. Social influence and habit formation increasingly override personal health assessments, driving choices that diverge further from individual health values. Interventions targeting dietary change may be most effective during the initial transition window before habits crystallize.
