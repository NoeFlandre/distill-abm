The model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. It captures how individual decision-making factors collectively drive macro-level market shifts. Success is measured by the model's ability to reproduce the historical crossover point where skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption surpassed whole milk consumption. The mean weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent initiates at effectively zero during the initial time step, consistent with the 1974 baseline where whole milk dominated consumer habits. Between steps 1 and 11, consumption undergoes rapid sigmoidal acceleration, rising sharply from near-zero levels to approximately 1,250 units. At step 10, the metric reaches roughly 650-750; by step 20, approximately 1300-1500; and by step 30, approximately 2000- Selections of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begin near zero and progress through distinct temporal phases of adoption. During the initial approximately seven steps, accumulation remains minimal, with cumulative choices remaining below 3,000 across the entire population of 1,000 agents. Between steps 7 and 15, the adoption rate accelerates substantially. From step 15 through step 32, the system maintains a steady, approximately linear accumulation rate. The persistence of whole milk consumption even after three decades, albeit at reduced levels, suggests the existence of a resilient consumer segment. Future policy and marketing strategies should account for this long tail of traditional consumption. The steep initial decline suggests that intervention strategies and market transitions occur most rapidly during early adoption phases. In 1974, whole milk consumption was deeply habitualized within the British population, creating immediate inertia against the incipient health movements of that era. The subsequent 5-7% increase in the habit multiplier over three decades implies that habitual behavior generated a persistent, self-reinforcing bias in choice functions. While habits create friction, they do not insulate whole milk from competitive threats indefinitely. The metric measuring mean deviation between agents' held health values and their milk choices exhibits a monotonic increase over the 32 time steps, following a distinct three-phase trajectory. The trajectory reveals that market transitions generate substantial consumer fragmentation rather than homogeneous shifts. Decision makers must recognize that mature transition markets contain highly heterogeneous consumer profiles. The mean environmental deviation metric exhibits a pronounced sigmoidal trajectory across the 32-step simulation period. Public health strategies should account for this growing deviation by providing not just information, but concrete habit-disruption mechanisms, as information alone appears insufficient to prevent escalating psychological tension during extended transition periods.
