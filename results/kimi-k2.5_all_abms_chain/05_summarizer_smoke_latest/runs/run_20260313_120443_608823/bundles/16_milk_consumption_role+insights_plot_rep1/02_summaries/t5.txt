Model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. Individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers. Between steps 1 and 10, consumption plummets rapidly from 2650 to approximately 950-1000 units, representing the most dramatic phase of decline. From step 10 to step 25, consumption gradually drops from around 650 to about 250-280 units by step 32. This final phase indicates an acceleration in the transition away from whole milk consumption. Over the 32-step period modeling 1974-2005, total cumulative selections of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begin near zero and progress through distinct temporal phases of adoption. Between steps 7 and 15, the adoption rate accelerates substantially, with cumulative totals increasing from approximately 3,000 to nearly 10,000 selections. The mean whole milk quantity decreases gradually from approximately 1,000 units to around 700-750 units by step 20. This phase exhibits reduced volatility compared to the initial steep decline. The downward trend continues albeit at a slower pace with some minor fluctuations. The habit factor registers at 1.00, indicating a neutral baseline before habit mechanisms activate. Between Step 0 and Step 1, the metric undergoes a sharp discontinuity, rapidly escalating to approximately 1.021. This abrupt increase reflects the immediate instantiation of the high initial habit strength assigned to whole milk as the incumbent choice. Agent populations begin overcoming cognitive dissonance thresholds (0.15) and social susceptibility mechanisms facilitate collective shifts away from automatic whole milk selection. Reinforcement mechanisms create self-sustaining behavioral momentum that continues strengthening through the end of the simulation period. This trend suggests interventions targeting milk consumption habits face a critical temporal constraint. This plateau phase represents a period of market transition where social influence mechanisms begin facilitating gradual adoption among network-connected agents. However, the persistent upward trend indicates that the rate of habit dissolution lags behind the continued evolution of health perceptions. Environmental attitude formation occurs substantially faster than behavioral adaptation in habitual consumption domains. The rapid initial rise (steps 0-5) demonstrates that consumers quickly internalize environmental information about product alternatives through social networks and experience. But the plateau at elevated deviation levels indicates that these updated attitudes translate imperfectly into actual choices.
