Over the 32-step period modeling 1974-2005, total cumulative selections of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begin near zero and progress through distinct temporal phases of adoption. During the initial approximately seven steps, accumulation remains minimal, with cumulative choices remaining below 3,000 across the entire population of 1,000 agents, reflecting the powerful inertia of incumbent habits. Between steps 7 and 15, the adoption rate accelerates substantially, with cumulative totals increasing from approximately 3,000 to nearly 10,000 selections. From step 15 through step 32, the system maintains a steady, approximately linear accumulation rate, adding roughly 15,000 additional choices to reach final cumulative totals between 25,500 and 26,000. This terminal value represents approximately 80 percent of maximum possible selections given the population size and temporal duration. High consistency across the repeated simulation runs indicates robust model behavior with minimal variance in outcomes under the specified parameter configuration.

This adoption pattern emerges from the interaction of habit maintenance mechanisms and social influence processes specified in the model. The minimal activity during the first seven steps reflects the difficulty of overcoming the high incumbent habit strength (10.0) and habit threshold (2.0) when initial perceptions of alternative health and environmental benefits remain modest (1.69 and 1.12, respectively), coupled with social blindness (0.51) that filters early social signals. The mid-period acceleration between steps 7 and 15 suggests that once sufficient early adopters stochastically overcome their thresholds, social interaction probabilities (p-interact = 0.3) and conformity pressures (0.28) operating through the Watts-Strogatz network topology (parameter 8.0) facilitate rapid diffusion of new consumption patterns. The sustained linear growth in later steps indicates that the combination of memory-lifetime effects (5.0) and justification mechanisms (0.61) generates consistent adoption flows rather than exponential saturation, as agents gradually accumulate experiences with alternative milk options while social norms progressively shift.

Implications for decision makers:
The seven-step inertia period demonstrates that consumer behavior interventions face significant delays when competing against established habits with strength levels near 10.0, suggesting that policymakers should not expect immediate measurable impacts from health or environmental messaging. However, the predictable linear growth phase indicates that persistence in early-stage intervention yields reliable compound returns once adoption initiates, with each subsequent period adding consistent numbers of converts. Organizations seeking to drive dietary transitions should budget for sustained multi-year campaigns capable of surviving the initial low-response phase, as the social influence mechanisms require time to generate sufficient network density of early adopters to overcome habitual inertia. The high terminal adoption rate (~80%) suggests that complete market transformation is achievable within the 32-year window when multiple psychological mechanisms (habit disruption, social influence, perception updating) operate simultaneously.
