The female prey population commences at 125 individuals and declines continuously to extinction by step 2312. The trajectory is characterized by three distinct phases. During the initial 475 to 600 steps, the population undergoes a precipitous decline from 125 to fewer than 20 individuals, representing a loss of over 80 percent of the initial population. This rapid collapse suggests intense hunting pressure acting upon the initially abundant and spatially distributed prey, with hunters efficiently exploiting the high encounter rates characteristic of dense prey populations.

Between steps 600 and 875, the rate of decline decelerates markedly as the population enters a prolonged phase of low-density persistence. During this middle phase, the population fluctuates primarily between 0 and 10 individuals, with brief increases occurring around steps 730, 1160 to 1391, and 1856 to 2087. These temporary recoveries reach maximum values of only 6 to 7 individuals, indicating that while reduced encounter rates at low density may temporarily buffer against hunting mortality, or demographic stochasticity may occasionally favor short-term population growth, these gains are insufficient to reestablish viable breeding populations.

The final phase is defined by continued oscillation at extremely low densities (0-5 individuals) until complete extinction occurs at step 2312. Individual simulation runs exhibit substantial variability in the timing of collapse, with some populations reaching near-zero levels by step 500 while others persist in small numbers until beyond step 2000. This variation suggests that local extinction timing depends on stochastic factors such as movement patterns and encounter probabilities, though the ultimate outcome of extinction remains consistent across runs.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The simulation results indicate that continuous hunting pressure from a moderate number of immortal foragers with food storage capability inevitably drives prey extinction, regardless of stochastic variation in the timing of collapse. The rapid decline to critical low-density levels within the first 600 steps suggests that hunting interventions or conservation measures must be implemented extremely early to prevent populations from reaching critically low numbers where demographic stochasticity and Allee effects dominate. Brief population recoveries observed at low densities should not be interpreted as signs of resilience or sustainable harvest levels; rather, they represent temporary fluctuations preceding inevitable extinction. Managers should recognize that once populations decline below approximately 20 individuals under sustained hunting pressure, recovery becomes demographically improbable even if hunting intensity temporarily decreases.
