**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The model demonstrates that sustained hunting pressure from a non-declining hunter population inevitably drives megaherbivore populations to extinction, though the timeline varies considerably due to stochastic factors. The rapid initial decline suggests that conservation interventions must be implemented extremely early—within the first 500 time steps—to prevent irreversible population collapse. Once prey energy falls below critical thresholds (approximately 10,000 units in this scenario), recovery becomes statistically improbable despite brief periods of slight population increase. Management strategies should account for high uncertainty in extinction timing during the terminal decline phase, as small remnant populations may persist unpredictably long before final exhaustion.
