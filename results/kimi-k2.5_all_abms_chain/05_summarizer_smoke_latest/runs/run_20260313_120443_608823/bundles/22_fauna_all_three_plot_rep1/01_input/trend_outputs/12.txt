The total energy stored in the female ungulate population begins at approximately 12,500 energy units across all simulation replicates. During the initial 200 steps, this aggregate energy declines precipitously to values ranging between 2,000 and 8,000 units depending on the replicate, reflecting immediate hunting pressure on the initially abundant population.

Between steps 200 and 500, the rate of energy depletion moderates but continues downward with substantial inter-replicate divergence; some populations stabilize temporarily around 3,000–4,000 units while others continue falling steadily. This period corresponds to the transition from hunting abundant adults to exploiting scarcer, reproductively active females, with stochastic variation in hunter foraging success and grass regrowth causing the observed heterogeneity across runs.

From step 500 onward, most replicates enter a terminal decline phase where female energy reserves approach zero, though the exact timing of extinction varies considerably from approximately step 600 to step 2,200. Several replicates exhibit minor transient increases in total energy during this late phase, likely reflecting brief episodes of calf recruitment or reduced hunting mortality, but these insufficiently offset continued losses from harvest and starvation.

The cross-simulation variance in time-to-extinction suggests that demographic stochasticity, coupled with the seasonal resource fluctuations (seasonality parameter of 0.8), creates unpredictable tipping points beyond which female populations cannot recover, even when initial conditions and hunting pressure are identical across replicates.
