The total energy contained within the prey population initiates at approximately 25,000 units and undergoes a monotonic decline toward zero across all simulation runs, though the temporal dynamics and rate of depletion vary considerably between iterations.

During the initial 400 time steps, energy levels fall precipitously from 25,000 to approximately 5,000, with the steepest trajectories reaching values below 2,000 by step 400. This phase represents the initial encounter period where human foragers begin systematically harvesting the herbivore population and exploiting available energy reserves.

Between steps 400 and 800, simulation runs diverge into distinct categories. Approximately half of the iterations continue rapid decline to near-zero energy by step 600, while the remainder exhibit prolonged persistence, maintaining energy levels between 2,000 and 8,000. Several trajectories within this group display temporary stabilization or modest rebounds between steps 500 and 700, suggesting intermittent reductions in hunting success, temporary spatial refugia formation, or brief periods of grass recovery that allow surviving animals to replenish reserves.

From step 800 onward, the remaining populations experience final collapse. Most runs reach minimal energy values (below 500) by step 1200, though a limited subset persists with trace energy reserves gradually diminishing to zero between steps 1500 and 2200. By the conclusion of the observed time frame, all iterations converge toward zero available energy.

The complete absence of stable non-zero equilibria across the ensemble indicates that the parameter configuration—characterized by 20 hunters with foraging radius 6, high environmental seasonality (0.8), and the specified energetic constraints—consistently drives the megaherbivore population toward functional extinction. The six-fold variation in persistence time (from approximately 400 to 2200 steps) reflects the influence of stochastic factors in animal movement and hunter-prey encounter rates, but does not alter the ultimate outcome of population collapse.

**Implications for decision makers:** The deterministic collapse observed across all runs suggests that management interventions must either reduce hunter numbers below the critical threshold evident in these simulations, expand refuge areas beyond the current foraging radius of 6 patches, or enhance prey reproductive rates and survival parameters to achieve sustainable coexistence. The variation in collapse timing indicates that short-term population persistence should not be interpreted as long-term stability, as delayed extinction remains inevitable under current conditions.
