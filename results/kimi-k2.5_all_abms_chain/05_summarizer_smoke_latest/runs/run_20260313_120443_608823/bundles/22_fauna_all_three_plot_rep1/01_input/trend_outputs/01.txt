The number of active human forager bands remains constant at the initialization value of 20 agents throughout the entire observed simulation sequence, from the initial time step through approximately 2,200 steps. This immutability persists across all replicate simulation runs, exhibiting zero variance over time. The stability reflects the specific model configuration wherein the forager population is initialized at 20 agents and the immortality parameter is activated, preventing mortality from energy depletion while simultaneously precluding mechanisms for forager recruitment, reproduction, or immigration.

This static demographic structure maintains a consistent predation pressure on megaherbivore populations throughout the simulation duration. The absence of numerical fluctuation in the hunter population ensures that hunting mortality rates remain temporally uniform and density-independent with respect to human demographics, though they may vary based on herbivore encounter rates and energetic profitability. Consequently, any observed changes in ungulate herd dynamics—including population collapses or stable coexistence outcomes—can be attributed exclusively to the interplay between fixed anthropogenic harvesting pressure, environmental productivity fluctuations, herbivore life history traits, and seasonal resource variation, rather than to feedback loops between prey abundance and hunter population size.

For decision makers evaluating extinction risk scenarios, this constancy provides a controlled experimental baseline that isolates ecological and biological parameters from anthropogenic demographic dynamics. The fixed predator population size simplifies causal attribution, allowing clear identification of critical thresholds in environmental productivity, grass regrowth rates, or herbivore reproductive intervals that precipitate population collapse under sustained, non-escalating hunting pressure.
