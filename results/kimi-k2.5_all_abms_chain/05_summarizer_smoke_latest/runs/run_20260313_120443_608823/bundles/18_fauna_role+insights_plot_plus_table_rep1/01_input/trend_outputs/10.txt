Across all simulation runs, megaherbivore populations exhibit a consistent trajectory of decline from initial levels toward eventual extinction. The aggregate mean population begins at approximately 167 individuals, though individual realizations initiated from a parameter setting of 250 animals, and declines to zero by step 2311. The decline follows a multi-phase pattern: an initial steep decrease occurs during the first 500 steps, followed by a protracted slower decline between steps 500 and 1500, and a final approach to extinction beyond step 1500. Statistical analysis identifies significant change points in the rate of decline at steps 125, 475, 625, and 875, indicating discrete transitions in population loss dynamics. Throughout the decline, populations exhibit oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of approximately 63 steps, likely reflecting seasonal environmental fluctuations superimposed on the directional extinction trend. Variability between individual simulation runs is highest in the initial phases and converges toward zero as populations approach extinction. In the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, this consistent extinction trajectory indicates that the combination of 20 human hunters, high environmental seasonality (0.8), and the specified energetic and reproductive parameters creates unsustainable pressure on megaherbivore populations. The rapid initial decline suggests that hunting efficiency is highest when prey populations are dense, while the extended tail toward extinction reflects the increasing difficulty of locating rare prey and possible demographic stochasticity at low population sizes. For decision makers, these results imply that megafauna conservation strategies must prioritize early intervention to reduce hunting pressure before populations enter the rapid decline phase. The presence of oscillatory dynamics indicates that temporary population rebounds should not be interpreted as recovery signals, particularly when occurring within the context of a long-term downward trend. Monitoring programs should track rates of change rather than absolute population sizes, with particular attention to acceleration points identified around steps 125, 475, and 625. Finally, the strong seasonality effects observed suggest that management interventions should account for temporal variations in resource availability, as environmental stressors compound hunting pressure during critical periods.
