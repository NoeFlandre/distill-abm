The cumulative number of livestock destocked begins at zero and follows a saturating growth trajectory over the 100-year simulation period, ultimately reaching an average of approximately 97 animals per household by year 100, though individual runs exhibit substantial variation ranging from roughly 58 to over 170 animals. The accumulation occurs in three distinct phases characterized by markedly different rates of change.

During the initial phase from years 0 to 25, destocking proceeds most rapidly, with rates approaching 5 animals per household annually in the first decade before decelerating to approximately 1.5 animals per year by year 25. This period accounts for the majority of total destocking activity, with cumulative values typically reaching 50–80 animals by the first change point at year 25. The pronounced early accumulation suggests immediate corrective adjustments to initial stocking levels as households respond to realized pasture conditions and implement their respective learning strategies.

Between years 25 and 60, the rate of destocking moderates considerably to slopes of approximately 0.3–0.4 animals per year, though remaining monotonically increasing across all observations. By year 60, cumulative destock totals generally range from 70 to 90 animals for most trajectories, with the second change point marking a transition where some households begin to plateau while others continue accumulating at varying rates. This period contains multiple inflection points between years 45 and 75, indicating strategic recalibration as the three learning strategy types—baseline rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—diverge in their responsiveness to vegetation feedbacks and neighbor observations.

From year 60 to 100, the accumulation continues at reduced velocity for many households, though significant variance emerges across the population. Some trajectories maintain steady increases of 0.3 animals per year through year 100, while others stabilize completely, creating the observed spread in final outcomes. Superimposed on these trends are cyclical patterns with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, suggesting that households periodically reassess stocking decisions in response to multi-decadal vegetation cycles or accumulated social information, generating waves of destocking activity particularly noticeable around years 22–26 and 49–61.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The concentration of 50–80 percent of total destocking activity in the first 25 years indicates that initial stocking decisions and early adaptation strategies critically determine long-term management trajectories. The high variance in final outcomes—spanning over 110 animals between the most and least destocked households—suggests that learning strategy selection fundamentally shapes livelihood resilience, with some approaches requiring continuous livestock liquidation while others achieve early stability. The presence of multi-decadal oscillation cycles implies that pastoral systems should anticipate periodic destocking pressures rather than expecting smooth convergence to equilibrium, and that policies supporting early-stage learning and adjustment may be more effective than interventions targeting established management routines.
