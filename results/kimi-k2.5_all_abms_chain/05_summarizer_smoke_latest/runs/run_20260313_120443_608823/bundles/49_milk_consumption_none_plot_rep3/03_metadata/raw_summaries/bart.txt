 This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005 . It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . The simulation integrates theoretical frameworks with empirical data, employing calibration against observed macro-level consumption patterns to validate outcomes .  The total number of milk choices made accumulates at a constant rate from zero at step 0 to approximately 32,000 at step 32 . The trajectory maintains a consistent rate of accumulation of roughly 70 selections per step throughout the entire period . The mean whole milk consumption metric exhibits a monotonic downward trajectory throughout the simulation period .  The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed initiates at approximately zero at Step 0, reflecting the initial market dominance of whole milk in 1974 . During the first ten to twelve steps, corresponding to the period from 1974 to approximately 1985, the metric undergoes rapid non-linear growth, escalating from near zero to values ranging between 1200 and 1280 .  At step zero, the metric registers effectively zero, indicating that all 1,000 agents begin with nearly identical choice function scores reflecting the 1974 market conditions dominated by whole milk consumption . Within the first three steps, the deviation surges rapidly to approximately 0.50, then continues climbing to roughly 0.58 by step five .
