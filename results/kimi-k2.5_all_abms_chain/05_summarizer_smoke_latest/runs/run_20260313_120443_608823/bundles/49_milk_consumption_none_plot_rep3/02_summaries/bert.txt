This agent - based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the british public from 1974 to 2005, replicating individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi - skimmed varieties. The primary objective is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence interact within individual decision - making processes regarding milk choice, with the main outcome metric being average weekly consumption per person for each milk type. Decision - making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence, and post - choice evaluation where agents may adjust future decisions. This trajectory reflects the simulated historical transition away from whole milk over the 1974 – 2005 period, with the steep early decline indicating rapid initial adoption of lower - fat alternatives, the moderating middle phase suggesting slowing transition rates among remaining consumers, and the final stepwise reductions implying threshold effects or discrete behavioral shifts in the diffusion process. Between steps 1 and 10, consumption undergoes a rapid, steep increase, rising from near zero to approximately 1, 200 - 1, 280 units. From step 25 onwards, consumption resumes growth, climbing from approximately 1, 220 - 1, 250 units to final values of roughly 1, 440 - 1, 480 units by step 32. The cumulative total of whole milk choices initiates at approximately zero and exhibits a strictly linear increase, reaching values ranging between 2, 000 and 2, 300 by the final time step across repeated simulation runs. This linear accumulation pattern suggests that, under the specified parameterization with high incumbent habit strength and moderate social susceptibility, whole milk retains a consistent baseline of consumption across the simulated population, with approximately 7 % of agents selecting it at each time step on average, resulting in steady cumulative growth throughout the 1974 - 2005 simulation period. The subsequent linear growth at a rate approaching the theoretical maximum of 1, 000 choices per step (equivalent to all 1, 000 agents selecting skimmed or semi - skimmed milk at each decision point) indicates that by approximately step 10 to 15, adoption had reached near - saturation levels. Later time steps, the vast majority of agents consistently choose skimmed or semi - skimmed varieties, resulting in the steady linear accumulation of approximately one choice per agent per time step.
