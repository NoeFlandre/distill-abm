The total prey population begins at approximately 250 individuals across all simulation runs. Over the first 500 time steps, the population undergoes a steep decline, with many trajectories falling below 50 animals and several reaching near-zero levels by this point. This rapid initial decrease suggests that the combined effects of human hunting pressure from 20 foragers and baseline mortality rates exceed the herd's reproductive capacity to maintain population stability, despite the two-year birth spacing and environmental grass regrowth cycles.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the remaining populations continue to decline erratically, with trajectories diverging considerably across runs. Some populations stabilize temporarily at low levels (between 10 and 40 animals) for several hundred steps, likely reflecting periods where reduced herd density lowers hunting encounter rates or where demographic stochasticity in reproduction temporarily offsets mortality. However, these low-level equilibria prove unstable, and by approximately step 1500, most populations have declined to fewer than 10 individuals.

Beyond step 1500, only a few simulation runs maintain small residual populations, which fluctuate briefly before ultimately declining to effective extinction (zero or near-zero individuals) by step 2200. The high variance in extinction timing across runs—ranging from approximately 400 steps to over 2000 steps—indicates that stochastic processes, including herd movement patterns, hunter-prey encounter probabilities, and the timing of reproduction relative to hunting events, significantly influence the pace of population collapse even when deterministic parameters remain constant. The complete extinction of all populations demonstrates that under these parameter configurations, the hunting pressure exerted by immortal foragers with energy storage capabilities creates an unsustainable predation regime that overwhelms the megaherbivore life history strategy of extended lifespans and intermittent reproduction.
