The cumulative number of males killed begins at zero across all simulation runs. Over the first 500 to 800 steps, this metric increases rapidly, indicating intensive hunting pressure on male prey during the initial phase when the subpopulation is abundant and encounter rates between hunters and targets are high. This early accumulation reflects optimal foraging behavior targeting males while they remain readily available within the foraging radius.

Between approximately 800 and 1200 steps, the rate of increase diminishes markedly across most runs as the cumulative count approaches asymptotic values. This deceleration corresponds to the depletion of the male prey pool, where reduced population density lowers encounter probabilities to levels that make continued targeting of males energetically inefficient compared to alternative foraging options.

By approximately 1500 steps, the cumulative count stabilizes in all runs, reaching final values ranging between approximately 105 and 125 individuals. These terminal values suggest effective elimination of the male subpopulation, consistent with an initial prey population of 250 individuals distributed across sexes. Variation in the final counts and the timing of stabilization across simulation runs— with some runs ceasing accumulation near step 900 at totals of approximately 105 and others continuing until beyond step 1500 to reach totals exceeding 120— indicates stochasticity in herd movement patterns, hunter-prey encounter rates, and demographic fluctuations under identical parameter configurations.
