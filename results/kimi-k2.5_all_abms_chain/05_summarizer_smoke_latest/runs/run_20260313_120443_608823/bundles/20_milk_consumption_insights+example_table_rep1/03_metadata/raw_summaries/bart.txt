 This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005 . It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . The primary objective is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence interact within individual decision-making processes regarding milk choice .  Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5.3 units per agent per week at the outset of the simulation period, reflecting the historical market dominance of whole milk in 1974 . Consumption undergoes a rapid and statistically significant increase, rising sharply toward roughly 1,265 units by the eleventh year .  The cumulative count of milk choices begins at zero at time step 0 and increases linearly throughout the simulation period, reaching exactly 32,000 by time step 32 . This trajectory captures the aggregate transition of 1,000 agents from whole milk consumption toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties across 1974-2005 timeframe .  Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5.3 units per person per week in 1974 . During the first decade of the simulation, from 1974 to the early 1980s, consumption undergoes rapid growth, exceeding 1,200 units by the ninth time step . Consumption stabilizes into a plateau phase between time steps 10 and 24, representing the period from the mid-1980s to the late 1990s .
