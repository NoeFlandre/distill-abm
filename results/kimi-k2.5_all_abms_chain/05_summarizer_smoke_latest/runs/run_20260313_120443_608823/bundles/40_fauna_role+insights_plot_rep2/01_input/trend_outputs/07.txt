Across all simulation runs, the cumulative death count initiates at zero and exhibits a characteristic saturation curve, ultimately approaching or achieving the initial population size of 250 individuals. The temporal dynamics reveal substantial heterogeneity in the rate of mortality accumulation, with simulations demonstrating divergent trajectories despite identical initial conditions.

During the initial phase (approximately steps 0–500), the cumulative death count increases rapidly across all runs, with mortality accumulating at rates ranging from roughly 0.3 to 0.6 deaths per step. By step 500, cumulative deaths vary substantially between approximately 150 and 230 individuals, indicating that some populations experienced accelerated mortality pulses while others maintained slower attrition rates.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the mortality rate decelerates considerably for most simulations as cumulative counts converge toward terminal values. During this interval, death counts typically reach plateaus between 220 and 250 individuals. The saturation values cluster tightly around 240–250 deaths for the majority of runs, though some trajectories terminate as low as approximately 220–230 cumulative deaths.

Extended simulations continuing beyond step 1000 exhibit minimal additional mortality, with most curves flattening completely by step 1500. However, several runs demonstrate prolonged low-level mortality accumulation, gradually approaching terminal values between steps 1500 and 2100. The maximum observed cumulative death count reaches approximately 248–250 individuals, effectively representing total population extirpation.

The coefficient of variation in cumulative deaths is highest at intermediate time steps (approximately 500–800 ticks), where between-run variability ranges across 50–80 individuals, before converging as populations approach exhaustion. This pattern indicates that while stochastic demographic and foraging events create divergent collapse timelines, the ultimate outcome of near-total population mortality remains consistent across parameter replicates.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The convergence of all trajectories toward cumulative mortality exceeding 220 deaths from an initial population of 250 indicates that the current configuration of hunting pressure (20 foragers), life history parameters, and high seasonality (0.8) creates unsustainable predation pressure that reliably drives megaherbivore populations toward extinction. Decision makers should note that while the timing of collapse is stochastic and may occur over intervals ranging from fewer than 500 to more than 2000 time steps, the terminal outcome is deterministic under these parameter settings. Interventions to reduce forager numbers, decrease hunting efficiency (foraging radius), or enhance vegetation recovery rates would be necessary to shift outcomes from inevitable collapse toward stable coexistence. The pronounced variation in collapse timing (spanning approximately 1600 time steps between fastest and slowest extirpation events) suggests that short-term population stability does not indicate long-term viability, and monitoring programs must account for delayed extinction dynamics.
