This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes. The average degree of deviation from recommended livestock levels exhibits a three-phase dynamic over the 100-timestep simulation horizon. The model implements updating mechanisms for current risk parameters (r-parameters) through social learning processes, this particular metric reflects an invariant initial condition that persists unchanged throughout the yearly sequences of vegetation dynamics and livestock placement decisions. At the outset, no destocking occurs, but within the first three time steps, the metric reaches its maximum value of approximately 6.6 animals per household on average. At the beginning of the simulation, the average number of livestock destocked exhibits a sharp initial peak followed by a rapid decline and long-term stabilization at low levels. After approximately time steps 33 and 43, the trajectory stabilizes. A 100-year simulation of 60 smallholder households in a private-property grazing system with stationary households and heterogeneous learning behaviors. The number of destocked livestock exhibits a monotonically increasing pattern, beginning at zero and accumulating to 96.8 livestock units by the final time step of the simulation. The simulation begins with 20 households employing the E-RO (non-learning) behavioral strategy. During the initial phase (timesteps 0–10), this population exhibits a modest increase, reaching a maximum of approximately 21.1 households around timestep 8. The decline continues through the mid-simulation period, though at a moderating rate. In the final phase, the population stabilizes with no significant directional trend.
