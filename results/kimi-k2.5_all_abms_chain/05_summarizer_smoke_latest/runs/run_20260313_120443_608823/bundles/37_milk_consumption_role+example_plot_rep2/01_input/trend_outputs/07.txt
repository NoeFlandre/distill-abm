The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed initiates at negligible levels near zero, consistent with the 1974 baseline conditions where whole milk maintained market dominance through strong incumbent habits. During the first ten time steps, consumption undergoes a rapid, non-linear acceleration, surging from near zero to approximately 1,200–1,280 units. This steep ascent suggests an initial phase of intensive behavioral transition where accumulating perceptions of health and environmental attributes, combined with social influence mechanisms, overwhelm the high initial habit strength associated with traditional milk choices.  

Following this explosive growth, the system enters a prolonged plateau phase spanning roughly steps 10 through 26, wherein consumption stabilizes in the range of 1,180–1,250 units. A temporary reduction occurs around step 18, dipping to approximately 1,180 units before recovering. This stagnation likely represents a market equilibrium where a substantial segment of the population maintains resistant habitual behaviors, requiring sustained social pressure or cumulative cognitive dissonance to trigger further adoption. The relatively tight clustering of outcomes during this period suggests robust model behavior despite stochastic elements in social network interactions.  

In the final segment from step 26 to step 32, the metric exhibits a secondary pronounced increase, climbing from approximately 1,220 units to between 1,450 and 1,480 units. This late-stage acceleration may reflect the cumulative activation of social norms and justification mechanisms enabling holdout consumers to rationalize behavioral change, or potentially represents delayed threshold crossings among agents with the highest resistance to habit disruption.  

Variance across simulation runs remains modest throughout most of the trajectory, with slightly greater divergence appearing during the final ascent. This pattern indicates that while early adoption dynamics are highly deterministic given the initial perception parameters and network structure, later-stage consumption levels exhibit greater sensitivity to stochastic variations in individual interaction probabilities and psychological processing thresholds.
