Model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. Individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers. Between time steps 1 and 10, the consumption undergoes a steep and sustained decline, falling rapidly to approximately 950–1,000 units. This pronounced decrease suggests an initial phase where accumulated perceptions regarding health and environmental attributes successfully overcome the default habitual patterns associated with whole milk consumption. From time step 20 to 25, the decline continues at a modest pace, reaching approximately 600 units, suggesting a steady but slowing erosion. Progressively intensifies, resulting in a divergence of approximately 300 units by step 32. The relatively modest cumulative totals imply that whole milk consumption is predominantly maintained through automatic habitual repetition rather than deliberate choice. From step 20 onward, the decline continues but with increased granularity and step-like fluctuations, dropping from approximately 750 units to roughly 200 to 250 units by step 32. This late-stage erosion occurs in distinct increments rather than smoothly, potentially reflecting the threshold-based psychological mechanisms where agents require accumulated motivation to overcome long-standing habits. S may have slowed the habitual reinforcement process, while the upper bound (approximately 1.055) reflects runs where network effects and individual psychology combined to produce stronger habit maintenance among whole milk consumers. This sharp initial rise suggests that as agents commence decision-making with initially homogeneous perceptions, the immediate introduction of social interactions and information processing generates rapid divergence in how consumers evaluate milk options.
