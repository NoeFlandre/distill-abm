Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. Consumption increases rapidly and significantly, exhibiting steep upward trends through index 4 and continuing through index 9. This initial surge indicates a period of rapid disposition triggering agents' perception of health and environmental characteristics. Following this rapid adoption phase, the system enters a prolonged period of oscillation and stabilization between indices 10 and 24, where values fluctuate around 1200 to 1265 units. The cumulative number of milk choices made by all agents combined starts at zero and increases linearly throughout the simulation period, reaching 32,000 total choices by the final time step. The constant rate of accumulation suggests that the disposition mechanisms and choice consideration processes remain active for all agents across every time period, without dropout or saturation effects. Habit factor is applied to skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choice functions. A subtle valley emerges at step 5, where the factor briefly declines to approximately 1.0167, representing minimal initial habit formation for alternative varieties. The growth accelerates between steps 10 and 14, achieving a maximum rate of increase at 0.030 per step during this interval. The deviation between agents' health values and the health implications of their actual milk choices begins at 0.0000 at the initial time step. This metric exhibits a consistent monotonic increase, terminating at 0.7251 with an average value of 0.4965 across all periods. The trajectory unfolds through three distinct acceleration phases demarcated by inflection points.
