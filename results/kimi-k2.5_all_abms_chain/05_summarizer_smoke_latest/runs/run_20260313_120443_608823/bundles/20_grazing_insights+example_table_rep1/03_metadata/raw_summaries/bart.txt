 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model . It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes .  The average degree of deviation from recommended livestock levels begins at approximately 0.007, indicating near-complete adherence to guidelines during the initial phase . Over the first 33 time steps, this deviation increases rapidly and significantly . This steep ascent suggests that agents quickly diverge from recommended stocking rates as they encounter ecological variability .  The average number of livestock destocked per household begins at zero, then surges to approximately 6.6 animals by the third yearly time step . The average total livestock per household commences at 90 animals and undergoes a precipitous decline throughout the initial 20 time steps, indicating rapid destocking . The recovery curve exhibits systematic oscillations with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 steps .  The inequality in livestock distribution begins at effectively zero, indicating an initially equitable allocation of livestock health across all households at the outset of the simulation . Over the 100 time steps, this inequality rises steadily to approximately 0.073, demonstrating a gradual concentration of healthy livestock among specific households despite the presence of learning mechanisms .  The number of households employing the baseline non-learning strategy (E-RO) begins at 20 and exhibits an initial increase during the first 8 time steps, reaching a peak of approximately 21.1 . The decline moderates, eventually reaching a minimum value of roughly 14.67 . The cyclical nature of strategy switching also implies that policy interventions should account for heterogeneous adoption rates .
