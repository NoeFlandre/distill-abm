Despite the presence of social influence mechanisms and agents' ability to process health and environmental information about alternatives, the habit formation parameters sustain a consistent baseline of whole milk selections without significant deceleration.

For decision makers, the persistent linear growth in cumulative whole milk choices demonstrates that entrenched habitual behavior, when initialized at maximum strength and maintained through active habit mechanisms, can resist transition pressures even over extended time horizons. This suggests that dietary shift interventions may require either more aggressive habit-disruption triggers or stronger social influence cascades than those currently parameterized, as the existing configuration of social-blindness (0.51) and social-conformity (0.28) proves insufficient to overcome the inertia of established consumption patterns within the simulated 31-year timeframe.
