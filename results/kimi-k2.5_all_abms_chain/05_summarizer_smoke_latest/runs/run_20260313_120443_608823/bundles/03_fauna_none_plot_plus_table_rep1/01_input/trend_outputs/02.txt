The aggregate trajectory of total forager energy across 40 simulation runs exhibits a monotonic, statistically significant decline over 2312 steps, beginning at an initial value of 2000 and terminating at approximately -38229. This downward trend remains consistent throughout all observed time windows, with Mann-Kendall tests indicating consistently decreasing patterns (tau values approaching -1.0, p < 0.001) across sequential intervals.

The rate of energy depletion varies across distinct phases. During the initial approximately 650 steps, the system demonstrates a moderate decline with a slope of roughly -11 units per step. Following the first detected change point at step 650, the rate of loss accelerates, transitioning through slopes of approximately -15.7 (steps 464-695) and -18.5 (steps 696-927) before stabilizing around -20 units per step from approximately step 1000 through step 2087. Additional change points at steps 1075, 1500, 1925, and 2310 mark transitions in these dynamic regimes, though the fundamental direction of energy loss remains unchanged.

Individual simulation trajectories display high concordance with the aggregate pattern, collectively ranging from the initial maximum of 2000 to a minimum observed value of -38233. Minor oscillatory behavior appears toward the conclusion of the simulation sequence (indices 2170-2265), though these fluctuations remain small relative to the magnitude of the overall downward trend.

Within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, this sustained linear erosion of energy reserves—occurring under conditions where forager mortality is disabled (immortal-foragers = True) and energy storage is enabled—indicates a persistent energetic deficit wherein movement and metabolic costs systematically exceed energy acquisition from prey. The stabilization of the depletion rate at approximately -20 units per aggregate step in later phases suggests the forager population, maintaining constant numbers due to immortality, settles into a steady state of net energy loss, accumulating energy debt linearly over time without demographic adjustment mechanisms to arrest the decline.
