The simulation reveals a consistent trajectory of increasing heterogeneity in consumer choice evaluations over the 33 time steps representing the 1974-2005 period. Initially, at step 0, the choice function deviation registers at 0.0000, indicating complete homogeneity among agents in their evaluation of milk options, which aligns with the historical starting condition where whole milk dominated and consumer preferences were largely uniform. By step 5, this metric rises sharply to approximately 0.58, representing a rapid divergence phase where agents begin developing distinct evaluation criteria based on health perceptions, environmental considerations, and social influences. The rate of divergence then moderates significantly, with the metric reaching approximately 0.67 by step 12 and continuing a gradual ascent to 0.7508 by the final step at 32. This latter phase demonstrates slower but persistent differentiation in consumer decision-making frameworks.

Statistical analysis confirms this pattern through six consecutive rolling windows, each showing significant increasing trends (p=0.027), though with diminishing slopes from 0.128 in the initial window to 0.004 in the final window. The inflection points at steps 7, 12, and 28 mark transitions where the pace of diversification shifts, particularly the step 7 inflection that separates the explosive early divergence from the subsequent steady-state differentiation. Throughout the simulation, the mean deviation across all time steps remains at 0.6323, with the median at 0.6729, indicating that the distribution skews toward higher divergence values in later periods.

This trajectory suggests that the transition from whole milk to reduced-fat varieties fundamentally transformed how consumers evaluate dairy products, evolving from a market characterized by uniform preferences to one with substantial diversity in choice criteria. The early rapid divergence (steps 0-7) corresponds to the initial adoption phase where pioneering consumers began prioritizing health attributes, while the sustained gradual increase (steps 7-32) reflects ongoing social learning and habit formation processes that continued differentiating consumer segments.

For decision makers in public health and agricultural policy, these trends indicate that interventions targeting the initial transition period (the first third of the simulation timeframe) would likely face a homogeneous population receptive to unified messaging, whereas later periods require segmented approaches addressing increasingly diverse consumer values. The substantial final divergence value of 0.7508 suggests that by 2005, the British milk market had fragmented into distinct consumer segments with vastly different prioritization of health, environmental, and social factors, necessitating differentiated product positioning and communication strategies rather than blanket campaigns.
