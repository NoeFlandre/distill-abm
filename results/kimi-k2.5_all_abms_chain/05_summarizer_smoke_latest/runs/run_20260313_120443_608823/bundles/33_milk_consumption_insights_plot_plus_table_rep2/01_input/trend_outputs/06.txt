The simulation results indicate a pronounced long-term decline in average whole milk consumption over the 33 time steps, with the metric falling from approximately 2,655 units at the initial step to roughly 253 units at the final step, representing a reduction of about 90% over the simulated period replicating the 1974-2005 transition.

The decline follows a markedly non-linear trajectory characterized by distinct phases. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 10 to 14 time steps, the series exhibits a steep downward slope, with values dropping rapidly from the initial high plateau toward the 1,000-unit threshold. This early phase demonstrates the most dramatic rate of change in the entire series, consistent with the initial disruption of established consumption habits and the emergence of health and environmental perceptions favoring alternatives.

Following this initial rapid decline, the trajectory enters a transitional phase where the rate of decrease moderates considerably while exhibiting increased volatility. Between approximately steps 15 and 24, the series continues to trend downward but displays localized fluctuations, including temporary elevations to approximately 774 units at step 19 and 621 units at step 27, interspersed with declines to roughly 760 units at step 18 and 594 units at step 26. These oscillations suggest a less stable adjustment period as agents with varying levels of habit strength and social susceptibility transition away from whole milk consumption at different rates.

In the final phase, from approximately step 25 onward, the values continue to descend toward the terminal level near 253 units, though the progression remains characterized by intermittent fluctuations, including a peak at approximately 335 units at step 30 following a valley at roughly 323 units at step 29. The persistence of measurable whole milk consumption even at the final time step indicates that a complete transition to alternative milk varieties does not occur within the simulated timeframe, with a residual segment of the agent population maintaining whole milk consumption habits despite the overall trend.

Statistical analysis of the series confirms significant monotonic decreasing trends across most sub-periods, with particularly strong negative trends in the early windows and continued significant declines in the middle segments, while the later periods show weaker statistical significance in the downward movement, consistent with the increased volatility and approaching floor effects as the system stabilizes at a lower equilibrium.

For decision makers, these results suggest that transitions in food consumption patterns follow predictable S-curve dynamics with rapid initial change followed by diminishing returns. The residual whole milk consumption indicates that universal adoption of alternatives may be unrealistic, and interventions should account for a persistent minority of resistant consumers. The volatility in middle and late phases suggests that monitoring systems should expect non-linear progress and avoid premature conclusions about intervention effectiveness during temporary plateau periods.
