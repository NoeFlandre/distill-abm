The aggregate energy reserves held by the female prey population commence at 12,500 units and follow a deterministic trajectory toward depletion, terminating at zero by tick 2,311. Superimposed upon this downward trend are regular oscillations with a dominant periodicity of roughly 63 ticks, visible as minor fluctuations between ticks 376 and 410 where values temporarily stabilize between 2,094 and 2,233 units. These cyclical variations likely reflect seasonal energy dynamics or reproductive pulses within the ungulate life history, yet they do not arrest the overall loss of biological capital.

Following the initial crash, the decline persists but undergoes further regime shifts at ticks 500, 625, and 875, indicating successive alterations in the rate of energy extraction. Notably, the series exhibits transient recoveries between ticks 1,160–1,391 and again between 1,856–2,087, where energy accumulation briefly outpaces depletion, potentially corresponding to successful reproductive events or temporary reductions in hunting efficiency. However, these intervals prove insufficient to reverse the trend, as the final change point at tick 2,310 precipitates a terminal collapse to zero. Statistically, the trajectory demonstrates high dispersion (standard deviation ≈ 3,083) and strong right skewness, evidenced by a median of 100 compared to a mean of 1,569, indicating that while most simulation runs rapidly depleted female energy reserves, some maintained higher levels for extended durations before eventual extinction.

For conservation strategists and model designers, the inexorable decline of female energy reserves—despite brief oscillatory recoveries—suggests that current hunting pressure and environmental parameters create a demographic trap where reproductive capital is extracted faster than it can be replenished. The transient increases highlight potential windows for intervention, but the high variance across runs indicates that stochastic factors significantly influence timing without altering the ultimate population outcome. Management efforts should prioritize reducing extraction pressure before critical energy thresholds are breached, as the model demonstrates limited resilience for recovery once decline phases begin.
