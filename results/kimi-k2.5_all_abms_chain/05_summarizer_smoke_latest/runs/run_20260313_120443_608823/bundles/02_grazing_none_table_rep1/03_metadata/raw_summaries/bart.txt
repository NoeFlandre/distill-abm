 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model . It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management .  The average deviation from recommended livestock levels among extension agents exhibits a distinct three-phase trajectory: an initial rapid escalation, a subsequent prolonged decline, and final stabilization at a moderate level . The series begins at near-zero deviation (0.0074 at timestep 0), indicating initial compliance with recommended stocking rates . The dominant oscillation period of 49 timesteps indicates cyclical adjustments in household stocking strategies .  The average livestock holdings per household, encompassing both active stock and destocked animals, follow a distinct trajectory of sharp initial decline, trough stabilization, and modest recovery over the 100-timestep simulation . By timestep 100, the biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units, representing a substantial increase of over 15,000 units from initial conditions .  The Gini coefficient of healthy livestock distribution begins at effectively zero, indicating perfect equality among the sixty households at initial point . Over the course of one hundred time steps, this measure of inequality exhibits a steady, monotonic increase . The low final destocking rates indicate that the heterogeneous learning mechanisms enable households to stabilize their herds at sustainable levels relative to available biomass .  The oscillatory patterns likely emerge from asynchronous destocking behaviors among the three coexisting behavioral types . Liquidation to stabilized long-term divestment reflects adaptive management responses to private property constraints and highly variable climatic conditions (rainfall mean 200, standard deviation 100)
