 The model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005 . It captures how individual decision-making factors collectively drive macro-level market shifts . Success is measured by the model's ability to reproduce the historical crossover point where skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption surpassed whole milk consumption .  The simulation tracks mean skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption across 33 time steps representing the period from 1974 to 2005 . The series maintains a mean of 1,079.81 units and a median of 816.85 units . The overall trend demonstrates a clear transition from near-zero consumption of alternative milk products to their establishment as the dominant consumer choice .  The trajectory follows an S-curve pattern characterized by three distinct phases . During the initial phase (steps 0–10), adoption proceeds gradually, reaching roughly 5,000 total selections by step 10 . In the final phase (step 20–32), growth continues at a strong linear rate of approximately 936 selections per step . This pattern reflects a classic diffusion of innovation process within the consumer population .  The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents follows a three-phase diffusion trajectory across the 33 time steps representing the 1974–2005 period . The trajectory features eight distinct inflection points at steps 5, 13, 16, 19, 24, 27, and 30, marking transitions between these phases of rapid, moderate, and fluctuating decline . These oscillations likely reflect the stochastic nature of the psychological mechanisms governing habit breaking .  The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits a distinct two-phase trajectory across the 33 time steps representing the 1974–2005 period . The overall curve exhibits an S-shaped adoption pattern with an asymmetric structure . The aggregate mean holds at 1.0359 with a median of 1,0376 and minimal variance .  The mean value health deviation exhibits a monotonic increasing trend throughout the 32 time steps, beginning at 0.0 and terminating at approximately 0.725 . This pattern captures the emergence of market segmentation during Britain's historical milk transition, beginning from a state of near universal preference for whole milk and evolving toward a diversified marketplace .  The mean value environmental deviation begins at zero, indicating initial alignment between consumers' environmental values and their milk choices . Semi-skimmed varieties surpassed whole milk consumption in the British milk market transition . The trajectory suggests that the system reaches an equilibrium where the persistent force of habit maintenance, combined with social conformity pressures, sustains a consistent gap between what consumers value environmentally and what they actually choose, settling at a deviation level of approximately 13.4 .
