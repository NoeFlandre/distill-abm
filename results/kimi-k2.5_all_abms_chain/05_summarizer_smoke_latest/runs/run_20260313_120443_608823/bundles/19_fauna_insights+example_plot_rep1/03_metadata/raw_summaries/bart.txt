 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction . It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges . The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics . For decision makers testing extinction hypotheses, this pattern suggests that male megaherbivore populations are vulnerable to rapid depletion under the modeled hunting intensity .  At approximately step 780, the first birth events occur across simulation runs, with cumulative counts rising abruptly from zero to values between one and three . By step 1000, cumulative births across different runs range between two and six . This variability expands through step 1500, where recorded values span from approximately three to ten births, and persists through the conclusion of the observation period .  Many simulations reach zero males by step 1000, others maintain small remnant populations of 1 to 5 individuals for extended durations, with some persisting until approximately step 2200 before final extinction . The absence of any stabilization or recovery episodes throughout the time series indicates that under the current parameterization, extinction represents an inevitable outcome rather than a probabilistic risk .  The total energy of male animals begins at approximately 12,500 units across all simulation runs . During the initial 500 steps, values undergo a steep decline, decreasing by roughly 60 to 80 percent to range between negligible levels and approximately 5,000 units . By step 1,000 to 1,500, the majority of trajectories converge toward zero, indicating near-complete depletion of male population's energy reserves .  The percentage of vegetated patches begins at approximately 33%, consistent with the initial grass coverage parameterization . For the initial 300 steps, this metric remains relatively stable with minimal variation across simulation runs . At approximately step 350, the system experiences a severe collapse, with green patch coverage plummeting to roughly 23–25% depending on the specific run . Following this minimum, rapid recovery occurs, with coverage returning to baseline levels near 33% by step 450–500 .
