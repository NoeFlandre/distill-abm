By approximately step 500, the aggregate mean drops below 50 individuals, though individual simulation trajectories exhibit considerable variance, with some runs collapsing to near-zero while others maintain higher densities temporarily.

These fluctuations generate transient local maxima in the aggregate mean at steps 709 (9.3 individuals), 730 (11.4 individuals), 803 (7.4 individuals), and 820 (6.9 individuals), likely reflecting the interaction of seasonal environmental constraints with the species' life history traits, particularly the 730-tick birth spacing interval. During the terminal phase from step 1,400 onward, the population persists at extremely low densities, with the overall median count falling to 1.48 individuals. This phase exhibits complex non-monotonic behavior including statistically significant temporary increases between steps 1,392-1,623 and 1,856-2,087, suggesting that demographic stochasticity allows occasional brief recoveries in remnant herds. However, continued foraging pressure from immortal hunters, combined with diminishing numbers that amplify Allee effects and mate-finding difficulties, ultimately prevents recovery. The population reaches complete extinction at step 2,311, with the aggregate mean converging to zero. This protracted persistence at low densities followed by final collapse indicates that while hunting pressure drives the initial rapid decline, terminal extinction involves stochastic processes operating when populations fall below critical viability thresholds.
