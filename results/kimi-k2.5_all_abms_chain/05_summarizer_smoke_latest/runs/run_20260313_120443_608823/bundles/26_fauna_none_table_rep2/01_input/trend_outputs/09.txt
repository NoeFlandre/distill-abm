The female prey population initialized at 125 individuals and underwent immediate and substantial decline, reaching near-extinction levels within the first 700 ticks. By tick 730, counts had fallen to approximately 6.5 individuals, representing a decline of nearly 95% from initial abundance. This early collapse was characterized by a strongly negative monotonic trend lasting through approximately tick 1159, with brief inflections in the rate of decline occurring around ticks 305 and 740.

Following the initial crash, the population entered a prolonged phase of low-density persistence lasting approximately 1,600 ticks, during which abundance oscillated at very low levels with a dominant periodicity of 63 ticks. During this interval, population counts fluctuated primarily between 1 and 6 individuals, with minor peaks registered around ticks 710, 730, 803, and 820, and corresponding valleys at ticks 707, 729, 802, and 818. The median abundance throughout the entire simulation remained at 1 individual, indicating that for the majority of the temporal duration, the population existed at or near minimum viable thresholds.

Statistically significant short-term reversals occurred within this low-density phase, including an increasing trend between ticks 1160 and 1391, followed by decreasing trends through tick 1855, and a final increasing trend between ticks 1856 and 2087. Despite these intermittent periods of positive growth, the overall trajectory remained toward extinction. Change points in the population dynamics were detected at ticks 475, 600, and 875, marking transitions in decline rates and the onset of the oscillatory low-density regime.

The population ultimately reached zero individuals at tick 2311. The disparity between the mean abundance of 16.5 individuals and the median of 1 reflects the highly skewed distribution of population sizes, driven by the rapid early collapse followed by extended persistence at minimal levels. The 63-tick oscillation period, roughly corresponding to seasonal cycles given the parameterization of 365 ticks per year, suggests that environmental seasonality continued to influence demographic fluctuations even as the population approached extinction.

In the context of the model, the trajectory indicates that the interaction of human hunting pressure from 20 foragers with the species' life history constraints—including a two-year birth spacing interval and delayed sexual maturity—prevented population recovery following the initial hunting-induced decline. The prolonged persistence at single-digit population sizes represents a demographic bottleneck where stochastic processes dominated, ultimately leading to final extinction after approximately 6.3 simulated years despite intermittent periods of positive growth.
