Across the repeated simulation runs, the trajectories remain tightly clustered in the early time steps, with minimal variation in the cumulative count. As the simulation progresses, stochastic differences between runs generate increasing divergence, resulting in a spread of approximately 300 units between the lowest and highest values by time step 32.

This linear accumulation indicates that whole milk maintains a stable, constant rate of consumption throughout the simulation period. With a population of 1000 agents, the observed rate suggests that approximately 6 to 7.5 percent of consumers select whole milk at each decision point. The stability of this rate persists despite the presence of social influence mechanisms, habit formation dynamics, and alternative milk options, suggesting that the strong initial habitual inertia specified in the model parameters sustains consistent whole milk consumption without significant decline or acceleration over the simulated timeframe.

For decision makers, these dynamics imply that established consumption habits create persistent behavioral patterns that resist modification even when alternative products are available and social influence mechanisms are active. Interventions aiming to shift consumption patterns may require strategies that substantially alter habit thresholds or perceptions of alternatives, or they may need to operate over extended periods to overcome incumbent product loyalty. The predictable stability in incumbent product selection rates also suggests that market demand for traditional products may remain reliable in the short to medium term, which has implications for supply chain planning and inventory management.
