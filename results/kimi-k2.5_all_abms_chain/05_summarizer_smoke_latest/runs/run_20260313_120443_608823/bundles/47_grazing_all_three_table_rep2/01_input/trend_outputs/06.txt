The average number of livestock destocked per household commences at zero, reflecting initial allocations that precede environmental stress realization. An acute destocking event materializes by year three, peaking at approximately 6.6 animals per household, indicating a severe initial adjustment as the opening herd size of ninety animals confronts the first episodes of rainfall variability. This spike represents the system's primary correction phase, where the initial stocking density proves incompatible with realized pasture productivity under the applied climatic stochasticity.

Following this initial shock, the system enters a damping oscillation phase extending through approximately year thirty. Notable secondary peaks occur around years nine and twelve, registering roughly 3.4 and 2.7 animals per household respectively, suggesting continued but diminishing overstocking pressures as households iteratively recalibrate their holdings. Statistical trends confirm significant decreasing patterns through the first three decades, with the rate of decline moderating after year six and again near year twenty-one as households progressively align their expectations with ecological feedback. The change point at year thirty marks a transition from active adjustment dynamics to a more quiescent regime.

Beyond year thirty, destocking rates stabilize at minimal levels, generally fluctuating below one animal per household for the majority of the subsequent period. Although a statistically significant increasing trend emerges between years sixty-six and seventy-six, these later fluctuations remain modest compared to the initial decade. By the conclusion of the hundred-year simulation, destocking rates converge toward approximately 0.3 animals per household, indicating that across all three learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—households eventually achieve relatively stable herd management aligned with long-term carrying capacity, though low-level destocking persists due to continued environmental variability.

For decision makers, the pronounced initial destocking wave suggests that fixed initial stocking recommendations significantly underestimate vulnerability to rainfall variability in these systems, warranting conservative starting densities or contingency reserves. The multi-decade adjustment period highlights that even with active learning mechanisms, achieving equilibrium between herd sizes and pasture productivity requires substantial time, implying that short-term interventions may be necessary to buffer households during the initial calibration phase. The persistence of minor destocking throughout the century underscores that in environments with high rainfall coefficient of variation, perfect stabilization remains elusive, and risk management protocols should anticipate occasional forced sales even among experienced managers.
