**Total Energy of All Animals**

The total energy contained within all prey populations initiates at approximately 25,000 energy units across simulation runs. During the initial phase spanning roughly the first 300 to 500 time steps, this aggregate energy undergoes a precipitous decline. By step 500, most trajectories have diminished to below 5,000 units, with numerous simulations approaching minimal energy states near zero. However, substantial variation exists in the rate of depletion: while some populations collapse rapidly and reach negligible energy levels within 300 steps, others demonstrate temporary persistence, maintaining 10,000 to 20,000 energy units until approximately step 400 or 500 before experiencing accelerated declines.

In the intermediate phase from step 500 to step 1,000, the remaining energy continues to decrease, though at attenuated rates compared to the initial collapse. A subset of simulations exhibits small-scale fluctuations during this period, with energy levels oscillating between approximately 1,000 and 8,000 units, reflecting brief population persistence or temporary recoveries before renewed depletion.

Beyond step 1,000, the total energy across nearly all runs converges toward zero and remains at minimal levels through the remainder of the observation period, which extends to approximately 2,000 steps. Occasional minor resurgences to values between 500 and 1,000 units occur in isolated simulations between steps 1,000 and 1,500, but these prove transient, with energy returning to near-zero levels shortly thereafter.

The consistent pattern of rapid energy depletion from 25,000 units to near-extinction levels within 500 to 1,000 time steps indicates that the combination of 20 human foragers with the specified hunting parameters and an initial prey population of 250 animals generates unsustainable predation pressure. The variation in timing of collapse across runs likely reflects stochasticity in encounter rates and hunting success, but the universal asymptotic approach to zero energy demonstrates that under these parameter settings, the prey populations undergo systematic collapse rather than achieving stable coexistence with human hunters. The temporary persistence of energy in certain trajectories may correspond to phases where reduced prey density decreases hunting efficiency, allowing small remnant populations to persist briefly before final depletion.
