The Gini coefficient measuring the distribution of healthy livestock across households initializes at 0.000, indicating perfect equality in starting livestock endowments. During the first 40 steps, inequality increases gradually across all simulation trajectories, with coefficient values rising to approximately 0.010 to 0.030. This early phase reflects initial differentiation in household outcomes as agents begin applying heterogeneous decision-making strategies—including E-RO (baseline behavior), E-LBD (learning-by-doing), and E-RO-SL1 (social learning)—to manage grazing on private property under variable rainfall conditions.

Between steps 40 and 60, the trajectories diverge substantially, with approximately half of the simulation runs showing accelerated growth in inequality while the remainder maintain more gradual increases. This bifurcation corresponds to the period when learning-by-doing processes and social learning mechanisms begin to generate distinctly heterogeneous performance across the population, as households with different risk attitudes (r-parameters) adaptively respond to accumulated environmental variability and neighbor observations.

From step 60 through the conclusion at step 100, the separation between high-inequality and low-inequality outcomes becomes pronounced. By the final timestep, the Gini coefficient spans a wide range from approximately 0.030 in the most equitable simulations to 0.095 in the most unequal scenarios. The majority of trajectories converge toward higher inequality levels between 0.060 and 0.090, while a smaller cluster maintains moderate inequality between 0.030 and 0.050, and a few outliers remain below 0.030.

This progressive divergence indicates that despite uniform initial conditions of 90 livestock per household, the combination of distinct behavioral types, private property tenure, and high rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 relative to a mean of 200) drives the system toward increasingly unequal distributions of healthy animals. The broad variance in final outcomes suggests that stochastic environmental realizations interact complexly with the three learning strategies, producing divergent social-ecological trajectories where some populations develop highly skewed livestock distributions while others maintain greater equity through different adaptive co-management pathways.
