The system exhibits a pronounced decline in destocking pressure over the century-long simulation, transitioning from acute initial resource crises to a low-level maintenance regime. At initialization, no destocking occurs as the system begins with full livestock endowments. However, by year 3, destocking reaches its maximum intensity at approximately 6.6 animals per household, reflecting immediate pressure on vegetation from the initial stocking density of 90 animals per household under variable rainfall conditions. This early phase (years 0-30) is characterized by high volatility, with successive peaks at years 9 (~3.4 animals), 12 (~2.7 animals), and 17-19 (~1.5-1.4 animals) followed by intervening troughs, all demonstrating a strong decreasing trend as households reduce herds toward sustainable levels.

Following a structural change point around year 30, the system enters a prolonged stabilization phase lasting until approximately year 65. During this middle period, destocking rates fluctuate minimally around low values, exhibiting no significant monotonic trend. The oscillation amplitude diminishes substantially, with values generally remaining below 1.0 animal per household, suggesting that learning mechanisms—whether experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning—have enabled households to align stocking decisions with pasture carrying capacity despite persistent rainfall variability (coefficient of variation of 50%).

In the final third of the simulation (years 65-100), the dynamics shift again, with a weak but statistically significant increasing trend emerging between years 66-76, followed by stabilization at approximately 0.31 animals per household by year 100. This late-phase elevation suggests either accumulated vegetation degradation under continuous grazing or the manifestation of low-frequency rainfall cycles, specifically an oscillation with a dominant period of roughly 49 years that generates persistent, low-amplitude fluctuations throughout the latter half of the century.

Throughout the simulation, the combination of single-loop learning within fixed behavioral strategies and initial risk disposition of 0.5 enables progressive adaptation, driving median destocking levels down to approximately 0.31 animals, though the right-skewed distribution (mean ~0.96) preserves the memory of early crisis conditions. The persistence of low-level destocking through year 100 indicates that even with learning, the system maintains a baseline of nutritional stress under the imposed rainfall variability, preventing complete elimination of herd reductions despite the absence of livestock reproduction.
