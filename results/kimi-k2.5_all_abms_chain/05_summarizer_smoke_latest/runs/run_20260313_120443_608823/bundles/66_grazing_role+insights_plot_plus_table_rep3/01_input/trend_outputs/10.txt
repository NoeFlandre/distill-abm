The population of households employing the baseline non-learning decision strategy (E-RO) exhibits a three-phase trajectory over the 100-year simulation horizon, beginning with 20 households. During the initial decade (years 0–10), the population undergoes a modest but statistically significant increase, reaching a maximum value of approximately 21.1 households around year 8. This initial growth phase transitions abruptly at year 11 into a prolonged period of decline. Between years 11 and 21, the population decreases steeply at a rate of approximately 0.22 households per year, dropping from roughly 20 to 17.5 households. The decline continues at moderated rates through year 65, with successive periods showing annual decreases of approximately 0.09, 0.08, and 0.04 households per year during decades three through seven. Around year 45, the rate of decline slows markedly, entering a stabilization phase (years 66–100) where the population fluctuates without significant directional trend around a mean of approximately 15 households. The series reaches its minimum value of 14.67 households at year 92, ending the simulation at 14.7 households—representing a net reduction of 26% from the initial cohort. Across the 30 replicate simulations, substantial variability exists around these mean trends, with individual realizations displaying high sensitivity to stochastic rainfall variation, ranging from scenarios where non-learning households nearly disappear to those where they proliferate significantly above initial levels.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The trajectory reveals that static, rule-of-thumb grazing strategies without active learning provide false security during initial years but prove unsustainable over decadal timescales in variable rainfall environments. The brief increase during the first decade suggests that simple heuristics may temporarily suffice when initial pasture conditions are favorable, but the subsequent 50-year decline indicates systematic vulnerability to rainfall variability (std = 100 units) and ecological feedback. Agricultural extension services should prioritize disseminating learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms, as non-adaptive farmers face approximately 25% attrition rates over a century. The high variability across stochastic realizations (coefficient of variation ~11.7%) further indicates that outcomes are highly path-dependent, suggesting that resilience-building policies must account for individual risk dispositions and local ecological conditions rather than applying uniform stocking recommendations. The stabilization around year 65 implies an equilibrium where a residual population of non-learning households persists, likely representing those with favorable land parcels or risk-averse dispositions that coincidentally align with sustainable practices, but policymakers should recognize this as a minority outcome rather than a reliable strategy.
