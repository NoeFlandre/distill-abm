The megaherbivore population exhibits a deterministic collapse trajectory from initial abundance to complete extinction over approximately 2,300 time steps. Beginning with a mean abundance of roughly 167 individuals across simulation runs (starting from an initial cohort of 250), the population undergoes a rapid decline phase during the first 475 time steps, with a significant change point detected at step 125. By step 475, the population has already declined substantially, losing approximately 70-80% of its initial numbers, as indicated by the steep negative slope of -0.320 in the early rolling windows.

Between time steps 475 and 875, the decline continues but exhibits decelerating dynamics with notable inflection points at steps 357, 747, and 825. During this interval, the population fluctuates between approximately 40 and 10 individuals, overlaid with oscillatory behavior showing a dominant period of 63 time steps that likely reflects seasonal resource availability patterns (consistent with the high seasonality parameter of 0.8) interacting with hunting pressure. Brief deviations from monotonic decline appear in rolling windows around steps 1,392-1,623 and 1,856-2,087, where slight increasing trends (tau = 0.203 and 0.445 respectively) suggest temporary stabilization or minor demographic recoveries, though these prove insufficient to reverse the overall trajectory.

After approximately 1,000 time steps, the population enters a terminal phase where numbers consistently fall below 10 individuals, with the median abundance across runs dropping to 1.48. The final change point occurs at step 2,310, after which the population reaches complete extinction (0 individuals) by step 2,311. The high initial variance across runs (standard deviation of 44.9, variance of 2,015.7) converges to zero as all trajectories terminate at the same endpoint, indicating that while the timing of collapse varies slightly between runs, extinction is inevitable under these parameter configurations.

For decision makers: The simulation results demonstrate that a hunting pressure of 20 human foragers combined with high environmental seasonality (0.8) and the specified megaherbivore life history parameters creates unsustainable pressure that drives populations to deterministic extinction within approximately 6.3 years (assuming daily time steps). This suggests that either human hunting pressure must be substantially reduced, environmental seasonality decreased, or megaherbivore reproductive rates increased to achieve population persistence. The lack of long-term coexistence trajectories across all 120 simulation runs indicates that small stochastic variations do not provide a sufficient buffer against extinction under these conditions, and management interventions would need to alter fundamental parameters rather than rely on demographic noise for population survival.
