The ensemble of simulations exhibits a consistent two-phase pattern of rapid accumulation followed by saturation. All trajectories initiate at zero cumulative postpartum female deaths and remain near zero for variable initial periods ranging from approximately 50 to 300 steps. Following this latent phase, all curves undergo steep monotonic increases, with the cumulative number of killed postpartum females rising from near-zero levels to between 100 and 125 individuals. The rate of accumulation varies substantially across replicates: the fastest trajectories reach counts of 100 by approximately step 400, while slower rising curves reach comparable levels only by step 800 or later.

By step 1000, the majority of simulations have stabilized at plateau values clustering between 110 and 125 cumulative kills. These plateaus persist without further appreciable accumulation through the remainder of the observation window (step 2000 and beyond). Final asymptotic values range narrowly from approximately 105 to 125 individuals across the ensemble, with most replicates converging on 115–122. 

Notable heterogeneity exists in the timing of saturation onset. Approximately half of the trajectories flatten by step 600–700, showing no additional kills through step 2000. Others exhibit protracted gradual increases, accumulating an additional 5–15 individuals between steps 800 and 1500 before finally stabilizing. One replicate shows distinctly delayed dynamics, maintaining counts below 20 until step 300, then rising steadily to reach approximately 115 by step 1500.

The convergence of all trajectories on similar terminal values (approximately 120 ± 10) despite heterogeneous accumulation rates suggests a system-level ceiling on postpartum female mortality. Given the initial prey population of 250 individuals and the two-year interbirth interval (730 ticks), the saturation at roughly 120 cumulative kills indicates that hunters encounter and successfully harvest the majority of females that enter the postpartum state during the simulated temporal horizon of approximately 5.5 years (2000 steps).
