Some simulations achieve cumulative counts exceeding 100 males killed by step 500, while others reach only 70-80 during the same interval.

Following this initial rapid accumulation phase, the rate of increase slows markedly across all runs between approximately steps 500 and 1000. The curves transition from steep ascent to gradual saturation, with cumulative kills increasing by only 10-30 individuals over this 500-step period. By step 1000, most simulations have accumulated between 100 and 120 males killed.

After approximately step 1000-1200, the data exhibit near-plateau behavior with minimal additional accumulation through the end of the simulation runs at steps 1500-2000. Final cumulative values across all simulations range from approximately 100 to 125 males killed, with the majority converging between 110 and 122. A few outlier runs terminate with lower final counts around 100-108, while several reach values of 120-125.

This pattern indicates that hunting pressure on male prey is highest during the initial simulation phase when prey populations are presumably at their starting density of 250 individuals. The subsequent saturation suggests that the system reaches a dynamic equilibrium where hunting mortality is balanced by demographic processes including reproduction and natural mortality, or that the male segment of the prey population has been reduced to a level where encounter rates with hunters become limiting. The variability between runs reflects stochastic elements in agent movement, foraging decisions, and demographic events inherent in the agent-based framework.
