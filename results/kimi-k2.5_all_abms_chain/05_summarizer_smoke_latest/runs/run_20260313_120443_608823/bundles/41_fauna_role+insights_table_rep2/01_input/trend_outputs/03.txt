The cumulative number of male prey animals harvested by forager bands exhibits a characteristic accumulation curve with distinct temporal phases and saturation dynamics. Beginning from zero at initialization, the metric undergoes a prolonged phase of rapid increase through approximately the first 1,150 ticks (roughly 3.15 years), during which the cumulative kill count rises steeply and consistently. This early phase is punctuated by multiple change points at 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850 ticks, indicating a sustained acceleration in hunting success as foragers encounter naive prey populations.

Following this initial expansion, the accumulation rate stabilizes between ticks 1,160 and 1,391, entering a plateau phase where the cumulative count remains relatively constant. This suggests a temporary equilibrium where the per-tick addition of new kills balances against the running total, potentially indicating prey depletion, behavioral adaptation, or seasonal resource constraints in the grassland environment.

A secondary increase resumes between ticks 1,392 and 1,623, adding approximately 15-20 additional cumulative kills before encountering another stabilization period from ticks 1,624 to 1,855. The metric reaches its maximum value of approximately 120 males killed at tick 1,876 (approximately 5.14 years), after which the cumulative count exhibits a modest but significant declining trend through tick 2,087, settling at a terminal value of 117.5 at the simulation conclusion (tick 2,312).

Superimposed on these broad trends is an oscillatory component with a dominant period of 63 ticks (approximately bi-monthly cycles), consistent with the model's seasonal environmental parameters (seasonality set at 0.8). These oscillations manifest prominently in the later phases of the simulation (ticks 2,170-2,265), suggesting that hunting success fluctuates with seasonal resource availability despite the cumulative nature of the metric.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The saturation dynamics observed—specifically the dual plateau phases and the eventual decline in cumulative harvests—suggest that male megaherbivore populations under this hunting pressure and environmental configuration face progressive depletion rather than sustainable coexistence. The early rapid accumulation indicates high initial vulnerability, likely due to the absence of evolved predator avoidance behaviors in the initial population. 

The oscillatory patterns superimposed on the trend suggest that seasonal environmental variation creates predictable windows of hunting vulnerability, information that could inform temporal management strategies if conservation were the objective. However, the terminal decline in cumulative kills—unusual for a strictly cumulative metric—strongly implies that in a subset of simulations, the male prey population experienced functional or absolute extinction, halting further accumulation and creating a negative trend in the cross-simulation average. This pattern warrants immediate attention regarding the sustainability of current hunting parameters and the potential for irreversible population collapse within the modeled timeframe.
