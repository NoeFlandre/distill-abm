 This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005 . It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . The simulation integrates theoretical frameworks with empirical data, employing calibration against observed macro-level consumption patterns to validate outcomes .  The cumulative count of whole milk choices initiates at approximately zero across all simulation runs and increases monotonically throughout the 32-step period . During the initial phase (steps 0–10), trajectories exhibit strong convergence, rising sharply to approximately 700–800 total choices with minimal variation across runs . By step 20, cumulative totals span a range of roughly 1,300 to 1,600, suggesting that stochastic social interactions and individual disposition mechanisms generate divergent consumption paths .  The simulations commence at step 0 with mean whole milk consumption at approximately 2,650 units, reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk . Between steps 0 and 10, the metric undergoes a steep monotonic decline, reaching values between 900 and 1,000 units . The accumulation continues through the final time steps, ultimately reaching roughly 32,000 total choices by step 32 .  The mean habit factor for skimmed/semi-skimmed milk initiates at a value of 1.0 at Step 0 . The metric exhibits sustained growth at an approximately constant rate of 0.02 to 0.025 per step . By Step 32, the habit factor stabilizes at approximately 1.58 to 1.60 per step, indicating that the most rapid differentiation occurring in the early stages of milk consumption .  At the initial time step (Step 0), the deviation registers approximately 0, indicating initial alignment between environmental values and consumption choices . At Step 10, the metric plateaus and stabilizes, remaining approximately constant at 13.5 through Step 32 . The rapid early increase suggests that social network effects and entrenched consumption habits quickly create a gap between what agents value environmentally and what they actually consume .
