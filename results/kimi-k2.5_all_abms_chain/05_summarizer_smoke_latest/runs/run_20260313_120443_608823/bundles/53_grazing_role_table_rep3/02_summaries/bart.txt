The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods. The simulation begins with households exhibiting minimal deviation from recommended livestock levels, with initial values near 0.007. During the first approximately 33 time steps, the system undergoes a rapid and substantial increase in deviation from recommendations. The overall trajectory exhibits an oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of approximately 49 time steps. The simulation concludes at year 100 with biomass reaching 60,535 units, substantially exceeding initial conditions by approximately 34%. The average number of livestock destocked per household initiates at zero before spiking sharply to approximately 6.6 animals in the third year. Households initially maintain stocking levels substantially exceeding the long-term carrying capacity of their private pastures. The number of households engaged in social learning begins at zero at the outset of the simulation. The researchers tracked livestock stocking strategies in 60 farming households. The authors conclude that the three learning strategies generate differential livestock management outcomes under conditions of rainfall variability and private pasture ownership. The temporal dynamics unfold through three distinct phases. During the initial decade, the population experiences a brief expansion, rising from 20 to a maximum of approximately 21.1 households around year 8. The population fluctuates around a mean of approximately 15 households, reaching its minimum value of about 14.67 near year 92 before ending at 14.7.
