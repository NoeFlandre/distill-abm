Model focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making. The initial risk disposition among social learning agents remains invariant throughout the 100-year simulation period. The metric exhibits no temporal variation, maintaining identical values across all observed time steps with zero variance. This stability reflects that the measure captures a fixed, time-independent attribute assigned to social learning agent at model initialization. The simulated grazing system initially maintains green biomass at approximately 45,000 units during the first decade. Between years 33 and 54, biomass increases rapidly, with the steepest gains occurring between years 44 and 54. This represents a substantial net gain of about 34% above initial conditions. The recovery rate diminished over time, with the smallest incremental gains occurring in the final decades. The pronounced initial reduction from 90 to 41 animals (a 54% decrease) demonstrates that initial endowments significantly exceeded sustainable levels given the ecological parameters. The early period (years 1-10) demonstrates minimal inequality accumulation. Inequality metrics remain close to zero despite the immediate application of heterogeneous learning strategies. Between years 11 and 50, the system enters a phase of gradual divergence as the three behavioral types respond differently to variable rainfall conditions. The terminal cumulative value of approximately 97 animals exceeds the initial herd size of 90 animals. The slowing accumulation rate likely reflects both the diminishing pool of animals available for destocking as herds contract, and potentially adaptive adjustments in farmer decision-making.
