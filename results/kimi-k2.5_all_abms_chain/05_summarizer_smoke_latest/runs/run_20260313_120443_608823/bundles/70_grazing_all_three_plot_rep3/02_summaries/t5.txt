Model focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making. The mean household initial risk attitude begins the simulation period concentrated near zero, with initial values ranging between approximately -0.11 and 0.2. A significant proportion of households developing strongly positive risk dispositions reaching between 0.6 and 0.8, while others shift toward negative values approaching -0.2. From time steps 60 to 100, the rate of increase slows considerably, with values gradually approaching plateau levels. However, simulation runs exhibit substantial divergence during this phase, with final values at time step 100 ranging widely from approximately 111,000 to 128,000 units. Variation across simulation runs is minimal during the initial decline. But increases markedly during the recovery phase. The difference in long-term livestock holdings indicates learning mechanisms are critical determinants of livelihood resilience. The pace of adoption varies considerably across runs. Some replicates reach 40 or more households by step 30, while others remain below 20 households at that point. The monotonic stepwise pattern suggests that social learning engagement represents an absorbing state. The count declines rapidly from the initial 20 reaching values between 0 and 10 by step 20-30. Some low-performing runs stabilize at intermediate levels (8-18 households) and others continue declining to near-zero by step 60-70. By the final third of the simulation, the system exhibits path-dependent lock-in.
