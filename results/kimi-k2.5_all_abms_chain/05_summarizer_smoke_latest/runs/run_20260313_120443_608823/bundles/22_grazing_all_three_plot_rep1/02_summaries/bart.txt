The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods. The mean household deviation from recommended livestock levels initiates at values near zero, with most households clustered between -0.1 and 0.2 during the first 10 steps. A substantial portion of households increases their livestock levels well above recommendations, reaching peak deviations of approximately 0.75 to 0.8 by step 25-30. A subset of households exhibits sharp declines in deviation around steps 40-50, transitioning from moderate positive values to sustained negative deviations. The mean livestock holdings across households, including animals designated for destocking, begins at 90 animals per household and undergoes a rapid decline over the first 20 time steps. Initial collapse reflects a system-wide adjustment from initial overstocking levels as pastures face heavy grazing pressure. A rapid recovery ensues between steps 40 and 70, characterized by a steep sigmoid increase as biomass climbs toward values between 55,000 and 64,000. The Gini coefficient measuring inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households begins at approximately 0.000, indicating perfect equality in initial conditions where all households possess similar numbers of healthy animals. Over the first 20 time steps, the coefficient increases rapidly to values between 0.010 and 0.015. This divergence indicates that stochastic environmental factors, particularly rainfall variability with a standard deviation of 100 units, interact with the three distinct learning strategies to produce increasingly heterogeneous outcomes. The rate of increase diminishes substantially for most runs, with counts stabilizing into persistent plateaus between steps 40 and 60 that continue through step 100. The rapid initial uptake suggests that social learning mechanisms can disseminate quickly through smallholder communities when communication channels exist, potentially facilitating rapid spread of sustainable grazing practices. The distribution of non-learning households spans nearly the entire possible range, from approximately 0 to 27 individuals across different runs. No convergence to a common equilibrium is observed, indicating that stochastic variation in initial conditions or early-period dynamics creates lasting path dependencies.
