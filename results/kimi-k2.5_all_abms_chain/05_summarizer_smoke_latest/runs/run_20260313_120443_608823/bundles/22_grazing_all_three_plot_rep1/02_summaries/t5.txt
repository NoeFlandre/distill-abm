Model focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making. Individual household values are distributed across a range spanning approximately -0.11 to +0.18, with multiple distinct constant levels observed across the population. The observed spread from negative to positive values indicates substantial heterogeneity in risk preferences among the simulated farming households. Households with negative initial values possess baseline propensities for greater experimentation. The inflection point around year 40 to 50 marks the transition from resource depletion to regeneration. Long-term equilibria ranging from approximately 111,000 to 128,000 units by year 100. The highest equilibrium values suggest scenarios where effective learning coincided with favorable rainfall sequences. Lower plateaus indicate instances where conservative stocking decisions or adverse climatic conditions limited biomass accumulation. The three learning strategies produce categorically different long-term stocking equilibria. The most successful strategies supporting herd sizes approximately 50% larger than the least successful approaches by year 100. Inequality in livestock health is an emergent property of this private pasture system. The wide variation in final inequality levels (0.030 to 0.095) indicates that stochastic environmental factors play a significant role in determining which households succeed. The wide variance in outcomes span nearly 115 units between the most and least intensive destocking scenarios. The specific learning mechanism employed (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning) profoundly influences long-term livestock retention and household viability.
