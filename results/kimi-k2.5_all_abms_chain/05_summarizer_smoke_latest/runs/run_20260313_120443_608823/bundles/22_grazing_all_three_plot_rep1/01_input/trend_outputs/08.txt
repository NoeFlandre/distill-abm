The count of households with social learning initiates at zero across all simulation runs. During the first approximately 40 time steps, this metric exhibits a rapid, stepwise ascent, with the steepest increases concentrated between steps 5 and 30. This pattern reflects a swift diffusion process where households sequentially engage in social learning as they access information from neighbors within the knowledge radius. The discrete, stair-step nature of the trajectories indicates individual adoption events triggered by specific interactions or observations, rather than gradual behavioral shifts.

Following the initial growth phase, the rate of increase diminishes substantially for most runs, with counts stabilizing into persistent plateaus between steps 40 and 60 that continue through step 100. Terminal values display substantial heterogeneity across simulation repetitions, ranging from approximately 18 to 54 households. This wide variance suggests strong path dependency, wherein early stochastic factors—such as initial spatial configurations, randomized risk dispositions, or rainfall variability—influence the ultimate penetration of social learning behaviors. The divergence of trajectories becomes noticeable early, within the first 20 steps, and persists throughout the simulation.

The saturation at distinct levels implies that universal adoption is not achieved; in various runs, between 6 and 42 households (out of 60 total) remain unengaged with social learning by the conclusion. The plateauing dynamics suggest that once immediate neighbor networks are exhausted or as less connected households fail to receive triggering stimuli, the diffusion process halts.

Implications for decision makers: The rapid initial uptake suggests that social learning mechanisms can disseminate quickly through smallholder communities when communication channels exist, potentially facilitating rapid spread of sustainable grazing practices. However, the substantial variance in final adoption rates indicates that reliance on organic social diffusion may leave 30–70% of households without exposure to socially transmitted strategies, depending on local stochastic conditions. Policymakers should anticipate this heterogeneity and consider complementary outreach mechanisms to reach spatially isolated or less socially connected households that fail to adopt through natural network dynamics.
