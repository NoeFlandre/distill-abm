 The model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005 . It captures how individual decision-making factors collectively drive macro-level market shifts . Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers in their network .  The decline occurs in distinct phases characterized by varying rates of change . During the initial phase spanning approximately the first five time steps, consumption decreases rapidly . This steep initial drop corresponds to the period when agents with lower habit thresholds or higher susceptibility to health and environmental information begin abandoning whole milk in favor of alternatives .  The total number of milk choices made by all agents increases linearly from 0 to 32,000 across the 33 time steps of the simulation . At each step, the cumulative count of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk selections rises by exactly 1,000 choices per step . The trajectory exhibits significant inflection points at steps 4, 12, 13, 16, 19, and 19, stabilizes with the series stabilizing with the final phase .  The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed initiates at 5.29 units at step 0 . The series concludes with a final decline to approximately 253 units, indicating the near-complete transition away from whole milk consumption .  The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk initiates at a neutral baseline of 1.0000, reflecting the absence of learned behavioral routines for alternative milk varieties at the simulation's outset . The stability of this increase across simulation replications suggests robust system dynamics where habitual behavior acts as a steadily strengthening anchor for remaining whole milk consumer base .  The mean environmental value deviation begins at zero and undergoes a rapid ascent during the initial nine time steps, reaching approximately 12.6 . The growth rate moderates significantly between steps 10 and 13, culminating in a peak value of 13.48 at step 13 . This plateau represents the maximum aggregate cognitive dissonance, where the gap between held environmental values and actual consumption choices reaches its widest point .
