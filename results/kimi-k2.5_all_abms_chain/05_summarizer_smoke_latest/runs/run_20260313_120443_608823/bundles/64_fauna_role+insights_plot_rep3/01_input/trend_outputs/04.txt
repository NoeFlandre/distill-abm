Across the simulation replicates, cumulative female mortality from hunting exhibits a consistent saturating trajectory, increasing monotonically from zero before asymptoting at levels between approximately 110 and 125 individuals. This terminal range indicates near-complete depletion of the initial female cohort, representing effectively the entire reproductive segment of the starting ungulate population of 250 animals.

Temporally, the accumulation of female kills follows a characteristic three-phase pattern. An initial establishment phase spanning roughly 0 to 200 steps records minimal mortality as forager bands initiate search patterns and encounter prey aggregations. This transitions into a rapid accumulation phase lasting until approximately 500 to 800 steps, during which hunting pressure removes the majority of the female population at high rates. Finally, a deceleration phase ensues as cumulative approaches asymptotic limits, with individual simulations ceasing to record additional female mortality at temporal endpoints ranging from as early as step 600 to as late as step 2200.

Substantial stochastic variation characterizes the temporal dynamics across replicates. Some forager bands achieve cumulative kills exceeding 100 females by step 400, while others require 600 to 800 steps to reach comparable thresholds. Similarly, the cessation of female hunting activity varies widely, with certain trajectories exhausting the female prey pool by step 700, while others continue encountering and harvesting females beyond step 2000. Despite this temporal heterogeneity, final cumulative mortality converges within a narrow band of 110–125 individuals, indicating that stochastic factors primarily influence the velocity rather than the ultimate magnitude of female depletion.

The stepwise increments in cumulative mortality reflect discrete hunting events consistent with individual-based predation dynamics. The upper asymptotes approaching 120 to 125 individuals imply that with 20 initial foragers operating under the specified energetic parameters (including immortal hunters with food storage capabilities), the removal of all reproductive females is an invariant outcome. Once the asymptotic plateau is reached, no additional female mortality occurs, indicating the complete extirpation of the cohort available to hunters.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The consistency of female population collapse across stochastic replicates indicates that the current configuration of hunting pressure, forager efficiency, and environmental parameters creates an inevitable extinction trajectory for the prey population. The model predicts no stable equilibrium that permits long-term female survival or demographic recovery. The wide variation in time-to-extinction—spanning approximately 1600 steps between the fastest and slowest depletion trajectories—suggests that while population collapse is certain, monitoring programs face high uncertainty in predicting the specific timing of reproductive failure. Conservation strategies would need to fundamentally alter encounter rates, forager energetics, or prey population structure to avoid the asymptotic depletion pattern observed across all replicates.
