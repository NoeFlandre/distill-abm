The simulations consistently begin with an aggregated ungulate energy reserve of approximately 25,000 units. Within the first 500 time steps, this collective biomass undergoes a precipitous reduction across all replicates, with most trajectories falling below 10,000 units and many declining to fewer than 5,000 units. Between steps 500 and 1000, energy depletion continues but at a decelerating rate, with typical values ranging between 0 and 2,500 units. By approximately step 1500, the vast majority of simulation runs have converged to near-zero energy states, though some heterogeneous trajectories exhibit prolonged persistence, maintaining marginal energy reserves beyond step 2000 before eventual collapse.

This pattern reflects an inexorable progression toward megafaunal extinction across all replicates, with stochastic variation in extinction chronology. The initial steep descent suggests that the configuration of 20 immortal human foragers with food storage capabilities, operating against an initial population of 250 ungulates under high environmental seasonality (0.8), generates immediate predation pressure that exceeds the population's replacement rate. The inter-simulation variability in collapse timing—spanning roughly 300 to over 2000 steps—likely emerges from stochastic elements including spatial heterogeneity in grass regrowth, probabilistic reproductive events governed by 730-tick birth-spacing intervals, and variability in encounter rates between mobile herds and hunters operating within 6-patch foraging radii.

The universal convergence to zero energy indicates that under these parameter settings, which include sexual dimorphism in prey value (males 30, females 27) and differential processing costs, the megafauna population cannot achieve stable coexistence with persistent human hunting pressure. The system inevitably progresses to functional extinction of the ungulate prey base, irrespective of variable environmental fluctuations or demographic stochasticity.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The model results demonstrate that even moderate initial numbers of human hunters (20 individuals) operating with energy storage and immortality can drive megafauna populations to collapse within timeframes equivalent to several years to a few decades. The absence of stable equilibria across all simulation replicates suggests that sustainable harvest scenarios may require substantial constraints on hunter longevity, elimination of food storage capabilities, or drastic reductions in hunting pressure. The pronounced early decline indicates that conservation interventions must be implemented immediately upon human arrival to prevent irreversible biomass depletion; once energy reserves fall below critical thresholds (approaching the starvation threshold of 25 units per animal), population recovery becomes probabilistically improbable. The documented heterogeneity in collapse timing, while substantial, confirms that local ecological stochasticity may delay but cannot prevent extinction under sustained hunting pressure, emphasizing the necessity of precautionary management approaches rather than reactive measures contingent on observing population declines.
