The combined energy stored in female ungulate populations initiates at 12,500 units, representing a robust starting population with substantial energetic reserves. This value undergoes a precipitous decline during the first 375 time steps, plummeting to approximately 2,232 units. This rapid depletion likely reflects intense initial hunting pressure combined with the establishment of foraging territories by human agents, who may target females preferentially due to their favorable processing costs relative to energy returns.

Following this initial collapse, the system enters a prolonged oscillatory phase characterized by cyclical fluctuations superimposed on a declining trajectory. Between ticks 376 and 875, energy levels exhibit periodic peaks around 2,200 units (notably at ticks 376-410) and troughs near 2,100 units, with a dominant oscillation period of approximately 63 ticks. These oscillations likely correspond to seasonal vegetation pulses interacting with reproductive cycles—given the 730-tick (two-year) birth spacing, reproductive events may cluster seasonally, creating brief energy recoveries that are subsequently depleted by sustained hunting pressure from the immortal forager population.

A significant regime shift occurs around tick 875, after which mean energy levels continue declining but with altered variability. Brief episodes of population stabilization or marginal recovery appear between ticks 1,160-1,391 and again at 1,856-2,087, where the trend temporarily reverses from decreasing to increasing. These interruptions suggest intermittent relaxation of hunting pressure or successful cohort recruitment events, possibly occurring when hunter energy stores reach capacity or when prey spatial refugia temporarily limit encounter rates.

Despite these transient recoveries, the long-term trajectory remains negative across the majority of analytical windows. After tick 2,000, the energy reserves enter a terminal decline phase, accelerating toward zero. By tick 2,311, the female population energy reaches complete exhaustion, indicating functional extinction of the female breeding stock. This endpoint occurs well before the maximum simulation duration, suggesting that under these parameter configurations—specifically with 20 immortal hunters possessing food storage capabilities and targeting preferences that may favor females due to lower processing costs—the megaherbivore population cannot sustain itself beyond approximately 6.3 years.

The substantial variance in energy levels (standard deviation of 3,083) relative to the mean of 1,569 indicates highly unstable population dynamics throughout the decline, with the median value of 100 units suggesting that for the majority of the simulation, the population operates at severely depressed energy levels compared to its initial state.

**Implications for decision makers:** The simulation demonstrates that moderate hunter densities can drive megaherbivore populations to extinction within a decade when hunters optimize for energetic efficiency and face no mortality risk. The presence of seasonal oscillations suggests that conservation interventions would be most effective if implemented during brief recovery windows (particularly around ticks 1,160 and 1,856) when population resilience temporarily increases. Additionally, the rapid initial decline indicates that delayed response to population stress may miss critical intervention thresholds, as the bulk of energetic reserves depletes within the first year of exposure to hunting pressure.
