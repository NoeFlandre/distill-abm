The female megaherbivore population initiates at 125 individuals and experiences catastrophic decline, dropping below 25 individuals within the first 475 ticks (approximately 1.3 years) and reaching single-digit levels by tick 700. This rapid collapse reflects unsustainable harvest pressure from the forager population operating under conditions of immortal hunters and energy storage capabilities.

Following the initial crash, the population exhibits oscillatory dynamics with a characteristic period of approximately 63 ticks (roughly two months), superimposed on a general downward trend. Local maxima of 4 to 7 individuals occur intermittently between ticks 700 and 900, corresponding to attempted recovery pulses. These fluctuations likely represent seasonal or environmentally-driven breeding events that temporarily offset mortality, though the amplitude of these oscillations decays over time as the population deteriorates.

A brief stabilization occurs between ticks 1160 and 1400 (years 3.2 to 3.8), during which the decline temporarily arrests and slight increases are observed. This represents a demographic bottleneck window where the population persists at extremely low density—exhibiting a median abundance of 1 individual across the full simulation—for several hundred ticks. However, the trend resumes its downward trajectory after tick 1400, punctuated by a final weak recovery attempt near tick 1900.

The population spends the majority of the simulation below 5 individuals, with extinction finalized at tick 2312 (approximately 6.3 years). The oscillation peaks diminish progressively from initial post-crash values of 6 to 7 individuals down to 1 to 2 individuals in the final oscillatory phase, indicating that recruitment becomes increasingly insufficient to maintain minimum viable population thresholds.

For decision makers: The extinction trajectory demonstrates that even moderate initial hunting pressure (20 foragers versus 250 prey) can drive female megaherbivore populations to extinction within 6 to 7 years when hunters possess advantages of immortality and food storage. The presence of oscillatory recovery attempts at two-month intervals suggests that brief seasonal windows exist for population stabilization, but once female numbers fall below approximately 5 individuals, deterministic extinction becomes inevitable without immediate hunting cessation. The rapid initial decline suggests that reactive management strategies will be ineffective; preventive measures must be implemented before population densities drop below critical thresholds.
