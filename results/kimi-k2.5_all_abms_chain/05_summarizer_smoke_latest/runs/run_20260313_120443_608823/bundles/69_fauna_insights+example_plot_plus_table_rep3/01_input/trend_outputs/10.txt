The total number of animals begins at 250 individuals and undergoes a deterministic decline toward extinction across all simulation runs, ending at 0 by step 2310 (approximately 6.3 years). The aggregate mean across 120 simulation runs starts at 166.67 individuals and follows a monotonically decreasing trajectory with brief periods of minor fluctuation at very low population levels (values below 11.37) in later stages. The decline proceeds through distinct phases demarcated by change points at steps 125, 475, 625, and 875 (0.34, 1.3, 1.7, and 2.4 years respectively). During the initial phase (steps 0–125), the population experiences its steepest decline, dropping precipitously as high-density prey populations encounter foraging hunters. By step 475, most populations have fallen below sustainable thresholds, and the median population across runs declines to approximately 1.48 individuals, indicating that the majority of simulations have reached near-extinction states. Some individual runs persist with small populations (1–10 individuals) for extended periods, creating high variance in extinction timing (standard deviation of 44.9) despite the consistent eventual outcome of zero. Statistical analysis reveals significant decreasing trends through step 1391 (3.8 years), after which the remaining populations exhibit oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of 63 steps (approximately two months) superimposed on the declining trend, likely reflecting seasonal resource cycles or reproductive periodicity, before final elimination at step 2310.

This pattern indicates that the configured hunting pressure—characterized by 20 immortal foragers operating under optimal foraging constraints, combined with ungulate life history traits including two-year birth spacing and 28-year lifespans in a seasonal environment—consistently exceeds the maximum sustainable yield of the prey population. The rapid initial decline suggests efficient predator exploitation of high-density prey, while the prolonged persistence of small remnant populations in some runs demonstrates demographic stochasticity and the 'sink' dynamics of sparse distributions. The absence of any recovery trajectories or equilibria indicates that once populations pass the critical threshold observed around step 475, extinction becomes inevitable regardless of temporary fluctuations.

For decision makers, these results suggest that intervention thresholds must be established well before populations decline to 100 individuals (approximately step 475 or 1.3 years), as the probability of irreversible collapse increases dramatically thereafter. The brief oscillation cycles imply that seasonal hunting moratoriums or resource supplementation might temporarily slow decline rates but cannot prevent ultimate extinction under sustained hunting pressure at this intensity. Conservation strategies must prioritize preventing the initial rapid decline phase rather than managing small remnant populations, as the high variance in final extinction timing offers no reliable window for recovery once populations enter the sparse regime below 50 individuals.
