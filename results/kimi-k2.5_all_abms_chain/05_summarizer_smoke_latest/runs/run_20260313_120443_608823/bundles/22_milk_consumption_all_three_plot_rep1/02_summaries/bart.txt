The model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. It captures how individual decision-making factors collectively drive macro-level market shifts. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers in their network. The mean weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately zero and remains negligible through the first two time steps. Consumption undergoes rapid exponential growth, increasing from near zero to approximately 1,200 to 1,280 units. Between steps 10 and 25, consumption moderates considerably, with consumption falling gradually from approximately 950 to 600 units. In the final steps (30-32), the metric stabilizes in the range of 220-250 units. The total number of milk choices made accumulates linearly from zero at time step 0, increasing at a constant rate of approximately 1,000 choices per time step to reach roughly 32,000 by time step 32. By the end of the simulation period, skimmed and semi-skimmed milk accounted for roughly 80 percent of all consumption decisions, capturing the historical transition from whole milk dominance to skimmed milk. The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed begins at approximately zero and undergoes a rapid increase throughout the initial phase. The trajectory indicates that market transitions away from established incumbent products follow a non-linear path with distinct phases—rapid initial response to new health and environmental information, a prolonged middle period of habit-driven resistance lasting 15-20 years, and a final acceleration as social norms solidify around new alternatives.
