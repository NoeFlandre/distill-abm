Mean whole milk consumption initiates at approximately 2,650 units at step 0, reflecting the initial market dominance of whole milk and strong incumbent habits among the agent population. Over the first ten steps, the metric undergoes a precipitous decline to roughly 950 units, representing a reduction of nearly 64%. This rapid early decrease suggests that despite high initial habit strength, agents rapidly incorporate health and environmental perception updates into their decision-making, triggering widespread abandonment of whole milk once the psychological threshold for habit disruption is crossed.

Between steps 10 and 25, the decline moderates considerably, with consumption falling gradually from approximately 950 to 600 units. This period exhibits minor fluctuations, including brief plateaus around steps 18-20 where consumption temporarily stabilizes or increases slightly before resuming its downward trajectory. The deceleration during this middle phase indicates the emergence of resistance mechanisms, where remaining whole milk consumers possess stronger habitual attachments or reduced susceptibility to social influence, requiring sustained normative pressure to induce further change.

A second sharp decline occurs between steps 25 and 30, with consumption dropping from roughly 600 to approximately 300 units. This late-stage acceleration likely reflects the activation of social conformity dynamics as alternative milk options achieve majority status, creating bandwagon effects that compel reluctant agents to switch. In the final steps (30-32), the metric stabilizes in the range of 220-250 units, indicating a persistent residual market of consumers resistant to dietary transition.

Across simulation runs, variance remains minimal during the initial phase but increases noticeably during the rapid decline periods (particularly steps 5-12 and 25-28), reflecting the stochastic nature of social interactions and probabilistic psychological mechanisms. Runs reconverge during stable periods, suggesting deterministic macro-level outcomes despite micro-level randomness in agent interactions.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The non-linear trajectory reveals that dietary transitions follow an S-curve pattern with distinct acceleration phases rather than linear decline. Public health interventions targeting the initial rapid decline phase (steps 0-10) would likely yield disproportionate impact by capitalizing on early adopter dynamics. However, decision makers should anticipate extended plateau periods requiring sustained effort before reaching the final tipping point (steps 25-30) where social norms drive rapid completion of the transition. The persistent stabilization at approximately 250 units suggests that universal adoption may be unattainable; resources may be more efficiently allocated to maintaining the transformed majority behavior rather than converting the final resistant minority.
