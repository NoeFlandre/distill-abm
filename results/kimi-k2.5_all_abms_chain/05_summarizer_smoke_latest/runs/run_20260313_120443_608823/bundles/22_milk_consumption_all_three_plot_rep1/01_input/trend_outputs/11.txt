The mean value health deviation metric begins at 0.0 at step 0, indicating initial alignment between consumer health perceptions and their milk consumption choices. During the first 10 time steps, the metric exhibits a steep upward trajectory, climbing to approximately 0.5 to 0.55. This rapid increase suggests the emergence of substantial cognitive dissonance as agents begin processing information about alternative milk health attributes while attempting to maintain strong habitual preferences for whole milk, creating growing tension between internal values and actual consumption behaviors.

Between steps 10 and 25, the growth rate attenuates significantly, entering a prolonged plateau phase where values increase gradually from roughly 0.55 to between 0.58 and 0.60. This deceleration corresponds to the core market transition period where social influence mechanisms and habit-breaking thresholds dominate decision-making, creating a temporary equilibrium in the population's value-behavior gap as agents partially justify new choices or update perceptions while residual habits persist.

From step 25 through the conclusion at step 32, the metric resumes a marked upward trend, accelerating to terminal values between 0.70 and 0.75. This late-stage increase coincides with the historical crossover period where skimmed and semi-skimmed consumption surpasses whole milk consumption, reflecting heightened psychological adjustments among the final cohort of consumers transitioning away from traditional preferences and experiencing amplified cognitive dissonance.

Across simulation runs, the metric displays low variance during the initial phase, with trajectories tightly clustered around the mean. Variance increases moderately during the middle and final stages, indicating divergent individual adjustment pathways as agents stochastically engage with social networks and justification mechanisms, though all runs converge on qualitatively similar macro-patterns of escalating value-behavior inconsistency.

The three-phase trajectory reveals that consumer health attitudes and behaviors diverge most dramatically during initial information exposure and final market transition phases, while remaining relatively stable during the mid-period habit-breaking process. The persistent upward trend and terminal acceleration suggest that the psychological mechanisms of justification and cognitive dissonance management insufficiently resolve the tension between evolving health perceptions and habitual consumption patterns over the 31-year simulation period.

For decision makers, the sustained growth in value-behavior deviation indicates that health-oriented messaging alone may generate psychological tension without immediate behavioral outcomes when confronting entrenched habits. The pronounced plateau phase suggests that interventions should specifically target habit maintenance thresholds during the middle transition period to prevent stagnation. Furthermore, the sharp acceleration in late-stage deviation implies that market tipping points create significant psychological resistance among remaining traditional consumers, suggesting that transitional support strategies or gradual habit-weakening approaches may be necessary to reduce cognitive dissonance and facilitate smoother adoption during rapid market shifts.
