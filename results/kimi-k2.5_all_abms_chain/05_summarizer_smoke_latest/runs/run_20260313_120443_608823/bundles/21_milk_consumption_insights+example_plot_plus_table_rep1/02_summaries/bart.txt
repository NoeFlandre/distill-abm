This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The simulation integrates theoretical frameworks with empirical data, employing calibration against observed macro-level consumption patterns to validate outcomes. An initial decline indicates that consumer populations are highly responsive to health and environmental messaging during early campaign phases. However, the persistent oscillations and gradual final-phase decline reveal that approximately 20-25% of consumers exhibit strong habit persistence requiring sustained social influence over extended periods. Policymakers should anticipate a residual market segment of hard-core consumers resistant to change. The total number of milk choices made by the agent population begins at zero and increases linearly throughout the simulation, reaching approximately 32,000 choices by the final time step. The average consumption of whole milk per person begins at approximately 2,655 units and undergoes a rapid decline over the first ten time steps, falling to roughly 950 units per week. The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed per agent begins at approximately 5.3 units during the initial time step, reflecting the historical market dominance of whole milk in 1974. Consumption continues its downward trajectory, ultimately reaching approximately 253 units. The trajectory exhibits decreasing trends across most intervals, with temporary stabilization periods around steps fifteen through nineteen and twenty-five through twenty-nine where the decline pauses briefly. The study found that habit formation for low-fat milk alternatives follows a predictable trajectory: an initial latent period where consumer behavior remains resistant to change, followed by rapid acceleration as social norms solidify, and finally a sustained linear growth phase representing entrenched behavioral patterns. Interventions targeting specific behavioral profiles must be deployed within the first quarter of the transition period. The persistent increase in deviation throughout the simulation suggests that aligning consumer behavior with health information requires addressing not only knowledge gaps but also the social influence and habit formation mechanisms that create friction in dietary transitions. For decision makers, these trends indicate that misalignment between environmental values and actual milk choices emerges almost instantaneously upon market introduction.
