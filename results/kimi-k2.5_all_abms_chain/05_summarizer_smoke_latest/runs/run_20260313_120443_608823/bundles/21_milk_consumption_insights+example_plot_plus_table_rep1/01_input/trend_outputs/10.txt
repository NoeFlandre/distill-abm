The divergence in choice function scores across the consumer population begins at zero, reflecting uniform initial preferences for whole milk among all agents at the start of the simulation. During the first seven time steps, heterogeneity increases rapidly, rising steeply to approximately 0.60 as agents begin differentiating their milk evaluations based on individual exposure to health and environmental information, social influence from network neighbors, and habit formation processes. This initial surge represents the quick emergence of distinct consumer segments, with some agents rapidly adopting favorable dispositions toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties while others maintain strong incumbent habits.

The rate of divergence moderates significantly around step 7 and again near step 12, transitioning from rapid differentiation to a more gradual expansion of the preference spectrum. Between steps 12 and 28, the metric increases slowly from approximately 0.64 to 0.73, indicating continued but decelerating polarization in consumer choice functions as the population settles into stable preference patterns. By step 32, the deviation reaches 0.75, suggesting that the system approaches a steady-state level of heterogeneity where the gap between the most progressive consumers and those most committed to traditional milk varieties stabilizes.

The progression exhibits remarkably low variance across simulation runs, indicating that the combination of Watts-Strogatz network topology, social influence mechanisms, and threshold-based disposition triggers produces consistent patterns of market segmentation. Brief oscillatory adjustments detected at intermediate intervals reflect periodic updates in agent evaluations as they process social information and resolve cognitive dissonance, though the overall trajectory remains monotonically increasing toward the asymptotic value.

For decision makers, the rapid initial divergence suggests that consumer segmentation in milk preferences emerges almost immediately following the introduction of alternative varieties and health information campaigns. Interventions targeting specific behavioral profiles must be deployed within the first quarter of the transition period, as distinct consumer archetypes solidify their evaluation frameworks early. The substantial persistent heterogeneity at later stages indicates that universal adoption of low-fat alternatives is unlikely; approximately 25% of the preference spectrum remains contested, with a stable minority resistant to social influence. This implies that dairy markets will sustain demand for traditional whole milk alongside alternative varieties, requiring continued product diversification rather than complete market transformation.
