This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The primary objective is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence interact within individual decision-making processes regarding milk choice. Average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5.3 units per agent, reflecting the historical market dominance of whole milk in the initial time period corresponding to 1974. Consumption increases rapidly and significantly, reaching approximately 1,265 units by step 11. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per person begins at approximately 5 units and undergoes rapid growth during the initial phase. The mean weekly whole milk consumption per person initiates at approximately 2,655 units, reflecting the 1974 market baseline of incumbent preference. The absence of oscillations or declines throughout the series suggests a monotonic, irreversible transition in aggregate consumption behavior. The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk initializes at a baseline value of 1.0, indicating no habitual reinforcement for alternative milk varieties at the simulation outset. The progression is marked by consistent monotonic growth with minimal volatility (standard deviation: 0.0110), maintaining significance across all rolling temporal windows. The population maintained moderate-to-high divergence in decision criteria across most of the simulation period. This suggests that the interaction between heterogeneous initial health perceptions (alt-health-mean-initial 1.69) and environmental concerns creates durable polarization in consumer decision frameworks.
