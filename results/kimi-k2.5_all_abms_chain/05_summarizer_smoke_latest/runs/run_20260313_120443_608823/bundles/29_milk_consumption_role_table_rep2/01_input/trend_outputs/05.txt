The simulation exhibits a perfectly linear accumulation of alternative milk choices over the 31-year period spanning 1974 to 2005. At the initial time step representing 1974, the metric stands at 0, indicating that no alternative milk choices have been recorded among the consumer population, consistent with the historical dominance of whole milk at the outset. From this origin, the metric increases at a constant rate of exactly 1,000 choices per time step throughout the entire simulation horizon. By the final time step corresponding to 2005, the cumulative total reaches 32,000 alternative milk choices. This uniform linear growth indicates that the transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties proceeded at a steady, unchanging pace without periods of accelerated adoption, saturation, or deceleration. In the context of the modeled psychological mechanisms, this constant rate suggests that the processes of habit disruption, social influence transmission, and perceptual updating regarding health and environmental attributes operated with uniform effectiveness throughout the period. Whether through threshold-based or probabilistic mechanisms of habit breaking, the aggregate consumer behavior produced a consistent flow of adoption decisions rather than the cascading dynamics or S-curve patterns typically associated with innovation diffusion processes. The absence of inflection points or oscillations further confirms that the modeled social network effects and normative pressures maintained a constant influence on individual decision-making across the entire temporal span.
