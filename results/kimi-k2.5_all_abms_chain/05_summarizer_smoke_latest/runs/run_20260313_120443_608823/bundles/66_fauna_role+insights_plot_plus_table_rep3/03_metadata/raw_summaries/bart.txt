 Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change . Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances . Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores .  Foragers-movement-cost ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1.0, and distance hunters can perceive and pursue prey is determined by foraging-radius . The number of human foragers remains constant at 20 individuals throughout the entire simulation period of 2,312 time steps .  The seasonal patterns indicate that interventions targeting resource availability during critical seasonal bottlenecks could potentially moderate the decline . Parameter adjustments reducing forager movement costs, increasing prey nutritional value, or reducing initial forager numbers may be necessary to achieve energetic sustainability .  80-90% of cumulative female mortality occurs within the first 500 days (1.4 years) of simulation . The model indicates that hunting pressure on female megaherbivores is front-loaded . Management interventions or policy responses to protect breeding females must be implemented extremely rapidly .  The trajectory reveals an extremely narrow intervention window for population conservation . The first 475 ticks (approximately 1.3 years) constitute the critical period during which hunting pressure drives the most precipitous decline in breeding female numbers . Once the cumulative mortality exceeds 60 individuals (tick 475), the reproductive foundation of the herd is already compromised .  The population of female megaherbivores exhibits a deterministic collapse from an initial cohort of 125 individuals to complete extinction by step 2,311 . The decline occurs in distinct phases demarcated by change points at approximately steps 475, 600, and 875 . After step 875, the population enters a terminal persistence phase characterized by prolonged residence at extremely low densities (fewer than 5 individuals) with high stochastic volatility .  The configuration of 20 human foragers with the specified energetic and environmental parameters creates an unsustainable hunting pressure that drives megaherbivores to extinction within 6.3 years (2,311 ticks) The rapid initial collapse suggests that management interventions would need to occur within the first 1-2 years (first 500-700 ticks) to prevent irreversible population decline .  The aggregate total energy of female megaherbivores initiated at approximately 12,500 units and underwent a deterministic collapse to zero by tick 2,311 (approximately 6.3 years), indicating complete population extinction across all simulation replicates . The current configuration drives an irreversible decline despite oscillatory fluctuations and temporary demographic recoveries .  The MHPM framework suggests that hunter densities of 20 individuals per patch landscape exceed the sustainable carrying capacity for megaherbivore populations of 250 animals, even with modest environmental seasonality . The results suggest that conservation interventions must occur within the first 1–1.5 years of observed population stress after which recovery probability diminishes significantly .
