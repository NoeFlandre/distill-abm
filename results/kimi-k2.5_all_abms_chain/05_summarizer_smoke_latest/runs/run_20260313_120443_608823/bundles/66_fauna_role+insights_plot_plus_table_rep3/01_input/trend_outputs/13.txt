  "content": "The aggregate mean trajectory of total prey energy across 120 simulation runs initiates at approximately 16,667 energy units and declines deterministically to extinction (0 units) by time step 2,311. The temporal dynamics exhibit three distinct phases superimposed with cyclical variation.\n\nDuring the initial phase (time steps 0–350), the system undergoes a steep monotonic decrease punctuated by high-amplitude oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 time steps. Local maxima reach roughly 4,100 units at time steps 385 and 388, declining progressively to approximately 3,760 by time step 433, while corresponding minima trough at similar magnitudes, reflecting seasonal energy fluctuations within an overall collapse trajectory. This rapid attrition reduces aggregate energy by roughly 75% within the first year (365 time steps), with an inflection point at time step 309 marking a brief deceleration before the descent continues.\n\nThe intermediate phase (time steps 350–875) witnesses continued decline but with diminishing oscillation amplitudes. By time step 709, peak values reach only approximately 883 units, with troughs near 869 units at time step 708, representing an order-of-magnitude reduction from initial oscillation peaks. Change points at time steps 500 and 625 demarcate shifts in decline rates, with the slope decreasing from -36.0 to -11.0 and eventually to -3.1 units per time step by time step 875, indicating progressively slowing but persistent energy depletion.\n\nThe terminal phase (time steps 875–2,311) exhibits low-level volatility around the extinction threshold. Following a change point near time step 875, energy fluctuates at reduced magnitudes (median 146 units across all observations), with transient increases detected between time steps 1,392–1,623 and 1,856–2,087, where brief positive slopes emerge despite the overall downward trajectory. However, these recovery attempts prove insufficient, culminating in deterministic extinction at the final time step.\n\nStatistically, the distribution of energy values remains highly right-skewed (mean 2,369 vs. median 146), indicating that while most simulations maintained low energy reserves for extended periods, a subset retained higher biomass temporarily. The standard deviation of 4,361 units reflects this heterogeneity in collapse timing across the 120 runs.\n\nInterpreted within the MHPM framework, the initial 16,667 units represent the energetic baseline of 250 initial ungulates facing 20 human foragers with food storage capabilities and a 6-patch foraging radius. The 63-time-step oscillation cycle corresponds to seasonal vegetation pulsing (seasonality parameter 0.8), creating regular energetic bottlenecks that accelerate when superimposed on hunting mortality. The eventual extinction across all replicates demonstrates that under these parameter configurations—specifically the combination of high seasonality, limited grass recovery (3 ticks), and sustained human predation with processing costs favoring males (30 units) over females (27 units)—the prey population experiences irreversible energetic collapse within approximately 6.3 years (2,311 days).\n\nFor decision-makers, these results indicate that hunter densities of 20 individuals per 211×211 patch landscape exceed the sustainable carrying capacity for megaherbivore populations of 250 animals, even with modest environmental seasonality. The pronounced early decline (first 350–500 time steps) suggests that conservation interventions must occur within the first 1–1.5 years of observed population stress, after which recovery probability diminishes significantly despite temporary fluctuations. Furthermore, the persistent 63-time-step oscillation pattern implies that management strategies must account for seasonal bottlenecks where combined environmental and predation pressures maximize extinction risk."
