The simulation of British milk consumption dynamics from 1974 to 2005 reveals a trajectory of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk adoption characterized by three distinct phases: rapid initial growth, an extended period of market stabilization, and a secondary expansion phase.

At the outset (step 0), aggregate consumption begins at a minimal baseline of approximately 5.3 units per agent per week, reflecting the historical dominance of whole milk in 1974. During the initial growth phase spanning steps 0 through 9, consumption increases monotonically and significantly, with statistical tests confirming strongly increasing trends (tau coefficients of 0.90 and 1.00). By step 9, consumption reaches approximately 1200 units, representing the first major wave of consumer transition away from whole milk. This acceleration aligns with initial shifts in health perceptions and the beginning of social influence propagation through the network structure.

Following this initial surge, the system enters a prolonged plateau phase from steps 10 through 24, where consumption stabilizes in the 1180–1265 unit range. Notable local maxima occur at step 11 (1265.5 units) and step 21 (1226.9 units), while local minima appear at steps 12 (1233.6), 18 (1183.4), 20 (1214.5), and 23 (1209.7). Statistical analysis confirms no significant trend during steps 10–14 and 15–19, with slightly negative but non-significant slopes, indicating market saturation or equilibrium between habit maintenance and persuasive influences during this period. This plateau likely represents the maturation of initial adopter segments and the temporary entrenchment of habitual behaviors among resistant consumers, with network interactions (occurring at probability 0.3) producing localized oscillations but no net macro-level change.

A final growth phase emerges from step 25 onward, characterized by a statistically significant increasing trend (tau=1.00, slope=48.5) that drives consumption from approximately 1220 units to a terminal value of 1456.6 units at step 32. This secondary expansion suggests the eventual success of cumulative social influence and perception shifts—driven by environmental and health attribute weightings—overcoming the initially strong habitual attachment to whole milk (incumbent habit strength 10.0). The final consumption level represents a 275-fold increase from initial conditions, indicating the complete reversal of market dominance from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties by 2005.

The variance structure shows high dispersion (standard deviation 405.1) relative to the mean (1051.6), with the median (1220.2) substantially exceeding the mean, indicating that the early low-consumption steps and transitional dynamics pull the average downward while the majority of the time series operates at elevated plateau levels. Oscillation patterns with a dominant period of 15 steps suggest underlying cyclical dynamics in consumer decision-making, possibly reflecting feedback loops between social conformity mechanisms and individual justification processes.
