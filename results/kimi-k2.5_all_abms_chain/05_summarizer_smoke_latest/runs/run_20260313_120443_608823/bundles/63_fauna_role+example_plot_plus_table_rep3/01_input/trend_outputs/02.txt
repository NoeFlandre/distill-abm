The total energy available to all forager bands initiates at 2000 units and experiences a sustained monotonic decline throughout the simulation, ultimately reaching approximately -38229 units by step 2310, with a minimum recorded value of -38233. This trajectory reflects a persistent energetic deficit wherein cumulative movement costs and processing expenditures consistently exceed caloric returns from hunting, driving the aggregate forager energy balance into increasingly negative territory despite the activation of energy storage mechanisms.

During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 400 steps, the aggregate energy plummets rapidly from 2000 to roughly -2000, exhibiting substantial transient variation across simulation runs. This early divergence suggests stochastic differences in initial spatial distributions of prey and hunting success, yet the uniform downward trajectory indicates that optimal foraging strategies cannot maintain positive energy balances against baseline movement costs of 1 unit per step and processing costs ranging from 3 to 6 units per carcass.

Between approximately step 400 and 1500, the rate of energy depletion accelerates markedly, with values declining from near zero to approximately -23800. Statistical analysis confirms significant monotonic decreasing trends across all examined time windows (Mann-Kendall tau approaching -1.0, p < 0.001), with the slope of decline steepening from approximately -11 units per step to -20 units per step. The progressive steepening of this decline, punctuated by change points at steps 650, 1075, and 1500, indicates deteriorating foraging efficiency likely corresponding to prey depletion dynamics and the increasing search costs associated with locating rare, dispersed megaherbivore herds.

In the final phase from step 1500 to 2310, the decline continues at a steep rate of approximately -20 units per step. Superimposed on this long-term trend, oscillations with a dominant periodicity of 63 steps reflect the model's seasonal component (seasonality = 0.8), where cyclic variations in grass availability and prey aggregation create rhythmic fluctuations in hunting returns. The convergence of simulation runs during the latter stages indicates deterministic outcomes emerging from resource depression, as the ungulate population subjected to birth spacing intervals of 730 ticks and external mortality pressures cannot sustain sufficient densities to support positive energy balance for the forager population, resulting in the observed progressive energy debt accumulation.
