The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model shows a sustained, monotonic decline in aggregate forager energy over 2,312 time steps. The number of foragers remained constant at 20 agents throughout the entire simulation duration. The metric exhibited zero variance and zero standard deviation, with minimum, maximum, mean, median values all fixed at 20. The early rapid accumulation phase (first 500-1000 steps) represents the critical window where hunting pressure most significantly impacts male demographics. Once approximately 110-120 males are culled, the system reaches equilibrium where additional hunting efforts yield diminishing returns, likely due to prey depletion or behavioral adaptations. Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model suggests that forager bands efficiently target postpartum females during initial simulation phases when encounter rates are favorable. The front-loaded mortality pattern demonstrates that hunting impacts on the most demographically vulnerable segment (reproductive females) concentrate within the first 500-600 time steps. The trajectory exhibited three distinct phases: an initial rapid collapse, an extended period of low-density fluctuation, and final extinction. By step 475, the population had fallen below 50 animals in most simulation runs. The terminal phase begins around step 2,000–2,310, characterized by final attrition. The simulation demonstrates that a forager density of 20 immortal hunters creates deterministic extinction pressure for prey populations of this life history type. Management interventions must either reduce hunter density, remove immortality constraints on hunters, or substantially increase prey fecundity (reduce birth spacing below 730 steps) to achieve sustainability. Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model simulations show rapid collapse from 12,500 to approximately 2,000–4,000 units occurring within the first 375 steps. The median energy value of 100 units across the simulation span, contrasted with the mean of 1,569 units, indicates a highly right-skewed distribution of extreme values during early steps. The percentage of green patches in the simulated grassland environment initiates at approximately 33.0%. Within the first annual cycle (at tick 365), the metric undergoes a precipitous decline, reaching its absolute minimum value of 24.91%. This represents a reduction of roughly eight percentage points, or 24% relative to initial coverage. Management approaches must therefore prioritize maintaining total population energy above the 10,000-unit threshold observed at steps 300-400.
