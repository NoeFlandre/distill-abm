The simulations consistently demonstrate deterministic extinction of female ungulate populations under the current parameter configuration. Beginning with an initial cohort of 125 individuals, all 40 replicate runs converge toward elimination of females, with the aggregate mean declining to zero by approximately step 2,311.

The extinction trajectory follows a markedly non-linear pattern characterized by three distinct phases. An initial rapid contraction occurs within the first 475 steps, during which populations drop precipitously from 125 to approximately 20–40 individuals. This phase transitions around step 475–600 into a prolonged period of decelerated decline marked by significant stochastic oscillations. During this middle phase (roughly steps 600–2,000), populations fluctuate between local maxima and minima, with temporary resurgences reaching mean values of approximately 6.5 individuals around step 730 and 5.0 around step 710, interspersed with declines to levels near 4.6–5.0. Secondary minor peaks occur around steps 800–900 with values of 4.3–2.7 individuals. These oscillations exhibit a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 steps, suggesting regular demographic cycles potentially linked to seasonal resource pulses (seasonality parameter 0.8) or reproductive intervals (birth spacing 730 ticks equaling two years).

The terminal phase begins around step 2,000–2,310, characterized by final attrition as remaining individuals dwindle to zero, confirmed by the change point detected at step 2,310. Throughout the trajectory, significant inflection points at steps 305, 740, 833, and 1,309 indicate shifts in the velocity of population decline, with the rate of decrease slowing substantially after the initial crash but never reversing sufficiently to prevent eventual extinction. Statistical trend analysis confirms predominantly monotonic decline across nearly all observation windows, with the rolling Mann-Kendall test revealing significantly decreasing trends (tau approaching -1.0) in early windows, followed by continued decline at attenuated rates (slopes of -0.019 to -0.006) in middle phases. Brief periods of statistically significant increase occur only at very low population densities (around steps 1,160–1,391 and 1,856–2,087), likely representing demographic stochasticity or Allee effect dynamics rather than recovery.

Cross-run variability remains substantial throughout the simulations, evidenced by the large standard deviation of 31.55 relative to the mean of 16.48, though the median value of 1.0 indicates that by the end of most individual runs, populations had collapsed to single individuals or zero. The oscillation indices spanning steps 45–140 confirm persistent cyclical fluctuations even as the overall trend declines.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The consistent outcome of complete extinction across all replications—despite initial population viability, moderate fecundity, and environmental grass regrowth—indicates that the current combination of hunting pressure (20 active foragers with low processing costs differential), environmental seasonality constraints, and habitat coverage (33% grass proportion) creates mortality rates exceeding reproductive replacement. The extended duration of decline, spanning over six years at the calibrated rate of 365 ticks per year, suggests a temporal window exists for potential intervention. However, the absence of any stable equilibria or recovery trajectories indicates that without parameter modification—such as reduced hunter density, enhanced resource availability, or modified processing costs—population persistence remains impossible under current conditions. The oscillatory pattern of decline during intermediate population levels (steps 600–1,500) presents a particular risk for monitoring, as temporary plateaus or small rebounds at 4–7 individuals could be misinterpreted as stabilization rather than the terminal fluctuations of a deterministic extinction trajectory.
