Across all repeated simulation runs, the cumulative count of postpartum female prey killed initiates at zero and increases monotonically throughout the observation period. The accumulation follows a characteristic trajectory of rapid early growth followed by marked deceleration and asymptotic stabilization at values ranging between approximately 105 and 125 individuals.

During the initial phase spanning roughly steps 0 to 600, the cumulative count rises steeply, with most simulations accumulating between 80 and 115 kills by step 500. The pace of accumulation varies considerably across runs; approximately half of the trajectories exceed 100 cumulative kills by step 500, while others reach comparable levels between steps 600 and 800. One anomalous run exhibits negligible accumulation for the first approximately 200 steps before undergoing rapid compensatory increase that aligns with the general cluster by step 500.

Following this initial rapid phase, the rate of accumulation decelerates substantially. Between approximately steps 600 and 1500, most trajectories plateau or exhibit only minimal incremental increases, stabilizing at final cumulative values predominantly between 115 and 122, with extremes ranging from approximately 105 to 125. The discrete, step-wise pattern of increase reflects the stochastic timing of individual hunting events inherent in agent-based interactions.

The pronounced targeting of postpartum females—who under the specified parameters offer the highest net energetic return (30 total food value minus 3 processing cost)—demonstrates the operation of dynamic optimal foraging theory among hunter agents. The consistent asymptotic ceiling of approximately 100 to 125 cumulative kills indicates that hunting pressure on this demographic eventually becomes negligible, likely reflecting the depletion of postpartum females from the prey population, severe reduction in encounter rates, or demographic restructuring of herds that reduces the availability of this high-value prey type. This concentration of mortality on reproductive females carrying dependent offspring represents a particularly severe predation pressure, as each successful hunt effectively removes both the adult female and her nursing calf from the population, contributing to the extinction dynamics under investigation.
