In all simulation runs, the total prey population initiated at 250 individuals and exhibited a consistent trajectory of steep decline toward extinction under hunting pressure. By approximately step 500, populations across most runs had diminished to between 0 and 50 animals, representing a decline of roughly 80-100% from initial conditions. The rate of decline showed substantial variation between runs: some populations collapsed to fewer than 10 individuals within the first 400 steps, while others maintained higher numbers (100-200 individuals) for several hundred steps before entering rapid decline phases. Between steps 500 and 1000, continued attrition reduced most populations to 10 or fewer individuals. By step 1500, the majority of runs had reached complete or near-complete extinction (0-5 animals), though isolated runs maintained small remnant populations of 1-3 individuals persisting until approximately step 2000. Given the model configuration with 20 immortal foragers (who cannot die from energy depletion) and active hunting behavior based on optimal foraging calculations, the data indicate that hunting pressure consistently exceeded the prey population's reproductive capacity to replace losses. The variation in extinction timing suggests stochastic effects in hunter-prey encounters, herd movement patterns, or demographic stochasticity in reproduction (governed by the 730-tick birth spacing interval), but the overarching pattern demonstrates that the combination of continuous hunting pressure, environmental constraints (seasonality set to 0.8 and grass proportion of 0.33), and life history parameters (maximum lifespan 10220 ticks, senescence at 7300 ticks) resulted in progressive population collapse across all replicates.
