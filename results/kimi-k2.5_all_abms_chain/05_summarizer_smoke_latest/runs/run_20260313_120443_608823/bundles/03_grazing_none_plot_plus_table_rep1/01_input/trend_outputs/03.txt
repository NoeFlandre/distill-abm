The mean reserve biomass begins at 90,000 units and follows a trajectory characterized by an initial decline, a minimum point, and subsequent recovery to levels exceeding initial conditions. A minor early fluctuation produces a local maximum of approximately 90,523 at step 3, after which the metric enters a sustained decreasing trend. Significant negative trends persist through step 21, with the most rapid declines occurring in the earliest steps. The trajectory reaches its minimum value of 84,114.07 at step 26, representing the lowest biomass state of the system.

Between steps 22 and 32, the dynamics stabilize, exhibiting no significant trend, which suggests a transitional period in the vegetation dynamics. From step 33 onward, a persistent increasing trend emerges and continues through the remainder of the simulation. The recovery accelerates significantly between steps 44 and 54, during which the biomass increases at a rate of approximately 1,909 units per step—the steepest positive slope observed. While the growth rate gradually decelerates in subsequent periods—slowing to roughly 643 units per step between steps 55 and 65, then to 93 units per step between steps 66 and 76, and continuing to diminish through step 98—the overall accumulation continues unabated, albeit at progressively slower rates.

By step 100, the reserve biomass reaches 121,072.59, marking a 34.5% increase from initial conditions and a 44% gain from the step 26 minimum. Key transition points occur at steps 8 and 48, indicating shifts between the initial decline and stabilization phases, and between rapid and slowing growth phases, respectively. A fundamental regime change is detected at step 50, separating the degradation phase from the recovery phase.

Across the 30 simulation replicates, final values converge tightly around 121,000 despite variability during the initial decline, with an overall series mean of 103,366.26 and median of 104,366.55. This aggregate trajectory demonstrates that initial livestock pressure from the starting herd size of 90 animals per household temporarily depletes reserve biomass below starting levels across the 60 private property patches. However, the presence of adaptive learning mechanisms—including learning-by-doing (E-LBD) and social learning (E-RO-SL1) behaviors—enables heterogeneous households to adjust their grazing strategies based on observed vegetation states and neighbor performance. This adaptation facilitates a system-wide convergence toward sustainable high-biomass equilibria that substantially exceed initial system conditions, suggesting that learning-enabled adaptive co-management in private property contexts can successfully reverse initial overgrazing trends and enhance long-term pasture resilience.
