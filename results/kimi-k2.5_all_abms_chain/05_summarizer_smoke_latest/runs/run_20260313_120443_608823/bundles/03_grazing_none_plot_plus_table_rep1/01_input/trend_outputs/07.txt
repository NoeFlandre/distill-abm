The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock across the 60-household system initiates at effectively zero, indicating perfect equality in livestock health distribution at the outset, and exhibits a monotonic increase throughout the 100-step simulation period. By the conclusion of the simulation timeframe, the metric reaches a mean value of approximately 0.073 across the 30 simulation runs, though individual trajectories display considerable heterogeneity in their terminal values, ranging from roughly 0.03 to above 0.09.

The temporal progression reveals distinct phases in the development of inequality. During the initial 20 steps, values remain relatively compressed near zero, with slow incremental growth generally below 0.015. Between steps 20 and 40, the rate of increase accelerates modestly, with values typically spanning 0.01 to 0.03. A pronounced inflection occurs around steps 40 to 60, where many trajectories exhibit steep acceleration; by step 60, values range approximately 0.03 to 0.05. In the final 40 steps, divergence intensifies dramatically, with some trajectories maintaining moderate growth rates while others experience rapid escalation, creating a wide dispersion by step 100 where the inter-simulation variance peaks.

Statistical analysis confirms consistently significant increasing trends across all decile windows of the time series, with monotonic upward progression maintained throughout the entire duration. Change points in the aggregated series occur at steps 51, 71, and 100, corresponding to transitions in the rate of inequality accumulation. Multiple inflection points cluster in the early phase (steps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25) and mid-phase (steps 37, 57), suggesting successive regime shifts in the underlying distribution dynamics.

Within the context of the private property grazing system with heterogeneous learning behaviors, this pattern indicates that initial parity in healthy livestock holdings gives way to increasing stratification as the three behavioral types—E-RO (baseline), E-LBD (learning-by-doing), and E-RO-SL1 (social learning)—diverge in their adaptive responses to ecological variability and learning opportunities. The widening spread across simulation runs suggests that stochastic elements such as rainfall variation interact with the fixed heterogeneity in risk attitudes and learning mechanisms to produce divergent accumulation trajectories. The acceleration phase beginning around step 40 implies positive feedback mechanisms, likely related to compound learning effects and biomass availability differences across privately held patches, whereby households that initially gain advantages in livestock health are able to leverage these gains disproportionately over time.
