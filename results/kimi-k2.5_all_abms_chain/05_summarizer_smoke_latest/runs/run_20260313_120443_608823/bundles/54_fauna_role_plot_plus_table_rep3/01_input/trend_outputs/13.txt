The aggregate total energy of the megaherbivore population exhibits a trajectory of rapid initial collapse followed by prolonged low-level persistence and eventual complete extinction. At initialization, the system maintains approximately 16,667 total energy units across the ungulate population. This value undergoes a steep decline during the first 350-500 ticks, with the rate of decrease reaching slopes as steep as -36 energy units per tick in the interval between ticks 232-463, representing the most rapid phase of population energy loss.

By approximately tick 500, the total energy has diminished by over 95% of its initial value, entering a phase of sustained low-level fluctuation. Between ticks 500 and 1400, the population energy persists at reduced levels, generally below 1,000 units, with localized peaks occurring around ticks 385-440 (where transient recoveries reach approximately 3,700-4,100 units) and again near ticks 709-730 (reaching 800-1,080 units). These fluctuations occur with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks, consistent with strong seasonal environmental forcing.

Notably, between ticks 1392-1623, the trend briefly reverses to a slightly increasing pattern (slope +0.034), suggesting a temporary demographic recovery or slowdown in hunting pressure. However, this is followed by resumed decline between ticks 1624-1855. A second brief stabilization occurs between ticks 1856-2087, though with negligible slope.

The final phase of collapse accelerates after tick 2310, culminating in complete extirpation of the megaherbivore population energy reserve by tick 2311 (approximately 6.3 years in simulation time). The statistical distribution is highly right-skewed, with a median energy value of only 146.375 units compared to the mean of 2,368.77 units, indicating that for the majority of the simulation duration, the population existed at critically reduced energy levels following the initial collapse. The oscillatory nature of the decline, superimposed on the overall decreasing trajectory, reflects the interplay between seasonal resource availability, ungulate reproductive biology (with interbirth intervals of 730 ticks), and sustained hunting pressure from the forager population.
