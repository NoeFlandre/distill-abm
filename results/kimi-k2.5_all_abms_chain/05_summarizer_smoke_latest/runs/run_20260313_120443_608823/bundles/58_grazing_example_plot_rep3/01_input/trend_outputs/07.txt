The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock begins at approximately zero across all simulation runs, reflecting an initially equal distribution of healthy animals among households. During the first 15 to 20 steps, values remain near zero, rising only marginally to between 0.005 and 0.015. This early period of parity corresponds to the initial phase where households operate with similar herd sizes and vegetation biomass has not yet differentiated significantly across private property patches.

Between steps 20 and 50, the metric increases at a moderate pace, reaching values ranging from approximately 0.010 to 0.030 by step 40, and between 0.025 and 0.045 by step 50. During this intermediate phase, divergence across simulation runs becomes noticeable as differences in household decision-making strategies, local pasture conditions, and learning trajectories begin to generate heterogeneity in livestock health outcomes.

After step 50, the rate of increase accelerates substantially, with the Gini coefficient rising steeply through step 100. By the simulation's conclusion, values span a broad range from approximately 0.030 to 0.095. This accelerating trajectory indicates that inequality in healthy livestock distribution compounds over time as the feedback loops between pasture quality, livestock health, and adaptive decision-making amplify initial small disparities. The wide spread of outcomes at step 100, covering a range of approximately 0.065, suggests that the interaction of heterogeneous risk attitudes, distinct behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), and stochastic vegetation dynamics under private property regimes produces significantly different inequality endpoints depending on the specific paths of household learning and ecological variation. The pattern demonstrates that even with initially equivalent conditions, the combination of private property boundaries, learning processes, and climatic variability inevitably generates increasing concentration of healthy livestock among subsets of households, with the pace of inequality generation intensifying markedly in the latter half of the simulation period.
