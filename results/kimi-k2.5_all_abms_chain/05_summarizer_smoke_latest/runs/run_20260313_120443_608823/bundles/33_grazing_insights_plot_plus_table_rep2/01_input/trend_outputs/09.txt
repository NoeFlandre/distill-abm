The mean destock total of households initiates at zero and demonstrates a consistent monotonic increase throughout the 100-step simulation period. During the initial phase spanning approximately steps 0 through 25, accumulation occurs rapidly with steep slopes measuring 4.96 units per step in the earliest interval and 1.47 units per step through step 21. Around step 25, identified as a primary change point, the rate of accumulation decelerates substantially to approximately 0.31 to 0.35 units per step in subsequent intervals. A secondary change point emerges near step 60, though the upward trajectory persists through the terminal step 100, reaching a mean value of 96.78 units across simulation series.

Across the 30 simulation series, the overall mean destock total averages 75.48 across all steps and series, with a median value of 81.17. However, substantial heterogeneity exists across individual simulation runs, with final values ranging from approximately 58 to 173, reflecting significant dispersion around the central tendency as evidenced by a standard deviation of 20.30. Statistical testing confirms consistently significant increasing trends across all temporal windows, with Mann-Kendall tau values of 1.000 and p-values of 0.000 throughout the entire simulation duration.

For decision makers, the persistent upward trend in destocking totals indicates that households continually adjust livestock holdings downward over time, suggesting initial stocking rates may exceed sustainable carrying capacities or that environmental variability necessitates ongoing herd reduction. The early rapid accumulation followed by slower but continued growth implies immediate destocking pressures are intense but extend throughout the simulation period, highlighting the need for early intervention strategies and long-term destocking support mechanisms. The wide variation across scenarios suggests behavioral diversity and initial conditions significantly impact destocking outcomes, indicating that uniform policy approaches may be less effective than adaptive management strategies accounting for household heterogeneity.
