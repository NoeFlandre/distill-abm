The simulation depicts a pronounced long-term decline in whole milk consumption, falling from approximately 2,655 units per agent per week to 253 units over the 32-year period spanning 1974 to 2005. This trajectory reflects the modeled transition from initial market conditions where whole milk dominated consumer preferences toward eventual prevalence of skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives.

The decline occurs through distinct phases characterized by markedly varying rates of change. During the initial five time steps, consumption decreases rapidly from starting levels. Between indices 5 and 9, the rate of decline accelerates substantially, representing the most intensive period of behavioral transition within the simulated population as agents begin considering and adopting alternatives. Subsequently, from indices 10 through 14, the descent moderates considerably, though remaining consistently negative with values decreasing at a gentler pace.

A notable shift emerges between indices 15 and 19, where the previously steady decline temporarily stabilizes. Consumption levels fluctuate around approximately 760 to 774 units during this phase, exhibiting local minima and maxima including a valley at index 18 followed by a peak at index 19. This plateau suggests a period of temporary equilibrium in adoption behaviors, potentially reflecting resistance to change among agents with strong habit formation or those operating near cognitive dissonance thresholds that delay further transitions.

Following this stabilization, a gradual decline resumes from indices 20 through 24, characterized by a substantially gentler slope compared to earlier phases. The trajectory then enters a variable period between indices 25 and 29, where no significant directional trend dominates statistically, and values oscillate between approximately 323 and 621 units. These fluctuations include a peak at index 27, giving way to final values near 253 units by the simulation's conclusion at index 32.

Superimposed on this long-term decline, the series exhibits oscillatory behavior with a dominant periodicity of approximately 15 time steps. Local peaks emerge at indices 19, 27, and 30, with corresponding troughs appearing at indices 18, 26, and 29. This cyclical variation suggests that social influence mechanisms operating through the Watts-Strogatz network topology create temporary reversals or pauses in the abandonment of whole milk consumption, even as the overall transition toward alternative milk varieties proceeds through the population.

The median consumption level of approximately 817 units indicates that for roughly half the simulation duration, weekly consumption remained above this value, concentrated particularly in the earlier phases before the accelerated decline of the late 1970s and early 1980s, followed by the subsequent plateau and final descent of the 1990s and early 2000s. The substantial variance in consumption levels across the series reflects heterogeneity in agent decision-making, where individual differences in social susceptibility, habit strength, and perception characteristics generate divergent trajectories within the population-level aggregate despite the overarching trend toward reduced whole milk consumption.
