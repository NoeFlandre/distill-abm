The consumption of whole milk exhibits a pronounced secular decline from an initial dominant market position to marginal consumption levels over the temporal span representing 1974 to 2005. At inception, average weekly consumption registers approximately 2655 units per person, reflecting the strong habitual attachment to whole milk characterizing the 1974 baseline population with high incumbent habit strength. This elevated initial value diminishes rapidly during the first ten time steps, proceeding through two consecutive phases of steep contraction with significantly decreasing trends observed between time steps 0–4 and 5–9. This early acceleration suggests an initial period where disposition mechanisms—whether threshold-based or probability-based—successfully trigger reconsideration of milk choices among agents possessing lower habit persistence or heightened susceptibility to health perceptions and environmental concerns.

Following this rapid transition, the rate of decline moderates considerably between time steps 10–14, indicating a slowing diffusion as the remaining pool of whole milk consumers likely comprises agents with stronger habit formations or reduced social connectivity. A distinct plateau emerges between time steps 15–19, where consumption stabilizes in the range of 760–774 units, exhibiting no statistically significant directional trend. This stagnation implies a temporarily resistant subpopulation maintaining whole milk consumption despite the accumulation of social norms favoring lower-fat alternatives within the Watts-Strogatz network topology.

The pattern resumes its downward trajectory between time steps 20–24, driving consumption from approximately 773 units down to roughly 594 units, representing a secondary wave of behavioral change among previously resistant agents potentially influenced by sustained social conformity pressures and cognitive dissonance mechanisms. Subsequently, between time steps 25–29, the series enters another period of relative stability with continued but statistically insignificant gradual decline, oscillating between approximately 323 and 621 units.

By the conclusion at time step 32, consumption converges toward approximately 253 units per person, representing roughly a 90 percent reduction from initial levels. Throughout the latter half of the observation window, minor oscillations punctuate the trend, with local peaks registered at time steps 19, 27, and 30, separated by valleys at time steps 18, 26, and 29. These fluctuations likely reflect temporary variations in social influence propagation and habit reinforcement dynamics among the 1,000 agents. The overall trajectory illustrates the interaction between memory-limited decision processes, social susceptibility parameters, and the progressive erosion of incumbent habits through repeated exposure to alternative choice characteristics, ultimately resulting in the historical transition from whole milk dominance to skimmed and semi-skimmed prevalence.
