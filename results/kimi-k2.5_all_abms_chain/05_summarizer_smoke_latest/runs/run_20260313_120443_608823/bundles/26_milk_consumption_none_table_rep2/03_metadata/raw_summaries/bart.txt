 This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005 . It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence, and post-choice evaluation .  The simulation of British milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 shows a continuous monotonic increase in the aggregate adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk alternatives among the 1,000-agent population . The trajectory unfolds by evolving velocities of behavioral change, characterized by evolving Velocities characterized by growing velocity of behavioral habits and high initial resistance to disposition to disposition .  The simulation of milk consumption adoption from 1974 to 2005 demonstrates a substantial long-term increase in the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per person . Social norms (enabled at conformity weight 0.28) and social susceptibility (0.2) combine with memory lifetimes (5.0) to facilitate irreversible habit formation toward skimmed milk varieties .  The mean habit factor applied to whole milk choice functions initiates at a baseline neutral weighting of 1.0000 and undergoes a gradual, monotonic increase throughout the simulation period . This trajectory indicates a cumulative reinforcement of habitual preferences for whole milk, with the multiplicative habit influence growing by roughly 5% relative to initial conditions .  The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the health values implied by their milk choices demonstrates a persistent monotonic increase from 0.0000 at the initial time step to 0.7251 at the conclusion of the simulation period . Inflection points in the trajectory occurred around 1981, 1986, and 2002, corresponding to structural shifts in how rapidly the population was differentiating .  The rapid increase during the first thirteen years suggests that as agents adopted new milk types, their environmental valuations did not immediately adjust to match these new consumption patterns . The subsequent stabilization at a high level from 1987 through 2005 suggests that this gap between environmental attitudes and actual consumption behaviors became entrenched, persisting throughout the later simulation years .
