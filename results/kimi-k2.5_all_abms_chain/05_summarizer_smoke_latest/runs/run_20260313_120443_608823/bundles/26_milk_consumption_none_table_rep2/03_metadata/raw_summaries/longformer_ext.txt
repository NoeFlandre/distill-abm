This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005, replicating individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The simulation integrates theoretical frameworks with empirical data, employing calibration against observed macro-level consumption patterns to validate outcomes. Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment, each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology, where they can sense and influence other agents' choice functions through interaction probabilities. Additionally, all agents in the simulation are members of the Watts-Strogatz network, representing the population as a whole, representing the market dominance of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk alternatives among the 1,000-agent population.The primary objective is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence interact within individual decision-making processes regarding milk choice, with the main outcome metric being average weekly consumption per person for each milk type.
