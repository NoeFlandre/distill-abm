The cumulative prey mortality exhibits a nonlinear accumulation pattern across the simulation timeframe, characterized by three distinct dynamical phases. Initially, from the start through approximately tick 850, deaths accumulate at a decelerating but consistently positive rate, transitioning through multiple regime changes at roughly 125-tick intervals. During this establishment phase, the mortality rate is highest, with the cumulative count rising steeply before the slope begins attenuating around tick 362.

From tick 850 through approximately tick 1856, the system enters a quasi-equilibrium phase where mortality accumulation slows dramatically and stabilizes. The cumulative death count plateaus around 237-240 individuals, with the absolute maximum mean value of approximately 240.3 deaths occurring at tick 1876. This plateau suggests the prey population has reached a demographic equilibrium where births approximately offset deaths from both natural causes and predation, or where hunting pressure has reduced population density sufficiently to limit further encounter rates.

Subsequent to tick 1876, the cumulative mean exhibits an anomalous slight decline from 240.3 to 235 by tick 2312, accompanied by oscillatory dynamics with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks (roughly seasonal or sub-seasonal cycles given the 365-tick annual parameterization). This late-phase decline indicates heterogeneity across simulation runs, wherein populations experiencing higher mortality rates likely underwent extinction earlier and were subsequently excluded from the ongoing mean calculation, leaving only lower-mortality persistent populations in the sample pool.

The rolling trend analysis confirms this phasing: strong monotonic increases characterize the first 1159 ticks (with progressively diminishing slopes from 0.346 to 0.017), followed by periods of no significant trend interspersed with minor increases through tick 1856. After tick 1856, a significant decreasing trend emerges, reflecting the selective loss of high-mortality replicates. Regular inflection points between ticks 1241-1828 indicate continued demographic responsiveness to seasonal forcing or density-dependent feedback throughout the equilibrium phase.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The saturation of cumulative mortality around 240 individuals suggests that hunting pressure under these parameter settings creates either a stable coexistence equilibrium or stochastic extinction rather than deterministic collapse. The pronounced oscillations in late-phase mortality indicate strong environmental seasonality effects on death rates. The divergence between peak and final values signals significant run-to-run variability in extinction timing, implying that demographic stochasticity and initial conditions critically influence outcomes. Interventions or policy scenarios targeting the 125-tick regime change intervals or the 63-tick seasonal cycles may prove most effective for population management, as these represent the natural periodicities of the mortality dynamics.
