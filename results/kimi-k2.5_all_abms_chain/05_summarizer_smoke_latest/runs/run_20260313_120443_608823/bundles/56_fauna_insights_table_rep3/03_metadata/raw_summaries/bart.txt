 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction . It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges . The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics .  The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation period . This static trajectory reflects the model configuration in which hunters neither reproduce nor die from energy depletion . The trajectory represents a progressive energetic failure, shifting from modest surplus to substantial debt accumulation .  The rapid saturation and subsequent decline of male-specific hunting success within approximately five years indicates that current forager densities and prey life histories generate unsustainable extraction profiles for the male subpopulation . Males impose greater processing costs (6 versus 3), potentially causing forager bands employing dynamic optimal foraging strategies to encounter diminishing returns as prey availability contracts within the 211×211 wrapped landscape .  The simulation reveals a demographic trajectory characterized by initial quiescence, sustained growth with seasonal oscillation, and ultimate reproductive collapse . Conservation strategies should prioritize protection of reproductive females during the initial simulation years, as early losses appear to drive long-term demographic constraints that persist throughout the simulation duration . Decision makers should prioritize mitigation strategies—such as hunting quotas or habitat enrichment strategies—to be implemented before the reproductive collapse becomes irreversible without intervention .  A male prey population of 125 individuals in the Allee simulation begins with an unsustainable harvest rate that far exceeds the species' reproductive capacity . Within the first 390 ticks, the population undergoes a steep demographic collapse, declining rapidly from its initial density as hunting pressure from 20 foragers intensifies . After tick 1856, a significant decreasing trend emerges, reflecting the selective loss of high-mortality replicates .  The population of female ungulates initiated at 125 individuals and underwent complete extinction over approximately 6.3 years . Decision makers should note that even moderate hunter densities generate sufficient predation pressure to overcome reproductive resilience of long-lived, slow-breeding species with two-year interbirth intervals . Management strategies must account for sexual dimorphism in hunting selectivity .  The prey population undergoes deterministic extinction following a multi-phase trajectory over approximately 6.3 years (2,311 ticks) Initially, abundance begins at roughly 167 individuals but collapses rapidly during the first 475 ticks . The early phase reflects immediate pressure from constant hunter presence combined with seasonal resource constraints and the species' biennial birth-spacing interval .  The trajectory is characterized by distinct nonlinear phases rather than a linear decline . The energy reserve undergoes catastrophic depletion, contracting at rates exceeding 22 units per tick during the first 231 ticks . Over the subsequent 2,311 ticks, this energy collapses irreversibly to zero, indicating complete population extinction .  The metric tracks the percentage of landscape patches covered by vegetation, representing fodder availability for megaherbivore herds . The median energy of 146 units across the trajectory indicates the system spends the majority of its duration in a depleted state prior to extinction . The trajectory demonstrates that chronic hunting pressure from a constant forager population produces prolonged extinction timelines characterized by deceptive resilience .
