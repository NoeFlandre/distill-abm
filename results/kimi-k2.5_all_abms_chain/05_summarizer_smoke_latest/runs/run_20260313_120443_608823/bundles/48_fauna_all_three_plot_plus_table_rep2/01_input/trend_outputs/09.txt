The population of female megaherbivores initiates at 125 individuals and undergoes a progressive collapse toward extirpation across all 40 simulation replicates, terminating at zero individuals by step 2312. The temporal trajectory exhibits distinct phases characterized by varying rates of decline, transient demographic rebounds, and increasing heterogeneity across simulation runs as extinction approaches.

During the initial phase spanning steps 0 to 305, the population experiences a precipitous decrease driven by intensive hunting pressure from 20 foraging agents pursuing high-value prey (food value 27, processing cost 3). This interval is marked by a steep negative trajectory, with statistical analyses confirming perfectly monotonic decline (Kendall's tau = -1.000) and slopes of approximately -0.23 individuals per step. By step 305, the first major inflection point, aggregate populations have declined to approximately 20 individuals, representing an 84% reduction from initial levels.

Between steps 305 and 875, the rate of decline decelerates substantially, though remaining negative. This transitional period contains significant structural change points at steps 475, 600, and 875, corresponding to the depletion of distinct demographic cohorts. Notably, secondary demographic peaks emerge at step 710 (5.1 individuals) and step 730 (6.5 individuals), coinciding precisely with the configured interbirth interval of 730 ticks. These transient increases likely reflect successful recruitment pulses from surviving breeding females before juveniles succumb to the combined pressures of starvation (threshold 25), predation, and hunting. During this interval, the population oscillates with a quasi-periodic cycle of approximately 63 steps, potentially reflecting seasonal resource availability fluctuations (seasonality parameter 0.8) or foraging encounter dynamics.

The final phase, extending from step 875 to termination, is characterized by persistence at extremely low densities (median 1.0, mean 16.5) followed by ultimate collapse. While localized temporal windows (steps 1160–1391 and 1856–2087) exhibit statistically significant temporary increases, these prove insufficient to reverse the terminal trajectory. Substantial variance characterizes extinction timing across replicates: most populations decline below 10 individuals by step 500, yet individual runs demonstrate persistence ranging from fewer than 500 steps to over 2000 steps, reflecting stochastic variation in hunter-prey encounters, spatial heterogeneity in vegetation regrowth (3-tick recovery), and the dynamic decision-making of immortal foragers operating under optimal foraging theory.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The deterministic progression toward extinction across all replicates indicates that the current configuration of hunting pressure, life history traits (730-tick birth spacing), and environmental seasonality creates an irreversible extinction vortex. The rapid initial decline to below 20 individuals within 305 steps suggests that intervention thresholds must trigger well before populations reach critically low levels, as recovery probability diminishes precipitously after the first inflection point. The high variance in extinction timing (spanning 1,500+ steps between earliest and latest extinctions) demonstrates that short-term population persistence at low densities provides unreliable signals of long-term viability; populations may linger for extended periods before sudden collapse. Conservation strategies should prioritize reducing hunter encounter rates or processing efficiency during the initial decline phase, as the system exhibits no stable equilibrium below the initial carrying capacity under present parameter settings. The 63-step oscillation cycle and biennial reproductive peaks (730 ticks) offer narrow windows for targeted protection measures coinciding with reproductive events.
