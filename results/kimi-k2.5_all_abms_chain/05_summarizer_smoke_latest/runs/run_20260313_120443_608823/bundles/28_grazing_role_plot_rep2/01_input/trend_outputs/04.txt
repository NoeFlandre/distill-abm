At initialization, the system exhibits mean green biomass levels of approximately 45,000 units. During the initial five steps, biomass undergoes a minor fluctuation, rising slightly before initiating a prolonged decline phase that extends through approximately step 40. This degradation period reaches minima ranging between 40,000 and 42,000 units for most trajectories, representing a reduction of roughly 8-11% from initial values, with some paths bottoming out near 40,000 units while others stabilize closer to 45,000 units toward the end of this phase.

Around step 40, the trajectories exhibit inflection points where vegetation recovery commences. Between steps 40 and 70, biomass increases steeply, with the most rapid acceleration occurring between steps 50 and 60, where values climb from approximately 45,000-50,000 units to between 55,000 and 64,000 units. This recovery represents gains of roughly 35-60% above the minimum levels observed during the trough.

Beyond step 70, the trajectories asymptotically approach distinct stable states that persist through step 100. Final biomass levels diverge considerably across simulation runs, clustering into multiple bands: a lower group stabilizing between approximately 55,000 and 58,000 units, a middle group reaching 59,000-62,000 units, and an upper group achieving 63,000-64,000 units or higher. This divergence results in a spread of approximately 8,000-9,000 units between the lowest and highest stable outcomes.

Interpreting these dynamics within the grazing system context: the initial decline reflects vegetation suppression under initial stocking densities of 90 animals per household distributed across the landscape. The prolonged depression lasting 30-40 steps suggests a lag in adaptive response as households rely on initial decision rules. The subsequent recovery indicates learning mechanisms activating as farmers adjust stocking decisions based on observed pasture conditions, with the steep rise between steps 50-60 representing successful destocking or rotation decisions that allow vegetation recovery. The final divergence among stable states suggests that different learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning) produce qualitatively different long-term vegetation outcomes despite identical initial conditions, with the most effective strategies maintaining biomass levels approximately 10-15% higher than less effective approaches.
