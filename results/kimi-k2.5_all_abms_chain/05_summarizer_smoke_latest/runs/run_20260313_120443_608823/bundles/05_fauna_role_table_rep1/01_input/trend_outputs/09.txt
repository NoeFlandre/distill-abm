The female megaherbivore population exhibits a deterministic collapse trajectory from carrying capacity to functional extinction over approximately 6.3 years (2,311 daily ticks). Initial abundance begins at 125 females but undergoes catastrophic depensation during the first 600 ticks (1.6 years), plummeting to fewer than 10 individuals by tick 475 and reaching a nadir of roughly 4–5 females by tick 710–730. This initial crash reflects intense predation pressure from the initial cohort of 20 human foragers operating under conditions of energy storage and immortality, effectively removing recruitment potential faster than the 730-tick interbirth interval can replenish the stock.

Following the primary collapse, the population enters a prolonged stochastic persistence phase characterized by critically low abundance (median = 1 female across replicate runs) and high variance. Between ticks 600 and 2,300, the system exhibits damped oscillations with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks (roughly bimonthly cycles), likely entrained by the strong environmental seasonality (0.8 amplitude) interacting with hunter foraging patterns. Superimposed on this declining trend are intermittent, statistically significant demographic rebounds detected in rolling trend analyses: a modest increase between ticks 1,160–1,391 (years 3.2–3.8) and a second, more pronounced recovery attempt between ticks 1,856–2,087 (years 5.1–5.7). These ephemeral increases align with the second and third reproductive cycles of surviving females, generating temporary pulses in female recruitment that peak at values of 1.6–4.1 individuals before succumbing to renewed hunting pressure.

The trajectory features distinct phase transitions identified at ticks 475, 600, and 875, marking shifts from exponential decline to regulated low-density equilibrium, and finally to the terminal extinction vortex. Despite temporary reprieves where slopes temporarily reverse (positive Kendall's tau in windows 6 and 9), the long-term trend remains monotonically decreasing with slopes steepening again after each failed recovery. The final extinction process initiates around tick 2,310, with abundance collapsing from low single digits to zero by tick 2,311. This terminal phase suggests the operation of an Allee effect or demographic stochasticity at critically low densities, where the combination of 2-year birth intervals, sustained predation by immortal hunters, and environmental variance overwhelms the minimum viable population threshold, precluding coexistence.
