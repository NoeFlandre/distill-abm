The cumulative mortality of male megaherbivore prey initiated at zero and underwent rapid escalation during the early phase of the simulation, with the rate of increase being steepest in the initial approximately 1,160 ticks. This phase reflects intensive hunting pressure where foragers, operating under optimal foraging constraints, encountered and dispatched male prey at high rates, likely targeting them preferentially due to their higher food value relative to processing costs compared to females.

Following this initial surge, the accumulation of male mortality transitioned to a pronounced plateau phase spanning roughly ticks 1,160 to 1,850, where the rate of increase diminished substantially. This deceleration suggests the emergence of density-dependent constraints, potentially including local depletion of male prey, increased foraging costs as surviving males became scarcer, or a strategic shift by hunters toward female prey as the energetic return on male hunting declined below optimal thresholds.

The trajectory reached a maximum cumulative mortality of approximately 120 individuals around tick 1,876, representing the apex of male prey extraction under the imposed hunting pressure and environmental conditions. Subsequent to this peak, the series exhibited slight oscillatory decline in the final phase (post-tick 1,856), with cumulative values settling near 117.5 by the termination of the observation period at tick 2,312. This late-phase fluctuation may reflect stochastic variation in hunting success, demographic stochasticity in small remaining prey populations, or boundary effects as the simulation approached temporal limits.

Superimposed upon these broad trends, the mortality accumulation displayed a cyclical oscillation with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, visible particularly in the plateau and decline phases. This rhythmic pattern corresponds to the strong seasonality parameter (0.8) in the environmental configuration, wherein grass availability fluctuates annually, driving periodic variation in ungulate energy states, herd movements, and consequent hunter foraging success across the 365-tick annual cycle.
