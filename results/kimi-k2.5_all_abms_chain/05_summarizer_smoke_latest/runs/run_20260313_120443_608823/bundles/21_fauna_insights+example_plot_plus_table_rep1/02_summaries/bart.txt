The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the simulation duration from step 0 through step 2312. This value exhibits zero variance with no detectable peaks, valleys, or directional trends. The model's configuration establishes an initial hunter population of 20 agents with the immortality parameter enabled and no reproductive capability for human agents. The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model shows that under the specified parameters, male ungulates face extreme hunting pressure that rapidly drives their numbers toward local extirpation. The maximum recorded value of approximately 120.2 occurs around step 1876, suggesting that hunters are capable of removing nearly the entire male cohort from the initial prey population of 250 animals. The number of births remains at zero for the initial approximately 700 steps, representing a reproductive dormancy phase as the founding population establishes itself. Around step 700, reproduction commences and increases rapidly, entering a sustained growth phase that continues until approximately step 1600. By step 1850, the birth rate decreases significantly from the maximum of 5.6 to 1.0 by step 2312. The population of female prey animals begins at 125 individuals and undergoes a precipitous decline throughout the simulation. During the initial 500 time steps, the population drops sharply from 125 to approximately 10-20 individuals, representing a collapse of over 80% of the initial male cohort. The population enters a terminal phase, oscillating at low densities between 0 and 5 individuals before reaching complete extinction at step 2284. The current configuration of hunting pressure, environmental seasonality, and prey life history traits leads to rapid population collapse within approximately six years. The cyclical nature of the decline highlights the importance of seasonal management strategies if conservation is the goal. By step 2311, the population reaches complete extinction with zero individuals remaining. The combined energy available in all prey animals declines deterministically to extinction by time step 2,311. The depletion follows a nonlinear trajectory characterized by distinct phases of rapid collapse, moderate fluctuation, and terminal decline. Interventions would need to substantially reduce hunter density or enhance reproductive rates to shift the system from deterministic collapse to sustainable coexistence. The trajectory indicates that a forager population of 20 individuals hunting prey drives deterministic extinction within approximately 6.3 simulation years. The critical window for intervention occurs within the first 350 time steps, before prey energy drops below 4,000 units. The percentage of green patches covering the landscape initiates at 33.0% and exhibits high volatility throughout the simulation.
