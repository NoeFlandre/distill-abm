The cumulative number of postpartum females killed begins at zero and undergoes rapid accumulation during the initial 475 time steps, reaching approximately 100 individuals and constituting the steepest phase of the hunting pressure curve. Inflection points at steps 245, 262, and 285 mark the period of maximum acceleration in kill rates, suggesting efficient targeting of high-value reproductive females when herd densities are highest. By step 500, most simulation runs have accumulated between 100 and 120 kills, representing the majority of the total hunting mortality observed across the full simulation period.

The accumulation rate decelerates significantly after step 475, with change points at steps 625 and 850 marking transitions to progressively slower accumulation phases. Statistical trend analysis confirms this deceleration, with Mann-Kendall tau values declining from 1.000 (perfect increasing trend) in early windows to 0.174 by step 1,391, eventually reaching a plateau with no significant trend thereafter. The cumulative count stabilizes at a mean of approximately 117 individuals by step 1,500, with a median of 116.86 and a maximum observed value of 119, indicating that the pool of available postpartum females becomes effectively exhausted within this timeframe.

Across the 40 simulation runs, final cumulative counts vary between approximately 105 and 125, demonstrating moderate variability in the timing and extent of demographiccollapse despite identical initial conditions. This variability emerges primarily during the middle phase (steps 500-1,000), where some trajectories plateau earlier while others continue gradual accumulation. Oscillation indices clustered between steps 2,170 and 2,265 reflect minor fluctuations in the aggregate mean during the final plateau phase, likely attributable to asynchronous termination of individual simulation runs rather than continued hunting activity.

The dynamics suggest that postpartum females face extreme vulnerability to hunting pressure due to their enhanced food value from nursing offspring, leading to rapid depletion of the reproductive segment of the population within approximately 1.4 to 2.3 simulated years (given 365 ticks per year). Decision makers should recognize that targeting reproductive females creates a demographic bottleneck that extinguishes recruitment potential quickly; populations cannot sustain even moderate hunting pressure (20 foragers) on this demographic without rapid collapse. The consistent plateau around 117 individuals—representing nearly the entire reproductive output of the initial 250-individual herd over multiple potential breeding cycles—indicates that such hunting pressure effectively eliminates generational replacement, accelerating extinction dynamics well before environmental carrying capacity or senescence factors become limiting.
