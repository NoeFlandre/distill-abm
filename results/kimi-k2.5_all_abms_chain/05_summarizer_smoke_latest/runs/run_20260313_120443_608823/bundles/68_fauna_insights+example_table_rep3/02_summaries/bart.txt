The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation period of 2,312 time steps. This static population level reflects the model configuration where foragers are immortal and do not reproduce. The aggregate energy available to forager bands begins at 2000 units but undergoes a precipitous decline across the simulation timeline. The rapid initial accumulation of male mortality suggests that male megaherbivores face acute vulnerability to hunting pressure early in the interaction period, with populations potentially losing over 100 reproductive-age males within the first several years of simulation time. The cumulative mortality of female ungulates begins at zero and increases monotonically through approximately the first 1,850 time steps. The number of births among megaherbivore herds initiates at zero and remains negligible through approximately the first 600 ticks. Between indices 613 and 1702, reproductive output accelerates markedly, exhibiting statistically significant monotonic increases with inflection points at 613, 733, 903, and 1121 indicating varying rates of growth. The male prey population initiates at 125 individuals and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction, ultimately collapsing to zero at approximately tick 2,284. The dynamics unfold across three distinct phases: an initial demographic collapse, an extended period of oscillatory decline, and a terminal phase of scarcity culminating in extinction. A population of female prey initiates at 125 individuals and undergoes a precipitous decline to extinction by tick 2311. The trajectory is characterized by nonlinear dynamics featuring multiple distinct phases, temporary demographic recoveries, and persistent oscillatory behavior superimposed over the long-term downward trend. Decision makers should note that extinction follows a prolonged attrition pattern rather than immediate collapse. The combined energy of male prey populations initiates at 12,500 units and undergoes a protracted decline over approximately 2,284 time steps. This trajectory reflects sustained removal rates exceeding the demographic replacement capacity of the male cohort under persistent hunting pressure. The progression demonstrates that even moderate hunter densities inevitably drive slowly reproducing megaherbivore populations to extinction within approximately six reproductive cycles. The combined energy available across all prey animals initiates at 16,666.67 units, representing the total biomass of the megaherbivore herds at the start of the simulation. During the first 350 time steps, this value undergoes a rapid and substantial decline to approximately 4,100 units, indicating immediate heavy exploitation by hunters before the prey population can implement demographic adjustments through reproduction or movement. The population enters a prolonged terminal phase marked by high volatility within a general downward trajectory. Complete Grassland environments can maintain stable fodder availability under herbivore pressure. But managers should anticipate severe seasonal bottlenecks occurring annually (at approximately day 365 in the simulation calendar) where vegetation drops by nearly 8 percentage points. These periodic resource scarcities may create critical stress periods for megaherbivore populations.
