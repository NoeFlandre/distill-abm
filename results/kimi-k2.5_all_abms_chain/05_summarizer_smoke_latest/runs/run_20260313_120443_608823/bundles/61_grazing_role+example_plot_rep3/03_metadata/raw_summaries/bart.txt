 The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system . Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods .  The mean household risk attitude exhibits substantial heterogeneity across the population throughout the 100-step simulation . This pattern suggests that the three distinct learning strategies—baseline experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—converge to different equilibrium risk dispositions .  The mean livestock plus destock across households initiates at 90 animals . The mean number of livestock destocked per household exhibits a pronounced initial crisis followed by progressive stabilization . The highest-performing strategies achieve herd sizes 40-50\% greater than the lowest-performing ones, suggesting that effective learning mechanisms enable households to maintain larger herds .  The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock distribution begins at 0 at step 0, reflecting the initial condition where all 60 households possess identical herds of 90 animals . Over the first 40 time steps, inequality increases gradually to values ranging between approximately 0.01 and 0.03, indicating modest differentiation in livestock holdings as households apply their distinct learning strategies to manage grazing pressures under variable rainfall conditions .  The count of non-learning households begins at 20 across all simulation runs . The remaining simulation runs demonstrate continuous accumulation of destocking throughout the 100-year period . The trajectories stabilizing at lower values indicate successful adaptation to pasture constraints, while the continuously rising trajectories reflect persistent overstocking pressures .
