Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. The simulation of milk consumption behaviors from 1974 to 2005 reveals a pronounced S-curve adoption trajectory for skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Average weekly consumption per agent begins at approximately 5.3 units in 1974, representing negligible market presence. During the first decade (1974–1983), consumption exhibits steep linear growth, reaching approximately 1,265 units by 1985. The gradual deceleration suggests that deep-seated habit persistence and social network reinforcement sustain whole milk consumption longer than might be predicted by threshold-based adoption models. The oscillatory patterns observed in the final years indicate that consumer commitment to alternatives remains vulnerable to periodic reconsideration. The series stabilizes into a plateau with no statistically significant directional trend, exhibiting localized oscillations including a temporary rebound to approximately 774 units at step 19 following a trough at step 18. A second phase of gradual decline resumes between steps 20 and 24, though at a reduced velocity relative to earlier periods. Non-linear adoption pattern suggests public health campaigns should prepare for distinct phases. Decision makers should anticipate oscillatory resistance periods and design sustained engagement strategies. The final 20% of population adoption required nearly a decade to achieve. From 1974 to 2005, the divergence in choice function scores among agents exhibited a continuous upward trajectory, beginning at complete uniformity (0.0000) and culminating in substantial heterogeneity (0.7508) by the end of the simulation period. Modest acceleration through time step 22 continued. The divergence had reached approximately 0.5584, with the mean value across all periods being 0.4965. This pattern suggests that the transition from whole milk to lower-fat alternatives created persistent psychological tension.
