**Implications for Decision Makers**

The rapid initial decline within the first 300–400 time steps identifies a critical vulnerability window where management interventions would be most urgent; once energetic reserves begin depleting, the trajectory toward collapse becomes difficult to reverse. The temporary stabilization around steps 375–500 suggests that seasonal resource availability creates brief buffering periods that could potentially support targeted conservation actions, but these natural reprieves cannot prevent eventual extinction under sustained hunting pressure without direct harvest limitation. The wide variation in extinction timing (spanning approximately 1,000 steps between the fastest and slowest trajectories) necessitates conservative buffer zones in population viability planning, as identical initial conditions produce divergent outcomes due to stochastic factors. Finally, the deterministic convergence to zero across all simulation runs demonstrates that the current configuration—20 hunters, high seasonality, and female-specific energetic costs—leads to inevitable female population extinction, indicating that any management strategy permitting these parameters will result in loss of reproductive capacity and population collapse.
