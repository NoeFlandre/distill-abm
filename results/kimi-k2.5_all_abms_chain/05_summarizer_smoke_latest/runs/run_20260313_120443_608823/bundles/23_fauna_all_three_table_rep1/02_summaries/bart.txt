Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change. Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances. Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores. Foragers-movement-cost ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1.0, and distance hunters can perceive and pursue prey is determined by foraging-radius. Environmental fluctuation is controlled by seasonality, representing the intensity of seasonal variation in resource availability. The number of human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation duration. The cumulative mortality of male ungulates begins at zero and undergoes a rapid accumulation phase throughout the initial 850 ticks. Between ticks 850 and 1856, the accumulation rate decelerates substantially, transitioning from rapid growth to a quasi-stable plateau oscillating around 118 to 120 individuals. In a subset of simulation runs, cumulative male mortality effectively stabilizes or reverses as populations become extirpated or hunters abandon male pursuit. The cumulative mortality of postpartum female ungulates initiates at zero and undergoes rapid accumulation during the first approximately 850 time steps. By approximately tick 1200, the cumulative kill count stabilizes near 117, entering a prolonged plateau phase that persists with minor oscillations between 117 and 119 kills. Conservation strategies should prioritize limiting cumulative mortality before the deceleration phase begins around tick 600. Simulations simulate the population of 250 megaherbivore animals in a landscape with 250 prey animals. System enters a phase of maximal reproductive output, reaching a peak value of approximately 5.6 births around tick 1702. Population enters an extinction vortex under sustained hunting pressure from 20 immortal foragers operating with food storage capacity and a six-patch foraging radius. The male megaherbivore population initiates at 125 individuals and undergoes deterministic collapse to extinction by tick 2284 (approximately 6.3 years) The trajectory comprises three distinct demographic phases: rapid initial decline, protracted low-density persistence with transient oscillations, and terminal extinction. Energy available within the megaherbivore population begins at approximately 16,667 units, representing the collective energy reserves of the initial cohort of 250 animals. Over the course of 2,311 ticks (approximately 6.3 years), this metric undergoes a complete collapse to zero, indicating total population extirpation. The trajectory reveals that under sustained hunting pressure combined with high environmental seasonality, megaherbivore populations exhibit rapid initial depletion followed by a prolonged period of low-density volatility rather than immediate collapse. Decision makers should recognize that temporary stabilization or minor recoveries during years 3–5 (ticks 875–1623) may create misleading signals of population resilience.
