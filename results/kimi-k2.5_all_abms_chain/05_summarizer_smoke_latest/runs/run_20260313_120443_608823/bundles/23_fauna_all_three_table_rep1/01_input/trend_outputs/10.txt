The population of megaherbivores begins at approximately 167 individuals and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction, reaching zero abundance by tick 2311. This precipitous decline unfolds through three distinct phases of population dynamics, characterized by decreasing rates of loss interrupted by seasonal fluctuations and intermittent, insufficient recoveries.

The initial phase spans roughly the first 475 ticks and exhibits the most dramatic population reduction. Within the first 125 ticks, the population undergoes a sharp decline with a slope of -0.251 as optimal foragers exploit the initially abundant, high-nutritional-value prey. An inflection point at tick 357 marks a transition in the trajectory, after which the decline accelerates temporarily to a slope of -0.320 between ticks 232 and 463, likely reflecting concentrated hunting pressure on the remaining accessible herds. By tick 475, the population has collapsed from initial levels to a small fraction of its starting abundance, establishing a pattern of chronic scarcity for the remainder of the simulation.

The second phase, extending from tick 475 to approximately tick 875, demonstrates a moderated but persistent negative trend with slopes decreasing to -0.117 and then -0.032. Throughout this interval, the population exhibits pronounced oscillations with a dominant period of 63 ticks, tracking seasonal resource pulses in the highly seasonal environment (seasonality parameter 0.8). Local peaks occur at ticks 709 and 730 with mean values of approximately 9.3 and 11.4 individuals, respectively, followed by subsequent peaks at ticks 803 and 820 with values near 7.4 and 6.9. These rhythmic fluctuations reflect temporary seasonal reprieves where reduced vegetation availability constrains both prey energy acquisition and hunter activity, creating brief windows where mortality temporarily balances with recruitment. However, change points at ticks 625 and 875 signal continued degradation of the population base, as the median abundance settles near 1.5 individuals—indicating that across most simulation runs, the population persists at critically low levels with limited demographic resilience.

The final phase, from tick 875 to the termination at tick 2311, represents a protracted extinction vortex where the population fluctuates between zero and approximately ten individuals. Despite the general declining trend, brief statistically significant increases occur during rolling windows starting at ticks 1392 and 1856, with positive Kendall's tau values of 0.203 and 0.445, respectively. These transient recoveries suggest moments when prey briefly evade detection or when seasonal resource peaks align with reproductive timing, allowing marginal population growth. However, these windows are immediately followed by resuming declines, and inflection points proliferate between ticks 1657 and 2167, indicating rapid alternation between minimal growth and continued attrition. The oscillation amplitude diminishes progressively, with later peaks at ticks 893 and 982 reaching only 4.6 and 2.8 individuals. Change point 2310 marks the final transition to complete extirpation as the last individuals succumb to the combined pressures of starvation, senescence, and inescapable hunting pressure from the immortal, food-storing forager population.

For decision makers, these dynamics illustrate that megaherbivore populations under moderate hunter pressure (20 foragers) and high environmental seasonality face a critical vulnerability window within the first 475 ticks (approximately 16 months), after which demographic momentum shifts irreversibly toward extinction. The presence of seasonal oscillations even at critically low densities (below 12 individuals) suggests that resource management strategies must account for cyclical bottlenecks that exacerbate Allee effects. Furthermore, the brief, statistically significant recoveries observed in the third phase represent false signals of population stabilization; decision makers should recognize that temporary population plateaus during collapse do not indicate recovery potential, but rather represent the terminal fluctuations of a system committed to extinction under sustained predation pressure.
