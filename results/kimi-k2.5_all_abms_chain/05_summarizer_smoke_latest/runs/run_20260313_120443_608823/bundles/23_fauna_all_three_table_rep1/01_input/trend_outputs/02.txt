The aggregate energy available to human forager bands initiates at 2,000 energy units but undergoes a profound and continuous degradation throughout the simulation, terminating at approximately -38,230. This trajectory reflects a systematic energy deficit wherein metabolic and movement expenditures perpetually exceed caloric gains from hunting, resulting in an accumulating energy debt rather than equilibrium or surplus.

During the initial phase spanning roughly the first 650 time steps, the decline proceeds at a rate of approximately 11 energy units per tick, reflecting the rapid drawdown of initial reserves as foragers exploit the naive prey population of 250 ungulates. By approximately tick 370, aggregate energy has fallen below zero, indicating that the forager cohort has collectively exhausted all stored energy and entered a deficit state. Short-term oscillations are evident even in this early period, with brief fluctuations between 1,256 and 1,989 energy units suggesting initial heterogeneity in hunting success across the 20-agent population.

Following the first major transition at tick 650, the rate of energy depletion accelerates significantly to slopes approaching 15-18 units per tick. This acceleration likely corresponds to the depletion of high-value prey targets, forcing foragers to expend increasing movement costs (1 unit per tick) while searching for scarcer resources. The energy level reaches approximately -2,000 by tick 394 and plummets to -23,800 by tick 1,594, indicating a near tenfold increase in the rate of decline compared to the initial phase.

A second inflection occurs around tick 1075, where the decline steepens further to approximately 20 units per tick—a rate maintained through subsequent transition points at 1500 and 1925. During this middle period, the aggregate energy crosses below -15,000 and continues its descent to approximately -28,400 by tick 1,826. The consistency of the -20 slope across multiple time windows suggests that the system has entered a terminal phase where prey availability has been reduced to levels insufficient to support even basic forager maintenance requirements.

Superimposed upon this downward trend are rhythmic oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks. These periodic fluctuations likely reflect seasonal pulses in vegetation availability (consistent with the high seasonality parameter of 0.8), which temporarily enhance prey condition and accessibility, creating brief intervals where the rate of energy loss moderates. The persistence of these cycles, evident in clustering around the 2,170-2,265 tick range, suggests that environmental seasonality continues to modulate hunting success even as the prey population nears collapse, though these oscillations represent only temporary decelerations within the overarching decline.

The final phase from tick 1925 to 2312 shows no signs of stabilization, with aggregate energy reaching its nadir of -38,233. The high variance across simulation runs (standard deviation of 12,170) indicates substantial stochasticity in the exact timing of energy crashes, likely driven by random encounters between foragers and the diminishing prey population, though all trajectories converge toward terminal energy debt.

For decision makers, these dynamics indicate that the initial forager density of 20 agents relative to 250 prey creates an immediately unsustainable extraction regime. The absence of any recovery phase or positive inflection suggests that without intervention to reduce hunting pressure or enhance prey recruitment, the system inevitably drives forager populations into severe energetic debt within approximately 6.3 years (2,312 days). The 63-tick cyclical pattern identifies recurring windows—roughly every two months of simulation time—where temporary resource abundance might allow for strategic intervention, though the consistently negative trend slopes across all temporal windows indicate that such windows provide insufficient opportunity for system recovery under current parameter configurations.
