The Gini coefficient measuring inequality in total healthy livestock across households exhibits consistent patterns of increase over the 100-timestep simulation period, though with considerable variation in magnitude across different simulation runs. At initialization, the coefficient equals approximately zero, reflecting the uniform starting condition of 90 livestock per household.

During timesteps 0 through 20, inequality increases minimally, with values generally remaining below 0.015. Between timesteps 20 and 40, the coefficient rises gradually, reaching levels ranging from approximately 0.010 to 0.030. After timestep 40, the rate of increase accelerates markedly for most simulation outcomes, with particular divergence becoming evident after timestep 60. By timestep 60, values range from roughly 0.025 to 0.050.

In the final 40 timesteps, inequality continues to intensify at an increasing rate, with the Gini coefficient reaching terminal values between approximately 0.030 and 0.095 by timestep 100. The spread between the minimum and maximum recorded values widens throughout the simulation, reaching approximately 0.065 by the conclusion.

This pattern indicates that despite identical starting conditions and the absence of livestock reproduction, stochastic rainfall variability (with standard deviation of 100) interacts with the three learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning with single-loop adaptation) to produce divergent distributional outcomes. The convex growth pattern suggests positive feedback mechanisms where early disparities in pasture condition or livestock health compound over time, leading to persistent inequality in healthy livestock holdings across households. Even at maximum observed levels near 0.095, inequality remains relatively modest in absolute terms, though the accelerating trajectory suggests potential for further divergence if the simulation continued beyond 100 timesteps.

**Implications for decision makers:** these results suggest that private pasture systems inherently tend toward increased inequality in livestock holdings even without biological growth of herds. The wide outcome range (0.030–0.095) indicates high sensitivity to environmental stochasticity and adaptive decision-making processes. Interventions to maintain equity may be most effective if implemented before timestep 60, after which inequality accelerates and becomes more entrenched due to compounding effects. The findings highlight the importance of accounting for path dependency in pastoral development policies; early random advantages—whether from favorable rainfall or successful early adaptations—can translate into substantial long-term disparities in household livestock assets. Policy support might need to focus on buffering farmers against early negative stochastic shocks to prevent the compounding of disadvantages observed in the later timesteps.
