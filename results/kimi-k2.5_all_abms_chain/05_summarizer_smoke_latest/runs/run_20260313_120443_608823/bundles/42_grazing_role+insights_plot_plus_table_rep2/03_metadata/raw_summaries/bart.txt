 The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system . Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods .  Simulation data reveal distinct temporal phases in how farming households adjust their risk disposition relative to recommended livestock stocking levels across 100 annual time steps . Initial risk dispositions function as static agent characteristics rather than adaptive variables within the simulation framework . This implies that in real-world smallholder systems with limited strategic adaptation, initial risk calibration during policy interventions may have lasting consequences for resource management outcomes across multi-decade timescales .  The mean green biomass across all simulation runs begins at approximately 45,000 units, representing the initial vegetation condition before substantial grazing pressure accumulates . The trajectory reveals three distinct phases: an initial degradation period (steps 0-27), a rapid recovery and transition phase (steps 27-50) and a consolidation phase . The dynamics reveal that grazing systems with adaptive learning strategies require approximately 25-30 years to transition from initial degradation to recovery .  The system exhibits an acute initial destocking crisis followed by rapid stabilization and long-term divergence across learning strategies . By year 30, the system undergoes a structural transition marked by stabilization at divergent levels . Policymakers should prioritize diffusion of high-performing learning behaviors to prevent persistent asset depletion across farming population .  The adoption of social learning among smallholder farmers begins at zero households and follows a sigmoidal diffusion trajectory over the century-long simulation . During the initial two decades, engagement increases rapidly, with the steepest growth occurring in the first ten years . The average engagement stands at approximately 27 households, indicating that sustained social learning behavior becomes prevalent only after the initial three decades .  The steep early destocking suggests that initial stocking levels (90 animals) exceed carrying capacity during dry years, forcing rapid adjustment regardless of learning type . The wide variation in final cumulative destocking values across realizations suggests that stochastic rainfall sequences generate substantially different household fates despite identical learning strategies .
