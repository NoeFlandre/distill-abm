Across the 100-step simulation runs, mean green biomass initiates at approximately 45,000 units. During the preliminary phase spanning roughly steps 0 through 20, the metric undergoes a pronounced decline, reaching minimum values between 40,000 and 44,000 units, with most trajectories bottoming out near step 20. This reduction corresponds to initial grazing pressure from livestock populations on available vegetation prior to compensatory regrowth.

Subsequent to this minimum, a robust recovery phase dominates steps 20 through 70, characterized by steep monotonic increases in biomass accumulation. Values rise rapidly from their nadirs to approach asymptotic levels, with the rate of increase gradually diminishing as the system approaches equilibrium.

From approximately step 70 through step 100, the trajectories stabilize into distinct plateau configurations, though exhibiting substantial heterogeneity across replicates. Final equilibrium values vary considerably, ranging from approximately 56,000 to 64,000 units. This divergence suggests that stochastic rainfall variation (operating with mean 200 and standard deviation 100) combined with heterogeneous learning processes among the three behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) generates multiple potential stable states rather than converging to a singular ecological equilibrium.

For decision makers, these dynamics indicate that private property grazing systems with adaptive learning mechanisms possess substantial resilience to initial overgrazing pressures, typically recovering within 20-30 time steps. However, the considerable variation in long-term ecological outcomes—spanning approximately 8,000 units or 14% of mean values—suggests that pasture conditions remain sensitive to specific climatic sequences and the emergent distribution of learning behaviors. This implies that supporting diverse behavioral strategies and climate-adaptive management practices may prove more effective for ensuring ecological stability than uniform prescriptive approaches, given the inherent uncertainty in social-ecological trajectories.
