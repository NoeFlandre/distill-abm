The Gini coefficient measuring inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock begins at 0 across all simulations, indicating perfect equality in initial livestock endowments. During the first 20 time steps, inequality remains minimal with values staying below 0.015 as all households maintain similar herd health status.

Between time steps 20 and 40, inequality begins to gradually increase, with Gini coefficients rising to the range of 0.01-0.03. From time step 40 to 60, the divergence accelerates moderately, with most trajectories reaching values between 0.03 and 0.045.

After time step 60, the simulations bifurcate into two distinct patterns. Approximately two-thirds of the simulation runs exhibit rapid acceleration in inequality, with Gini coefficients increasing steeply from around 0.04 to reach terminal values between 0.075 and 0.095 by time step 100. The remaining trajectories show more gradual linear increases, ending with Gini coefficients between 0.03 and 0.06 at time step 100.

This trajectory divergence reflects the compounding effects of stochastic rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100) interacting with the three heterogeneous behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1). The widening inequality after time step 60 suggests that early random advantages in vegetation conditions or learning effectiveness create self-reinforcing feedback loops, where households employing more adaptive strategies or experiencing favorable early conditions accumulate healthier livestock while others fall behind.

**Implications for decision makers:** The emergence of inequality appears inevitable in this system due to climatic stochasticity and behavioral heterogeneity, but the extent of inequality (ranging from 0.03 to 0.095) is highly sensitive to early-phase dynamics. Interventions targeting the period before time step 60—when inequality trajectories begin to diverge—may be critical for preventing the formation of entrenched inequality. The substantial variation in outcomes suggests that policies should account for high uncertainty in distributional outcomes and consider safety nets for households that fall into the lower performance trajectories.
