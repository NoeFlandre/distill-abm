The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 32-step simulation period representing 1974 to 2005. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first ten steps, the deviation rises sharply from zero to a range of 0.50 to 0.55 across simulation runs. This indicates that early in the historical transition period, agents rapidly developed substantial misalignment between their personal health valuations and their actual milk consumption choices, likely reflecting the tension between emerging health information about low-fat alternatives and strong incumbent habits parameterized at high initial strength.

Following this initial surge, the rate of increase attenuates considerably between steps 10 and 25, with the deviation growing gradually from approximately 0.55 to only 0.58-0.62. This plateau-like behavior suggests a temporary stabilization during the core transition years where habit formation, social influence mechanisms, and cognitive dissonance thresholds reach a dynamic balance, slowing the divergence between internal health values and external consumption behaviors.

In the final phase from step 25 to step 32, the deviation accelerates again, climbing from roughly 0.60 to reach terminal values between 0.70 and 0.75. This late-stage acceleration indicates that network effects and social norm pressures become increasingly dominant toward the end of the simulation period. The trajectories remain tightly clustered with low variance during the initial ten steps, but begin to diverge noticeably in the middle phase, with the spread between simulation runs widening to approximately 0.05 units by the final step. This growing variance suggests that while early behavioral patterns are highly deterministic due to uniform initial conditions, later outcomes become more sensitive to stochastic elements in agent interactions within the Watts-Strogatz network topology.

The persistent increase in health deviation throughout the entire simulation implies that social influence, environmental considerations, and habit persistence drive consumer choices increasingly away from pure health-value optimization. The parameter settings for social susceptibility (0.2), social conformity (0.28), and enabled social norms create conditions where adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties occurs partly through social conformance rather than alignment with personal health preferences, particularly pronounced among late adopters in the final phase.

Implications for decision makers indicate that public health campaigns emphasizing personal health benefits may lose effectiveness over time as social dynamics decouple consumption behavior from individual health valuations. Interventions targeting the initial phase (years 0-10) are critical when deviation remains below 0.55 and behavioral patterns are more malleable. During the plateau phase (years 10-25), integrated strategies addressing both social influence networks and habit disruption offer the best opportunity to align choices with health values. However, once the system enters the final acceleration phase (after year 25), traditional health education becomes less effective than interventions leveraging social norm mechanisms or addressing the specific non-health drivers that have become dominant in the decision-making architecture.
