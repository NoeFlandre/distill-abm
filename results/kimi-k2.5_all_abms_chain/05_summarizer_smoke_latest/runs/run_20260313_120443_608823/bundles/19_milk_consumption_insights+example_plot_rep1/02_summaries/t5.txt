Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk initiates at approximately zero during the first time step. Between steps 1 and 11, consumption undergoes rapid exponential-like growth, accelerating from near-zero values to approximately 1,200–1,280 units. Over the first five time steps, the metric rises gradually to roughly 1,000–1,500 total choices, indicating an initial phase where only a small fraction of agents overcome incumbent habit strengths and cognitive dissonance thresholds to consider lower-fat alternatives. This acceleration reflects the activation of social influence mechanisms within the Watts-Strogatz network topology. Effects within the social network as threshold-based or probability-based disposition mechanisms trigger mass reconsideration of milk choices among remaining whole milk consumers. Trajectories reconverge toward the end, suggesting that interventions targeting early adoption stages may yield the most rapid reductions in whole milk consumption. The constant upward trend in the habit factor suggests that agents continuing to consume whole milk experience progressively stronger behavioral reinforcement, increasing the psychological barrier to switching to alternative milk varieties. The widening variance across simulation runs indicates that stochastic elements in social network interactions create divergent habit reinforcement pathways. Social influence, environmental considerations, and habit persistence drive consumer choices increasingly away from pure health-value optimization. Social norms create conditions where adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties occurs partly through social conformance rather than alignment with personal health preferences.
