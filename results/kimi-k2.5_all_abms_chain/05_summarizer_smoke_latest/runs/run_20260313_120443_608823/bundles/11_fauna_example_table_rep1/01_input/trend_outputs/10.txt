The megaherbivore population initiates at approximately 167 individuals and undergoes a prolonged trajectory toward extinction over 2,311 time steps. The initial phase witnesses a rapid demographic contraction within the first 125 time steps, representing the immediate impact of hunting pressure from 20 foragers operating under dynamic optimal foraging logic with energy storage capabilities. This precipitous decline transitions around time step 475 into a phase characterized by oscillatory dynamics with a periodicity of approximately 63 time steps, superimposed upon a continued downward trend.

Between time steps 700 and 1000, the population exhibits dampened oscillations, temporarily stabilizing at levels between approximately 2 and 11 individuals. These fluctuations likely reflect intermittent reproductive pulses constrained by the two-year birth spacing interval, counteracted by persistent hunting mortality that differentially targets males and females based on their distinct food values and processing costs. The demographic volatility is moderate during this middle phase, with the population demonstrating transient resistance to extinction through brief recruitment events despite high seasonality in resource availability.

Notably, during the terminal phase at low population densities, the system exhibits two periods of temporary demographic reversal. Between approximately time steps 1,390 and 1,620, the population transitions from decline to a slight increasing trend, followed by another brief recovery between time steps 1,850 and 2,090. These ephemeral increases, occurring when abundance has fallen to fewer than 3 individuals, suggest momentary relaxations in hunting pressure potentially arising from forager movement costs and search inefficiencies at low prey densities, allowing for brief reproductive gains. However, these insufficient recoveries cannot overcome the Allee effects and demographic stochasticity inherent to small population sizes.

By time step 2,311, the population reaches zero abundance, indicating complete extinction. The final disappearance occurs despite the high maximum lifespan potential of approximately 28 years and biennial reproductive capacity, demonstrating that the interaction of immortal human foragers with energy storage, sexual dimorphism in prey value, and seasonal environmental variation creates a predation sink that exceeds the megaherbivores' capacity for demographic replacement.
