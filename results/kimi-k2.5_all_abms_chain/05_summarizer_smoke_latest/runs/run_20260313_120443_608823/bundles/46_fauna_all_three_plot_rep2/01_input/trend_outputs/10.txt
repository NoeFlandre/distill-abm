The total prey population begins at 250 individuals across all simulation runs. A rapid decline commences immediately, with most populations falling below 150 individuals within the first 250 time steps and dropping to fewer than 50 individuals by approximately step 500. This steep initial decrease reflects the impact of sustained hunting pressure from 20 human foragers combined with the environmental constraints of seasonal resource availability and baseline mortality factors. The decline rate varies substantially across simulation runs during this phase, with some populations collapsing to near-zero levels by step 400 while others maintain 50-100 individuals until step 600-700, likely due to stochastic variation in herd movement patterns, hunter-prey encounter rates, and grass regrowth dynamics. Between steps 500 and 1000, the decline continues but at a decelerated pace, with populations fluctuating between 0 and 40 individuals as small remnant groups persist. The parameter configuration of immortal hunters with food storage capability ensures continuous predation pressure regardless of prey density, preventing population recovery during this phase. By step 1000, the majority of runs have reached extinction or near-extinction states (0-5 individuals), though several trajectories maintain small populations of 5-10 individuals until steps 1200-1500. After step 1500, virtually all populations have declined to zero or single-digit counts, indicating systematic extinction under the current parameter combination of 20 hunters, moderate sexual dimorphism in hunting value, and high seasonality (0.8). The absence of any stable coexistence trajectories suggests that the hunting pressure exceeds the maximum sustainable yield for the prey life history parameters, particularly given the 730-tick interbirth interval and energy constraints imposed by the grassland environment with 33% initial vegetation cover.
