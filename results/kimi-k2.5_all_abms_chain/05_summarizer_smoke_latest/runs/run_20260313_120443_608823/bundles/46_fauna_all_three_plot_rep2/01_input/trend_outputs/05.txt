The cumulative mortality of postpartum female ungulates initiates at zero and follows a saturating trajectory across all simulation replicates. During the initial phase from approximately step 0 to step 100, accumulation remains minimal as the 20 initialized foragers establish spatial distribution and encounter prey. Between step 100 and step 500, the count increases steeply and roughly linearly, with most trajectories rising from near zero to values between 80 and 120 individuals killed. This rapid accumulation reflects sustained hunting pressure on the breeding female segment of the initial 250-animal prey population.

Following this initial surge, the rate of increase decelerates markedly between step 500 and step 1000. Most replicates transition from steep growth to shallow asymptotic approaches, with cumulative values clustering between 100 and 125 postpartum females killed by step 1000. Beyond step 1000, the trajectories flatten and remain stable through the simulation terminus at step 2000, indicating cessation of additional mortality events for this demographic. Final cumulative values converge within a narrow range of approximately 105 to 125 individuals across runs, though the specific timing of plateau onset varies from approximately step 600 to step 1200 depending on the replicate.

The stepwise increments visible throughout the trajectories indicate discrete hunting events rather than continuous mortality. The early lag observed in some replicates—where accumulation remains flat for the first 50 to 150 steps before accelerating—suggests stochastic variation in initial forager-prey spatial matching, yet eventual cumulative impacts remain consistent across runs. The universal plateau suggests systematic depletion of the postpartum female pool, likely reflecting demographic collapse or extirpation of breeding females given the 730-tick interbirth interval and the high energetic efficiency of hunting this demographic (net value of 24 energy units after processing costs).

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The convergence of all trajectories to a narrow mortality band indicates that postpartum female harvesting is highly efficient and leads inevitably to demographic exhaustion regardless of initial encounter stochasticity. Conservation or management protocols must prioritize protection of breeding females, as the model demonstrates that even moderate forager densities (20 agents) can extract the entire reproductive potential of the prey population within approximately 1000 time steps (less than three reproductive cycles). The rapid saturation suggests that sustainable coexistence requires either strict constraints on female harvest rates or spatial refugia that reduce encounter probabilities below the threshold observed in these simulations.
