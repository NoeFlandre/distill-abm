Available green biomass initiates at approximately 45,000 units. An initial degradation phase persists through the first 27 years, driving biomass to a minimum of roughly 42,057 units. This early decline reflects the pressure exerted by initial stocking rates of 90 animals across 60 private pastures, where cumulative grazing temporarily exceeds the regenerative capacity of vegetation under highly variable rainfall conditions (mean 200 units, standard deviation 100).

Following the trough at year 27, the system enters a transitional stabilization period lasting until approximately year 50. During this interval, biomass fluctuates without a clear directional trend as the three learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—begin processing feedback from degraded pasture conditions. By year 50, a distinct inflection point marks the transition from degradation to sustained recovery.

From year 50 onward, biomass enters a prolonged increasing trajectory, ultimately reaching approximately 60,535 units by year 100. The recovery proceeds through distinct phases: an initial rapid acceleration between years 44 and 54 with growth rates exceeding 950 units per year, followed by progressively slowing increments that decrease to roughly 3 units per year by the final decade. Deceleration suggests the system is approaching a new equilibrium near the climatic potential biomass for the rainfall regime.

Superimposed on this trend is a dominant oscillation cycle of approximately 49 years, with notable fluctuations occurring between years 10-20 and 41-49. These periodic variations likely reflect cyclical adjustments in farmer stocking decisions as learning mechanisms process lagged feedback from vegetation conditions, creating alternating periods of conservative and moderately intensive grazing within the overall recovery pattern.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The 27-year lag between initial management and system recovery indicates that early intervention protocols may be necessary to prevent prolonged degradation of private pastures. The eventual biomass levels exceeding initial conditions by over 34% demonstrate that decentralized learning strategies can successfully manage grazing lands under high climatic variability without centralized coordination, though the 49-year oscillation cycle warns that system stability requires decades rather than years to achieve. The pronounced recovery after year 50 suggests that policies supporting conservative stocking rate adjustments—rather than fixed carrying capacity rules—may better accommodate the uncertainty inherent in variable rainfall environments.
