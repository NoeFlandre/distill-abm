Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. The simulation of British milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 reveals a trajectory of skimmed and semi-skimmed adoption. Consumption reaches approximately 1265 units, representing an early peak in the adoption curve. The overall pattern suggests an S-curve adoption dynamic interrupted by social inertia. The simulation reveals a monotonic increase in aggregate alternative milk consumption across the entire 1974-2005 period. The trajectory reflects a classic innovation diffusion process within the agent population, driven by the interaction of social influence, habit formation, and perception mechanisms embedded in the Watts-Strogatz network topology. A transitional moderation characterizes the middle period, roughly corresponding to 1984 through 1993, wherein the rate of decline temporarily stabilizes with no statistically significant downward movement.
