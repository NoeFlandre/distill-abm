**Context and Goals**

This agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Unlike the original RAGE model which examined common property scenarios where herders move livestock across shared rangelands, this extension focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods.

The simulation tracks the interaction between vegetation condition and household decisions about livestock numbers over time. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors.

The central purpose is understanding how these different ways of adapting to changing conditions influence pasture health, herd sizes, and the persistence of households. The model particularly examines how farmers handle uncertainty and incomplete information when making stocking decisions, represented through individual risk dispositions that affect how they perceive and react to environmental feedback.

**Key Parameters**

*Population and Spatial Setup:*
The **number-households** parameter establishes how many farming families occupy the landscape, which can range from 1 to 100 families, with this study using 60 households. Each household begins with **livestock-init** set at 90 animals. The simulation runs for **timesteps** set at 100 years. Farmers begin the simulation positioned on their designated land parcels, with **start-on-homepatch?** set to True. Each farmer can observe conditions within a **knowledge-radius** that ranges from 0 to 5 patches, set here at 1 patch distance.

*Behavioral Type Configuration:*
The study examines three specific learning strategies with equal representation: **number-E-RO** (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb), **number-E-LBD** (learning-by-doing), and **number-E-RO-SL1** (social learning with single-loop adaptation), each set to 20 households. The **homog-behav-types?** parameter set to True ensures each type is evaluated in uniform groups. The original RAGE model behavioral types are inactive in this extension: **number-SAT**, **number-TRAD**, **number-MAX**, and **number-random** are all set to 0. The default reference **behavioral-type** is set to E-RO.

*Grazing Management Practices:*
Formal pasture resting is disabled with **resting?** set to False. The threshold for initiating rest periods, **resting-threshold**, ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0.1. The **intrinsic-preference** for livestock relative to other values ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0. Similarly, **social-susceptibility** to neighbor influence ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0, indicating decisions rely on other factors.

*Original Model Parameters:*
Several parameters from the foundational RAGE model are retained. The **satisficing-threshold** for satisfied decision-making ranges from 0 to 100 and is set at 0. The **satisficing-trials** parameter, ranging from 0 to 100, is set at 21. These control how the original satisficer type would search for acceptable solutions, though this type is not active in the current setup.

*Ecological Conditions:*
Rainfall is applied uniformly across the landscape with **global-rain?** set to True. The average rainfall **rain-mean** is set at 200 units annually. Year-to-year variation in precipitation is controlled by **rain-std** set at 100, indicating significant variability in annual rainfall totals. Vegetation growth responds through two rates: **gr1** set at 0.5 and **gr2** set at 0.1. The parameter **w** from the original vegetation model is set at 0.8, influencing how pastures recover from grazing. Livestock reproduction is not active in this extension, with **b** set to 0.

*Learning and Risk Mechanisms:*
The extension module is activated with **extension-model?** set to True. Uncertainty handling is engaged with **risk-mode?** set to True. Each agent begins with an initial risk disposition **risk-att-init** set at 0.5, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance. The ability to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False, confining agents to single-loop learning within their assigned type.

The average initial risk attitude among social learning agents remains constant throughout the entire 100-year simulation period. At the aggregate level across all 30 simulation runs, this metric holds steady at approximately 0.0074 from the initial time step through the final time step, exhibiting zero variance and no temporal trend.

Individual simulation runs display constant but heterogeneous initial risk attitude values that vary across the ensemble, ranging from approximately -0.11 to 0.18. These values remain fixed within each respective run for the duration of the simulation, reflecting that the initial risk disposition is assigned at the outset and does not undergo modification through the single-loop learning process. The distribution of these constant values across runs centers near zero, with the aggregate mean of 0.0074 indicating a slight positive bias in initial risk disposition across the population of social learning agents.

This stability indicates that the parameter serves as a fixed baseline characteristic for each agent, representing their inherent disposition toward deviation from recommended livestock numbers that persists unchanged throughout the simulation timeframe.

The mean household risk attitude initiates at a near-neutral level of 0.0074 and increases rapidly during the first third of the simulation, reaching a maximum value of 0.3814 by year 33. This early phase is characterized by statistically significant upward trends, with the strongest positive momentum occurring between years 11 and 21, where the slope indicates a consistent annual increase. The ascent reflects the initial adaptation period as farmers with diverse learning strategies begin processing environmental feedback and adjusting their risk dispositions in response to pasture conditions.

Following the peak at year 33, the aggregate risk attitude enters a prolonged decline lasting approximately three decades. By year 46, values have fallen to 0.2691, and the downward trajectory continues through year 65, where the metric approaches 0.1913. This middle period exhibits statistically significant decreasing trends across consecutive analysis windows, with the rate of decline being steepest immediately after the peak (years 33-43) at a slope of -0.010 per year. The decrease suggests a collective recalibration toward more conservative risk postures as farmers accumulate experience with the variable rainfall conditions (mean 200, standard deviation 100) and vegetation dynamics.

After year 65, the system stabilizes with the mean risk attitude fluctuating around 0.1911 through year 100, showing no statistically significant directional trends in the final decades. The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 years, with inflection points at years 46, 51, and subsequent intervals indicating the transition from active adjustment to equilibrium.

While the aggregate trajectory follows this rise-plateau-fall pattern, individual simulation runs demonstrate substantial path-dependent divergence. From approximately year 20 onward, distinct farming populations separate into divergent risk cultures: some communities evolve highly risk-seeking dispositions stabilizing near 0.8, others maintain moderate risk tolerance between 0.2 and 0.6, and some develop strongly risk-averse postures declining to -0.4. This heterogeneity suggests that despite homogeneous initial conditions and identical learning strategy distributions, stochastic variation in early environmental feedback and neighbor interactions leads to the emergence of divergent collective risk attitudes. The dispersion becomes permanent by mid-simulation, indicating that single-loop learning within fixed behavioral types produces persistent divergence in how farming communities interpret and respond to uncertainty in stocking decisions.

**Pattern Description**

The reserve biomass across the landscape begins at 90,000 units and undergoes a characteristic U-shaped trajectory over the 100-year simulation period, ultimately reaching approximately 121,073 units. Following a brief increase to 90,523 units by step 3, the biomass enters a sustained degradation phase, decreasing to a minimum value of 84,114 units at step 26. This initial decline occurs at a significant rate of approximately 359 units per step during the first ten steps, accelerating to a consistent decrease of 196 units per step between steps 11 and 21, before moderating as the system approaches its nadir.

After step 26, the trajectory reverses, initiating a recovery phase that persists throughout the remainder of the simulation. The recovery begins gradually, with no statistically significant trend detectable between steps 22 and 32, but accelerates markedly after step 33, achieving growth rates exceeding 1,900 units per step between steps 44 and 54. A significant change point occurs at step 50, marking the transition from the degradation phase to sustained positive growth. Inflection points at steps 8 and 48 indicate shifts in the curvature of the trajectory—first moderating the initial decline, then transitioning from rapid to decelerating growth.

From step 60 onward, while the biomass continues to increase toward its asymptotic maximum, the rate of recovery diminishes progressively from 643 units per step down to approximately 5 units per step by the final ten-step window. This deceleration suggests the system is approaching a new equilibrium state. The entire sequence exhibits a dominant oscillation period of 49 steps, indicating cyclical feedback dynamics within the vegetation-livestock system.

**Interpretation within Grazing System Context**

The initial decline from 90,000 to 84,114 units over the first 26 steps indicates a period of vegetation degradation likely caused by initial stocking rates exceeding sustainable pasture productivity under the variable rainfall regime. The brief peak at step 3 may represent a transient favorable rainfall year or initial pasture condition before cumulative grazing pressure from the 60 households manifests.

The subsequent recovery aligns with the activation of learning mechanisms, as households adjust animal numbers based on observed pasture conditions within their knowledge radius and their specific behavioral strategies—whether experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning. The rapid growth phase between steps 48 and 60, with biomass increasing at rates exceeding 1,900 units per step, suggests that once critical biomass thresholds are crossed, vegetation recovery accelerates due to reduced grazing pressure and positive growth feedbacks.

The deceleration after step 60 reflects the system approaching a new equilibrium where biomass stabilizes at levels substantially higher (approximately 35% above) than the initial conditions, suggesting that the adaptive learning strategies ultimately lead to improved pasture management compared to the starting configuration. The 49-step oscillation period indicates a cyclical interaction between vegetation biomass and stocking decisions, potentially reflecting the time lag between biomass observation, decision-making under uncertainty (with initial risk disposition of 0.5), and subsequent herd size adjustments.

The mean green biomass across all patches begins at approximately 45,000 units. This early decline suggests that initial stocking rates exceeded immediate pasture carrying capacity, leading to vegetation depletion as grazing pressure intensified.

Thereafter, a sustained recovery commences around step 33, initially proceeding gradually, then accelerating sharply between steps 44 and 54. During this rapid growth phase, the rate of increase peaks at nearly 955 units per step, representing the most aggressive recovery period in the simulation.

However, substantial variance persists across the 30 simulation series at termination, with final values ranging between approximately 56,000 and 64,000 units.
