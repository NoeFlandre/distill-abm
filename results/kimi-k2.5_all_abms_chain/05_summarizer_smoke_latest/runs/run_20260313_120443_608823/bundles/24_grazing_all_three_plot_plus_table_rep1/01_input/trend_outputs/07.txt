The distribution of healthy livestock across households begins with perfect equality at the initiation of the simulation. Over the 100-year timeframe, inequality increases consistently and significantly, with the Gini coefficient rising from approximately zero to terminal values near 0.07 to 0.09 across different simulation runs.

The trajectory of increasing inequality follows a non-linear pattern characterized by multiple inflection points occurring at approximately 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, 37, and 57 years. These inflections indicate shifts in the rate at which disparity accumulates among households. Notably, change points at years 51 and 71 mark transitions to accelerated divergence, with the rate of inequality growth increasing substantially in the latter half of the simulation compared to the earlier decades.

Throughout the first 50 years, inequality accumulates gradually as households begin to diverge in their livestock management outcomes. After year 51, the pace of divergence intensifies, and by year 71, the system enters a phase of rapid polarization where the gap between households with healthy herds and those with declining herds widens substantially. This acceleration continues through the terminal period, with inequality increasing at its fastest rate in the final decades.

Variability across simulation runs remains minimal initially but expands considerably after the first quarter of the century, indicating that while all trajectories trend toward greater inequality, the specific magnitude and timing of divergence depend on particular sequences of decisions and environmental conditions. Oscillation patterns detected in the later years (around years 78-93) suggest temporary fluctuations in the rate of divergence, yet the overall direction remains consistently toward greater heterogeneity in livestock holdings.

This progressive stratification reflects the cumulative impact of differing learning strategies and risk dispositions among households. Even with uniform initial conditions and landscape-wide rainfall, the interaction between individual decision-making approaches and stochastic environmental variability generates persistent differential advantages. Households employing distinct learning mechanisms—whether rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning—experience divergent trajectories of success, leading to an increasingly skewed distribution of healthy animals.

For decision makers and development practitioners, these findings indicate that policy interventions aiming to maintain equitable livestock distributions must account for the compounding effects of learning heterogeneity and individual risk responses. Without targeted support mechanisms, natural differentiation processes inherent in adaptive management systems tend to amplify initial small disparities into substantial inequality over multi-decade periods. Early intervention periods, particularly within the first 20-40 years before the critical change point at year 51, may offer critical windows for equitable resource distribution before polarization becomes entrenched and increasingly difficult to reverse.
