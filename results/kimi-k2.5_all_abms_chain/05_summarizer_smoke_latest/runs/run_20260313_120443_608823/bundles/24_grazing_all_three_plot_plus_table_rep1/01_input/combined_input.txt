**Context and Goals**

This agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Unlike the original RAGE model which examined common property scenarios where herders move livestock across shared rangelands, this extension focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods.

The simulation tracks the interaction between vegetation condition and household decisions about livestock numbers over time. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors.

The central purpose is understanding how these different ways of adapting to changing conditions influence pasture health, herd sizes, and the persistence of households. The model particularly examines how farmers handle uncertainty and incomplete information when making stocking decisions, represented through individual risk dispositions that affect how they perceive and react to environmental feedback.

**Key Parameters**

*Population and Spatial Setup:*
The **number-households** parameter establishes how many farming families occupy the landscape, which can range from 1 to 100 families, with this study using 60 households. Each household begins with **livestock-init** set at 90 animals. The simulation runs for **timesteps** set at 100 years. Farmers begin the simulation positioned on their designated land parcels, with **start-on-homepatch?** set to True. Each farmer can observe conditions within a **knowledge-radius** that ranges from 0 to 5 patches, set here at 1 patch distance.

*Behavioral Type Configuration:*
The study examines three specific learning strategies with equal representation: **number-E-RO** (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb), **number-E-LBD** (learning-by-doing), and **number-E-RO-SL1** (social learning with single-loop adaptation), each set to 20 households. The **homog-behav-types?** parameter set to True ensures each type is evaluated in uniform groups. The original RAGE model behavioral types are inactive in this extension: **number-SAT**, **number-TRAD**, **number-MAX**, and **number-random** are all set to 0. The default reference **behavioral-type** is set to E-RO.

*Grazing Management Practices:*
Formal pasture resting is disabled with **resting?** set to False. The threshold for initiating rest periods, **resting-threshold**, ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0.1. The **intrinsic-preference** for livestock relative to other values ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0. Similarly, **social-susceptibility** to neighbor influence ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0, indicating decisions rely on other factors.

*Original Model Parameters:*
Several parameters from the foundational RAGE model are retained. The **satisficing-threshold** for satisfied decision-making ranges from 0 to 100 and is set at 0. The **satisficing-trials** parameter, ranging from 0 to 100, is set at 21. These control how the original satisficer type would search for acceptable solutions, though this type is not active in the current setup.

*Ecological Conditions:*
Rainfall is applied uniformly across the landscape with **global-rain?** set to True. The average rainfall **rain-mean** is set at 200 units annually. Year-to-year variation in precipitation is controlled by **rain-std** set at 100, indicating significant variability in annual rainfall totals. Vegetation growth responds through two rates: **gr1** set at 0.5 and **gr2** set at 0.1. The parameter **w** from the original vegetation model is set at 0.8, influencing how pastures recover from grazing. Livestock reproduction is not active in this extension, with **b** set to 0.

*Learning and Risk Mechanisms:*
The extension module is activated with **extension-model?** set to True. Uncertainty handling is engaged with **risk-mode?** set to True. Each agent begins with an initial risk disposition **risk-att-init** set at 0.5, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance. The ability to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False, confining agents to single-loop learning within their assigned type.

The mean initial risk attitude across the social learning agent population remains constant at 0.0074 throughout the entire 100-step simulation period. This value holds steady from step 0 through step 100, exhibiting zero variance and no statistically significant temporal trends in any analysis window.  

While the aggregate mean stabilizes at this near-zero value, the underlying simulation runs display heterogeneous static levels distributed between approximately -0.11 and +0.18. These constant trajectories indicate that initial risk dispositions are fixed properties assigned at model initialization; they represent inherent farmer characteristics that do not evolve through learning, environmental feedback, or social interaction over time. The symmetric distribution of individual run values around the central mean of 0.0074 suggests that while specific farmer cohorts may start with conservative (negative) or adventurous (positive) risk tendencies, the overall population begins with a balanced, risk-neutral disposition relative to recommended livestock numbers.  

For decision makers, this stability implies that differential long-term outcomes observed under various learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning) emerge solely from how agents process information and adapt behavior, rather than from changes in their fundamental risk baselines. The presence of heterogeneous yet static initial attitudes highlights the importance of accounting for pre-existing farmer risk profiles when designing interventions, as agents with initially conservative versus adventurous dispositions will respond differently to the same environmental signals despite starting with an average population risk neutrality.

The mean household risk attitude across the population begins at approximately zero (0.0074), indicating that initial stocking decisions align closely with formal recommendations. During the first thirty-three steps, the system undergoes a rapid and statistically significant increase, reaching a maximum value of 0.3814. This upward trend suggests that households initially adopt increasingly conservative strategies, potentially understocking their pastures as they encounter environmental variability or process early-warning signals from pasture conditions and neighbor observations.

Following this peak at step thirty-three, the trajectory reverses significantly through step sixty-five, declining to approximately 0.19. This correction phase indicates that learning mechanisms—whether through individual experience or social observation—enable households to recognize and moderate their initial over-conservatism, adjusting stocking rates back toward recommended levels while maintaining a positive safety margin.

After step sixty-five, the system stabilizes with no statistically significant trend through the remainder of the simulation, settling at a terminal value of 0.1911. This persistent positive deviation suggests that long-term learning outcomes converge on a moderately conservative equilibrium rather than returning to baseline recommendations or continuing to decline.

However, substantial heterogeneity characterizes individual household trajectories within this aggregate pattern. While the population mean stabilizes near 0.2, individual simulation runs exhibit terminal values spanning from highly conservative positions (0.8) to risk-seeking orientations (-0.4). This divergence emerges primarily between steps ten and forty, after which individual households largely maintain their relative risk positions with minor oscillations. Multiple inflection points between steps seven and ninety indicate that the learning process involves continuous micro-adjustments rather than simple linear progression, with notable oscillations occurring between steps twenty and thirty-five coinciding with the period of maximum strategic divergence.

For decision makers, these trends suggest that smallholder learning systems naturally produce an initial period of excessive caution that may temporarily reduce livestock productivity below optimal levels. The high variance in outcomes indicates that uniform extension recommendations may prove insufficient; instead, differentiated support is needed for households converging toward risky overstocking behaviors, while conservative households may require encouragement to utilize pasture capacity more fully. The persistent moderate conservatism in the population mean suggests that risk-averse behaviors become embedded in the social-ecological system, potentially providing resilience against extreme environmental shocks but also indicating systematic underutilization of grazing resources.

The mean reserve biomass begins at 90,000 units and experiences a brief initial increase to approximately 90,523 by step 3. Following this, it undergoes a significant and sustained decline, reaching a minimum value of 84,115 around step 26. This initial degradation phase, representing a reduction of roughly 6.5% from the starting baseline, likely reflects the initial overstocking pressure as 60 households each maintain 90 livestock units on their private pastures without immediate adaptive adjustments. The presence of an inflection point at step 8 indicates the rate of decline begins moderating early, suggesting some initial behavioral responses, yet the lag before reaching the minimum at step 26 indicates continued ecological deterioration as vegetation depletion outpaces the early learning effects.

Between steps 26 and 48, the system enters a transition period characterized by statistical stabilization with no clear directional trend, as indicated by the absence of significant monotonic changes during steps 22–32. This plateau suggests that learning mechanisms, including learning-by-doing and social learning from neighbors within the knowledge radius of one patch, begin counteracting the degradation pressure, halting further biomass loss despite ongoing rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100).

From step 48 onward, marked by a distinct change point at step 50, the biomass enters a prolonged recovery phase characterized by statistically significant monotonic increases across all subsequent measurement windows. The growth accelerates rapidly between steps 33 and 54, with rates exceeding 1,900 units per step, as farmers' accumulated experiences and observations of neighbor outcomes lead to effective adjustments in stocking decisions. As the simulation progresses beyond step 65, the rate of increase gradually decelerates from approximately 640 units per step down to roughly 5 units per step by the final stages, though remaining statistically significant through step 98. This deceleration, combined with the inflection dynamics, suggests the system is converging toward a new equilibrium carrying capacity under adapted management regimes.

By step 100, the metric reaches approximately 121,073 units, representing a 34.5% increase over the initial value and a 44% recovery from the minimum trough. The oscillation periods detected between steps 10–20 and 41–49 suggest intermediate adjustments and temporary fluctuations as different learning strategies interact with variable climatic conditions before converging toward improved outcomes. The trajectory indicates that while initial pasture conditions deteriorate under fixed stocking rates, the incorporation of adaptive learning eventually leads to management practices that not only recover lost biomass but substantially improve pasture health beyond original levels.

For decision makers, these results suggest that encouraging adaptive learning strategies among smallholder farmers can reverse initial resource degradation, but requires patience given the 25–30 step lag before recovery initiates. The substantial long-term gains in reserve biomass indicate that short-term productivity sacrifices during the learning phase yield significant ecological benefits. The divergence in trajectories after step 50 implies that the specific learning strategy employed materially affects outcomes, suggesting investment in social learning networks and experiential feedback mechanisms may enhance long-term pasture sustainability. The final approach to equilibrium demonstrates that even with adaptation, biological limits exist, highlighting the need for realistic expectations about maximum sustainable biomass levels under variable climatic conditions.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The 27-year initial decline represents a critical vulnerability window where pasture degradation could compromise household livelihoods before learning mechanisms effectively reduce grazing pressure. This suggests that initial livestock endowments may exceed immediate sustainable carrying capacity, and temporary policy interventions—such as destocking subsidies or supplementary feeding programs during startup—could prevent resource depletion during this high-risk period. The subsequent recovery to levels significantly exceeding initial conditions demonstrates that adaptive learning strategies eventually improve pasture health, though the system requires nearly three decades to surpass baseline biomass. 

The substantial variation in final outcomes across simulations (spanning approximately 8000 units) indicates that stochastic environmental factors and heterogeneous risk dispositions create divergent long-term ecological trajectories even with identical learning strategies. This variability underscores the need for robust social safety nets and risk-sharing mechanisms during the transition period. The rapid growth phase occurring between steps 44 and 54 suggests that once critical ecological thresholds are crossed, vegetation recovery can accelerate significantly, presenting opportunities for targeted interventions to shorten the recovery period and reduce vulnerability duration.

The average combined measure of current livestock holdings plus cumulative destocking per household begins at 90 animals, reflecting the initial endowment for each farming family. Over the first 20 time steps, this metric declines sharply and significantly, dropping by approximately 50 percent as households engage in aggressive destocking. This rapid reduction reflects the immediate ecological pressure exerted by the initial high stocking rate of 90 animals under conditions of significant rainfall variability, forcing households to rapidly reduce herd sizes to prevent pasture degradation.

From step 20 to step 37, the decline continues but at a markedly slower rate, reaching a minimum value of approximately 41 animals around step 37. This deceleration suggests that households are approaching the lower bounds of viable herd sizes or that initial pasture shock has stabilized. Between steps 33 and 44, the system enters a temporary plateau phase where no significant trend is detected, indicating a short-term equilibrium where stocking decisions and pasture conditions are momentarily balanced.

Following this stabilization, a significant increasing trend emerges from step 44 through step 100, with the metric recovering from approximately 41 to around 48 animals. However, the rate of increase slows progressively over this period, with the slope of recovery declining from 0.313 in the early recovery phase (steps 44-54) to 0.002 by the final periods (steps 88-98). This asymptotic approach suggests that households are gradually rebuilding their herds through adaptive learning and improved pasture management, but the system stabilizes well below the initial stocking level, indicating that the starting value of 90 was ecologically unsustainable.

Across the 30 simulation runs, substantial variance emerges particularly after step 60, with final values ranging from approximately 41 to over 60 animals by step 100. This divergence reflects the differential performance of the three learning strategies tested—baseline rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—in navigating environmental uncertainty. The oscillation patterns detected around the 49-step cycle suggest underlying cyclical adjustments as households iteratively respond to vegetation feedback and neighbor behaviors.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The trajectory demonstrates that high initial stocking rates inevitably force rapid destocking that jeopardizes short-term livelihood security. Decision makers should recognize that while adaptive learning strategies enable gradual recovery, the long-term sustainable carrying capacity stabilizes substantially below initial endowment levels—approximately 47 to 48 animals on average. The significant variance in outcomes between different learning approaches suggests that investment in improving farmer learning networks and adaptive capacity can meaningfully affect long-term herd sustainability, with some strategies achieving carrying capacities 20-30 percent higher than others. Policymakers should prioritize early warning systems that prevent initial overstocking, as the recovery from aggressive destocking takes over 60 time steps and never fully returns to initial levels in this context.

The distribution of healthy livestock across households begins with perfect equality at the initiation of the simulation. Over the 100-year timeframe, inequality increases consistently and significantly, with the Gini coefficient rising from approximately zero to terminal values near 0.07 to 0.09 across different simulation runs.

The trajectory of increasing inequality follows a non-linear pattern characterized by multiple inflection points occurring at approximately 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, 37, and 57 years. These inflections indicate shifts in the rate at which disparity accumulates among households. Notably, change points at years 51 and 71 mark transitions to accelerated divergence, with the rate of inequality growth increasing substantially in the latter half of the simulation compared to the earlier decades.

Throughout the first 50 years, inequality accumulates gradually as households begin to diverge in their livestock management outcomes. After year 51, the pace of divergence intensifies, and by year 71, the system enters a phase of rapid polarization where the gap between households with healthy herds and those with declining herds widens substantially. This acceleration continues through the terminal period, with inequality increasing at its fastest rate in the final decades.

Variability across simulation runs remains minimal initially but expands considerably after the first quarter of the century, indicating that while all trajectories trend toward greater inequality, the specific magnitude and timing of divergence depend on particular sequences of decisions and environmental conditions. Oscillation patterns detected in the later years (around years 78-93) suggest temporary fluctuations in the rate of divergence, yet the overall direction remains consistently toward greater heterogeneity in livestock holdings.

This progressive stratification reflects the cumulative impact of differing learning strategies and risk dispositions among households. Even with uniform initial conditions and landscape-wide rainfall, the interaction between individual decision-making approaches and stochastic environmental variability generates persistent differential advantages. Households employing distinct learning mechanisms—whether rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning—experience divergent trajectories of success, leading to an increasingly skewed distribution of healthy animals.

For decision makers and development practitioners, these findings indicate that policy interventions aiming to maintain equitable livestock distributions must account for the compounding effects of learning heterogeneity and individual risk responses. Without targeted support mechanisms, natural differentiation processes inherent in adaptive management systems tend to amplify initial small disparities into substantial inequality over multi-decade periods. Early intervention periods, particularly within the first 20-40 years before the critical change point at year 51, may offer critical windows for equitable resource distribution before polarization becomes entrenched and increasingly difficult to reverse.

The diffusion of social learning among households initiates from zero and undergoes rapid acceleration within the first 7 steps, reaching approximately 20 households on average by step 20. Growth rates decelerate substantially after step 28, with notable inflection points at steps 30, 33, and 38 marking progressively slower adoption as the system approaches equilibrium. A structural shift occurs near step 40, transitioning from active diffusion to stabilization, with the aggregate mean peaking at approximately 32 households by step 63 and maintaining this level through step 100.

However, individual simulation trajectories exhibit substantial divergence rather than convergence. While the mean stabilizes around 32 households (53% of the population), actual outcomes range widely from 18 to 54 households (30% to 90% adoption) by the conclusion of the simulation. Distinct clusters emerge: some trajectories plateau near 18 households, others between 30 and 42, and the remainder achieve near-complete adoption at 48 to 54 households. Oscillations between steps 51 and 59 indicate temporary fluctuations in engagement as households potentially reassess the utility of social information against private experience.

Implications for decision makers:
The persistent variability in final adoption rates, despite identical starting conditions, indicates that social learning diffusion is highly sensitive to stochastic early-phase factors and path-dependent dynamics. Agricultural extension programs should not assume uniform adoption; instead, they should prepare for scenarios where 30% to 90% of farmers may ultimately engage with social learning. The critical intervention window occurs within the first 20 steps, after which adoption trajectories become largely fixed. To achieve universal or near-universal adoption, complementary targeted interventions may be necessary after step 40 to engage the 40-50% of households who do not adopt through spontaneous peer diffusion alone.

The cumulative number of livestock destocked across households begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation period, reaching a mean value of approximately 97 animals per household by the final time step, with a range spanning from roughly 58 to 172 across different simulation runs.

The middle phase, spanning years 25 to 60, shows continued but moderated accumulation at a rate of approximately 0.3 to 0.4 animals per year. Change points detected around year 25 and year 60 mark transitions between these accumulation regimes. The slowing rate during this period indicates that households are adapting their stocking decisions based on accumulated experience, yet the continued positive slope demonstrates that destocking pressure persists and cumulative losses continue to grow.

The final phase from year 60 to 100 maintains a steady accumulation rate of approximately 0.3 animals per year, indicating that the system has reached a quasi-equilibrium where households continue to destock periodically in response to ongoing environmental variability and pasture degradation, but at a stabilized rate. The presence of multiple inflection points between years 45 and 75 suggests complex feedback dynamics in the learning and adaptation processes, with curvature shifts indicating changing balances between herd accumulation and destocking pressures.

The substantial variance across simulation runs, with a standard deviation of approximately 20 animals and a range exceeding 100 animals between minimum and maximum trajectories, indicates high sensitivity to stochastic factors such as rainfall variability and initial learning outcomes. This divergence suggests that under identical learning strategies, some households face dramatically higher cumulative losses than others due to chance factors in environmental conditions and the timing of adaptation decisions.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that destocking is an ongoing necessity throughout the simulation period rather than a one-time adjustment, with the highest pressure occurring in the early years of pasture use. The persistence of destocking even after 100 years suggests that sustainable stocking rates remain elusive under the modeled learning strategies and environmental variability, pointing to the need for more aggressive early destocking or alternative management approaches to prevent the accumulation of long-term losses. The high variability between outcomes underscores the risk that individual households face substantially different fates despite employing the same decision-making strategies, highlighting the importance of risk management and social safety nets in grazing systems subject to environmental stochasticity.
