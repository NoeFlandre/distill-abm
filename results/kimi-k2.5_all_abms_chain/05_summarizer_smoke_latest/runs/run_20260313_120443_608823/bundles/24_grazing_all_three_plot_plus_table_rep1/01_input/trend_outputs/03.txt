The mean reserve biomass begins at 90,000 units and experiences a brief initial increase to approximately 90,523 by step 3. Following this, it undergoes a significant and sustained decline, reaching a minimum value of 84,115 around step 26. This initial degradation phase, representing a reduction of roughly 6.5% from the starting baseline, likely reflects the initial overstocking pressure as 60 households each maintain 90 livestock units on their private pastures without immediate adaptive adjustments. The presence of an inflection point at step 8 indicates the rate of decline begins moderating early, suggesting some initial behavioral responses, yet the lag before reaching the minimum at step 26 indicates continued ecological deterioration as vegetation depletion outpaces the early learning effects.

Between steps 26 and 48, the system enters a transition period characterized by statistical stabilization with no clear directional trend, as indicated by the absence of significant monotonic changes during steps 22–32. This plateau suggests that learning mechanisms, including learning-by-doing and social learning from neighbors within the knowledge radius of one patch, begin counteracting the degradation pressure, halting further biomass loss despite ongoing rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100).

From step 48 onward, marked by a distinct change point at step 50, the biomass enters a prolonged recovery phase characterized by statistically significant monotonic increases across all subsequent measurement windows. The growth accelerates rapidly between steps 33 and 54, with rates exceeding 1,900 units per step, as farmers' accumulated experiences and observations of neighbor outcomes lead to effective adjustments in stocking decisions. As the simulation progresses beyond step 65, the rate of increase gradually decelerates from approximately 640 units per step down to roughly 5 units per step by the final stages, though remaining statistically significant through step 98. This deceleration, combined with the inflection dynamics, suggests the system is converging toward a new equilibrium carrying capacity under adapted management regimes.

By step 100, the metric reaches approximately 121,073 units, representing a 34.5% increase over the initial value and a 44% recovery from the minimum trough. The oscillation periods detected between steps 10–20 and 41–49 suggest intermediate adjustments and temporary fluctuations as different learning strategies interact with variable climatic conditions before converging toward improved outcomes. The trajectory indicates that while initial pasture conditions deteriorate under fixed stocking rates, the incorporation of adaptive learning eventually leads to management practices that not only recover lost biomass but substantially improve pasture health beyond original levels.

For decision makers, these results suggest that encouraging adaptive learning strategies among smallholder farmers can reverse initial resource degradation, but requires patience given the 25–30 step lag before recovery initiates. The substantial long-term gains in reserve biomass indicate that short-term productivity sacrifices during the learning phase yield significant ecological benefits. The divergence in trajectories after step 50 implies that the specific learning strategy employed materially affects outcomes, suggesting investment in social learning networks and experiential feedback mechanisms may enhance long-term pasture sustainability. The final approach to equilibrium demonstrates that even with adaptation, biological limits exist, highlighting the need for realistic expectations about maximum sustainable biomass levels under variable climatic conditions.
