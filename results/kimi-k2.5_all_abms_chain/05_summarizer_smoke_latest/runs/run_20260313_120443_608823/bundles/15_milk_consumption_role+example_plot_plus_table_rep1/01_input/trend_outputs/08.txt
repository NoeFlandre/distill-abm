The mean habit factor for whole milk commences at a neutral baseline value of 1.0000. During the initial four to five time steps, this metric undergoes a rapid acceleration, ascending sharply to approximately 1.022. This initial surge likely reflects the immediate activation and reinforcement of habitual consumption patterns among agents beginning from the 1974 baseline, wherein whole milk held dominant market position and initial habit strengths were set at maximum values.  

Following this sharp initial increase, the habit factor transitions into a phase of steady, approximately linear growth throughout the remainder of the simulation period. Statistical analysis identifies inflection points at time steps 7 and 12, where the rate of increase moderates slightly before resuming its consistent upward trajectory. Across the full 33-step timeframe, corresponding to the 1974–2005 historical period, the metric exhibits a monotonically increasing trend with no detected peaks or valleys, ultimately reaching a terminal value of 1.0502. This represents a cumulative increase of approximately 5% above the baseline habitual tendency.  

The trajectory demonstrates remarkable consistency across the 30 simulation runs, maintaining a standard deviation of merely 0.0110 despite the stochastic nature of social network interactions and individual decision-making processes. Rolling Mann-Kendall trend analysis confirms statistically significant increasing patterns (τ = 1.000, p = 0.027) across all examined windows from steps 0–4 through 25–29, indicating robust, uninterrupted habit intensification throughout the three-decade period. Superimposed upon this upward trend are oscillatory variations with a dominant period of 15 steps, particularly evident around time indices 3–7 and 26–30, suggesting cyclical fluctuations in habit strength possibly associated with periodic reassessment cycles or waves of social influence propagation through the network.  

Within the context of the British milk market transition, this gradual strengthening of the whole milk habit factor—despite the overarching historical shift toward skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives—suggests that among consumers who maintained their consumption of whole milk, habitual reinforcement intensified over time. This dynamic may reflect increasing psychological commitment to traditional consumption patterns among a shrinking subset of the population, or alternatively, represent the mathematical properties of habit accumulation where continued repetition strengthens the habitual baseline multiplier even as overall market participation declines. The low variance across simulation runs further indicates that this intensification pattern is a structural property of the modeled habit-formation mechanism rather than an artifact of random social network configurations.
