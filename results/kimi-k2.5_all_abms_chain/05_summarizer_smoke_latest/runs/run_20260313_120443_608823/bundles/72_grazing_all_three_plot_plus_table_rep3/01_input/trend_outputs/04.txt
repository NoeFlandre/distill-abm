The mean green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units, exhibiting a brief initial increase to 45,261 by step 4. Following this early peak, the metric enters a pronounced decline phase, decreasing significantly until reaching a minimum value of 42,057 at step 27. This initial deterioration reflects the immediate grazing pressure from the initial livestock endowment of 90 animals per household, where stationary farmers have not yet adapted their stocking decisions to the carrying capacity of their private parcels, exacerbated by significant annual rainfall variability (σ = 100 units).

After step 27, the system transitions into a sustained recovery phase lasting through step 100. From steps 33 to 54, biomass increases sharply with an acceleration around step 50, indicating a critical transition point where learning mechanisms begin to dominate stocking decisions. The rate of increase moderates progressively through subsequent periods (steps 55-65, 66-76, and 77-87), transitioning from steep growth to gradual stabilization as the metric approaches approximately 60,535 units by the final timestep. This trajectory suggests that experiential learning-by-doing and social learning from neighbors enable farmers to gradually reduce grazing pressure or optimize stocking rates, allowing vegetation recovery. The oscillation period of approximately 49 steps aligns with the transition from depletion to recovery phases, potentially reflecting the time required for adaptation cycles to propagate through the network of 60 households with knowledge radii of one patch.

Across the 30 simulation runs representing different combinations of learning strategies (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), substantial variation emerges after step 40, with outcomes diverging significantly (standard deviation of 8,006 units). While all trajectories follow the general U-shaped pattern—declining to minima between steps 20-40 before recovering—the terminal values range widely, with some simulations stabilizing near 56,000 units while others approach 64,000 units. This heterogeneity indicates that the specific learning strategy employed critically determines long-term pasture health, even when all agents face identical initial conditions and ecological parameters.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The trajectory reveals that private pasture systems experience an inevitable initial degradation period lasting roughly 27 steps (years) before learning effects manifest, suggesting that short-term assessments of new grazing management policies may misleadingly indicate failure. The critical inflection around step 50 highlights that institutional patience is required, as sustainable stocking behaviors require approximately half the simulation timeframe to stabilize. The wide variance in terminal outcomes across learning strategies suggests that promoting social learning mechanisms and experiential adaptation may be more effective for long-term land health than static rule-of-thumb approaches. Furthermore, the sustained divergence between minimum (42,057) and maximum (64,000) potential biomass outcomes implies that farmer decision-making quality is as significant as ecological conditions in determining pasture productivity, emphasizing the need for extension services that facilitate knowledge sharing within limited spatial neighborhoods.
