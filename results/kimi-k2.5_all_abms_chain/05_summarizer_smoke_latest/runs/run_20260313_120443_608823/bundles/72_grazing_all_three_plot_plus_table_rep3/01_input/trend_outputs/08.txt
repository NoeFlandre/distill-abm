The count of households engaged in social learning initiates at zero and undergoes rapid expansion during the first phase of the simulation. Between steps 0 and approximately 20, the metric increases steeply with an initial mean slope of 1.43, indicating swift adoption as households begin observing and emulating neighbors within the restricted knowledge radius of one patch. Significant inflection occurs around step 7, where the acceleration rate first begins to moderate.

From steps 20 to 40, growth continues but decelerates markedly, with mean slopes declining to approximately 0.18. Multiple inflection points cluster in this interval, particularly near steps 28, 30, 33, and 38, reflecting fluctuating rates of engagement as the system approaches saturation. A detected change point at step 40 signals the transition from active growth to stabilization. By step 63, the mean count reaches its maximum value of approximately 32 households, representing roughly 53 percent of the total population of 60 farming households.

Following step 63, the system enters a prolonged plateau with minimal net change, ending at a mean value of 32.07 by step 100. However, substantial variance exists across the 30 simulation runs, with a standard deviation of 8.92. Individual trajectories range from approximately 18 to 54 households at termination, indicating that while the average saturation point lies near 32, stochastic factors such as rainfall variability and initial spatial configurations can drive outcomes significantly higher or lower. Secondary oscillations appear around steps 51–59, suggesting minor adjustments in engagement even after the primary stabilization.

This saturation pattern implies that spatial constraints and behavioral heterogeneity create inherent limits to social learning diffusion. The knowledge radius of one patch and the presence of 20 households initially assigned to non-social learning types establish a ceiling where approximately half the population may remain unengaged despite prolonged exposure. The high variability across runs further indicates that adoption success depends heavily on idiosyncratic early conditions and environmental stochasticity rather than deterministic spread.

For decision makers, these trends suggest that interventions promoting social learning can expect rapid initial uptake within the first two decades, but should anticipate asymptotic behavior around 50 percent penetration under current spatial and informational constraints. To achieve broader adoption, policy designs may need to expand knowledge networks beyond the one-patch radius or specifically target isolated households. Additionally, the high outcome variability indicates that monitoring systems should account for significant community-level differences in adoption rates when evaluating intervention success.
