The mean household initial risk attitude remains constant at approximately 0.0074 throughout the entire 100-step simulation period. This metric exhibits no temporal variation, maintaining identical values at the start and end of the simulation with zero standard deviation and variance across all time steps. Statistical analysis confirms the absence of significant trends in any examined time window, indicating no upward or downward movements, cyclical patterns, or structural changes occur during the simulation duration.

Individual household initial risk attitudes remain fixed at their respective constant values across all 100 steps, reflecting static parameters established at model initialization that do not respond to environmental feedback, learning experiences, or social interactions. These values represent inherent baseline dispositions toward uncertainty that persist unchanged, serving as stable reference points for household decision-making regarding livestock stocking levels.

For decision makers, the immutable nature of initial risk attitudes suggests that heterogeneity in farmer risk dispositions constitutes a persistent structural feature of the farming population. Since these attitudes do not adapt over time, observed variations in long-term outcomes between households emerge from the interaction between fixed individual risk profiles, distinct learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning), and stochastic ecological conditions rather than from endogenous shifts in risk perception. This implies that policy interventions or extension services should account for the distribution of fixed risk attitudes within the population, tailoring support mechanisms to how different inherent risk dispositions interact with available information and learning opportunities under variable rainfall regimes.
