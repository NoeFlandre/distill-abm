The consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk demonstrates a three-phase adoption trajectory over the 1974-2005 simulation period, moving from minimal adoption to dominant market presence.

In the initial phase spanning time steps 0 through 11, consumption begins at 5.29 units and undergoes rapid acceleration, reaching approximately 1,265 units by step 11. This period exhibits strong monotonic growth with statistically significant positive trends (p < 0.05), characterized by steep slopes exceeding 139 units per time step. The dynamics reflect the initial challenge of displacing entrenched whole milk consumption patterns despite starting from low baseline health perceptions (1.69) and environmental perceptions (1.12) regarding alternative milk options.

The middle phase, covering time steps 11 through 25, manifests as a stabilization period with oscillating values centered around 1,220 units, fluctuating between valleys of approximately 1,183-1,210 units and peaks of 1,221-1,265 units. During this interval, the trend becomes statistically flat (p > 0.05), indicating market equilibrium where adoption-driving forces balance habitual resistance mechanisms. The presence of peaks at steps 11, 13, 19, and 21, alternating with valleys at steps 12, 18, 20, and 23, suggests cyclical fluctuations within a saturated adoption state.

The final phase, encompassing time steps 25 through 32, resumes significant growth (p = 0.027, slope = 48.544), advancing from roughly 1,220 units to terminate at 1,456.60 units by step 32. This late-stage increase, though less steep than the initial surge, represents continued conversion of remaining consumers holding strong habitual attachments to whole milk.

Across the entire simulation, mean consumption averages 1,051.59 units with a median of 1,220.21, indicating a left-skewed distribution influenced by the prolonged low starting values. The substantial variance (164,144.32) reflects heterogeneous adoption timing across the consumer population.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The distinct phases suggest that public health campaigns and marketing strategies for alternative milk products require temporal adaptation. The early period demands intensive social influence activation and perception-shaping investments to overcome high habitual barriers, as the data show that even with social influence mechanisms active (probability of interaction at 0.3, susceptibility at 0.2), significant momentum is required to displace incumbent habits. The middle plateau indicates that once approximately 85% of peak consumption is achieved, market saturation effects necessitate differentiated strategies targeting resistant consumer segments.
