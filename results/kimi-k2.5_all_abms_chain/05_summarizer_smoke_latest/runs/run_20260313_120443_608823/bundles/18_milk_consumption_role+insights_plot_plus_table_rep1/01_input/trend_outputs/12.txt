The metric measuring mean environmental value deviation exhibits a characteristic three-phase trajectory across the 33-step simulation period, reflecting the temporal dynamics of value-action gap formation in sustainable consumption transitions.

**Phase 1: Rapid Dissonance Accumulation (Steps 0–5)**
The series initiates at a baseline of 0.0000, indicating perfect alignment between consumer environmental values and purchase choices at the 1974 starting point dominated by whole milk consumption. During the initial five steps, the deviation undergoes accelerated growth, reaching approximately 12.5–13.0. Statistical validation confirms this as a period of significant monotonic increase (Mann-Kendall tau = 1.000, slope = 2.840, p = 0.027), representing the rapid emergence of environmental consciousness regarding milk alternatives (initial environmental perception = 1.12) confronting entrenched habitual behavior (incumbent habit strength = 10.0). This steep ascent captures the psychological tension generated when consumers acquire new environmental information but remain behaviorally constrained by high-switching-cost habits.

**Phase 2: Decelerating Growth (Steps 5–13)**
Between steps 5 and 13, the rate of deviation increase attenuates substantially, with the mean value converging toward a maximum observed value of 13.4756 at step 13. The rolling trend analysis reveals continued but significantly slower increase (window 5–9: slope = 0.109, p = 0.027), suggesting the onset of behavioral transition as social influence mechanisms (p-interact = 0.3, social susceptibility = 0.2) and threshold-breaking psychological processes begin overcoming habitual inertia for a subset of agents. The reduced slope indicates that early adopters switching to skimmed/semi-skimmed varieties partially offset the growing environmental consciousness of the remaining whole milk consumers.

**Phase 3: High-Level Stabilization (Steps 13–32)**
From step 13 onward, the system enters a plateau phase characterized by minimal variation around a stable equilibrium of approximately 13.4–13.5. The terminal value at step 32 registers 13.4186, marginally below the step 13 peak (13.4756), with a series mean of 12.6015 and median of 13.4302. Statistical tests indicate no significant trend in successive windows (steps 10–14 through 25–29: all p-values > 0.05), confirming structural stability. Minor oscillations appear at inflection points (steps 16, 19, 24, 26, 28, 31) with peak-trough differentials not exceeding 0.06, suggesting system-wide equilibration where aggregate value-action gaps persist despite market share crossover to alternative milk products.

**Behavioral Interpretation**
The trajectory reveals that despite the historical transition from whole to reduced-fat milk varieties, substantial environmental value-action gaps stabilize rather than resolve, maintaining a persistent deviation of approximately 13.4 units throughout the latter half of the simulation. This implies that even as consumers adopt environmentally preferable alternatives, the gap between their held environmental values and realized consumption choices remains structurally embedded, potentially due to justification mechanisms (justification = 0.61) allowing consumers to rationalize remaining discrepancies or cognitive dissonance thresholds (0.15) preventing complete value-behavior alignment.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The compression of 95% of total deviation growth into the first 10 steps indicates that intervention windows for disrupting habitual consumption are extraordinarily narrow; policy mechanisms targeting habit reformation must activate concurrently with or immediately following environmental awareness campaigns, as the value-action gap solidifies rapidly and becomes resistant to reduction after step 10. Furthermore, the persistent high plateau suggests that market transitions alone do not eliminate consumer-environment misalignment—complementary interventions addressing cognitive dissonance and justification behaviors remain necessary even after aggregate consumption patterns shift, as the deviation metric reveals lingering psychological tension in the consumer base.
