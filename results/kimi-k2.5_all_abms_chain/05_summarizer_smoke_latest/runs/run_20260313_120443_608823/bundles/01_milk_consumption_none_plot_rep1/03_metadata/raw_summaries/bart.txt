 This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005 . It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . The primary objective is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence interact within individual decision-making processes regarding milk choice .  The mean weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk initiates at approximately zero at step 0 . At the conclusion of the simulation at step 32, the cumulative total reaches approximately 32,000 choices . The trajectory demonstrates a sustained transition from whole milk to alternative varieties across the simulated population .  The mean habit factor for whole milk exhibits a consistent pattern of increase across all simulation runs, beginning at a baseline value of 1.00 at step 0.00 . Between step 1 and step 32, the factor increases from roughly 1.02 to terminal values ranging between approximately 1.044 and 1.055 across the ensemble of simulations .  The mean deviation between agents' held environmental values and the implied values of their milk choices exhibits consistent dynamics across simulation replications . The metric initiates at zero at Step 0, indicating initial alignment between values and choices . During Steps 1 through 3, the deviation increases rapidly, reaching values between 10 and 12 units . Growth rate decelerates markedly between Steps 3 and 10, with the metric rising to approximately 13 to 13.5 units .
