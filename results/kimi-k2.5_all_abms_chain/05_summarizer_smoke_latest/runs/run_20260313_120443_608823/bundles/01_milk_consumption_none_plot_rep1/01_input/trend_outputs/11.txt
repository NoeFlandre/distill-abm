The mean value health deviation, quantifying the average discrepancy between agents' held health values and the health characteristics implied by their actual milk choices, follows a distinct three-phase trajectory across the 32-step simulation representing the 1974-2005 period.
At initialization (step 0), the deviation registers at zero, indicating complete alignment between agents' health perceptions and their consumption behaviors when whole milk dominates the market. Over the first ten steps, the metric increases rapidly and monotonically, reaching values between approximately 0.50 and 0.52. This represents the steepest growth phase in the simulation.
During the subsequent fifteen steps (steps 10-25), the growth rate diminishes substantially. The deviation increases gradually from roughly 0.55 to approximately 0.58-0.62, exhibiting a plateau-like behavior with reduced net change compared to the initial phase. 
In the final seven steps (steps 25-32), the metric resumes an accelerated upward trajectory, climbing to terminal values ranging between 0.70 and 0.74. Across multiple simulation runs, trajectories remain tightly clustered with minimal variation throughout the first 25 steps. In the final segment, modest divergence emerges between runs, with the spread between minimum and maximum values expanding to approximately 0.04-0.05 units by step 32.

In the context of the milk consumption transition model, the monotonic increase from 0 to 0.70-0.74 indicates a progressive decoupling of agents' health beliefs from their actual consumption behaviors over the historical transition period. The rapid early growth (steps 0-10) reflects the initial information diffusion phase where agents receive signals about the superior health characteristics of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk varieties while maintaining consumption patterns dominated by whole milk due to strong incumbent habits (parameterized at 10.0) and social blindness effects (0.51).
The intermediate plateau phase (steps 10-25) suggests a transitional period where behavioral change begins partially catching up with shifting perceptions, as disposition mechanisms trigger consideration of alternatives and some agents overcome habit thresholds to adopt lower-fat varieties, thereby moderating the rate of divergence growth.
The renewed acceleration in the final phase (steps 25-32) indicates that by the later stages of the simulation timeframe, persistent gaps remain between internal health valuations and consumption choices. The terminal deviation levels of approximately 0.70-0.74 suggest that even by 2005, the alignment between health perceptions and actual consumption behaviors remains incomplete, with a substantial proportion of the population maintaining consumption patterns that diverge from their updated health beliefs. The increasing variability across simulation runs in the final steps reflects sensitivity to stochastic network interactions and individual threshold crossings during the late-stage transition dynamics.
