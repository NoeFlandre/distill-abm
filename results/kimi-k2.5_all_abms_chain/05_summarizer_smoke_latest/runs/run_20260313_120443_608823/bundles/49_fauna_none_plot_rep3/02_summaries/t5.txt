The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 during the simulation period from step 0 through approximately step 2200. The total energy available to all forager bands exhibits a consistent downward trajectory throughout the simulation runs. By step 1000, total energy reaches approximately -12,000 units. The final plateau values represent approximately 43 to 50 percent of the initial prey population of 250 agents. The cumulative mortality of postpartum females commences at zero and undergoes a phase of rapid accumulation during the initial 300 to 600 steps. In most runs, the count increases steeply during this period. Cumulative death counts increase from roughly 200 to between 220 and 245. A few simulations reach stabilization earlier, around step 800 to 1000. Some simulations continue accumulating deaths until approximately step 1500 or beyond. By step 400, nearly all replicates had declined to fewer than 10 individuals. A small subset of simulation replicates exhibited prolonged persistence. By step 500, most trajectories have fallen below 2,000 units, with several having reached zero. By 500 steps, most simulations have reached energy levels below 2,000. Measurable prey energy remains in only a few replicates, generally below 1,000. One replicate exhibits exceptional persistence, maintaining low but detectable energy levels until approximately 2,000-2,200 steps.
