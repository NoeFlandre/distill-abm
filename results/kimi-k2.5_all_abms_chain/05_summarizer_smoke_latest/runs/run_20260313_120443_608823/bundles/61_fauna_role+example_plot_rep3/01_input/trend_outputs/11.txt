Following this initial crash, the decline continues at a reduced but steady rate through approximately step 1,000, though with considerable heterogeneity across stochastic runs. These transient plateaus likely reflect temporary reductions in hunting efficiency or brief episodes of favorable resource availability during seasonal pulses, allowing male animals to momentarily maintain or slightly recover their energetic state.

By step 1,500, the vast majority of simulation runs converge toward near-zero total energy values, indicating the functional extirpation of the male subpopulation or reduction to individual animals below starvation thresholds. However, certain runs maintain low but persistent energy levels—ranging from 100 to 500 units—until approximately step 2,200, suggesting rare instances where male animals evade detection or access sufficient vegetation patches to prolong survival despite sustained hunting pressure.

The variance across replicate simulations remains low during the initial 200 steps, indicating consistent initial conditions and predictable early-phase hunting dynamics. Variability increases substantially between steps 300 and 800, where trajectories diverge significantly, with some runs declining to near-zero within 600 steps while others retain 3,000 to 5,000 units. This divergence likely stems from stochastic differences in hunter movement patterns, herd aggregation behaviors, and the spatial distribution of remaining vegetation patches. After step 1,000, variance decreases again as most runs approach the zero boundary condition.

The pronounced variability observed during the middle phase emerges from stochastic interactions between hunter search patterns, herd movement dynamics, and seasonal fluctuations in grass availability. The eventual convergence to zero across all replicates indicates that with immortal foragers capable of energy storage, continued hunting pressure systematically eliminates the male subpopulation regardless of initial stochastic differences, though the timing of final collapse varies by as much as 1,500 steps between the fastest and slowest extinction trajectories.
