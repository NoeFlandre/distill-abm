The cumulative count of female prey killed by forager bands begins at zero and follows a sigmoid-like trajectory characterized by rapid initial accumulation, decelerating growth, eventual plateauing near maximum exploitation levels, and terminal oscillations. 

During the initial phase from tick 0 to approximately tick 245, the cumulative mortality exhibits rapid acceleration, transitioning from zero to steeply increasing values as the initial forager population of 20 hunters encounters the naive prey population of 250 ungulates. This inflection around tick 245 marks the transition from accelerating to decelerating accumulation as the hunting pressure begins depleting the available female prey.

From tick 245 through tick 1826, the cumulative count continues rising but at progressively diminishing rates, reflecting the combined effects of prey depletion, potential behavioral avoidance by herds, and the constraints imposed by strong environmental seasonality (0.8 intensity) and limited vegetation regrowth (3 ticks). This prolonged phase of decelerating growth includes temporary variations in velocity, with sub-periods of slight re-acceleration and slowdown, eventually reaching the maximum cumulative value of approximately 123.67 kills around tick 1826.

Following this peak, the trajectory enters a plateau and adjustment phase extending to the simulation terminus at tick 2312. The cumulative count exhibits a slight decline from the maximum to a final value of 123.0, accompanied by pronounced oscillatory fluctuations characterized by a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks. These oscillations cluster particularly between ticks 2170 and 2265, suggesting cyclical patterns in hunting success likely driven by the interaction of seasonal resource availability, ungulate reproductive biology (birth-spacing of 730 ticks), and the food storage capabilities of foragers enabling pulsed exploitation.

Interpreted within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, this trajectory indicates near-complete exploitation of the initial female cohort—approximately 123 of the roughly 125 females present in the starting population of 250 ungulates. The terminal oscillations and plateau suggest that after the initial demographic collapse, the system entered a stochastic regime where occasional successful hunts of the few remaining females or new recruits occurred against a background of failed encounters, creating the observed 63-tick cyclicality in cumulative mortality accumulation.
