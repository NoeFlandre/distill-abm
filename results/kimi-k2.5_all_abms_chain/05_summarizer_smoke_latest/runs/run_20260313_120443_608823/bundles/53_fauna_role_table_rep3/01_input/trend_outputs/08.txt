The male megaherbivore population initiated at 125 individuals and followed a deterministic trajectory toward extinction, reaching zero abundance by tick 2284 (approximately 6.25 years into the simulation). The demographic collapse unfolded through three distinct phases characterized by differing rates of decline and stability properties.

Phase 1 (ticks 0–875) comprised a rapid, monotonic decline with steep negative slopes (ranging from -0.244 to -0.029 per tick in successive windows). During this initial 2.4-year period, the population crashed through serial thresholds marked by change points at ticks 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 875, indicating stepwise degradation rather than smooth exponential decay. By tick 875, the population had diminished to low single-digit abundance, reflecting intense hunting pressure targeting males with higher energetic returns (food value 30 versus 27 for females) despite their elevated processing costs.

Phase 2 (ticks ~900–2100) established a volatile low-density regime characterized by oscillatory dynamics with a dominant periodicity of 63 ticks (approximately bimonthly cycles). Within this phase, the trajectory exhibited numerous inflection points concentrated between ticks 1262 and 2167, indicating sustained demographic instability. A temporary recovery occurred between ticks 1392 and 1623 (years 3.8–4.4), during which the declining trend reversed to a weak positive slope (0.001 per tick), briefly elevating abundance from near-extinction levels. However, this respite proved temporary; the population subsequently entered windows of no significant trend (ticks 1624–2087), fluctuating at densities where the median abundance (1.35 individuals) remained far below the mean (21.03), indicating a long-tailed distribution skewed toward quasi-extinction states.

Phase 3 (ticks ~2100–2284) represented the terminal collapse. Following change point detection at tick 2310, the final males expired by tick 2284, marking functional extinction of the male cohort. The oscillatory signatures (detected at indices spanning 45–140 ticks) persisted until near-extinction, suggesting that seasonal environmental variation (seasonality parameter 0.8) continued to modulate population dynamics even at critically low densities.

The trajectory reveals the consequences of asymmetric hunting pressure under conditions of immortal predators with food-storage capability. The initial preferential targeting of high-value males drove rapid population masculinization (loss of males), while the 730-tick (2-year) interbirth interval prevented demographic recovery during the temporary reprieve in mid-simulation. The 63-tick oscillatory pattern likely reflects seasonal resource pulses interacting with the remaining males' energetic requirements (threshold 25 units) and movement costs, creating periodic windows of vulnerability to the persistent foraging radius (6 patches) of human agents.
