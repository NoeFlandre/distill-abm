Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change. Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances. Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores. Foragers-movement-cost ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1.0, and distance hunters can perceive and pursue prey is determined by foraging-radius. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remained constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation duration of 2312 ticks. The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals killed by forager bands exhibits a trajectory characterized by rapid early accumulation, followed by deceleration, plateauing, and terminal fluctuation. From tick 0 to approximately tick 245, the cumulative mortality exhibits rapid acceleration, transitioning from zero to steeply increasing values as the initial forager population of 20 hunters encounters the naive prey population of 250 ungulates. The cumulative mortality trajectory of the megaherbivore population exhibits a saturating increase over the observed 2,312 time steps (approximately 6.3 years), approaching an asymptote near 235–240 total deaths. Despite initial reproductive success, the combined pressures of human hunting pressure, seasonal resource bottlenecks, and density-dependent vegetation dynamics (regrowth time of 3 ticks) eventually suppress the population's replacement rate. The male megaherbivore population initiated at 125 individuals and followed a deterministic trajectory toward extinction. The demographic collapse unfolded through three distinct phases characterized by differing rates of decline and stability properties. The trajectory reveals the consequences of asymmetric hunting pressure under conditions of immortal predators with food-storage capability. The megaherbivore population undergoes deterministic collapse from an initial mean abundance of 167 individuals to complete extinction within 2,312 time steps (approximately 6.3 years) The demographic trajectory follows a non-linear decline characterized by distinct phases of rapid attrition, decelerating mortality, and persistent seasonal oscillations superimposed upon an overarching downward trend. The population exhibited extreme variance (standard deviation of 31.6 around a mean of 16.5), indicating high volatility. The simulation begins with a total prey energy of approximately 16,667 units, representing the initial energy reserves of the megaherbivore population across 250 individuals. From tick 875 onward, the system enters a prolonged terminal phase characterized by progressively lower energetic baselines. The population reaches energetic extinction (0 units) at tick 2,284, approximately 6.3 years into the simulation. The system demonstrates robust resilience, maintaining vegetation coverage within a narrow band (24.9% to 33.0%) despite strong environmental forcing (seasonality 0.8) and herbivore population pressure. By tick 2,311, the total prey energy declines to zero, indicating complete population extinction.
