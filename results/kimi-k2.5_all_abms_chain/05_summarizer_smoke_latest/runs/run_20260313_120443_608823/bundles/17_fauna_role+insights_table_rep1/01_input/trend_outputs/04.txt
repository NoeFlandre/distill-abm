The cumulative female mortality metric exhibits a four-phase trajectory over the approximately 6.3-year observation window (2,312 ticks). Initiating at zero, the series demonstrates immediate and rapid accumulation, reaching an inflection point at tick 245 where the rate of increase begins to decouple from initial exponential growth. This acceleration continues through multiple regime shifts detected at ticks 125, 375, 475, 625, and 850, with the steepest ascension occurring between ticks 0 and 625 (Mann-Kendall slope = 0.213 to 0.023 per tick), reflecting intense initial harvesting pressure on the female ungulate segment.

Between ticks 625 and 1,600, the accumulation rate decelerates markedly (slope declining to 0.002 by window 1160–1391), approaching an asymptotic plateau around 119–120 individuals. This saturation suggests the interaction between hunter energetic optimization—which favors females due to lower processing costs (3 units versus 6 for males) despite marginally lower caloric returns—and the underlying population dynamics of the 250 initial ungulates. Notably, the metric stabilizes near 119.5 from tick 1,335 through 1,516, indicating a temporary equilibrium where new kills balance against the depletion of accessible females.

A secondary pulse emerges between ticks 1,624 and 1,855, where the accumulation rate reaccelerates to 0.007 per tick, peaking at 123.7 individuals at tick 1,826 (approximately year 5). This resurgence potentially reflects seasonal cycles (dominant oscillation period: 63 ticks, roughly bi-monthly) affecting herd vulnerability or vegetation-driven congregation patterns in the high-seasonality environment (seasonality parameter 0.8).

In the terminal phase (post-tick 1,856), the trend inverts significantly (Mann-Kendall tau = -0.453, p < 0.001), with values declining from the maximum of 123.7 to 123.0 by termination at tick 2,312. For a cumulative mortality metric, this reduction implies either the exhaustion of the female subpopulation—preventing further accumulation across the 40 replicated simulations—or selective censoring of high-mortality simulation runs.

Variability across the ensemble remains substantial throughout (standard deviation: 31.1; range: 0–123.7), with the median final cumulative kill (119.1) exceeding the temporal mean (104.6), indicating right-skewed distributions dominated by early-phase low-mortality outcomes but converging toward high-mortality equilibria.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The rapid approach to saturation within five years suggests that female megaherbivore populations face acute vulnerability to sustained foraging pressure, particularly when hunters optimize for processing efficiency. The 63-tick cyclic variation superimposed on the trend indicates predictable seasonal windows of heightened mortality risk that could inform temporal conservation restrictions. Finally, the asymptotic behavior near 120 cumulative deaths—roughly half the initial population—represents a critical threshold beyond which female demographic collapse may occur, signaling the need for intervention before year five to prevent Allee effects in this sexually dimorphic species.
