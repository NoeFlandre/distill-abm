Mean green biomass initiates at 45,000 units and exhibits an immediate brief elevation to approximately 45,261 by step 4, followed by a pronounced decline reaching a minimum value of roughly 42,057 at step 27. This initial reduction reflects the immediate impact of livestock grazing as households deploy their initial herds across private pasture patches, temporarily outpacing vegetation regrowth capacity. The consistency across simulation runs during this phase indicates predictable transient depletion regardless of specific stochastic variations in household decision-making.

Between steps 27 and 33, the metric stabilizes at minimal levels, exhibiting no significant trend as the system transitions from net depletion to net accumulation. Subsequently, from step 33 through step 49, biomass enters a significant growth phase, followed by rapid acceleration between steps 49 and 60 with growth rates exceeding 950 units per step. During this recovery, the combined effects of vegetation dynamics, learning-by-doing processes, and social learning among the heterogeneous agent population enable more sustainable grazing pressure management on private plots. After step 60, the growth rate progressively decelerates while remaining positive, as the system approaches a new equilibrium. By step 100, mean green biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units and stabilizes, representing a 34% increase above initial conditions.

The divergence across simulation runs becomes pronounced during the recovery phase, particularly after step 40, suggesting that while initial grazing impacts are deterministic, long-term outcomes depend heavily on specific learning trajectories and risk-attitude adaptations within the household population. The final values range across individual simulation runs, with most stabilizing between approximately 56,000 and 64,000 units, indicating variability in the ultimate productivity achieved under different stochastic realizations of the learning and decision-making processes.

**Insights for Decision Makers:**

These results suggest that private grazing systems implementing learning-based management should anticipate an initial 25-30 step period of biomass depletion during system establishment, followed by recovery and long-term enhancement of pasture productivity. The substantial final biomass levels indicate that adaptive learning behaviors can overcome initial overgrazing tendencies and improve ecological outcomes compared to baseline conditions. However, the increasing variance across runs during recovery suggests that outcomes depend critically on supporting effective learning processes among heterogeneous households. Policymakers should consider transitional support mechanisms during the initial depletion phase and invest in facilitating social learning and learning-by-doing capabilities to achieve the higher equilibrium biomass levels observed in successful simulation trajectories.
