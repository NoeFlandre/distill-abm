 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction . It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges . The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics .  The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands maintains a constant value of 20 throughout the simulation sequence, from step 0 through approximately step 2200 . Hunters are immortal (preventing mortality from energy depletion), do not reproduce, and therefore maintain the initial population size of 20 bands indefinitely without response to environmental conditions or prey availability .  The cumulative number of postpartum female prey killed begins at zero and increases rapidly during the initial phase of the simulation, rising steeply within the first 500 time steps to reach values between 100 and 120 individuals . By steps 1000–1500, the cumulative count stabilizes in most simulations, approaching asymptotic values ranging from 115 to 125 by the time the rapid growth phase concludes .  The cumulative number of births exhibits an initial dormant phase lasting approximately 700 steps, during which no reproductive events occur in any simulation run . This quiescent period is followed by a synchronized onset of births around step 730 . Subsequently, the cumulative birth counts increase in a step-wise manner, reflecting discrete reproductive intervals consistent with the specified birth-spacing parameter of 730 ticks .  The rapid collapse aligns with hunting pressure parameters where males possess higher nutritional value (30 units) than females (27 units) The processing cost differential (6 for males versus 3 for females) appears insufficient to offset the food value advantage, resulting in male-biased mortality . Conservation strategies must account for hunter preference structures where higher-value individuals face disproportionate mortality risk .  The results demonstrate that sustained hunting pressure from a fixed number of efficient foragers (who do not die and can store food) reliably drives megaherbivore populations to extinction . For conservation or management applications, this suggests that even moderate numbers of persistent hunters can be sufficient to extirpate prey populations over timescales ranging from short-term collapse to prolonged decline .  The rapid initial decline suggests high vulnerability to early depletion pressures, while the variability in persistence times indicates that stochastic factors significantly influence the exact timing of population collapse, complicating prediction of extinction timing . Management interventions would need to account for event-driven mortality processes rather than continuous population flows .  The combination of fast grass regrowth (3 ticks), moderate hunting pressure (20 foragers), and herbivore life history constraints creates a sustainable equilibrium that prevents long-term overgrazing while supporting stable prey populations . The equilibrium is the initial transient phase .
