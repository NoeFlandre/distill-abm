This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The simulation integrates theoretical frameworks with empirical data, employing calibration against observed macro-level consumption patterns to validate outcomes. The simulation reveals a three-phase adoption trajectory for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption. The persistence of low-level whole milk consumption through the final time steps implies that complete market transition may require extended periods even after majority adoption of alternatives has occurred. The study examined milk consumption from 1974 to 2005. The number of milk choices accumulated linearly throughout the simulation period. Each of the 1,000 agents made precisely one milk choice during each of the 32 time steps, with uniform participation across the entire agent population throughout all decision cycles. Simulation of milk consumption adoption from 1974 to 2005 reveals a three-phase trajectory in the mean weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per person. The simulation shows rapid adoption of low-fat milk in 1974, but plateau phases indicate the presence of resistant subpopulations where habit strength dominates decision-making. The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits a trajectory marked by initial stability followed by sustained, gradually decelerating growth across the 33-step simulation period representing 1974 to 2005. Public health strategies should prioritize disrupting habitual consumption patterns during the initial simulation steps before the habit factor accumulates substantial momentum. The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the health values implied by their milk choices exhibits a monotonic increase from 0.0000 at the initial time step to 0.7251 at the final time step (step 32) This metric demonstrates a three-phase trajectory characterized by varying rates of divergence between stated health preferences and actual consumption behaviors. For decision makers, these findings imply that interventions targeting dietary transitions face distinct strategic windows. The S-curve trajectory between milk consumption and environmental values exhibits a characteristic S-Curve trajectory over the 33-step simulation period. The overall series maintains a mean of 12.6015 and median of 13.4302 across all steps. After step 10, the series exhibits no statistically significant trend, suggesting the system has reached equilibrium.
