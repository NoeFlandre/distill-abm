The count of households engaged in social learning begins at zero across all simulations. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first ten time steps, the number of households with social learning increases rapidly, with most runs reaching between 6 and 30 households by this point. This rapid early diffusion suggests that social learning processes activate quickly once the simulation begins, likely driven by initial observations of neighbor performance and the updating of uncertainty management parameters among households with social learning capabilities.

Between time steps 10 and 40, the adoption of social learning continues to spread but at varying rates across different simulation runs. During this period, trajectories diverge substantially: some simulations show continued steep increases reaching approximately 48 to 54 households, while others plateau earlier at levels between 12 and 30 households. The step-like increments observed during this phase indicate discrete adoption events rather than gradual continuous uptake, consistent with the social learning mechanism where households update their behavioral strategies based on observed neighbor performance within the knowledge radius.

By approximately time step 40, most simulations reach a stable equilibrium where the count of households with social learning remains constant through the end of the simulation at step 100. Final values range from approximately 18 households at the lower extreme to 54 households at the upper extreme, representing between 30% and 90% of the total household population. This wide variance across runs suggests that stochastic elements in rainfall patterns, initial household configurations, and neighborhood interaction dynamics create divergent pathways for social learning diffusion, with some populations achieving near-universal adoption while others remain at low levels.

The high variability in final adoption rates indicates that social learning does not necessarily spread to the entire population, even over extended time periods. In some runs, the process stagnates with less than one-third of households engaging in social learning, while in others, nearly all households adopt these practices. This pattern implies that the diffusion of adaptive management strategies in private property grazing systems depends heavily on specific historical and ecological trajectories.

For decision makers, these trends suggest that relying solely on spontaneous social learning diffusion may result in inconsistent adoption rates across communities. Interventions aimed at facilitating information exchange or reducing uncertainty in the early phases (first 40 steps) could be critical for achieving broader uptake of adaptive management practices. The persistence of low-adoption equilibria in some simulations indicates that without external support, certain households may remain isolated from social learning networks, potentially limiting the overall resilience and adaptive capacity of the grazing system.
