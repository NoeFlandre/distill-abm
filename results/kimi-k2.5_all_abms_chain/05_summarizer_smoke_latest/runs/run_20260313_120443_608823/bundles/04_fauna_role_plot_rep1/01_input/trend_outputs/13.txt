Across the 20 replicate simulations, the aggregate prey energy initializes at approximately 25,000 units, consistent with the starting population of 250 ungulates each holding roughly 100 energy units. The system exhibits deterministic collapse dynamics characterized by a rapid decay phase followed by variable persistence times.

Within the first 400 time steps, the ensemble undergoes a precipitous decline, with most realizations losing 75-90% of initial energy and falling below 5,000 units by step 400-500. Some simulations maintain slightly elevated totals longer, with one outlier retaining approximately 22,000 units until step 300 before joining the rapid decline. The decay rate during this phase substantially exceeds metabolic expenditure rates, indicating hunting mortality as the primary driver of energy depletion.

Approximately 20-30% of runs exhibit non-monotonic dynamics between steps 400-800, displaying temporary stabilization or secondary oscillations in the 2,000-8,000 unit range. These transient states persist for 100-400 steps before resuming decline, likely reflecting demographic stochasticity, spatial resource heterogeneity, or temporary reductions in hunting efficiency due to prey scarcity.

By step 1,000, approximately 80% of simulations have depleted prey energy to minimal levels (below 1,000 units). The remaining replicates demonstrate extended persistence tails, maintaining low-energy populations (500-2,000 units) until steps 1,400-1,600. One realization sustains measurable energy until approximately step 2,100 before terminal depletion.

Statistically, the median time to reach 1,000 units occurs between steps 500-700, with substantial inter-simulation variance spanning step 300 to step 900. The coefficient of variation increases with time, indicating divergent stochastic paths as small population sizes amplify demographic noise.

The consistent asymptotic outcome—complete exhaustion of prey energy across all replicates—suggests the parameter configuration creates an unsustainable predation sink. With 20 immortal hunters operating under optimal foraging theory, prey food values of 27-30 units, processing costs of 3-6 units, and biennial reproduction (birth-spacing of 730 steps), the per-step harvest rate exceeds the population's maximum sustainable yield. High environmental seasonality (0.8) likely creates energetic bottlenecks that prevent recovery during seasonal low-productivity periods, despite rapid vegetation regrowth (3-step regeneration) and moderate initial grass cover (33% of landscape). The combination of immortal hunters with food storage capabilities and high encounter rates (foraging radius of 6 patches) generates continuous predation pressure that the ungulate life history strategy cannot withstand.
