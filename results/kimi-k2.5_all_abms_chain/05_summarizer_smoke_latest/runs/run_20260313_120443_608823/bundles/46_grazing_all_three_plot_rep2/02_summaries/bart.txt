The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors. Every trajectory begins at its respective initial value at step 0 and maintains that exact value through step 100 without deviation. This temporal consistency reflects the inherent nature of initial risk attitude as a static parameter fixed at the start of each simulation. Within the first 20 time steps, households undergo rapid divergence as learning mechanisms activate, with values diverging sharply across population. The mean green biomass across the landscape begins at approximately 45,000 units. During the initial five time steps, it increases modestly to roughly 45,500 units. Between steps 5 and 40, the biomass enters a pronounced decline phase, decreasing steadily. The trough around years 30-40 represents a critical degradation threshold where vegetation condition reaches its lowest point before feedback mechanisms prompt adjusted stocking decisions. The mean number of livestock destocked per household initiates at effectively zero, surging rapidly to peak values between approximately 6 and 7.5 animals during the first 5 to 10 years. This initial spike reflects the pressure on households beginning with 90 animals to rapidly reduce herds when faced with insufficient pasture resources under highly variable rainfall conditions. After year 40, while the overall range narrows, the separation between high and low stable equilibria persists. The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across households begins at zero and undergoes a rapid increase during the first approximately 20 time steps, reaching values predominantly between 50 and 80 animals. Some households continue destocking at steady or accelerating rates throughout the 100-year period, with cumulative totals reaching 130 to 173 animals by the final time step. This variability implies that promoting exclusive reliance on traditional non-adaptive grazing practices may lead to unstable community structures. Interventions supporting adaptive learning mechanisms may be necessary to ensure more predictable household persistence in variable rangeland environments, such as rangelands.
