:The mean number of livestock destocked per household initiates at effectively zero, surging rapidly to peak values between approximately 6 and 7.5 animals during the first 5 to 10 years. This initial spike reflects the pressure on households beginning with 90 animals to rapidly reduce herds when faced with insufficient pasture resources under highly variable rainfall conditions (mean of 200 units with standard deviation of 100).

Following this early maximum, destocking rates decline steeply from year 10 through approximately year 30, with mean values dropping from above 6 to below 2 animals per household. By year 20, most trajectories have fallen below 2 animals, and by year 30, the majority range between 0 and 1.5 animals. This decline indicates that the learning mechanisms—whether experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning with single-loop adaptation—enable farmers to progressively calibrate stocking decisions based on observed pasture conditions and information gathered within the knowledge radius of 1 patch.

From roughly year 40 through year 100, destocking rates stabilize at low baseline levels, with most simulation runs settling between 0 and 1 animal destocked per household, though several trajectories maintain slightly higher stable rates approaching 1.2 animals. The persistence of minor destocking throughout this period suggests that even after adaptation, significant inter-annual rainfall variability continues to necessitate occasional herd reductions when precipitation inadequately supports vegetation recovery at the specified growth rates.

Substantial heterogeneity across simulation runs characterizes the first 20 years, with particular divergence between years 10 and 30 where different trajectories separate into distinct clusters. After year 40, while the overall range narrows, the separation between high and low stable equilibria persists, indicating that the specific learning strategy employed or the particular sequence of rainfall events produces durable differences in long-term vulnerability to resource constraints.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that initial destocking pressures are severe but temporary, with stabilization typically achieved within 30 to 40 years as learning improves stocking decisions. However, the variation in final destocking levels suggests that the choice of learning approach significantly affects long-term resilience, and the persistence of baseline destocking even after adaptation highlights the continued risk posed by high climate variability, necessitating flexible management strategies that maintain the ability to adjust herd sizes in response to annual conditions.
