The average total livestock inventory, inclusive of destocked animals, commences at 90 animals per household at step 0, consistent with the initial stocking parameter. A rapid decline occurs over the first 10 steps, falling to approximately 60 animals, followed by a continued gradual descent reaching a minimum of roughly 40-42 animals around step 40. This initial reduction reflects the system's correction from initial overstocking toward levels aligned with pasture carrying capacity under variable rainfall conditions.

From step 40 onward, simulation trajectories diverge markedly. Approximately one-third of runs remain stable at 40-45 animals through step 100, while others exhibit recovery trajectories. The most successful simulations rise steadily between steps 40 and 70, reaching approximately 58-62 animals by step 100, with intermediate trajectories stabilizing between 45 and 55 animals.

Variance across simulation runs remains minimal during the initial decline (steps 0-35) but increases substantially after step 40, indicating that stochastic rainfall patterns and vegetation responses during the early adaptation period create path-dependent outcomes. This divergence suggests the existence of multiple stable equilibria, where favorable early environmental conditions combined with effective learning enable some systems to rebuild toward initial stocking levels, while adverse early conditions or less effective adaptation lead to persistent low-level equilibria.

For decision makers, the wide range of final outcomes—spanning from 40 to 62 animals—demonstrates that early adaptation responses are critical determinants of long-term livelihood security. The recovery observed in higher trajectories indicates that sustainable management is achievable, but the substantial proportion of runs remaining at depressed levels highlights the risk of long-term poverty traps when households fail to adapt effectively to initial environmental shocks.
