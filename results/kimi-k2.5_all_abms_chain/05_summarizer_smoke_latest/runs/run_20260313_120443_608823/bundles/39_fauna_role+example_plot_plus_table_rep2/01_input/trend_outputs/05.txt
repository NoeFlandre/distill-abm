The cumulative count of postpartum females killed initiates at zero and undergoes an initial phase of rapid accumulation, reaching approximately half of the terminal maximum within the first 475 ticks. During this early interval, specifically between ticks 245 and 285, the rate of increase peaks with slope coefficients exceeding 0.21, reflecting efficient foraging by the initial cohort of 20 human agents on nursing females when prey densities are highest and landscape vegetation is at initial carrying capacity.

Between ticks 475 and 850, the accumulation rate decelerates markedly through sequential structural transitions at ticks 625 and 850. Trend slopes diminish progressively from 0.059 to 0.015 and ultimately to 0.003 by tick 1159, indicating declining encounter rates as the pool of available postpartum females depletes and foragers face increased energetic costs from movement and prey processing. Inflection points at ticks 1180, 1311, and subsequent intervals mark the transition from convex to concave growth as the system approaches saturation limits imposed by the initial population bottleneck of 250 ungulates and the reproductive constraints of a 730-tick interbirth interval.

From approximately tick 1400 to 1826, the cumulative count stabilizes near its asymptotic maximum of 119 individuals. During this plateau phase, specifically between ticks 1392 and 1623, directional trends become statistically indistinguishable from zero, suggesting that additional kills effectively cease as postpartum female availability drops below profitable foraging thresholds. Across 40 simulation runs, terminal values converge tightly around a median of 116.86, though individual runs exhibit substantial variation in the timing of saturation—some plateauing as early as tick 500 while others continue accumulating kills until nearly tick 2000. This variance reflects stochastic effects in herd movement dynamics, grass regeneration rates (set at 3 ticks recovery time), and seasonal resource pulsing (seasonality parameter 0.8).

After tick 1856, the aggregate mean exhibits a slight but statistically significant declining trend, decreasing from the maximum of 119 to 117 by the simulation conclusion at tick 2312. This late-stage reduction, occurring despite the monotonic nature of cumulative counts within individual runs, results from asynchronous termination times across replicate simulations. Minor oscillations detected in the 2170–2265 tick range, with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks (roughly bi-monthly given the 365-ticks-per-year calibration), likely reflect residual seasonal effects on birth scheduling and vegetation availability, though these fluctuations remain constrained within a narrow band of 117–119 individuals.

The overall trajectory describes a resource depletion curve characterized by an initial explosive harvest followed by progressive saturation. The time-averaged mean of 102.44 individuals reflects the prolonged accumulation phase, while the convergence toward 117–119 terminal kills suggests that the hunting pressure effectively removes the entire accessible cohort of postpartum females from the initial population, at which point this demographic becomes functionally unavailable to foragers due to extirpation or retreat to inaccessible regions of the landscape.
