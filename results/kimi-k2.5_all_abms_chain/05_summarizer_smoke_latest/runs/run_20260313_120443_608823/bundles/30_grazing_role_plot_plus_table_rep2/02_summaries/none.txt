**Context and Goals**

This agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Unlike the original RAGE model which examined common property scenarios where herders move livestock across shared rangelands, this extension focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods.

The simulation tracks the interaction between vegetation condition and household decisions about livestock numbers over time. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors.

The central purpose is understanding how these different ways of adapting to changing conditions influence pasture health, herd sizes, and the persistence of households. The model particularly examines how farmers handle uncertainty and incomplete information when making stocking decisions, represented through individual risk dispositions that affect how they perceive and react to environmental feedback.

**Key Parameters**

*Population and Spatial Setup:*
The **number-households** parameter establishes how many farming families occupy the landscape, which can range from 1 to 100 families, with this study using 60 households. Each household begins with **livestock-init** set at 90 animals. The simulation runs for **timesteps** set at 100 years. Farmers begin the simulation positioned on their designated land parcels, with **start-on-homepatch?** set to True. Each farmer can observe conditions within a **knowledge-radius** that ranges from 0 to 5 patches, set here at 1 patch distance.

*Behavioral Type Configuration:*
The study examines three specific learning strategies with equal representation: **number-E-RO** (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb), **number-E-LBD** (learning-by-doing), and **number-E-RO-SL1** (social learning with single-loop adaptation), each set to 20 households. The **homog-behav-types?** parameter set to True ensures each type is evaluated in uniform groups. The original RAGE model behavioral types are inactive in this extension: **number-SAT**, **number-TRAD**, **number-MAX**, and **number-random** are all set to 0. The default reference **behavioral-type** is set to E-RO.

*Grazing Management Practices:*
Formal pasture resting is disabled with **resting?** set to False. The threshold for initiating rest periods, **resting-threshold**, ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0.1. The **intrinsic-preference** for livestock relative to other values ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0. Similarly, **social-susceptibility** to neighbor influence ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0, indicating decisions rely on other factors.

*Original Model Parameters:*
Several parameters from the foundational RAGE model are retained. The **satisficing-threshold** for satisfied decision-making ranges from 0 to 100 and is set at 0. The **satisficing-trials** parameter, ranging from 0 to 100, is set at 21. These control how the original satisficer type would search for acceptable solutions, though this type is not active in the current setup.

*Ecological Conditions:*
Rainfall is applied uniformly across the landscape with **global-rain?** set to True. The average rainfall **rain-mean** is set at 200 units annually. Year-to-year variation in precipitation is controlled by **rain-std** set at 100, indicating significant variability in annual rainfall totals. Vegetation growth responds through two rates: **gr1** set at 0.5 and **gr2** set at 0.1. The parameter **w** from the original vegetation model is set at 0.8, influencing how pastures recover from grazing. Livestock reproduction is not active in this extension, with **b** set to 0.

*Learning and Risk Mechanisms:*
The extension module is activated with **extension-model?** set to True. Uncertainty handling is engaged with **risk-mode?** set to True. Each agent begins with an initial risk disposition **risk-att-init** set at 0.5, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance. The ability to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False, confining agents to single-loop learning within their assigned type.

The mean household initial risk attitude remains constant at approximately 0.0074 throughout the entire 100-step simulation period. This metric shows no temporal variation, maintaining identical values from the initial time step through to the conclusion of the simulation. Across 30 repeated simulation runs, the aggregate mean exhibits no fluctuation, with zero variance and no detectable trends in any temporal window.

This static pattern indicates that the initial risk attitude functions as a fixed attribute assigned to social learning agents at the simulation's outset, representing their baseline disposition to deviate from recommended livestock numbers. Unlike dynamic variables that might adjust based on pasture conditions, rainfall variability, or accumulated experience, this parameter serves as an unchanging reference point that persists despite the ecological and decision-making dynamics occurring within the model.

The aggregate mean household risk attitude, representing the average deviation from recommended livestock numbers across all simulation runs, initiates at nearly neutral levels (0.0074) at timestep 0. Over the first third of the simulation (approximately timesteps 0–33), the metric undergoes a sharp, statistically significant escalation (Mann-Kendall slope=0.016–0.018, p<0.01), culminating in a maximum deviation of 0.3814 at timestep 33. This upward trajectory indicates an initial period where households increasingly diverge from optimal stocking recommendations as they apply their distinct learning strategies—baseline experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—under conditions of high rainfall variability (rain-std=100).

Subsequently, from approximately timesteps 33 to 65, the system exhibits a pronounced and statistically significant decreasing trend (slope=-0.004 to -0.010, p<0.001), with the mean risk attitude declining from its peak toward values ranging between 0.19–0.27. This phase reflects an adaptive response where accumulated experience, ecological feedback from private pasture conditions, and observations within the knowledge radius (1 patch) prompt households to moderate their initial deviations from recommended livestock levels.

During the final third of the simulation (timesteps 66–100), the metric stabilizes, reaching an end value of 0.1911. Statistical tests during this period reveal no significant directional trend (p>0.05), indicating the system has reached a steady-state equilibrium. At this point, households maintain a persistent but moderate deviation from recommended stocking rates, suggesting that the interplay of individual risk dispositions (initially set at 0.5), single-loop learning mechanisms, and environmental uncertainty results in a long-term equilibrium that systematically differs from theoretical optima by approximately 0.19 units.

The temporal dynamics are further characterized by oscillatory patterns with a dominant periodicity of 49 timesteps, and notable inflection points at timesteps 12–13, 39, 46, and 51 mark transitions between acceleration and deceleration phases. The aggregate variance (0.0088) and standard deviation (0.0936) across the 30 simulation series indicate consistent heterogeneity in household risk attitudes throughout the temporal progression, reflecting the divergent adaptation pathways of the three behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, E-RO-SL1) as they respond to vegetation feedback and neighbor observations without double-loop strategy switching.

Reserve biomass exhibits a pronounced U-shaped trajectory over the 100-year simulation period. The system begins with approximately 90,000 units of reserve biomass and undergoes a significant decline during the first 26 years, reaching a minimum value of approximately 84,114 units at year 26. This initial degradation phase is characterized by statistically significant negative trends through year 21, representing the pressure of initial livestock stocking on vegetation reserves.

A transition phase occurs between approximately years 22 and 32, during which the decline stabilizes and the trajectory reverses. Following year 33, biomass enters a sustained accumulation phase that continues through the end of the simulation. The recovery accelerates significantly between years 44 and 54, exhibiting the steepest positive slopes during this mid-simulation period. Growth continues at moderating but consistent rates through year 100, when reserve biomass reaches approximately 121,073 units—roughly 34% above initial conditions.

The overall pattern demonstrates asymmetric dynamics: a relatively rapid 26-year decline followed by a 74-year recovery and growth period. Oscillatory fluctuations characterize the transition periods, particularly during years 10–20 of the initial degradation and years 41–49 as the system shifts from net biomass loss to net accumulation.

This trajectory reflects the interplay between initial grazing pressure and adaptive learning. At simulation start, 60 households each maintain 90 livestock (5,400 total animals), applying substantial pressure on private pastures. As farmers employ experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning strategies, they adjust stocking decisions based on observed pasture conditions and neighbor outcomes. With livestock reproduction disabled, natural herd attrition combined with conservative stocking decisions reduces grazing pressure below carrying capacity, enabling vegetation recovery. The sustained growth beyond initial biomass levels indicates that learned management strategies, operating within a single-loop learning framework with initial risk disposition of 0.5, successfully maintain conservative stocking rates that allow reserve accumulation despite high annual rainfall variability (coefficient of variation of 50%).

The mean green biomass across all patches begins at approximately 45,000 units. During the initial phase spanning roughly the first decade, the biomass remains relatively stable with minor fluctuations. A decline commences around step 10, continuing through step 27 where the system reaches a minimum value of approximately 42,057 units, representing the lowest vegetation biomass observed across the 100-year period.

Following this trough at step 27, a recovery phase initiates. From approximately step 33 onward, the biomass exhibits a sustained increasing trend. A particularly pronounced growth period occurs between steps 44 and 54, during which the biomass increases at a rate of approximately 955 units per step. This rapid growth gradually moderates in subsequent decades but remains positive throughout the remainder of the simulation.

By the conclusion of the simulation at step 100, the mean green biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units, representing a substantial increase of over 34% from the initial conditions and 44% from the minimum value observed at step 27. The overall trajectory exhibits a U-shaped pattern characterized by initial stability, a mid-term decline to a distinct minimum, followed by sustained recovery and long-term growth to levels exceeding initial conditions.

In the context of the grazing system, the initial decline from steps 10 to 27 reflects vegetation degradation resulting from grazing pressure exerted by the initial livestock endowment of 90 animals per household across the 60 private pastures. The minimum biomass at step 27 indicates the point of maximum vegetation stress before adaptive management responses take effect. The subsequent recovery and sustained growth demonstrate that the three learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—enable households to adjust stocking decisions and grazing practices. By step 100, the biomass levels substantially exceed initial conditions, indicating that adaptive learning facilitates the transition from initial overstocking to sustainable pasture management under conditions of high rainfall variability.

At the outset of the simulation, households begin with an average endowment of 90 livestock units. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 37 steps, the system undergoes a pronounced decline characterized by three distinct rates of decrease. An initial sharp contraction occurs between steps 0 and 3, followed by continued but moderating declines through step 20, and further slowing degradation until reaching a minimum value of approximately 41 livestock units around step 37. This nadir represents a 54% reduction from initial stocking levels and coincides with a period of stagnation where no significant trend is detectable between steps 33 and 43.

Following this minimum, the system enters a recovery phase from step 46 onward, exhibiting a statistically significant increasing trend that persists through the end of the simulation. However, the rate of recovery diminishes over time: the initial rebound between steps 44 and 54 proceeds at a moderate pace, slows considerably after step 55, and decelerates further toward step 100, ultimately stabilizing at an average value of approximately 47.5 livestock units—roughly 47% below the starting endowment and only 16% above the minimum observed level.

Superimposed on these trends is a cyclical pattern with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, suggesting inherent oscillatory dynamics in the grazing system. Furthermore, while aggregate metrics converge toward intermediate values, the trajectories reveal substantial heterogeneity in outcomes across different learning strategies. Some households utilizing particular decision-making approaches stabilize at markedly higher equilibrium levels, maintaining herds of approximately 58 to 62 units, while others converge toward lower stable states around 40 to 44 units. By step 80, most trajectories have largely stabilized, with minimal change occurring in the final 20 steps of the 100-step simulation, indicating that households reach steady-state adaptation to local ecological conditions and learning constraints.

The average number of livestock destocked exhibits a pronounced initial spike followed by a steep decline and eventual stabilization at minimal levels. At initialization (year 0), the system begins with zero destocking. By year 3, destocking reaches its maximum value of 6.61 animals per household on average, indicating significant resource pressure and forced animal removal during the early simulation period. This peak represents the system's initial inability to sustain the starting herd size of 90 animals per household given pasture conditions and the farmers' initial decision-making rules.

Following this peak, destocking declines significantly through the first three decades. Between years 3 and 30, statistically significant decreasing trends occur across consecutive windows, with the steepest decline (slope = -0.381) occurring between years 0 and 10. By year 8, destocking falls to approximately 3.33 animals, and by year 21, it reaches roughly 0.84 animals. This rapid reduction reflects the effectiveness of the learning mechanisms—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—as farmers adapt their stocking decisions based on observed pasture conditions and neighbor outcomes within the knowledge radius of one patch.

Around year 30, a change point occurs where the steep decline transitions to relative stability. From years 33 to 43, no significant trend is detected, and destocking fluctuates at low levels. Between years 44 and 54, a slight but significant decreasing trend continues (slope = -0.001), pushing destocking values lower still.

By year 100, the average destocking stabilizes at 0.31 animals per household, with the median across all observations being 0.31 and the overall mean across the full time series being 0.96. This end-state value represents a 95% reduction from the peak levels observed in year 3. Notably, between years 66 and 76, a minor but statistically significant increasing trend emerges (slope = 0.001), though values remain well below 1.0 animal per household.

Throughout the simulation, substantial variation exists across the 30 simulation series during the initial decades, with individual runs showing divergent trajectories between years 10 and 30. However, by year 40, most series converge toward low destocking levels, though some maintain persistently higher baseline destocking rates of approximately 0.7 to 1.0 animals per household through year 100, while others stabilize near zero. This persistent separation suggests that the three learning strategies (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning with single-loop adaptation) achieve different long-term equilibria in terms of resource security, despite all reducing destocking substantially from initial levels.

The overall pattern indicates that under conditions of high rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200) and initial risk disposition of 0.5, households rapidly learn to adjust stocking rates to minimize forced destocking, though the learning strategy employed affects the final level of residual vulnerability to resource shortfalls.

Inequality in healthy livestock holdings begins at effectively zero across all simulation instances, reflecting the uniform initial condition of 90 animals per household. Over the 100-year simulation horizon, the Gini coefficient exhibits a consistent monotonic increase, rising from initial values near 0.000 to terminal values averaging 0.073, with individual trajectories ranging approximately between 0.030 and 0.095 by the final time step. This indicates a progressive divergence in productive outcomes across the 60 households despite identical starting endowments.

The accumulation of inequality follows a nonlinear trajectory characterized by distinct phases. An initial period of minimal differentiation persists through approximately the first 20-25 time steps, during which the metric remains below 0.015. Subsequently, the rate of inequality growth accelerates, as evidenced by inflection points detected at steps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, and 37. This acceleration intensifies markedly after the first change point at step 51, where the Gini coefficient typically ranges between 0.020 and 0.050 across runs. A second regime shift occurs around step 71, corresponding to values roughly between 0.040 and 0.070, after which inequality continues mounting but exhibits heightened variability between simulation instances.

Throughout all analyzed windows (1–10, 11–20, 21–30, 31–40, 41–50, 51–60, 61–70, 71–80, 81–90, and 91–100), the Mann-Kendall trend test confirms statistically significant increasing trends (tau values of 0.978–1.000, p < 0.001). However, the slope magnitude varies, beginning at 0.000 in early windows and increasing to 0.001 in later periods. Notably, individual simulation runs display substantial heterogeneity in terminal inequality levels, with some trajectories plateauing near 0.030–0.045 while others exceed 0.090, suggesting path-dependent divergence sensitive to the stochastic rainfall variability (σ = 100) and the interaction effects among the three learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning). The oscillation indices concentrated between steps 78–93 indicate increased volatility in inequality dynamics during the late simulation phase, corresponding to values between approximately 0.060 and 0.085.

At initialization, no households have engaged in social learning. The number of households utilizing social learning increases monotonically from this starting point, exhibiting rapid early growth that progressively decelerates until stabilizing approximately two-thirds through the 100-year simulation period.

During the initial decade, adoption occurs at a rate of roughly 1.43 households per year, with an early inflection point at year 7 signaling the transition from initial acceleration to sustained expansion. From years 11 to 21, growth continues at 1.14 households per year. A marked deceleration begins around year 22, with the rate dropping to 0.18 households per year through year 43, accompanied by successive inflection points at years 28, 30, 33, and 38 that indicate stepwise adjustments in the adoption trajectory. Following a detected change point at year 40, the expansion slows dramatically to 0.029 households per year through year 54, and further diminishes to 0.008 households per year through year 65.

The system approaches a plateau near year 63, reaching a maximum mean value of 32.07 households (approximately 53% of the 60 households) engaged in social learning. This level persists through year 100 with a terminal value of 32.07. The overall temporal mean stands at 27.43 households, with a median of 31.90, reflecting that for the majority of the simulation duration, adoption remains near the upper asymptote.

Oscillatory dynamics are superimposed on this growth pattern, particularly evident around years 22–32 and 51–59, suggesting periodic fluctuations in adoption rates. Across the 30 stochastic realizations, final outcomes vary substantially, ranging from approximately 18 to 54 households, indicating that social learning penetration spans from roughly 30% to 90% of the farming population depending on stochastic environmental and social factors.

In the context of the grazing system, this pattern reflects the diffusion of social learning strategies among smallholder farmers managing private pastures under variable rainfall conditions. The initial absence of social learning suggests that farmers begin with their assigned baseline or experiential strategies, then progressively adopt social observation as they gather information about neighbors' outcomes. The eventual saturation below universal adoption indicates that a significant subset of farmers—averaging 28 households but ranging from 6 to 42 depending on the stochastic realization—maintains non-social learning approaches throughout the simulation period. The oscillations in adoption rates likely correspond to the high annual rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200), which creates episodic conditions prompting reassessment of stocking decisions and triggering waves of social learning adoption.

The average cumulative number of destocked livestock initiates at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation, terminating at approximately 96.8 animals per household with a temporal mean accumulation of 75.5 animals. The trajectory exhibits a pronounced deceleration pattern with notable inflection occurring between years 45 and 62, dividing the simulation into distinct phases of adjustment.

During the initial establishment phase from year 0 to 25, households destock rapidly at rates declining from approximately 4.96 animals annually in the first decade to 0.69 animals annually by year 32, accumulating roughly 65-70 destocked animals as they adjust initial stocking levels to pasture conditions. The change point at year 25 marks a transition to a sustained moderate phase lasting until year 60, where accumulation continues at reduced rates of approximately 0.32-0.36 animals annually, bringing the cumulative total to roughly 85 animals.

In the final phase from year 60 to 100, destocking persists at a steady slow rate of approximately 0.31 animals annually, with multiple inflection points between years 67 and 75 indicating continued gradual flattening of the accumulation curve. Despite the aggregate slowing trend, individual simulation trajectories diverge substantially by year 100, ranging from approximately 58 animals to over 170 animals, reflecting heterogeneous outcomes across the farming population.

The distribution of values is left-skewed with a median of 81.2 animals exceeding the mean, consistent with the concave trajectory spending proportionally more time at higher accumulation levels. Statistical confirmation of monotonic increase appears across all nine analyzed temporal windows, with highly significant positive trends throughout, indicating that destocking pressure remains continuous across all learning strategies and rainfall scenarios, though the decelerating rates suggest progressive adaptation toward maintenance stocking levels rather than ongoing herd liquidation.

At initialization, 20 households adopt the non-learning E-RO strategy. During the first decade (steps 0–10), this population exhibits a modest but significant increase, reaching a maximum average count of approximately 21.1 households by step 8. A pronounced reversal follows, with a steep decline occurring between steps 11 and 21 (characterized by a slope of −0.221), after which the attrition continues at a moderated yet significant pace through approximately step 65; marked change points in the rate of loss occur around steps 20 and 45. By step 65, the average population of non-learning households falls to roughly 15.5. Subsequent to this period, the decline ceases and the system enters a stable phase from steps 66–100, fluctuating without significant trend around a mean of approximately 15.3 households and concluding with 14.7 non-learning households at step 100, with the minimum average value of 14.67 observed near step 92.

Across the 30 replicate simulations, outcomes exhibit high variability despite identical initial conditions (uniform risk disposition of 0.5 and initial livestock of 90 animals per household). While the mean trajectory shows a 26.5% reduction in non-learning households over the century, individual simulation runs display divergent fates, with some realizations experiencing near-total attrition of non-learning agents and others maintaining substantially higher persistence rates. This heterogeneity reflects the stochastic influence of high annual rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200) interacting with stationary land tenure and fixed rule-of-thumb decision making. The stabilization of the population after step 65 suggests that a resilient subset of non-learning households achieves sustainable configurations, whereas the initial decline indicates that a significant fraction of non-learning farmers lack the adaptive capacity to persist under the erratic ecological conditions modeled.
