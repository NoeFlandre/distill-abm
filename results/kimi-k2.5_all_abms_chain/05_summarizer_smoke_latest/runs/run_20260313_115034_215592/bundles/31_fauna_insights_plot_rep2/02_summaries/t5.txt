The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. Forager population provides controlled experimental condition where hunting pressure remains constant over time. Constant invariance persists across multiple replicates, indicating no mortality or recruitment events affecting forager populations. Forager movement costs consistently exceed energy gains from hunting success throughout the simulation period. The consistent saturation of cumulative female mortality at levels approaching the initial female population size suggests that hunting pressure in this model systematically depletes the female cohort over timeframes ranging from approximately 500 to 1500 steps. However, the timing of this depletion remains unpredictable. High variability in cumulative births indicates that populations cannot recover from mortality events. A subset of simulations exhibits intermediate persistence, plateauing between steps 800 and 1200 at cumulative death counts of approximately 220 to 235. The metric initiates at approximately 120–125 female animals across all simulation replicates, consistent with the initial prey parameter of 250 total animals and sexual dimorphism. From the initial time step, every simulation trajectory exhibits a monotonic decline toward zero population. Rapid extinction pathways show populations collapsing below 20 individuals by step 300–400 and reaching zero by step 500. Ivore population consistently trends toward extinction. Extinction timing indicates current hunting pressure and environmental parameters create fundamentally unsustainable conditions. The variability in population dynamics demonstrates that short-term population stability does not guarantee long-term survival, and monitoring systems must account for extended time horizons. The combined total energy of all prey animals begins at approximately 25,000 energy units. This metric undergoes a pronounced decline throughout the simulation period. The depletion occurs in two primary phases; total energy drops sharply from the initial 25,000 to roughly 5,000 or below, representing a decline of 80% or more.
