The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The spatial scale is unitless but can be conceptualized relative to daily movement distances of megaherbivores. The model integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Agents include human hunters and sexually dimorphic ungulate herds. Hunters operate under logic approximating dynamic optimal foraging theory, making pursuit decisions based on their current energy status, prey food value, prey density in the landscape, and the costs of pursuit, hunting, and processing. Hunters do not reproduce, expend energy when moving, can die from energy depletion, and can store energy surpluses. Ungulates move as herds with environmental feedback, age over time, reproduce with user-controlled birth spacing intervals, and exhibit increased mortality risk after reaching senescence. Nursing offspring add to the food value of females. The environment consists of grass patches that can be static or dynamic, with optional seasonality limiting regrowth to specific seasonal cycles.

The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. It seeks to determine whether these interactions accelerate extinction, buffer against extinction, or create equilibrium conditions that prevent extinction events. The model allows users to isolate and test specific hypotheses by manipulating variables related to fodder availability, seasonality, relative animal food value, animal fecundity, lifespans, relative hunting danger, and movement energy costs. Experimental protocols involve establishing baselines without hunting or environmental change, then iteratively adding variables such as environmental degradation, hunter population density, sexual dimorphism ( differential food values and processing costs), and life history constraints (interbirth intervals, lifespan) to observe their individual and combined effects on extinction probability, herd demography, herd movement patterns, and hunting selectivity.

The number of foragers remains constant at the initial value of 20 across all simulation runs throughout the observed period from step 0 through approximately step 2200. This invariance persists across multiple replicates, indicating no mortality or recruitment events affecting the forager population. The constancy reflects the model configuration where foragers are designated as immortal and do not reproduce, thereby maintaining the initial population count established by the initialization parameter. For decision makers: The stable forager population provides a controlled experimental condition where hunting pressure remains constant over time. This allows researchers to attribute any observed changes in prey population dynamics solely to factors such as environmental variability, prey life history characteristics, or direct hunting mortality, without the confounding influence of fluctuating hunter numbers or changing hunting efficiency due to forager population dynamics.

The total energy available to all forager bands exhibits a consistent negative trajectory across all simulation runs. At initialization (step 0), the aggregate forager energy begins near zero. During the first approximately 500 time steps, the energy declines rapidly with notable variability between simulation runs, with some trajectories dropping to approximately -4,000 to -7,000 while others remain closer to -2,000. Between steps 500 and 1000, the divergence between runs diminishes and the decline stabilizes into a roughly linear decrease. From step 1000 onward, all simulation runs follow highly similar downward trajectories, losing energy at a steady rate. By step 2000, the total forager energy reaches approximately -30,000 to -32,000 across most runs, with one simulation continuing to approximately step 2200 where the energy declines to roughly -38,000.

The sustained negative trend indicates that forager movement costs consistently exceed energy gains from hunting success throughout the simulation period. The initial variability suggests stochastic differences in early hunting success or prey encounter rates, while the subsequent convergence implies that the system reaches a stable state of net energy depletion. Since foragers in this parameter configuration are immortal, the simulation continues despite the accumulating energy deficit, though the deepening negative values indicate that hunting returns are insufficient to offset the metabolic and movement costs of foraging activity.

**Implications for decision makers:** The consistent energy deficit suggests that the current parameter configuration produces an unsustainable foraging system where hunter populations cannot maintain energetic viability through the available prey resources. This indicates that either prey density (initially set at 250), prey food values, or foraging efficiency parameters may be set at levels that prevent forager energy balance, which would likely lead to population collapse in a model without immortal foragers. Adjustments to reduce movement costs, increase prey availability, or enhance hunting success rates would be necessary to achieve energetic sustainability for the forager population.

The variation in extinction timing (400–2,000 steps) reflects demographic stochasticity and encounter probabilities, but the zero-survival outcome indicates that stochastic factors delay rather than prevent extinction under these hunting pressure and life history conditions.
