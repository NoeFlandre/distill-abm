The cumulative number of male prey killed exhibits a classic saturation trajectory characterized by three distinct phases. During the initial accumulation phase from tick 0 to approximately tick 850, the metric increases rapidly from a starting value of 0, with statistically significant upward trends detected across multiple rolling windows (p < 0.001). Change point analysis identifies structural breaks at regular intervals throughout this phase (ticks 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850), indicating stepwise accelerations in hunting success as foragers established efficient search patterns. By tick 362, the trajectory exhibits a primary inflection point where the growth rate begins to decelerate, though remaining strongly positive.

The transition phase spans roughly ticks 850 to 1200, during which the rate of increase diminishes substantially. While windowed trend analysis between ticks 1160-1391 shows no significant trend (p = 0.084), a secondary pulse of hunting activity occurs between ticks 1392-1623 (significant increase, p < 0.001). The aggregate mean reaches its maximum value of approximately 120.17 males killed at tick 1876, though individual simulation runs plateau at varying asymptotic levels ranging from roughly 105 to 125 cumulative kills.

The saturation phase dominates from tick 1200 onward, with the mean trajectory stabilizing around 116-118 killed males. Trend analysis between ticks 1624-1855 shows no significant change (p = 0.127), while the final window (ticks 1856-2087) exhibits a slight decreasing trend (p < 0.001), reflecting the convergence of simulations toward terminal values and the dropout of high-accumulation runs. The series terminates at tick 2312 with a mean value of 117.5 males killed. Notably, the late phase (post-tick 2170) displays oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of 63 ticks, likely reflecting seasonal pulses in hunting efficiency aligned with the strong environmental seasonality parameter (0.8) driving prey aggregation and visibility cycles.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The rapid saturation of cumulative male mortality—achieving 98% of maximum kills within the first 2.3 years of simulation (first 850 ticks)—indicates that male megaherbivore populations face swift depletion under the modeled hunting pressure of 20 foragers with a 6-patch foraging radius. The asymptotic plateau around 117-120 kills suggests that the male segment of the initial 250-animal population approaches functional extinction or becomes so cryptic that encounter rates drop to near zero. Given that males and females offer equivalent net energetic returns (24 energy units after processing costs of 6 and 3 respectively), the pronounced male-biased mortality profile implies that hunters actively target males or that males exhibit higher vulnerability due to behavioral or spatial patterns.

The absence of sustained growth beyond tick 1200 demonstrates that grass regrowth every 3 ticks and high seasonality (0.8) provide insufficient demographic resilience to offset hunting mortality, even with moderate baseline mortality rates (0.025 internal, 0.0025 external). For conservation or management applications, these results suggest that male-biased harvesting strategies rapidly exhaust the harvestable surplus of large ungulate populations, with recovery likely requiring extended periods given the 730-tick interbirth interval. The 63-tick oscillation cycle in late-phase hunting activity warrants monitoring in field applications, as it may represent a critical window for intervention before reproductive viability collapses.
