The mean household risk attitude exhibits complex non-monotonic dynamics over the 100-step simulation period, characterized by rapid initial growth, subsequent divergence into distinct trajectory clusters, and eventual stabilization at heterogeneous equilibrium values.

During the initial phase (approximately steps 0-33), the aggregate metric increases sharply from near-zero values (approximately 0.007) to a maximum of roughly 0.38 around step 33. This period coincides with substantial heterogeneity emergence as individual simulation runs diverge rapidly, with some trajectories reaching high values between 0.6 and 0.8 while others remain at moderate levels around 0.2 to 0.4.

Following the peak at step 33, the aggregate trend enters a decline phase lasting until approximately step 65, decreasing from 0.38 to roughly 0.19. This period reveals pronounced path dependency as trajectories separate into three distinct clusters: a high-stable group maintaining values between 0.6 and 0.8, a medium-stable group oscillating around 0.4 to 0.6, and a declining group falling to negative values as low as -0.4.

After step 65, the system stabilizes with the aggregate metric maintaining approximately 0.19 through step 100, though individual trajectories remain separated into their distinct equilibrium states. The final values at step 100 range from approximately -0.35 to 0.78 across different simulation runs, indicating persistent heterogeneity in household risk attitudes despite aggregate stability.

This pattern reflects the model's learning dynamics where households initially increase their risk tolerance (higher r-parameters) as they accumulate experience, but subsequently diverge based on performance feedback, local interactions, and behavioral type differences (E-RO, E-RO-SL1, E-LBD). The stabilization suggests that risk attitudes reach equilibrium configurations determined by household-specific learning histories and social-ecological feedbacks, with some households maintaining optimistic/high-risk strategies while others adopt conservative/negative-risk postures.
