The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The number of human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation period. This absolute stability reflects the parametric configuration where foragers are immortal (imortal-foragers = True) and do not reproduce. Second phase (ticks 1160–1391) transitions to a stable plateau characterized by negligible trend (slope approximately –0.000) values oscillating around 118. A brief third phase exhibits a slight resurgence in accumulation. This secondary increase may reflect seasonal demographic shifts. The series reaches a maximum value of approximately 119 kills around tick 1826. The cumulative count exhibits a slight decline, concluding at 117. This pattern indicates that hunting pressure on postpartum females was concentrated in the first half of the simulation period. From tick 0 to approximately tick 850, deaths accumulate rapidly from zero. This phase reflects the immediate impact of hunting pressure from the initial forager population on the founding prey herds. From tick 1160 through 1856, the accumulation transitions toward a plateau regime. Despite these intermittent fluctuations, the overarching trend remained monotonically decreasing across all analysis windows. The oscillations at low population densities—ranging between peaks of approximately 6.5 individuals early in the decline to less than 2 individuals in later periods—proved insufficient to prevent ultimate extinction. The total energy content of the male prey population initiated at 12,500 units. The trajectory exhibited three distinct phases superimposed with regular oscillations. By tick 377, values had fallen to approximately 4,000 units, representing a 66% reduction from the starting value. Values progressively eroded from roughly 2,100 units toward substantially lower levels by tick 875. The trend reversed temporarily to weak positive growth, indicating possible density-dependent relaxation of hunting efficiency. However, this recovery proved insufficient to reverse the overall trajectory. The six-year timeline to extinction provides multiple potential intervention points, particularly during the stagnation and weak recovery phases observed between years 3.2 and 4.5. However, the severity of the initial 75-percent biomass crash within the first year suggests that management strategies must prioritize the critical initial window.
