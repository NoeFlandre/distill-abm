The cumulative number of destocked livestock begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation, reaching approximately 97 animals by the final time step. This trajectory indicates a continuous process of animal removal from the system, with the total accumulated destocking eventually exceeding the initial herd size of 90 animals per household, implying that households acquire replacement stock during the simulation period.

The rate of destocking exhibits distinct temporal phases. During the initial decade, accumulation occurs most rapidly with a steep slope of approximately 5 animals per year, suggesting immediate pressure on pastures that forces early liquidation or strategic selling as households confront the reality of their land's carrying capacity under variable rainfall conditions. Between years 11 and 25, the rate moderates to roughly 1.5 animals annually, then slows further to about 0.7 animals per year through year 32, indicating an adjustment period where households refine their stocking decisions through experiential or social learning.

After approximately year 25, and particularly following year 60, the accumulation stabilizes at a slower rate of roughly 0.3 animals per year through the remainder of the simulation. This prolonged period of gradual increase suggests that once households have destocked substantially during the first quarter-century, subsequent removals represent maintenance-level adjustments or responses to inter-annual rainfall variability rather than fundamental corrections of overstocking. The inflection points detected between years 45 and 75 reflect subtle shifts in the acceleration of this cumulative process, likely corresponding to the onset and conclusion of learning phases where risk dispositions and neighborhood observations significantly influence destocking decisions.

A cyclical pattern with a periodicity of approximately 49 years overlays these trends, potentially reflecting long-term rainfall oscillations interacting with household risk management strategies. This suggests that even after initial adaptation, destocking activities respond to multi-decadal environmental cycles.

For decision makers, the concentration of destocking in early years highlights the critical importance of initial stocking guidelines and rapid learning mechanisms to prevent premature liquidation of assets. The eventual stabilization indicates that learning strategies enable households to achieve relative equilibrium with their resource base, though the system retains sensitivity to long-term environmental variability requiring sustained adaptive capacity.
