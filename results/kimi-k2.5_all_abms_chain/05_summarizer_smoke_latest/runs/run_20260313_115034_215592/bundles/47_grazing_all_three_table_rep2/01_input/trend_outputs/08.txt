The number of households engaged in social learning begins at zero and undergoes a sustained, statistically significant increase throughout the 100-year simulation, ultimately reaching approximately 32 households by the final timestep. This trajectory indicates that over half of the farming population eventually utilizes social learning practices, despite an initial state where no households employed this strategy.

The adoption follows a distinct phased pattern. During the initial seven timesteps, the count remains near zero, representing an initialization phase where households likely accumulate baseline observations of pasture conditions and neighbor states before activating social learning mechanisms. Between approximately timesteps 7 and 40, the metric exhibits rapid acceleration with pronounced inflection points at timesteps 7, 28, 30, 33, and 38, characterized by steep positive slopes exceeding 0.18 additional households per timestep. This period marks the active diffusion of social learning across the community. A detected change point at timestep 40 signals a transition to a saturation phase, where the rate of increase diminishes substantially to near-zero slopes (0.008), stabilizing around a maximum value of approximately 32.07 households reached by timestep 63. The system maintains this plateau through the simulation conclusion, with minor oscillations evident particularly between timesteps 51 and 59.

These dynamics suggest that social learning requires an initial observational latency period before uptake accelerates rapidly. The eventual saturation level—exceeding the 20 households initially designated as social learners—implies that households employing learning-by-doing or baseline rule-of-thumb strategies may temporarily engage with social information during periods of high uncertainty, or that the strategy demonstrates sufficient adaptive value to attract participation beyond its initial cohort. The prominent oscillation period of 49 years, with fluctuation clusters around timesteps 22–32 and 51–59, indicates that engagement in social learning varies cyclically, likely reflecting responses to the high inter-annual rainfall variability that drives fluctuating perceptions of risk and the need for external information.

For decision makers, these trends highlight that promoting social learning requires accounting for initial adoption delays of approximately 7–10 years while anticipating rapid diffusion once critical observational thresholds are crossed. The saturation at roughly 53% penetration suggests that universal adoption may be constrained by spatial limitations (knowledge radius of 1 patch), heterogeneous risk dispositions, or persistent preferences for individual learning strategies, yet the sustained high engagement indicates that social learning serves as a robust adaptation mechanism for managing climatic uncertainty in grazing systems.
