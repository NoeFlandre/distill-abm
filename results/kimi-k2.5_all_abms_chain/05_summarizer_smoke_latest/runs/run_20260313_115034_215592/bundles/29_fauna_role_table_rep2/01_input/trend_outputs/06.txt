The cumulative birth count initiates at zero and exhibits distinct temporal phases over the 2,312-tick observation period, corresponding to approximately 6.3 years in the model's daily timescale. Following an initial establishment phase extending through tick 613, the system enters a prolonged period of increasing reproductive output characterized by statistically significant upward trends (Mann-Kendall τ > 0.78, p < 0.001) between ticks 696 and 1623. During this expansion phase, the rate of increase accelerates, with the slope of accumulation reaching 0.007 births per tick during the interval spanning ticks 1392 to 1623.

Superimposed upon this growth trajectory are regular oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 50 ticks, particularly evident between ticks 625 and 810. These high-frequency fluctuations likely reflect the model's strong seasonality parameter (0.8), generating pulsed resource availability that modulates reproductive success within each annual cycle. Inflection points at ticks 733, 903, 1121, and 1227 indicate successive shifts in the velocity of accumulation as the population responds to the interaction between seasonal grass regrowth and the 730-tick (2-year) birth-spacing interval governing ungulate reproduction.

The reproductive trajectory reaches a maximum mean value of 5.6 at tick 1673, immediately following a change point detected at tick 1325 that marked an acceleration phase. However, a critical transition occurs around tick 1850, where the trend reverses significantly (Mann-Kendall τ = -0.495, p < 0.001). Between ticks 1624 and 2087, the cumulative count declines with a slope of -0.005 per tick, indicating that mortality from human hunting (initiated with 20 foragers) and external pressures exceeds new recruitment into the reproductive pool. Secondary valleys at ticks 1281, 1422, and interim fluctuations reflect demographic stochasticity within the declining phase.

By the terminal observation at tick 2312, the cumulative birth count retreats to a value of 1.0, representing an 82% reduction from the observed maximum. This terminal decline, intersecting with change points at 2050 and 2310, suggests that sustained hunting pressure combined with high environmental seasonality ultimately suppresses the megaherbivore population's reproductive capacity below replacement levels. The multiphasic dynamics—initial exponential growth, mid-simulation peak, and late-stage collapse—demonstrate how the 2-year interbirth interval creates demographic momentum that initially masks predation pressure until a critical threshold is crossed, after which cumulative reproductive output contracts toward extinction levels within the final 1.3 years of the simulation period.
