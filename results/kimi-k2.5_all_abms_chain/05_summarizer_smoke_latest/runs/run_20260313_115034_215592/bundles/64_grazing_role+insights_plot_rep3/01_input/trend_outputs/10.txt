The simulations reveal highly variable trajectories for non-learning household counts, initiating at 20 households across all runs but diverging substantially within the first 20 time steps. Approximately one-third of simulation runs exhibit rapid growth in non-learning households during the initial phase, with counts escalating to between 30 and 42 households by time steps 15 to 25. Conversely, the remaining runs demonstrate immediate decline, with counts falling to ranges of 0 to 15 households within the same period.

During the middle phase (approximately time steps 20 to 60), the high-count trajectories undergo correction, declining from their peaks of 35-42 down to stabilized ranges of 23 to 28 households by time step 60. Meanwhile, low-count trajectories either stabilize at intermediate levels of approximately 10 to 18 households or continue descending toward extinction. Notably, several runs reach critically low levels of 0 to 5 households by time step 40, with one specific trajectory maintaining zero households from time step 65 through the remainder of the simulation.

By the terminal time step 100, outcomes segregate into two primary clusters: an upper group maintaining 23 to 26 non-learning households, and a lower group situated at or near zero households (0 to 5). One distinct trajectory stabilizes at an intermediate level of approximately 8 households throughout the latter half of the simulation.

The persistent volatility observed in most trajectories, characterized by continuous fluctuation even after step 60, indicates ongoing demographic turnover among non-learning households. The divergence pattern suggests that system dynamics create divergent fitness landscapes for non-learning strategies, where stochastic factors determine whether these households proliferate, stabilize, or collapse across the 100-year simulation horizon.

**Implications for decision makers:** The bifurcation into high-persistence and low-persistence outcomes indicates that regions relying on traditional non-adaptive management face divergent futures: either unexpected resilience with population expansion beyond initial levels, or rapid collapse toward extinction. The presence of irreversible collapse trajectories suggests that early intervention thresholds may exist, beyond which non-learning household populations cannot recover without external support or influx of new participants.
