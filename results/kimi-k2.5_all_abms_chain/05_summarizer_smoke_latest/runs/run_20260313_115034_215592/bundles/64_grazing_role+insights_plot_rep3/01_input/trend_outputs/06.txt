Across 100 simulation steps, the mean number of destocked livestock per household exhibits three distinct phases. During the initial five steps, values rise sharply from zero to peak levels between 7.0 and 7.5 animals, indicating an immediate system-wide adjustment shock as households implement their respective learning strategies. Following this peak, a prolonged decline occurs from step 5 through approximately step 40, characterized by high volatility with oscillations of decreasing amplitude; during this period, mean destocking drops from above 6.0 to below 2.0, with transient spikes recurring at irregular intervals. After step 40, the system stabilizes into a steady-state regime where values flatten and converge to differentiated equilibrium levels ranging from near zero to approximately 1.1 animals per household, with most trajectories settling between 0.2 and 0.8. This persistent divergence in final outcomes across simulation runs suggests that initial configurations of learning strategies produce heterogeneous long-term resilience profiles, with certain decision-making approaches achieving near-elimination of destocking events while others maintain low but persistent vulnerability to livestock loss. For decision makers, these patterns indicate that early implementation phases involve high transitional costs and uncertainty, but strategic choices in learning mechanisms significantly determine long-term stability, implying that investment in adaptive capacity building during initial steps can substantially reduce chronic destocking pressure over multi-decade horizons.
