Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change. Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances. Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores. Foragers-movement-cost ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1.0, and distance hunters can perceive and pursue prey is determined by foraging-radius. The number of human foragers remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation, from the initial step at 0 through to approximately 2,300 steps. During the first approximately 500 time steps, the cumulative number of kills begins at zero across all simulation runs. By step 500, the majority of runs have accumulated between 60 and 80 kills, while by step 1000, most of these counts have already reached their terminal values. The timing of this rapid increase in the number of deaths is likely to be due to a lack of females. The male ungulate population initiates at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation trials and follows a deterministic downward trajectory toward extinction in every replicate. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, deaths accumulate rapidly, with most simulation runs recording between 150 and 230 cumulative fatalities by this point. The total number of animals initiates at 250 individuals across all simulation runs, consistent with the established initial prey population. Within the first 400 to 500 time steps, this metric undergoes a steep decline, plummeting from 250 to below 50 animals in most replicates. This rapid collapse reflects the immediate impact of hunting pressure from 20 human foragers operating under optimal foraging strategies. The total energy of female animals initializes at approximately 12,000 units, representing the collective energetic biomass of the starting cohort. This aggregate metric undergoes a precipitous decline during the first 400 to 500 steps. By step 2,000, all trajectories converge to effectively zero, indicating complete depletion of female energetic reserves. After approximately time step 1600, the percentage stabilizes around 33% with reduced variance across simulation runs, indicating that vegetation dynamics have reached a quasi-equilibrium with herbivore consumption patterns. The trajectory remains near this theoretical maximum (constrained by keep-initial-grass-pattern) through the remainder of the observation period.
