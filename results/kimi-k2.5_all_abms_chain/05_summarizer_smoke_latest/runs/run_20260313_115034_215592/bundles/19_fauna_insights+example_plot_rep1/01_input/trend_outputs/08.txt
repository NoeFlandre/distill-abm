The male animal population initiates at approximately 125 individuals and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction across all simulation runs, ultimately declining to zero. The decay process exhibits distinct temporal phases characterized by differing rates of attrition.

In the initial interval from step 0 to approximately step 500, the population experiences rapid depletion, falling sharply from 125 to below 20 individuals. This steep decline suggests immediate and sustained hunting pressure exceeding the population's capacity for reproduction and survival. The trajectory during this phase consists of discrete downward steps, reflecting episodic mortality events consistent with hunting encounters in the agent-based framework.

Following this initial crash, the period between steps 500 and 1500 exhibits highly variable extinction dynamics across runs. While many simulations reach zero males by step 1000, others maintain small remnant populations of 1 to 5 individuals for extended durations, with some persisting until approximately step 2200 before final extinction. This long-tail distribution in extinction timing indicates stochastic elements in the model—such as herd movement patterns, hunter-prey encounter rates, or demographic fluctuations—allowing temporary survival of isolated male cohorts despite unsustainable overall population trends.

The absence of any stabilization or recovery episodes throughout the time series indicates that under the current parameterization—including immortal foragers and the defined food-value-to-cost ratios—extinction represents an inevitable outcome rather than a probabilistic risk. For researchers examining megafaunal extinction hypotheses, these results demonstrate that the specified hunting pressure creates a deterministic extinction vortex for male populations, with variability confined to the temporal duration of population persistence rather than the ultimate demographic fate.
