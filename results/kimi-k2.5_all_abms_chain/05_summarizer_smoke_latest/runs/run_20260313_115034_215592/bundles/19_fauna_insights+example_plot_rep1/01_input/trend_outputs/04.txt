The cumulative count of female prey deaths begins at zero and exhibits a rapid, approximately linear increase during the initial 500 time steps, with values rising to between 60 and 120 individuals across different simulation runs. This early acceleration suggests immediate, intensive hunting pressure targeting females, likely driven by their favorable cost-benefit ratio under the model's parameters—where processing costs are lower and food values comparable to males when accounting for nursing offspring.

After approximately 500 steps, the rate of accumulation decelerates markedly across all runs, transitioning from steep increases to gradual stepwise gains. By 1,000 steps, most trajectories approach asymptotic plateaus ranging between 108 and 124 total females killed, though the specific timing and magnitude vary substantially. Termination points for active female hunting range from approximately 1,100 steps to over 2,200 steps, indicating high stochasticity in the duration required to reach maximum cumulative mortality.

The deceleration pattern implies either prey depletion effects, where remaining female populations become increasingly difficult to locate due to declining density and herd fragmentation, or adaptive shifts in hunter foraging strategy as the energetic returns from pursuing scarce females diminish relative to search costs. The stepwise nature of the curves reflects discrete encounter and kill events rather than continuous harvesting.

Variance between runs remains relatively constrained during the first 250 steps but expands significantly thereafter, particularly regarding the final plateau levels achieved. Some runs culminate near 108 deaths while others exceed 124, suggesting that demographic stochasticity, herd movement patterns, and spatial distribution of grass resources create divergent extinction trajectories even under identical initial conditions.

For decision makers, the predictable rapid early mortality indicates that interventions to manage hunting pressure must occur within the first 500 steps to be effective, as the majority of irreversible female population loss occurs during this initial phase. The subsequent variability in time-to-extinction—spanning over 1,000 steps between the fastest and slowest runs—implies that management strategies should account for significant uncertainty in predicting specific collapse timing, even when aggregate outcomes converge toward similar final mortality totals. The consistency of ultimate kill counts near or exceeding half the initial prey population suggests near-complete female extinction represents the dominant equilibrium outcome under these parameter settings.
