The average number of livestock destocked across all households begins at zero and immediately surges to a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals by the third year. This initial spike reflects the immediate pressure on pasture resources as households begin with livestock levels that exceed the sustainable carrying capacity of their private parcels, forcing rapid adjustments through destocking in the critical opening phase of the simulation.

Following this peak, destocking rates decline sharply and significantly throughout the first decade, dropping by roughly 50% by year 9 and continuing to decrease through year 30. This rapid reduction suggests that farmers across all learning strategies quickly adjust their stocking decisions in response to observed pasture degradation, with the experiential and social learning mechanisms enabling households to calibrate their herd sizes to avoid future resource shortfalls. The pronounced oscillations during this period, with secondary peaks occurring around years 9 and 12 before diminishing, indicate ongoing adaptation to the high inter-annual rainfall variability, where dry years trigger temporary destocking events as households respond to stochastic environmental conditions.

After approximately year 30, the system undergoes a structural shift toward stabilization. Destocking rates plateau at low levels, with the series mean of 0.96 masking the convergence toward near-zero values by year 100, where the average destocking settles around 0.31 animals per household. This long-term equilibrium suggests that persistent households have successfully aligned their livestock holdings with the expected productivity of their private pastures, given the stochastic rainfall regime.

Throughout the simulation, substantial variance exists across the population of 60 households, reflecting the differential effectiveness of the three learning strategies. While some trajectories stabilize at minimal destocking levels by year 40, others maintain low but persistent destocking rates around one animal per year, indicating that households employing certain learning approaches or facing particular spatial configurations of pasture quality continue to experience occasional resource constraints even after the initial adjustment period. The oscillation pattern with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, despite the 100-year timeframe, suggests that long-term climatic cycles continue to influence destocking decisions even as overall rates diminish.
