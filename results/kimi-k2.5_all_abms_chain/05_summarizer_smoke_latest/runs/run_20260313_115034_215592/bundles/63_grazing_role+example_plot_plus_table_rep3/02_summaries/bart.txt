The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods. The aggregate mean green biomass initiates at 45,000 units and maintains relative stability during the initial phase, with only minor fluctuations through step 10. Between steps 11 and 27, the metric undergoes a statistically significant decline, dropping sharply to a minimum value of 42,057 units near step 27. The most rapid expansion occurs between steps 44 and 54 during which the growth rate reaches its maximum slope of approximately 954 units per step. The average number of livestock destocked across all households begins at zero and immediately surges to a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals by the third year. Destocking rates decline sharply and significantly throughout the first decade, dropping by roughly 50% by year 9 and continuing to decrease through year 30. After approximately year 30, the system undergoes a structural shift toward stabilization. The count of households that have engaged in social learning initiates at zero and follows a saturating growth trajectory over the 100-year simulation. By year 100, the system exhibits high variance across runs, with oscillation patterns in the final decades overlaying the dominant increasing trend. The median household accumulates approximately 81 destocked animals by year 100, with the population showing considerable variance (standard deviation 20.3) around the mean of 75.5. The trajectory reveals substantial heterogeneity across individual simulation runs, with some realizations showing complete attrition of non-learning households while others maintain higher counts.
