The simulations reveal a consistent pattern of rapid decline in total available energy across all animal populations, though with notable variation in the rate and timeline of collapse. Initially, all model runs begin with approximately 25,000 units of total energy distributed among the 250 ungulate agents. Within the first 500 time steps, this collective energy undergoes a steep decline across all trajectories, with many simulations dropping below 10,000 units and several falling below 5,000 units by this point.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the decline continues but at more variable rates. Some populations experience near-total collapse, approaching zero energy, while others maintain residual energy levels between 1,000 and 5,000 units, suggesting temporary stabilization or slower depletion rates. This variability likely reflects stochastic differences in hunter-prey encounters and the patchy distribution of vegetation resources.

By approximately step 1500, the majority of simulation runs have reached near-zero total energy, indicating population collapse. However, a subset of trajectories demonstrates prolonged persistence, maintaining small but measurable energy reserves extending beyond step 2000. These late-persisting populations represent scenarios where animals managed to avoid hunters for extended periods despite the high foraging radius (6 patches) and optimal foraging behavior of human agents.

The trajectories display considerable heterogeneity in their descent profiles. While some runs follow a relatively smooth exponential decay from the initial state, others exhibit stepwise declines with temporary plateaus—particularly noticeable around steps 300-800 where certain populations maintained 7,000-8,000 energy units briefly before resuming decline. These plateaus may correspond to periods of reduced hunting success or temporary resource abundance during favorable seasonal conditions (given the high seasonality parameter of 0.8).

Interpreted within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, the rapid initial decline reflects the combined pressure of 20 human foragers operating under dynamic optimal foraging theory with food storage capabilities, targeting a relatively small founding population of 250 prey. The differential return rates—where males provide 30 energy units and females 27—create constant hunting pressure that, combined with movement costs (1 unit per step) and the high baseline mortality (0.025), drives the system toward rapid depletion. The eventual convergence toward zero energy across all runs suggests that under these parameter settings, stable coexistence between human foragers and ungulate populations is not achieved within the simulated timeframe, with sexual dimorphism and energetic constraints preventing population recovery despite grass regrowth occurring every 3 ticks.
