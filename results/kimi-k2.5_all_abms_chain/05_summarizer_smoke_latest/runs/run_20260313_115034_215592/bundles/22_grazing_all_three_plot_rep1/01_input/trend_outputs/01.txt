The mean household initial risk attitude remains constant throughout the 100-step simulation period, with values fixed at their starting levels from step 0 through step 100. Individual household values are distributed across a range spanning approximately -0.11 to +0.18, with multiple distinct constant levels observed across the population. These invariant trajectories confirm that the metric captures a static baseline characteristic assigned at model initialization, representing each social learning agent's predetermined disposition to deviate from recommended livestock numbers when confronting uncertainty about pasture conditions.

The observed spread from negative to positive values indicates substantial heterogeneity in risk preferences among the simulated farming households. Households with negative initial risk attitudes demonstrate inherent conservatism in their decision-making frameworks, predisposing them toward cautious stocking strategies that adhere closely to ecological recommendations. Conversely, households with positive initial values possess baseline propensities for greater experimentation, rendering them more likely to maintain livestock numbers that substantially deviate from advised levels when interpreting environmental feedback and neighbor behavior. This diversity reflects realistic variation in how smallholder farmers psychologically approach uncertainty regarding land management under the social learning regime.

For decision makers, the stability of these initial risk dispositions throughout the simulation suggests that baseline risk profiles act as persistent filters through which all learning and adaptation occur. Since risk attitudes do not shift over time in this model, agricultural extension services and policy interventions should prioritize early identification of farmer risk types and tailor communication strategies accordingly. Risk-averse farmers may require explicit demonstration of safety margins and gradual adjustment protocols before modifying stocking rates, while risk-tolerant farmers might benefit from boundary-setting mechanisms to prevent overstocking. Furthermore, the wide dispersion in initial attitudes implies that community-level pasture management outcomes will vary significantly based on the specific distribution of risk types within local farming populations, necessitating community-specific rather than uniform policy approaches.
