The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock initializes at approximately zero, reflecting perfect equality across all 60 households at the start of the simulation when each household holds exactly 90 livestock. Over the 100-year period, this inequality metric follows a persistently upward trajectory, reaching a mean value of 0.0726 by the final timestep, with individual simulation runs exhibiting terminal values ranging up to approximately 0.091. The mean value across the entire temporal series stands at 0.0302, with a median of 0.0229, indicating that inequality remains right-skewed during the early and middle phases before accelerating markedly in later years.

The temporal progression reveals distinct phases in the accumulation of inequality. During the initial approximately 20 timesteps, divergence proceeds gradually, followed by a period of accelerated increase between timesteps 20 and 50. Inflection points detected at timesteps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, and 37 mark successive shifts in the growth rate, suggesting that households begin differentiating their livestock management outcomes at varying speeds as learning strategies take effect. A substantive structural change occurs around timestep 51, identified as a primary changepoint where the trajectory of inequality growth undergoes significant modulation, with a secondary changepoint at timestep 71 indicating further evolution in the distribution dynamics.

Statistical validation confirms the robustness of increasing inequality throughout the entire simulation. Rolling Mann-Kendall trend tests across ten sequential windows covering the full 100-timestep span demonstrate consistently significant monotonic increases (Kendall's tau values of 1.000 for windows 2 through 10, p<0.001). The slope of this increase grows from negligible values in early windows to approximately 0.001 per timestep in later periods, with the most pronounced divergence occurring after timestep 60. Oscillatory behavior detected between timesteps 78 and 93 suggests ongoing fluctuations in relative household performance, yet these fluctuations occur around the dominant increasing trend without reversing the overall inequality accumulation.

This emergent inequality arises despite identical initial endowments, uniform rainfall distribution across the landscape (mean 200, standard deviation 100), and stationary private property arrangements. The divergence stems fundamentally from the three heterogeneous learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—which generate differential competencies in maintaining livestock health under environmental uncertainty. Because livestock reproduction is disabled (b=0), early learning failures or successes compound directly into lasting disparities, while the restricted knowledge radius of one patch limits the diffusion of successful practices among neighbors. The moderate terminal Gini coefficients (0.06–0.09) indicate that while complete polarization does not occur, substantial welfare stratification emerges as a path-dependent consequence of adaptive decision-making under incomplete information.

For decision makers managing grazing systems, these findings indicate that inequality in livestock health represents an emergent structural property that intensifies over time regardless of initial equality in endowments. Interventions should target the critical window before timestep 50 when inequality accelerates most rapidly, potentially through expanding information networks beyond the current one-patch knowledge radius or providing transitional support during the initial learning phase. The persistence of increasing trends across all simulation runs suggests that reliance on autonomous adaptive learning without institutional support mechanisms will naturally produce stratified outcomes, warranting policy attention to households falling behind during early divergence phases to prevent long-term welfare disparities in pastoral communities.
