The total energy contained within the male animal population initiates at approximately 12,500 energy units, representing the collective biomass of the initial cohort, and undergoes a precipitous decline throughout the simulation period, ultimately collapsing to zero by approximately step 2,312. The trajectory is characterized by distinct phases of depletion, marked by change points at steps 125, 350, 500, 625, and 875, with significant inflections in the rate of decline occurring at steps 316 and 453.

During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 350 steps, the total male energy decreases rapidly from the initial 12,500 to roughly 4,000 energy units. This decline exhibits oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of 63 steps during the first 150 steps, likely reflecting seasonal variation in resource availability interacting with intense hunting pressure. The pronounced initial drop corresponds to the establishment of hunting pressure on the male subpopulation, which carries a higher nutritional value and represents preferential targets for foragers operating under optimal foraging strategies. By step 125, the first major change point indicates a transition from the initial shock to a period of continued but variable depletion.

Between steps 350 and 875, the decline continues but at a decelerating rate, with the energy level fluctuating around 3,000 to 4,000 units before entering a second rapid decline phase. Statistical analysis reveals multiple localized peaks in this interval, particularly around steps 377 through 408, where temporary stabilization occurs with values hovering near 3,950 to 4,000 units. These plateaus may represent brief periods where surviving males accumulate energy during favorable seasonal conditions or where reduced population density makes detection by hunters more difficult, temporarily slowing the rate of energy extraction from the population.

After step 875, the system enters a terminal depletion phase characterized by a steady decrease toward zero, with the final significant change point occurring at step 2,310 just before complete exhaustion. The median energy level across all simulation runs stands at 134.5 units, indicating that for the majority of the temporal sequence, the male population operates at severely depleted energy levels. The substantial standard deviation of 3,478 units reflects high variability in extinction trajectories across different simulation runs; while some populations approach zero energy by step 1,500, others persist with small remaining energy reserves until after step 2,000.

The rolling trend analysis confirms predominantly decreasing dynamics throughout, with statistically significant negative trends (p < 0.001) across consecutive windows from the start through step 1,391. Brief periods of statistically significant increasing trend appear in windows between steps 1,392–1,623 and 1,856–2,087, representing minor recoveries or demographic stochasticity in the terminal phase of population collapse rather than genuine population rebounds.

For decision makers, these results indicate that under current hunting pressures and environmental conditions, male megaherbivore populations face rapid functional extinction within approximately six years (2,300 ticks), with critical vulnerability occurring within the first year (first 365 steps). The high variability in persistence times suggests that stochastic factors play a significant role in determining exact extinction timing, but the inevitability of collapse indicates that current hunting intensities are unsustainable for male subpopulation maintenance. Interventions aimed at preserving male energy stores and population viability must be implemented before step 350 to prevent irreversible decline trajectories, and the brief stabilization periods observed around steps 377–408 suggest that temporary relief measures may only delay rather than prevent ultimate collapse.
