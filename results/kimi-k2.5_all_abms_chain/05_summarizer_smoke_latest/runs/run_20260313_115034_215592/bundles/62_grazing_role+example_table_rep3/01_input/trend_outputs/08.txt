The engagement of households in social learning commences at zero and follows a trajectory of rapid initial growth followed by progressive deceleration and eventual stabilization. During the first seven years, the number of engaged households increases sharply, reflecting immediate adoption of information-seeking behaviors as farmers confront initial environmental uncertainty and incomplete information about pasture conditions. By year 28, the growth rate moderates considerably as the system transitions from acute establishment to sustained accumulation, with the rate of new engagement diminishing through years 30, 33, and 38.  

The count approaches an asymptote near 32 households around year 63, maintaining this plateau through the conclusion of the simulation at year 100. This saturation level suggests that approximately half of the farming population ultimately participates in social learning, indicating a stable equilibrium where observation of neighbors becomes a persistent strategy for a substantial minority of households despite the presence of alternative decision-making approaches.  

Superimposed on this general upward trend are periodic oscillations with a dominant cycle of approximately 49 years, particularly evident between years 22-32 and again between years 51-59. These fluctuations likely reflect the influence of high inter-annual rainfall variability on decision-making cycles, where deviations in precipitation trigger temporary variations in the propensity to seek social information. The oscillations diminish in amplitude as the system approaches its plateau, suggesting that long-term engagement patterns become less sensitive to environmental shocks once social learning networks are established.  

This pattern indicates that social learning diffuses rapidly when initial pasture assessments prove ambiguous under uncertainty, but reaches a ceiling well below universal adoption. The partial penetration may reflect the model's constraint against strategy-switching, which preserves distinct learning typologies across the population. The cyclic variations align with the vegetation recovery dynamics and stochastic rainfall, suggesting that households adjust their reliance on neighbor observation according to transient visibility of environmental feedback signals.
