 The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system . Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and farmers' livelihoods .  The mean household risk attitude, representing the average deviation from recommended livestock numbers, initiates at a near-neutral value of 0.0074 . Over the first 33 steps, the metric undergoes a statistically significant increasing trend . This rapid ascent suggests an initial phase of active learning or experimentation where households collectively deviate toward higher-risk strategies .  The system initializes with 90 animals per household . During the first 20 time steps, a steep reduction occurs, with animal numbers declining rapidly as households adjust to pasture conditions . This trajectory suggests an initial period of pasture degradation potentially caused by stocking rates exceeding carrying capacity, followed by a recovery phase .  Simulations track the diffusion of social learning among 60 households over 100 annual steps . At the first approximately 7 to 20 steps, the adoption count increases rapidly from zero . At step 40 onward, growth continues at a greatly diminished pace, with slopes approaching zero (0.008 to 0.03 households per step) The aggregate mean reaches its maximum value of approximately 32 households around step 63 .  The population of non-learning households (E-RO strategy) initiates at 20 households and undergoes a characteristic three-phase trajectory over the 100-year simulation horizon . During the initial establishment phase (approximately years 0-8), the count exhibits a modest but statistically significant increasing trend, reaching a maximum value of approximately 21.1 households around year 8 .
