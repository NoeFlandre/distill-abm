The count of non-learning households begins at 20 across all simulation runs. During the initial 10 to 20 steps, trajectories diverge sharply: approximately half of the simulation runs exhibit a rapid increase in non-learning households, peaking between 38 and 41, while the remainder decline to values between 0 and 10. This suggests an initial period of behavioral instability where either learning behaviors fail to establish themselves or non-learning strategies temporarily proliferate through social contagion or superior short-term performance.

Between steps 20 and 50, the trajectories exhibiting high initial values undergo a marked decline, stabilizing in the range of 23 to 28 non-learning households. Concurrently, trajectories that initially declined remain at low levels or continue a gradual descent. By step 100, the system settles into a heterogeneous distribution of outcomes spanning from 0 to approximately 26 non-learning households, with notable clustering around 23-26, 16-18, 8-13, and 0-5.

The persistence of multiple stable configurations indicates that the diffusion of learning behaviors is not deterministic. In some simulation paths, learning-by-doing and social learning successfully displace baseline non-learning strategies, reducing their prevalence to minimal levels. In others, non-learning households maintain or even increase their representation, suggesting that under certain ecological or social conditions, the baseline strategy proves resilient against innovative behaviors. The wide variance in outcomes implies that system history, stochastic factors in rainfall variability, or initial heterogeneity in household risk attitudes create path dependencies that lock the system into divergent social-ecological states.

Insights for decision makers: The unpredictability of behavioral adoption suggests that promoting learning-based grazing management requires targeted intervention early in the process when trajectories are most malleable. Once the system stabilizes, the high persistence of non-learning households in approximately half of the scenarios indicates that relying solely on endogenous social learning may be insufficient for universal adoption of improved practices. External support mechanisms may be necessary to shift systems trapped in high non-learning equilibria toward more adaptive management configurations.
