The average cumulative number of destocked livestock begins at zero across all simulations and increases rapidly during the initial 20 time steps, reaching values ranging from approximately 40 to 70. This initial phase reflects a period of active household adjustment where destocking occurs as households respond to initial pasture biomass conditions and implement early livestock management strategies.

Following time step 20, the trajectories diverge considerably, with the spread between the lowest and highest values increasing monotonically through the remainder of the simulation. By time step 40, values range from approximately 60 to 110. By time step 60, the range extends from roughly 70 to 130. At time step 100, the final values span from approximately 58 to 172, indicating nearly a threefold difference in cumulative destocking across simulation runs.

The divergence pattern suggests that stochastic variation in ecological conditions and household learning dynamics generate path-dependent outcomes in livestock management. The trajectories that continue rising steeply likely reflect households engaging in proactive, adaptive destocking strategies to maintain pasture health and livelihood resilience, while those that flatten may indicate either successful early optimization requiring minimal subsequent adjustment or conversely, constrained adaptive capacity. The wide variance in outcomes highlights the sensitivity of long-term destocking behavior to early-period decisions and feedbacks between learning processes and ecological conditions.

For decision makers, the high variability in destocking outcomes underscores the inherent unpredictability in social-ecological grazing systems and the critical importance of supporting robust learning mechanisms during the initial phases of pasture management. The existence of divergent trajectory groups suggests that policies should be designed to accommodate diverse household responses and foster adaptive capacities, as static intervention strategies may be insufficient given the path-dependent nature of outcomes. Investments in social learning infrastructure and early-warning systems could help households achieve more favorable destocking patterns that balance livestock productivity with long-term pasture sustainability.
