The metric begins at 90 units at step 0, representing the initial livestock endowment across households. A rapid decline occurs over the first 20 steps, with values falling sharply to approximately 45 units. This initial collapse reflects severe overstocking relative to pasture carrying capacity, forcing households to destock significantly as vegetation biomass becomes depleted under variable rainfall conditions. Between steps 20 and 40, the decline continues at a slower rate, reaching a minimum threshold of roughly 40 to 42 units around step 40. This nadir represents the system's low point, where herds have contracted to levels that can be sustained by degraded pasture resources.

Following step 40, the metric enters a recovery phase that diverges markedly across simulation runs. From step 40 to step 60, values rise from their minimum to range between approximately 42 and 56 units. After step 60, the trajectories separate into three distinct stable clusters. This divergence indicates the emergence of multiple stable equilibria stemming from identical initial conditions and parameter settings. The recovery patterns suggest that while all households experience initial resource depletion, stochastic factors combined with heterogeneous learning capabilities (learning-by-doing and social learning) create divergent long-term outcomes. Some runs achieve resilient herd sizes through successful adaptation, while others remain trapped in low-livestock states despite the presence of learning mechanisms.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The existence of multiple equilibrium states implies that smallholder grazing systems face significant path dependency and potential poverty traps. Interventions should prioritize preventing the initial severe destocking shock or supporting households during the critical recovery window between steps 40 and 60. Policies targeting the support of social learning and learning-by-doing processes may help shift households toward the higher equilibrium outcomes (58–62 units) rather than allowing them to settle into the lower stability traps (41–45 units). Early warning systems and destocking incentives before step 20 could mitigate the initial collapse, preserving sufficient capital for the adaptive learning processes that drive recovery after step 40.
