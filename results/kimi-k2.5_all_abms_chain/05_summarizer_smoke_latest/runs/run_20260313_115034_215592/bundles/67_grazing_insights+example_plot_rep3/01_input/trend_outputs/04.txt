Following this minimum, the biomass enters a rapid recovery phase between steps 40 and 70, increasing sharply from approximately 40,000-47,000 to values ranging between 56,000 and 64,000. The steep increase suggests that the combination of these three behavioral types successfully reduces grazing pressure and allows vegetation recovery.

After step 70, the biomass stabilizes, though substantial variation persists across simulation runs through step 100. Final values range from approximately 56,000 to 64,000, indicating divergent long-term ecological outcomes despite identical initial conditions. This variation likely stems from the interaction of stochastic rainfall patterns (mean 200, standard deviation 100) with the three distinct learning mechanisms, where some runs achieve higher sustainable biomass levels than others.

The variance across runs remains moderate during the initial decline but increases substantially during the recovery phase and persists into the steady state, suggesting that small differences in early learning trajectories or climatic realizations compound to produce significantly different pasture conditions over the long term.

**Implications for decision makers:** The trajectory indicates that grazing systems face an initial degradation period of approximately 40 steps before adaptive learning enables recovery. Interventions may be most critical during steps 30-40 when biomass reaches its minimum. The wide variation in final biomass levels (spanning 8,000 units) suggests that relying solely on decentralized learning produces inconsistent ecological outcomes, and supplementary governance mechanisms may be necessary to ensure systems reach higher sustainable states rather than remaining at lower biomass equilibria.
