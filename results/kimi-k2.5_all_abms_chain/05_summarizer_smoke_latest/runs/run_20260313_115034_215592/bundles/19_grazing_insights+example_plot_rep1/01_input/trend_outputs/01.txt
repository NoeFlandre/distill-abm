This stability characterizes the initial r-parameter values for E-RO-SL1 households, which serve as persistent baseline references representing each household's original disposition to deviate from recommended livestock numbers. Since the initial risk attitude functions as a stored historical parameter rather than a dynamically updated variable, it remains unchanged even as households engage in learning-by-doing or social learning processes that may modify their current risk attitudes during the yearly decision-making sequence.

For decision makers, the static nature of these initial values indicates that heterogeneity in risk disposition is permanently established at simulation inception and persists as a fixed characteristic throughout the modeling period. This underscores the critical importance of initial risk attitude calibration in determining long-term household behavior, as these baseline values represent permanent reference points that cannot be altered through subsequent learning mechanisms. Consequently, interventions targeting grazing system resilience must account for the initial distribution of risk attitudes among social learning agents, recognizing that baseline conditions will perpetually anchor decision-making trajectories regardless of adaptive processes or experiential learning.
