This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. The mean household risk attitude, representing the r-parameter that captures uncertainty management and risk disposition, exhibits complex temporal dynamics across the 100-step simulation horizon. The mean reserve biomass initiates at approximately 90,000 units and declines during the first 20 steps, reaching minimum values between 80,000 and 88,000. The mean green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units across all simulation runs. The initial decline reflects the immediate impact of grazing pressure as households deploy livestock endowments onto private pastures. The subsequent recovery aligns with the activation of learning mechanisms. Policy interventions should prioritize supporting effective learning mechanisms and uncertainty management strategies. The trajectory demonstrates that despite initial overstocking and high adjustment costs, the combination of private property grazing and adaptive learning mechanisms enables households to progressively optimize herd sizes relative to pasture carrying capacity. The system exhibits self-organizing properties that move toward sustainable stocking rates, though the initial 20 time steps involve significant destocking. The count of non-learning households (E-RO type) initiates at 20 across all simulation runs. Within the first 5 to 15 steps, the population diverges rapidly, with some trajectories surging to between 30 and 42 households, while others decline to fewer than 10. Early destocking decisions (within the first 20-30 steps) significantly influence whether households achieve stable livestock levels or enter trajectories of continuous asset liquidation.
