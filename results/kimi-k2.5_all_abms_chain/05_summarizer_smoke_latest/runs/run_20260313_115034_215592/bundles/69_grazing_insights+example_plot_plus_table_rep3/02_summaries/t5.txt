Model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making. The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. The initial surge reflects the adaptive response of heterogeneous agents employing learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms. The system enters a sustained correction phase through step 65, decreasing significantly at rates between -0.004 and -0.010 per step to approximately 0.19. This downward adjustment represents a self-organizing stabilization process. Pronounced initial decline lasting approximately 26 steps indicates that new grazing management implementations inevitably face a 'valley of vulnerability' the subsequent rapid recovery demonstrates that adaptive learning capabilities are effective but require 40–50 steps to generate significant ecological benefits. Convergence of outcomes across diverse behavioral types suggests that mixed populations of learning agents can reliably achieve sustainable biomass levels. Over the 100-year simulation period, inequality exhibits a persistent and statistically significant increasing trend, reaching a mean final value of 0.0726. Despite this variation, all trajectories maintain the consistent upward trend, indicating that increasing inequality is a robust feature of the system dynamics. The cumulative destock total begins at 0 and increases monotonically throughout the 100-step simulation period. The most rapid accumulation occurs during the initial phase from step 0 to step 25, where change point analysis detects the first significant regime shift. By step 25, trajectories have already diverged significantly across simulation runs, with individual run values ranging from approximately 50 to 90 units. Progressive attrition of non-learning households suggests that in the absence of adaptive capabilities such as learning-by-doing or social learning, approximately one quarter of agents face unsustainable conditions leading to exit from the system within the first 100 periods.
