The number of male animals begins at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation runs. This initial population experiences a sustained decline throughout the simulation period, ultimately approaching zero in most runs. The depletion follows a stepped trajectory, indicating discrete mortality events rather than continuous attrition.

During the first 500 time steps, the population undergoes its most rapid reduction, typically falling from 125 to between 20 and 60 individuals depending on the specific simulation run. This steep initial decline likely reflects the immediate impact of human hunting pressure combined with baseline mortality sources including external predation and environmental stress. The stepped pattern visible during this phase suggests that hunting occurs in pulses, possibly corresponding to forager search patterns or seasonal resource availability.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the decline continues but at a more variable pace across different runs. Populations typically decrease from the 20-60 range down to fewer than 10 individuals. The increasing divergence between simulation runs during this period indicates that stochastic factors such as encounter rates between hunters and prey, individual animal energy levels, and grass regeneration patterns become more influential as population densities drop.

After step 1000, most male populations have declined to fewer than 5 individuals, with many runs reaching zero by step 1500. However, some simulations show extended persistence, with small numbers of males surviving beyond step 2000. These prolonged trajectories likely represent scenarios where remaining animals occupy low-accessibility areas or where hunter efficiency decreases due to sparse prey distribution.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that male megaherbivore populations face rapid depletion under moderate hunting pressure, with critical thresholds occurring within the first 500 time steps. The high variability in extinction timing suggests that small population management must account for stochastic extinction risks, and that refuge areas or hunting restrictions implemented early in the process may be necessary to prevent rapid collapse.
