The cumulative mortality count of postpartum females begins at zero across all simulation runs. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, the number of killed individuals increases rapidly, with most trajectories accumulating between 80 and 120 deaths by step 500. This represents the period of highest hunting pressure on nursing females, driven by high encounter rates and favorable energetic returns relative to search costs under optimal foraging conditions.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of accumulation decelerates markedly across the ensemble. During this transition period, cumulative totals rise from roughly 100 to asymptotic values ranging between approximately 105 and 125 individuals. The reduced rate of increase indicates declining encounter rates as the postpartum female cohort diminishes through mortality, or potential behavioral shifts by foragers to alternative prey as the relative abundance and encounter probability of this demographic declines.

By approximately step 1000 to 1500, nearly all trajectories stabilize, indicating cessation of postpartum female mortality. This plateau persists through the remainder of the observation period (up to 2000 steps), with final cumulative values clustering tightly between 110 and 125 individuals, though some runs stabilize at lower values near 105.

Notable stochastic variation exists in the temporal dynamics. At least one simulation trajectory exhibits substantially delayed accumulation during the first 400 steps, reaching only approximately 20 cumulative deaths compared to 60-80 in other runs, before accelerating to achieve comparable final totals by step 800. Additionally, the timing of stabilization varies, with some runs reaching asymptotic values by step 800 while others continue gradual accumulation until step 1200.

Interpreted within the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model context, these trends demonstrate that postpartum females constitute a highly vulnerable demographic experiencing concentrated mortality during initial hunting phases. The asymptotic behavior at 100-120 individuals—representing approximately 40-50 percent of the initial prey population assuming equal sex ratios—suggests functional depletion of the breeding cohort within the first 500-1000 time steps. Given equivalent net energetic values for adult males and females (24 units each after processing costs), the disproportionate exploitation of nursing females likely reflects reduced mobility while accompanied by dependent offspring or the additional nutritional acquisition from associated calves.

**Insights for Decision Makers:** The data reveal front-loaded mortality risk, with the majority of postpartum female deaths occurring within the first 500 time steps and effective elimination of this demographic by step 1000-1500. This indicates an extremely narrow intervention window; sustainable management requires immediate protection of breeding females during initial contact periods, as delayed action results in irreversible loss of reproductive capacity. The deterministic convergence toward 100-120 cumulative deaths across stochastic runs suggests that without specific prohibitions against targeting nursing females, this demographic faces inevitable depletion regardless of initial hunting efficiency variation, necessitating strict seasonal closures during calving periods or complete protection of postpartum individuals to maintain population viability.
