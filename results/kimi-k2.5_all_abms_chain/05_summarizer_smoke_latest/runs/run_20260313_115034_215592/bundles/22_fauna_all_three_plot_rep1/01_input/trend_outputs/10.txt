The megaherbivore population initiates at 250 animals across all simulations. An immediate and sustained decline reduces numbers to below 100 individuals within the first 400 to 800 time steps, depending on the specific trajectory. The most rapid declines reach near-zero levels by approximately step 500, while more gradual declines maintain populations of 40 to 60 individuals through step 800. Between steps 800 and 1200, a secondary phase of decline affects the remaining populations, with most falling below 20 animals. By step 1500, the majority of trajectories have reached fewer than 5 animals, with complete extinction or near-extinction occurring between steps 1500 and 2200 across all runs.

The consistent downward trend indicates that the initial population of 250 megaherbivores cannot withstand the predation pressure from 20 human hunters under the specified environmental and life history conditions. The inevitable progression toward extinction reflects the combination of constant hunter numbers (due to immortal-foragers setting), energy storage capabilities that buffer hunters against lean periods, and high seasonal resource variability. Because hunters do not experience mortality from energy depletion and can store resources, hunting pressure remains constant or even intensifies relative to prey availability as populations decline, preventing predator-prey stabilization at intermediate densities. The high seasonality parameter creates periodic resource stress that likely accelerates mortality during low-productivity seasons, while the wide divergence in extinction timing—spanning approximately 1,700 time steps between the first and last trajectories to reach extinction—demonstrates high sensitivity to stochastic demographic events and the probabilistic nature of encounter rates between hunters and prey.

Populations that persist at intermediate levels (20-50 animals) during steps 600-1000 likely experience temporarily reduced per-capita hunting mortality due to sparser prey distribution making encounter rates less frequent, creating a predation refuge effect that delays but does not prevent ultimate collapse. This phenomenon is particularly pronounced inagent-based systems where reduced animal density lowers the probability of hunter-prey interactions, though continued mortality from starvation, senescence, and age-related mortality slowly erodes these remnant populations toward final extinction.

For decision makers: The results demonstrate that even moderate hunter densities can inevitably drive megaherbivore populations to extinction when hunters possess energy storage capabilities and do not face starvation mortality themselves, though the temporal pathway may vary considerably. This suggests that monitoring programs should not interpret short-term population persistence at low densities (20-50 individuals) as recovery or stability, as these represent delayed extinction trajectories rather than sustainable equilibria. The high variability in collapse timing also implies that identifying causal factors for extinction in archaeological or paleontological records will be statistically challenging due to inherent demographic stochasticity, and that conservation efforts must account for the inability of small megaherbivore populations to recover once hunter populations are established and buffered against resource fluctuations.
