**Context and Goals**

This agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Unlike the original RAGE model which examined common property scenarios where herders move livestock across shared rangelands, this extension focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods.

The simulation tracks the interaction between vegetation condition and household decisions about livestock numbers over time. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors.

The central purpose is understanding how these different ways of adapting to changing conditions influence pasture health, herd sizes, and the persistence of households. The model particularly examines how farmers handle uncertainty and incomplete information when making stocking decisions, represented through individual risk dispositions that affect how they perceive and react to environmental feedback.

**Key Parameters**

*Population and Spatial Setup:*
The **number-households** parameter establishes how many farming families occupy the landscape, which can range from 1 to 100 families, with this study using 60 households. Each household begins with **livestock-init** set at 90 animals. The simulation runs for **timesteps** set at 100 years. Farmers begin the simulation positioned on their designated land parcels, with **start-on-homepatch?** set to True. Each farmer can observe conditions within a **knowledge-radius** that ranges from 0 to 5 patches, set here at 1 patch distance.

*Behavioral Type Configuration:*
The study examines three specific learning strategies with equal representation: **number-E-RO** (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb), **number-E-LBD** (learning-by-doing), and **number-E-RO-SL1** (social learning with single-loop adaptation), each set to 20 households. The **homog-behav-types?** parameter set to True ensures each type is evaluated in uniform groups. The original RAGE model behavioral types are inactive in this extension: **number-SAT**, **number-TRAD**, **number-MAX**, and **number-random** are all set to 0. The default reference **behavioral-type** is set to E-RO.

*Grazing Management Practices:*
Formal pasture resting is disabled with **resting?** set to False. The threshold for initiating rest periods, **resting-threshold**, ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0.1. The **intrinsic-preference** for livestock relative to other values ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0. Similarly, **social-susceptibility** to neighbor influence ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0, indicating decisions rely on other factors.

*Original Model Parameters:*
Several parameters from the foundational RAGE model are retained. The **satisficing-threshold** for satisfied decision-making ranges from 0 to 100 and is set at 0. The **satisficing-trials** parameter, ranging from 0 to 100, is set at 21. These control how the original satisficer type would search for acceptable solutions, though this type is not active in the current setup.

*Ecological Conditions:*
Rainfall is applied uniformly across the landscape with **global-rain?** set to True. The average rainfall **rain-mean** is set at 200 units annually. Year-to-year variation in precipitation is controlled by **rain-std** set at 100, indicating significant variability in annual rainfall totals. Vegetation growth responds through two rates: **gr1** set at 0.5 and **gr2** set at 0.1. The parameter **w** from the original vegetation model is set at 0.8, influencing how pastures recover from grazing. Livestock reproduction is not active in this extension, with **b** set to 0.

*Learning and Risk Mechanisms:*
The extension module is activated with **extension-model?** set to True. Uncertainty handling is engaged with **risk-mode?** set to True. Each agent begins with an initial risk disposition **risk-att-init** set at 0.5, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance. The ability to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False, confining agents to single-loop learning within their assigned type.

The mean household initial risk attitude remains constant at approximately 0.0074 throughout the 100-year simulation period. This temporal stability reflects the fixed nature of initial risk dispositions, which serve as static reference points for how farmers perceive uncertainty when evaluating pasture conditions and determining deviations from recommended livestock numbers.

The metric exhibits zero variance and no significant trends across any observation window, indicating that the population-level baseline risk attitude does not drift or fluctuate over time. Individual households maintain their specific initial risk values—whether negative, neutral, or positive—throughout all simulation years, as these attitudes represent inherent characteristics assigned at initialization rather than learned or adapted behaviors. This constancy persists regardless of the distinct learning strategies employed by different household groups, confirming that initial risk dispositions remain unchanged even as farmers accumulate experience or observe neighbors over the century-long simulation.

By the final time step, the aggregate destocking rate diminishes to 0.31 animals, demonstrating that across all behavioral types, households largely converge toward sustainable stocking practices, though stochastic environmental variability continues to force occasional destocking events even in the stabilized system.

The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock begins at effectively zero, indicating perfect equality in the initial distribution of healthy animals across all 60 households. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 40 to 50 time steps, inequality increases gradually but remains relatively contained, with values typically below 0.015. This early period represents the establishment of initial differentiation as households employing distinct learning strategies—baseline experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—begin processing environmental feedback and adjusting their stocking decisions based on local vegetation conditions within their knowledge radius.

A notable inflection occurs around step 51, marking a transition toward more rapid divergence in household fortunes. Following this point, the rate of increase in the Gini coefficient accelerates substantially, with values rising from approximately 0.03 to over 0.06 by step 70. This acceleration suggests that early differences in decision-making outcomes compound over time, as households successful in maintaining healthier livestock accumulate advantages while those experiencing pasture degradation face increasing difficulty in rebuilding their herds under conditions of high rainfall variability.

From step 71 through the conclusion of the simulation, inequality continues to intensify, though the variance across simulation runs becomes more pronounced during this period. The substantial heterogeneity in final outcomes—with some simulations producing relatively equitable distributions while others result in highly concentrated holdings of healthy livestock—likely stems from the interaction between stochastic rainfall patterns, individual risk dispositions initialized at 0.5, and the specific learning mechanisms employed. Households with effective adaptation strategies increasingly outperform those whose decision rules prove less suitable for their particular environmental realizations.

The consistent significance of increasing trends across all analytical windows confirms that the emergence of inequality is a robust feature of this system, driven by the differential success of the three behavioral types in managing private pastures under uncertainty. The absence of double-loop learning locks households into their initial approaches, amplifying the divergence between those whose assigned learning strategy matches their environmental conditions and those facing persistent mismatches between their decision heuristics and actual pasture dynamics.

The count of households engaged in social learning initiates at zero across all simulation runs, indicating that no households activate this behavior at the outset despite the presence of agents predisposed to social learning strategies. Over the first approximately 40 time steps, the number of households adopting social learning undergoes a rapid increase, though the rate of adoption decelerates progressively. A notable inflection occurs around step 7, where the initial steep ascent begins to moderate, transitioning from a rapid expansion phase to a more gradual accumulation.

By step 40, the system reaches a critical change point, after which the growth in social learning adoption largely stagnates. The average count stabilizes near 32 households by step 63, representing approximately 53 percent of the total population, and maintains this level through the simulation's conclusion at step 100. However, substantial heterogeneity exists across simulation runs, with final counts varying widely between approximately 18 and 54 households. This divergence suggests that stochastic elements—such as rainfall variability, initial risk dispositions, or the spatial arrangement of early adopters within the restricted knowledge radius—create divergent trajectories of social diffusion. Oscillations in the count between steps 22-32 and 51-59 indicate temporary adjustments in adoption status, reflecting the experimental nature of learning-by-doing and social observation as households evaluate pasture conditions and neighbor outcomes before committing to sustained engagement with social learning strategies.

The plateauing behavior implies that social learning does not achieve universal adoption within the 100-step timeframe, but rather saturates at different equilibria depending on localized ecological and social feedbacks. The stabilization of the count, combined with the absence of double-loop learning (preventing permanent strategy switching), indicates that households either permanently activate social learning mechanisms early in the simulation or remain locked into individual experiential approaches for the duration, resulting in a persistent heterogeneous distribution of learning strategies across the farming population.

The cumulative number of livestock destocked per household begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation period, reaching an average of approximately 97 animals by year 100. The trajectory exhibits three distinct phases characterized by differing rates of accumulation.

During the initial phase from year 0 to approximately year 25, destocking accumulates rapidly with an average slope of nearly 5 animals per year in the first decade. This steep initial increase reflects the immediate adjustment period as households respond to initial pasture conditions and begin implementing their respective learning strategies. By year 25, the cumulative destocking reaches approximately 40–50 animals on average, representing the initial correction from the starting herd size of 90 animals toward sustainable stocking levels.

From year 25 to year 60, the rate of destocking accumulation decelerates substantially, with the slope decreasing to approximately 0.3–0.4 animals per year. This middle phase contains multiple inflection points between years 45 and 75, indicating subtle shifts in the curvature of the accumulation curve as the system processes ongoing environmental variability. By year 60, cumulative destocking reaches approximately 75–80 animals on average.

In the final phase from year 60 to year 100, the accumulation rate stabilizes at a low but persistent level of approximately 0.3 animals per year, continuing the gradual upward trend established in the middle phase. The curve approaches but does not reach a clear asymptote by year 100, suggesting that destocking decisions continue to occur in response to persistent rainfall variability throughout the century.

Across the 30 simulation runs, final cumulative destocking values exhibit substantial heterogeneity, ranging from approximately 58 to 173 animals per household by year 100. This wide dispersion indicates that while the average trajectory follows the described pattern, individual realizations of rainfall sequences and neighborhood interactions produce markedly different cumulative pressure on livestock holdings. The distribution appears skewed, with most runs clustering between 80 and 120 animals, but with a notable tail of high-destocking outcomes exceeding 140 animals.

The overall pattern reflects a system under continuous environmental pressure from high rainfall variability, where households perpetually adjust stocking rates downward over time. The declining rate of destocking accumulation suggests that while initial learning and adjustment periods require significant herd reductions, subsequent decades involve finer-scale management responses to stochastic environmental fluctuations rather than fundamental corrections to unsustainable stocking levels.
