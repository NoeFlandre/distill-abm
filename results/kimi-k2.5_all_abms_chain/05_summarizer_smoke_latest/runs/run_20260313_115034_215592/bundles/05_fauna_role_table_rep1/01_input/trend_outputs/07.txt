The aggregate time series of cumulative prey mortality exhibits a saturating trajectory characterized by three distinct temporal regimes: an initial phase of rapid decelerating accumulation, an extended plateau with superimposed seasonal oscillations, and a terminal moderating decline.

From initialization through approximately tick 850, cumulative mortality undergoes rapid acceleration from zero, though the rate of accumulation decreases substantially across six identified change points at ticks 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850. Rolling trend analysis confirms this deceleration, with initial slopes of 0.346 (ticks 0–231) and 0.438 (ticks 232–463) declining monotonically to 0.017 by tick 1159. An inflection point at tick 362 marks the transition from convex to concave growth, suggesting the initial interaction between the founding population of 250 prey and 20 human foragers rapidly depletes the most vulnerable demographic classes (calves, senescent individuals) before encountering diminishing returns as prey density declines or behavioral adaptation occurs.

Between approximately ticks 850 and 1856, the system enters a quasi-equilibrium state where cumulative mortality stabilizes around a median value of 237 individuals, with the mean trajectory hovering between 236 and 240 deaths. During this interval, rolling Mann-Kendall tests detect no significant directional trend (p > 0.05) in windows spanning ticks 1160–1391 and 1624–1855, indicating a temporary demographic balance where recruitment partially offsets continued mortality. Superimposed on this plateau are regular oscillations with a dominant period of 63 ticks—consistent with the strong environmental seasonality parameterization (0.8 intensity)—driving pulsed variation in cumulative deaths as resource availability fluctuates. The trajectory reaches its maximum value of 240.33 at tick 1876, representing approximately 96% cumulative mortality relative to the initial population.

Following tick 1856, the series exhibits a statistically significant decreasing trend (tau = −0.446, p < 0.001) through tick 2087, ultimately settling at a terminal value of 235 by tick 2312. This late-phase decline from the peak suggests increasing heterogeneity across the 40 replicate simulations, wherein subset populations either collapsed to extinction (halting further mortality accumulation) or stabilized at low densities with negligible deaths, thereby reducing the aggregate mean below the plateau level observed at tick 1876.

Ecologically, the trajectory indicates that the parameterized hunting pressure—operating through dynamic optimal foraging with sexual dimorphism in prey value (males 30, females 27) and processing costs—generates unsustainable predation pressure resulting in near-complete population turnover within the analytical window. The mid-series stabilization reflects density-dependent feedback mechanisms and hunter resource selection switching to lower-value prey as high-value individuals are depleted, while the 63-tick oscillatory component embeds the seasonal vegetation regeneration cycle (regrowth time 3 ticks) into the mortality signal, with deaths peaking during resource bottlenecks.
