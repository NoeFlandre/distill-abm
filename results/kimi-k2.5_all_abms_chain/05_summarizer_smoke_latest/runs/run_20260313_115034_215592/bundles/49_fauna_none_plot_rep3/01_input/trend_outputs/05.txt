Across simulation replicates, the cumulative mortality of postpartum females commences at zero and undergoes a phase of rapid accumulation during the initial 300 to 600 steps. In most runs, the count increases steeply during this period, reaching cumulative values between 80 and 120 individuals by step 600, though stochastic variation produces outliers with both slower accumulation rates and lower intermediate values.

Subsequently, between approximately steps 600 and 1000, the accumulation rate decelerates substantially across all replicates. During this transitional phase, trajectories diverge, with some runs exhibiting stepwise increases punctuated by extended periods of stability, while others demonstrate more gradual asymptotic approaches to maximum values.

After approximately step 1000 to 1500, the metric stabilizes at saturation points across nearly all runs. Final cumulative values range from approximately 105 to 125 individuals, with minor variation in the timing of stabilization—some replicates plateau as early as step 700, while others continue gradual accumulation until step 1600 or beyond.

Within the framework of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these dynamics indicate that hunter agents initially prioritize postpartum females as prey, consistent with optimal foraging predictions given the elevated food value conferred by nursing offspring and reduced processing costs relative to adult males. The pronounced deceleration in kill rates suggests that sustained hunting pressure rapidly depletes the availability of this demographic or renders their pursuit energetically inefficient as encounter rates decline. The two-year birth-spacing interval (730 ticks) limits the recruitment rate of new postpartum females, contributing to the observed saturation as the demographic becomes a limiting resource. The stochastic variation in accumulation rates reflects random elements in herd movement, reproduction timing, and hunter-prey spatial dynamics within the toroidal environment. The final saturation levels, representing approximately 40-50 percent of the initial prey population, indicate substantial but incomplete removal of postpartum females over the simulated time horizon.
