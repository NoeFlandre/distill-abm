The total energy contained within the male prey population begins at approximately 12,500 units across all simulation runs, representing the initial energetic biomass of approximately 125 male ungulates (assuming roughly half of the 250-animal starting population is male). This value undergoes a precipitous decline over the first 400 to 500 time steps, with most trajectories falling below 1,000 units by step 600. The rate of decline varies substantially across simulation runs, with some populations losing 60% of their energy within the first 200 steps while others maintain levels above 8,000 until step 300-350. This variability likely reflects stochastic differences in hunter-prey encounter rates and the spatial distribution of vegetation patches affecting male movement patterns. Between steps 300 and 600, several runs exhibit brief plateaus or slowed decline phases where energy levels stabilize temporarily between 2,000 and 5,000 units, potentially corresponding to periods when hunters switch to alternative prey or encounter processing constraints, though the higher food value of males (30 versus 27 for females) generally sustains hunter focus on this demographic. By step 800, the majority of runs have converged near zero, indicating functional extinction of the male population. However, a small subset of trajectories demonstrates extended persistence, with isolated runs maintaining low energy levels (100-500 units) until steps 1,200-1,500, suggesting that in rare cases, spatial refugia or reduced hunter efficiency can delay but not prevent population collapse. The pronounced divergence in trajectories between steps 200 and 600, followed by reconvergence at extinction, indicates that while the eventual outcome is highly deterministic under these parameter settings, the timing of collapse is sensitive to initial conditions and stochastic events. For decision makers, this pattern indicates that megaherbivore populations under moderate hunting pressure (20 hunters) face extremely high risk of rapid male-biased collapse within 1-2 years, with limited opportunities for intervention once hunting commences due to the steepness of the initial decline and the irreversibility of the energetic depletion.
