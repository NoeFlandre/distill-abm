The number of male animals initiates at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation replicates, representing roughly half of the initial prey population of 250 animals. From the outset, all replicates exhibit a consistent downward trajectory characterized by stepwise declines, indicating ongoing mortality that exceeds recruitment.  

During the initial phase spanning approximately steps 0 to 500, the population experiences rapid attrition, with male counts falling from roughly 125 to below 40 in most replicates, and several replicates reaching near-zero levels by step 500. This steep decline reflects the immediate impact of the initial hunting pressure from 20 human foragers combined with baseline mortality sources.  

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of decline moderates but remains directional, with male populations in most replicates decreasing from the 20–40 range to fewer than 10 individuals. By step 1000, the majority of simulation runs have reached extinction or maintain only 1–5 male animals.  

A subset of replicates exhibits extended persistence beyond step 1000, with small remnant populations of 1–5 males surviving in scattered runs until steps 1500 to 2200. These prolonged persistence phases suggest stochastic variation in spatial encounter rates or temporary refuge effects, though eventual extinction occurs in all observed replicates, with the final male animals disappearing by approximately step 2200 in the longest-running simulation.  

Interpreted within the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model framework, these trends indicate that the parameter configuration produces an unsustainable offtake rate for males. Despite high vegetation regrowth rates (regrowth-time of 3 ticks) and the potential for seasonal resource fluctuations (seasonality of 0.8), the energetic returns provided by male prey (net value of 24 energy units after processing costs) combined with the foraging radius of 6 patches enable 20 human hunters to systematically deplete the male population. The absence of recovery phases or stabilization plateaus suggests that hunting mortality constitutes the primary demographic driver, overwhelming the species' reproductive capacity (governed by birth-spacing of 730 ticks and maximum lifespan exceeding 10,000 ticks).  

**Implications for decision makers:** The results demonstrate that current hunting pressure levels exceed sustainable thresholds, leading to deterministic extinction of male megaherbivores within 500–2000 time steps (approximately 1.4 to 5.5 years given the 365 ticks-per-year calibration). Wildlife managers should prioritize reducing hunter numbers below the critical threshold of 20 or implementing spatial refugia where hunting is prohibited to allow population persistence. Conservation strategies must account for the rapid initial decline phase, indicating that intervention delays of even a few hundred days could result in irreversible population collapse. For researchers investigating extinction dynamics, the consistent extinction outcomes across stochastic replicates confirm that the model successfully captures scenarios where anthropogenic hunting pressure overrides natural population regulatory mechanisms.
