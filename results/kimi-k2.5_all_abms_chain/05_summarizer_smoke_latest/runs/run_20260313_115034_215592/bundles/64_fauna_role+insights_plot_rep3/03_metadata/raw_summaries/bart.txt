 Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change . Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances . Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores .  Foragers-movement-cost ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1.0, and distance hunters can perceive and pursue prey is determined by foraging-radius . Two critical behavioral switches control hunter mortality and storage: imortal-foragers is set to True, meaning hunters do not die from energy depletion . Environmental fluctuation is controlled by seasonality, representing the intensity of seasonal variation in resource availability .  Analysis of the cumulative male mortality trajectories reveals a consistent three-phase pattern across simulation replicates . The linear nature of the decline after tick 500 indicates that the system has reached a stable but energetically impoverished equilibrium that will persist indefinitely without intervention . By step 1000, additional mortality has slowed to fewer than 5 males per 100 steps .  Conservation strategies would need to fundamentally alter encounter rates, forager energetics, or prey population structure to avoid the asymptotic depletion pattern observed across all replicates . The wide variation in time-to-extinction suggests monitoring programs face high uncertainty in predicting the specific timing of reproductive failure .  The cumulative mortality trajectories exhibit three distinct phases . Death counts rise steeply from 0 to between 200 and 240 individuals . By step 600, most replicates have recorded 80-96% of their total final mortality . Since the initial prey population was set at 250 individuals, this indicates total mortality ranging from 90% to 100% across all replicates .  The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model shows that hunting pressure from 20 human foragers exceeds the maximum sustainable reproductive capacity of the ungulate population . The decline dynamics are characterized by stepwise, monotonic decreases rather than smooth exponential decay . By step 1000, the majority of simulation runs have reached extinction or maintain only 1–5 male animals .  Conservation strategies must prioritize immediate reduction of hunting pressure on males, as the current rate leads to population collapse within a few years . Hunting pressure from as few as 20 efficient foragers constitutes a deterministic extinction threat for megaherbivore populations . Conservation interventions must prioritize reducing hunter efficiency .
