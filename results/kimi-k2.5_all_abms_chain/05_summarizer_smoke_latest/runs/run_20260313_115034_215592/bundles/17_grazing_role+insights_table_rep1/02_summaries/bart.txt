The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods. The mean risk disposition across the farming population follows a distinct trajectory characterized by an initial rapid increase, a mid-simulation peak, and a stable equilibrium. For decision makers, these dynamics indicate that farmer risk attitudes are adaptive rather than fixed, trending toward aggressive utilization of resources before experiential feedback induces conservative adjustments. The aggregate livestock count across all households begins at 90 animals per household and undergoes a pronounced three-phase trajectory over the century-long simulation. During the first two decades, a steep and statistically significant decline occurs, with aggregate livestock dropping rapidly from initial 90 animals to approximately 60 animals by year 20. The subsequent recovery indicates that farmers successfully reduce herd sizes or adjust grazing patterns in response to observed pasture degradation.
