The combined total energy available in the population of female prey animals initiated at 12,500 units and declined deterministically to complete extinction (0 units) by tick 2,311, representing approximately 6.3 years of simulated time given the parameterization of 365 ticks per year.

The decline occurred in distinct phases. An initial rapid decrease transpired from tick 0 to approximately tick 375–500, during which total energy plummeted from 12,500 to values ranging between 2,000 and 4,000 units. This phase exhibited a strongly significant monotonic decreasing trend with a slope of approximately -22.6 units per tick.

Between approximately ticks 376 and 410, transient local peaks emerged with values oscillating around 2,100–2,200 units, superimposed on an underlying oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks. These oscillations likely reflect the interaction of strong seasonal environmental constraints (seasonality parameter set to 0.8), biological rhythms including the birth spacing interval of 730 ticks (two years), and hunting pressure dynamics.

Following tick 500, the system entered a prolonged decline phase punctuated by temporary stabilization and minor recoveries. Notably, between ticks 1,160 and 1,391, a statistically significant increasing trend emerged (tau = 0.113, slope = 0.019), suggesting a temporary recovery in female prey energy reserves. A second significant increasing trend occurred between ticks 1,856 and 2,087 (tau = 0.453), indicating another partial recovery.

Despite these intermittent increases, the overall trajectory remained downward. After tick 2,000, total energy declined precipitously from residual values below 500 units to complete extinction at tick 2,311. The median energy value across the entire simulation was 100 units, substantially lower than the mean of 1,569 units, indicating that for the majority of the simulation duration, the female prey population operated at severely reduced energy levels compared to the initial state.

The complete depletion of energy reserves by the termination of the simulation indicates that under the specified parameter configuration—including 20 initial foragers, differential processing costs between sexes (6 for males, 3 for females), food values favoring males slightly (30 vs. 27), maximum lifespan of 10,220 ticks (28 years), age of senescence at 7,300 ticks (20 years), and environmental seasonality—the female megaherbivore population experienced deterministic extinction driven by the interaction of human hunting pressure and life history constraints.
