The data reveal trajectories of cumulative mortality for postpartum females that follow a consistent three-phase pattern across simulation runs. All trajectories originate at zero and exhibit monotonic increases, terminating at asymptotic values ranging between approximately 105 and 125 individuals killed. 

During the initial phase spanning roughly the first 500 steps, the cumulative count increases most rapidly, rising sharply from zero to values between 80 and 110. This period represents the highest intensity of hunting mortality for nursing females and those with dependent offspring. Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of accumulation decelerates substantially, with most trajectories adding only 10 to 20 additional individuals as the curves begin to flatten. After approximately 1000 to 1500 steps, the cumulative counts stabilize, entering a plateau phase where additional mortality of postpartum females effectively ceases.

While all runs converge toward similar final cumulative totals, variability across trajectories is most pronounced during the middle transition period. Some simulations approach their asymptotic values by step 600, exhibiting early plateaus around 105, whereas others demonstrate more gradual increases that continue until step 1500 or beyond, occasionally reaching values near 125. The final range of approximately 20 individuals between the lowest and highest cumulative totals suggests robust consistency in the magnitude of demographic impact despite stochastic variation in the timing of individual hunting events.

Within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these patterns indicate that hunting pressure on postpartum females is heavily front-loaded during the early stages of human-ungulate interaction. The plateauing of cumulative mortality after 1000–1500 steps, despite continued simulation runtime, suggests effective extirpation of the postpartum female demographic from the landscape. Given that postpartum females represent high nutritional return (27 energy units with low processing costs of 3 units), hunters target this cohort aggressively when available, but the resource becomes depleted through either direct mortality or disruption of the reproductive cycle that prevents new females from entering the postpartum state. The asymptotic values approaching 120 individuals represent a substantial proportion of the initial breeding population and likely indicate population-level collapse or severe demographic bottlenecks within the first 1000–1500 steps of the simulation.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

These results demonstrate that postpartum females face critical vulnerability during the initial phases of hunting pressure, with the majority of cumulative mortality occurring within the first 500 time steps and effective elimination of this demographic by step 1000–1500. Conservation or management strategies must prioritize immediate protection of breeding females during colonization or initial contact phases, as delayed intervention would occur after the most severe demographic damage has been inflicted. The narrow convergence of final cumulative totals across runs suggests that under the tested parameter settings—representing moderate hunting pressure with favorable energetic returns—impacts on postpartum females are both severe and predictable, with expected losses of 40–50 percent of the breeding female population occurring rapidly without active regulatory mechanisms. Any management protocols requiring sustained population viability must therefore implement protections before cumulative mortality exceeds 20 individuals, as the trajectories indicate irreversible demographic momentum once counts surpass 80 killed.
