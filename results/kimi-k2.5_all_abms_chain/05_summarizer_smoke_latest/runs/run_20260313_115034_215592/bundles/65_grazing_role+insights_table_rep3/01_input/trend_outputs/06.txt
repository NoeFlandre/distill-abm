Destocking rates commence at zero but immediately escalate, reaching a pronounced maximum average of approximately 6.6 animals per household by year three. This initial spike indicates severe resource pressure as the initial allocation of 90 livestock per household exceeds the carrying capacity under highly variable rainfall conditions (mean 200 units with standard deviation of 100). Following this peak, destocking declines markedly over the first three decades, transitioning through progressively lower peaks—3.4 animals at year 9, 2.7 at year 12, and below 1.5 animals by year 17—before stabilizing.

By year 30, the system reaches a critical transition point where destocking shifts from high volatility to relative stability. The contrast between the century-long mean of approximately 0.96 animals and the median of 0.31 animals reflects the heavy influence of these early high destocking events; after year 30, typical destocking rates remain minimal, oscillating at low amplitude around values close to zero. Between years 33 and 65, no significant directional trend prevails, indicating that the three learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning) have successfully enabled households to converge on sustainable stocking levels that account for their risk dispositions and localized pasture observations within the one-patch knowledge radius.

A modest resurgence occurs between years 66 and 76, where destocking rates increase slightly before declining again to approximately 0.31 animals per household by year 100. This late-century fluctuation, superimposed on a dominant oscillatory cycle of roughly 49 years, suggests ongoing sensitivity to rainfall variability even after long-term adaptation. Because livestock reproduction is disabled in this system, destocking represents the sole mechanism for herd reduction, making the trajectory from high initial forced removals to near-zero destocking particularly significant for household persistence.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The three-decade adjustment period required to stabilize destocking rates suggests that even with active learning mechanisms, grazing systems experience substantial time lags before achieving sustainable equilibrium. Extension services should anticipate high early destocking rates and consider temporary support mechanisms to prevent severe resource degradation during initial adaptation phases. The effectiveness of diverse learning strategies—particularly the combination of individual experience and social observation—indicates that policies promoting knowledge sharing among neighboring households can accelerate convergence toward sustainable management. However, the persistent low-level volatility and late-century increase in destocking demonstrate that private pasture management under high rainfall variability remains vulnerable to periodic resource shortfalls, suggesting that risk management tools beyond single-loop learning may be necessary for long-term resilience.
