The mean green biomass across all simulation runs begins at approximately 45,000 units, representing the initial vegetation condition before substantial grazing pressure accumulates. During the first 27 time steps, biomass declines significantly to a minimum value of roughly 42,057, indicating an initial degradation phase where livestock pressure exceeds sustainable carrying capacity while farmers have not yet adapted their management strategies. This decline follows a brief initial plateau with an inflection around step 9, transitioning into a statistically significant decreasing trend between steps 11 and 21.

Following the minimum at step 27, biomass enters a prolonged recovery phase. Between steps 33 and 54, the system exhibits a steep, statistically significant increasing trend with the most rapid growth occurring around step 50, which represents a critical change point in the system dynamics. During this transition, the rate of increase accelerates dramatically, with biomass accumulation rates exceeding 950 units per time step in the window around steps 44-54, before moderating to slower but sustained growth rates of approximately 11-55 units per step in later periods.

By the conclusion of the 100-year simulation, biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units on average, though outcomes vary substantially across the 30 simulation runs, with final values ranging from roughly 55,000 to 64,000. This divergence becomes pronounced after step 50, where different learning strategies produce differentiated outcomes. The oscillation patterns reveal a dominant cycle of approximately 49 time steps, suggesting the system requires nearly half the simulation duration to complete a full degradation-recovery cycle.

The trajectory reveals three distinct phases: an initial degradation period (steps 0-27), a rapid recovery and transition phase (steps 27-50), and a consolidation phase (steps 50-100) where growth continues but at decelerating rates. The variance in final outcomes across simulations indicates that while all learning strategies eventually facilitate recovery from initial overgrazing, the choice of decision-making approach—whether baseline rule-of-thumb, experiential learning, or social learning—generates persistent differences in long-term pasture productivity, with some strategies achieving biomass levels 15-20% higher than others.

For decision makers, these patterns suggest that smallholder grazing systems may endure an initial "crisis" period of resource degradation lasting roughly 25-30 years before learning effects manifest in improved vegetation outcomes. Investment in social learning mechanisms appears to yield superior long-term biomass outcomes compared to individual experiential approaches, though all adaptive strategies eventually outperform static management. The 50-year mark emerges as a critical evaluation point where the benefits of different learning strategies become distinguishable, suggesting that policy assessments should consider medium-to-long timeframes rather than short-term outcomes.
