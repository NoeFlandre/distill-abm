 The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system . Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods .  The mean household risk attitude across the population begins near zero (0.0074) at the initiation of the simulation . The mean reserve biomass begins at 90,000 units and undergoes a characteristic pattern of initial decline followed by sustained recovery over the 100-step simulation period . The rapid crystallization of divergent risk attitudes within the first 60 steps suggests that early intervention windows are critical for influencing long-term grazing behaviors .  The specific mechanism by which farmers learn and adapt profoundly influences long-term ecological sustainability . Strategies that achieve the upper range of biomass outcomes demonstrate significantly greater resilience and productive capacity than those plateauing at lower levels . Policy interventions supporting effective learning mechanisms could yield substantial ecological dividends over multi-decadal timeframes .  The aggregate series for mean livestock destocking per household begins at 0.0, indicating no immediate removals at initialization . By step 3, the metric reaches its maximum value of 6.6144, reflecting an acute crisis where initial stocking levels of 90 animals per household vastly exceed available forage under the stochastic rainfall regime (mean 200)  The number of households engaged in social learning begins at zero and increases throughout the 100-step simulation period, reaching an aggregate mean of 32 households by the terminal step . The acceleration of inequality after the 50-year mark suggests that early intervention periods are critical—policies aimed at preventing asset polarization must be implemented well before the mid-century point .  The number of livestock destocked begins at zero and follows a saturating growth trajectory over the 100-year simulation period . The accumulation occurs in three distinct phases characterized by markedly different rates of change . The concentration of 50–80 percent of total destocking activity in the first 25 years indicates that initial stocking decisions and early adaptation strategies critically determine long-term management trajectories .
