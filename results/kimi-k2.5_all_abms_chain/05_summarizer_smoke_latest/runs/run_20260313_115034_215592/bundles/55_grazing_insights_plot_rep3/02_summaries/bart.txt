This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. Trend Analysis of Mean Household Risk Attitude (Deviation from Recommended Livestock Numbers) captures a fixed initial condition—the baseline disposition to deviate from recommended livestock numbers. The constant nature of these values indicates that the simulations are treating initial risk attitude as a static experimental parameter to test how different baseline risk dispositions affect social-ecological system performance. The mean reserve biomass metric begins at approximately 90,000 units during the initial timestep, followed by a marginal increase to roughly 90,500-91,000 in the first 5 steps. Between steps 5 and 35, biomass undergoes a pronounced decline, reaching minimum values ranging from 80,000 to 88,000. From steps 70 to 100, the metric stabilizes into distinct plateau levels. The final stabilized values consistently exceed the initial conditions by 23% to 42%. The system demonstrates high vulnerability to initial overstocking, with livestock losses of 50% or more virtually certain within the first 20 periods. The time series data reveal a characteristic three-phase pattern in mean household destocking rates across the 100-step simulation horizon. The system exhibits a wide dispersion of outcomes spanning approximately 41 to 62 livestock units. The data show the diffusion of social learning behaviors across a population of 60 households over 100 time steps. At initial phase spanning approximately steps 0 to 20, all simulation runs exhibit synchronous rapid accumulation of mean household destock totals, rising uniformly from initial values of 0 to between 60 and 80 animals per household. The critical window for intervention occurs between steps 40 and 60, before divergence becomes entrenched.
