The time series data reveal a characteristic three-phase pattern in mean household destocking rates across the 100-step simulation horizon. During the initial phase (Steps 0-5), destocking surges rapidly from near-zero baseline values to pronounced peaks ranging between 6 and 8 animals per household, indicating acute resource scarcity as the initial livestock endowment (90 animals per household) exceeds immediate pasture carrying capacity. 

The second phase (Steps 6-40) exhibits a steep decline in destocking rates accompanied by high volatility. By Step 10, mean destocking decreases to the 2-4 animal range, with substantial between-simulation variation (coefficient of variation remaining high). This declining trajectory continues through Step 20, where values fluctuate between 0.5 and 2.5 animals, and by Step 40, most trajectories have descended below 1.0 animal per household. The volatility during this period reflects the model's learning dynamics, where E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types adjust stocking decisions through learning-by-doing and social learning processes.

In the third phase (Steps 41-100), the system stabilizes at low equilibrium destocking rates, with most converging to values between 0.0 and 0.8 animals per household. However, the data exhibit persistent heterogeneity in long-term outcomes: while the majority of simulation runs stabilize near zero destocking, a distinct subset (approximately 5-10% of runs) maintains elevated rates of 0.8-1.2 animals throughout the final 60 steps, suggesting that household-specific risk parameters (r-parameter) and initial conditions create divergent resilience outcomes even within the mixed learning population.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

Policy interventions should account for the predictable initial destocking crisis (6-8 animals per household) during the first 5 time steps, requiring emergency fodder support or destocking subsidies during system initialization or shock periods. The 40-step transition period to equilibrium suggests that grazing management policies require medium-term persistence before judging behavioral intervention effectiveness. Finally, the persistent long-tail of households maintaining higher destocking rates (0.8-1.2 animals) indicates that universal policy approaches may fail for high-risk-attitude households; targeted support for uncertainty-sensitive agents (high r-parameter households) remains necessary even after system-wide stabilization.
