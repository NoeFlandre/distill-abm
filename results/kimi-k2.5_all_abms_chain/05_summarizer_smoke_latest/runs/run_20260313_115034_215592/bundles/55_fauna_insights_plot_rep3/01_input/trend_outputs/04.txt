The cumulative count of female prey killed exhibits a three-phase temporal pattern across replicate simulations. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, accumulated female mortality rises rapidly from zero to values between 80 and 120. The most pronounced accumulation occurs within the first 200 steps, when cumulative kills reach 40–60 individuals. Certain simulation runs exhibit delayed onset of female hunting, with cumulative mortality remaining below 20 individuals during the initial 300 steps before accelerating.

In the transition phase between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of additional kills decelerates substantially across all runs, with cumulative counts increasing by only 10–25 individuals to reach approximately 110–120. By steps 1000–1500, the cumulative count stabilizes in most simulations, approaching asymptotic values ranging from 115 to 125. The near-complete cessation of additional mortality after this point indicates effective depletion of the female prey population. Despite temporal variation in hunting intensity—where some replicates exhibit slower initial accumulation—all runs converge toward terminal values exceeding 115 kills.

Interpreted within the model context, the terminal plateau near 120–125 individuals represents functional extinction of females from the initial prey population of 250. Given that processing costs for females (3 energy units) are substantially lower than for males (6 energy units) and nursing offspring increase female food value, hunters preferentially target females. The consistency of population collapse—with nearly all females killed within 1000–2000 steps (approximately 3–5 model years)—demonstrates that hunting pressure overwhelms prey reproductive capacity, particularly given the 730-tick (2-year) birth spacing interval.

**Implications for decision makers:** The model indicates that sex-selective hunting focused on energetically efficient females reliably drives prey populations to collapse rather than sustainable equilibrium. The rapid progression from initial hunting to effective extinction suggests that female-biased hunting represents a highly efficient pathway to megafaunal extinction. While stochastic factors affect the timing of collapse—with delayed hunting in some runs eventually converging to the same terminal outcome—the absence of equilibrium conditions implies no natural buffering mechanism against female-directed hunting pressure. Interventions that reduce hunter encounter rates with females—such as spatial refugia or behavioral adaptations that reduce female vulnerability—would be critical for preventing extinction, as the current parameterization demonstrates that immortal foragers with food storage capabilities will persist until the female population is virtually eliminated.
