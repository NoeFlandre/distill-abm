Total energy of male animals initiates at approximately 12,000 to 12,500 units across all simulation runs. During the initial phase spanning roughly steps 0 to 300, the metric undergoes a precipitous decline of approximately 60-75%, dropping to values ranging between 3,000 and 5,000. This rapid depletion continues at a moderated rate through approximately step 800, with values gradually descending toward zero.

Substantial stochastic variation characterizes the persistence of male energy across runs. While the majority of trajectories reach negligible levels (at or near zero) by step 800 to 1,000, a subset of simulations exhibits extended persistence. In these cases, trace amounts of male energy remain detectable until approximately step 1,500, with extreme outliers maintaining minimal energy levels until roughly step 2,200.

By step 1,500, virtually all simulation runs have converged to zero total energy for males, indicating complete depletion of energy reserves or functional extinction of the male subpopulation across all stochastic realizations.

Within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these dynamics indicate that the specified hunting pressure—with 20 initial foragers, male food value of 30, processing cost of 6, and optimal foraging decision logic—exceeds the demographic replacement capacity of male animals. The rapid initial decline corresponds to the establishment phase of hunting activity targeting males, who provide higher absolute food returns (despite net energy returns equal to females when processing costs are accounted for). The variation in extinction timing reflects stochastic elements in hunter movement patterns, pursuit success rates, and herd demographic processes, though all runs eventually converge to the same extinction outcome.

For decision makers utilizing this model to test extinction hypotheses, these results demonstrate that the current parameter configuration produces deterministic male extinction within approximately 2-3 years (given 365 ticks per year), with the most rapid collapses occurring within the first year. This suggests that hunting intensity may be too high to observe nuanced interactions between environmental change and hunting pressure, or that the sexual dimorphism parameters create excessive targeting of males. To buffering against extinction or establish equilibrium conditions, researchers might consider reducing initial forager density below 20, increasing the initial prey population above 250, decreasing interbirth intervals from the current 730 ticks (2 years), or reducing the food value differential between sexes to decrease hunting selectivity for males.
