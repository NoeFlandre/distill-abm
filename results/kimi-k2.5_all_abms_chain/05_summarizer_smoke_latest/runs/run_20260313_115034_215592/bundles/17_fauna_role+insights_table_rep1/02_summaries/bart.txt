Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change. Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances. Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remained constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation duration. The simulation reveals a pronounced and persistent decline in aggregate forager energy stores over the temporal sequence. The decline exhibits distinct phase characteristics punctuated by structural breakpoints at approximately ticks 650, 1075, 1500, and 1925. The trajectory indicates that forager populations exhaust initial energy reserves within the first several hundred ticks. The accumulation of male kills proceeds with steep, though decelerating, growth rates. The trajectory demonstrates that male-biased harvesting, while initially productive, depletes the sexually dimorphic subpopulation to functional extinction within approximately 5–6 simulated years (ticks 1800+) after which hunting effort reallocates to females and calves. The cumulative mortality of postpartum female ungulates initiates at zero and undergoes a protracted acceleration phase spanning approximately the first 850 ticks. During this initial period, mortality accumulates rapidly as the cumulative count rises from zero to approximately 100 individuals, reflecting intensive hunting pressure on reproductive females. The accumulation rate decelerates substantially, entering a plateau interval between ticks 1392 and 1623 where no significant trend is detected. The metric tracking prey mortality exhibits a complex four-phase trajectory across the 2,312-tick simulation horizon. During the initial phase spanning approximately ticks 0 to 1,160, the accumulated mortality count rises monotonically from 0 to roughly 237, though the rate of accumulation decelerates progressively from steep early growth (slopes exceeding 0.3 per tick in the first 500 ticks) to gradual increments near 0.02 per tick. The megaherbivore population exhibits a deterministic extinction trajectory over approximately 2,311 daily time steps (roughly 6.3 years), collapsing from an initial aggregate mean abundance of 166.7 individuals to complete extirpation. The decline follows a non-linear pattern characterized by distinct phases demarcated by structural change points at ticks 125, 475, 625, and 875, with final collapse occurring at the simulation terminus. The population reaches complete energetic extinction at tick 2,284, with all male energy reserves depleted. The distribution of energy values exhibits extreme right-skewness, with a mean of 1,984 contrasting sharply with a median of 134.5, indicating that the majority of the simulation duration operates at severely depleted energy levels. The trajectory of aggregate megaherbivore energy reserves exhibits a deterministic collapse pattern punctuated by distinct phases of deceleration and temporary stabilization. Conservation strategies must recognize that temporary stabilization in female energy stores does not indicate sustainable coexistence, but rather a delayed collapse mechanism. Despite strong seasonality driving significant intra-annual variation, grassland vegetation demonstrates robust resilience under current megaherbivore grazing and hunter foraging pressures, maintaining consistent baseline coverage over centennial timescales. Smaller decreasing trends emerge between time steps 1,624 and 2,088, representing phase adjustments within the stable oscillatory cycle rather than indicating long-term degradation.
