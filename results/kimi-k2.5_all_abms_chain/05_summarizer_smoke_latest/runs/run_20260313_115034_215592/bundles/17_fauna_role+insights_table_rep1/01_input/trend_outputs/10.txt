The megaherbivore population exhibits a deterministic extinction trajectory over approximately 2,311 daily time steps (roughly 6.3 years), collapsing from an initial aggregate mean abundance of 166.7 individuals to complete extirpation. The decline follows a non-linear pattern characterized by distinct phases demarcated by structural change points at ticks 125, 475, 625, and 875, with final collapse occurring at the simulation terminus.

During the initial phase (ticks 0–125), the population undergoes rapid attrition, losing approximately 25 percent of its initial abundance immediately upon simulation commencement as hunting pressure from 20 human foragers imposes mortality rates exceeding natural replacement capacity. This steep decline continues through ticks 475 and 625, exhibiting statistically significant monotonic decreases across consecutive temporal windows, though the rate of decline moderates slightly after the 475-tick mark.

Superimposed on this downward trajectory are persistent oscillations with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks (roughly bi-monthly cycles), manifesting as recurring peaks and troughs in population abundance. Early oscillations achieve peak values of approximately 11.4 individuals around tick 730, coinciding with the 730-tick (2-year) interbirth interval, while the amplitude of these fluctuations attenuates over time, with later peaks barely exceeding 2.3 individuals by tick 1,030. These oscillations likely reflect the interaction between high environmental seasonality (0.8 intensity), reproductive pulses, and optimal foraging decisions by hunters who target females (processing cost 3) over males (processing cost 6) due to lower handling costs, creating sex-biased mortality that disproportionately removes reproductive individuals.

Following tick 875, the population enters a phase of low-abundance volatility, fluctuating below five individuals. Temporary statistically significant increases occur during rolling windows spanning ticks 1,392–1,623 and 1,856–2,087, representing brief demographic rebounds potentially driven by reduced predation pressure at very low densities or stochastic reproductive events. However, these insufficient recoveries cannot overcome Allee effects or the depensatory mortality imposed by persistent hunting, ultimately yielding to irreversible collapse to zero individuals by tick 2,311.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The inevitability of extinction under these parameter combinations—specifically 20 immortal hunters with food storage capabilities operating in highly seasonal environments with 2-year birth intervals—indicates that current hunting pressures exceed sustainable thresholds. The presence of temporary population rebounds during reproductive peaks (particularly around year 2) creates a critical monitoring trap: short-term increases during these windows may signal false stability while masking the underlying deterministic decline toward extinction. Additionally, the sex-biased hunting preference for females due to lower processing costs accelerates population collapse by directly removing reproductive capital. Conservation strategies must prioritize immediate reduction of hunting pressure below levels where interbirth intervals can sustain population replacement, as the model demonstrates that even brief periods of population growth cannot compensate for continuous predation once abundance drops below approximately 10–20 individuals. Furthermore, the rapid initial decline within the first 125 ticks (approximately 4 months) suggests that intervention windows are narrow and delayed responses will likely fail to prevent extirpation.
