The male megaherbivore population initiates at 125 individuals and undergoes deterministic collapse to extinction by tick 2284 (approximately 6.3 years). The trajectory comprises three distinct demographic phases: rapid initial decline, protracted low-density persistence with transient oscillations, and terminal extinction.

During the first 875 ticks (2.4 years), the population experiences a steep monotonic decrease characterized by significant structural shifts at ticks 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 875, with the most precipitous declines occurring in the initial 463 ticks (slope -0.244). This rapid depletion reflects sustained hunting pressure from 20 human foragers operating under dynamic optimal foraging theory with energy storage capabilities and immortality, systematically removing males who offer higher absolute food value (30 units) despite equivalent net returns after processing costs.

Following the primary collapse, the population enters an extended low-density phase (ticks 875-2284) where abundance oscillates around a median of approximately 1.3 individuals. Transient demographic pulses occur around ticks 709-730 (year 2), 803 (year 2.2), and 893 (year 2.4), with peak abundances reaching 10.5, 8.8, and 6.7 individuals respectively, potentially corresponding to birth pulses following the 730-tick (2-year) interbirth interval. A temporary increasing trend emerges between ticks 1392-1623 (years 3.8-4.4), suggesting brief reproductive recovery, but the population subsequently fluctuates without directional trend until final extinction.

The oscillation pattern, with a dominant periodicity of 63 ticks, likely reflects the interaction between strong environmental seasonality (0.8 intensity) and hunter foraging behavior, creating regular survival bottlenecks. The sustained decline despite low population densities indicates that immortal hunters with food storage capabilities maintain constant per capita predation pressure, preventing density-dependent refuge and driving the population to complete extirpation rather than stable coexistence.

Implications for decision makers: The collapse of the male subpopulation within 2.4 years demonstrates that moderate hunter densities (20 individuals) can eliminate half the initial prey population (125 males from 250 total) before the first reproductive cycle completes, creating irreversible demographic skew. The 28-tick extinction debt period (2284-2312) at zero abundance confirms that recovery is impossible once males are extirpated, given the absence of density-dependent hunter release. Conservation interventions must occur within the first 500 ticks (1.4 years) to prevent the transition to the low-density phase where stochastic extinction becomes inevitable.
