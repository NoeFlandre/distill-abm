The average available green biomass across all patches begins at approximately 45,000 units. During the initial phase spanning the first 27 time steps, biomass undergoes a significant decline, reaching a minimum value of approximately 42,057 units at time step 27. This degradation phase is characterized by a statistically significant decreasing trend between time steps 11 and 21.

Following the minimum at time step 27, the system enters a transition phase lasting until approximately time step 40, during which biomass stabilizes and begins to recover. From time step 40 onwards, biomass enters a sustained period of increase that continues through the remainder of the simulation. The recovery phase exhibits distinct sub-periods: between time steps 33 and 43, biomass increases significantly; between time steps 44 and 54, the rate of increase accelerates substantially; and from time step 55 onwards, while the increasing trend continues, the rate of growth gradually decelerates.

By time step 100, the average biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units, representing an increase of over 34% compared to initial conditions and approximately 44% above the minimum observed at time step 27. Across the 30 simulation replications, final biomass values at time step 100 exhibit variability, ranging from approximately 55,000 to 64,000 units, with divergence among runs becoming pronounced particularly after time step 60.

In the context of the grazing system model, the initial decline in biomass suggests that initial grazing pressure exceeds sustainable levels, causing pasture degradation during the first 27 time steps. The subsequent recovery and long-term increase indicate that learning mechanisms—particularly the learning-by-doing processes in E-LBD agents and social learning processes in E-RO-SL1 agents—enable households to adapt their grazing strategies effectively. The system achieves biomass levels substantially exceeding initial conditions, suggesting that adaptive co-management through learning facilitates the transition from degraded to improved pasture states in private property grazing systems. The variability across simulation runs suggests that stochastic factors, including heterogeneity in the r-parameter for uncertainty management among the mixed population of 20 E-RO, 20 E-LBD, and 20 E-RO-SL1 agents, lead to divergent outcomes in pasture conditions despite identical initial conditions.
