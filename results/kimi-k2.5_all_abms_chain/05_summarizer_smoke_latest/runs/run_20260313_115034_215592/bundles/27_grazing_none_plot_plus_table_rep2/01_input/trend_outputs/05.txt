The simulation begins with an average livestock holding of 90 animals per household. Over the first 37 time steps, the average undergoes a steep monotonic decline, reaching a minimum value of approximately 41 animals. This represents a reduction of over 54% from the initial stocking level. The most rapid decrease occurs during the earliest phase (steps 0–20), after which the rate of decline moderates but continues until the minimum is reached at step 37.

Following this low point, the system enters a recovery phase. From step 37 to approximately step 60, the average livestock count increases significantly. However, the recovery trajectories diverge considerably across the 30 replicate simulations. While the aggregate mean increases to approximately 47.5 animals by step 100, individual simulation runs separate into distinct clusters. By the final time step, some trajectories stabilize at elevated levels between 60 and 62 animals, others plateau at intermediate levels around 47–50, and a third group remains at lower levels of approximately 40–45 animals.

This pattern indicates that the initial stocking level of 90 animals substantially exceeded the sustainable carrying capacity of the private pastures under the stochastic rainfall regime (mean 200, standard deviation 100), precipitating a system-wide crash. The minimum at step 37 represents a point of maximum destocking and pasture degradation. The subsequent divergence into multiple stable clusters suggests that the interaction between stochastic environmental variability and the heterogeneous learning behaviors (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) creates path-dependent outcomes, where the sequence of rainfall events and learning dynamics channel different simulation runs into distinct long-term equilibria ranging from approximately 40 to 62 animals per household.
