The number of male animals initiates at 125 individuals across all simulation runs. Over the course of the simulations, the population undergoes a steep, deterministic decline toward extinction, ultimately reaching zero in all observed instances, though the specific timing of final depletion varies substantially among individual runs.

During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 375 to 500 steps, the population decreases precipitously, with aggregate counts falling from 125 to below 10 individuals. By step 500, the majority of simulation runs have effectively reached zero or maintain only residual populations of fewer than 5 individuals. This rapid depletion reflects the constant hunting pressure exerted by 20 immortal foragers operating under optimal foraging constraints, targeting males due to their higher food value (30) relative to females (27).

Following this initial collapse, a minority of simulation runs exhibit extended persistence, with small residual populations maintaining low numbers (typically 1–5 individuals) through step 1000 and, in exceptional cases, persisting until approximately step 2200 before final extinction. This variation demonstrates the influence of demographic stochasticity and spatial heterogeneity in herd movement patterns, where certain herds temporarily avoid detection or experience fortuitous reproductive events aligned with the 730-tick (two-year) birth spacing interval.

The population trajectory exhibits a characteristic stepped descent rather than smooth decay, with statistical change points detected at regular intervals (125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 875 steps). These plateaus correspond to discrete predation events and reproductive cycles. Aggregate statistics across the 40 simulation runs reveal a mean population of approximately 21 individuals over time, with a median of roughly 1.3 and a standard deviation of 36, indicating a heavily right-skewed distribution where most runs spend the majority of their duration at near-extinction levels following the initial rapid decline. Rolling trend analysis confirms statistically significant monotonic decreases through step 1391, after which the population stabilizes near zero with no significant directional change.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

These results indicate that under conditions of immortal foragers with fixed hunting pressure and male-biased prey selection, extinction of the male subpopulation is inevitable regardless of seasonal grass regeneration or environmental stochasticity. The rapid initial decline (reaching near-zero within 500 steps) suggests that sustained hunting pressure quickly overwhelms the species' reproductive capacity, particularly given the two-year birth spacing interval. While demographic stochasticity may create temporary refugia that delay extinction in some scenarios—extending persistence up to 2,200 steps—such delays do not alter the ultimate outcome without intervention. For conservation and management strategies, this implies that sustainable populations require either: (1) mechanisms to introduce forager mortality or senescence to reduce hunting pressure over time, (2) restrictions on male-targeted hunting to alleviate the bias toward high-value prey, or (3) significantly shortened interbirth intervals or larger initial breeding pools to outpace the constant harvest rate.
