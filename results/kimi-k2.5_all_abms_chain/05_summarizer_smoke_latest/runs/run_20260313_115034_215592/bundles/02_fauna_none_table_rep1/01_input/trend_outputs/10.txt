The simulation exhibits a deterministic extinction trajectory spanning approximately 6.3 simulation years. The megaherbivore population undergoes catastrophic collapse during the initial two-year period, plummeting from approximately 167 individuals at initialization to fewer than 10 individuals by tick 700. This initial decline features distinct regime changes around ticks 125, 357, and 747, with the steepest rates of loss occurring between ticks 232 and 463.

Following the initial crash, the system enters a prolonged low-density phase characterized by extreme volatility. For the subsequent roughly 1,600 ticks, the population persists at critically low levels, oscillating with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks—corresponding to sub-annual seasonal cycles within the model's temporal framework. These oscillations manifest as periodic fluctuations between peaks of roughly 2 to 11 individuals and valleys approaching single-digit abundance, with amplitudes dampening progressively over time.

Superimposed upon this declining trend are two notable intervals of temporary demographic recovery. Between ticks 1392 and 1623, and again between 1856 and 2087, the population exhibits weak increasing trends that briefly interrupt the general collapse. However, these recoveries prove insufficient to reverse the long-term trajectory, with the decline resuming between these periods.

The terminal phase accelerates toward complete extirpation, with the final population loss occurring precisely at tick 2311. Throughout the simulation, the median population level remains near 1.5 individuals, reflecting the heavily skewed distribution toward the prolonged low-density phase preceding extinction.

Within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these dynamics illustrate the consequences of sustained predation pressure from 20 immortal foragers equipped with energy storage capabilities. The rapid initial collapse suggests that the foragers' optimal foraging strategies quickly deplete the initial population of 250 prey. The subsequent oscillatory pattern at low densities likely reflects interactions between the 2-year birth spacing intervals, seasonal grass regrowth cycles (operating at 80% seasonality), and the predators' persistent foraging efforts. Despite demographic buffering mechanisms including 20-year senescence onset and continuous grass regeneration, the immortal hunter population maintains unrelenting predation pressure that prevents stabilization at equilibrium densities, ultimately driving the megaherbivore population to irreversible extinction.
