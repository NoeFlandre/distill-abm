The cumulative mortality of ungulates initiates at zero and follows a sigmoidal accumulation pattern, ultimately reaching approximately 240 deaths by tick 1876 before stabilizing near 235 deaths at the conclusion of the observation window at tick 2312. 

The initial phase, spanning roughly the first 850 ticks, is characterized by accelerating accumulation rates that peak at approximately 0.44 deaths per tick between ticks 232 and 463, then progressively decelerate through marked change points at regular 125-tick intervals (125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850). This early rapid accumulation reflects the initial encounter dynamics between the founding population of 250 ungulates and 20 human foragers, where high prey density and naive prey behavior facilitate intensive harvest rates under the model's optimal foraging regime with sexual dimorphism in processing costs.

A transitional plateau emerges between ticks 1160 and 1391, during which the cumulative death count exhibits no significant directional trend. This hiatus likely represents a demographic equilibrium interval where hunting mortality is offset by recruitment from the 730-tick interbirth interval, moderated by strong seasonal forcing (0.8 intensity) and vegetation regrowth cycles. A secondary, moderate increase resumes between ticks 1392 and 1623, potentially corresponding to reproductive pulses entering vulnerable age classes or seasonal aggregation effects, before a second stationary phase occurs from ticks 1624 to 1855.

Beyond tick 1856, the trajectory exhibits a decreasing trend despite the cumulative nature of the count, declining from the maximum of approximately 240 to the terminal value of 235. This paradoxical late-phase reduction indicates selective ensemble attrition, wherein simulation runs experiencing extreme mortality (approaching total population collapse) terminate and exit the sample, leaving only replicates with lower cumulative mortality at the final observation points.

Superimposed throughout the entire 2312-tick sequence is a robust oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of 63 ticks, generating regular fluctuations in mortality accumulation. These cycles align with seasonal resource pulses on the 365-tick annual calendar, creating approximately six vulnerability peaks per year as vegetation availability fluctuates and forager success rates respond to prey aggregation and energy state dynamics.
