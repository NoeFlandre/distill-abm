The male ungulate population initiated at 125 individuals and exhibited a deterministic trajectory toward extinction across all simulation replicates, with effective elimination occurring at approximately tick 2,284 (roughly 6.3 years when calibrated to 365 ticks per year). The temporal dynamics followed a non-linear decline characterized by distinct phases: an initial rapid collapse from 125 to approximately 20–40 individuals within the first 500 ticks, followed by a protracted decay phase where values fluctuated between 0 and 20 until final extinction.

Statistical analysis reveals a highly right-skewed distribution of population sizes over the observation window (mean 21.03, median 1.35, standard deviation 36.04), indicating that while early observations include high initial densities, the bulk of the temporal record clusters near zero. Mann-Kendall trend tests confirm statistically significant monotonic decreases throughout the first 1,391 ticks, with the steepest decline rates occurring in the earliest intervals (slopes of −0.149 to −0.244 in windows 0–696), moderating to −0.002 by ticks 1,160–1,391. Between ticks 1,392 and 1,623, a transient period of slight increase appears (slope +0.001), after which the population stabilizes at near-zero levels with no significant directional trend in the final observation windows.

The trajectory exhibits oscillatory components with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks (roughly bimonthly cycles), consistent with the strong seasonality parameter (0.8) driving pulsed resource availability. Change point analysis identifies structural shifts in the rate of decline at ticks 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 875, suggesting discrete transition points in population viability, with a final change point at tick 2,310 marking the terminal approach to extinction. The presence of 18 distinct inflection points throughout the series indicates repeated alterations between concave and convex decline patterns, likely reflecting seasonal bottlenecks in vegetation availability (regrowth time of 3 ticks) interacting with hunting pressure.

Interpreted within the model context, the deterministic collapse of the male subpopulation under 20 human foragers reflects the combined pressures of direct predation—where males represent higher nutritional value (30 versus 27 for females) despite greater processing costs (6 versus 3)—and environmental constraints governed by seasonal resource scarcity and limited initial vegetation cover (grass proportion 0.33). The high initial rate of decline (exceeding 0.1 individuals per tick initially) suggests that the starting density of 125 males exceeds the sustainable threshold under the specified hunting pressure and energetic constraints. The eventual convergence to zero across all 40 replicates, despite variability in the timing of intermediate peaks and valleys, indicates that the population structure is not viable under the current parameterization of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model.
