Across all simulation runs, the total prey population exhibits a consistent pattern of decline from the initial condition of 250 animals toward eventual extinction. The trajectories demonstrate high variability in the rate of population decrease and the timing of final extinction events, with some populations collapsing to near-zero levels within approximately 400 to 600 steps, while others persist with small numbers of individuals until between steps 1,500 and 2,200.

During the initial phase, populations undergo rapid decline, typically losing 60-80% of individuals within the first 300-500 steps. This steep decrease reflects the immediate impact of hunting pressure from the 20 immortal foragers, whose constant presence and energy-optimal pursuit decisions continuously remove individuals from the prey population. The decline rate slows as populations drop below 50 animals, with many runs showing irregular, step-like decreases interspersed with brief plateaus, suggesting periods where small remnant herds temporarily avoid encounters with hunters or exploit limited grass resources.

Stochastic effects are pronounced throughout the extinction process. While the majority of simulations reach fewer than 10 animals by step 800, a subset maintains small populations of 5-15 individuals for extended durations, indicating that in some replicates, herds successfully exploit spatial refugia or demographic stochasticity temporarily buffers against complete extinction. However, no simulation maintains a stable population above zero; all trajectories terminate at or near extinction (0-3 animals) by the conclusion of the recorded steps.

This universal pattern of delayed but inevitable extinction suggests that under the specified parameter configuration—including the combination of 20 immortal hunters, birth spacing of 730 ticks, and the specified food values and processing costs—the per-capita hunting mortality exceeds the maximum potential recruitment rate of the prey population. The extended persistence tail in some runs highlights the role of spatial heterogeneity and demographic stochasticity in prolonging extinction events, even when the underlying deterministic trend is toward population elimination.
