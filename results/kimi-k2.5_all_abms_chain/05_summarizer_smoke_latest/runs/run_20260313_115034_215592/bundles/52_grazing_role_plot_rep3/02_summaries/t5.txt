Model focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making. The average initial risk attitude of social learning agents remains constant at fixed values throughout the 100-timestep simulation period, exhibiting no temporal variation from timestep 0 to timestep 100. The metric maintains constant values distributed across the range of approximately -0.11 to +0.18, with individual simulation runs preserving specific values such as -0.21, 0.09, 0.06, 0.04, 0.00, +0.07, +0.28, and +0.18 without deviation over time. From step 60 onward, the metric enters a plateau phase where growth decelerates and stabilizes. By step 100, the simulations diverge into distinct equilibrium levels spanning a range from approximately 111,000 to 128,000 units. This divergence results in a stratification of final biomass states, with different simulation runs settling at specific stable values. By approximately step 20, values have fallen to a range between 0.5 and 2.5 animals per household. This phase indicates active adaptation as households respond to negative feedback from degraded pastures and reduce herd sizes to more sustainable levels. A subset of trajectories, comprising roughly one-fifth of the runs, shows continuous steep accumulation throughout the entire 100-step period, reaching final destock totals of 140 to 175 animals per household. Households engage in repeated cycles of acquiring and subsequently destocking animals in response to variable rainfall conditions and learning experiences.
