**Context and Goals**

This agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Unlike the original RAGE model which examined common property scenarios where herders move livestock across shared rangelands, this extension focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods.

The simulation tracks the interaction between vegetation condition and household decisions about livestock numbers over time. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors.

The central purpose is understanding how these different ways of adapting to changing conditions influence pasture health, herd sizes, and the persistence of households. The model particularly examines how farmers handle uncertainty and incomplete information when making stocking decisions, represented through individual risk dispositions that affect how they perceive and react to environmental feedback.

**Key Parameters**

*Population and Spatial Setup:*
The **number-households** parameter establishes how many farming families occupy the landscape, which can range from 1 to 100 families, with this study using 60 households. Each household begins with **livestock-init** set at 90 animals. The simulation runs for **timesteps** set at 100 years. Farmers begin the simulation positioned on their designated land parcels, with **start-on-homepatch?** set to True. Each farmer can observe conditions within a **knowledge-radius** that ranges from 0 to 5 patches, set here at 1 patch distance.

*Behavioral Type Configuration:*
The study examines three specific learning strategies with equal representation: **number-E-RO** (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb), **number-E-LBD** (learning-by-doing), and **number-E-RO-SL1** (social learning with single-loop adaptation), each set to 20 households. The **homog-behav-types?** parameter set to True ensures each type is evaluated in uniform groups. The original RAGE model behavioral types are inactive in this extension: **number-SAT**, **number-TRAD**, **number-MAX**, and **number-random** are all set to 0. The default reference **behavioral-type** is set to E-RO.

*Grazing Management Practices:*
Formal pasture resting is disabled with **resting?** set to False. The threshold for initiating rest periods, **resting-threshold**, ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0.1. The **intrinsic-preference** for livestock relative to other values ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0. Similarly, **social-susceptibility** to neighbor influence ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0, indicating decisions rely on other factors.

*Original Model Parameters:*
Several parameters from the foundational RAGE model are retained. The **satisficing-threshold** for satisfied decision-making ranges from 0 to 100 and is set at 0. The **satisficing-trials** parameter, ranging from 0 to 100, is set at 21. These control how the original satisficer type would search for acceptable solutions, though this type is not active in the current setup.

*Ecological Conditions:*
Rainfall is applied uniformly across the landscape with **global-rain?** set to True. The average rainfall **rain-mean** is set at 200 units annually. Year-to-year variation in precipitation is controlled by **rain-std** set at 100, indicating significant variability in annual rainfall totals. Vegetation growth responds through two rates: **gr1** set at 0.5 and **gr2** set at 0.1. The parameter **w** from the original vegetation model is set at 0.8, influencing how pastures recover from grazing. Livestock reproduction is not active in this extension, with **b** set to 0.

*Learning and Risk Mechanisms:*
The extension module is activated with **extension-model?** set to True. Uncertainty handling is engaged with **risk-mode?** set to True. Each agent begins with an initial risk disposition **risk-att-init** set at 0.5, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance. The ability to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False, confining agents to single-loop learning within their assigned type.

The average initial risk attitude among social learning agents remains fixed at approximately 0.0074 throughout the entire 100-year simulation period, showing no variation from the initial time step to the final observation. This constancy indicates that initial risk disposition is treated as a static attribute within the model, representing an unchanging baseline propensity of farmers to deviate from recommended livestock stocking rates. The metric preserves its initial value without fluctuation because it captures starting conditions rather than dynamically evolving risk perceptions.

Insights for decision makers: The stability of initial risk attitudes suggests that observed behavioral adaptations in stocking decisions arise from learning mechanisms and environmental feedback rather than shifts in fundamental risk predispositions. Policy instruments aimed at modifying grazing behavior should recognize that baseline risk tolerances remain constant over time, emphasizing the importance of fostering effective learning processes rather than attempting to alter inherent risk profiles.

The average deviation from recommended livestock levels begins at approximately 0.007, indicating that farmers initially operate very close to recommended stocking rates. During the first decade, this deviation increases significantly as farmers begin adjusting their strategies in response to local pasture conditions and rainfall variability.

Between years 11 and 32, the deviation continues to climb rapidly, reaching its maximum value of approximately 0.38 around year 33. This peak represents the point of greatest divergence from recommended stocking levels across the farming population, suggesting a period of intensive experimentation or stress-induced adjustment as farmers respond to variable ecological conditions without the benefit of formal pasture resting mechanisms.

Following this peak, the deviation enters a sustained decline from year 33 through year 65, decreasing steadily as farmers' learning processes converge toward more stable practices. The rate of decrease is steepest in the immediate post-peak period and continues through the subsequent decades, though the decline gradually moderates over time.

After year 65, the deviation stabilizes around 0.19 for the remainder of the simulation, maintaining this level through year 100 with no significant directional trend. This suggests that while single-loop learning strategies reduce initial extreme deviations, a persistent gap of approximately 0.19 remains between actual and recommended stocking levels, possibly reflecting inherent uncertainty, risk aversion among farmers, or the limitations of learning approaches that cannot switch fundamental strategies.

The system exhibits cyclical behavior with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, indicating that long-term oscillations in deviation patterns may be linked to vegetation recovery cycles or extended rainfall patterns. This cyclicality suggests that even as farmers learn and adapt, they remain subject to ecological feedback loops that operate across multi-decadal timescales, creating recurring patterns of deviation from recommendations.

**Implications for decision makers:** The trajectory suggests that initial learning periods may involve significant deviation from recommended stocking rates as farmers experiment with different strategies, but adaptive learning eventually reduces these deviations by approximately 50% from peak levels. However, the persistent residual deviation indicates that relying solely on individual and social learning without formal pasture resting mechanisms or the ability to fundamentally switch strategies may leave farmers systematically deviating from optimal stocking rates. The 49-year cyclical pattern suggests that policy interventions should account for long-term ecological oscillations rather than expecting linear improvements in management outcomes, and that support systems should be designed to accommodate periodic fluctuations in pasture productivity.

The average reserve biomass across private pastures commences at approximately 90,000 units and exhibits minor fluctuations during the initial triennium, peaking briefly around year 3 at roughly 90,500 units before entering a sustained decline. This downward trajectory continues through year 26, when reserve biomass reaches its minimum at approximately 84,100 units. This initial depletion suggests that the initial stocking rate of 90 animals per household places excessive pressure on the vegetation, overwhelming the regenerative capacity of pastures characterized by growth rates of 0.5 and 0.1 under variable rainfall conditions with significant annual standard deviation.

Subsequent to the nadir at year 26, the system transitions toward recovery, with biomass stabilizing during the late twenties and early thirties before embarking on a pronounced upward trend from approximately year 33 onward. The recovery accelerates substantially through the middle decades, with the most rapid accumulation occurring between years 44 and 54, after which the rate of increase gradually decelerates through the remainder of the simulation. By year 100, reserve biomass climbs to approximately 121,000 units, substantially exceeding initial conditions and indicating that the diverse learning strategies—including experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—successfully facilitate adjustment of stocking densities to sustainable levels.

The trajectory exhibits oscillatory patterns concentrated in two intervals: during the initial decline phase between years 10 and 20, and again during the early recovery period spanning years 41 to 49. These fluctuations likely reflect the interaction between significant annual rainfall variability and the households' risk dispositions of 0.5, as farmers within the knowledge radius of one patch adjust livestock numbers in response to observed pasture conditions and neighbor behaviors.

For decision makers, the 26-year period of biomass decline underscores the vulnerability of newly established private pasture systems to initial overstocking, suggesting that temporary grazing limits or conservative initial allocations may prevent ecological degradation during the learning phase. The eventual recovery and overshoot of initial biomass levels demonstrates that adaptive learning mechanisms can effectively reconcile livestock production with pasture health, though the multi-decadal timescale required to approach equilibrium indicates that policy evaluations must incorporate long-term horizons to accurately assess sustainability outcomes in smallholder grazing systems.

The average available green biomass across all pasture patches begins at approximately 45,000 units and follows a trajectory of initial decline followed by sustained long-term recovery, ultimately reaching approximately 60,535 units by the conclusion of the 100-year period.

In the first decade, the biomass remains relatively stable near initial levels before entering a significant decreasing trend. This decline intensifies through approximately year 27, when the system reaches its minimum value of roughly 42,057 units. This early degradation likely reflects the initial impact of grazing pressure as the 60 farming households, managing herds starting at 90 animals each, apply stock densities that initially exceed sustainable carrying capacities before learning mechanisms activate. The depletion during this phase suggests that without immediate adjustment, fixed rule-of-thumb strategies may drive pastures toward degradation before experiential feedback manifests.

Following this nadir at year 27, the system enters a brief stabilization period lasting until approximately year 32, after which a marked recovery begins. From year 33 onward, the biomass exhibits a consistently increasing trend that persists for the remainder of the simulation. Notably, the rate of recovery varies across this growth phase: the period between years 33 and 50 shows strong increases, with a particularly sharp rise occurring around years 44 to 54, corresponding to a change point identified at year 50. This acceleration suggests that learning-by-doing and social learning strategies have by this time effectively propagated through the farming community, enabling households to calibrate stocking rates against observed pasture conditions and neighbor outcomes. The growth rate gradually decelerates in subsequent decades (years 55-100), indicating the system is approaching a new, higher equilibrium state as grazing pressure and vegetation recovery reach balance under adaptive management.

The 49-year oscillation pattern evident in the dynamics suggests that households engage in periodic adjustments of livestock numbers as they respond to cyclic variations in rainfall and vegetation feedback, creating gentle fluctuations atop the overall recovery trend.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The trajectory reveals that adaptive learning strategies require approximately three decades to transform degraded pastures into recovering systems, but eventually generate biomass levels 34% above initial conditions and 44% above the degradation minimum. This suggests that policy interventions supporting learning-by-doing and social learning among smallholders can yield substantial long-term ecological benefits, though managers should anticipate significant lag periods before measurable improvements emerge. Early support mechanisms may be necessary to sustain farmer livelihoods during the initial 27-year period of biomass decline while learning systems mature.

The average livestock holdings per household begin at 90 animals and undergo a pronounced decline throughout the first 37 years of the simulation, after which the trajectory gradually reverses toward a modest recovery. During the initial decade, the system experiences a rapid reduction in animal numbers, dropping steeply as farmers are forced to destock in response to deteriorating pasture conditions. This decline continues sharply through approximately year 20, by which point holdings have fallen by nearly half from their initial level. Between years 20 and 37, the rate of decrease slows considerably, eventually reaching a minimum average of approximately 41 animals per household around year 37. This minimum represents the nadir of the crisis, where vegetation degradation has severely constrained the available forage base.

Following this trough, the system enters a prolonged stabilization and recovery phase. From roughly year 44 onward, average livestock numbers exhibit a consistent upward trend, though the rate of recovery varies significantly over time. The most substantial gains occur between years 44 and 55, after which the growth rate attenuates progressively through the remainder of the century. By year 100, holdings stabilize at approximately 47.5 animals per household—substantially below the initial endowment but above the crisis minimum. The system exhibits cyclical adjustments with a characteristic period of approximately 49 years, suggesting that feedback mechanisms between vegetation recovery and stocking decisions generate long-term oscillations around the declining trend.

These dynamics reveal that initial stocking levels exceeded the sustainable carrying capacity of the private pastures, triggering ecological degradation that necessitated severe destocking. The subsequent recovery demonstrates that learning-by-doing and social learning strategies enable farmers to adapt their management practices, gradually rebuilding herds once pasture conditions begin to improve. However, the failure to return to initial stocking levels suggests either permanent ecological degradation or the adoption of more conservative, risk-averse strategies that prioritize system stability over maximum productivity.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The trajectory indicates that initial stocking rates were approximately double the long-term sustainable carrying capacity, highlighting the critical need for conservative initial stocking guidelines or early warning systems to prevent severe destocking crises. The 60-year recovery period from the crisis minimum to stabilization underscores the long time horizons required for pasture rehabilitation under private management, suggesting that short-term productivity metrics may undermine long-term livelihood security. Finally, the partial recovery demonstrates that while experiential and social learning can prevent total system collapse, they cannot fully compensate for initial overstocking, implying that policy interventions should focus on preventing the degradation cycle rather than managing its aftermath.

The average number of livestock destocked per household initiates at zero but surges to approximately 6.6 animals by year 3, indicating severe resource scarcity during the initial adaptation phase. This early spike reflects the immediate pressure on vegetation when 60 households commence with 90 animals each on fixed private parcels under highly variable rainfall conditions, forcing substantial emergency removals before learning mechanisms can effectively calibrate stocking rates to local carrying capacity.

Following this initial crisis, destocking rates exhibit a pronounced declining trend through year 32, dropping from the peak of 6.6 animals to roughly 0.8 animals by year 23, with notable secondary crests at year 9 (3.4 animals) and year 12 (2.7 animals) corresponding to successive poor rainfall periods. This reduction suggests that regardless of whether households employ baseline rule-of-thumb heuristics, experiential learning-by-doing, or social observation of neighbors within the one-patch knowledge radius, all three strategies progressively adjust expectations and reduce livestock holdings to levels better aligned with observed pasture feedback, thereby diminishing the frequency of forced animal removal.

Between years 33 and 65, the system enters a stabilization phase characterized by low-magnitude oscillations between approximately 0.5 and 1.0 animals destocked annually, with no significant directional trends during intervals such as years 33-43 and 55-65. This plateau indicates that surviving households have largely converged on sustainable equilibrium stocking densities for their private parcels, though continued environmental variability necessitates occasional small-scale destocking as vegetation recovery rates (governed by gr1=0.5 and gr2=0.1) fluctuate with unpredictable precipitation.

A statistically significant increasing trend emerges between years 66 and 76, with destocking rates rising before subsiding to approximately 0.3 animals by year 100. This late-period fluctuation likely reflects the cumulative impact of extended rainfall variability interacting with the constraints of single-loop learning, wherein agents adjust tactics within fixed strategies but cannot switch fundamental behavioral types. The final minimal destocking rate suggests that by century's end, the combined learning processes have enabled most households to establish resilient herd sizes that accommodate inherent environmental uncertainty without requiring substantial livestock removal.

For decision makers, these dynamics indicate that initial stocking recommendations of 90 animals significantly exceed sustainable carrying capacity under variable rainfall conditions, necessitating substantial early destocking regardless of learning approach. The convergence toward low destocking rates demonstrates that stationary private property regimes can achieve sustainable equilibrium through adaptive learning, though the stabilization process requires approximately three decades. The late-period increase in destocking risk suggests that long-term climate variability may periodically challenge even well-adapted systems, warranting contingency provisions for extended drought cycles beyond the typical adaptation timeline.

The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households begins at effectively zero, reflecting the uniform initial endowment of 90 animals per household. Over the 100-year simulation, this metric rises monotonically to approximately 0.073, indicating a gradual but persistent trend toward uneven distribution of livestock wealth, though remaining within a low-inequality range (Gini coefficient below 0.1).

The trajectory exhibits several distinct phases of accumulation. During the initial approximately 25 years, inequality accumulates most rapidly, marked by inflection points at years 8, 13, 15, 20, and 25. This early divergence likely corresponds to the initial differentiation in household fortunes as the three learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—begin responding heterogeneously to spatial vegetation variation and annual rainfall fluctuations within the knowledge radius of one patch.

Between years 25 and 50, the rate of inequality growth moderates, with an inflection point at year 37 indicating an adjustment in the accumulation pace. A significant transition occurs around year 51 (change point), marking a shift to a new dynamic regime where inequality continues to increase but through different underlying processes. A second change point at year 71 indicates another structural adjustment, after which the metric progresses toward its final value of 0.0726 with superimposed oscillatory patterns (dominant period approximately 49 years) reflecting cyclical environmental variations interacting with adaptive stocking decisions.

The consistent, statistically significant increasing trend across all analysis windows (years 1–100) suggests that once divergence begins, it accumulates irreversibly under the current parameterization. The relatively modest final inequality level, despite significant rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200) and divergent learning strategies, suggests that private property arrangements with single-loop learning adaptation provide inherent constraints against extreme wealth concentration, even as they permit stratification based on differential adaptive capacity and local ecological conditions.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that promoting diverse learning strategies among farmers naturally produces some stratification in livestock holdings over time, but the system exhibits structural resilience against severe inequality. Interventions aimed at maintaining distributional equity—such as knowledge-sharing mechanisms or early warning systems—would likely yield greatest impact if implemented within the first 50 years before structural inequality patterns solidify at the identified change points. The persistence of low final inequality despite high environmental variability suggests that private property frameworks combined with adaptive learning can support both ecological sustainability and reasonable distributional outcomes in grazing systems.

The number of households engaging in social learning begins at zero and follows a logistic diffusion trajectory, asymptotically approaching approximately 32 households by the 63rd year and maintaining this level through the end of the simulation period.

During the initial decade, social learning propagates rapidly through the farming community, accumulating roughly 14 households by year 10 at a rate exceeding one new household per year. This vigorous uptake continues through approximately year 21, bringing the total to roughly 24 households—already surpassing the 20 households initially designated as the dedicated social learning type. This indicates that the practice spills over to approximately 4 households from other behavioral categories during this early phase.

A marked deceleration occurs around year 22, with adoption rates dropping substantially during the subsequent two decades. By year 40, the system exhibits a distinct phase transition as the diffusion process nears saturation, with the accumulation of new social learners slowing to a crawl. Between years 55 and 65, the curve flattens almost completely, reaching its maximum of approximately 32 households—representing roughly 53% of the total farming population—by year 63.

The stabilization at approximately 32 households suggests that while social learning proves attractive enough to draw in about 12 additional households beyond the initial social learning cohort, a substantial equilibrium minority of roughly 28 households maintains alternative decision-making strategies. This saturation implies heterogeneous suitability of social learning across different farm contexts, risk dispositions, or spatial positions within the knowledge radius, rather than a universal optimal strategy.

For decision makers, the natural diffusion ceiling at roughly half the population indicates that universal adoption of social learning should not be expected even over extended timeframes, and that complementary support strategies may be necessary for the remaining households. The persistence of the plateau from year 63 onward suggests that social learning, once adopted, becomes a stable behavioral fixture that persists without continuous external intervention, implying that temporary investments in establishing information-sharing networks may yield sustained long-term benefits for grazing management. However, the hard equilibrium at 32 households highlights the presence of structural or preference-based barriers that may require targeted outreach strategies beyond standard peer-to-peer diffusion mechanisms to reach holdout populations.

This persistent decline throughout the majority of the simulation suggests that static rule-of-thumb approaches are unsustainable for a significant portion of farmers facing stochastic environmental conditions.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that purely experiential strategies lacking active learning mechanisms pose substantial risks to long-term household persistence in variable grazing systems. The pronounced vulnerability of non-learning agents during the first two decades suggests that interventions promoting adaptive capacity—whether through individual learning-by-doing or social observation—may be critical for preventing early household exit.
