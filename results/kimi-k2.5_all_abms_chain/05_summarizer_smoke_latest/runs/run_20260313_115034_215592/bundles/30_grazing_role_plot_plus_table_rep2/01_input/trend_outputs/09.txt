The average cumulative number of destocked livestock initiates at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation, terminating at approximately 96.8 animals per household with a temporal mean accumulation of 75.5 animals. The trajectory exhibits a pronounced deceleration pattern with notable inflection occurring between years 45 and 62, dividing the simulation into distinct phases of adjustment.

During the initial establishment phase from year 0 to 25, households destock rapidly at rates declining from approximately 4.96 animals annually in the first decade to 0.69 animals annually by year 32, accumulating roughly 65-70 destocked animals as they adjust initial stocking levels to pasture conditions. The change point at year 25 marks a transition to a sustained moderate phase lasting until year 60, where accumulation continues at reduced rates of approximately 0.32-0.36 animals annually, bringing the cumulative total to roughly 85 animals.

In the final phase from year 60 to 100, destocking persists at a steady slow rate of approximately 0.31 animals annually, with multiple inflection points between years 67 and 75 indicating continued gradual flattening of the accumulation curve. Despite the aggregate slowing trend, individual simulation trajectories diverge substantially by year 100, ranging from approximately 58 animals to over 170 animals, reflecting heterogeneous outcomes across the farming population.

The distribution of values is left-skewed with a median of 81.2 animals exceeding the mean, consistent with the concave trajectory spending proportionally more time at higher accumulation levels. Statistical confirmation of monotonic increase appears across all nine analyzed temporal windows, with highly significant positive trends throughout, indicating that destocking pressure remains continuous across all learning strategies and rainfall scenarios, though the decelerating rates suggest progressive adaptation toward maintenance stocking levels rather than ongoing herd liquidation.
