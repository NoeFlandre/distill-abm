This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. The mean reserve biomass across all patches initiates at approximately 90,000 units. During the first 26 time steps, the system undergoes a marked decline. During this adjustment period, average overstocking declines from 0.38 to approximately 0.19, as households likely respond to ecological feedbacks, resource scarcity, or social learning about sustainable practices. Policy interventions should target the early simulation phase (years 0-33) to prevent the escalation of over-stocking. At step 27, the system transitions into a recovery phase characterized by accelerating biomass accumulation. After step 60, the rate of biomass increase gradually decelerates, though remaining positive through the conclusion of the simulation at step 100. The pronounced minimum at step 27 represents a critical ecological threshold where grazing pressure temporarily exceeds sustainable levels before behavioral adaptations and vegetation feedbacks restore system productivity. The trajectory of inequality accumulation follows a non-linear pattern characterized by distinct phases of acceleration. The trajectory reveals high variability across simulation runs during the volatile first 20 steps, gradually converging toward low variance as households equilibrate. The persistence of low-level destocking through year 100 indicates that even adapted systems remain vulnerable to stochastic events. The adoption dynamics feature discrete, step-like transitions rather than perfectly smooth growth, evidenced by inflection points at steps 28, 30, 33, and again at 57 and 60. For decision makers, social learning interventions in private grazing systems can expect rapid initial uptake, with the majority of adoption occurring within the first 40 time steps. The population of households employing the non-learning E-RO behavioral strategy initializes at 20 households. During the first approximately 10 time steps, this population exhibits a modest increase, reaching a maximum value of roughly 21 households around step 8. The most rapid reduction occurs between steps 11 and 21, where the population decreases steeply from its peak toward approximately 17 households. Following this sharp drop, a more gradual but steady decline continues through step 65.
