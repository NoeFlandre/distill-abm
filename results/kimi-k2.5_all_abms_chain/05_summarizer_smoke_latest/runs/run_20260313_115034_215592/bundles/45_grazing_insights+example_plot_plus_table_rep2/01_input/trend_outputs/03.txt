The mean reserve biomass across all patches initiates at approximately 90,000 units. During the first 26 time steps, the system undergoes a marked decline, reaching a minimum value of approximately 84,115 units. This initial drawdown reflects the pressure exerted by the initial livestock endowment of 90 animals per household across the 60 households, as grazing demand temporarily exceeds the regenerative capacity of the private pasture patches, depleting the reserve biomass accumulated prior to the simulation start.

Following the trough at step 26, the system transitions into a sustained recovery and growth phase that persists through the remainder of the 100-step simulation. Between approximately steps 33 and 65, the reserve biomass increases rapidly, with particularly pronounced growth occurring between steps 44 and 54 where the accumulation rate reaches its maximum slope of approximately 1,909 units per step. This recovery trajectory suggests that the learning mechanisms embedded in the heterogeneous agent population—comprising E-RO (baseline), E-LBD (learning-by-doing), and E-RO-SL1 (social learning) behavioral types—begin to effectively adapt grazing strategies to local ecological conditions, allowing vegetation recovery while maintaining livestock herds.

After step 65, the rate of biomass accumulation gradually decelerates, though the upward trend continues until the final time step. By step 100, the mean reserve biomass reaches approximately 121,073 units, representing a 34% increase above the initial value and a 44% increase from the minimum observed at step 26. The slowing growth rate in the final decades, with slopes decreasing from approximately 643 units per step (steps 55-65) to roughly 5 units per step (steps 88-98), suggests the system is asymptotically approaching a new equilibrium state where the adaptive management strategies of the three behavioral types have stabilized in relation to the carrying capacity of the private pastures under the prevailing climatic conditions.

The oscillation patterns detected in the series, with a dominant period of approximately 49 steps and clustered oscillation indices around steps 10-20 and 41-49, indicate the presence of cyclical dynamics likely corresponding to annual vegetation growth cycles or periodic strategy adjustments by the learning agents. The change point detected at step 50 marks the transition from the initial adjustment period to the sustained growth phase.

For decision makers, these results demonstrate that private property grazing systems with heterogeneous learning capabilities can not only recover from initial overstocking pressures but can achieve substantially improved ecological conditions compared to initial states. The critical transition at step 26 highlights the importance of maintaining system resilience during the initial adaptation period, as this represents the point of maximum ecological vulnerability. The sustained growth thereafter indicates that investments in learning mechanisms—both individual learning-by-doing and social learning—can yield significant long-term ecological benefits in smallholder grazing systems, with the system approaching a higher biomass equilibrium that suggests improved pasture health and potentially greater long-term livestock carrying capacity.
