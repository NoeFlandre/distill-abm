The total energy available in the male animal population begins at 12,500 units and declines progressively toward zero over the course of approximately 2,300 simulation steps, exhibiting distinct phases of depletion marked by changing rates of decline and superimposed periodic oscillations.

**Phase 1: Rapid Decline (Steps 0–350)**
The initial phase is characterized by a precipitous decrease, with the population losing energy at a rate of roughly 14.8 to 28.1 units per step during the first 463 steps. This rapid depletion reduces the total male energy from the initial 12,500 to approximately 4,000 by step 377, representing a 68% decline. This steep trajectory suggests immediate and intensive hunting pressure on male herds, likely driven by their higher relative food value compared to females (30 versus 27) despite their higher processing cost.

**Phase 2: Decelerating Decline (Steps 350–875)**
The rate of decline moderates significantly during this intermediate period, with the depletion rate slowing from approximately 10.7 units per step to 2.8 units per step, and eventually to 0.8 units per step by step 1,159. The total energy drops from roughly 4,000 to below 1,000, indicating that while hunting pressure continues, the reduced population density and smaller herd sizes result in slower absolute energy extraction from the male population.

**Phase 3: Terminal Depletion (Steps 875–2,284)**
The final phase shows the total energy approaching zero asymptotically, with complete depletion occurring by step 2,284. During this period, the decline rate becomes negligible, with slopes approaching zero as the remaining male animals are hunted to extinction. The median energy value across all simulations falls to 134.5, reflecting that by the aggregate midpoint, most replicates have reduced male energy to minimal levels. Late-stage windows showing slight increasing trends likely represent temporary persistence of small remnant populations or demographic stochasticity in isolated herds before final extinction.

**Variability and Cyclical Patterns**
Throughout the decline, the system exhibits high variance across the 40 simulation runs (standard deviation of 3,478), indicating substantial heterogeneity in extinction trajectories. Superimposed on the downward trend are periodic oscillations with a dominant cycle of approximately 63 steps, consistent with the model's seasonal parameters (seasonality set to 0.8 with 365 ticks per year), suggesting that energy levels fluctuate seasonally even as the overall population declines toward extinction.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The multi-phase decline pattern indicates that male megafauna populations under sustained hunting pressure experience an initial rapid collapse followed by a prolonged terminal decline, suggesting that management interventions would be most effective if implemented early before the population falls below critical energy thresholds. The high variance between simulation runs implies that extinction outcomes are sensitive to stochastic demographic and hunting events, meaning extinction can occur within a wide time window even under identical initial conditions. The presence of persistent seasonal oscillations throughout the decline suggests that hunting restrictions during critical seasonal bottlenecks could potentially extend population persistence, though the eventual convergence to zero indicates that without fundamental reduction in hunting pressure or enhancement of population resilience, male megafauna extinction remains the deterministic outcome under these parameter settings.
