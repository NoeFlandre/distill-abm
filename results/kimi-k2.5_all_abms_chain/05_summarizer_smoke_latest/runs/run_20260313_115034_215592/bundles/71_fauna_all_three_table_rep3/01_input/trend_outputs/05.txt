The cumulative mortality of postpartum female ungulates initiates at zero and follows a decelerating growth trajectory across the simulation, asymptotically approaching a saturation threshold near 119 individuals before exhibiting marginal terminal decline. The accumulation process undergoes distinct kinematic phases marked by specific temporal thresholds.

**Initial Colonization and Rapid Accumulation (Ticks 0–475)**
During the first 475 ticks, the system exhibits the steepest mortality accumulation, with rolling trend analyses indicating slopes exceeding 0.21 kills per tick in the initial 231-tick window and maintaining approximately 0.23 kills per tick through tick 463. This phase corresponds to the initial deployment of 20 foragers within the 211-by-211 patch landscape, exploiting a naive prey population of 250 ungulates. The inflection points clustered at ticks 245, 262, and 285 reflect rapid adjustments in hunter search efficiency as foragers establish optimal foraging territories and encounter nursing females at high rates before prey behavioral avoidance or spatial depletion manifests.

**Deceleration Phase (Ticks 475–1180)**
Change points at ticks 475, 625, and 850 demarcate progressive deceleration in accumulation rates. Between ticks 464 and 695, the rate drops to 0.059 kills per tick—approximately one-quarter of the initial velocity—and further declines to 0.015 kills per tick by tick 927. This deceleration likely reflects the combined effects of prey population depression, as nursing females are selectively removed from the reproductive pool, and potential prey spatial redistribution away from high-risk areas. By ticks 928–1159, the accumulation slows to 0.003 kills per tick, indicating approaching saturation of either hunter capacity or prey availability.

**Plateau and Oscillatory Dynamics (Ticks 1180–1856)**
Around tick 1180, the series transitions into a plateau phase characterized by multiple inflection points (1180, 1311, 1394, 1404, 1485, 1616, 1713) signaling minimal net accumulation. The rate drops to 0.001 kills per tick between ticks 1160–1391, effectively flatlining at 0.000 through tick 1623. Superimposed upon this plateau is a stable oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, manifesting most clearly between ticks 2170–2265. These oscillations likely reflect seasonal reproductive pulses (given the 365-tick annual cycle and 0.8 seasonality parameter) or cyclical resource availability driving periodic encounters between hunters and vulnerable postpartum females. The maximum cumulative mortality reaches 119 individuals at tick 1826, with secondary peaks at tick 1933 maintaining this value.

**Terminal Decline (Ticks 1856–2312)**
After tick 1856, the aggregate series exhibits a statistically significant decreasing trend through the simulation terminus at tick 2312, concluding with a value of 117—two units below the recorded maximum. This terminal decline, unusual for a cumulative metric, suggests heterogeneity across the 40 simulation runs, wherein some trajectories experienced prey population collapse or hunter strategy shifts that effectively halted postpartum female targeting, combined with mean regression as high-mortality runs stabilized while lower-mortality runs continued accumulating slowly.

**Statistical Distribution Characteristics**
The substantial divergence between the mean (102.4) and median (116.9) cumulative mortality indicates a left-skewed distribution across simulation runs. Most replicates converge on high-mortality outcomes near 117 individuals, but a subset maintains substantially lower accumulated predation, creating a tail of conserved populations. The standard deviation of 30.0 reflects this bifurcation, suggesting path-dependent outcomes where initial stochastic hunter-prey encounters determine long-term trajectories.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The rapid initial accumulation phase (first 475 ticks) represents a critical intervention window; management strategies must prioritize protection of nursery groups during this colonization period before cumulative mortality approaches irreversible thresholds. The 63-tick oscillatory pattern provides predictive capacity for seasonal vulnerability peaks, allowing for temporal targeting of anti-poaching efforts. The left-skewed outcome distribution indicates that while complete reproductive female depletion is the modal outcome under current parameters, alternative stable states with minimal postpartum predation are achievable, likely through spatial refugia creation or hunter diversion to alternative prey (particularly given identical net caloric returns between male and female targets when processing costs are accounted for). The terminal plateau suggests that once approximately 117 nursing females have been removed, the effective breeding population collapses or becomes cryptic, necessitating population monitoring protocols that track demographic structure rather than aggregate abundance alone.
