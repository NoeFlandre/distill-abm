The aggregate mean cumulative destocking across all simulation runs initiates at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-step period, concluding at approximately 96.8 units. This trajectory exhibits a concave form with distinct phases of decelerating accumulation. The initial phase spanning steps 0–25 demonstrates rapid acceleration, with rates decreasing from 4.96 units per step in the earliest window (steps 0–10) to 1.47 units per step by step 21, reflecting immediate destocking responses to initial pasture conditions and the starting livestock initialization of 90 units. A structural shift occurs at step 25, where the accumulation rate declines substantially to approximately 0.36 units per step, followed by a further moderation to around 0.31 units per step after step 60, indicating progressive stabilization of household destocking decisions as adaptive learning mechanisms and behavioral strategies mature.

Individual simulation runs display considerable divergence from this central tendency. By step 100, cumulative destocking totals range from approximately 58 to 173 across different runs, with a median value of 81.17 and a mean of 75.48. This heterogeneity becomes pronounced after step 20, when trajectories bifurcate into distinct pathways: some series stabilize at lower levels between 60 and 85 units for the majority of the simulation, while others continue steady linear or curvilinear ascent throughout the full period, achieving values exceeding 140 units and reaching up to 173 units. Inflection points detected between steps 45 and 75 suggest minor oscillatory behavior in specific runs, though the dominant pattern remains one of increasing accumulation with a characteristic slowing of the rate over time.

In the context of private property grazing systems with heterogeneous behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), these dynamics indicate that initial destocking pressures are universally high across all agent types as households respond to ecological conditions and private pasture constraints. The steep early accumulation corresponds to shock responses and immediate adjustments to biomass availability, while the progressive flattening of aggregate rates reflects successful adaptation through learning-by-doing and social learning processes. The persistent wide variance at step 100, with a standard deviation of approximately 20.3, demonstrates that heterogeneity in risk attitudes and learning capabilities produces fundamentally different long-term outcomes: some households maintain conservative destocking profiles suggesting stable livelihood management, while others accumulate substantially higher cumulative totals, potentially reflecting either greater vulnerability to stochastic rainfall events (mean 200, standard deviation 100) or more aggressive conservative strategies that prioritize pasture preservation over herd maintenance.
