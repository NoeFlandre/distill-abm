 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management .  The mean reserve biomass begins at 90,000 units and follows a trajectory characterized by an initial decline, a minimum point, and subsequent recovery to levels exceeding initial conditions . The model's feedback mechanisms between biomass observation, social comparison, and the updating of r-parameters that govern uncertainty management in grazing decisions .  Initial livestock pressure from the starting herd size of 90 animals per household temporarily depletes reserve biomass below starting levels across the 60 private property patches . However, the presence of adaptive learning mechanisms—including learning-by-doing (E-LBD) and social learning . behaviors—enables heterogeneous households to adjust their grazing strategies based on observed vegetation states and neighbor performance .  The data reveal a pronounced three-phase dynamic in livestock destocking behavior across the 100-step simulation horizon . At the beginning of the simulation, no destocking occurs, reflecting the starting condition where households possess initial livestock endowments without immediate resource constraints . By step 3, the system experiences an acute stress event, with the aggregate mean destocking reaching 6.6144 (with individual simulation peaks approaching 7.5)  In the final phase from steps 55-65, the rate of increase diminishes to nearly zero (slope approximately 0.008), with change points at steps 40 and 100 demarcating these distinct behavioral regimes . The aggregate mean across 30 simulations reaches its maximum value of approximately 32 households by step 63 and maintains this level through step 100 .  From step 66 onward, the aggregate trend stabilizes, exhibiting no statistically significant directional movement through step 100 . A residual population of approximately 15 households (representing roughly 25 percent of the total 60 households) persists with the non-learning E-RO strategy . The persistent variance across simulations suggests that initial conditions, stochastic ecological factors, or localized interactions within the private property grazing system create path-dependent outcomes .
