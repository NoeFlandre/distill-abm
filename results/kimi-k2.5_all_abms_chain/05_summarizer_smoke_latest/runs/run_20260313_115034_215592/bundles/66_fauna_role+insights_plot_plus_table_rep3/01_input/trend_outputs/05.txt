The cumulative mortality of postpartum female ungulates follows a pronounced saturation trajectory over the 2,312-tick simulation horizon. Beginning at zero, the aggregate count across 40 simulation runs exhibits strong monotonic growth through approximately tick 850, followed by a deceleration phase and eventual stabilization near an asymptotic maximum of 119 individuals, with terminal values clustering around 117.  

**Temporal Phases and Key Milestones**

*Initial Accumulation (Ticks 0–475):* The series initiates with near-zero values, rapidly accelerating after tick 245. By the first change point at tick 475, the cumulative count has already exceeded 60 kills on average, representing the initial depletion of the most accessible postpartum females within the founding population of 250 ungulates.

*Rapid Growth Phase (Ticks 475–850):* Between the first and third change points (ticks 475, 625, and 850), the trajectory maintains a steep positive slope, with rolling Mann-Kendall analyses confirming strongly significant increasing trends (tau coefficients approaching 1.0, p < 0.001). During this interval, the cumulative kill count rises from approximately 60 to exceed 100 individuals, indicating that forager bands are successfully targeting and processing nursing females at a rate that outpaces the emergence of new postpartum individuals from the herd's reproductive cycle.

*Saturation and Plateau (Ticks 850–1826):* Following tick 850, the rate of increase diminishes substantially. The inflection points clustered between ticks 1180 and 1485 mark the transition to a near-horizontal asymptote. The mean count reaches its maximum value of 119 at tick 1826, after which the series exhibits negligible change. Rolling trend analysis confirms this flattening, with window 7 (ticks 1392–1623) showing no significant directional trend (tau = -0.078, p = 0.066). The final segment (window 9, ticks 1856–2087) registers a statistically significant but minimal decreasing tendency (tau = -0.324, slope ≈ 0), stabilizing at an end-value of 117.

**Biological Interpretation within MHPM Dynamics**

The plateau at approximately 117–119 postpartum female deaths reflects the total cumulative hunting mortality of breeding females over the simulation duration. Given the initial prey population of 250 animals and a birth-spacing parameter of 730 ticks (approximately two years), the asymptote suggests that foragers effectively remove nearly the entire reproductive cohort of adult females—accounting for roughly 47 percent of the initial population—within the first 1,000 to 1,500 ticks (2.7–4.1 years).

The inflection dynamics align with the model's reproductive biology: the initial steep accumulation corresponds to the harvesting of the starting population's postpartum females, while the subsequent flattening indicates either (1) the successful elimination of all nursing females followed by a reproductive collapse, or (2) the removal of individuals as they enter the postpartum state at intervals determined by the 730-tick interbirth interval. The lack of sustained growth beyond tick 1,850 implies that recruitment of new postpartum females has ceased or dropped below detection thresholds, consistent with a population collapse scenario under sustained hunting pressure.

Notably, the dynamics proceed independent of forager mortality (imortal-foragers = True) and with energy storage enabled, allowing human agents to maintain persistent hunting pressure without demographic constraints or energetic interruptions. The high seasonality parameter (0.8) and rapid grass regrowth (3 ticks) create fluctuating resource environments, yet the cumulative kill curve shows no cyclical pattern, suggesting that forager targeting of postpartum females remains constant regardless of seasonal vegetation pulses.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The trajectory reveals an extremely narrow intervention window for population conservation. The first 475 ticks (approximately 1.3 years) constitute the critical period during which hunting pressure drives the most precipitous decline in breeding female numbers. Once the cumulative mortality exceeds 60 individuals (tick 475), the reproductive foundation of the herd is already compromised, and the population proceeds toward the terminal plateau where functional extinction of breeding capacity is inevitable.

Management strategies must prioritize restrictions on hunting pressure targeted at postpartum females within the first year of simulation time. Delayed action beyond tick 625 (approximately 1.7 years) appears insufficient to prevent the asymptotic removal of the breeding population, given that over 100 postpartum females have been lost by this stage. The model predicts that current parameter configurations—specifically the combination of immortal foragers, food storage capacity, and a foraging radius of 6 patches—produce unsustainable predation pressure that exhausts the supply of reproductive females well before the maximum simulation extent of 73,000 ticks is reached.

Stakeholders should consider modifying either the forager demographic parameters (reducing initial forager numbers or removing immortality), reducing the foraging radius to decrease encounter rates with nursing herds, or implementing seasonal hunting moratoriums that align with the 730-tick reproductive cycle to allow recovery periods between birth events.
