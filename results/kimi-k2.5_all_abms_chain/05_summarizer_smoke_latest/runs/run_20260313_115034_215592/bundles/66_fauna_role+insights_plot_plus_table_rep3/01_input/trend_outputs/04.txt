The cumulative mortality of female megaherbivores exhibits a consistent trajectory across 40 simulation replicates, beginning at zero and rising to terminal values ranging between approximately 108 and 124 individuals. The aggregate series spans 2,312 time steps (approximately 6.3 years), achieving a mean cumulative count of 104.6 and a median of 119.1, indicating a left-skewed distribution where the majority of runs converge toward the upper range of 120-124 deaths while a subset plateau at lower values.

The dynamics unfold through three distinct temporal phases. During the initial rapid accumulation phase (steps 0-500, approximately 0-1.4 years), the mortality rate is steepest, with rolling trend analysis revealing slopes of 0.2-0.23 female deaths per step and near-perfect monotonic increase (Mann-Kendall τ > 0.99). This period accounts for the majority of total mortality, with cumulative counts rising from 0 to 80-110 individuals depending on the specific replicate.

A deceleration phase follows (steps 500-1000, approximately 1.4-2.7 years), where the rate of accumulation drops precipitously to slopes of 0.02-0.06 per step. By step 850, statistical change-point detection identifies a transition to slower accumulation dynamics. During this interval, cumulative counts rise from approximately 100 to 115-120 individuals across most runs.

The final saturation phase (steps 1000-2312, approximately 2.7-6.3 years) shows minimal additional mortality, with slopes declining to 0.002-0.006 per step and ultimately approaching zero. The maximum mean value of 123.7 occurs around step 1826 (5 years), after which the series stabilizes or exhibits negligible fluctuation.

Notable variability exists in the timing of saturation: some replicates achieve maximum cumulative mortality by step 600-800, while others continue gradual accumulation until step 1500-2000. However, nearly all replicates converge to terminal values within a narrow band of 120-124 deaths, suggesting a consistent ceiling on female harvest regardless of the specific trajectory taken.

**Implications for Decision Makers**: The model indicates that hunting pressure on female megaherbivores is front-loaded, with 80-90% of cumulative female mortality occurring within the first 500 days (1.4 years) of simulation. This suggests that management interventions or policy responses to protect breeding females must be implemented extremely rapidly—within the first year of detected hunting pressure—to be effective. Once the initial high-mortality phase passes (approximately 2-3 years), the damage to the female breeding stock is largely complete, and the population either collapses or becomes effectively unavailable to hunters, independent of subsequent management actions. The narrow convergence of terminal values across diverse stochastic trajectories implies that the system has a robust carrying capacity for hunter harvest of approximately 120 females before becoming depleted under the specified parameter configuration.
