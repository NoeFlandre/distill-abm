 Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change . Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances . Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores .  The number of active human hunter-gatherers remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation period . The simulations reveal a consistent pattern of energy depletion across all stochastic runs . The system exhibits substantial variability between runs, with total energy declining to values ranging between approximately -4,000 and -6,000 units .  The saturating dynamics suggest that hunting pressure progressively depletes the available male prey population toward functional extinction or densities below viable hunting thresholds . Given the initial ungulate population of 250 animals, the asymptotic values approaching 120 males indicate that hunters successfully encounter and kill the vast majority of males present in the system .  The initial population of 250 animals requires time to reach reproductive maturity or accumulate sufficient energetic reserves for breeding . Within the first 200 to 400 steps, deaths accumulate rapidly, with most trajectories reaching between 150 and 200 cumulative deaths by approximately step 400 to 500 . By step 500, male counts range from near zero to approximately 60 individuals across different replicates, indicating high sensitivity to stochastic processes during the early phase of population reduction .  Some populations declined to zero within approximately 300 steps, others maintained small numbers of females—ranging from 1 to 20 individuals—for 1,000 steps or more . By the conclusion of the extended simulation period (approaching 2,200 steps), nearly all replicates had converged on zero or one remaining females .  The total energy of female animals initiates at approximately 12,000 to 12,500 units and follows a general decline trajectory toward zero, with substantial variation in the rate and timing of collapse across replicates . The complete convergence to zero across all replicates demonstrates that the initial male population is unsustainable under these hunting and environmental conditions .  The percentage of vegetated landscape patches initiates at approximately 33%, consistent with the grass-proportion parameter setting of 0.33.8 . High environmental seasonality (0.8) likely creates energetic bottlenecks that prevent recovery during seasonal low-productivity periods .
