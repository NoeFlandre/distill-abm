Cumulative livestock destocking increases monotonically from zero to approximately 97 animals per household over the 100-year simulation period, following a markedly decelerating trajectory superimposed with quasi-periodic fluctuations. The accumulation rate is most intense during the initial quarter-century, averaging nearly 5 animals annually in the first decade and remaining above 1.4 animals annually through year 21. By year 25, the system exhibits a significant behavioral shift as the rate of destocking drops sharply to less than 0.7 animals annually, then further stabilizes around 0.31 to 0.36 animals per year from year 33 through the end of the simulation.

This long-term deceleration is punctuated by oscillatory dynamics concentrated around years 22–26, 49–61, and 79–80, corresponding to an approximate 49-year cycle. These fluctuations generate multiple inflection points between years 45 and 75, modulating the rate of destocking while maintaining the overall upward accumulation. A secondary change point at year 60 marks the consolidation of the low-rate destocking regime, after which the metric continues its slow ascent toward the terminal value of 96.8 animals per household.

Given the initial herd endowment of 90 animals per household and the absence of reproduction in this parameterization, the cumulative destocking exceeding initial holdings suggests sustained livestock acquisition throughout the simulation, with households continuously rotating animals through destocking events. The progression from rapid initial liquidation to stabilized long-term divestment reflects adaptive management responses to private property constraints and highly variable climatic conditions (rainfall mean 200, standard deviation 100).
