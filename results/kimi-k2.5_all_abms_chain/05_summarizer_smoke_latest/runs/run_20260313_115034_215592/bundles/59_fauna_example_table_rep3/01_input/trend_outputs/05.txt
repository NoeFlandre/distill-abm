By approximately tick 475, the rate of increase decelerates markedly as the easily accessible portion of the postpartum demographic is depleted and the prey population's reproductive capacity begins to constrain the availability of new targets, consistent with the modeled interbirth interval of 730 ticks.

The cumulative count continues to rise at a gradually slowing pace until reaching a maximum value of approximately 119 individuals around tick 1826 (roughly five years into the simulation), after which it stabilizes and undergoes a slight decline to approximately 117 by the conclusion of the run at tick 2312. This plateau suggests the system approaches a saturation point where hunting pressure has suppressed the prey population below thresholds capable of sustaining postpartum female availability, with the minor late-term decline possibly indicating prey extinction events in a subset of simulation runs. Superimposed upon this general accumulation trend are regular oscillations with a period of approximately 63 ticks, reflecting seasonal pulses in the availability of postpartum females aligned with the strong seasonality parameter (0.8) and breeding cycles, creating predictable windows of heightened hunting vulnerability.

Across the 40 simulation runs, the distribution of cumulative mortality exhibits considerable variability, with a median outcome near 117 but a mean closer to 102, indicating that while most runs resulted in high cumulative kills of postpartum females, some runs experienced substantially lower totals. This divergence likely stems from stochastic variation in early hunting success and demographic fluctuations that led to premature prey extinction or divergent herd dynamics in certain replicates, particularly evident in the latter half of the simulation where the trend becomes increasingly unstable before the final decline.
