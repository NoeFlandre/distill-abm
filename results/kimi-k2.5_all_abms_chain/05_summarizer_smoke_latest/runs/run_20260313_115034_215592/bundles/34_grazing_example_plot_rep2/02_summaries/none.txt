This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. These operate through a yearly sequence where green biomass updates, households with E-RO-SL1 types update their r-parameters, households update behavioral strategies (if switching is enabled), make livestock placement decisions, livestock feeds on biomass, households observe neighbor performance, reserve biomass updates, and households observe and remember reserve biomass states.

The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. It seeks to evaluate how learning capabilities affect household resilience and pasture conditions in private property grazing systems. Specific objectives include analyzing the impact of social learning double-loop processes through strategy switching in heterogeneous populations, observing the diffusion and competition between different learning behaviors in mixed populations, and assessing how demographic settings and initial herd sizes influence system outcomes. The model allows for the evaluation of outcomes across social, ecological, and economic dimensions including average herd size, pasture biomass condition, and surviving number of households.

The mean initial risk attitude for social learning agents remains constant across the entire simulation period, showing no variation over the 100-step sequence. Values are fixed at their initialization levels, spanning a range from approximately -0.11 to +0.18, which indicates substantial heterogeneity in baseline risk dispositions among households. The distribution encompasses negative values, reflecting risk-averse orientations, and positive values, indicating risk-seeking propensities, with distinct clustering observed at multiple intervals across this spectrum. This temporal stability reflects that the metric captures fixed initial parameters characterizing household uncertainty management and risk attitudes at the outset, representing each household's initial disposition to deviate from recommended livestock numbers, which remains unchanged throughout the simulation in this configuration.

The mean household risk attitude begins at values clustered near zero across all simulation runs. During the initial phase from steps 0 to 20, the metric undergoes rapid divergence, with trajectories spreading across a range from approximately -0.2 to 0.8. This rapid differentiation suggests that learning processes—whether social learning or learning-by-doing—quickly amplify initial variations in household experiences or local conditions into substantially different risk management strategies.

By step 30, most trajectories have reached temporary plateaus or continue adjusting toward distinct equilibrium levels. A subset of runs stabilizes at high values around 0.7 to 0.8, indicating the development of conservative or highly uncertainty-averse attitudes. Another group stabilizes at moderate positive values between 0.4 and 0.6, while additional trajectories settle at lower positive values between 0.1 and 0.3.

Between steps 40 and 60, several trajectories exhibit notable declines, with some falling from moderate positive values (around 0.4-0.5) to near zero or negative values (as low as -0.3 to -0.4). These declines suggest episodes where households substantially revise their risk attitudes downward, possibly in response to accumulated experiences of pasture productivity or observed neighbor performance.

After step 60, the vast majority of trajectories stabilize with minor fluctuations, maintaining their relative positions until the end of the simulation at step 100. The final values span a wide range from approximately -0.35 to +0.78, indicating persistent heterogeneity in risk attitudes across the simulated populations. This persistent divergence implies that the learning mechanisms in the model create path-dependent outcomes where early stochastic differences become locked into distinct long-term uncertainty management strategies.

The variability across runs is highest in the middle phase (steps 20-50) and decreases in the final phase as trajectories settle into their respective steady states. This pattern is consistent with an adaptive learning system that explores different behavioral strategies early on before converging on stable risk attitudes suited to specific local ecological and social conditions.

Following this minimum, the biomass enters a phase of rapid recovery between approximately steps 30 and 60, with values increasing steeply toward levels between 110,000 and 130,000. The rate of increase varies across simulation runs, with some trajectories showing accelerated ascents beginning around step 40, while others demonstrate more gradual recovery starting closer to step 50, potentially reflecting differences in how quickly households adjust their risk parameters and grazing decisions through learning-by-doing and social learning processes.

After approximately step 60, the biomass stabilizes into distinct equilibrium states that persist through step 100. Final values at the end of the simulation span a range from roughly 111,000 to 128,000, indicating substantial heterogeneity in long-term pasture outcomes. The majority of trajectories settle at values exceeding the initial 90,000 units, suggesting that the interplay between vegetation regrowth dynamics and adaptive household behaviors generally leads to improved reserve biomass conditions relative to the starting state, despite the initial degradation period.

The variability across simulation runs is pronounced, particularly regarding the final equilibrium levels and the depth of the initial trough, reflecting the influence of stochastic rainfall patterns (mean 200, standard deviation 100) and heterogeneous agent decision-making. The dispersion in final biomass values, spanning approximately 17,000 units between the lowest and highest stable equilibria, indicates that initial conditions and random variations in early rainfall or grazing interactions lead to path-dependent outcomes in the social-ecological system's long-term productivity.

The average number of livestock destocked begins at approximately zero and rises sharply within the first 5 time steps, reaching peak values around 7.5. This initial surge reflects the immediate pressure on the system as 60 households, each starting with 90 livestock units, simultaneously exploit their private patches before adaptive learning mechanisms can be activated. The rapid accumulation of destocked animals suggests that initial grazing decisions, made under conditions of uncertainty (with initial risk attitudes of 0.5), lead to significant resource depletion and livestock losses across all three behavioral types.

Following this peak, the metric declines rapidly between steps 5 and 20, dropping to values predominantly between 0.5 and 2. This steep reduction indicates that learning processes—particularly learning-by-doing in E-LBD agents and social learning in E-RO-SL1 agents—enable households to rapidly adjust their uncertainty management and improve livestock placement decisions. The presence of high variance during this period reflects the stochastic rainfall patterns (with standard deviation of 100 around a mean of 200) interacting with heterogeneous behavioral responses.

From step 20 to approximately step 60, the decline continues at a slower rate with considerable fluctuations, eventually stabilizing at low levels. By step 100, most trajectories converge to values between 0 and 0.8, with a small subset of simulation runs stabilizing near 1. This convergence demonstrates that despite initial heterogeneity in risk attitudes and the presence of three distinct behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), the system reaches a steady state where destocking becomes minimal.

The variance across simulation runs is pronounced in the first 30 steps, with some runs maintaining destocking levels above 2 until step 25 while others drop below 0.5 by step 15. After step 40, this variability diminishes substantially, suggesting that the combination of private property institutions and adaptive learning capabilities drives the system toward consistent long-term outcomes regardless of initial stochastic conditions. The stabilization at near-zero destocking levels by the end of the simulation indicates that stationary households with learning capabilities successfully adapt to their localized pasture conditions, achieving resilient livestock management in the private property grazing system.

Between steps 40 and 60, the trajectories diverge substantially, with approximately half of the simulation runs showing accelerated growth in inequality while the remainder maintain more gradual increases. Between steps 20 and 40, the rate of increase slows considerably, though continued incremental growth occurs. The trajectories exhibit a step-like pattern during this phase, indicating discrete annual adoption events where additional households switch to or activate social learning mechanisms at specific yearly intervals aligned with vegetation and household sub-model updates.
