The total energy contained within the prey population initiates at approximately 25,000 energy units and exhibits a pervasive declining trend across all simulation replicates, ultimately approaching zero in every run. During the initial 500 time steps, the aggregate energy undergoes rapid depletion, though with considerable inter-run variation. Several trajectories collapse precipitously, reaching near-zero levels by step 400 to 500. Other replicates demonstrate more gradual decline, maintaining 5,000 to 8,000 energy units at step 500, with one outlier sustaining approximately 8,000 units through step 600 before accelerating downward.

The decline patterns frequently exhibit a stepped morphology, characterized by intervals of relative stability punctuated by sharp drops, consistent with discrete successful hunting events or herd fragmentation. Between steps 500 and 1,000, most remaining populations continue their descent, with many runs reaching minimal energy levels below 1,000 units by step 800 to 1,000. A subset of replicates maintains low-level persistence, fluctuating between 1,000 and 3,000 units until approximately step 1,200.

One distinctive trajectory demonstrates exceptional longevity, declining gradually from 5,000 units at step 500 to near-zero levels only at approximately step 2,000, substantially exceeding the persistence time of all other replicates.

The consistent convergence toward zero energy across the parameter ensemble, despite initial variation in decline rates, indicates that the configuration of 20 human foragers with food-storage capability and a foraging radius of 6 patches generates sufficient hunting pressure to drive the megaherbivore population toward collapse. The temporal range of collapse—spanning 400 to 2,000 time steps—reflects stochastic elements in agent movement, spatial vegetation distribution with 33% initial grass cover, and the high seasonality value of 0.8 creating fluctuating resource availability that affects both prey energetics and hunter success rates.
