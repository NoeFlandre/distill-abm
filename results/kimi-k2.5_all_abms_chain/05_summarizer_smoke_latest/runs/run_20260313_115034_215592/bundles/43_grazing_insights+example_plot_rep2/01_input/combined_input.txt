This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. These operate through a yearly sequence where green biomass updates, households with E-RO-SL1 types update their r-parameters, households update behavioral strategies (if switching is enabled), make livestock placement decisions, livestock feeds on biomass, households observe neighbor performance, reserve biomass updates, and households observe and remember reserve biomass states.

The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. It seeks to evaluate how learning capabilities affect household resilience and pasture conditions in private property grazing systems. Specific objectives include analyzing the impact of social learning double-loop processes through strategy switching in heterogeneous populations, observing the diffusion and competition between different learning behaviors in mixed populations, and assessing how demographic settings and initial herd sizes influence system outcomes. The model allows for the evaluation of outcomes across social, ecological, and economic dimensions including average herd size, pasture biomass condition, and surviving number of households.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The emergence of divergent, persistent risk attitude clusters suggests that initial conditions and early learning experiences (within the first 20–40 time steps) critically determine long-term household resilience outcomes. The presence of simulation runs settling into negative or near-zero risk attitudes indicates a potential "poverty trap" dynamic where conservative learning or system failures lock households into suboptimal grazing strategies. Conversely, simulations stabilizing at high positive values suggest successful adaptation through social and experiential learning. Policymakers should prioritize early intervention windows and support mechanisms that prevent initial negative learning trajectories, as the model demonstrates that once established, these risk management regimes tend to persist rather than converge toward uniform optimal behavior.
