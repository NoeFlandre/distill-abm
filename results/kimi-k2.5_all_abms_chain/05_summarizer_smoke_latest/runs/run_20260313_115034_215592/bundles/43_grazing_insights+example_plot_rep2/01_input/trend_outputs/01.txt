For decision makers, the absolute stability of initial risk attitudes demonstrates that without explicit learning mechanisms or external interventions targeting risk perception, household dispositions toward grazing deviations remain entrenched throughout the simulation. This persistence underscores the critical importance of initial condition calibration in determining long-term system outcomes, suggesting that early-stage policies—such as initial risk socialization or targeted onboarding of households into grazing cooperatives—may exert lasting influence on pasture management behaviors. The substantial heterogeneity observed, spanning from strongly risk-averse (-0.11) to strongly risk-seeking (0.18) orientations, indicates that uniform regulatory approaches may encounter divergent responses across the population, particularly in mixed behavioral contexts where social learning must reconcile significant initial disparities in risk tolerance to achieve collective action.
