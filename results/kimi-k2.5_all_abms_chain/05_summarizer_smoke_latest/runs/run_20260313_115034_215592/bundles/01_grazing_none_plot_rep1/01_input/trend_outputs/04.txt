Across all simulation runs, the mean green biomass follows a consistent four-phase trajectory characterized by initial depletion, trough maintenance, rapid recovery, and heterogeneous stabilization. At initialization, the metric begins at approximately 45,000 units. During the first 20 steps, biomass declines sharply under grazing pressure, reaching minimum values between 40,000 and 44,000 units by step 20. Most trajectories bottom out near 40,000–42,000 units, though individual runs maintain higher minima near 44,000.  

Following this trough, a steep sigmoid recovery phase dominates steps 25 through 60. Growth accelerates most rapidly between steps 40 and 50, with values climbing from roughly 42,000–45,000 to between 55,000 and 62,000. During this interval, the simulation runs exhibit increasing divergence, indicating that stochastic vegetation dynamics and heterogeneous household decision-making—particularly distinctions between E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 learning behaviors—generate divergent pasture recovery rates despite uniform climatic forcing.  

After step 60, the expansion rate diminishes as the system approaches saturation. Between steps 60 and 100, biomass asymptotically stabilizes at run-specific equilibria ranging from approximately 56,000 to 64,000 units. This final 8,000-unit spread across runs at step 100 suggests that learning mechanisms, risk attitudes (r-parameters), and localized grazing histories produce lasting differences in long-term pasture carrying capacity. Notably, all terminal values exceed initial conditions, indicating net biomass accumulation over the 100-step sequence, yet the system does not converge to a single equilibrium state, reflecting the persistent ecological footprint of divergent adaptive behaviors in the private property grazing context.
