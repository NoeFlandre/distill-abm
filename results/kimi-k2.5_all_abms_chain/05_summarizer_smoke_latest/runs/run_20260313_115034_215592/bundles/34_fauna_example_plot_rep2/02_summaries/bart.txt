The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the simulation period, from the initial time step through approximately step 2,200. The static population size reflects the model configuration where foragers are immortal and do not reproduce. The cumulative number of postpartum females killed initiates at zero and increases monotonically throughout the simulation period. During the initial approximately 500 steps, the metric rises steeply, accumulating between 100 and 120 total kills in most instances. The rate of accumulation varies considerably across simulation runs, with some trajectories showing rapid successive successive births reaching cumulative totals of eight to ten by step 1500. The combined energy of female animals begins at approximately 12,000 to 12,500 units, representing the initial population of 250 prey with their baseline energy reserves. Over the first 500 steps, the system undergoes a rapid depletion phase, with most simulation runs declining precipitously to levels below 5,000 units and several approaching negligible values. By step 1500, nearly all simulation runs have converged to values approaching zero. A second, attenuated crash manifests around time step 750, declining to approximately 29-30% before recovering to the baseline by step 900. Subsequent fluctuations show diminishing amplitude: a dip to approximately 31-32% near step 1100, and a minor reduction to roughly 32.5% around step 1450. After approximately time step 1600, the system achieves relative stability.
