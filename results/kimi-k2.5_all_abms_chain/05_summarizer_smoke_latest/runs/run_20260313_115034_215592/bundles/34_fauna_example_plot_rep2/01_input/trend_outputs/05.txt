The cumulative count of postpartum females killed initiates at zero and increases monotonically throughout the simulation period. During the initial approximately 500 steps, the metric rises steeply, accumulating between 100 and 120 total kills in most instances. This rapid accumulation phase suggests intensive hunting pressure targeting postpartum females, which offer enhanced food value due to nursing offspring.

Following the initial rapid increase, the rate of accumulation decelerates markedly. Between approximately 500 and 1,000 steps, most trajectories approach asymptotic values ranging from roughly 110 to 125 total postpartum females killed. 

Substantial variation exists in the temporal duration required to reach maximum cumulative values. Some simulation runs stabilize as early as 600 to 800 steps, while others continue accumulating kills through 1,500 steps or beyond, with a few extending past 2,000 steps. Despite these temporal differences, final cumulative values concentrate primarily in the 115 to 125 range, with occasional runs terminating at lower asymptotes around 100 to 110.

The pronounced flattening of accumulation rates after the initial phase indicates that postpartum females become increasingly unavailable for hunting. This depletion likely reflects either the removal of this demographic from the prey population through sustained hunting mortality or demographic constraints within the herd dynamics that limit the production of new postpartum females. The eventual plateau across all runs suggests that the pool of postpartum females is finite and exhaustible under sustained hunting pressure.
