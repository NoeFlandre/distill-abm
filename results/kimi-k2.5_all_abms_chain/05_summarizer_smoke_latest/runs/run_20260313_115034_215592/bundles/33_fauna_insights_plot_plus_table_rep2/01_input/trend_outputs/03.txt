The cumulative number of male prey killed begins at zero and exhibits a sigmoidal trajectory across the simulation steps. During the initial phase, from step 0 through approximately step 500, the cumulative count rises steeply, reaching values between 80 and 110. This rapid accumulation indicates intensive hunting pressure on male prey during early simulation stages. Between steps 500 and approximately 1800, the rate of increase moderates but remains positive, with the cumulative total gradually climbing from roughly 110 to maximum values approaching 120. The aggregate mean across 80 simulation runs reaches its highest value of approximately 120.2 around step 1876, with a terminal value near 117.5 at step 2312. Following step 1850, statistical analyses reveal a decreasing trend in the accumulation rate, with some windows showing no significant trend or significant decreases, indicating that the cumulative count plateaus or grows minimally as the simulations progress toward their conclusion. Within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these patterns suggest that male prey populations experience heavy initial exploitation, followed by a prolonged period of sustained but diminishing hunting success. The eventual plateau implies that either male prey availability becomes limiting due to population depletion, or hunter energy optimization strategies shift away from male prey pursuit as costs increase relative to returns. For decision makers analyzing megafauna extinction dynamics, the data indicates that hunting pressure on males is front-loaded in the interaction timeline, with the majority of male mortality occurring within the first 500 steps. Conservation or management strategies must account for this rapid initial impact, as delays in intervention may result in substantial male population depletion before mitigation measures take effect. The sustained accumulation through step 1800 suggests that male hunting contributes to long-term population pressure, potentially altering herd sex ratios and demographic structures over extended periods.
