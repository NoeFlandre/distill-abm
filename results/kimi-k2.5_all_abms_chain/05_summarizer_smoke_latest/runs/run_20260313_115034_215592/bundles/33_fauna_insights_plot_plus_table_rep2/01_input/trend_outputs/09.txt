The simulation series demonstrates a deterministic trajectory toward extinction of the female megaherbivore population under the tested parameter configuration. Beginning with an initial cohort of 125 individuals, the population undergoes a precipitous decline characterized by high variability in extinction timing across the 40 replicated runs, with final extinction confirmed at step 2311.

The decline unfolds in three principal phases. During the initial rapid depredation phase (steps 0–475), the population experiences a statistically significant monotonic decrease (τ = -1.000, p < 0.001), dropping from 125 to approximately 5 individuals by step 710. This phase exhibits oscillatory dynamics with a dominant period of 63 steps, corresponding to seasonal cycling (approximately 5.8 cycles per year given the 365-step annual parameterization), causing regular fluctuations of 2–4 individuals around the declining trend line.

A transitional persistence phase occurs between steps 475 and 875, marked by reduced rates of decline (slope = -0.019 to -0.061) and intermittent stabilization. Brief, statistically significant increasing trends appear in windows 1160–1391 (τ = 0.093) and 1856–2087 (τ = 0.453), suggesting attempted demographic recovery. During these intervals, sub-populations occasionally rebound from near-extinction thresholds of 1–2 individuals to levels of 4–6 individuals, particularly around steps 730, 803, and 820, before resuming decline.

The terminal phase (steps 875–2311) features an asymptotic approach to zero, with the median population remaining at 1 individual for extended durations while the mean gradually converges toward zero (final mean: 16.478 across all time steps). The final extinction event occurs at step 2311, with 100% of simulation runs reaching zero female animals despite varying trajectories in individual replicates—some declining rapidly to extinction by step 500, while others persist with 1–2 individuals until steps 1500–2000.

The dynamics reveal that the combination of 20 hunters operating under optimal foraging logic, sexual dimorphism favoring female targeting (lower processing cost of 3 versus 6 for males, with comparable food value), and biennial birth spacing (730 steps) creates an unsustainable harvest pressure. Seasonal environmental constraints (seasonality = 0.8) introduce periodic resource limitations that exacerbate vulnerability during low-population bottlenecks, preventing recovery even when hunting pressure temporarily relaxes due to hunter energy dynamics or spatial displacement.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The parameter configuration tested results in inevitable extinction across all stochastic replicates, indicating that sustainable coexistence requires intervention. Decision makers should prioritize reducing hunter density below the critical threshold of 20 individuals or extending birth spacing intervals beyond 730 steps to allow demographic recovery. The brief recovery episodes observed suggest that temporary hunting moratoriums could potentially prevent extinction if implemented before populations drop below 5 individuals. Additionally, adjusting the sexual dimorphism parameters to reduce the relative profitability of female harvests may shift hunter selectivity toward males, potentially buffering the reproductive core of the population. The consistent 63-step oscillation period indicates that seasonal protection measures aligned with resource scarcity cycles could provide critical windows for population stabilization.
