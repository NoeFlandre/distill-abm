The total energetic reserves of female ungulate populations initiate at 12,500 units and follow a deterministic downward trajectory toward complete depletion, reaching zero by step 2,312 across all 40 simulation replicates. The aggregate mean energy across the simulation series is 1,569 units, though the median value of 100 indicates substantial right-skewness, reflecting early population crashes in some runs contrasted with extended persistence in others.

The decline proceeds through several distinct phases. An initial rapid descent dominates steps 0 through approximately 300, during which the aggregated mean drops precipitously from its maximum value. This steep decline moderates at inflection point 299, transitioning into a phase of slower decrease marked by transient oscillations. Between steps 375 and 500—identified as critical change points—the aggregate decline rate diminishes further, with secondary peaks occurring between steps 376 and 410, where local maxima reach approximately 2,100 to 2,230 units before the downward trend resumes.

From steps 500 through 1,000, significant heterogeneity emerges across simulation runs. While some populations collapse to near-zero levels within the first 500 steps, others maintain total energy between 1,000 and 4,000 units through step 800 or 900, generating a standard deviation of 3,083 units in the aggregate series. Rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms significantly decreasing trends (tau approaching -1.0) through step 1,159, with only minor deviations including brief statistically significant increasing periods between steps 1,160–1,391 and steps 1,856–2,087, corresponding to temporary stabilization of small surviving populations before terminal decline.

After step 1,000, remaining populations enter a phase of terminal depletion, with energy levels falling below 1,000 units for most trajectories. Additional change points at steps 625 and 875 mark further transitions in decline rates, followed by a prolonged low-energy phase extending from step 1,400 to 1,700. Throughout the entire collapse sequence, oscillations with a dominant period of 63 steps are superimposed upon the downward trend, consistent with seasonal environmental forcing or demographic cycles.

Decision makers should recognize that under the current configuration of 20 initial foragers, high environmental seasonality (0.8), and specified life-history constraints, megaherbivore extinction is deterministic rather than stochastic, with complete biomass loss occurring systematically across all replicates. However, the wide variation in persistence time—from immediate collapse to survival beyond step 2,000—indicates that micro-scale stochastic factors create substantial uncertainty in extinction timing. The rapid initial energy loss within the first 300 steps suggests that hunting pressure immediately exceeds sustainable yield, while the temporary energetic plateaus observed around steps 400–500 represent critical windows where intervention might extend population persistence, though not prevent ultimate extinction given the observed deterministic endpoint.
