 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model . It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes .  The metric measures the average deviation from recommended livestock numbers across extension agents . Positive values indicate risk-seeking behavior (stocking above recommendations) and negative values indicating risk-averse behavior . Interventions should accommodate diverse risk-management strategies rather than attempting to enforce uniform adherence to recommendations .  The mean green biomass exhibits a characteristic pattern of initial decline followed by substantial recovery, though with considerable heterogeneity in long-term outcomes . The wide variation in final biomass outcomes (111,000–128,000) suggests that specific social-ecological configurations strongly influence productivity; thus, policies should prioritize facilitating rapid transitions from degradation to recovery phases .  The agent-based simulations consistently begin with an average livestock holding of 90 animals per household . During the first 40 time steps, the system undergoes a severe contraction phase . By step 80, most trajectories have stabilized into distinct equilibrium clusters, with final values at step 100 spanning a range of approximately 20 animals between the lowest and highest outcomes .  The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock across the 60-household population exhibits a general temporal increase from near-perfect equality to substantially higher levels of inequality by the 100th timestep . In the first 20 steps, inequality remains minimal across all simulation runs, with coefficients generally below 0.015 . Between steps 20 and 60, values increase gradually to ranges between approximately 0.010 and 0.045 .
