**Context and Goals**

This agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Unlike the original RAGE model which examined common property scenarios where herders move livestock across shared rangelands, this extension focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods.

The simulation tracks the interaction between vegetation condition and household decisions about livestock numbers over time. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors.

The central purpose is understanding how these different ways of adapting to changing conditions influence pasture health, herd sizes, and the persistence of households. The model particularly examines how farmers handle uncertainty and incomplete information when making stocking decisions, represented through individual risk dispositions that affect how they perceive and react to environmental feedback.

**Key Parameters**

*Population and Spatial Setup:*
The **number-households** parameter establishes how many farming families occupy the landscape, which can range from 1 to 100 families, with this study using 60 households. Each household begins with **livestock-init** set at 90 animals. The simulation runs for **timesteps** set at 100 years. Farmers begin the simulation positioned on their designated land parcels, with **start-on-homepatch?** set to True. Each farmer can observe conditions within a **knowledge-radius** that ranges from 0 to 5 patches, set here at 1 patch distance.

*Behavioral Type Configuration:*
The study examines three specific learning strategies with equal representation: **number-E-RO** (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb), **number-E-LBD** (learning-by-doing), and **number-E-RO-SL1** (social learning with single-loop adaptation), each set to 20 households. The **homog-behav-types?** parameter set to True ensures each type is evaluated in uniform groups. The original RAGE model behavioral types are inactive in this extension: **number-SAT**, **number-TRAD**, **number-MAX**, and **number-random** are all set to 0. The default reference **behavioral-type** is set to E-RO.

*Grazing Management Practices:*
Formal pasture resting is disabled with **resting?** set to False. The threshold for initiating rest periods, **resting-threshold**, ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0.1. The **intrinsic-preference** for livestock relative to other values ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0. Similarly, **social-susceptibility** to neighbor influence ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0, indicating decisions rely on other factors.

*Original Model Parameters:*
Several parameters from the foundational RAGE model are retained. The **satisficing-threshold** for satisfied decision-making ranges from 0 to 100 and is set at 0. The **satisficing-trials** parameter, ranging from 0 to 100, is set at 21. These control how the original satisficer type would search for acceptable solutions, though this type is not active in the current setup.

*Ecological Conditions:*
Rainfall is applied uniformly across the landscape with **global-rain?** set to True. The average rainfall **rain-mean** is set at 200 units annually. Year-to-year variation in precipitation is controlled by **rain-std** set at 100, indicating significant variability in annual rainfall totals. Vegetation growth responds through two rates: **gr1** set at 0.5 and **gr2** set at 0.1. The parameter **w** from the original vegetation model is set at 0.8, influencing how pastures recover from grazing. Livestock reproduction is not active in this extension, with **b** set to 0.

*Learning and Risk Mechanisms:*
The extension module is activated with **extension-model?** set to True. Uncertainty handling is engaged with **risk-mode?** set to True. Each agent begins with an initial risk disposition **risk-att-init** set at 0.5, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance. The ability to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False, confining agents to single-loop learning within their assigned type.

The average initial risk attitude of social learning agents remains constant throughout the 100-year simulation period across all repeated runs, with values distributed between approximately -0.11 and 0.18. This temporal invariance persists from the initial time step through the final time step, with each simulation run maintaining its specific constant value without deviation.

The range of values, spanning from negative dispositions around -0.11 to positive values around 0.18, indicates that the repeated simulations explore diverse baseline risk attitudes. In the context of the grazing system, these constant values represent the fixed initial disposition of farmers to deviate from recommended livestock numbers, established at the outset of each simulation. The lack of temporal adaptation reflects that this metric captures a static parameter rather than a learning or evolving risk perception, enabling analysis of how different starting risk profiles influence long-term pasture management outcomes and household persistence under stationary behavioral assumptions.

The mean household risk attitude exhibits substantial heterogeneity across the population throughout the 100-step simulation. At initialization (step 0), risk attitudes are distributed narrowly around a central tendency near 0.0, with the majority of values falling between -0.1 and 0.1, though the full initial range spans approximately -0.1 to 0.2.

During the first 20 steps, the population undergoes rapid stratification. Approximately one-third of households experience steep monotonic increases in risk attitude, ascending from near-zero values to reach approximately 0.7-0.8 by step 25. The maximum observed value reaches approximately 0.78 and remains stable at this elevated plateau for the subsequent 75 steps. Other households exhibit more moderate increases, stabilizing temporarily at intermediate values ranging from 0.2 to 0.6, while a subset maintains values below 0.2.

Between steps 20 and 60, the trajectories display divergent dynamics. While the highest-risk-attitude cluster remains stable, several households in the middle range (0.2-0.4) experience dramatic declines. Specifically, around steps 45-55, multiple households undergo rapid decreases, with values falling from approximately 0.2-0.3 to negative values between -0.3 and -0.35. These declines occur abruptly over 5-10 step intervals. Concurrently, other households exhibit moderate volatility characterized by oscillations of ±0.05 around slowly declining or stable means.

From step 60 to step 100, the system stabilizes into distinct, persistent risk attitude categories. The highest cluster maintains values of 0.75-0.8. Secondary stable plateaus emerge at approximately 0.7, 0.65, 0.6, and 0.55. A middle group stabilizes between 0.45 and 0.5, while lower tiers settle at approximately 0.35, 0.25-0.3, and 0.1-0.15. Several households remain near zero (0.0 to 0.05), and a distinct minority maintains negative values of approximately -0.2 and -0.35. Variability within these categories diminishes substantially after step 60, with fluctuations rarely exceeding ±0.03 from established means.

This pattern suggests that the three distinct learning strategies—baseline experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—converge to different equilibrium risk dispositions. The early divergence indicates that initial environmental feedback and learning mechanisms quickly differentiate household risk attitudes. The stability achieved by step 60 reflects the absence of double-loop learning (strategy switching), as households remain locked in their single-loop adaptation patterns. The existence of both high positive values (0.8) and strongly negative values (-0.35) indicates that the same environmental conditions and social network structure can produce diametrically opposed risk orientations depending on the specific learning algorithm employed and stochastic environmental variations experienced during the critical first 60 steps.

The average reserve biomass across all patches begins at approximately 90,000 units. Following a marginal initial increase to roughly 90,500-91,000 units within the first 2-3 time steps, the biomass enters a sustained decline phase that continues until approximately time step 30-40, reaching minimum values between 80,000 and 82,000 units. This initial degradation suggests that the initial stocking density of 90 animals per household exerts grazing pressure exceeding the sustainable carrying capacity of the pastures, depleting reserve biomass during the early years of the simulation.

Beginning around time step 40-45, the biomass undergoes a rapid recovery, increasing sharply from its minimum values to exceed 110,000 units by approximately time step 60-70. The steepest rate of increase occurs between time steps 45 and 60, indicating accelerated vegetation recovery as farmers adjust their management practices. This recovery reflects the learning processes—whether through personal experience or social observation—enabling households to reduce stocking rates and alleviate grazing pressure on the vegetation.

After time step 70-75, the rate of biomass increase diminishes substantially, and the system transitions to a plateau phase. By time step 100, the biomass stabilizes at values ranging from approximately 111,000 to 128,000 units, with most simulation runs converging between 115,000 and 125,000 units. Notably, the final biomass levels substantially exceed the initial starting values, indicating that the learning strategies eventually facilitate pasture conditions superior to the original state.

Considerable variation emerges across simulation runs during the recovery phase and persists through the final plateau, with a spread of roughly 15,000-17,000 units between the lowest and highest final values. This divergence suggests that while all three learning strategies (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning) successfully enable recovery from initial overgrazing, the specific long-term ecological outcomes depend significantly on the particular decision-making approach employed and the interaction with stochastic rainfall variability.

The mean green biomass commences at approximately 45,000 units. Over the first 5 steps, values remain stable with a marginal peak near 45,500. A subsequent decline phase persists through step 35, with biomass decreasing to minimum values between 40,000 and 47,000 across different simulation runs. One particular trajectory diverges notably during this period, maintaining biomass above 43,000 throughout the decline phase before converging with the broader recovery pattern.

Between steps 35 and 65, all runs exhibit a rapid increase, with biomass ascending sharply to reach levels between 55,000 and 64,000. This recovery transitions into a plateau phase from step 65 to 100, where values stabilize. Final biomass at step 100 ranges from approximately 56,000 to 64,000 across runs, representing an improvement over initial conditions for most scenarios, though with substantial variation in ultimate outcomes.

The pronounced variance during the minimum phase and the sustained divergence in final values suggest that household decision-making strategies produce differential impacts on pasture health. The general recovery and establishment of higher equilibrium biomass levels indicate that learning mechanisms enable farmers to adjust stocking rates or management practices effectively enough to allow vegetation recovery beyond initial states, despite significant annual rainfall variability.

The mean livestock plus destock across households initiates at 90 animals. During the first 20 time steps, this measure undergoes a steep decline to approximately 45 animals, with all simulation trajectories following nearly identical paths. This uniformity indicates that initial destocking is driven primarily by the universal constraint of pasture carrying capacity rather than behavioral differences, as all household types must reduce herds from the initial overstocking level.

Between steps 20 and 40, the decline moderates, reaching a minimum ranging from 40 to 42 animals around step 40. This nadir represents the ecologically constrained baseline where vegetation recovery and grazing pressure temporarily equilibrate at low stock densities following the initial correction.

After step 40, the trajectories bifurcate into three distinct clusters. One group remains constrained, stabilizing between 41 and 44 animals from step 50 through step 100. A second cluster exhibits moderate recovery, ascending to stabilize between 46 and 51 animals by step 100. Two trajectories demonstrate superior performance, with one rising to approximately 59 animals and another reaching approximately 62 animals by step 100.

This stratification into outcome tiers—low (~41-44), moderate (~46-51), and high (~59-62)—corresponds to the three learning strategies under investigation. The spread of roughly 20 animals between the lowest and highest performing strategies by step 100 indicates that decision-making approach substantially affects long-term herd size. The low-variability phase during steps 0-30 transitions to high variability thereafter, demonstrating that behavioral adaptations to ecological feedback generate divergent outcomes only after the initial crisis of overstocking has been resolved. The highest-performing strategies achieve herd sizes 40-50\% greater than the lowest-performing ones, suggesting that effective learning mechanisms enable households to maintain larger herds within sustainable limits by more accurately calibrating stock levels to actual pasture conditions.

The mean number of livestock destocked per household exhibits a pronounced initial crisis followed by progressive stabilization. At initialization, destocking is negligible, but within the first 2-3 time steps, the metric surges sharply to peak values ranging from 6 to 7.5 animals per household. This immediate spike reflects the resource crisis when initial stocking of 90 animals per household encounters pasture biomass insufficient to support them, forcing rapid removal of excess livestock to prevent total resource depletion.

Following the initial peak, destock rates undergo a steep decline between time steps 5 and 25, falling from values above 5 to approximately 0.5-2 animals. This rapid reduction indicates that initial forced destocking successfully alleviated grazing pressure, allowing households to retain most remaining animals. During the interval from step 15 to step 40, destock rates display substantial volatility with fluctuations oscillating between 0 and 2.5 animals, reflecting ongoing adjustments as farmers with different learning strategies calibrate decisions against highly variable rainfall conditions (standard deviation of 100 around a mean of 200) and spatially heterogeneous pasture productivity.

Notable heterogeneity emerges across simulation runs during the middle phase. Most trajectories converge toward low destock rates by step 50, but some maintain elevated destocking of 1-2 animals until approximately step 40 before declining. By step 60, the system approaches steady-state with destock rates stabilizing predominantly between 0 and 1 animal per household. 

The final equilibrium exhibits stratification across runs: approximately one-third stabilize near zero destocking (below 0.2 animals), another third converge to intermediate values of 0.3-0.6 animals, and the remainder maintain steady-state destocking of 0.7-1.0 animals. This persistent variation reflects differential effectiveness of the three behavioral strategies (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) in achieving long-term stock-pasture equilibrium. The trajectories stabilizing at higher levels suggest that certain learning configurations or initial risk dispositions result in systematically conservative stocking strategies requiring ongoing periodic destocking, while those approaching zero indicate successful adaptation to local carrying capacity. The simulation concludes with destock rates remaining permanently above zero for most households, indicating that persistent environmental uncertainty sustains baseline livestock removal even after 100 years of adaptation.

The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock distribution begins at 0 at step 0, reflecting the initial condition where all 60 households possess identical herds of 90 animals. Over the first 40 time steps, inequality increases gradually to values ranging between approximately 0.01 and 0.03, indicating modest differentiation in livestock holdings as households begin applying their distinct learning strategies to manage grazing pressures under variable rainfall conditions.

Between steps 40 and 60, the trajectories diverge more noticeably, with values spreading across a range of roughly 0.025 to 0.05. This divergence accelerates substantially in the final 40 steps, with some simulation runs reaching Gini coefficients of 0.09 to 0.095 by step 100, while others plateau at much lower levels around 0.03 to 0.045. The spread between the lowest and highest inequality outcomes widens to approximately 0.06 by the conclusion of the simulation.

This pattern suggests that while all households begin with equal resource endowments, the interaction between the three learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning) and stochastic environmental variation drives increasing differentiation in livestock holdings over time. The wide variance in terminal inequality values indicates that initial random factors and specific sequences of rainfall variability can compound through the decision-making processes, leading to substantially different distributional outcomes across otherwise identical starting conditions. The steep increases observed in high-inequality runs after step 60 imply the emergence of positive feedback mechanisms where differential success in pasture management allows some households to maintain larger healthy herds while others experience relative decline.

The count of households that have engaged in social learning begins at zero across all simulation runs. Within the first approximately 10 to 15 time steps, the count increases rapidly in a stepwise fashion, with most trajectories rising from 0 to between 20 and 40 households. This initial rapid adoption reflects the activation of the dedicated social learning households (E-RO-SL1 type) and early diffusion to additional households through neighbor observation and experience accumulation.

Between time steps 15 and 40, the growth continues for most runs but at a slower pace, with intermittent plateaus where the count remains constant for several steps before jumping to higher levels. By time step 40, the majority of trajectories have stabilized, though some exhibit minor increases up to approximately step 60.

The final values at time step 100 show substantial variation across simulation runs, ranging from approximately 18 households at the minimum to 54 households at the maximum. The minimum plateau of roughly 18 households aligns closely with the 20 dedicated E-RO-SL1 social learning households, suggesting that in some runs social learning remains largely confined to this core group. The maximum of 54 indicates that in other runs, social learning spreads extensively to include up to 34 additional households from the remaining 40 non-dedicated households.

This divergence across runs likely stems from the stochastic rainfall patterns (rain-std set at 100) and the spatial arrangement of households, where favorable initial conditions or well-positioned social learners in some runs facilitate widespread adoption, while adverse conditions or isolated social learners in other runs limit diffusion to the core group. The stepwise nature of the curves reflects the discrete, threshold-based decision-making process where households switch to social learning behavior only after accumulating sufficient evidence from observations or experience.

The average cumulative number of livestock destocked per household begins at zero and increases rapidly during the initial 20 time steps, reaching approximately 55 to 80 animals across all simulation runs. This initial surge represents the immediate adjustment phase where farmers reduce herds from the initial stocking level of 90 animals in response to observed pasture conditions and perceived carrying capacity constraints.

Following this initial adjustment period, the trajectories diverge markedly into two distinct behavioral patterns. Approximately one-third of the simulation runs exhibit early saturation, with cumulative destocking plateauing between 58 and 85 animals by time step 40 and remaining relatively stable through step 100. These saturating trajectories suggest that households in these runs successfully identified and maintained sustainable herd sizes after the initial correction, requiring minimal additional destocking in subsequent decades.

The remaining simulation runs demonstrate continuous accumulation of destocking throughout the 100-year period, with cumulative totals reaching between approximately 100 and 173 animals by the final time step. Within this continuously rising group, moderate trajectories increase steadily to values between 100 and 130, while the steepest trajectories sustain high linear growth rates, reaching 145 to 173 animals by step 100. The divergence between saturating and continuously rising trajectories becomes pronounced after time step 30, indicating that differences in learning effectiveness and adaptation to environmental variability compound significantly over time.

The variance across simulation runs increases substantially after the initial adjustment phase. While all runs cluster tightly with a spread of approximately 25 animals during steps 0-20, the range between minimum and maximum cumulative destock values expands to over 50 animals by step 40 and exceeds 110 animals by step 100. This growing divergence suggests that stochastic variation in rainfall conditions, combined with the specific learning strategies employed by households, generates fundamentally different long-term livestock management outcomes. The trajectories stabilizing at lower values indicate successful adaptation to pasture constraints, while the continuously rising trajectories reflect persistent overstocking pressures and the repeated need for herd reduction throughout the simulation period.

The count of non-learning households begins at 20 across all simulation runs. During the initial phase (approximately steps 0-20), the trajectories exhibit rapid divergence: several runs show increases to between 30 and 42 households, while others decline to between 0 and 15. This early divergence suggests high sensitivity to initial environmental conditions and stochastic rainfall variability in determining the success of the fixed rule-of-thumb strategy.

In the early-to-middle period (approximately steps 20-40), the trajectories that increased early generally maintain elevated levels between 35 and 42 households, indicating sustained success of the non-learning approach in those particular environmental sequences. Conversely, trajectories that declined early continue to decrease, with several reaching single-digit counts or near-zero levels by step 40.

During the middle phase (approximately steps 40-60), a pronounced decline occurs in several previously high trajectories, with counts dropping from around 40 to approximately 25-28. This convergence suggests that the initial advantages of the fixed rule-of-thumb strategy may be temporary in many runs, with prolonged exposure to rainfall variability eventually eroding the viability of non-learning approaches. Other trajectories stabilize during this period, maintaining counts between 15 and 25.

In the final phase (steps 60-100), the trajectories stabilize at distinct equilibrium levels ranging from 0 to approximately 26 households. Several runs maintain counts between 23 and 26, others stabilize between 15 and 18, several remain between 8 and 12, and some reach near-zero levels. This persistent divergence indicates that the long-term viability of non-learning strategies is highly path-dependent, with stochastic rainfall sequences determining whether fixed rule-of-thumb approaches lead to household persistence or failure.

The high variance across runs, with final counts ranging from complete extinction to a 30% increase over initial levels, demonstrates that the non-learning strategy produces highly unpredictable outcomes under significant rainfall variability (rain-std = 100). The stabilization at different non-zero levels in many runs suggests that the system reaches locally stable configurations where the number of non-learning households reflects the specific history of environmental conditions encountered during that simulation run.
