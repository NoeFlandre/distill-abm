The total energy contained within the male ungulate population begins at approximately 12,500 energy units across all simulation runs, reflecting the initial cohort of 250 animals distributed across the 211 by 211 patch landscape. The descent is characterized by a stepwise pattern in numerous runs, indicating discrete reduction events consistent with individual hunting encounters rather than gradual dissipation.

Between steps 500 and 1,000, the remaining energy continues to decrease, though substantial variation emerges across simulation trajectories. While some runs reach complete depletion (zero energy) by step 600, others maintain residual energy pools ranging from 500 to 2,000 units until approximately step 800-1,000. A subset of simulations exhibits extended persistence tails, with small energy quantities (below 500 units) fluctuating between steps 1,000 and 1,500, and isolated cases maintaining minimal energy levels approaching step 2,200 before ultimately declining to zero.

By step 1,500, convergence is achieved across nearly all runs toward negligible or zero energy levels, indicating complete depletion of the male energy reserve. The early phase (steps 0-300) shows high consistency across runs with rapid downward trajectories, whereas the mid-phase (steps 300-800) displays the greatest variability, with some runs showing temporary stabilization or slight rebounds between steps 300-500 before resuming decline.

The combination of 20 human foragers operating with a foraging radius of 6 patches, energy storage capabilities, and optimal foraging decision-making creates extraction pressure that overwhelms the population's ability to maintain energy reserves through grazing (1.5 units per consumption event) and movement. The stepwise decline corresponds to successive hunting events, while the variability in persistence times reflects stochastic elements in herd movement patterns and hunter-prey encounter rates. The eventual extinction of male energy reserves across all parameter combinations suggests that under these initial conditions (20 hunters, 250 prey, high seasonality of 0.8, and rapid grass regrowth of 3 ticks), the male population cannot sustain itself against directed hunting pressure.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

These results indicate that hunting pressure from 20 foragers targeting a population of 250 ungulates creates deterministic extinction of the male cohort within 500-1,000 time steps (approximately 1.4-2.7 years), rendering the population non-viable. To prevent rapid depletion, management interventions would need to either reduce hunter numbers substantially, restrict foraging radii below the current setting of 6 patches, or implement seasonal restrictions that align with periods of low male vulnerability. The high variability observed in persistence times (ranging from 600 to 1,500+ steps) suggests that small modifications to landscape configuration or hunting protocols could significantly extend population survival, offering actionable leverage points for conservation strategies. Additionally, the preferential targeting of higher-value males may create severe sex-ratio imbalances that compromise reproductive viability before total energy depletion occurs, necessitating immediate intervention well before the 500-step threshold.
