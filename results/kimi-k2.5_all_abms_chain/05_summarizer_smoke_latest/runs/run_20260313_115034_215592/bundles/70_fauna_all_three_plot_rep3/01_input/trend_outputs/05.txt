The cumulative number of postpartum females killed exhibits a saturating growth pattern characteristic of resource depletion dynamics, beginning at zero and accumulating rapidly during the initial phase before stabilizing at asymptotic values ranging approximately from 106 to 124 individuals. The rate of accumulation is highest during the interval from roughly 0 to 600 time steps, during which most trajectories increase steeply to reach between 100 and 120 cumulative kills. By approximately 800 to 1000 time steps, the majority of simulation runs have ceased accumulating new kills entirely, with final values maintained constant through the remainder of the observation period.

Substantial variability exists across replicates in the temporal progression of hunting mortality. Early accumulation rates diverge considerably, with some runs reaching 60 kills by approximately 300 time steps while others have accumulated fewer than 20 by this point. One notable trajectory exhibits a pronounced lag phase, remaining below 10 kills for the first 200 steps before undergoing rapid acceleration to converge with the upper range of final values near 117. Despite these timing differences, most runs ultimately stabilize within a relatively narrow band of approximately 15 individuals, suggesting that stochastic factors influence the rate of encounter but not the total harvestable pool of vulnerable postpartum females.

The plateauing behavior observed across all trajectories indicates that postpartum females are effectively eliminated from the available prey pool within the first 1000 to 1500 time steps, well before the maximum temporal limit of the simulation. This rapid saturation implies that nursing females face disproportionately high hunting pressure relative to their abundance in the population, likely reflecting behavioral constraints associated with offspring care that limit mobility or habitat selection, combined with forager decision-making that targets this demographic despite processing costs and energetic trade-offs. The consistency of final asymptotic values across runs suggests that the extinction or functional elimination of the reproductively active female cohort is a deterministic outcome under the specified parameter configuration.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The convergence of all simulation runs toward similar final depletion levels (110-120 postpartum females) indicates that stochastic environmental factors have limited influence on the ultimate impact of hunting pressure on breeding stock. The targeting of nursing females, who have food values (27) and processing costs (3) comparable to or lower than adult males (30 and 6 respectively), suggests that encounter probability and behavioral vulnerability outweigh energetic optimization in forager decision-making. The rapid timeline of depletion—occurring within the first 2-3% of the maximum simulation duration—implies that management interventions to protect reproductive females would need to be implemented immediately at simulation onset to be effective, as delays of even a few hundred time steps would fail to prevent the demographic collapse of the breeding population. The delayed but eventual catch-up observed in the slowest trajectory indicates that temporary spatial refugia or behavioral avoidance by postpartum females only postpones rather than prevents their eventual removal from the population under sustained hunting pressure.
