The male megaherbivore population begins at approximately 125 individuals, representing roughly half of the initial ungulate population of 250 animals. During the initial 500 time steps, this number declines precipitously to between 0 and 20 individuals across all simulation runs. This rapid collapse reflects the intense hunting pressure exerted by the initial cohort of 20 human foragers operating under dynamic optimal foraging theory, wherein the higher nutritional value of males (30 energy units compared to 27 for females) renders them preferential targets despite their increased processing costs (6 energy units versus 3 for females).

While all population trajectories converge toward extinction, the temporal dynamics of collapse exhibit substantial heterogeneity across runs. Some male subpopulations approach effective extinction (fewer than 5 individuals) within 400 to 500 steps, whereas others persist at low densities for extended durations reaching 1,500 to over 2,200 steps. This variability likely emerges from stochastic elements in hunter-prey encounter rates, spatial heterogeneity in vegetation distribution governed by the high seasonality setting (0.8), and the probabilistic nature of survival and energetic maintenance within the 211 by 211 patch landscape.

After approximately 1,000 steps, the vast majority of simulation runs have reduced male populations to fewer than 10 individuals, with many reaching complete elimination. The stepwise nature of the decline indicates discrete mortality events corresponding to successful hunting encounters rather than continuous population attrition. Prolonged persistence in a minority of runs suggests scenarios where remaining males exploit the limited foraging radius of hunters (6 patches), survive in vegetation refugia with rapid regrowth cycles (3 ticks), or temporarily avoid detection through stochastic movement patterns, though these low-density populations eventually succumb to the sustained predation pressure.

The consistent trajectory toward male-biased mortality highlights the disproportionate impact of selective hunting on population viability. For decision makers, these results suggest that conservation interventions must prioritize protecting high-value demographic segments, particularly adult males, as their depletion occurs rapidly and irreversibly under optimal foraging pressures. The substantial variability in extinction timing indicates that small population refugia or temporary environmental amelioration may delay but not prevent collapse under sustained hunting; consequently, management thresholds should be established well before populations decline below 20 individuals to maintain demographic resilience and genetic diversity. Furthermore, the rapid progression toward extinction within the first 500 steps emphasizes the need for immediate regulatory response rather than gradual intervention when human forager populations occupy the landscape.
