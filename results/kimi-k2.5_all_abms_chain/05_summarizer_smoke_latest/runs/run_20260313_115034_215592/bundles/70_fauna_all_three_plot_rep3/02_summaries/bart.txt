Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change. Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances. Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores. Foragers-movement-cost ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1.0, and distance hunters can perceive and pursue prey is determined by foraging-radius. Two critical behavioral switches control hunter mortality and storage: imortal-foragers is set to True, meaning hunters do not die from energy depletion. The number of human foragers remains fixed at 20 individuals throughout the entire simulation trajectory. From time step 0 to approximately 400–500, the cumulative kill count increases rapidly and approximately linearly. This initial phase represents high-efficiency hunting when male prey remain abundant and provide optimal energetic returns relative to their processing costs. Between time steps 500 and 1000, the rate of accumulation decelerates markedly as the totals approach asymptotic limits. After approximately 1000–1200 time steps, cumulative counts stabilize at plateaus ranging from roughly 105 to 130 individuals. The cumulative number of births across the prey population exhibits a pronounced initial lag phase, remaining at zero for approximately the first 700 to 750 time steps. The maximum cumulative birth count observed reaches 10 births in one trajectory by approximately step 1,500, while other runs terminate with substantially lower reproductive output, including one run achieving only a single birth. The male megaherbivore population begins at approximately 125 individuals, representing roughly half of the initial ungulate population of 250 animals. By step 500, the population undergoes a precipitous collapse across all simulation populations before entering a rapid decline. This rapid collapse reflects intense hunting pressure exerted by the initial cohort of 20 human foragers. By step 400, the majority of populations have declined to fewer than 40 individuals, and by step 500, most counts range between 0 and 20 females. By step 1000, the trend converges toward universal extinction. The final extinction events occur between step 1500 and step 2200, at which point all remaining populations cease to exist. The early phase (steps 0-300) shows high consistency across runs with rapid downward trajectories, whereas the mid-phase (steps 300-800) displays the greatest variability. By step 1,500, convergence is achieved across nearly all runs toward negligible or zero energy levels, indicating complete depletion of the male energy reserve. The initial encounter rates between human foragers and ungulate herds are extremely high, with hunters exploiting naive, high-density prey population before spatial avoidance behaviors or population reductions take effect. The presence of food storage allows hunters to maintain pressure even during periods of low prey availability, preventing population recovery. Initial crash across all replicates indicates that localized overgrazing poses systemic risks to habitat carrying capacity.
