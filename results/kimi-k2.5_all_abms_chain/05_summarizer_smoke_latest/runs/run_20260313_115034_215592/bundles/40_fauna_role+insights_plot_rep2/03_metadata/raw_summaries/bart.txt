 Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change . Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances . Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores .  The number of active human forager agents remains constant at 20 throughout the entire temporal sequence, from time step 0 through approximately 2,200 steps, with no deviation from this initial value observed at any point . Simulations reveal a consistent pattern of linear energy depletion among forager bands, with aggregate hunter population experiencing a steady decline in total stored energy throughout the temporal extent of the model runs .  With 20 human foragers operating under the specified energetic and environmental parameters, male megaherbivore populations face functional extinction within approximately 2,000 daily time steps . Conservation strategies must account for the rapid depletion potential inherent in the 20-hunter scenario, as the window for sustainable coexistence appears to close within the first 1,000 time steps (approximately 2.7 years)  The cumulative number of postpartum females killed begins at zero across all simulation instances . During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, the metric increases rapidly and monotonically . After approximately step 1000, accumulation becomes negligible, with trajectories stabilizing at terminal values between roughly 110 and 125, occasionally reaching slightly above 120 .  Management strategies should prioritize protecting reproductive females during the initial two-year period, as populations that fail to produce sufficient births in this window face near-certain collapse . Decision makers should note that while the timing of collapse is stochastic and may occur over intervals ranging from fewer than 500 to more than 2000 time steps, the terminal outcome is deterministic .  The number of female animals initiates at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation replicates and follows a universal trajectory toward extinction under the specified parameters . By step 500, most populations have fallen below 20 individuals . The temporal dynamics of collapse vary stochastically, with complete extinction occurring between step 400 and step 2100 .  All populations terminate at or near zero abundance by the conclusion of the simulation period . The rapid extinction observed in the fastest-declining replicates (within 400-500 steps) suggests the system operates far from sustainability, indicating that marginal adjustments to parameters would likely be insufficient to alter outcome .  A hunting pressure of 20 immortal foragers with food storage capability systematically depletes an initial population of 250 megaherbivores under high seasonality (0.8) and moderate vegetation regrowth (3 ticks) By step 1,500, most simulations have reached energy minima approaching zero, with only isolated outliers maintaining residual energy above 200 .  These dynamics reflect consumer-resource oscillations characteristic of herbivore-vegetation systems . The initial crashes correspond to periods of intense grazing pressure from the growing ungulate population (initialized at 250 individuals) The data indicate that megaherbivore populations and vegetation resources can achieve long-term stability even under substantial hunting pressure .
