The number of households employing the E-RO (non-learning) strategy begins at 20.0 and initially exhibits a modest increase, reaching a peak value of 21.1 at time step 8. This early rise likely reflects transient demographic adjustments as the simulation establishes initial conditions across the heterogeneous population of 60 households.

Following this peak, the E-RO household count enters a sustained period of decline. Between time steps 11 and 21, the number decreases sharply and significantly, falling from approximately 20.5 to 17.5. This rapid attrition suggests that non-learning households struggle to maintain viability once the system moves beyond initialization, likely due to their inability to adapt grazing decisions to pasture variability under the modeled rainfall regime (mean 200, standard deviation 100).

The decline continues through time step 45 but at a decelerating rate, with values dropping from roughly 17.5 to 15.6. After step 45, the rate of decrease slows considerably, and from approximately step 66 onward the series stabilizes with no statistically significant trend, fluctuating around a level of 15.0 before reaching a final value of 14.7 at step 100. The minimum observed value of 14.7 occurs near the simulation end at step 92.

This trajectory indicates that in private property grazing systems with stationary households and mixed behavioral types, E-RO agents lacking learning capabilities face gradual marginalization over the long term. The pronounced decline from the initial 20 households to approximately 15 by the conclusion suggests that fixed risk attitudes and absence of adaptive learning place these households at a competitive disadvantage relative to E-LBD (learning-by-doing) and E-RO-SL1 (social learning) counterparts.

For decision makers, these trends suggest that reliance on static, non-adaptive management strategies in smallholder grazing contexts leads to reduced household survival rates over multi-decadal periods. Interventions supporting learning mechanisms—whether through experiential adaptation or social network knowledge exchange—may be essential for improving long-term resilience. Additionally, the stabilization of E-RO numbers in later stages implies that while most non-learning households exit the system, a persistent residual minority remains, indicating the need for targeted support programs to address the specific vulnerabilities of households unable to adopt adaptive behaviors.
