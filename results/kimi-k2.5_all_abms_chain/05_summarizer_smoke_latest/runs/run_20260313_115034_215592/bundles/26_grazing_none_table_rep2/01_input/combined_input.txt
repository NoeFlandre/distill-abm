This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. These operate through a yearly sequence where green biomass updates, households with E-RO-SL1 types update their r-parameters, households update behavioral strategies (if switching is enabled), make livestock placement decisions, livestock feeds on biomass, households observe neighbor performance, reserve biomass updates, and households observe and remember reserve biomass states.

The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. It seeks to evaluate how learning capabilities affect household resilience and pasture conditions in private property grazing systems. Specific objectives include analyzing the impact of social learning double-loop processes through strategy switching in heterogeneous populations, observing the diffusion and competition between different learning behaviors in mixed populations, and assessing how demographic settings and initial herd sizes influence system outcomes. The model allows for the evaluation of outcomes across social, ecological, and economic dimensions including average herd size, pasture biomass condition, and surviving number of households.

The average initial risk attitude among social learning agents (E-RO-SL1) remains constant at approximately 0.0074 throughout the entire simulation period from timestep 0 to timestep 100. This metric exhibits zero variance, maintaining identical values at the beginning and conclusion of the simulation sequence with no detectable upward or downward trends, oscillations, or inflection points across any observed interval.

In the context of the adaptive co-management framework, this stability indicates that the initial risk attitude serves as a fixed baseline attribute established at initialization, representing the homogeneous starting disposition of social learning households regarding deviation from recommended livestock levels. While the model implements updating mechanisms for current risk parameters (r-parameters) through social learning processes, this particular metric reflects an invariant initial condition that persists unchanged throughout the yearly sequences of vegetation dynamics, livestock placement decisions, and household observations, functioning as a static reference point against which adaptive behavioral changes can be evaluated.

The average degree of deviation from recommended livestock levels exhibits a three-phase dynamic over the 100-timestep simulation horizon. Initially, the metric begins at a negligible value of approximately 0.007, indicating near-complete adherence to recommended stocking rates during the establishment phase. Between timesteps 0 and 33, the deviation undergoes a steep monotonic increase, reaching a maximum value of 0.3814 at timestep 33. This rapid escalation suggests that agents quickly diverge from externally recommended livestock levels as they engage in learning processes and adapt to local pasture conditions on their private property patches.

Following the peak at timestep 33, the metric enters a transitional decline phase lasting until approximately timestep 50. During this interval, the deviation decreases from its maximum toward intermediate values, with statistical change-point analysis confirming timestep 50 as the boundary where the decreasing trend terminates. This decline likely reflects adjustment processes where agents moderate their initial divergence from recommendations based on observed ecological feedbacks or social learning outcomes.

From timestep 50 through the conclusion at timestep 100, the deviation stabilizes at a persistent plateau around 0.19 (ending at 0.1911), exhibiting no significant directional trend in the final intervals. This stabilization indicates that the system converges to a steady-state equilibrium where households maintain a moderate, persistent deviation from recommended stocking rates—neither returning to the initial compliance levels nor continuing the exploratory divergence seen in the early phase. The final deviation level of approximately 0.19 suggests that learning-by-doing and social learning processes lead to enduring customized grazing strategies that differ substantially from theoretical recommendations, reflecting the adaptation of stationary households to their specific private pasture conditions under heterogeneous uncertainty management.

The reserve biomass exhibits a pronounced U-shaped trajectory over the 100-timestep simulation period, characterized by an initial degradation phase followed by sustained recovery and growth. At initialization, the system maintains approximately 90,000 units of reserve biomass. During the first 26 timesteps, biomass undergoes a steep decline, reaching a minimum of approximately 84,114 units at timestep 26. This early contraction reflects initial grazing pressure as stationary households on private property patches deploy their livestock herds.

Following this nadir, the system enters a brief stabilization period between timesteps 26 and 33, where the decline halts and biomass levels plateau. From timestep 33 onward, the trajectory reverses definitively, initiating a prolonged recovery phase that continues through the remainder of the simulation. Between timesteps 33 and 54, the recovery accelerates markedly, with the steepest rates of increase occurring around timesteps 44-54. This period corresponds to the implementation of adaptive learning mechanisms, as households employing learning-by-doing (E-LBD) and social learning (E-RO-SL1) processes alongside baseline E-RO behaviors adjust their management strategies based on observed pasture conditions and neighbor performance.

After timestep 54, while the upward trend persists, the rate of biomass accumulation gradually decelerates, transitioning from steep increases to more moderate gains as the system approaches timestep 100. By the conclusion of the simulation at timestep 100, reserve biomass reaches approximately 121,073 units, representing a substantial increase above both initial levels and the minimum recorded at timestep 26. Notable inflection points occur at timestep 8 during the initial decline and at timestep 48 during the recovery phase, the latter marking a transition within the broader acceleration of biomass accumulation. The system exhibits oscillatory dynamics with a dominant period of approximately 49 timesteps, superimposed over the overarching recovery trend, suggesting cyclical vegetation responses to climatic variability and grazing pressure within the context of adaptive co-management.

This trajectory reflects the dynamics of a private-property grazing system where initial livestock pressure depletes vegetation, followed by adaptive recovery facilitated by learning mechanisms. The three behavioral types present—E-RO (baseline), E-LBD (learning-by-doing), and E-RO-SL1 (social learning)—appear to adjust grazing strategies through updating of risk parameters and experiential learning, allowing pasture recovery while maintaining household operations. The sustained growth beyond initial levels suggests that the combination of stationary households on private patches with adaptive learning behaviors eventually yields improved pasture conditions compared to the starting state, despite the absence of strategy switching between behavioral types.

At initialization, the system begins with an average livestock endowment of 90.0 animals per household. Over the subsequent 37 time steps, the aggregate herd size undergoes a pronounced decline, reaching a minimum value of approximately 41.0. This descent occurs with steeply diminishing velocity: the most rapid decrease takes place immediately following the start, with the rate of loss attenuating significantly around time step 3 and again near time step 20. Between approximately time steps 33 and 43, the trajectory stabilizes, exhibiting no significant directional trend and forming a transitional plateau that includes the minimum value observed at time step 37.  

Following this nadir, a recovery phase commences around time step 44 and continues through the remainder of the simulation period. The aggregate livestock count increases monotonically from approximately 41.0 to 47.5 by time step 100. This growth proceeds with progressively decreasing momentum; after an initial robust increase between time steps 44 and 54, the rate of recovery slows markedly following time step 55, with successive intervals showing ever-diminishing positive slopes. By the conclusion of the simulation at time step 100, the average herd size approaches 47.5, a value consistent with the median level of 47.3 maintained throughout the latter portion of the time series, yet remaining substantially below the initial stocking density.  

This pattern indicates an initial state of overstocking relative to the private pasture system's carrying capacity under variable climatic conditions, prompting a destocking adjustment that reaches its extreme around year 37. The subsequent partial recovery suggests that the heterogeneous population of agents—employing baseline, learning-by-doing, and social learning behavioral strategies—adaptively adjusts grazing and destocking decisions following the initial crisis. The system thus converges toward a lower equilibrium state characteristic of long-term social-ecological sustainability, where aggregate herd sizes stabilize at roughly half the initial density, reflecting the constraints imposed by private property boundaries, stationary household positioning, and stochastic rainfall variability.

The pattern reflects the model's learning mechanisms, where heterogeneous households with varying risk attitudes and learning capabilities (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types) initially overstock private pastures, necessitating significant destocking, but rapidly converge toward more sustainable stocking rates through adaptive decision-making processes.

This rapid diffusion continues between time steps 11 and 21 at a slightly decelerated rate of 1.14 households per step, bringing the engaged population to approximately 26 households.

A marked deceleration occurs around time step 22, with the growth rate dropping to approximately 0.18 households per step through time step 32, followed by a similarly modest increase of 0.18 households per step through time step 43. This slowing adoption continues through subsequent intervals, with rates declining to 0.029 households per step between time steps 44 and 54, and finally to 0.008 households per step between time steps 55 and 65. Time step 40 represents a significant change point where the system transitions from active diffusion toward saturation.

This trajectory reveals that social learning diffuses rapidly through approximately half the population in the first 40-60 time steps, driven by the interaction of heterogeneous behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under stationary private-property grazing conditions, but encounters strong saturation effects that prevent universal adoption despite the presence of social learning mechanisms in the system.
