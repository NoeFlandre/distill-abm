The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the simulation. Constant value directly reflects the immortal-foragers parameter setting. This prevents hunter mortality from energy depletion regardless of foraging success. Male-targeted hunting pressure rapidly extirpates the male subpopulation within approximately 3 to 5 years (1,000 to 2,000 ticks) this sex-biased mortality creates severe demographic consequences for herd viability, potentially leading to reproductive collapse. The rapid depletion of postpartum females within the first 500 steps suggests that hunting pressure concentrates on reproductively active females carrying dependents. The consistent plateau across runs indicates that once this demographic cohort is depleted, hunting pressure does not continue to accumulate on this specific group. The model consistently drives prey populations to near-extinction levels. A forager population of 20 individuals represents unsustainable hunting pressure. The population of male animals begins at approximately 125 individuals and undergoes a steady, monotonic decline toward extinction. Despite this variance in timing, the general pattern follows an initially steep descent during the first 500–1000 steps, followed by slower attrition as remnant populations succumb to continued hunting pressure and demographic constraints. The high variability in persistence times—ranging from rapid collapse within 500 steps to protracted decline over 2000 steps—reflects stochastic influences including random hunter-herd encounter rates. Between steps 500 and 1,000, the decline continues but exhibits increasing variance across simulation runs. Total energy falls from the 2,000-5,000 range toward near-zero values for most replicates, though some runs maintain 500 to 1,000 units. This suggests stochastic hunting success as male prey become scarce, forcing hunters to expend additional energy searching for rare targets. Grass regrowth mechanisms can replenish depleted vegetation when consumption pressures moderate. A second, attenuated decline occurs around step 750, followed by rapid restoration to 33% by step 800.
