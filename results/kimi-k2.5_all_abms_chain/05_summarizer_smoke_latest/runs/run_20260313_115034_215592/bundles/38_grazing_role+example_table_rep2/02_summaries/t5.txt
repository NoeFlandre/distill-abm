Model focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making. The average initial risk attitude among social learning households remains constant at approximately 0.0074 throughout the entire 100-year simulation period. This stability indicates that the cohort of social learning agents maintains its original composition with respect to initial risk dispositions. The fixed value reflects that this metric represents a static initialization parameter. Flection points around years nine and forty-nine, with the latter corresponding to a dominant oscillation period of roughly forty years. Adaptive management leads to more conservative grazing pressures than those characterizing the simulation's start, enabling pastures to stabilize at higher productivity levels than under initial stocking regimes. Despite identical initial conditions, the three distinct learning strategies produce differential outcomes in livestock management effectiveness. The accumulation of inequality proceeds through several distinct phases characterized by rapid adjustments in the rate at which inequality builds. A notable structural shift occurs around year 51, followed by another at year 71. Destocking rate asymptotically approaches 0.31 animals per year through year 100. Cyclical patterns correspond to multi-decadal vegetation succession cycles. Progressive dampening of oscillation amplitude through time suggests that both individual experience accumulation and social information diffusion reduced the volatility of stocking decisions as the simulation progressed.
