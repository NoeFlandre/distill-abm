**Context and Goals**

This agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Unlike the original RAGE model which examined common property scenarios where herders move livestock across shared rangelands, this extension focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods.

The simulation tracks the interaction between vegetation condition and household decisions about livestock numbers over time. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors.

The central purpose is understanding how these different ways of adapting to changing conditions influence pasture health, herd sizes, and the persistence of households. The model particularly examines how farmers handle uncertainty and incomplete information when making stocking decisions, represented through individual risk dispositions that affect how they perceive and react to environmental feedback.

**Key Parameters**

*Population and Spatial Setup:*
The **number-households** parameter establishes how many farming families occupy the landscape, which can range from 1 to 100 families, with this study using 60 households. Each household begins with **livestock-init** set at 90 animals. The simulation runs for **timesteps** set at 100 years. Farmers begin the simulation positioned on their designated land parcels, with **start-on-homepatch?** set to True. Each farmer can observe conditions within a **knowledge-radius** that ranges from 0 to 5 patches, set here at 1 patch distance.

*Behavioral Type Configuration:*
The study examines three specific learning strategies with equal representation: **number-E-RO** (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb), **number-E-LBD** (learning-by-doing), and **number-E-RO-SL1** (social learning with single-loop adaptation), each set to 20 households. The **homog-behav-types?** parameter set to True ensures each type is evaluated in uniform groups. The original RAGE model behavioral types are inactive in this extension: **number-SAT**, **number-TRAD**, **number-MAX**, and **number-random** are all set to 0. The default reference **behavioral-type** is set to E-RO.

*Grazing Management Practices:*
Formal pasture resting is disabled with **resting?** set to False. The threshold for initiating rest periods, **resting-threshold**, ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0.1. The **intrinsic-preference** for livestock relative to other values ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0. Similarly, **social-susceptibility** to neighbor influence ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0, indicating decisions rely on other factors.

*Original Model Parameters:*
Several parameters from the foundational RAGE model are retained. The **satisficing-threshold** for satisfied decision-making ranges from 0 to 100 and is set at 0. The **satisficing-trials** parameter, ranging from 0 to 100, is set at 21. These control how the original satisficer type would search for acceptable solutions, though this type is not active in the current setup.

*Ecological Conditions:*
Rainfall is applied uniformly across the landscape with **global-rain?** set to True. The average rainfall **rain-mean** is set at 200 units annually. Year-to-year variation in precipitation is controlled by **rain-std** set at 100, indicating significant variability in annual rainfall totals. Vegetation growth responds through two rates: **gr1** set at 0.5 and **gr2** set at 0.1. The parameter **w** from the original vegetation model is set at 0.8, influencing how pastures recover from grazing. Livestock reproduction is not active in this extension, with **b** set to 0.

*Learning and Risk Mechanisms:*
The extension module is activated with **extension-model?** set to True. Uncertainty handling is engaged with **risk-mode?** set to True. Each agent begins with an initial risk disposition **risk-att-init** set at 0.5, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance. The ability to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False, confining agents to single-loop learning within their assigned type.

The average initial risk attitude of social learning agents remains constant at approximately 0.0074 across all 100 time steps of the simulation. This stability reflects that initial risk disposition is a fixed attribute assigned to the 20 social learning households at the start of the simulation, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance.

The average degree of deviation from recommended livestock numbers follows a distinct non-monotonic trajectory over the 100-year simulation horizon. Initiating at minimal levels of approximately 0.007 during the early time steps (with the lowest values recorded around year 3), the metric undergoes a rapid ascent throughout the first three decades. This early phase (years 0 through approximately 32) exhibits consistently significant increasing trends, reflecting a progressive divergence from recommended stocking rates as farmers with varying learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning) accumulate experience and respond to environmental feedback on their private parcels.

Deviation reaches its maximum extent around year 33, peaking at approximately 0.38. This apex represents the most pronounced divergence from optimal stocking recommendations observed during the simulation. Following this peak, the trajectory reverses into a prolonged adjustment phase extending through approximately year 65, characterized by significant decreasing trends. During this interval, deviation declines substantially from the maximum, passing through a structural change point around year 50, though remaining well above the minimal initial levels. This decline suggests adaptive mechanisms gradually pulling stocking decisions back toward recommended levels after an initial period of over- or under-stocking relative to guidelines.

In the final decades (years 66 through 100), the deviation stabilizes in a relatively steady fluctuating equilibrium around approximately 0.19, exhibiting no statistically significant directional trends. By the conclusion of the simulation at year 100, values settle near 0.19—markedly below the mid-simulation peak yet substantially elevated compared to initial conditions. This persistent moderate deviation of roughly 0.19 suggests that while the learning processes partially correct stocking rates toward recommendations, factors such as individual risk dispositions, spatial heterogeneity across the 60 private pastures, and significant rainfall variability (with standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200) sustain residual differences from theoretical optima. Throughout the series, the metric displays oscillatory dynamics with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 years, indicating cyclical adjustments in how farmers balance experiential knowledge and social observations against ecological conditions.

Reserve biomass follows a pronounced U-shaped trajectory over the century-long simulation, marked by an initial degradation phase, a critical minimum, and a subsequent sustained recovery that eventually exceeds baseline conditions. The system begins with 90,000 units of biomass, which undergoes steep decline through the first decade as households apply initial stocking levels of 90 animals without full adaptation to local pasture dynamics and rainfall variability. This degradation moderates after year 8 but continues until reaching a minimum of approximately 84,114 units at year 26, representing the nadir of vegetation condition under persistent grazing pressure.  

After year 26, the system enters a recovery phase characterized by progressively increasing vegetation reserves. Between years 33 and 54, biomass accumulation accelerates dramatically, with the most rapid expansion occurring between years 44 and 54 when growth rates exceed 1,900 units annually. This period reflects the effectiveness of experiential learning and social observation within the one-patch knowledge radius, as households adjust stocking decisions to allow pasture recovery despite high inter-annual rainfall variation.  

The trajectory exhibits distinct inflection points at years 8 and 48. The first inflection marks the transition from rapid to moderating decline, while the second signals the shift from aggressive recovery to slower maturation. A structural change occurs around year 50, after which growth rates decelerate through successive phases: approximately 643 units annually during years 55–65, roughly 93 units during years 66–76, and finally 5–20 units annually in the closing decades (years 77–98).  

By year 100, reserve biomass reaches 121,073 units, surpassing initial conditions by approximately 34% and indicating that adaptive learning strategies eventually facilitate pasture conditions superior to the original state. Throughout the dynamics, cyclical oscillations appear around years 10–20 and 41–49, suggesting periodic adjustments in vegetation response as farmers repeatedly adapt stocking rates to environmental feedback and neighbor observations.

The average available green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units at year zero. During the initial twenty-seven years, the system undergoes a decline, reaching a minimum value of approximately 42,057 units at year twenty-seven. This period represents a degradation phase where vegetation biomass decreases by roughly seven percent from the initial state.

Following the minimum at year twenty-seven, the trend reverses and enters a sustained recovery phase. Between years twenty-seven and fifty, biomass increases steadily, with a notable acceleration in the rate of recovery occurring after year forty-four. From year fifty through year one hundred, the system continues to exhibit a consistent upward trajectory, though the rate of increase gradually moderates over time.

By the final time step at year one hundred, the average green biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units. This represents a net increase of roughly thirty-five percent above the initial starting value and a forty-four percent recovery from the minimum value observed at year twenty-seven.

In the context of the grazing system, the initial decline suggests that farmers' initial stocking decisions or learning strategies initially led to overgrazing and pasture degradation. The inflection point at year twenty-seven likely represents a critical threshold where accumulated experiential learning or social observation enabled households to adjust management practices effectively. The subsequent sustained improvement indicates that the learning mechanisms—whether through personal experience or neighbor observation—eventually facilitated the emergence of sustainable stocking rates that allowed vegetation recovery while supporting livestock populations.

The aggregate livestock holdings exhibit a pronounced non-monotonic trajectory characterized by an initial sharp decline followed by a gradual recovery toward a stable equilibrium. The system initiates with 90 animals per household. Over the first 37 years, holdings undergo a steep reduction, reaching a minimum value of 41.0089 animals per household at year 37. This contraction phase features an inflection around year 3, where the rate of decrease moderates from an initial steep slope, and a change in dynamics near year 20 as the decline continues but approaches its lower bound.

Following the minimum at year 37, the system enters a transitional period through approximately year 43 where livestock numbers stabilize without significant directional trend. A second inflection occurs around year 46, marking the onset of a recovery phase that becomes pronounced after year 55. During this recovery period, animal numbers increase steadily but with continuously diminishing marginal gains: rising from the minimum with moderate speed initially (years 44-54), then slowing progressively through years 55-65, 66-76, and 77-87, ultimately approaching an asymptote. By year 100, holdings reach 47.5472 animals per household, converging toward a long-term mean of 48.7946 animals.

This trajectory reflects the adaptation of stocking decisions to ecological constraints under variable rainfall conditions. The initial decline from 90 to 41 animals represents destocking responses to pasture degradation caused by initially excessive grazing pressure. The trough at year 37 indicates the point where reduced stocking allows vegetation recovery, while the subsequent slow recovery toward approximately 48 animals demonstrates how learning—whether through individual experience or observation of neighbors—enables households to cautiously rebuild herds toward a sustainable carrying capacity that accommodates environmental risk and uncertainty. The asymptotic approach to the final value suggests that farmers increasingly stabilize their decisions as they converge on risk-adjusted equilibrium stocking rates.

The system exhibits a pronounced decline in destocking pressure over the century-long simulation, transitioning from acute initial resource crises to a low-level maintenance regime. At initialization, no destocking occurs as the system begins with full livestock endowments. However, by year 3, destocking reaches its maximum intensity at approximately 6.6 animals per household, reflecting immediate pressure on vegetation from the initial stocking density of 90 animals per household under variable rainfall conditions. This early phase (years 0-30) is characterized by high volatility, with successive peaks at years 9 (~3.4 animals), 12 (~2.7 animals), and 17-19 (~1.5-1.4 animals) followed by intervening troughs, all demonstrating a strong decreasing trend as households reduce herds toward sustainable levels.

Following a structural change point around year 30, the system enters a prolonged stabilization phase lasting until approximately year 65. During this middle period, destocking rates fluctuate minimally around low values, exhibiting no significant monotonic trend. The oscillation amplitude diminishes substantially, with values generally remaining below 1.0 animal per household, suggesting that learning mechanisms—whether experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning—have enabled households to align stocking decisions with pasture carrying capacity despite persistent rainfall variability (coefficient of variation of 50%).

In the final third of the simulation (years 65-100), the dynamics shift again, with a weak but statistically significant increasing trend emerging between years 66-76, followed by stabilization at approximately 0.31 animals per household by year 100. This late-phase elevation suggests either accumulated vegetation degradation under continuous grazing or the manifestation of low-frequency rainfall cycles, specifically an oscillation with a dominant period of roughly 49 years that generates persistent, low-amplitude fluctuations throughout the latter half of the century.

Throughout the simulation, the combination of single-loop learning within fixed behavioral strategies and initial risk disposition of 0.5 enables progressive adaptation, driving median destocking levels down to approximately 0.31 animals, though the right-skewed distribution (mean ~0.96) preserves the memory of early crisis conditions. The persistence of low-level destocking through year 100 indicates that even with learning, the system maintains a baseline of nutritional stress under the imposed rainfall variability, preventing complete elimination of herd reductions despite the absence of livestock reproduction.

The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across the 60 households exhibits a sustained monotonic increase throughout the 100-year simulation horizon. Beginning from a state of perfect equality with a Gini coefficient of effectively zero, the metric rises progressively to reach 0.0726 by year 100, indicating a modest but consistent divergence in livestock health outcomes among households. The temporal mean of 0.0302 and median of 0.0229 confirm that the majority of the simulation period operates at relatively low inequality levels, with approximately half the duration occurring below the 0.023 threshold before accelerating toward higher values in later decades.

The trajectory is characterized by ten consecutive decades of statistically significant increasing trends (tau = 1.000, p < 0.001 across all ten-year rolling windows), though the rate of inequality accumulation varies across distinct phases. An initial establishment period through year 8 shows early divergence, followed by inflection points at years 13, 15, 20, and 25 suggesting successive adjustments in the rate at which inequality develops. The period between years 25 and 37 exhibits continued divergence before approaching a transitional phase marked by change points at years 51 and 71, where structural shifts in the inequality dynamics occur.

In the final three decades, the upward trend persists but becomes superimposed with oscillatory behavior detected between years 78 and 93, exhibiting a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 years. This late-stage fluctuation occurs while the overall inequality continues its ascent toward the terminal value of 0.0726.

Given the model context where all households begin with identical endowments of 90 livestock but employ three distinct learning strategies—baseline experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning with single-loop adaptation—the increasing inequality indicates that these different approaches to decision-making under uncertainty produce divergent long-term outcomes in livestock health. The gradual rather than immediate divergence suggests that differences in how households process environmental feedback and neighbor information compound slowly over time, with rainfall variability (std = 100) and individual risk dispositions (initially set at 0.5) interacting with the learning mechanisms to generate heterogeneity in pasture management effectiveness. The persistent upward trend across all observation windows implies that without behavioral strategy switching (double-loop learning disabled), initial performance differences between the three learning types continue to amplify rather than converge over the century-long simulation.

At the initiation of the simulation, no households engage in social learning. A rapid expansion phase follows during the first two decades, characterized by steeply increasing engagement as the learning strategy disseminates through the farming population. While remaining positive, the rate of increase decelerates substantially after approximately year 22, transitioning to a more gradual accumulation phase that persists through year 40. Around this point, a structural shift occurs, and the growth rate diminishes further to near-zero levels.

The system approaches saturation by year 63, reaching approximately 32 households—representing roughly 53 percent of the 60 farming households in the landscape. This level remains stable through the conclusion of the 100-year simulation. Minor oscillations in the engagement trajectory occur during the transition periods, particularly around years 22 through 32 and again between years 51 and 59, indicating temporary fluctuations as the system settles toward equilibrium. By the final year, social learning engagement has stabilized at approximately 32 households, suggesting that just over half of the farming community consistently employs social learning mechanisms in their pasture management decisions.

Over the 100-year simulation period, cumulative destocking exhibits a monotonic increase from zero to approximately 97 animals per household, following a decelerating trajectory with three distinct phases of accumulation.

The initial phase spanning approximately the first 25 years features the most rapid livestock removal, with the annual rate declining steeply from roughly 5 animals per year in the first decade to approximately 0.7 animals per year by year 25. This early period accounts for the majority of total destocking accumulation as households adjust from the initial stocking level of 90 animals toward sustainable levels appropriate for the highly variable rainfall regime.

Between years 25 and 60, the system enters a transitional phase where the destocking rate continues to diminish, falling from roughly 0.7 to 0.3 animals per year. This middle period contains numerous inflection points concentrated between years 45 and 75, indicating dynamic adjustments in the pace of livestock removal as experiential and social learning mechanisms refine stocking decisions and as households respond to environmental stochasticity. The change point at year 60 marks the transition to a stabilized regime.

From year 60 through year 100, cumulative destocking proceeds at a nearly constant rate of approximately 0.3 animals per year, contributing the final 12 animals to reach the terminal value of approximately 97. This late-phase stability suggests the system has approached equilibrium, where destocking represents normal turnover rather than crisis-driven reduction, with households having largely adapted their risk dispositions and stocking strategies to the variable ecological conditions. The median cumulative destocking of approximately 81 animals occurs around year 81, reflecting the left-skewed temporal distribution dominated by early rapid accumulation.

The oscillation pattern with a dominant period of approximately 49 years suggests that destocking decisions follow long cyclical patterns embedded within the overall trend, likely reflecting extended rainfall variability cycles. The absence of peaks or valleys confirms the irreversible nature of cumulative destocking, while the smooth deceleration indicates that learning mechanisms—whether through individual experience or observation of neighbors—gradually reduce the need for drastic livestock adjustments over time.

The simulation initializes with 20 households employing the E-RO (experiential rule-of-thumb) strategy. During the initial decade, the number of households following this approach exhibits a modest increase, peaking at approximately 21 households around year 8. 

Following this early peak, the system undergoes a marked transition characterized by a sustained decline in E-RO household counts. Between approximately years 10 and 65, the number of households decreases significantly, dropping from values near 20 to roughly 15. This downward trajectory is punctuated by change points around years 20 and 45, indicating shifts in the rate of decline. By year 45, the count falls to approximately 15.6, continuing to diminish toward a minimum near 14.7 reached by year 92.

In the final decades of the simulation (years 65 through 100), the decline attenuates and the series stabilizes, showing no statistically significant trend. The terminal value of approximately 14.7 households indicates that roughly one-quarter of the initially assigned E-RO households exit the system over the century-long simulation.

Overall, the trajectory reveals an early expansion or stability followed by a prolonged period of household attrition among non-learning agents, ultimately settling into a steady-state with reduced numbers persisting through the final third of the simulation period.
