The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remained constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation period. This metric exhibited no variation from the initial time step through the conclusion of the experiments, maintaining the starting value without deviation across all recorded intervals. The fixed hunter population isolates the effect of constant hunting pressure on megaherbivore extinction dynamics. Behavioral shifts occurring at regular 125-tick intervals (125, 250, 375, 500, 625) and a final inflection near tick 850. Pattern suggests initial intensive harvest of males followed by rapid depletion of accessible targets. Plateau indicates temporary cessation of net hunting mortality accumulation. The cumulative count increases by fewer than 3 individuals over approximately 750 ticks, reaching roughly 119 by tick 1336 and stabilizing near 122–123 by tick 1600. This plateau suggests effective cessation of female hunting, potentially indicating functional extinction of female herds, extreme prey scarcity forcing complete prey switching. Trend analysis confirms transition from monotonic increase in early windows to negligible or slightly negative trends in the final observation periods. Rapid saturation of cumulative mortality within approximately five years suggests that postpartum females face extreme vulnerability to hunting pressure due to their enhanced nutritional value. A secondary accumulation phase occurs from approximately tick 1,400 to tick 1,876, during which cumulative deaths rise again to reach a maximum value of approximately 240.3 deaths. The trajectory enters a stabilization phase where cumulative deaths hover between 235 and 240. This represents the upper bound of mortality observed across the simulation series. Temporary recovery during phase three may indicate a window of reduced hunting efficiency, stochastic demographic escape, or the maturation of subsequent cohorts temporarily offsetting mortality. However, the sustained low median population level of approximately 1.3 individuals across the full temporal sequence indicates that for the majority of the simulation, the male population existed in a critically endangered state. Local population maxima occur with progressively diminishing amplitude. The population enters its terminal decline after tick 2087, representing complete megaherbivore extinction. Decision makers should interpret the intermittent population increases occurring during years 4–6 as stochastic demographic noise. Rapid initial depletion within the first 350 ticks indicates intervention thresholds must be established and triggered almost immediately upon detection of hunting activity. However, the ultimate extinction outcome despite this resilience implies that sustained hunting pressure creates an extinction debt that manifests after approximately 6.25 years (2,284 ticks), rendering short-term recovery indicators unreliable. The total available energy oscillated with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks (roughly bi-monthly cycles given the temporal scaling of 365 ticks per year) these oscillations generated temporary stabilization points where energy levels briefly plateaued—evidenced by recurring local peaks and valleys.
