 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model . It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes .  The average available green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units, declining to a minimum of roughly 42,057 units by time step 27 . The reserve biomass begins a marked decline during the initial quarter of the simulation period, reaching its minimum value of approximately 84,114 units by step 26 . The growth rate diminishes from nearly 1,909 units per time step in the mid-range intervals to approximately 5 units by the final periods .  The average level of livestock destocking begins at zero, then surges to a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals per household by year 3 . After the initial spike, destocking declines precipitously through the first decade, dropping sharply from the peak to roughly 3.3 by year 8, with secondary fluctuations occurring around years 9 and 12 at values of approximately 3.4 and 2.7 respectively .  The pattern reflects the differential performance of stationary households operating under private property regimes with distinct adaptive strategies . While all households begin with equivalent livestock endowments, the divergence in outcomes indicates that baseline optimization, learning-by-doing processes, and social learning mechanisms produce heterogeneous success in maintaining livestock health over time .  The persistence of approximately 14 to 15 E-RO households throughout the latter half of the simulation suggests that a subset of non-learning agents achieves long-term stability despite the absence of adaptive mechanisms . This may be due to favorable local pasture conditions or conservative initial strategies .
