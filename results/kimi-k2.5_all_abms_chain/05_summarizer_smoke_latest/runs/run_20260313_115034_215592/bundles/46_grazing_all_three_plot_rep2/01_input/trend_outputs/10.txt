The count of non-learning households, represented by agents following the baseline experiential rule-of-thumb (E-RO) strategy, commences at 20 across all simulation runs, consistent with the initial population configuration. Within the first 10 steps, the trajectories diverge substantially, indicating high sensitivity to early stochastic conditions in the grazing system.

During the initial phase spanning steps 10 to 40, distinct patterns emerge across different simulation realizations. Several runs exhibit increases in non-learning households, reaching peak values between 35 and 42 approximately around steps 25 to 35. In contrast, other runs experience precipitous declines during this same interval, with counts falling below 5 households by step 40, and some approaching zero.

From steps 40 through 100, the system stabilizes into heterogeneous persistence regimes. A subset of runs maintains elevated counts of non-learning households, settling between 23 and 28 by the conclusion of the simulation. Another subset stabilizes at intermediate levels, specifically between 10 and 18 households, while exhibiting moderate fluctuations. A third group reaches minimal persistence, with counts remaining at or below 5 households from approximately step 60 onward, including scenarios where the metric approaches zero and persists at that level through step 100.

By the final timestep, the distribution of outcomes spans the full range from near zero to approximately 26 households, with substantial variance across runs. This indicates that reliance on static rule-of-thumb decision-making without active learning produces highly unpredictable long-term viability, exposing stationary farmers to significant risk of exit from the system while allowing for sustained persistence under specific environmental and interaction conditions.

For decision makers, this variability implies that promoting exclusive reliance on traditional non-adaptive grazing practices may lead to unstable community structures, with some realizations resulting in near-complete loss of this population segment while others maintain moderate representation. Interventions supporting adaptive learning mechanisms may be necessary to ensure more predictable household persistence in variable rangeland environments.
