 The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system . Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods .  The system exhibits a pattern of initial degradation followed by sustained recovery over the first three decades . The recovery phase features distinct sub-periods: an initial acceleration of biomass accumulation from years 33 to 54, followed by a prolonged deceleration phase from years 55 to 100 .  The simulation begins with each household maintaining 90 livestock . Initial stocking levels of 90 animals per household exceed sustainable carrying capacity under the variable rainfall regime . As farmers employ learning-by-doing and social learning strategies, they progressively reduce herd sizes . Because livestock reproduction is inactive, these reductions permanently decrease animal numbers .  The simulation reveals a consistent long-term trend toward increasing inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock among the 60 households . Despite identical starting conditions, the combination of distinct learning strategies and environmental variability drives households toward divergent outcomes over the century-long period . The accumulation of inequality proceeds through distinct phases marked by changing rates of divergence .  The population of households employing the non-learning E-RO strategy undergoes a characteristic trajectory of initial stability followed by sustained attrition and eventual stabilization . A cyclical oscillation with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, manifesting at indices clustered around years 22–26, 49–61, and 79–80 . This suggests that destocking decisions pulse in response to recurring drought or recovery cycles .
