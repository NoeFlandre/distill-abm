The cumulative number of prey deaths begins at zero and increases rapidly during the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, reaching values between 150 and 230. This initial surge represents the loss of roughly 60 to 90 percent of the initial population of 250 individuals, indicating intense mortality pressure from the combined effects of hunting and natural causes during the early simulation period.

Following this rapid initial increase, the rate of mortality accumulation decelerates markedly between approximately 500 and 1000 time steps. During this transitional phase, cumulative deaths continue to rise but at a substantially reduced pace, reaching approximately 220 to 240 total deaths. This slowing suggests that the remaining prey population has become increasingly scarce, making encounters between hunters and prey less frequent and allowing some individuals to persist in refugia or through stochastic avoidance of detection.

After approximately 1000 to 1500 time steps, the cumulative death count stabilizes and plateaus, with final values ranging between approximately 220 and 250 across different simulation runs. The cessation of new mortality events indicates that the prey population has been effectively eliminated or reduced to levels where deaths no longer occur, with the cumulative total approaching the initial population size and suggesting near-complete turnover of the initial cohort.

Considerable variability exists across simulation runs in both the timing and trajectory of mortality accumulation. Some runs reach 200 cumulative deaths by approximately step 400, while others require 600 or more steps to achieve comparable mortality levels. Final cumulative death totals vary between approximately 220 and 250, and the point at which mortality ceases ranges from roughly 1200 to 2000 steps. This variability reflects stochastic elements in hunter-prey encounter rates, movement patterns, and demographic events.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The model demonstrates that megaherbivore populations of 250 individuals face high extinction risk within approximately 1000 to 1500 time steps (roughly 3 to 4 years) under the specified hunting pressure of 20 foragers. The rapid initial mortality surge leaves minimal time for population recovery through reproduction, suggesting that management interventions must address hunting pressure immediately to prevent rapid population collapse. The variability across runs indicates that while extinction is highly probable, stochastic factors create uncertainty in timing, requiring robust monitoring systems to detect population declines before they become irreversible.
