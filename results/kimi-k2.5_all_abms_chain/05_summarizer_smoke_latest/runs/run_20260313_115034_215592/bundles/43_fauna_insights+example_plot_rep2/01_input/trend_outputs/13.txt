The combined total energy available in all prey animals starts at approximately 25,000 units across simulation runs. It declines rapidly over the first 500 time steps, dropping to values predominantly between 0 and 8,000 units, with the majority of trajectories falling below 5,000 units. This initial rapid depletion indicates that the interaction between the initial population of 250 prey animals and 20 human hunters creates immediate unsustainable predation pressure, where hunting mortality and processing costs exceed the herds' capacity for reproduction, energy accumulation from grass resources, and demographic replacement.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the decline continues but exhibits considerable variability across different simulation runs. Some trajectories show temporary plateaus or minor fluctuations between steps 400 and 800, suggesting brief periods where stochastic elements in herd movement, hunter foraging decisions, or demographic events temporarily slow the depletion of prey energy reserves. However, these variations do not reverse the overall downward trajectory, and the energy levels continue to trend toward zero.

By approximately step 1000 to 1500, the total energy in most simulation runs approaches zero, and by step 2000, nearly all runs have reached values at or near zero. This terminal phase indicates the functional extinction of the megaherbivore population, where remaining individuals lack sufficient energy reserves to sustain the population against continued hunting pressure, and the system has reached a state where prey energy availability is effectively exhausted.

The high variance observed between steps 300 and 800 suggests that stochastic factors in animal movement, hunter decision-making, and demographic stochasticity create divergent extinction trajectories during the middle phase of population collapse, even though all runs ultimately converge on the same endpoint of near-zero energy availability. The initial low variance in the first 100 steps indicates that the early response to hunting pressure is highly deterministic, while later stages introduce more complexity and unpredictability in the timing of energy depletion.

Implications for decision makers: The rapid and irreversible decline in prey energy reserves under the tested parameter configuration suggests that hunting pressure from 20 foragers represents a catastrophic threat to megaherbivore populations with the specified life history traits (28-year maximum lifespan, 2-year birth spacing, 20-year senescence onset). Management strategies would need to either reduce hunter density significantly, implement measures to increase prey fecundity or reduce hunting efficiency, or enhance environmental productivity to prevent rapid population collapse. The variability in middle-phase trajectories suggests that stochastic factors might offer brief windows for intervention, but the overall deterministic trend toward extinction indicates that sustainable coexistence between hunters and prey is unlikely without substantial parameter modification or regulatory intervention.
