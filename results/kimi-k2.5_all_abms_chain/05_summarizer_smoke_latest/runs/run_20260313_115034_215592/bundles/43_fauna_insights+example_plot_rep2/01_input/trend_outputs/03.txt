The cumulative mortality of male ungulates begins at zero and increases rapidly through discrete hunting events. During the initial 500 time steps, representing approximately the first 1.4 years of simulation time, kills accumulate steeply from 0 to values ranging between 80 and 115 individuals. This corresponds to an average extraction rate of roughly 0.16 to 0.23 males per step, reflecting intensive hunting pressure by the 20 forager agents targeting males with their higher food value of 30 and moderate processing cost of 6.

Between steps 500 and 1000 (years 1.4 to 2.7), the accumulation rate decelerates markedly as cumulative kills approach 100 to 120 individuals. This slowdown indicates declining encounter rates as the male population, initially comprising roughly 125 individuals assuming equal sex distribution within the starting cohort of 250 prey, becomes depleted. Hunters must expend greater search effort or wait longer between successful pursuits, reducing the per-step probability of male kills.

After approximately 1000 to 1500 steps, cumulative mortality stabilizes into plateaus across most simulation runs, with final values converging between 110 and 125 males killed. Some trajectories reach saturation as early as step 1000, while others continue accumulating sporadic kills until approximately step 2000. The final extraction totals approach the theoretical maximum initial male population size, indicating effective extirpation of the male cohort within 3 to 5.5 years.

The substantial variation in saturation timing—spanning roughly 1000 steps between the earliest and latest plateaus—suggests that stochastic elements in herd movement patterns, grass distribution (set to 33% coverage with seasonality of 0.8), and forager search behavior introduce significant uncertainty in the extinction trajectory. However, the deterministic convergence toward the maximum possible extraction indicates that male population collapse is inevitable under the specified parameter configuration.

Implications for decision makers: The data demonstrates that male megaherbivore populations face extremely rapid collapse under hunting pressure, with effective extinction occurring within 2–5 years. Given the sexual dimorphism in food value (males valued at 30 versus females at 27) and the higher processing cost for males (6 versus 3 for females), hunters exhibit clear preference for males, driving their population to exhaustion while the broader herd may persist. This creates a functional extinction scenario where breeding capacity is eliminated even if female survivors remain. Management strategies must implement sex-specific protections for males or reduce forager density below the critical threshold of 20 agents to prevent the reproductive collapse that precedes total population extinction.
