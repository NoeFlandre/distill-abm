Model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making. The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. Household-risk-att-init represents predetermined baseline disposition of socially learning agents. Initial risk configuration remains fixed regardless of ecological feedbacks, livestock dynamics, neighborhood observations, or strategy updates occurring throughout the 100-year simulation period. The 49-timestep oscillation period reveals inherent cyclical dynamics that persist despite agent adaptation. The subsequent recovery demonstrates that patience in allowing social and experiential learning mechanisms to mature yield superior long-term pasture conditions. The initial recovery phase (timesteps 44–54) shows relatively robust growth with a slope of 0.313 units per timestep. The simulation concludes with average total livestock stabilizing at approximately 47.5 units per household. Key inflection points at timesteps 3 and 46 mark transitions between the steep decline phase and the moderating decline phase. Inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households begins at approximately zero, indicating perfect equality in initial conditions. Over the course of 100 time steps, this metric exhibits a consistent upward trajectory, reaching a final value of 0.0726. Ating adoption curve at approximately half the population suggests uniform social learning policies may face inherent ceilings in heterogeneous smallholder contexts. Rapid initial growth phase demonstrates that early investment in social learning infrastructure yield diminishing returns after saturation. Average cumulative number of destocked livestock increases monotonically throughout the 100-timestep simulation, beginning at 0 and reaching approximately 96.8 animals per household by the final timestep. Heterogeneity in risk management and local conditions allows some non-learning households to persist, despite the competitive advantage held by E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types. Early simulation periods may temporarily mask this vulnerability, but adaptive capacity becomes critical after approximately 10–20 timesteps.
