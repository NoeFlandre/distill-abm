The simulations initiate with a total prey population of 250 individuals. Across all stochastic replicates, the population exhibits a consistent monotonic decline from this initial state, progressing systematically toward extinction over the temporal course of the experiments.

The rate of population reduction varies substantially across simulation runs, reflecting the stochastic nature of agent interactions. The most rapid collapses reduce populations to zero by approximately step 500, while more persistent trajectories maintain populations numbering approximately 100 individuals at this same temporal benchmark. By step 1000, the majority of simulation runs have reduced prey numbers to fewer than 50 individuals, with many approaching zero.

The final phase of population persistence occurs between steps 1000 and 2000, during which the remaining individuals in slower-declining runs are gradually eliminated through continued hunting pressure. By step 2000, essentially all simulation trajectories have converged upon population extinction or near-extinction states with fewer than 5 individuals remaining.

This consistent pattern of population collapse across all stochastic replicates indicates that the hunting pressure exerted by the initial cohort of 20 human foragers, operating under conditions of food storage and immortality, generates a per-capita mortality rate that systematically exceeds the reproductive replacement capacity of the prey population throughout the simulation duration. The variation in extinction timing across runs reflects stochastic differences in encounter rates and spatial dynamics, while the ultimate convergence on extinction demonstrates the deterministic outcome of the specified parameter configuration, wherein 20 hunters with processing capabilities and energy storage inevitably deplete the initial population of 250 ungulates despite the presence of vegetation regrowth mechanisms.
