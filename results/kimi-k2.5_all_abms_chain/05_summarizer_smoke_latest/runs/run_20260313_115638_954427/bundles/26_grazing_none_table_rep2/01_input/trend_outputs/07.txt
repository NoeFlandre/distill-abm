The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households begins at effectively zero at the initiation of the simulation, indicating near-perfect equality in livestock health endowments among the 60 households. Over the subsequent 100 timesteps, this inequality metric exhibits a consistent monotonic increase, reaching approximately 0.073 by the final timestep. The trajectory is characterized by a steady upward trend with an overall mean value of roughly 0.03 and a median around 0.023, indicating that for most of the simulation period, inequality remains relatively modest despite the continuous accumulation of differences among households.

The rate of increase in inequality is not strictly linear, with notable inflections occurring around timesteps 8, 13, 15, and 25, suggesting episodic accelerations in the differentiation of livestock health outcomes. More substantial shifts in the trajectory are evident around timestep 51 and again at timestep 71, where the trend exhibits change points that demarcate periods of altered accumulation dynamics. These temporal patterns suggest that the initial decades of the simulation establish baseline differentiation, followed by accelerated divergence in the middle period (around years 50-70), and continued but potentially stabilizing accumulation toward the end of the century-long simulation.

In the context of this private-property grazing system with stationary households and heterogeneous learning behaviors, the observed trends indicate that despite starting from equivalent livestock health conditions, the combination of environmental variability (rainfall mean 200, standard deviation 100), distinct behavioral strategies (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), and localized social learning within a knowledge radius of 1, generates progressive differentiation in livestock health outcomes. The final Gini coefficient of approximately 0.07 suggests that while inequality in healthy livestock distribution increases substantially from its initial state, the system maintains a relatively low overall level of inequality, implying that the learning mechanisms and social interactions among the 60 households serve to constrain extreme disparities in livestock health even as adaptive differentiation occurs.
