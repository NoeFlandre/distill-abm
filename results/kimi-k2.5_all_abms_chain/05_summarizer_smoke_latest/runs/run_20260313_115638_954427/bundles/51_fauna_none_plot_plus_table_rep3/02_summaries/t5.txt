The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The population of human hunter-gatherer bands remained fixed at exactly 20 agents throughout the observed simulation period spanning 2,312 time steps. The metric exhibited zero variance, maintaining the initial value of 20 from the simulation start at step 0 through the final recorded step. The subsequent plateau indicates that the hunter population of 20 immortal foragers effectively exploits the male prey population to near-local extinction within the foraging radius (6 patches) the cumulative mortality of female prey initiates at zero and follows a markedly sigmoidal trajectory over the course of the simulations. Metric exhibits sharp acceleration during the first 500 steps, punctuated by change points at regular 125-step intervals (125, 250, 375, 500, 625) and a major inflection around step 362. Between approximately step 500 and step 1200, the rate of mortality accumulation decelerates substantially. While the trend remains significantly increasing through step 1159, the slope diminishes considerably after step 850. Male megaherbivores cannot sustain a viable population against the imposed hunting pressure, despite their food value of 30 energy units and the two-year birth interval limiting recruitment. The population undergoes a precipitous reduction from 125 to approximately 20 individuals, representing the most rapid rate of decrease. Median population value across simulation runs remains below 1.5 individuals for the majority of the temporal sequence. Some simulations exhibit rapid extinction occurring within the first 500 to 1000 steps. Others demonstrate prolonged persistence with populations fluctuating at low densities for 2000 or more steps before final collapse. Following tick 500, the system entered a prolonged decline phase punctuated by temporary stabilization and minor recoveries. A second significant increasing trend occurred between ticks 1,856 and 2,087 (tau = 0.453), suggesting a temporary recovery in female prey energy reserves. After tick 2,000, total energy declined precipitously from residual values below 500 units. The metric maintains long-term stability around a mean of 32.66%. The oscillatory behavior reflects the interaction of strong seasonal constraints. Rapid grass regeneration (3-tick regrowth time) and herbivore grazing pressure.
