This initial decline suggests that grazing pressure from the initial livestock endowment temporarily exceeds the vegetation's regenerative capacity, depleting the green biomass available across the private pasture patches.

Following the minimum at time step 27, the biomass enters a recovery phase. From time steps 33 to 43, a significant increasing trend emerges, marking the beginning of vegetation recovery. This recovery accelerates substantially between time steps 44 and 54, where the rate of increase reaches its maximum slope. The increasing trend continues steadily through the remainder of the simulation, though with gradually decreasing rates of change as the system approaches higher biomass levels.

By time step 100, the average green biomass reaches approximately 60,535, representing a 34% increase from the initial value and a 44% increase from the minimum value observed at step 27. The trajectory indicates that after an initial period of resource depletion, the adaptive behaviors of the heterogeneous agent population—comprising E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types—enable the system to transition toward improved pasture management. The learning processes, particularly learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms, appear to facilitate grazing decisions that allow vegetation recovery and sustained biomass accumulation over the long term, ultimately establishing pasture conditions that exceed the initial state.
