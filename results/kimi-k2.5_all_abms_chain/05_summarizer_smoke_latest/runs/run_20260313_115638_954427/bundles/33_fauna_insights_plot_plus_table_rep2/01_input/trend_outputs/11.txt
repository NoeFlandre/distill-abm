The total energy available in the male prey population initiated at approximately 12,500 energy units and declined monotonically to complete depletion (zero) by approximately tick 2,284. The trajectory exhibited a strongly non-linear decrease characterized by three distinct phases of varying decay rates.

During the initial phase spanning roughly ticks 0-500, the population experienced rapid attrition, with total male energy falling sharply from the initial 12,500 to approximately 4,000 by tick 377. This represents a decline of roughly 68% within the first simulation year. The rate of decline reached its maximum velocity between ticks 232-463, with slopes approaching 28 energy units per tick. A temporary stabilization occurred around tick 65, where the population registered approximately 11,608 energy units before resuming its descent.

The intermediate phase (approximately ticks 500-1,400) was marked by moderated depletion rates and increased variability across simulation runs. Between ticks 464-927, the rate of decline slowed substantially to slopes of approximately 2.8-10.7 units per tick, and further decelerated to less than one unit per tick between ticks 928-1,159. Notably, between ticks 1,392-1,623, the aggregate trend reversed temporarily, exhibiting a weak positive slope of approximately 0.091 units per tick, suggesting brief demographic recovery or density-dependent reductions in hunting efficiency in the remaining populations.

The terminal phase (approximately ticks 1,400-2,284) witnessed the final depletion of male energy reserves. While the median energy value across all observations was 134.5—substantially lower than the mean of 1,984, indicating right-skewed distribution toward depleted states—the system exhibited prolonged persistence at low energy levels before ultimate extinction. By tick 2,284, all simulation runs converged on zero male energy, though individual runs reached depletion at varying times, with the majority exhausting male populations between ticks 500-1,500.

Superimposed on the overall decline, the dynamics exhibited an oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks (roughly 2-month cycles), manifesting as regular fluctuations in the rate of energy depletion. Statistical change points at ticks 125, 350, 500, 625, and 875 delineate transitions between distinct regimes of hunting pressure and population response.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The simulation results indicate that under the current parameter configuration—featuring 20 immortal hunters, male food value of 30, processing cost of 6, and the specified life history traits—male megaherbivore populations face rapid functional extinction within approximately 6-7 years. The steep initial decline phase (68% reduction in year one) suggests that hunting pressure substantially exceeds the population's maximum sustainable yield. While temporary stabilization around 4,000 energy units and weak recovery periods demonstrate some density-dependent resilience, these mechanisms prove insufficient to prevent ultimate extinction. Management interventions would need to reduce hunter density or increase processing costs/energy expenditure for hunters to shift the system away from the rapid depletion regime observed in the initial 500 ticks. The 63-tick oscillation cycles indicate that population monitoring should account for regular bi-monthly fluctuations when assessing conservation status, as these may temporarily obscure the underlying extinction trajectory.
