 Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change . Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances . Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores .  The population of human forager agents remains constant at the initial value of 20 individuals throughout the entire simulation period, from step 0 through approximately step 2200, with no observed fluctuations, growth, or decline in hunter numbers . The fixed hunter population represents a constant predation pressure that does not respond to changes in prey availability or foraging returns .  Given an initial population of 250 ungulates and the temporal calibration of 365 steps per year, the asymptotic values approaching 120 deaths within approximately 5.5 years suggest hunters successfully kill the vast majority of available males . The rapid sex-specific mortality creates severely male-depleted populations that may trigger reproductive Allee effects or genetic bottlenecks, potentially leading to population collapse .  The data reveal trajectories of cumulative mortality for postpartum females that follow a consistent three-phase pattern across simulation runs . During the initial phase spanning roughly the first 500 steps, the cumulative count increases most rapidly, rising sharply from zero to values between 80 and 110 . This period represents the highest intensity of hunting mortality for nursing females and those with dependent offspring . Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of accumulation decelerates substantially, with most trajectories adding only 10 to 20 additional  The number of male animals began at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation replicates . From this initial state, every simulation exhibited a consistent downward trajectory, with populations declining progressively over time . By step 1000, most populations had fallen below 20 individuals, though some trajectories maintained 5–10 animals for extended periods .  The simulation ensemble exhibits consistent population collapse dynamics across all replicates, with deterministic extinction trajectories despite stochastic variation in the rate of decline . The total energy of male animals initiates at approximately 12,000 units, reflecting the initial energetic state of the male ungulate subpopulation distributed across the landscape grid . By step 1000, the vast majority of trajectories (approximately 95%) have reached populations of 10 or fewer animals .  The simulations demonstrate a consistent pattern of rapid depletion in the aggregate energy reserves of female ungulate populations, followed by eventual collapse to zero across all experimental runs . Conservation strategies must specifically address hunter selectivity for large-bodied males, as this trait combination drives the rapid energetic trajectories observed .  At approximately step 250, all simulation runs undergo a catastrophic decline, with vegetation coverage plummeting to between 23 and 24 percent in most runs . This nadir represents a period of intense grazing pressure where herbivore consumption substantially exceeds the regrowth rate of 3 ticks per patch . By step 400, vegetation coverage rebounds to approximately 32.5 to 33 percent across nearly all simulations .
