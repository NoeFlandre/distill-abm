Available green biomass across the pasture system exhibited a characteristic trajectory of initial pressure followed by adaptive recovery over the 100-timestep simulation period. Beginning at approximately 45,000 units, biomass remained relatively stable through the first nine timesteps before entering a decline phase. This reduction intensified between timesteps 11 and 21, reaching a system-wide minimum of roughly 42,057 units at timestep 27, representing the nadir of pasture conditions under initial grazing pressure.

Following this trough, the system underwent a protracted recovery spanning the remaining 73 timesteps. A transitional stabilization occurred between timesteps 22 and 32, after which biomass entered a sustained increasing trend from timestep 33 onward. The recovery accelerated significantly between timesteps 33 and 54, with rapid accumulation of biomass, before transitioning to a more gradual, decelerating growth pattern from timestep 55 through timestep 100. By the conclusion of the simulation, green biomass attained its maximum value of approximately 60,535 units—marking a net increase of over 34% from initial conditions and roughly 44% above the minimum recorded value.

This dynamic reflects the interplay between substantial initial livestock endowments (90 animals per household across 60 stationary private-property households) and the model's learning mechanisms. The early decline suggests that initial stocking rates exceeded immediate sustainable carrying capacity, or that agents required time to calibrate their uncertainty management parameters (initially set to 0.5) to local conditions. The pronounced recovery from timestep 27 onward indicates that the heterogenous population of E-RO (baseline), E-LBD (learning-by-doing), and E-RO-SL1 (social learning) behavioral types successfully adapted their grazing strategies. The steep growth phase between timesteps 33 and 54 likely corresponds to the diffusion of effective management practices through social learning and the refinement of individual decision-making through experience, enabling biomass accumulation despite high rainfall variability (coefficient of variation of 0.5). The subsequent deceleration of growth rates suggests the system approached a sustainable equilibrium under adaptive co-management, where improved pasture conditions stabilized at higher productivity levels than those observed initially.
