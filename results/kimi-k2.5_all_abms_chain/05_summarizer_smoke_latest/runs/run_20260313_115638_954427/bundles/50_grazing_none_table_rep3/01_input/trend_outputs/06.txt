At initialization, no destocking occurs. Immediately thereafter, at timestep 3, the average number of destocked livestock reaches a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals per household. This initial shock represents a period of severe resource constraint where households are forced to rapidly reduce herd sizes.

Following this peak, the system enters a phase of high volatility but declining amplitude. Local maxima occur at timesteps 9 (approximately 3.4 animals), 12 (approximately 2.7 animals), 17 (approximately 1.5 animals), and 22 (approximately 1.2 animals), with each successive peak representing lower destocking pressure than the previous. Intervening troughs show corresponding decreases, falling below 1.0 animal by timestep 18 and continuing to diminish.

After approximately timestep 30, the dynamics stabilize significantly. The system converges toward a low steady state, with values fluctuating narrowly around 0.3 animals by timestep 100. This represents a 95% reduction from the initial crisis peak.

Statistically, the early period (timesteps 0-32) exhibits significant decreasing trends across multiple consecutive windows, indicating consistent improvement in resource management. The middle period shows no significant trend, while late periods demonstrate slight variability but maintain near-zero destocking levels.

Interpreted within the context of the RAGE extension model, these dynamics reflect the efficacy of adaptive learning mechanisms in private property grazing systems. The initial destocking spike corresponds to the adjustment period where stationary households face initial resource scarcity. The subsequent decline illustrates how learning-by-doing (E-LBD) and social learning (E-RO-SL1) behaviors, combined with baseline E-RO strategies, enable households to progressively optimize stocking rates and reduce forced destocking. The final stabilization at low levels indicates that after approximately 30 timesteps, the mixed population of learning agents achieves a sustainable equilibrium where resource constraints rarely necessitate emergency herd reductions.
