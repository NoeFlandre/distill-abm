The number of households engaged in social learning initiates at zero and follows a saturation trajectory over the 100-timestep simulation period, ultimately stabilizing at approximately 32 households. This progression exhibits distinct phases of adoption characteristic of diffusion processes within the stationary, private-property grazing context.

During the initial phase spanning timesteps 0 to 7, engagement remains negligible at near-zero levels, representing a lag period before the establishment of social learning networks. Following this initiation lag, a rapid acceleration phase occurs between timesteps 7 and 28, characterized by steep monotonic increases where the number of participating households rises sharply from near-zero to approximately 20-25 households. This period corresponds to the active diffusion of social learning behaviors as households observe neighbor performance and update their uncertainty management parameters.

A deceleration phase follows from approximately timestep 28 to timestep 63, where the rate of new adoption diminishes considerably though the absolute number continues to increase gradually from roughly 25 to the maximum value of approximately 32.07 households observed at timestep 63. This slowing reflects the saturation of the adoption curve as the pool of remaining non-adopting households diminishes within the fixed population of 60 households.

From timestep 63 through the conclusion at timestep 100, the system enters a stabilization phase where engagement maintains approximately 32 households with minimal variation, ending at 32.07. This plateau represents an equilibrium where roughly 53% of the household population maintains active social learning engagement, suggesting that under the specified parameter configuration with heterogeneous behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), social learning processes diffuse beyond the initially designated E-RO-SL1 agents to encompass a substantial portion of the farming community, despite the absence of strategy switching mechanisms.
