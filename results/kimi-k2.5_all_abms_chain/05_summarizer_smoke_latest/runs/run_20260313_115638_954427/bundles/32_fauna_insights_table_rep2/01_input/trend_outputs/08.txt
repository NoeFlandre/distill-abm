The male megaherbivore population initiated at 125 individuals and underwent a pronounced, sustained decline to extinction over the simulation period. The trajectory was characterized by three distinct phases: an initial steep decline, a brief period of modest recovery, and a final terminal decline to zero.

During the initial phase from the start through approximately tick 875, the population experienced a rapid decrease from 125 individuals, with statistically significant downward trends persisting through tick 1391. This period represents intensive hunting pressure overwhelming the population's demographic resilience, with foragers efficiently exploiting the initially abundant prey.

Between approximately ticks 1392 and 1623, the population exhibited a temporary reversal, showing a statistically significant increasing trend despite the overall declining trajectory. This brief recovery likely reflects a demographic lag, temporary spatial refugia as herds adjusted movement patterns, or reduced hunting efficiency as prey densities declined and foraging costs increased for hunters operating under optimal foraging constraints.

Following this respite, from tick 1624 through approximately tick 2284, the population entered its terminal phase with no significant directional trend but fluctuating at low densities before finally reaching zero individuals at tick 2284. The median population size across the entire simulation was approximately 1.35 individuals, underscoring the severity of the decline. The extinction event occurred approximately 62.7 simulation years into the 200-year potential timeframe.

Insights for decision makers: The model demonstrates that with 20 hunters operating under the specified foraging parameters, male megaherbivore populations face inevitable extinction due to unsustainable harvest rates. The brief recovery phase between ticks 1392-1623 suggests that temporary population rebounds should not be interpreted as recovery, but rather as demographic noise preceding extinction. Management strategies must prioritize reducing hunter numbers or encounter rates well before populations decline to critically low levels, as recovery potential diminishes precipitously once densities fall below approximately 10 individuals.
