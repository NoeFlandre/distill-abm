The total energy contained within the male megaherbivore population initiates at 12,500 units and follows a deterministic trajectory toward complete depletion, reaching zero by approximately tick 2,284 across all 40 simulation replicates. The decline unfolds through several distinct phases characterized by markedly different rates of decrease.

During the initial phase from tick 0 through tick 695, the population experiences catastrophic energy loss with steep negative slopes ranging from -28.1 to -10.7 units per tick. This rapid descent reduces total male energy from 12,500 to approximately 4,000 by tick 377, with a brief local maximum of 11,608 observed around tick 65 before the precipitous fall continues.

Between ticks 696 and 1,391, the rate of decline moderates substantially (slopes decreasing from -2.8 to -0.155), yet remains consistently negative. Total energy falls from roughly 4,000 to below 200 during this interval. Superimposed upon this downward trend are regular oscillations with a dominant period of 63 ticks, reflecting the interaction between seasonal environmental fluctuations (seasonality intensity 0.8) and hunting pressure.

A notable demographic recovery occurs between ticks 1,392 and 1,623, where the Mann-Kendall trend reverses significantly (tau = 0.548, slope = +0.091, p < 0.001), indicating a temporary increase in aggregate male energy. This suggests density-dependent compensation where reduced population levels temporarily alleviate pressure on remaining individuals or resources. However, this recovery proves insufficient to prevent ultimate collapse. Subsequent fluctuations between ticks 1,624 and 2,087 show mixed trends (decreasing then increasing) but at negligible magnitudes near zero.

The simulation ensemble exhibits high initial variability, with a standard deviation of 3,478 units and individual trajectories diverging considerably in the early stages (evident in the spread from 12,500 down to 5,000), yet all replicates converge toward the shared outcome of male population elimination. The median energy value of 134.5 indicates that half of the temporal observations and many simulation runs maintained residual male populations longer than the mean trajectory suggested, before the final decline to extinction between ticks 2,156 and 2,284.

This pattern reflects the sustained hunting pressure from immortal forager agents targeting males due to their higher energetic return (food value 30 versus 27 for females), combined with the population's inability to maintain replacement rates against the continuous predation mortality. The oscillatory dynamics embedded within the decline correspond to seasonal resource pulses, while the change points at ticks 125, 350, 500, 625, and 875 mark transitions where hunting efficiency or population density thresholds altered the rate of energy extraction from the male cohort.
