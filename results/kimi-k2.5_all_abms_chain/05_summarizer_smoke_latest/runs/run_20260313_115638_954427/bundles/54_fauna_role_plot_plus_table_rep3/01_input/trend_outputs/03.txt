:The cumulative predation mortality of male megaherbivores follows a saturating trajectory indicative of progressive population depletion under sustained hunting pressure. Beginning from zero at initialization, mortality accumulates rapidly during the initial approximately 360–500 ticks (representing the first 1–1.4 years given the daily timestep calibration), with steepness varying considerably across stochastic replicates. 

Between approximately ticks 500–1000, the rate of accumulation decelerates markedly as the cumulative count approaches the carrying capacity of the initial male population. Substantial divergence emerges across simulation runs during this interval: while some trajectories plateau at intermediate values ranging from roughly 10 to 80 cumulative deaths, others continue ascending toward higher totals. By approximately tick 1200 (3.3 years), most simulation runs have entered a stabilization phase where additional male mortality becomes increasingly sporadic or ceases entirely.

The maximum observed cumulative mortality reaches approximately 120 individuals near tick 1876 (5.1 years), approaching the expected initial male population size derived from the starting parameters of 250 ungulates. Statistical trend analyses reveal distinct temporal regimes in hunting pressure: significantly increasing trends dominate the early period (ticks 0–1159), transition to non-significant trends during middle phases (ticks 1160–1391 and 1624–1855), and exhibit decreasing tendencies in final periods (ticks 1856–2087) as prey availability becomes exhausted.

Across the full temporal extent of 2312 ticks, the mean cumulative mortality stabilizes near 100 individuals, with a median of approximately 116.5 and terminal values averaging 117.5. This distribution indicates that in typical outcomes, hunters successfully exploited the majority of available males, with individual runs ranging from minimal mortality to maxima of roughly 120, suggesting near-complete extirpation of the male cohort in high-exploitation scenarios. Oscillatory patterns with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks emerge during late-stage simulations, potentially reflecting seasonal resource fluctuations affecting hunter foraging efficiency given the high seasonality parameter of 0.8.
