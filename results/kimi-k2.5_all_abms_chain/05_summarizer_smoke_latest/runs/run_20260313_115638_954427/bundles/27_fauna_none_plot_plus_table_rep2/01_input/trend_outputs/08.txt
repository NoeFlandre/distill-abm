The populations of male animals initiated at 125 individuals across all 40 simulation replicates and followed trajectories of progressive depletion toward elimination. The aggregate mean descended from 125 to 0, reaching complete extinction by step 2284, with a series mean of 21.0324 and median of 1.3462 indicating strongly right-skewed distributions where populations spent most timesteps at very low abundances relative to their starting point. The high variance of 36.0441 reflects substantial between-simulation heterogeneity in extinction timing.

The collapse occurred in distinct phases demarcated by change points at steps 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 875, with the most precipitous declines occurring during the initial 500 steps when numbers fell from 125 to below 10 individuals. Inflection points at steps 390, 755, and 817 mark transitions in the velocity of decline, after which rates of decrease attenuated as populations entered a prolonged terminal phase characterized by persistence at densities below 10 individuals until final elimination near step 2310.

Throughout the descent, populations exhibited oscillations with a dominant period of 63 steps, potentially reflecting seasonal cycles within the model's framework of 365 ticks per year and seasonality set to 0.8. These cyclic fluctuations produced temporary interruptions to the decline, generating localized peaks at step 730 (10.5385 individuals), step 709 (8.8462 individuals), step 803 (6.7632 individuals), and step 820 (6.2432 individuals), followed by progressively diminished fluctuations at steps 893 (4.2500), 982 (2.6286), and 997 (2.4412) as populations approached extinction.

Statistical trend analysis confirms significant decreasing patterns throughout the first approximately 1400 steps, with highly significant negative slopes detected in sequential windows covering steps 0 through 1391. Between steps 1392 and 1623, a temporary increasing trend emerged before the series stabilized with no significant directional trend from step 1624 through 2087 as populations fluctuated near zero. Rolling analysis indicates that after the initial rapid decline, the final approach to extinction involved prolonged periods of low-amplitude fluctuation at very low population sizes.

Under the modeled conditions where males possess higher food value (30) but higher processing costs (6) compared to females, combined with human hunter populations operating under optimal foraging constraints, the differential vulnerability of males to hunting pressure likely drove the consistent extinction trajectories observed across all replicates. The variability in extinction timing, ranging from approximately 400 steps to over 2200 steps across individual simulation runs, demonstrates the stochastic nature of demographic processes and hunter-prey interactions even under standardized parameterizations including initial prey of 250 animals, birth spacing of 730 ticks, and maximum lifespan of 10220 ticks.
