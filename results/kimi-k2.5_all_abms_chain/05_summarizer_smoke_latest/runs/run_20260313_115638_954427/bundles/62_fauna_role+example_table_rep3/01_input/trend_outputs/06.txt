The number of births among the prey population commences at zero and remains negligible through approximately the first 600 ticks, corresponding to the lag period before the initial cohort of 250 ungulates reaches reproductive maturity. Around tick 725, the system undergoes a critical transition marked by the first significant change point, after which birth rates enter a sustained growth phase. Over the subsequent 900 ticks, reproductive output increases with statistical significance, rising from near-zero levels to reach localized maxima at ticks 1073 (2.875 births), 1214 (3.45 births), and 1392 (4.3 births), superimposed upon an underlying upward trend.

This expansion accelerates between ticks 1392 and 1623, with the strongest increasing trend detected in the Mann-Kendall analysis (tau=0.784), culminating in an absolute maximum of 5.6 births at tick 1702. This peak represents the demographic apex where the maximum number of females simultaneously occupy reproductive states given the 730-tick birth spacing parameter.

Immediately following this maximum, the trajectory reverses sharply. A change point at tick 1850 initiates a significant declining trend that persists through the remainder of the simulation, with a secondary change point at tick 2050 marking further acceleration of the decline. The birth rate falls from the peak of 5.6 to a terminal value of 1.0 by tick 2312, representing an 82% reduction in reproductive output. This collapse likely reflects the depletion of the breeding female population below replacement thresholds due to the sustained hunting pressure exerted by the 20 immortal foragers operating with food storage capabilities and a six-patch foraging radius.

Throughout both phases, the data exhibit regular oscillations with a dominant period of 50 ticks, visible in the sequence of inflection points and local extrema, suggesting that seasonal environmental fluctuations (operating at seasonality=0.8) modulate reproductive success even as the overall population trajectory trends toward expansion and subsequent collapse.
