Model focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making. Double-loop learning is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False. Initial risk attitudes distributed across a range from approximately -0.11 to +0.17 depending on the specific run. The first 26 time steps reach a minimum value of approximately 84,114 units. This represents a degradation phase where vegetation condition deteriorates by roughly 6.5% from initial levels. From step 33 onward, a sustained recovery and growth phase dominates. This rapid destocking continues steeply through step 20, with aggregate holdings falling to roughly 45–50 animals per household. The subsequent slowing suggests diminishing returns to passive diffusion, potentially requiring targeted engagement to reach remaining holdout households. Stabilization of attrition rates after year 65 suggests surviving non-learning households either occupy ecological niches that buffer them from environmental variability. Promoting adaptive learning strategies may improve overall household persistence, but blanket recommendations may be inappropriate.
