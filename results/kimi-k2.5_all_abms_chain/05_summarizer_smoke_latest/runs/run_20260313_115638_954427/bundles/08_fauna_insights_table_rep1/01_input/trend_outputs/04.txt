The cumulative mortality of female megaherbivores exhibits a sigmoid accumulation pattern characterized by rapid initial growth, progressive deceleration, an extended stagnation period, a brief terminal resurgence, and ultimate stabilization. The temporal dynamics reveal distinct phases of hunting pressure modulation across the simulation horizon of 2,312 ticks.

**Phase 1: Rapid Accumulation (Ticks 0–125)**
The initial period demonstrates steep linear growth with Sen's slope coefficients exceeding 0.21 females killed per tick. By tick 125, the cumulative mortality reaches approximately 26–27 individuals. This phase reflects high initial hunting efficiency, likely corresponding to naive prey populations or high initial hunter-prey encounter rates before behavioral adaptation or population depletion occurs.

**Phase 2: Decelerating Growth (Ticks 125–850)**
Following the first change point at tick 125, the accumulation rate declines progressively through multiple regime shifts at ticks 375, 475, and 625. Sen's slope decreases from approximately 0.059 (ticks 464–695) to 0.023 (ticks 696–927), then to 0.006 (ticks 928–1159). By tick 850, cumulative mortality reaches approximately 104 individuals. This deceleration aligns with optimal foraging predictions where hunters may switch to higher-value male prey (food value 30 versus 27, despite higher processing costs of 6 versus 3) as female encounter rates decline due to population reduction.

**Phase 3: Stagnation (Ticks 850–1600)**
Between ticks 850 and 1600, the cumulative count exhibits minimal growth with Sen's slope approaching 0.000. The accumulation increases by fewer than 3 individuals over approximately 750 ticks, reaching roughly 119 by tick 1336 and stabilizing near 122–123 by tick 1600. This plateau suggests effective cessation of female hunting, potentially indicating functional extinction of female herds, extreme prey scarcity forcing complete prey switching, or habitat saturation preventing successful encounters.

**Phase 4: Terminal Resurgence and Stabilization (Ticks 1600–2312)**
At tick 1600, a brief resurgence occurs with Sen's slope increasing to 0.007, driving the cumulative mortality to its maximum of approximately 123.7 individuals at tick 1826. This uptick may reflect seasonal migration patterns (consistent with the detected oscillation period of 63 ticks, approximately bi-monthly cycles) introducing new females into the hunting radius, or temporary breakdown of avoidance behaviors. Following this peak, the metric declines slightly to 123.0 by tick 2312, with Sen's slope turning negative (-0.453) in the final window (ticks 1856–2087), suggesting either data censoring effects or terminal population exhaustion preventing further accumulation.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The non-linear accumulation pattern demonstrates that hunting pressure on female megaherbivores is self-limiting through prey depletion rather than constant, creating an extinction vortex where initial high harvest rates (0.21 females/tick) rapidly decelerate as populations crash. The extended stagnation phase (ticks 850–1600) indicates that once female mortality accumulates to approximately 120 individuals, the population becomes functionally unavailable to hunters, suggesting that intervention thresholds must occur well before cumulative mortality reaches 100 individuals to prevent irreversible demographic collapse. The brief terminal resurgence highlights that even severely depleted populations may exhibit temporary availability during seasonal peaks, which could mislead managers into assuming recovery has occurred when the population remains critically endangered.
