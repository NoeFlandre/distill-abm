The simulations initialize with an average livestock holding of 90 animals per household. Over the first 37 time steps, the system undergoes a severe decline, with livestock numbers plummeting to a minimum of approximately 41 animals. This collapse is particularly pronounced during the initial 20 time steps, characterized by steep decreases, after which the rate of decline moderates but continues until reaching the nadir around time step 37.

Between time steps 37 and 55, the system stabilizes near this minimum level, with average livestock counts maintaining a relatively steady state around 41 to 42 animals. This plateau suggests the system has reached a constrained equilibrium where vegetation availability limits further herd reduction while also preventing growth.

From time step 55 onward, a gradual recovery phase initiates, with livestock numbers slowly increasing to approximately 47.5 animals by the final time step. This upward trend persists throughout the remaining 45 steps but proceeds at a decelerating rate, with the slope of increase diminishing from 0.313 between steps 44-54 to merely 0.002 in the final window.

The ensemble mean across 30 simulation runs masks considerable heterogeneity in individual trajectory outcomes. While all runs follow the initial collapse pattern, they diverge substantially during the recovery phase, with final livestock levels at time step 100 ranging from approximately 41 to over 60 animals per household. This divergence likely reflects the differential effectiveness of the three behavioral strategies—baseline E-RO, learning-by-doing E-LBD, and social learning E-RO-SL1—in adapting to the degraded pasture conditions following the initial overstocking crisis.

The long-term average of 48.8 animals and median of 47.3 indicate that the system spends the majority of the simulation period operating well below initial stocking levels, suggesting that the initial high livestock density (90 animals) substantially exceeds the carrying capacity of the private pasture system, necessitating significant destocking and leading to a new equilibrium around 47 animals where learning-enabled adaptation allows for modest recovery from the crash nadir.
