Over the 100-timestep simulation period, the average deviation from recommended livestock levels among extension agents exhibits a distinct three-phase trajectory: an initial rapid escalation, a subsequent prolonged decline, and final stabilization at a moderate level.

The series begins at near-zero deviation (0.0074 at timestep 0), indicating initial compliance with recommended stocking rates. Following a brief minimum of 0.0074 observed at timestep 3, the metric enters a significant expansionary phase. Between timesteps 0 and 10, deviation increases significantly (slope = 0.018), accelerating through timestep 21. This growth continues through timestep 32, though at a decelerated rate (slope = 0.001), culminating in a maximum deviation of 0.3814 at timestep 33. This peak represents a nearly 50-fold increase from initial conditions, suggesting a period of substantial experimentation or stress-induced departure from recommended grazing pressures. Notable local maxima cluster between timesteps 21 and 37, with sustained high deviation (>0.35) indicating persistent overstocking or understocking relative to ecological carrying capacity before corrective feedbacks activate.

A sharp reversal initiates at timestep 33, with deviation declining significantly through timestep 65 (slopes ranging from -0.010 to -0.004). By timestep 46, values fall to approximately 0.27, and by timestep 64 reach 0.1913. This contraction likely reflects adaptive responses to degraded pasture conditions or accumulated learning outcomes, as households adjust herd sizes back toward recommended levels.

From timestep 66 onward, the series stabilizes, exhibiting no significant trend through the simulation end. Values fluctuate around 0.19, concluding at 0.1911 at timestep 100. This persistent deviation of approximately 19% from recommendations—while substantially lower than the peak—suggests that heterogeneous households settle into a steady-state where optimal risk management or livelihood strategies inherently diverge from theoretical recommendations, possibly due to the r-parameter heterogeneity and learning processes (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1) embedded in the model. The dominant oscillation period of 49 timesteps indicates long-term cyclical adjustments in household stocking strategies relative to ecological feedbacks, consistent with adaptive co-management dynamics in private property grazing systems.
