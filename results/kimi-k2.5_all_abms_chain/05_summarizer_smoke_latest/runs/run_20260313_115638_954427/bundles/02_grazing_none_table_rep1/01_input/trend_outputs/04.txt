The average actual available green biomass initiates at approximately 45,000 units and exhibits relative stability throughout the initial nine timesteps. Between timesteps 11 and 21, the biomass undergoes a statistically significant decreasing trend, continuing downward until reaching a minimum value of approximately 42,057 units at timestep 27. This initial depletion phase reflects the intensive grazing pressure exerted by stationary households managing substantial initial livestock endowments across private pasture patches before adaptive mechanisms take effect.

Following the nadir at timestep 27, the system enters a brief transitional period through approximately timestep 33, during which biomass levels stabilize without clear directional movement. A sustained recovery commences around timestep 33, transitioning into a significant increasing trend. The recovery accelerates markedly between timesteps 44 and 54, exhibiting the steepest growth rates during this interval. After timestep 49—a key inflection point indicating a shift in the trajectory—the rate of increase gradually decelerates, though remaining positive, through the remainder of the simulation.

By timestep 100, the biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units, representing a substantial increase of over 15,000 units from initial conditions and exceeding the minimum value by more than 18,000 units. This trajectory—from initial decline through adaptive recovery to enhanced pasture conditions—demonstrates how the combination of learning-by-doing, social learning, and baseline decision-making processes across the heterogeneous population enables grazing systems to overcome initial overstocking pressures. The progressive deceleration of growth during the latter half of the simulation suggests the system is approaching a higher equilibrium state facilitated by improved adaptive management strategies, despite stationary household constraints and climatic variability.
