The cumulative mortality trajectory for male megaherbivores exhibits a sigmoidal accumulation pattern characterized by rapid initial growth, successive saturation plateaus, and a terminal decline phase, superimposed with regular seasonal oscillations.  

During the initial phase (approximately ticks 0–850), the accumulation of male kills proceeds with steep, though decelerating, growth rates. The most rapid increases occur within the first 250 ticks, with structural change points detected at regular 125-tick intervals (125, 250, 375, 500, 625), suggesting stepwise adjustments in hunting efficiency as foragers establish search patterns across the 211×211 landscape. By tick 850, the accumulation rate has slowed significantly, indicating that initial encounter rates with the naive male population have begun to decline.  

The middle phase (approximately ticks 1160–1855) is defined by protracted plateaus interrupted by brief resurgences. Between ticks 1160–1391, the cumulative count stabilizes near 118 individuals, reflecting a functional saturation where hunter effort encounters diminishing returns as male prey become scarce. A temporary increase in accumulation occurs between ticks 1392–1623, potentially driven by seasonal vegetation pulses (consistent with the 0.8 seasonality parameter) that concentrate prey movement, before a second plateau emerges between ticks 1624–1855. Inflection points at ticks 1241, 1330, 1397, and 1455 mark transitions between these quasi-stable states.  

The late phase (approximately ticks 1856–2312) diverges from typical cumulative growth patterns, reaching a maximum value of approximately 120.2 males killed at tick 1876 before entering a statistically significant declining trend through tick 2087, ultimately settling at approximately 117.5 by the simulation terminus. This reduction suggests that as male populations approach local extirpation, optimal foraging dynamics drive hunters to switch to alternative prey classes (females and calves, which offer lower processing costs of 3 versus 6 energy units), effectively capping male-specific mortality. The decline in the cross-simulation mean further indicates that runs experiencing intensive early male harvesting concluded with depleted male subpopulations, while surviving simulations contributed lower cumulative counts to the aggregate average.  

Superimposed upon these trends is a regular oscillatory component with a dominant period of 63 ticks (approximately bi-monthly cycles), manifesting most prominently after tick 2170. These oscillations align with the strong seasonality parameter (0.8) and regrowth-time of 3 ticks, creating predictable pulses in grass availability that drive prey distribution and, consequently, hunter encounter rates with male animals.  

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The trajectory demonstrates that male-biased harvesting, while initially productive, depletes the sexually dimorphic subpopulation to functional extinction within approximately 5–6 simulated years (ticks 1800+), after which hunting effort reallocates to females and calves. This rapid transition suggests that monitoring programs must detect male population declines before tick 1000 (approximately year 3) to intervene effectively. The 63-tick oscillatory component indicates that temporary hunting moratoria during seasonal resource peaks could moderate encounter rates and extend the sustainability of male harvests. Furthermore, the existence of processing cost differentials between sexes (3 for females versus 6 for males) accelerates the switch to alternative prey once males become rare, implying that management strategies must account for how hunter economic decisions amplify demographic stochasticity in sexually dimorphic populations.
