The simulation results reveal the complete energetics collapse of the male ungulate subpopulation over approximately 6.3 years (2,284 daily ticks), with aggregate energy reserves declining from an initial value of 12,500 to eventual extinction at zero. The trajectory follows a multi-phase pattern characterized by rapid initial depletion, subsequent oscillatory decline, and terminal collapse.

**Initial Collapse Phase (Ticks 0–125)**
The population experiences immediate and severe energy loss, with total male energy plummeting from 12,500 to approximately 4,000 within the first 125 ticks (roughly four months). An early localized fluctuation occurs around tick 65, where energy momentarily reaches 11,608 before resuming decline. This phase exhibits the steepest downward trajectory observed, with statistical trends showing near-maximum negative correlation (tau approaching -1.0) and slopes exceeding -14 energy units per tick.

**Intermediate Volatility Phase (Ticks 125–875)**
Following the initial collapse, the system enters a prolonged period of declining volatility lasting approximately 2.4 years. During this interval, the energy reservoir stabilizes in the 3,800–4,000 range before entering stepwise deterioration marked by change points at ticks 350, 500, 625, and 875. Notable secondary peaks appear between ticks 377 and 408, with values oscillating between approximately 3,847 and 4,003, superimposed on a dominant downward trend. Throughout this phase, a regular oscillatory pattern with a periodicity of 63 ticks (approximately bimonthly cycles) modulates the decline, likely reflecting the model's strong seasonality parameter (0.8) interacting with grass regrowth dynamics.

**Late-Stage Dynamics and Extinction (Ticks 875–2284)**
Between years 2.4 and 6.3, the system exhibits weakening directional trends punctuated by a significant anomalous reversal between ticks 1,392 and 1,623 (approximately years 3.8–4.4). During this window, male energy demonstrates a statistically significant increasing trend with a positive slope of 0.091 energy units per tick, temporarily interrupting the decades-long decline. However, this recovery proves unsustainable; subsequent intervals (ticks 1,624–2,087) show weakening fluctuation around near-zero slopes before the final terminal decline commences after tick 2,310. The population reaches complete energetic extinction at tick 2,284, with all male energy reserves depleted.

**Statistical Characteristics**
The distribution of energy values exhibits extreme right-skewness, with a mean of 1,984 contrasting sharply with a median of 134.5, indicating that the majority of the simulation duration operates at severely depleted energy levels despite the high initial values. The coefficient of variation exceeds 175%, reflecting high volatility consistent with boom-bust population dynamics under strong seasonal forcing and persistent predation pressure.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The approximately six-year timeline to complete male energetic collapse suggests that even moderate initial hunting pressure (20 foragers against 250 ungulates) with immortal predator dynamics generates rapid extinction trajectories. The temporary recovery observed during years 3.8–4.4 identifies a critical intervention window where conservation measures might capitalize on naturally occurring resilience peaks. Furthermore, the 63-day oscillation cycle indicates predictable high-vulnerability periods potentially addressable through seasonal hunting moratoria or targeted male protection during low-energy intervals. The persistent decline despite high grass availability (regrowth time of 3 ticks) suggests that predator energy storage capabilities and sexual dimorphism in prey value create irreversible extraction pressure that habitat management alone cannot mitigate.
