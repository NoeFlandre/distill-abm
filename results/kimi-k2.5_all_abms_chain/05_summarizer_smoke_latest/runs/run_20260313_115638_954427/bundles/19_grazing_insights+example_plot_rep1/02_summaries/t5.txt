Model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making. The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. Metric maintains fixed values, showing no change from step 0 to step 100. The initial risk attitude functions as a stored historical parameter. It remains unchanged even as households engage in learning-by-doing or social learning processes. The variance across simulations remains constrained during the initial decline but expands considerably during the recovery and stabilization phases. This pattern suggests that short-term pasture degradation is highly deterministic given initial stocking densities. Long-term resilience depends critically on the specific trajectories of adaptive co-management and climatic stochasticity encountered during recovery period. Policy interventions should prioritize supporting effective learning mechanisms and uncertainty management strategies. Initial surge reflects pressure on households beginning with initial livestock endowments of 90 animals to rapidly reduce herd sizes. The destocking rate continues to decrease gradually, eventually stabilizing at low equilibrium values. Despite initial overstocking and high adjustment costs, the combination of private property grazing and adaptive learning mechanisms enables households to progressively optimize herd size. Some trajectories reach approximately 30 households by step 20. Some continue to exhibit increases until approximately step 60. Social learning diffuses to between 70% and 90% of the 60 total households. The existence of high-level stable equilibria (24–26 households) demonstrates that traditional non-learning strategies can remain resilient or even expand under certain ecological feedbacks or social learning barriers. Decision makers should anticipate heterogeneous adoption patterns.
