This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. These operate through a yearly sequence where green biomass updates, households with E-RO-SL1 types update their r-parameters, households update behavioral strategies (if switching is enabled), make livestock placement decisions, livestock feeds on biomass, households observe neighbor performance, reserve biomass updates, and households observe and remember reserve biomass states.

The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. It seeks to evaluate how learning capabilities affect household resilience and pasture conditions in private property grazing systems. Specific objectives include analyzing the impact of social learning double-loop processes through strategy switching in heterogeneous populations, observing the diffusion and competition between different learning behaviors in mixed populations, and assessing how demographic settings and initial herd sizes influence system outcomes. The model allows for the evaluation of outcomes across social, ecological, and economic dimensions including average herd size, pasture biomass condition, and surviving number of households.

The mean household initial risk attitude remains constant throughout the 100-step simulation period for all observations, exhibiting values distributed approximately between -0.11 and 0.18 without temporal variation. From initialization through the final time step, the metric maintains fixed values, showing no change from step 0 to step 100.

This stability characterizes the initial r-parameter values for E-RO-SL1 households, which serve as persistent baseline references representing each household's original disposition to deviate from recommended livestock numbers. Since the initial risk attitude functions as a stored historical parameter rather than a dynamically updated variable, it remains unchanged even as households engage in learning-by-doing or social learning processes that may modify their current risk attitudes during the yearly decision-making sequence.

For decision makers, the static nature of these initial values indicates that heterogeneity in risk disposition is permanently established at simulation inception and persists as a fixed characteristic throughout the modeling period. This underscores the critical importance of initial risk attitude calibration in determining long-term household behavior, as these baseline values represent permanent reference points that cannot be altered through subsequent learning mechanisms. Consequently, interventions targeting grazing system resilience must account for the initial distribution of risk attitudes among social learning agents, recognizing that baseline conditions will perpetually anchor decision-making trajectories regardless of adaptive processes or experiential learning.

The mean household risk attitude, representing the r-parameter that captures uncertainty management and risk disposition, exhibits complex temporal dynamics across the 100-step simulation horizon. Initially, at step 0, the metric starts near zero with minor variance across households, approximately ranging between -0.1 and 0.1. During the first phase spanning steps 0 to 20, the data indicate a rapid and substantial increase across nearly all trajectories, with values climbing sharply to between 0.2 and 0.7. This suggests an initial period of rapid confidence-building and uncertainty reduction as households begin processing information and adapting to the grazing system.

In the second phase from steps 20 to 40, the growth rate decelerates considerably, and the data series begin to diverge into distinct clusters. While some trajectories continue ascending to reach approximately 0.75 to 0.8 by step 40, others plateau at intermediate levels around 0.5 to 0.6, and a subset begins declining toward values near 0.2. This divergence reflects the differentiation of household experiences and the emergence of path-dependent risk attitudes driven by heterogeneous initial conditions, local ecological variability, and the specific learning mechanisms—social learning versus learning-by-doing—employed by different behavioral types.

The final phase from steps 40 to 100 is characterized by stabilization and lock-in effects. A significant cluster stabilizes at high risk attitude values between 0.75 and 0.8, indicating entrenched risk tolerance and low uncertainty. A middle cluster stabilizes between 0.6 and 0.7, while other groups settle at lower positive values ranging from 0.15 to 0.45. Notably, several trajectories that began declining after step 40 continue falling, reaching negative values between -0.3 and -0.4 by step 60 and remaining stable at these low levels through step 100. These negative values signify the development of high uncertainty, extreme risk aversion, or poor information quality among those households.

For decision makers and policy designers, these trends indicate that the initial 40 steps represent a critical window for intervention, during which risk attitudes are malleable and rapidly evolving. Once stabilized after step 40, risk attitudes become highly persistent, with households locking into either high confidence, moderate uncertainty, or entrenched risk-averse states. The existence of households stabilizing at significantly negative risk attitudes suggests that uniform policies will be ineffective; targeted support mechanisms are required for those trapped in high-uncertainty states. Furthermore, the early divergence implies that initial conditions and early-stage information access are crucial determinants of long-term household resilience in private property grazing systems.
