The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock begins at approximately zero across all simulation runs, indicating initial equality in the distribution of livestock health among the 60 stationary households. During the first 40 steps, inequality accumulates gradually, with coefficient values rising to between 0.01 and 0.03 as households apply their distinct behavioral strategies—E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1—to manage private pastures under stochastic rainfall conditions.

Between steps 40 and 60, the trajectories begin to bifurcate. While some simulation runs maintain moderate inequality levels between 0.025 and 0.035, others accelerate rapidly toward values exceeding 0.045. This divergence intensifies substantially during steps 60 through 100. In high-inequality pathways, the Gini coefficient climbs steeply to reach approximately 0.095 by the end of the simulation, while low-inequality pathways plateau between 0.03 and 0.065. This wide range of terminal values—from modest to highly elevated inequality—suggests strong path dependency in the accumulation of livestock health disparities.

The accelerating inequality over time reflects the compounding effects of heterogeneous risk management and learning capabilities within the private property structure. Differences in the r-parameter and learning effectiveness appear to create positive feedback loops, where households with advantageous risk-coping strategies accumulate healthy livestock while others experience relative decline. The timing of the divergence around step 40-60 coincides with the period when learning-by-doing and social learning processes would begin to manifest significant differences in pasture management outcomes across the heterogeneous population.

Implications for decision makers: The persistent trend toward inequality suggests that private grazing systems naturally stratify based on differential learning effectiveness and initial risk attitudes. Interventions may be most effective if targeted during the critical 40-60 step window before trajectories fully diverge, potentially through measures that enhance information sharing or provide early support to households showing signs of declining livestock health, thereby preventing the emergence of extreme and persistent disparities.
