Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change. Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances. Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores. The population of human hunter-gatherer bands remained absolutely invariant throughout the observation period, maintaining a constant value of 20 active agents across 2,312 recorded time steps. The aggregate energy available to forager bands demonstrates a pronounced monotonic decline throughout the temporal sequence, initiating at 2000 energy units and descending to approximately -38229 units by tick 2312. The trajectory exemplifies how anthropogenic pressure can drive coupled human-ecological systems into deep overshoot regimes despite technological buffers like food storage. The cumulative number of male prey animals harvested by forager bands exhibits a characteristic accumulation curve with distinct temporal phases and saturation dynamics. The saturation plateau observed at tick 1826 (approximately year five) suggests the system reaches either demographic equilibrium or a limiting threshold where the remaining female population becomes too sparse to sustain further cumulative kills. Conservation interventions must be implemented immediately upon human arrival, as the critical window for preventing irreversible female population depletion closes rapidly. The cumulative mortality of megaherbivore populations exhibits a characteristic trajectory marked by rapid initial accumulation followed by deceleration and eventual plateau. The 50-day oscillation cycles reveal predictable reproductive vulnerability windows that could inform seasonal hunting moratoria or pulsed resource supplementation strategies in analogous conservation contexts. The male megaherbivore population exhibits a deterministic extinction trajectory over approximately 6.3 years. The trajectory was characterized by two distinct phases: an initial rapid decline followed by a prolonged persistence at critically low densities before terminal expiration. Conservation protocols should implement immediate, sustained hunting moratoria once populations fall below approximately 20 individuals. The population entered a prolonged period of persistence at critically low densities, fluctuating between approximately 2 and 11 individuals from tick 700 onward. The population ultimately declined to extinction by tick 2312 (approximately 6.3 years), with the final decrease occurring between change points at tick 875 and tick 2310. An initial cohort of 250 ungulates encounters hunting pressure from 20 foragers operating under optimal foraging protocols with immortal status and energy storage capabilities. The population experiences immediate catastrophic decline, shedding approximately 84% of collective energetic capital to reach roughly 2,100 units by tick 375. The dynamics enter a prolonged oscillatory decline phase between ticks 375 and 1,159.
