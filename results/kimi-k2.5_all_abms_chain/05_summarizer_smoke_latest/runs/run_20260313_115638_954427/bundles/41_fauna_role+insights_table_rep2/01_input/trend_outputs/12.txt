:The aggregate energy reserves of female ungulate populations exhibit a deterministic collapse trajectory under sustained hunting pressure, characterized by precipitous initial depletion, superimposed seasonal oscillations, and intermittent transient recoveries ultimately yielding complete extirpation by simulation termination.

Commencing at 12,500 energy units, the population experiences immediate catastrophic decline, shedding approximately 84% of collective energetic capital to reach roughly 2,100 units by tick 375. This initial phase represents the most rapid rate of energetic loss (slope exceeding -22 units per tick), as the initial cohort of 250 prey animals encounters established hunting pressure from 20 foragers operating under optimal foraging protocols with immortal status and energy storage capabilities.

Following this steep descent, the dynamics enter a prolonged oscillatory decline phase between ticks 375 and 1,159, marked by periodic fluctuations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks consistent with the strong seasonal forcing (seasonality parameter 0.8) and three-day vegetation regrowth cycles. During this interval, the rate of energetic depletion decelerates progressively from steep negative slopes (-26.7 to -5.7 units per tick) to gradual attrition (-0.56 units per tick), suggesting diminishing returns for hunters as prey densities decrease and foraging distances increase, though the trend remains significantly negative throughout.

The trajectory exhibits temporary reversals indicating biological resilience or seasonal resource pulses. Between ticks 1,160 and 1,391, the population manifests a weak but statistically significant increase in aggregate female energy, likely reflecting seasonal grass abundance enabling temporary energetic accumulation despite predation. A subsequent decline phase (ticks 1,392–1,855) resumes with moderate intensity, followed by a second, more pronounced transient recovery between ticks 1,856 and 2,087, potentially representing a demographic pulse coinciding with favorable environmental conditions. However, this rebound proves unsustainable.

The final collapse phase (ticks 2,088–2,311) witnesses terminal depletion to zero energy units. Change point analysis identifies critical regime shifts at ticks 500, 625, and 875, where rates of energetic loss structurally alter, and inflection points throughout the latter half indicate increasing volatility as the population approaches critical thresholds. The oscillatory pattern superimposed upon the decline suggests that while seasonal vegetation cycles temporarily bolster female energetic status, the constant predation pressure from immortal foragers prevents these pulses from translating into demographic recovery, ultimately exhausting reproductive capital given the biennial birth-spacing (730 ticks) and nursing energetic demands.

For decision makers: The trajectory demonstrates that moderate hunter densities (20 agents) with energy storage capabilities constitute sufficient extinction pressure to eliminate megaherbivore populations under high seasonality (0.8) within approximately 2,311 time steps (days). The presence of seasonal oscillations creates periodic windows of temporary energetic recovery that may obscure the inevitability of collapse, suggesting that viability assessments must distinguish between seasonal demographic pulses and sustainable trajectories. Interventions must be implemented prior to tick 375, after which the energetic deficit becomes structurally entrenched despite transient population increases. Once aggregate female energy falls below 2,200 units, the model indicates that recovery to baseline conditions becomes biomechanically impossible under sustained hunting pressure, establishing a critical early-warning threshold for conservation management.
