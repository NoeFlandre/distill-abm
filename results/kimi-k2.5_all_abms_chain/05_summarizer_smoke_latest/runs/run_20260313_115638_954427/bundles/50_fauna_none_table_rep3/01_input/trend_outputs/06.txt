The simulation record of cumulative births among prey populations exhibits complex demographic dynamics characterized by an initial expansion phase, a mid-simulation peak, and subsequent decline over the 200-year simulation duration (73,000 ticks). Beginning at zero (index 0), reproductive activity commences and trends generally upward through approximately index 1702 (roughly year 147), reaching a maximum mean value of 5.6 across the 40 simulation runs. This growth period is non-linear, featuring significant oscillatory behavior with a dominant periodicity of approximately 50 sample indices (corresponding to multi-year cycles), and includes local maxima at indices 1073 (value 2.875), 1214 (value 3.45), and 1392 (value 4.3) before attaining the global peak. Inflection points at indices 613, 733, 903, 1121, 1227, 1328, and 1415 demarcate shifts in growth rates, with secular increasing trends statistically confirmed through index 1623 via rolling Mann-Kendall analysis.

Following the peak, the demographic trajectory reverses sharply. Change points at indices 1850 and 2050 mark transitions into persistent decline phases, with the metric falling from its maximum to a terminal value of 1.0 by simulation end (index 2312). This late-period decrease is characterized by reduced amplitude in oscillations and significant negative trends in rolling windows (tau = -0.495 and -0.453). Notable valleys interrupting the descent occur at indices 1422 (value 3.889), 1281 (value 3.059), and 1097 (value 2.871), while oscillation clusters concentrated around indices 625–690 and 785–810 suggest periodic demographic contractions during the expansion phase.

In the contextof the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these patterns suggest that prey populations initially establish and expand under parameter settings of moderate hunting pressure (20 foragers with differential processing costs by sex), seasonal grassland dynamics (seasonality=0.8, regrowth-time=3), and life history constraints (birth-spacing=730 ticks, senescence at 7300 ticks). The oscillatory component likely reflects the interaction between seasonal resource availability and the approximately biennial birth-spacing interval, creating multi-year demographic cycles. The mid-simulation peak around year 147 represents maximum reproductive output, possibly coinciding with population densities where breeding encounters are optimized before resource competition or hunting pressure intensifies. The subsequent decline indicates demographic collapse or suppression, consistent with either progressive environmental degradation, sustained predation pressure from immortal foragers utilizing energy storage, or density-dependent feedbacks as herds approach carrying capacity in a grass-proportion-constrained environment (0.33 coverage), ultimately resulting in minimal reproductive activity by year 200.
