This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. These operate through a yearly sequence where green biomass updates, households with E-RO-SL1 types update their r-parameters, households update behavioral strategies (if switching is enabled), make livestock placement decisions, livestock feeds on biomass, households observe neighbor performance, reserve biomass updates, and households observe and remember reserve biomass states.

The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. It seeks to evaluate how learning capabilities affect household resilience and pasture conditions in private property grazing systems. Specific objectives include analyzing the impact of social learning double-loop processes through strategy switching in heterogeneous populations, observing the diffusion and competition between different learning behaviors in mixed populations, and assessing how demographic settings and initial herd sizes influence system outcomes. The model allows for the evaluation of outcomes across social, ecological, and economic dimensions including average herd size, pasture biomass condition, and surviving number of households.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The existence of multiple equilibrium states implies that smallholder grazing systems face significant path dependency and potential poverty traps. Interventions should prioritize preventing the initial severe destocking shock or supporting households during the critical recovery window between steps 40 and 60. Policies targeting the support of social learning and learning-by-doing processes may help shift households toward the higher equilibrium outcomes (58–62 units) rather than allowing them to settle into the lower stability traps (41–45 units). Early warning systems and destocking incentives before step 20 could mitigate the initial collapse, preserving sufficient capital for the adaptive learning processes that drive recovery after step 40.

The mean number of livestock destocked per household initiates at zero and undergoes a rapid escalation during the first five time steps, attaining peak values ranging between approximately 6 and 7.5 animals. This initial surge reflects immediate resource constraints as the initial herd size of 90 animals exceeds sustainable pasture carrying capacity before adaptation mechanisms activate, forcing households to liquidate excess stock.

Subsequent to this peak, the destocking rate exhibits a pronounced downward trajectory from step 5 through approximately step 40, declining from values above 6 to predominantly below 2. This decrease occurs with substantial oscillations ranging between 1 and 4 animals, reflecting the interaction of high inter-annual rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100) with household learning processes. The oscillations indicate boom-bust cycles in biomass availability as agents adjust their strategies in response to stochastic climate forcing.

From step 40 through step 100, the system stabilizes into a low-level equilibrium with destocking rates converging to values predominantly below 1 animal per household. However, the simulation runs do not converge to a single uniform value; instead, they segregate into three distinct clusters by the final time steps. One cluster stabilizes near zero (0–0.2), indicating near-optimal resource management with minimal forced destocking. A second cluster maintains rates around 0.4 to 0.6 animals, while a third cluster persists at approximately 0.8 to 1.0 animal.

This stratification corresponds to the three behavioral types implemented in the heterogeneous population: the baseline E-RO type, the learning-by-doing E-LBD type, and the social learning E-RO-SL1 type. The near-zero destocking cluster represents households employing learning-by-doing mechanisms, which enable the most effective adaptation to local pasture conditions through individual experience accumulation. The intermediate cluster reflects social learning capabilities, which improve performance relative to baseline but remain constrained by information delays and neighbor effect heterogeneity. The highest destocking cluster represents baseline households lacking advanced learning mechanisms, continuing to experience resource management failures even after 100 time steps.

The high variance across simulation runs during the initial 40 steps gradually diminishes but never fully disappears, indicating that stochastic rainfall variability continues to influence household destocking decisions even after behavioral strategies have stabilized. This persistent variability suggests that no learning mechanism can fully eliminate the impacts of climatic uncertainty, though learning-by-doing approaches most effectively minimize destocking losses.

**Implications for decision makers:** The results demonstrate that individual learning capabilities (learning-by-doing) outperform social learning and baseline behaviors in reducing livestock losses, suggesting that extension services should prioritize hands-on, experience-based training programs over information-sharing networks alone. The persistent performance gaps after 100 steps indicate that behavioral differences create lasting structural inequalities in resource management outcomes, potentially justifying targeted interventions or safety nets specifically designed for households lacking adaptive capacities. The high initial destocking rates suggest that initial livestock endowments may exceed sustainable carrying capacities before adaptation occurs, indicating that restocking programs or initial herd size regulations should account for learning curves and adaptation periods to prevent early asset depletion. Finally, the continued oscillations in destocking rates throughout the simulation highlight that climatic variability remains a dominant driver of household vulnerability regardless of behavioral adaptation, underscoring the need for climate-resilient safety nets and insurance mechanisms that operate independently of behavioral learning processes.
