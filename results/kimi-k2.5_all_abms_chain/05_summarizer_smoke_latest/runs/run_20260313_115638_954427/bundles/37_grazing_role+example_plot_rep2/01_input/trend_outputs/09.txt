The mean cumulative destocking of livestock commences at zero and undergoes a rapid, concave increase during the initial 20 time steps, with most trajectories converging to values between 60 and 80. This initial surge represents an adjustment period where households reduce stocking densities from the initial level of 90 animals in response to observed pasture conditions and high inter-annual rainfall variability.

Following step 20, the trajectories diverge into distinct behavioral patterns. Approximately one-third of the simulation outcomes plateau between steps 30 and 40, stabilizing at cumulative destocking levels ranging from 58 to 85, which remain constant through step 100. These stabilizing patterns indicate that certain learning strategies or environmental realizations enable households to rapidly identify sustainable stocking rates and subsequently maintain stable herds without further animal liquidation.

The remaining trajectories exhibit continuous linear or near-linear increases in cumulative destocking from step 20 through step 100, with terminal values ranging from 100 to 175. The highest trajectory reaches approximately 175 by step 100, signifying sustained annual destocking activity. This pattern suggests that under some decision-making approaches or stochastic environmental sequences, households engage in ongoing portfolio adjustments, repeatedly destocking in response to fluctuating pasture conditions rather than reaching equilibrium.

The substantial divergence between outcomes—with a differential of over 100 animals between the lowest plateaued trajectories and the highest rising trajectories by step 100—indicates that learning strategy significantly affects long-term destocking dynamics. The clustering of trajectories into plateaued versus continuously rising groups suggests systematic differences in how the baseline rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning strategies process environmental feedback and uncertainty, with some approaches facilitating early system stabilization while others result in prolonged periods requiring continuous herd reductions.
