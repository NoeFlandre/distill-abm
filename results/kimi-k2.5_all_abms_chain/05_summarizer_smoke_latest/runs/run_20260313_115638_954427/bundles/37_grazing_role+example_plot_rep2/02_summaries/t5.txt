Model focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making. The average initial risk attitude of social learning agents exhibits no temporal variation throughout the 100-year simulation period. The distribution includes clusters near zero (approximately 0.00 to 0.01), positive dispositions centered around 0.055, 0.075, 0.115, and 0.14. Extreme values reaching approximately -0.11 at the lower bound and +0.18 at the upper bound. The minimum represents a period of maximum vegetation stress. Grazing pressure temporarily exceeds sustainable carrying capacity. The subsequent sigmoidal recovery indicates that adaptive learning strategies eventually reduce stocking rates sufficiently to allow vegetation regeneration. From time step 25 to approximately time step 45, the decline continues at a moderated pace. The system stabilizes into a sustained low-destocking regime that persists through time step 100. Some simulations maintain relatively low inequality levels near 0.02, others rise to approximately 0.05 by step 60. Ries reflects the discrete, annual decision-making cycle of the farmers.
