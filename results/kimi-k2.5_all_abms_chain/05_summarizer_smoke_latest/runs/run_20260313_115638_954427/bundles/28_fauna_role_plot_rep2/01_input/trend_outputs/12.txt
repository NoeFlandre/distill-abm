Across all simulation replicates, the total energy contained within the female ungulate population exhibits a consistent trajectory of collapse from initial values of approximately 12,000-12,500 energy units down to effective extinction (values near zero) over the course of roughly 500 to 2,000 time steps. 

The initial phase (steps 0-100) maintains high energy levels with minor fluctuations around the starting maximum. Between steps 100 and 500, the majority of simulation runs undergo a steep decline characterized by irregular, stepwise reductions interspersed with brief plateaus and occasional minor recoveries. During this critical window, total female energy drops by 60-90%, with most replicates falling below 4,000 units by step 500. The rate of decline varies substantially across stochastic replicates: some populations collapse to near-zero energy by step 300-400, while others maintain 2,000-4,000 units through step 500.

From steps 500-1000, the remaining populations continue to decline, though at reduced rates compared to the initial crash phase. Several replicates exhibit temporary stabilization or modest rebounds between steps 400-800, with energy levels fluctuating between 500-2,000 units before resuming downward trajectories. By step 1000, the majority of simulations have diminished to less than 500 energy units.

The final phase (steps 1000-2000+) shows the last surviving female subpopulations winking out sequentially. A few replicates maintain small residual energy pools (100-400 units) until steps 1,200-1,600, and one replicate persists with trace energy until approximately step 2,100.

The stepwise decline structure suggests discrete hunting mortality events rather than smooth exponential decay, consistent with individual-based predation. The temporary plateaus around steps 400-800 in several runs likely correspond to brief periods where reduced population density or seasonal vegetation peaks temporarily reduced hunting efficiency or improved foraging success for the remaining females, though these reprieves prove insufficient to prevent ultimate collapse under sustained hunting pressure.
