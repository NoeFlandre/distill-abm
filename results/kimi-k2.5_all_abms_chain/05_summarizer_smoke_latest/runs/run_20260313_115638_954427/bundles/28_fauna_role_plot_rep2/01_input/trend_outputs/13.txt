The simulations initiate with a total prey energy of approximately 25,000 units. Across all replicates, this metric undergoes a systematic decline toward effective extinction. During the initial phase spanning roughly steps 0 to 500, total energy decreases precipitously, with the most rapid declines observed within the first 300 steps. By step 500, the majority of trajectories have fallen below 5,000 units, representing a depletion of approximately 80% or more of the initial energy pool.

Considerable stochastic variation characterizes the rate of decline across simulation runs. While some trajectories exhibit rapid, nearly monotonic decreases reaching near-zero levels by step 500, others demonstrate more gradual descent patterns featuring temporary plateaus or oscillatory behavior between steps 300 and 800, where energy levels fluctuate in the 2,000 to 8,000 unit range before resuming downward trajectories.

By approximately step 1,000, nearly all replicates have converged to total energy levels below 1,000 units, with most approaching zero. A subset of simulations exhibits extended persistence, maintaining low-level energy reserves (typically under 2,000 units) until steps 1,200 to 1,500. One exceptional trajectory demonstrates prolonged survival with minimal energy levels persisting until approximately step 2,100.

The terminal state across all replicates is the complete or near-complete depletion of total prey energy, indicating inevitable population collapse under the specified parameter configuration of 20 immortal human foragers with food storage capabilities hunting an initially stable population of 250 sexually dimorphic ungulates. The temporal variation in collapse timing—ranging from approximately 500 to 2,100 steps—reflects the stochastic nature of agent interactions, movement patterns, and demographic stochasticity inherent in the model, though the qualitative outcome of extinction remains consistent across all runs.
