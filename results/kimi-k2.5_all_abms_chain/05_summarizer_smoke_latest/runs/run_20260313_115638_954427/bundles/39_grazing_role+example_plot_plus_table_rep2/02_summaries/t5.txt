Model focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making. The mean household initial risk attitude remains constant at approximately 0.0074 throughout the 100-year simulation period. The metric exhibits zero variance and no significant trends across any observation window, indicating that the population-level baseline risk attitude does not drift or fluctuate over time. Farmers adjust their livestock numbers downward based on observed pasture degradation and neighbor outcomes. After step 55, the rate of increase gradually decelerates, with the slope decreasing progressively through steps 66-76, 77-87, and 88-98. By the conclusion of the simulation at step 100, the biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units, exceeding the initial condition by roughly 15,500 units. The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock begins at effectively zero. The metric demonstrates a consistent upward trajectory, reaching approximately 0.073 by the final time step. A notable inflection occurs around step 51, marking a transition toward more rapid divergence in household fortunes. By year 25, the cumulative destocking reaches approximately 40–50 animals on average, representing the initial correction from the starting herd size of 90 animals toward sustainable stocking levels. By year 60, the accumulation rate stabilizes at a low but persistent level of approximately 0.3 animals per year, continuing the gradual upward trend established in the middle phase.
