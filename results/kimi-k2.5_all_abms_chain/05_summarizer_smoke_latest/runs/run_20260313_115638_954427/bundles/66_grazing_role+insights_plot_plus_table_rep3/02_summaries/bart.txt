The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods. The simulation reveals a characteristic temporal pattern in household risk attitudes, beginning with values concentrated near zero (approximately 0.007) and undergoing distinct phases of escalation, adjustment, and stabilization over the 100-step period. The early peak in risk attitude around step 33 represents a critical vulnerability window where overstocking pressures and deviation from recommended livestock levels are maximized. The initial decline reflects the adjustment costs as 60 households, each beginning with 90 livestock units, adapt to localized pasture conditions under uncertainty without prior experience. The minimum at step 26 represents a critical threshold where vegetation degradation peaks before experiential and social learning mechanisms effectively modify stocking behaviors. The subsequent sigmoid recovery suggests agents within the knowledge radius of 1 patch utilize feedback to optimize grazing pressure. Policymakers should anticipate that transitions to sustainable grazing management may involve significant time lags and initial resource degradation before improvement manifests. The eventual biomass levels exceeding initial conditions indicate that learning-based adaptation can create win-win outcomes where both vegetation health and potentially livestock productivity improve over the long term. The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock initializes at approximately zero, reflecting perfect equality across all 60 households at the start of the simulation. Over the 100-year period, this inequality metric follows a persistently upward trajectory, reaching a mean value of 0.0726 by the final timestep. The metric tracking engagement in social learning begins at zero and exhibits a pronounced S-curve trajectory over the 100-year simulation period. The critical window for promoting social learning occurs within the first 40 years, during which adoption rates are highest and interventions can most effectively accelerate the spread of information-sharing practices. The population of households employing the baseline non-learning decision strategy (E-RO) exhibits a three-phase trajectory over the 100-year simulation horizon. Suboptimal decision-making approaches resulting in cumulative livestock losses nearly triple those of effective strategies. Policy should prioritize facilitating facilitating information exchange and adaptive capacity during the early establishment period to prevent chronic overstocking cycles.
