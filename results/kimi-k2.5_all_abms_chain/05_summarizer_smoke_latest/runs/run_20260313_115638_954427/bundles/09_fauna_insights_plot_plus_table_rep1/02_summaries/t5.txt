The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The number of foragers remained constant at 20 agents throughout the entire simulation duration. The metric exhibited zero variance and zero standard deviation, with minimum, maximum, mean, and median values all fixed at 20. Forager population establishes a controlled baseline of steady hunting pressure. Extinction dynamics can be attributed to environmental factors, prey life history traits, or the fixed intensity of hunting pressure itself. Cumulative hunting mortality follows predictable saturation curves rather than linear escalation. The early rapid accumulation phase (first 500-1000 steps) represents the critical window where hunting pressure most significantly impacts male demographics. Trajectories saturate as early as step 600 with approximately 105 kills, while others continue gradual accumulation beyond step 1500. Spanning roughly the first 500 to 600 time steps, accumulation occurred rapidly, with counts increasing sharply from zero to between 100 and 110 individuals. The aggregate mean trajectory reached approximately 117 individuals by step 2312, having attained maximum values of 119 around step 1826. Subsequent periods exhibited no significant trend or slight decrease in the rate of accumulation. Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of death accumulation decelerates significantly, with statistical slopes dropping to 0.017 and eventually approaching zero. The average cumulative death count stabilizes at 233, with individual simulation runs varying between approximately 225 and 250 total deaths. Temporary increases during steps 1,400–1,600 represent demographic noise rather than recovery capacity. The model demonstrates no capacity for recovery once populations enter the low-density phase characterized by fewer than 10 males. The population persisted at low densities with high volatility, exhibiting oscillatory dynamics with a dominant period of 63 steps. During this phase, the population fluctuated around a median of approximately 1.5 animals, with transient peaks reaching 11.4 animals at step 730 and 9.3 animals.
