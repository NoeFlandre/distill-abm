The percentage of green patches in the simulated grassland environment initiates at approximately 33.0%, consistent with the initialized grass proportion. Within the first annual cycle (at tick 365), the metric undergoes a precipitous decline, reaching its absolute minimum value of 24.91%. This represents a reduction of roughly eight percentage points, or 24% relative to initial coverage. Following this nadir, rapid recovery occurs, with values returning to the 32–33% range by approximately tick 500.

Over the simulation duration of 2,312 ticks (approximately 6.3 years), the percentage of green patches maintains a mean value of 32.66% with a standard deviation of 0.95%. The terminal value of 32.998% closely approximates the initial condition, suggesting long-term stability of baseline grass coverage. However, the trajectory is characterized by significant periodic volatility. The system exhibits a dominant oscillatory pattern with a period of 63 ticks, indicating sub-annual cycles of vegetation availability superimposed upon annual rhythms.

Trend analysis reveals distinct temporal phases in grassland dynamics. During the initial period spanning ticks 0 through 1623 (years 0–4.4), consecutive rolling windows demonstrate significant increasing trends in vegetation coverage (Mann-Kendall tau values ranging from 0.211 to 0.931). Conversely, the later period (ticks 1624–2087, years 4.4–5.7) exhibits significant decreasing trends, with the final analyzed window showing a strong negative trajectory (tau = -0.446). Critical change points in the series occur at ticks 150, 300, 475, 725, and 2310, corresponding to trajectory shifts at irregular intervals averaging 150–225 ticks (roughly semi-annual to annual periodicity).

The severe reduction at tick 365 aligns with the completion of the first year, likely reflecting maximum seasonal grazing pressure from the initial prey population of 250 herds coinciding with constrained regrowth during metabolically limiting seasons (seasonality parameter = 0.8). The rapid recovery indicates that with a regrowth time of three ticks, the system possesses substantial resilience against transient depletion provided herbivore density does not exceed critical thresholds. However, the statistically significant shift to decreasing trends after tick 1624 signals emerging pressure that may slowly erode grass coverage over extended timescales, despite the apparent equilibrium maintained through year six.

Implications for decision makers:
The model demonstrates that grassland systems under megaherbivore grazing pressure exhibit predictable annual vulnerability windows wherein fodder availability contracts by nearly one-quarter. Conservation protocols should prioritize protecting residual vegetation during these cyclic minima, as the rapid regrowth capacity suggests the system can sustain itself if absolute thresholds are maintained. The identification of a 63-tick oscillation pattern provides actionable sub-annual intervention points to buffer against periodic shortages. Nonetheless, the trend reversal toward declining coverage after year four indicates that long-term equilibrium conditions may be metastable; management strategies should incorporate decadal monitoring to detect whether the observed decreasing trajectory accelerates into a regime shift or stabilizes at a new, lower carrying capacity.
