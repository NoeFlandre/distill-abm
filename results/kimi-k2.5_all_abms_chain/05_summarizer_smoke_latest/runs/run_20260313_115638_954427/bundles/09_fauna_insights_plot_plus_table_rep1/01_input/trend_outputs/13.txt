In the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model simulations, the aggregate energy stored in prey populations initiated at approximately 16,667 units, representing the combined energetic reserves of the initial cohort of 250 ungulates distributed across the 211×211 patch matrix. Across all 120 simulation replicates, this metric followed a deterministic trajectory toward extirpation, ultimately declining to zero by the conclusion of the runs, with a mean value across all time steps of roughly 2,369 units and a median of 146 units, indicating highly right-skewed distributions characteristic of rapid initial crashes followed by prolonged low-density phases.

The temporal dynamics exhibited distinct phases of decelerating decline punctuated by oscillatory fluctuations. During the initial 350 steps, populations experienced precipitous energy loss at rates approximating 25 units per step, driven by the establishment of hunting pressure from 20 human foragers operating under dynamic optimal foraging constraints. This phase featured prominent oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 steps, likely reflecting the interaction of seasonal grass regrowth cycles (seasonality parameter 0.8, regrowth time 3 steps) and herd demographic pulsations including reproductive intervals (birth-spacing 730 steps) and nursing calf energy values.

Between steps 350 and 500, the rate of energy depletion intensified to roughly 36 units per step, representing the critical transition where hunting efficiency peaked and herd recruitment failed to compensate for mortality. Processing cost differentials between males (6 units) and females (3 units) contributed to selective harvesting pressures during this window, though total aggregate energy continued its monotonic decline.

From step 500 through the subsequent 375 steps, the trajectory moderated considerably. Change points at steps 500, 625, and 875 marked transitions to progressively slower decay rates—from approximately 11 units per step down to 3 units per step, and finally to less than 1 unit per step—suggesting that as prey densities dropped below thresholds of 5,000 total energy units, encounter rates between hunters and herds diminished, temporarily buffering the remaining individuals. However, this alleviation proved insufficient for recovery; rolling trend analyses confirmed statistically significant monotonic decreases throughout all temporal windows, with only negligible and non-sustained increases detected after step 1,392.

The terminal phase, extending from roughly step 875 to 2,310, manifested as an extended low-energy regime where total animal energy fluctuated below 1,000 units, occasionally spiking to local maxima near 4,000 units due to stochastic recruitment events or temporary reductions in hunting pressure, but ultimately trending toward extinction. The high variance observed across replicates (standard deviation 4,360) indicates substantial stochasticity in extinction timing, with some populations persisting longer than others despite identical initial parameters, likely due to random walk effects in herd movements and hunter-prey encounter probabilities.

For decision makers examining megafaunal conservation or management strategies, these results demonstrate that under conditions of moderate hunting pressure (20 foragers), high environmental seasonality, and the specified life history parameters (maximum lifespan 10,220 steps, senescence at 7,300 steps), megaherbivore populations exhibit deterministic extinction trajectories regardless of stochastic variation. The prolonged persistence in the low-energy state suggests that late-stage interventions would face diminishing returns, as populations below critical energy thresholds of approximately 10,000 units lose demographic resilience. The consistent oscillation patterns indicate that reproductive pulsing and environmental seasonality create temporary windows of higher population vigor, but these do not translate into sustainable equilibria when hunting pressure remains constant. Management approaches must therefore prioritize maintaining total population energy above the 10,000-unit threshold observed at steps 300-400, as recovery becomes progressively improbable once populations enter the prolonged decline phase characteristic of steps 500 through 2,000.
