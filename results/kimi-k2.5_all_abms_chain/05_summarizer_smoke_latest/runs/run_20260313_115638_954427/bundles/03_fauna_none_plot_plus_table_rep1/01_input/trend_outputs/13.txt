The simulation series began with an aggregate prey energy of approximately 16,667 units and terminated at complete depletion (zero energy) at step 2,311. Across the 2,312 recorded steps, the median energy level was 146 units, substantially lower than the mean of 2,369 units, indicating that the system spent the majority of its duration in a depleted state.

The decline proceeded through distinct phases with markedly different rates. The initial phase (steps 0–350) exhibited a steep decline of approximately 25 units per step. The most rapid depletion occurred between steps 232 and 463, where energy loss accelerated to 36 units per step. By step 500, aggregate energy had fallen below 5,000 units, and by step 625, it had declined to approximately 1,000 units.

Between steps 625 and 875, the rate of decline decelerated to 3–10 units per step, with superimposed oscillations having a dominant period of approximately 63 steps. During this interval, transient stabilization occurred with local peaks at steps 385–440 (fluctuating between 3,700 and 4,100 units) and steps 709–730 (reaching 880–1,083 units).

From step 875 through step 1,391, the system persisted at low energy levels with minimal average decline (0.07 units per step). Brief periods of slight energy increase occurred between steps 1,392–1,623 (+0.034 units per step) and steps 1,856–2,087, interrupted by a decline phase between steps 1,624–1,855 (-0.111 units per step). A final brief increase appeared between steps 1,856–2,087.

The terminal phase began around step 2,087, with energy fluctuating near minimal values until the final change point at step 2,310, followed by complete extinction at step 2,311.

In the context of the model parameters, the trajectory reflects sustained hunting pressure from an initial population of 20 immortal foragers with food storage capability acting upon 250 initial prey. The initial rapid decline corresponds to efficient exploitation of accessible prey. The subsequent slowing reflects the combined effects of increasing search costs within the parameterized foraging radius (6 patches) as prey became scarce, and demographic constraints on the prey population. The brief late-stage increases suggest temporary relief from hunting pressure when prey densities fell below thresholds required for efficient foraging, allowing marginal demographic recovery (potentially from reproduction at the parameterized birth spacing of 730 ticks or reduced density-dependent mortality) before final collapse to extinction.
