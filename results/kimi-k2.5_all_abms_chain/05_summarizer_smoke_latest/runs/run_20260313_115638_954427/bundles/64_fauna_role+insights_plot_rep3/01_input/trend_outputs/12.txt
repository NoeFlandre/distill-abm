**Trend Analysis**

The data document a universal collapse trajectory across all simulation replicates, with aggregate female energy initialized at approximately 12,000 to 12,500 units and converging toward zero across all runs. The temporal progression follows a three-phase pattern of depletion.

**Phase 1: Rapid Decline (Steps 0–500)**
Initial total female energy begins at consistently high levels (12,000–12,500), reflecting the starting population of 250 individuals with baseline energy reserves. Within the first 400 to 500 steps, the system undergoes steep energy depletion, with most trajectories falling below 4,000 units by step 500—a reduction exceeding 65%. This phase exhibits high variability in descent rates; some populations lose energy precipitously, reaching below 2,000 units by step 300, while others maintain energy above 8,000 until steps 400–600. Several trajectories display temporary stabilization or minor oscillations between steps 300–600, with energy briefly plateauing or increasing by 500–1,000 units before resuming decline. These intermittent recoveries likely correspond to reproductive events (birth spacing of 730 ticks notwithstanding, stochastic reproduction can occur) or seasonal resource pulses coinciding with the high seasonality parameter (0.8), temporarily offsetting energy losses from hunting mortality.

**Phase 2: Terminal Depletion (Steps 500–1,000)**
Between steps 500 and 1,000, the remaining energy reserves continue declining toward extinction, though at decelerating rates compared to the initial phase. Populations that maintained higher energy through step 500 enter this phase with 2,000–4,000 units, while others have already collapsed to near-zero. By step 1,000, the vast majority of trajectories (approximately 90%) have reached energy levels below 500, with many at or approaching zero. The variance across runs narrows considerably during this interval as stochastic differences in early hunting success give way to deterministic population collapse under sustained predation pressure.

**Phase 3: Extinction Tail (Steps 1,000–2,200)**
A minority of simulation runs exhibit extended persistence, with total female energy hovering between 0–500 units until steps 1,500–2,200. These final populations represent remnant individuals that evaded hunters temporarily, supported by the fast vegetation regrowth (3 ticks) and grass growth enabled in the model. However, even these outliers eventually reach zero energy, indicating complete female population extinction across all parameter configurations tested. The longest surviving trajectory persists until approximately step 2,200, suggesting that under favorable stochastic conditions, small female cohorts might survive 1.5–2 years (550–850 days beyond the initial collapse phase) before ultimate elimination.

**Interpretation in Context**
The consistent trajectory toward zero energy reflects the demographic unsustainability of the megaherbivore population under the specified hunting pressure (20 foragers with high female food value of 27 and low processing cost of 3). With an interbirth interval of 730 ticks (2 years) and baseline mortality sources, the population cannot replace individuals lost to hunting fast enough to maintain biomass. The high seasonality (0.8) creates resource bottlenecks that amplify hunting impacts, as energy gains from foraging fluctuate while hunter pressure remains constant. The absence of any trajectory stabilizing above zero indicates that the current parameter configuration—particularly the combination of 20 hunters, high female vulnerability, and reproductive constraints—exceeds the population's compensatory capacity.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The temporal dynamics suggest an intervention window of approximately 300–500 days (steps) before irreversible population collapse; once total female energy falls below 4,000 units (approximately 60% depletion), recovery becomes statistically improbable regardless of subsequent management changes. The variability in extinction timing (ranging from ~800 to ~2,200 steps) indicates that stochastic environmental or demographic factors can delay but not prevent collapse under current hunting pressure. Conservation strategies must either reduce hunting pressure substantially within the first simulation year or enhance reproductive output beyond the current 730-tick birth spacing to alter the deterministic extinction outcome. The specific targeting of females (high food value, low processing cost) accelerates collapse by removing reproductive individuals; management protocols prioritizing female protection would significantly extend population persistence.
