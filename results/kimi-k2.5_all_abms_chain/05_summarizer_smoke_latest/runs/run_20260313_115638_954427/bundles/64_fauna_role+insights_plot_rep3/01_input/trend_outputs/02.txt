Aggregate energy levels for the forager population demonstrate a consistent negative trajectory across all replicate simulations, with total energy declining from near-zero initial values to approximately -35,000 to -40,000 energy units by simulation termination. 

During the initial phase from tick 0 to approximately tick 500, the forager population exhibits a modest energy decline to roughly -5,000 aggregate units, accompanied by notable variability between simulation runs. This early divergence suggests stochastic differences in initial hunting success as foragers begin exploiting the naive prey population, with some replicates experiencing brief periods of energy maintenance while others immediately enter deficit states.

After approximately tick 500, the trajectories converge and stabilize into a remarkably linear decline continuing through tick 2000 and beyond. During this phase, energy decreases at a roughly constant rate of approximately 15 to 18 units per tick for the aggregate population. By tick 1000, total energy reaches approximately -12,500 to -15,000 units; by tick 1500, values range between -22,000 and -25,000; and by tick 2000, most simulations record totals between -30,000 and -32,000. The persistence of this linear trend across all replicates indicates consistent energetic insufficiency, wherein movement costs of 1 unit per forager per tick (totaling 20 units for the full cohort) are not fully offset by caloric returns from hunting megaherbivores.

The progressive accumulation of negative energy, enabled by the immortal-foragers parameter setting, indicates that hunting returns per unit effort remain insufficient to sustain the forager population energetically, despite the food-storage mechanism allowing theoretically unlimited energy accumulation. The convergence of trajectories after the initial phase suggests that regardless of stochastic variation in early hunting success, the system stabilizes into a predictable steady-state of energy deficit as prey populations become depleted or more difficult to locate.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The consistent energy deficit across all simulation replicates suggests that a forager group of 20 individuals may be unsustainable given current prey densities of 250 animals and the specified foraging radius of 6 patches. Decision makers should consider that continued forager persistence in the landscape will likely require either reduced group sizes, expanded foraging ranges, supplemental resource procurement strategies, or prey population management to prevent excessive depletion that compromises forager energetic viability. The linear nature of the decline after tick 500 indicates that the system has reached a stable but energetically impoverished equilibrium that will persist indefinitely without intervention.
