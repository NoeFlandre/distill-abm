The cumulative mortality of female megaherbivores exhibits a characteristic saturation curve with distinct temporal phases reflecting the interaction between forager efficiency and prey population dynamics. The sequence initiates at zero and accumulates to approximately 123 individuals by tick 2312, though the trajectory is markedly non-linear with the maximum mean value of 123.67 occurring earlier at tick 1826 (approximately the fifth simulation year), followed by a slight regression toward the terminal value.

**Initial Establishment Phase (Ticks 0–245):** The series undergoes rapid accumulation immediately upon simulation commencement, with the steepest rates of increase occurring within the first 245 ticks. Statistical trend analysis confirms significantly positive monotonic increases throughout this interval (Mann-Kendall tau approaching 1.0, p<0.001), with slopes exceeding 0.21 kills per tick. This reflects the immediate exploitation of the initial population of 250 prey by 20 forager agents, during which hunters encounter naïve prey aggregations and accumulate energy stores through successful pursuits.

**Deceleration and Saturation Phase (Ticks 245–1826):** Following the initial burst, the accumulation rate undergoes successive decelerations marked by inflection points at ticks 262, 285, 1204, 1336, 1487, 1631, 1725, and 1800. The Mann-Kendall slopes decline progressively from 0.226 to 0.023, then to 0.006 and 0.002 (all p<0.001), indicating that while female kills continue to accumulate, the incremental rate diminishes substantially. This deceleration corresponds to the depletion of the prey population and the increased foraging costs as herd sizes contract across the grassland landscape. Change points at ticks 375, 475, 625, and 850 demarcate shifts in the accumulation regime, likely reflecting the exhaustion of initial high-density prey patches and the transition to more dispersed, harder-to-locate targets.

**Peak and Plateau Dynamics (Ticks 1826–2312):** The cumulative count reaches its asymptote near tick 1826, after which the series exhibits stability with minor negative drift. Notably, rolling trend analysis reveals a significant decreasing trend (tau=-0.453, p<0.001) in the final temporal window (ticks 1856–2087), suggesting that in the aggregate mean across the 40 simulation runs, the accumulation of female kills not only stalled but slightly reversed. This pattern implies that in individual simulation runs reaching extinction events, the cumulative totals ceased increasing, while the cross-run average declined as high-mortality runs concluded, leaving only runs with lower cumulative harvests in the sample.

**Late-Stage Oscillations:** The terminal interval (ticks 2170–2265) reveals persistent oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks (roughly bi-monthly cycles given the 365-tick annual calibration). These oscillations likely reflect the strong seasonality parameter (0.8) driving fluctuations in grass availability and prey aggregation patterns, which in turn modulate hunter success rates even as the overall population approaches collapse. The presence of these cycles in the aggregate data suggests that hunting pressure on females remains synchronized with environmental productivity pulses until the simulation termination.

**Overall Trajectory:** The median cumulative kill (119.06) exceeds the mean (104.56), indicating a left-skewed distribution across runs where most simulations accumulated kills rapidly toward the upper asymptote, while a subset exhibited lower cumulative mortality. This divergence captures the model's exploration of variable outcomes where stochastic events and initial forager-prey spatial configurations lead either to rapid overexploitation or more sustainable, lower-impact hunting patterns across the 200-year simulation horizon.
