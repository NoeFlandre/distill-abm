 The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system . Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods .  The mean household risk attitude begins the simulation period concentrated near zero, with initial values ranging between approximately -0.1 and 0.2 . During the first 20 time steps, these attitudes diverge rapidly, with a substantial proportion of households developing strongly positive risk dispositions . From step 60 through the conclusion at step 100, individual household risk attitudes stabilize across a wide spectrum .  The mean green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units across all simulation runs . During the initial 10 steps, values exhibit a marginal increase to roughly 45,500 units before initiating a sustained decline . This decline accelerates through step 40, indicating significant pasture degradation where grazing pressure from the initial livestock endowment exceeds vegetation regrowth capacity .  The average number of livestock destocked per household begins at zero and immediately surges to peak values between 6 and 7.5 animals within the first 5 time steps . This initial spike reflects the immediate pressure on vegetation resources when all households begin with 90 animals, forcing rapid reductions as pastures cannot support the initial stocking density .  The number of livestock destocked commences at zero across all simulation runs and increases rapidly during the initial 20 steps . The cumulative destocking trajectories diverge considerably across stochastic replicates . By step 100, these trajectories reach values between 110 and 173 animals .  This high outcome variability suggests that the performance of non-learning approaches in variable grazing systems is extremely sensitive to initial environmental realizations and neighborhood effects . The implication is that promoting rigid rule-of-thumb management carries substantial risk: under some stochastic conditions it may become the dominant norm through social diffusion .
