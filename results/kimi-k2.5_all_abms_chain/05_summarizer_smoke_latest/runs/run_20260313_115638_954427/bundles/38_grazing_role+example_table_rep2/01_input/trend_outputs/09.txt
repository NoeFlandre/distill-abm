The cumulative number of destocked livestock across the 60 household agents begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation, reaching an average of 96.78 animals per household by the terminal timestep. This trajectory indicates substantial livestock turnover, exceeding the initial herd size of 90 animals, which implies that households engaged in repeated cycles of destocking and restocking rather than maintaining static herds.

During the initial 25-year phase, the rate of destocking declines precipitously from approximately 4.96 animals per year in the first decade to 0.69 animals per year between years 22 and 32. This rapid deceleration reflects the initial adjustment period where farmers reduced herd densities from the initial stocking level toward sustainable carrying capacities aligned with private pasture productivity and highly variable rainfall conditions (mean 200, standard deviation 100). The inflection points detected between years 45 and 57 suggest oscillatory adjustments in destocking intensity as the three learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—interacted with stochastic environmental feedback.

A significant structural change occurs around year 25, where the accumulation rate shifts from steep decline to a more gradual slope of approximately 0.36, marking the transition from initial overstocking correction to maintenance-phase management. A second change point at year 60 further stabilizes the system, after which the destocking rate asymptotically approaches 0.31 animals per year through year 100. This late-phase stability indicates that households had largely converged on sustainable stocking strategies, with subsequent destocking representing routine management responses to environmental stochasticity rather than structural herd reduction.

The analysis reveals a dominant oscillation period of approximately 49 years in the destocking rate, with clustered activity around years 22-26, 49-61, and 79-80. These cyclical patterns likely correspond to multi-decadal vegetation succession cycles—mediated by the recovery parameter w=0.8—or periodic extreme rainfall events that triggered coordinated destocking responses across the learning types. The progressive dampening of oscillation amplitude through time suggests that both individual experience accumulation and social information diffusion reduced the volatility of stocking decisions as the simulation progressed.
