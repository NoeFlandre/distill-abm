Cumulative mortality of female megaherbivores initiates at zero and undergoes a prolonged phase of rapid escalation during the initial 850 time steps, with the rate of increase gradually attenuating through successive intervals marked by inflection points at approximately ticks 125, 375, 475, 625, and 850. During this establishment phase, the accumulation of kills exhibits strong monotonic growth, transitioning from steep initial trajectories to progressively shallower slopes as hunter-forager agents exploit the initial prey population of 250 herds.\n\nBetween ticks 850 and 1600, the accumulation rate diminishes substantially, entering a quasi-equilibrium phase characterized by minimal slope values and intermittent plateau behavior around values of 119 to 120 total kills. This period suggests increasing difficulty in locating female prey or potential shifts in forager decision-making under optimal foraging constraints, as the initial high-density prey environment becomes depleted.\n\nA secondary phase of renewed accumulation emerges between ticks 1600 and 1850, driving the cumulative total to its maximum observed value of approximately 123.7 kills near tick 1851. This resurgence potentially reflects seasonal demographic fluctuations in the prey population—consistent with the model's 0.8 seasonality parameter and 63-tick oscillatory cycle—or temporary increases in female vulnerability related to birth spacing intervals (730 ticks) and nursing behaviors that augment food value.\n\nFollowing this peak, the cumulative count exhibits a declining trend through approximately tick 2087, stabilizing at terminal values around 123 kills by the conclusion of the simulation at tick 2312. This late-phase reduction in the aggregated cumulative metric likely indicates differential extinction dynamics across simulation replicates, wherein runs experiencing higher female mortality rates reach prey depletion or population collapse earlier, thereby influencing the cross-run average, or alternatively, reflect systematic shifts in hunter selectivity toward male prey (food value 30) as female densities decline and processing cost differentials become less favorable relative to search costs.\n\nThe trajectory reveals a dominant oscillatory period of 63 ticks superimposed on the long-term trend, particularly evident in the late simulation phases (ticks 2170-2265), suggesting regular pulsatile variation in hunting success potentially aligned with seasonal resource availability or herd movement patterns.\n\nImplications for decision makers: The front-loaded nature of female mortality—reaching 80% of peak accumulation within the first 850 ticks (approximately 2.3 years)—indicates that conservation interventions must be implemented immediately upon human entry into the system, as delayed action faces severely diminished population viability. The oscillatory pattern superimposed on the cumulative curve identifies predictable windows of heightened vulnerability occurring every 63 ticks, offering strategic opportunities for targeted protection measures. Finally, the plateau and subsequent decline after year 5 (tick ~1850) signals that either population collapse becomes inevitable in high-exposure scenarios or fundamental behavioral shifts in hunting strategy occur, necessitating monitoring protocols that track not merely cumulative mortality but active prey population densities and hunter selectivity patterns to distinguish between sustainable harvest and extinction trajectories.
