**Implications for Decision Makers**  
The nonlinear extinction trajectory demonstrates that sustained hunting pressure produces initial rapid depletion followed by misleading stability phases where population decline appears to slow or reverse, potentially creating false confidence in resource sustainability. Decision makers should recognize that temporary plateaus or minor recoveries in population metrics do not indicate resilience under constant exploitation pressure; rather, they represent lag effects in predator-prey dynamics preceding inevitable collapse. The pronounced 63-tick oscillatory cycles indicate predictable seasonal vulnerability windows where hunting intervention would be particularly devastating. Management protocols must account for these temporal heterogeneities and avoid interpreting density-dependent slowdowns in decline as evidence of sustainable harvest levels. Furthermore, the irreversibility of the final extinction phase, occurring after prolonged low-density persistence, underscores the importance of maintaining prey population thresholds well above the minimum viable levels observed during the transient stabilization periods.
