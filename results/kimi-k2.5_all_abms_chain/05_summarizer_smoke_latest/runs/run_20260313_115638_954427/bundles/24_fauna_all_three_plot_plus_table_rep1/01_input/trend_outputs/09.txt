The female megaherbivore population initializes at 125 individuals across all simulation replicates, ultimately declining to extinction at approximately 2,310 steps. The trajectory follows a markedly non-linear pattern characterized by rapid initial attrition, moderate deceleration, and protracted persistence at critically low densities with a mean abundance of 16.48 and median of 1 individual.\n\nDuring the first 300 to 500 steps, the population experiences steep declines from 125 to between 20 and 40 individuals, with substantial inter-replicate variance indicating stochastic effects in hunter-prey encounter dynamics under the pressure of 20 human foragers. An inflection point at step 305 marks a transition from exponential collapse to slower attrition as encounter probabilities diminish. By step 475, the first structural change point indicates transition to a low-density regime, with populations declining further to near-zero levels by step 875.\n\nBetween steps 730 and 800, secondary peaks and valleys suggest brief demographic pulses coinciding with the 730-tick birth spacing reproductive interval, wherein aggregate numbers temporarily stabilize around 5 to 6 individuals. Similarly, a transient increasing trend occurs between steps 1,160 and 1,390, reflecting temporary demographic recoveries. However, these oscillations—occurring with a period of approximately 63 steps likely tied to the 0.8 seasonality parameter—prove insufficient to counteract continuous predation pressure from immortal hunters with food storage capabilities.\n\nThe final phase from step 875 to 2,310 exhibits prolonged persistence at extinction-vortex densities, where populations fluctuate briefly before final collapse to zero. The high standard deviation of 31.55 relative to the mean indicates extreme variability in extinction timing across replicates, with some populations maintaining 1–2 individuals for extended durations while others expire earlier.\n\nFor decision makers: The deterministic extinction outcome under the current configuration indicates that sustainable coexistence requires intervention to reduce hunter numbers below the critical threshold of 20, implement hunter mortality (removing the immortality condition) to create prey refuge periods, or adjust sexual dimorphism parameters that currently render females nearly as valuable to hunters as males despite lower processing costs. The brief reproductive recoveries observed around biennial birth intervals suggest that minor reductions in hunting pressure could potentially allow seasonal and demographic pulses to maintain populations above critical minimum thresholds.
