 The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system . Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods .  Simulations exhibit a pronounced non-linear trajectory characterized by an initial decline, a prolonged trough, and a subsequent sustained recovery culminating in biomass levels substantially exceeding initial conditions . The mean household risk attitude across the farming population exhibits a distinct trajectory over the 100-year simulation period .  The simulation begins with an initial mean livestock plus destock value of 90 animals per household . During the first 37 steps, the system undergoes a pronounced decline, reaching a minimum value of approximately 41 animals by step 37 . The decline is steepest in the initial phase (steps 0–10) with a slope of -3.47, moderating but continuing significantly through steps 11–32 .  Statistical trend analysis confirms significant decreasing patterns across three consecutive windows spanning years 0–10, 11–21, and 22–32, with slope magnitudes moderating from -0381 to -0.060, indicating slowing but consistent improvement . Destocking rates stabilize near the median value of 0.31 animals, with minimal variation through year 100 .  Some trajectories stabilize early at lower saturation levels near 18 households, others plateau at intermediate values around 30 to 42, and some reach higher equilibrium states of 48 to 54 households . Half to three-quarters of households may eventually engage in social learning behaviors, depending on the specific stochastic path of the simulation .
