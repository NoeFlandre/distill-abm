Simulations exhibit a pronounced non-linear trajectory characterized by an initial decline, a prolonged trough, and a subsequent sustained recovery culminating in biomass levels substantially exceeding initial conditions. The system initiates at approximately 90,000 units, undergoing a brief fluctuation that peaks near 90,523 at step 3 before entering a statistically significant decreasing phase. This decline persists until reaching a minimum value of approximately 84,115 at step 26, representing a roughly 6.5% reduction from initial biomass.

Following this nadir, the system undergoes a regime shift around steps 33 to 50. A significant increasing trend emerges near step 33, with the rate of biomass accumulation peaking between steps 44 and 54 at approximately 1,909 units per step. During this steep recovery phase, biomass increases rapidly from the minimum value, surpassing initial conditions by approximately step 50 (the first change point) and continuing to rise through step 100.

The recovery rate moderates progressively in subsequent intervals: decelerating to roughly 643 units per step between steps 55 and 65, then to 93 units between steps 66 and 76, and finally approaching an asymptotic level with minimal gains of approximately 5 to 20 units per step in the final periods (steps 77 to 98). By the conclusion of the simulations at step 100, mean reserve biomass stabilizes near 121,073 units, representing a 34.5% net increase above initial conditions and a 44% increase from the minimum value observed at step 26.

This trajectory suggests that initial stocking levels (90 animals per household across 60 households) impose grazing pressure exceeding sustainable utilization rates given the high rainfall variability (mean 200 units, standard deviation 100 units) and vegetation growth parameters. The decline phase reflects cumulative pressure as utilization temporarily outpaces vegetation recovery. The subsequent sustained increase indicates that the implemented learning mechanisms—encompassing baseline rule-of-thumb processing, learning-by-doing, and social learning—successfully enable farmers to adjust stocking decisions, reducing pressure sufficiently to allow substantial biomass accumulation beyond initial states. The progressive slowing of growth rates in later stages suggests convergence toward a higher equilibrium carrying capacity under adapted management regimes, with the inflection around step 48 marking the transition from accelerating to decelerating recovery. Oscillation patterns detected between steps 10 through 20 and steps 41 through 49 align with the transition from decline to stagnation and the subsequent steep recovery phase, indicating dynamic adjustment periods as the system responds to stochastic rainfall variation and adaptive management interventions.
