The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The number of active hunter agents remains fixed at 20 individuals throughout the entire simulation, from the initial step through all recorded time steps. This value shows zero variance across the temporal sequence, with no detectable increases, decreases, or fluctuations occurring at any point in the modeled period. The cumulative count of male prey mortality from hunting begins at zero and exhibits a characteristic sigmoid growth pattern over the 2,312-step observation period, ultimately saturating at approximately 117.5 individuals. The system undergoes rapid acceleration, with the steepest rates of increase occurring between steps 232 and 463, where the effective accumulation slope reaches 0.219 kills per step. By step 500, the cumulative mortality approaches 80-90 individuals, representing intensive predation pressure on the initial male ungulate cohort ivore populations to effective extinction within approximately 1,800 time steps. The near-total elimination of females suggests that the hunting pressure is unsustainable. This leads to rapid demographic collapse of the prey population. The high variance in terminal values indicates substantial stochastic divergence in demographic trajectories. Key transition points at steps 1325, 1850, and 2050 demarcate shifts between increasing and decreasing birth accumulation rates. The extended initial period of reproductive inactivity (700+ steps) demonstrates that monitoring programs must account for long maturation and interbirth intervals when assessing megaherbivore population viability. Population trajectory exhibits a characteristic stepped descent rather than smooth decay, with statistical change points detected at regular intervals (125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 875 steps) a minority of simulation runs exhibit extended persistence, with small residual populations maintaining low numbers through step 1000 and persisting until approximately step 2200 before final extinction. The total number of animals begins at 250 individuals and undergoes a deterministic decline toward extinction across all simulation runs, ending at 0 by step 2310. The aggregate mean across 120 simulation runs starts at 166.67 individuals and follows a monotonically decreasing trajectory with brief periods of minor fluctuation at very low population levels. By step 475, most populations have fallen below sustainable thresholds, and the median population across runs declines to approximately 1.48 individuals. Individual simulation trajectories show heterogeneity in extinction timing. Some maintain small male populations until after step 2,000. A brief anomalous increasing trend detected between steps 1,392 and 1,623 likely represents a transient recovery or demographic rebound. The critical intervention window occurs within the first 375 steps, before populations lose 80% of their initial energy. The total energy of all animals begins at approximately 16,667 units. This energy undergoes a precipitous decline, dropping sharply as hunting pressure rapidly depletes accessible herds. Between steps 500 and 1500, the system maintains a metastable state generally fluctuating between 32.5% and 33.0%, interrupted by secondary dips of smaller magnitude (2–4 percentage points) the system exhibits increased divergence across simulation instances, suggesting effective resilience in the grassland recovery dynamics.
