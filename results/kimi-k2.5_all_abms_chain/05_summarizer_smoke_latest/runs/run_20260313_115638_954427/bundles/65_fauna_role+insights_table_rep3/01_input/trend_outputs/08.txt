The male megaherbivore subpopulation initiated at 125 individuals, representing half of the initial ungulate cohort. From tick 0, the trajectory exhibits a pronounced declining trend toward extirpation, reaching zero individuals by tick 2284 despite the simulation continuing toward substantially longer durations. This decline proceeds through distinct phases: an initial steep reduction during the first 700 ticks, followed by a decelerating descent characterized by significant oscillatory fluctuations superimposed on the downward trend.

Periodic oscillations with a dominant wavelength of approximately 63 ticks reflect the strong environmental seasonality parameter driving resource availability pulses throughout the annual cycle. Ensemble mean values demonstrate residual peaks of roughly 10.5 individuals around tick 730, declining to secondary peaks near 8.8 at tick 709 and 6.8 at tick 803, with successive peak amplitudes diminishing as the population approaches critically low densities. Valley nadirs occur between these peaks, creating a sawtooth pattern of temporary demographic contractions and expansions synchronized with seasonal resource abundance.

Notably, between ticks 1392 and 1623, the trend reverses temporarily to a significant increasing pattern, representing a density-dependent relaxation of hunting pressure or reduced intraspecific competition that permitted partial population rebound. However, this resurgence proves insufficient for long-term viability; following this interval, the population stabilizes at low densities near 2-3 individuals before terminal decline to extinction.

Given model parameters where males provide marginally higher gross energetic value compared to females despite similar net returns after processing, the complete extirpation of males while the simulation continues implicates behavioral or demographic mechanisms that concentrated hunting mortality on this sex. The high seasonality coefficient of 0.8 amplifies this pressure by creating periodic resource bottlenecks that compound anthropogenic stress, driving oscillations of increasing amplitude relative to the declining mean.

Decision makers should recognize that sex-selective harvesting strategies, whether resulting from energetic preferences or behavioral traits, risk rapid demographic collapse under seasonal environmental stress. The observed temporary recovery phase between ticks 1392-1623 indicates that brief population rebounds should not be interpreted as signs of sustainability; rather, they represent transient dynamics within an unsustainable extraction regime. Conservation efforts must account for Allee effects and mating system constraints that emerge when male densities fall below critical thresholds, particularly in highly seasonal environments where resource bottlenecks exacerbate anthropogenic mortality.
