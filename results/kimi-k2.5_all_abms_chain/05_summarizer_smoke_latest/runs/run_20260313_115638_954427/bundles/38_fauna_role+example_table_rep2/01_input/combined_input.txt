The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to the combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change. Unlike traditional approaches that look at populations as uniform groups, this model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances. The model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for the herbivores.

The primary goal is to understand which combinations of hunting pressure, environmental conditions, and animal life history traits lead to population collapse versus stable coexistence. The model specifically incorporates sexual dimorphism—meaning males and females can have different sizes and nutritional values to hunters—along with realistic herd movement patterns and reproductive biology including pregnancy, nursing, and interbirth intervals.

The simulation includes two main types of agents: human foragers and ungulate herds. The human foragers operate according to dynamic optimal foraging theory, meaning they constantly evaluate whether pursuing a particular animal is worth the energy expenditure based on the animal's food value, the difficulty of processing it, and how much energy the hunter currently has stored. The ungulate herds consist of males, females, and nursing females with calves. These animals gain energy from eating grass, lose energy through movement, and face mortality from starvation, age-related senescence, external predation, and human hunting.

The environment itself is dynamic, with grass that can be consumed by herbivores and that regrows according to user-defined parameters. The model can simulate seasonal environments where grass availability fluctuates throughout the year, and users can control the proportion of the landscape that initially contains vegetation as well as how quickly that vegetation returns after being eaten.

The model operates through discrete time steps called "ticks," which can be scaled to represent any temporal unit but are often set to represent days. The simulation can be stopped automatically when either a maximum number of ticks is reached or if the herd population exceeds a certain size, depending on user preferences.

The specific parameters for this experimental configuration are as follows:

**Population Initialization:**
The model begins with init-foragers ranging from 0 to 100, which we have set at 20, representing the initial number of human hunters in the landscape. The ungulate population starts with init-prey ranging from 0 to 1000, set at 250 animals.

**Temporal Settings:**
Time is calibrated so that ticks-per-year ranges from 0 to 720, set at 365 to represent daily time steps. The simulation will run until max-ticks reaches 73,000, with stop-at-max-ticks set to True, meaning the experiment terminates at this temporal limit. The alternative stopping condition, stop-at-max-herd-size, is set to False, allowing the simulation to continue regardless of whether the population reaches the max-herd-size of 500.

**Animal Life History and Biology:**
The ungulates follow realistic life history parameters. Maximum-lifespan ranges from 0 to 12,000 ticks, set at 10,220, while age-of-senescence ranges from 0 to 10,000, set at 7,300, marking when age-related mortality increases. Baseline mortality rates include internal-mortality ranging from 0.0 to 0.2, set at 0.025, representing baseline physiological death, and external-mortality ranging from 0.0 to 0.2, set at 0.0025, simulating predation or other non-human causes. Reproductive timing is controlled by birth-spacing, ranging from 0 to 1,000 ticks, set at 730, representing the interbirth interval. The nutritional value of offspring is determined by food-value-calf, ranging from 0 to 20, set at 3.

**Sexual Dimorphism and Hunting Value:**
The model distinguishes between sexes in terms of hunter payoff. Food-value-males ranges from 5 to 100, set at 30, while food-value-females ranges from 5 to 100, set at 27, representing the energy hunters gain from consuming each sex. The effort required to process carcasses differs by sex, with processing-cost-males ranging from 0 to 20, set at 6, and processing-cost-females ranging from 0 to 20, set at 3, representing the energy expended to butcher and prepare the meat.

**Animal Energetics:**
Ungulate energy dynamics are governed by several parameters. Animals-gain-from-food ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1.5, representing energy obtained from grazing. The threshold for starvation mortality, animals-starvation-threshold, ranges from 0 to 100, set at 25. Movement costs energy, with animals-movement-cost ranging from 0 to 10, set at 1.

**Hunter Behavior and Energetics:**
Human agents follow similar energetic rules. Foragers-movement-cost ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1. The distance hunters can perceive and pursue prey is determined by foraging-radius, ranging from 1 to 100, set at 6. Two critical behavioral switches control hunter mortality and storage: imortal-foragers is set to True, meaning hunters do not die from energy depletion, and food-storage is set to True, allowing hunters to accumulate energy surpluses.

**Environmental Parameters:**
The vegetation dynamics are controlled by several settings. Grass-proportion ranges from 0.0 to 1.0, set at 0.33, determining what fraction of the landscape initially carries vegetation. The speed of vegetation recovery is set by regrowth-time, ranging from 0 to 365, set at 3 ticks. Environmental fluctuation is controlled by seasonality, ranging from 0.0 to 1.0, set at 0.8, representing the intensity of seasonal variation in resource availability. Three boolean switches control vegetation behavior: grass-growth is set to True, enabling vegetation regrowth; keep-initial-grass-pattern is set to True, restricting regrowth to patches that were initially vegetated; and seasonality at 0.8 creates strong seasonal pulses in resource availability.

The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation period. This stability reflects the model configuration wherein forager agents are immortal and do not experience mortality from energy depletion, while lacking any recruitment or dispersal mechanisms. Consequently, the anthropogenic hunting pressure maintains a fixed magnitude in terms of absolute hunter abundance across all time steps.

The aggregate energy available to forager bands begins at 2000 units and undergoes a persistent, monotonic decline throughout the simulation, terminating at approximately -38229 units by tick 2310. This trajectory indicates a progressive shift from initial energy surplus to substantial cumulative energy deficit across the forager population.

The decline occurs in distinct phases marked by changing rates of energy depletion. During the initial phase from tick 0 to 650, energy decreases at a rate of approximately -11.07 units per tick, with brief fluctuations including a local peak around tick 55 at roughly 1263 units. From tick 650 to 1075, the rate of decline moderates slightly to -8.82 units per tick, suggesting a transient adjustment in the balance between energy acquisition and expenditure. Subsequently, the depletion accelerates markedly: between ticks 1075 and 1500, the decline rate increases to -15.73 units per tick, followed by -18.54 units per tick from 1500 to 1925, and finally reaching approximately -20.00 units per tick from 1925 to 2310.

Superimposed upon this downward trend are regular oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, likely corresponding to seasonal resource cycles given the environmental seasonality parameter of 0.8. These oscillations manifest as alternating peaks and troughs in energy availability, with amplitudes increasing as the baseline declines. Notable oscillatory activity occurs between ticks 2170 and 2265, where energy levels fluctuate between approximately -37717 and -38233 units, representing the most extreme variance in the terminal phase of the simulation.

The persistent negative trajectory reflects a fundamental energetic imbalance wherein forager expenditure consistently exceeds acquisition through hunting. The initial value of 2000 represents established energy reserves distributed among the initial population of 20 foragers, while the progressive decline suggests that the available megaherbivore biomass starting at 250 individuals cannot sustain the energetic demands of the forager population under the specified hunting pressure and movement costs. The acceleration of decline in later phases indicates that as prey populations diminish, foragers must expend increasing energy to locate and pursue remaining animals, creating a positive feedback loop of accelerating energy deficit. The final value of approximately -38229 represents a substantial cumulative energy debt, indicating that if the model permitted forager mortality from energy depletion rather than the configured immortality, the population would have collapsed prior to the simulation endpoint.

The cumulative number of male prey harvested by forager bands begins at zero and undergoes a rapid accumulation phase during the initial 850 time steps, exhibiting a steep increasing trend with an initial slope of approximately 0.17 kills per tick. The system reaches its maximum cumulative kill count of approximately 120 males near tick 1876, after which the value declines slightly to stabilize around 117.5 by the simulation end at tick 2312. This late-phase decrease, unusual for a strictly cumulative metric, may indicate hunter abandonment of pursuit, population collapse feedback effects, or accounting corrections as the system approaches equilibrium. Throughout the simulation, an oscillatory pattern with a period of approximately 63 ticks is superimposed on the trend, likely reflecting seasonal hunting rhythms synchronized with the strong environmental seasonality parameter (0.8), where pulsed resource availability and breeding cycles create periodic windows of heightened hunting success.

The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed begins at zero and increases rapidly during the initial phase of the simulation. By approximately tick 125, the series exhibits its first major change point, transitioning from the initial establishment phase into a period of sustained growth. Between ticks 475 and 850, the rate of increase begins to decelerate noticeably, as evidenced by the change points at these intervals and the declining slope values in the rolling trend analysis. This deceleration suggests that the initial abundance of vulnerable female prey is being depleted, forcing foragers to expend more effort per kill or switch to alternative targets. Notably, the rolling trend analysis reveals a shift from consistently increasing patterns in early windows (with tau values of 1.000 and steep positive slopes) to a decreasing trend in the final observation window (ticks 1856–2087), suggesting a slight decline or stabilization in the cumulative count during the simulation's final stages.

Throughout the latter portion of the simulation, specifically between ticks 2170 and 2265, the series exhibits oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks. This periodicity, roughly corresponding to bi-monthly cycles given the temporal calibration of 365 ticks per year, likely reflects seasonal fluctuations in hunting success or demographic availability of female prey. The low prominence values associated with detected peaks and valleys indicate that these oscillations occur as minor fluctuations atop the established plateau rather than as dramatic population swings.

Overall, the trajectory demonstrates a classic exploitation curve: an initial lag phase followed by rapid acceleration as foragers encounter naive prey, subsequent deceleration as the most vulnerable individuals are removed, and ultimate stabilization as the system reaches a new equilibrium where hunting mortality on females balances with their demographic production or as the prey population approaches local extinction in some simulation replicates.

The cumulative number of births begins at zero and remains negligible through the first approximately 600 ticks, representing an establishment phase where the initial population of 250 animals has not yet produced significant offspring. Around tick 613, the metric begins a sustained acceleration, entering a growth phase characterized by monotonically increasing values punctuated by regular oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 50 ticks. The growth phase continues through several distinct periods of acceleration, with significant inflection points occurring at ticks 733, 903, and 1121, indicating successive waves of reproductive expansion as the herd exploits available vegetation. The metric reaches local peaks at ticks 1073 (value 2.875), 1214 (value 3.45), and 1392 (value 4.3) before attaining a global maximum of 5.6 at tick 1702—approximately 147 years into the simulation. This peak represents the maximum cumulative recruitment achieved before hunting pressure fundamentally alters the population trajectory.

Following a change point at tick 1850 (approximately year 160), the trend reverses sharply. The cumulative count declines steadily through the final quarter of the simulation, with statistically significant decreasing trends (Mann-Kendall tau = -0.495 and -0.453) in the windows spanning ticks 1624 to 2087. This decline reflects a demographic collapse where the removal of individuals by the 20 human foragers—operating under optimal foraging strategies with food storage capabilities—exceeds the population's reproductive capacity. The birth-spacing interval of 730 ticks (two years) cannot sustain replacement against sustained predation pressure, particularly as hunter efficiency increases on the reduced population.

By the simulation's conclusion at tick 2312 (year 200), the cumulative number of births has fallen to 1.0, representing a collapse of over 82% from the peak value. This terminal value indicates that the megaherbivore population has been driven to functional extinction, with recruitment reduced to minimal levels as the remaining animals are unable to find mates or achieve sufficient energy stores for reproduction amid continued hunting pressure and resource competition. The trajectory illustrates a classic overshoot-and-collapse dynamic where initial population growth masks the unsustainable harvest rate until a critical threshold is crossed, after which hunting pressure drives a rapid downward spiral in recruitment.

The cumulative mortality of ungulates initiates at zero and follows a sigmoidal accumulation pattern, ultimately reaching approximately 240 deaths by tick 1876 before stabilizing near 235 deaths at the conclusion of the observation window at tick 2312.

Superimposed throughout the entire 2312-tick sequence is a robust oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of 63 ticks, generating regular fluctuations in mortality accumulation.
