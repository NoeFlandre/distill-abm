The population of female megaherbivores initiates at 125 individuals and undergoes a deterministic decline toward extinction across the simulation duration. The trajectory exhibits three distinct phases: an initial rapid collapse, an intermediate period of decelerated decay with oscillatory dynamics, and a terminal low-density phase characterized by high stochasticity and eventual elimination to zero abundance.

During the first approximately 475 time steps, the population experiences its steepest reduction, plummeting from 125 to below 20 individuals. This precipitous decline coincides with the first detected change point at 475, reflecting intense hunting pressure from 20 human foragers operating with optimal foraging strategies, food storage capabilities, and a perception radius of 6 patches. The inflection point at 305 marks a subtle acceleration in the decay rate, likely indicating the exhaustion of the initial reproductive buffer as the population confronts the combined mortality from hunting, baseline physiological costs (internal mortality of 0.025), and energetic constraints in a seasonal environment with 0.8 seasonality intensity.

Between approximately 475 and 875 time steps, the rate of decline moderates considerably, with the population fluctuating between roughly 15 and 5 individuals. This phase contains significant inflection points at 740 and 833, demarcating shifts in the trajectory slope. A dominant oscillation period of 63 time steps suggests regular pulsing in survival rates, likely synchronized with seasonal vegetation cycles where grass regrowth every 3 ticks creates temporary resource abundance. The second change point at 600 and third at 875 bracket this interval, which represents a transitional regime where demographic stochasticity begins to dominate over deterministic hunting pressure.

After approximately 875 time steps, the population enters a critically low-density regime with mean abundance below 5 individuals, though individual simulation runs exhibit high variance in persistence time. Despite statistically significant brief increasing trends detected between ticks 1160-1391 (tau = 0.093) and 1856-2087 (tau = 0.453)—where temporary positive slopes suggest successful reproduction events aligned with the 730-tick interbirth interval—the overall trajectory remains inexorably decreasing. These ephemeral recoveries to values of 6 or fewer individuals prove insufficient to overcome the Allee effects and continued hunting pressure from immortal foragers.

The median abundance of 1.0 across the full simulation indicates that for the majority of the temporal domain, the population operates at critically low numbers, while the mean of 16.48 is heavily skewed by the high initial values. The oscillation indices spanning 45 to 140 time steps reveal complex cyclical dynamics at the population nadir, likely reflecting the interaction between birth pulses, seasonal resource availability, and stochastic hunting encounters. All 40 simulation replicates ultimately converge to zero abundance by the final time step, with the last individuals persisting in scattered runs until beyond 2000 ticks, demonstrating that even with grass regrowth and reproductive potential, the combination of human hunting efficiency and long interbirth intervals precludes population recovery.
