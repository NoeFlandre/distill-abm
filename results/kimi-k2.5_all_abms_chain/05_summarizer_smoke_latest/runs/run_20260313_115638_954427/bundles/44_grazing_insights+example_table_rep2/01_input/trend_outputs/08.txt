The number of households engaging in social learning begins at zero and follows a logistic diffusion pattern throughout the simulation period. In the first seven time steps, engagement increases sharply from zero to approximately fourteen households, reflecting rapid early adoption as agents initially observe neighbor performance and activate social learning protocols within their knowledge radius. This acceleration continues through approximately time step forty, though at markedly decreasing rates—from an initial steep ascent to incremental gains of less than one household per step—as the pool of susceptible agents diminishes and private property boundaries constrain information flow.

The system approaches saturation around time step sixty-three, stabilizing at approximately thirty-two households, where it remains constant through the conclusion of the simulation at time step one hundred. This plateau at roughly fifty-three percent of the total population indicates persistent behavioral heterogeneity, suggesting that in stationary smallholder systems with limited knowledge radii, social learning reaches an equilibrium below universal adoption. The remaining households likely maintain exclusive reliance on internal learning-by-doing processes or baseline satisficing behaviors.

Notable fluctuations occur between time steps twenty-two and thirty-two, and again between steps fifty-one and fifty-nine, indicating temporary variations in engagement as households potentially cycle into and out of social learning states in response to stochastic rainfall variability or shifting perceptions of neighbor success. The primary transition in adoption dynamics occurs near time step forty, where the accumulation of engaged households shifts from active growth to the stable equilibrium observed in the final thirty-seven time steps.

**Implications for decision makers:** The saturation of social learning at approximately half of all households suggests that adaptive co-management interventions in private-property grazing systems face structural ceiling effects, even with high initial receptivity. While early-adopter programs can achieve rapid uptake within the first decade of simulation time, extending reach to the remaining households may require strategies that overcome spatial fragmentation—such as extending knowledge networks beyond immediate neighbors—or addressing the heterogeneous risk attitudes that prevent persistent engagement. The persistence of a substantial non-learning minority could undermine system resilience if coordinated adaptation becomes necessary under extreme climatic scenarios, indicating that complementary policy instruments may be needed to ensure adequate coverage of adaptive behaviors across the entire farming community.
