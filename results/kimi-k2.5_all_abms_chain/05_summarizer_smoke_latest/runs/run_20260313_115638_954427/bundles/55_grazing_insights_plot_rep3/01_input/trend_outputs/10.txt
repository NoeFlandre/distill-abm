The time series tracks the count of E-RO (non-learning) households over 100 simulation steps across multiple stochastic runs. All simulations initialize with approximately 20 non-learning households. During the initial phase (steps 0-10), the trajectories exhibit immediate and extreme divergence: in several runs, the count of non-learning households surges rapidly to peaks between 38 and 42, while in other runs, the count plummets sharply to values between 0 and 10.

During the middle phase (steps 10-40), the high-count trajectories generally maintain elevated levels between 35 and 40 before beginning a gradual decline around step 35. Concurrently, a distinct cluster of runs stabilizes at intermediate levels of approximately 25-30 households, while low-count runs either remain suppressed or continue declining toward zero.

In the late phase (steps 40-100), the majority of surviving high-count trajectories converge downward to join the intermediate cluster, stabilizing within a range of 23-28 households. However, substantial heterogeneity persists across the ensemble: by step 100, final values range from 0 (complete displacement of non-learning households) to approximately 26, with a notable clustering around 24-26 for populations where non-learning strategies persist.

Implications for decision makers: The pronounced divergence observed in early steps indicates high sensitivity to stochastic conditions or initial configurations, suggesting that early-phase interventions or conditions critically influence whether non-learning strategies dominate or fail. The persistence of non-learning households at levels roughly comparable to or exceeding initial counts (stabilizing around 24-26 from an initial 20) in many runs demonstrates that non-learning strategies can exhibit considerable resilience against learning-based alternatives. Conversely, the complete displacement observed in other runs indicates that under certain conditions, learning strategies can entirely supplant non-learning behaviors. This heterogeneity implies that policy makers should anticipate persistent diversity in household decision-making strategies rather than expecting universal adoption of learning behaviors, and should design interventions robust to this strategic heterogeneity.
