The data show the diffusion of social learning behaviors across a population of 60 households over 100 time steps. At initialization (step 0), no households have engaged in social learning. Between approximately steps 5 and 25, all simulation runs exhibit a rapid, step-like increase in the count of households with social learning, with adoption occurring in discrete increments rather than gradually. By step 25, most trajectories have reached 60-90 percent of their final saturation levels. 

The saturation phase occurs between steps 25 and 100, where the adoption curves flatten considerably. Most runs stabilize by step 40, though several trajectories show late adoption events around step 60, with counts jumping from approximately 30 to 42 households. 

Final adoption levels vary substantially across the 60-household population, ranging from approximately 18 households (30% adoption) to 54 households (90% adoption) by step 100. This high variability suggests that stochastic factors or initial conditions significantly influence whether social learning becomes nearly universal or remains limited to a minority of households, despite all runs starting from identical parameter configurations.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The rapid initial diffusion (within 25 steps) indicates that social learning interventions can achieve quick uptake when conditions are favorable. However, the wide variation in final adoption rates (30-90%) suggests that relying on organic diffusion alone may leave substantial portions of the population non-adopters. Decision makers should prepare for the possibility that 10-70% of households may never adopt social learning behaviors spontaneously, potentially requiring targeted interventions or alternative support mechanisms for these persistent non-adopters after the initial rapid diffusion phase concludes.
