The cumulative number of destocked livestock increases monotonically throughout the 100-timestep simulation period. Starting from zero, the metric accumulates most rapidly during the initial 20-25 timesteps, then exhibits a progressively decelerating rate of increase through the remainder of the period. By the final timestep, the mean cumulative destocking reaches approximately 97 livestock units, with substantial variation across the 30 simulation runs ranging from approximately 58 to 173.

The trajectory is characterized by a steep initial accumulation phase with a slope of approximately 5 units per timestep during the first 10 steps, declining to approximately 1.5 units per timestep between timesteps 11-21, and further stabilizing at approximately 0.3 units per timestep from timestep 55 onward. Notable structural shifts in the accumulation rate occur around timesteps 25 and 60, corresponding to transitions between distinct phases of destocking pressure.

In the context of the private property grazing system with heterogeneous behavioral types, these patterns indicate that destocking events accumulate continuously over the century-long simulation as households respond to pasture conditions and climatic variability. The decelerating accumulation rate suggests that initial decades experience more intensive destocking pressures, possibly reflecting adjustment to initial conditions or early learning phases, while later periods show more stabilized management patterns despite continued cumulative stress. The wide variation across simulation runs highlights the sensitivity of destocking outcomes to stochastic ecological factors and the differential effectiveness of the three behavioral strategies in managing livestock numbers under private property arrangements.
