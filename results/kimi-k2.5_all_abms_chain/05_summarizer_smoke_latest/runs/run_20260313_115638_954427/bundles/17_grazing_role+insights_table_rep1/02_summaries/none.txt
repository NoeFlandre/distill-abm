**Context and Goals**

This agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Unlike the original RAGE model which examined common property scenarios where herders move livestock across shared rangelands, this extension focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods.

The simulation tracks the interaction between vegetation condition and household decisions about livestock numbers over time. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors.

The central purpose is understanding how these different ways of adapting to changing conditions influence pasture health, herd sizes, and the persistence of households. The model particularly examines how farmers handle uncertainty and incomplete information when making stocking decisions, represented through individual risk dispositions that affect how they perceive and react to environmental feedback.

**Key Parameters**

*Population and Spatial Setup:*
The **number-households** parameter establishes how many farming families occupy the landscape, which can range from 1 to 100 families, with this study using 60 households. Each household begins with **livestock-init** set at 90 animals. The simulation runs for **timesteps** set at 100 years. Farmers begin the simulation positioned on their designated land parcels, with **start-on-homepatch?** set to True. Each farmer can observe conditions within a **knowledge-radius** that ranges from 0 to 5 patches, set here at 1 patch distance.

*Behavioral Type Configuration:*
The study examines three specific learning strategies with equal representation: **number-E-RO** (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb), **number-E-LBD** (learning-by-doing), and **number-E-RO-SL1** (social learning with single-loop adaptation), each set to 20 households. The **homog-behav-types?** parameter set to True ensures each type is evaluated in uniform groups. The original RAGE model behavioral types are inactive in this extension: **number-SAT**, **number-TRAD**, **number-MAX**, and **number-random** are all set to 0. The default reference **behavioral-type** is set to E-RO.

*Grazing Management Practices:*
Formal pasture resting is disabled with **resting?** set to False. The threshold for initiating rest periods, **resting-threshold**, ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0.1. The **intrinsic-preference** for livestock relative to other values ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0. Similarly, **social-susceptibility** to neighbor influence ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0, indicating decisions rely on other factors.

*Original Model Parameters:*
Several parameters from the foundational RAGE model are retained. The **satisficing-threshold** for satisfied decision-making ranges from 0 to 100 and is set at 0. The **satisficing-trials** parameter, ranging from 0 to 100, is set at 21. These control how the original satisficer type would search for acceptable solutions, though this type is not active in the current setup.

*Ecological Conditions:*
Rainfall is applied uniformly across the landscape with **global-rain?** set to True. The average rainfall **rain-mean** is set at 200 units annually. Year-to-year variation in precipitation is controlled by **rain-std** set at 100, indicating significant variability in annual rainfall totals. Vegetation growth responds through two rates: **gr1** set at 0.5 and **gr2** set at 0.1. The parameter **w** from the original vegetation model is set at 0.8, influencing how pastures recover from grazing. Livestock reproduction is not active in this extension, with **b** set to 0.

*Learning and Risk Mechanisms:*
The extension module is activated with **extension-model?** set to True. Uncertainty handling is engaged with **risk-mode?** set to True. Each agent begins with an initial risk disposition **risk-att-init** set at 0.5, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance. The ability to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False, confining agents to single-loop learning within their assigned type.

The average initial risk attitude among social learning agents maintains a constant value of 0.0074 across all time steps from year 0 through year 100, exhibiting no temporal variation throughout the simulation. This stability reflects the model's treatment of initial risk disposition as a fixed parameter established at initialization for the 20 social learning households, representing their baseline propensity to deviate from recommended livestock levels. 

For decision makers, this constancy indicates that the simulation provides a controlled comparison of learning strategies where observed differences in grazing outcomes can be attributed to the learning mechanisms themselves rather than to changes in initial risk attitudes, ensuring that the baseline behavioral disposition remains uniform when evaluating the effectiveness of social learning in pasture management.

The mean risk disposition across the farming population follows a distinct trajectory characterized by an initial rapid increase, a mid-simulation peak, and subsequent moderation toward a stable equilibrium. Beginning at minimal values near 0.007 during the third year, the aggregate risk attitude escalates sharply through the first two decades, achieving a maximum of approximately 0.381 around year 33. This represents a substantial amplification from initial conditions, indicating that households collectively adopt more pronounced risk-taking or uncertainty-accepting behaviors as they gain initial experience with private pasture dynamics.

Following this apex, the system enters a prolonged decline spanning roughly from year 33 to year 65, with values decreasing by nearly one-third to approximately 0.272 around year 48 and continuing downward to stabilize near 0.191 by year 65. The descent gradually attenuates thereafter, giving way to a stabilization phase in the final third of the simulation where the series exhibits no statistically significant directional trend, ultimately settling near 0.191 at year 100.

This pattern suggests three distinct temporal regimes: an exploratory phase of escalating risk acceptance, a correction phase of risk moderation, and a mature phase of stable risk disposition. For decision makers, these dynamics indicate that farmer risk attitudes are adaptive rather than fixed, initially trending toward aggressive utilization of resources before experiential feedback induces conservative adjustments. The approximately thirty-year duration of the correction phase highlights the temporal lag inherent in learning-based behavioral adaptation, suggesting that policy support should anticipate sustained periods of heightened risk exposure during system transitions while recognizing that long-run stabilization at moderate risk levels may emerge without external intervention.

The system exhibits a pronounced U-shaped trajectory in vegetation condition over the century-long simulation. Initially, reserve biomass begins at approximately 90,000 units and undergoes a steep decline during the first decade, with the rate of decrease moderating slightly around year 8 before continuing downward through year 21. By year 26, the metric reaches its minimum value of approximately 84,114 units, representing the nadir of pasture degradation.

Following this low point, the system enters a transitional phase between years 22 and 32 where biomass stabilizes without significant directional trend, creating a plateau near the minimum values. This stabilization period gives way to a sustained recovery beginning around year 33, marked by initially rapid increases in biomass accumulation. The most vigorous growth occurs between years 44 and 54, after which the rate of increase gradually decelerates through subsequent decades while maintaining a consistent upward trajectory.

By year 48, an inflection point signals the transition from rapid recovery to more gradual growth, with a structural change point occurring at year 50 that divides the series into distinct degradation and recovery phases. The system ultimately reaches approximately 121,073 units by year 100, exceeding initial conditions by roughly 34%.

Superimposed on these long-term trends, the vegetation condition oscillates with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 years, suggesting cyclical fluctuations that likely reflect interactions between high rainfall variability (with standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200) and the adaptive responses of the 60 households employing diverse learning strategies.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The initial 25-year decline indicates that even with experiential and social learning mechanisms active, private pastoral systems may experience significant vegetation degradation before effective adaptation emerges. The subsequent robust recovery demonstrates that stationary households managing individual parcels can achieve substantial biomass improvements over multi-decadal timescales, ultimately surpassing initial carrying capacity. However, the 49-year oscillation cycle suggests that rainfall variability creates persistent boom-bust dynamics that learning strategies cannot fully eliminate, indicating a need for supplementary risk management tools beyond adaptive stocking decisions. The concentration of rapid recovery between years 33-54 highlights a critical window where management interventions may accelerate system restoration.

The simulated grazing system exhibits a distinct pattern of initial resource decline followed by sustained ecological recovery over the century-long timeframe. At initiation, green biomass stands at approximately 45,000 units across the landscape. During the first decade, biomass remains relatively stable with minor fluctuations, but enters a significant decline between years 11 and 21, reaching a system-wide minimum of roughly 42,057 units by year 27. This trough represents a 6.5% reduction from initial conditions and marks the critical turning point in pasture condition.  

Following this nadir, the system demonstrates marked resilience. Between years 33 and 50, biomass increases rapidly with an inflection point around year 49, transitioning from steep recovery to more gradual accumulation. By year 50, the metric reaches approximately 55,000 units, surpassing initial conditions. The growth continues throughout the remaining half-century, though at progressively decelerating rates—from steep annual gains in the middle period to modest increments in the final decades—ultimately reaching approximately 60,535 units by year 100, representing a 34% net increase from the starting value.  

This trajectory suggests that the combined learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning from neighbors—induce a characteristic response lag of roughly three decades before effective grazing pressure adjustment manifests in vegetation recovery. The initial decline likely reflects the collective impact of the initial stocking rate of 90 animals per household across 60 private parcels, while the subsequent recovery indicates that farmers successfully reduce herd sizes or adjust grazing patterns in response to observed pasture degradation. The decelerating growth in later years implies the system is converging toward a new equilibrium where adaptive stocking decisions balance livestock needs with vegetation regeneration rates under variable rainfall conditions.  

**Implications for Decision Makers**  

The 27-year lag between initial stocking and minimum biomass reveals a critical vulnerability window where pasture degradation may appear manageable before sudden ecological thresholds are crossed. Policy interventions should anticipate this delayed feedback and support early monitoring systems rather than reactive measures. The eventual recovery demonstrates that decentralized learning—through personal experience and neighbor observation—can achieve sustainable outcomes without centralized coordination, provided farmers have sufficient time to adapt. However, the prolonged recovery period suggests that initial stocking decisions carry long-term consequences; conservative initial limits may prevent the degradation phase entirely while still allowing gradual build-up as farmers learn optimal carrying capacities for their specific parcels.

The aggregate livestock count across all households begins at 90 animals per household and undergoes a pronounced three-phase trajectory over the century-long simulation. During the first two decades, a steep and statistically significant decline occurs, with aggregate livestock dropping rapidly from the initial 90 animals to approximately 60 animals by year 20. This initial phase reflects the immediate pressure exerted on vegetation by the starting herd sizes under variable rainfall conditions.

Following this sharp reduction, the decline continues but at a markedly decelerating rate between years 20 and 37, reaching a system-wide minimum of approximately 41 animals per household around year 37. This period represents the nadir of the adjustment process, where the interaction between pasture degradation feedback and farmer decision-making drives stock numbers to their lowest point. Between years 37 and 55, the system enters a stabilization phase with no clear directional trend, oscillating around this minimum level as households struggle to find viable stocking rates amid ecological stress.

A significant inflection occurs around year 46, after which the trajectory gradually shifts. From year 55 onward, the system enters a prolonged recovery phase characterized by slow but consistent increases in aggregate livestock numbers. This upward trend continues through the remainder of the simulation, though the rate of increase diminishes over time, ending at approximately 47.5 animals per household by year 100. Notably, this recovery never returns to initial levels, settling into a new equilibrium roughly 47% below starting values.

The 49-year oscillation cycle evident throughout suggests that farmer learning behaviors, constrained to single-loop adaptation within fixed strategy types, generate cyclical adjustments as households respond to lagged ecological feedback and neighbor observations within their knowledge radius.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The data suggests that initial stocking rates of 90 animals per household substantially exceed sustainable carrying capacity under the modeled rainfall variability, necessitating a painful adjustment period of nearly 40 years before stabilization. Decision makers should anticipate that transitions to sustainable management require decades rather than years, with an intermediate crisis period where livelihoods are most vulnerable. The eventual recovery demonstrates that learning-enabled adaptation can restore some productivity, but long-term sustainable stocking levels appear to be roughly half initial allocations, indicating the critical importance of conservative initial stocking rates or rapid destocking mechanisms to avoid extended periods of minimal livestock holdings.

The average number of livestock destocked across households begins at zero but exhibits an immediate sharp increase, reaching a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals per household by year 3. This initial spike represents the most severe destocking event within the simulation timeframe. Following this peak, the system enters a pronounced adjustment phase characterized by a statistically significant declining trend through approximately year 30, with destocking values decreasing from above 3.0 to below 1.0 animals during this period.

Between years 30 and 65, destocking stabilizes at relatively low levels, fluctuating between approximately 0.3 and 1.5 animals without significant directional trend. This middle period demonstrates oscillatory behavior with diminishing amplitude compared to the initial decade. A secondary pattern emerges around years 66 through 76, where destocking exhibits a modest but significant increasing trend before resuming decline.

By the conclusion of the 100-year simulation, destocking rates converge to approximately 0.31 animals per household, substantially below the long-term mean of 0.96 and approaching the median value of 0.31. The system exhibits a dominant oscillatory cycle of approximately 49 years, suggesting underlying long-term periodicity in resource constraints or household adaptation patterns.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The pronounced destocking peak during the initial three years indicates that households face acute resource pressure immediately upon establishing their initial stocking rates of 90 animals, particularly given the high rainfall variability (coefficient of variation of 50%) and lack of prior local knowledge. This suggests that early intervention mechanisms, such as initial stocking guidance or emergency fodder reserves during establishment phases, could reduce early livelihood shocks.

The consistent decline in destocking through the first three decades demonstrates that experiential and social learning strategies effectively enable households to calibrate livestock numbers to local carrying capacity, even under significant climatic uncertainty. However, the persistent low-level destocking throughout the simulation (never reaching zero) and the late-period increase around years 66-76 indicate that long-term climatic variability continues to generate resource shortfalls even after decades of adaptation. The 49-year oscillation cycle implies that decision makers should plan for multi-decadal variability in forage availability rather than expecting stationary conditions, potentially requiring institutional memory or long-term monitoring systems to capture these slow dynamics.

The distribution of healthy livestock among households begins in a state of perfect equality, with the inequality measure effectively at zero. This represents a gradual but steady divergence in livestock holdings across the farming population.

The progression is not uniform, exhibiting several distinct phases of adjustment. Following initialization, the first notable shift occurs approximately one decade into the simulation, with subsequent inflections in the rate of increase occurring around years 13 to 15, 20, 25, 37, and 57. These transitions suggest periods where the differential effectiveness of the three learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—became more pronounced, causing household fortunes to diverge at varying rates.

A significant structural change emerges at the temporal midpoint around year 51, followed by another shift near year 71, marking the transition into a late-phase regime characterized by oscillatory dynamics. During the final two decades, particularly between years 78 and 93, the inequality measure exhibits periodic fluctuations superimposed upon the underlying growth trend. These oscillations, with a dominant cycle of approximately 49 years, suggest that households' adaptive responses to environmental variability and social observation generated temporary convergences and divergences in livestock holdings.

Throughout the simulation, the average inequality level remains modest at approximately 0.03, with the median value of 0.02 indicating that for the majority of the time horizon, the distribution remained relatively equitable despite the persistent upward trend. The terminal value of roughly 0.07, while representing a substantial increase from the initial condition, still indicates a fairly balanced distribution of productive animals across the farming community, suggesting that even with distinct learning approaches and significant rainfall variability, the system avoids extreme concentration of livestock assets.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The emergence of mild but persistent inequality over time suggests that extension programs promoting specific learning strategies may inadvertently create differential advantages among farmers. The oscillatory patterns in later years indicate that inequality is not purely cumulative but responds dynamically to environmental feedback and social observation. Policymakers should recognize that while learning-based approaches improve individual outcomes, they may also generate modest stratification; however, the low terminal inequality level suggests these disparities remain manageable and do not lead to severe asset concentration over century-scale time horizons. The mid-simulation structural shifts imply that inequality dynamics may have threshold effects that require monitoring at decadal scales.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The saturation of social learning adoption at approximately half the farming community suggests inherent limits to spontaneous diffusion, possibly due to heterogeneous risk dispositions or spatial isolation that constrain observational learning beyond the immediate knowledge radius. Decision makers should recognize that the initial decade offers a critical window for engagement, during which receptive households rapidly adopt new strategies, whereas the remaining households may require targeted interventions to overcome apparent barriers to adoption. The pronounced cyclicality implies that social learning is utilized strategically—likely intensifying during periods of environmental stress or pasture degradation—rather than representing a constant behavioral mode. Consequently, support programs should anticipate temporal variation in participation rates and consider timing outreach efforts to coincide with low-condition periods when farmers are most receptive to neighbor observations. Finally, the coexistence of multiple learning strategies, with approximately 47 percent of households persistently relying on individualistic rule-of-thumb or experiential approaches, suggests that policy frameworks should maintain diverse support systems rather than assuming universal adoption of collective knowledge-sharing practices.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The accumulation of nearly 100 animals destocked per household over the century—exceeding the initial herd size—indicates substantial livestock turnover as households actively manage stocking levels in response to highly variable rainfall conditions. The pronounced initial liquidation during the first 25 years suggests a necessary adaptation phase where farmers reduce herds to align private pasture carrying capacity with actual resource availability. The continued, albeit slower, accumulation throughout subsequent decades, coupled with multi-decadal oscillation cycles, demonstrates that stationary households operating under uncertainty and incomplete information do not converge on stable equilibrium herd sizes. Instead, they maintain flexibility through persistent destocking activity, indicating that long-term grazing management requires institutional and financial mechanisms capable of supporting ongoing livestock marketing rather than expecting a one-time adjustment to optimal stocking rates.
