The male megaherbivore population exhibits a deterministic collapse from an initial abundance of 125 individuals to complete extinction (0 individuals) by approximately tick 2284, equivalent to roughly 6.25 years in the model's temporal framework. The trajectory is characterized by a steep monotonic decline throughout the observation period, with the population mean averaging 21.03 males across all time steps but exhibiting a highly right-skewed distribution with a median of only 1.35, indicating that the majority of the temporal duration is spent at very low population levels following the initial collapse.

The most rapid population reduction occurs within the first 500 ticks (approximately 1.4 years), during which the abundance drops precipitously from 125 to below 20 individuals across all simulation replicates. By tick 1000 (approximately 2.7 years), the population has declined to near-zero levels in most replicates, with only sporadic small peaks occurring thereafter, such as temporary increases to approximately 10.5 individuals around tick 730 and 8.8 individuals around tick 709, representing brief demographic fluctuations during the terminal decline phase. After tick 1500 (approximately 4.1 years), the population remains at or near zero with minimal oscillation until final extinction is achieved.

The absence of population recovery or stabilization, despite the potential for reproduction and the 0.8 seasonality setting with grass regrowth, suggests that hunting mortality consistently exceeds the maximum sustainable yield for males under these parameter configurations.

For decision makers examining megafaunal extinction hypotheses, these results suggest that moderate hunter densities (20 individuals in this spatially explicit context) can drive rapid male megafaunal extinction within approximately 6 years when prey species exhibit the specified life history traits and processing costs. This implies that hunting pressure need not be extreme to induce extinction when coupled with specific demographic constraints, and that male-biased hunting (whether due to higher food value, lower processing costs, or behavioral vulnerability) can eliminate the male cohort well before the total population collapses, potentially accelerating overall population decline through Allee effects or reproductive limitation.
