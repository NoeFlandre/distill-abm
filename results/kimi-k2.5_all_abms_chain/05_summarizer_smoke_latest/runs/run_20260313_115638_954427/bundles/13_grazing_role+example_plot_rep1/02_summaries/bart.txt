The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and farmers' livelihoods. The observed values range from approximately -0.11 to +0.18, distributed across approximately 20 distinct constant levels. These levels represent heterogeneous baseline risk dispositions among the social learning agents regarding deviations from recommended livestock numbers. The mean reserve biomass begins at approximately 90,000 units across all simulation runs. The mean green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units, showing a brief initial fluctuation before entering a pronounced decline phase. This pattern indicates that different learning strategies lead to divergent long-term outcomes in pasture health rather than convergence to a single landscape-scale equilibrium. The most successful strategies appear to achieve biomass levels roughly 40% higher than the minimum levels observed during the degradation phase. The count of households engaging in social learning begins at zero across all simulation runs. During the initial 20 time steps, every trajectory exhibits rapid accumulation, rising sharply to reach values between approximately 60 and 80. The trajectories settling near zero indicate successful convergence to sustainable herd sizes that rarely necessitate liquidation. Approximately one-third of simulation runs settle between 23 and 28 non-learning households. Lower levels either persist or continue decreasing toward zero. This mid-simulation adjustment likely reflects selective pressure from ecological dynamics, where learning-by-doing and social learning strategies may outperform the static E-RO approach in variable environments.
