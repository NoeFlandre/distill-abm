The cumulative destocking of livestock exhibits a monotonically increasing trajectory over the 100-timestep simulation period, beginning at zero and reaching approximately 97 animals per household by the conclusion. The accumulation follows a markedly decelerating pattern characterized by three distinct phases of growth intensity.

During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 25 timesteps, the system experiences rapid destocking with the steepest rates of accumulation observed in the first decade. By timestep 25, households have cumulatively destocked roughly two-thirds of the final total observed at timestep 100. This suggests an immediate and substantial reduction in herd sizes as heterogeneous agents—including those employing learning-by-doing (E-LBD) and social learning (E-RO-SL1) alongside baseline behaviors (E-RO)—adapt to initial pasture conditions and high rainfall variability within the stationary, private-property grazing context.

A transition occurs around timestep 25, marked by a significant reduction in the rate of destocking accumulation. Between timesteps 25 and 60, the cumulative count continues to rise but at a substantially moderated pace. Within this interval, subtle variations in the accumulation rate occur around timesteps 45 and 53-57, indicating minor behavioral adjustments as the mixed population responds to ecological feedbacks and observed neighbor performance without strategy switching.

Following timestep 60, the system enters a phase of slow, steady accumulation with the rate stabilizing at approximately 0.31 animals per timestep through the remainder of the simulation. Despite the presence of learning mechanisms and heterogeneity in household risk attitudes (r-parameters), the cumulative destocking approaches nearly 97 animals per household by timestep 100, with a median accumulation of approximately 81 animals across the full time series.

For decision makers, the sustained upward trend throughout the entire simulation horizon indicates that under high climatic variability and private property regimes, livestock populations face continuous destocking pressure rather than achieving long-term equilibrium without herd reduction. The early intense destocking phase highlights the critical initial period where households must make significant livestock adjustments to avoid pasture degradation. The eventual stabilization of destocking rates, while still positive, suggests that learning mechanisms facilitate adaptation that slows—but does not halt—livestock reduction, implying that supplementary policy interventions may be necessary to prevent gradual herd depletion over multi-decadal timeframes in smallholder grazing systems.
