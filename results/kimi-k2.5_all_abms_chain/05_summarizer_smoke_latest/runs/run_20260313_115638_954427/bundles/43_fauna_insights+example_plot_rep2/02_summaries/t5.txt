The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the simulation period from initialization through approximately step 2200. This static behavior reflects the parameter configuration where forager agents are immortal and do not reproduce, preventing both mortality from energy depletion and recruitment through population growth. However, the hunting pressure remains temporally invariant and deterministic, providing a fixed predation intensity of 20 active hunters. Trajectories reach saturation as early as step 1000, while others continue accumulating sporadic kills until approximately step 2000. The final extraction totals approach the theoretical maximum initial male population size, indicating effective extirpation within 3 to 5.5 years. At approximately step 750, discrete reproductive events begin to occur, triggering stepwise increases in the cumulative birth count. A subset of runs maintains reproductive activity longer, with births continuing until steps 1800 to 2000, suggesting variable persistence times for reproductive-age females. The number of male animals begins at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation runs. The population undergoes a deterministic decline toward extinction throughout the experimental period. Between steps 500 and 1000, the remaining populations continue to decrease at a slower rate, with final extinction occurring at various points between steps 1000 and 2200. Trajectories approach extinction (0–5 animals) by step 800. A subset demonstrates prolonged persistence, fluctuating between 5 and 40 animals until approximately step 1000. This variation likely stems from stochastic differences in herd movement patterns, temporary spatial refugia, and demographic stochaasticity. Most runs lose 50-70% of their initial energy within the first 200 time steps. The majority of simulation runs have declined to below 2,000 energy units. Some trajectories exhibit extremely rapid collapse, reaching near-zero energy levels by step 300-400. Deterministic trend toward extinction indicates sustainable coexistence is unlikely without substantial parameter modification or regulatory intervention. The percentage of vegetated landscape patches begins at approximately 33% and undergoes a rapid decline to roughly 32% within the first 100 time steps as initial grass stocks are depleted by prey consumption.
