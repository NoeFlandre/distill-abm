The cumulative number of livestock destocked begins at zero across all simulation runs and exhibits a monotonically increasing trajectory throughout the century-long timeframe. By year 100, the population-level average reaches approximately 97 animals destocked per household, aggregated across the 60-farmer communities, though individual simulation runs display substantial heterogeneity with terminal values ranging from roughly 58 to 173 animals. This spread reflects the divergent long-term outcomes emerging from the three distinct learning strategies operating under identical initial conditions of 90 livestock per household and uniform rainfall variability.

The initial 25-year period witnesses the most rapid accumulation, with destocking rates declining from approximately 5 animals annually to under 1.5 animals per year. This steep early trajectory indicates immediate adjustment pressure as households confront the carrying capacity constraints of their private pastures under stochastic rainfall conditions averaging 200 units annually with high variance. The first change point at year 25 marks a transition from reactive destocking to strategy-stabilized management, where experiential rule-of-thumb households may approach equilibrium stocking levels while learning-by-doing and social learning households continue adaptive adjustments.

Between years 25 and 60, the system exhibits multiple inflection dynamics around years 45, 47, 53, and 56, corresponding to continued but decelerating destocking at rates falling from 0.69 to 0.32 animals annually. During this phase, cumulative destock totals diverge across the population, ranging from approximately 40 to 80 animals by year 25 and widening to 70–115 by year 60, as the fixed risk dispositions interact with local pasture conditions and neighbor observations within the knowledge radius of one patch. The oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of 49 years manifests in clustering of destocking activity around years 22–26, 49–61, and 79–80, potentially corresponding to vegetation recovery cycles governed by growth parameters or rainfall anomalies that periodically trigger renewed destocking waves.

After year 60, the second change point initiates a mature phase characterized by minimal marginal destocking rates of approximately 0.3 animals annually. Many trajectories flatten substantially during this final quarter-century, indicating households that have achieved stable stocking equilibria, while others maintain gentle positive slopes suggesting persistent vulnerability to ecological variability or incomplete learning convergence. The median household accumulates approximately 81 destocked animals by year 100, with the population showing considerable variance (standard deviation 20.3) around the mean of 75.5, reflecting the enduring behavioral heterogeneity between baseline experiential learning, individual learning-by-doing, and socially-informed adaptation strategies under conditions of uncertainty and private property management.
