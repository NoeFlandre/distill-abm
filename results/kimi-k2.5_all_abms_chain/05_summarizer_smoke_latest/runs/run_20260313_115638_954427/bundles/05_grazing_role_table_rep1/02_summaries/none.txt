**Context and Goals**

This agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Unlike the original RAGE model which examined common property scenarios where herders move livestock across shared rangelands, this extension focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods.

The simulation tracks the interaction between vegetation condition and household decisions about livestock numbers over time. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors.

The central purpose is understanding how these different ways of adapting to changing conditions influence pasture health, herd sizes, and the persistence of households. The model particularly examines how farmers handle uncertainty and incomplete information when making stocking decisions, represented through individual risk dispositions that affect how they perceive and react to environmental feedback.

**Key Parameters**

*Population and Spatial Setup:*
The **number-households** parameter establishes how many farming families occupy the landscape, which can range from 1 to 100 families, with this study using 60 households. Each household begins with **livestock-init** set at 90 animals. The simulation runs for **timesteps** set at 100 years. Farmers begin the simulation positioned on their designated land parcels, with **start-on-homepatch?** set to True. Each farmer can observe conditions within a **knowledge-radius** that ranges from 0 to 5 patches, set here at 1 patch distance.

*Behavioral Type Configuration:*
The study examines three specific learning strategies with equal representation: **number-E-RO** (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb), **number-E-LBD** (learning-by-doing), and **number-E-RO-SL1** (social learning with single-loop adaptation), each set to 20 households. The **homog-behav-types?** parameter set to True ensures each type is evaluated in uniform groups. The original RAGE model behavioral types are inactive in this extension: **number-SAT**, **number-TRAD**, **number-MAX**, and **number-random** are all set to 0. The default reference **behavioral-type** is set to E-RO.

*Grazing Management Practices:*
Formal pasture resting is disabled with **resting?** set to False. The threshold for initiating rest periods, **resting-threshold**, ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0.1. The **intrinsic-preference** for livestock relative to other values ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0. Similarly, **social-susceptibility** to neighbor influence ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0, indicating decisions rely on other factors.

*Original Model Parameters:*
Several parameters from the foundational RAGE model are retained. The **satisficing-threshold** for satisfied decision-making ranges from 0 to 100 and is set at 0. The **satisficing-trials** parameter, ranging from 0 to 100, is set at 21. These control how the original satisficer type would search for acceptable solutions, though this type is not active in the current setup.

*Ecological Conditions:*
Rainfall is applied uniformly across the landscape with **global-rain?** set to True. The average rainfall **rain-mean** is set at 200 units annually. Year-to-year variation in precipitation is controlled by **rain-std** set at 100, indicating significant variability in annual rainfall totals. Vegetation growth responds through two rates: **gr1** set at 0.5 and **gr2** set at 0.1. The parameter **w** from the original vegetation model is set at 0.8, influencing how pastures recover from grazing. Livestock reproduction is not active in this extension, with **b** set to 0.

*Learning and Risk Mechanisms:*
The extension module is activated with **extension-model?** set to True. Uncertainty handling is engaged with **risk-mode?** set to True. Each agent begins with an initial risk disposition **risk-att-init** set at 0.5, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance. The ability to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False, confining agents to single-loop learning within their assigned type.

The average initial risk attitude among social learning households maintains a constant value of 0.0074 from the initial time step through year 100. This fixed value shows no deviation across the entire simulation duration, with the metric beginning at 0.0074 and ending at 0.0074 without experiencing any intermediate variation, trends, or oscillations. The absence of temporal dynamics indicates that this metric captures a static baseline risk disposition assigned to agents at initialization, representing an inherent, unchanging characteristic of the social learning behavioral type rather than a state variable that responds to accumulated experience or changing environmental conditions.

The average household risk disposition initiates the simulation at a negligible value of approximately 0.007. During the first two decades, this metric undergoes a pronounced and statistically significant increase, rising steeply to roughly 0.36 by year 21. This ascending trajectory continues with high-frequency oscillations, reaching a maximum value of approximately 0.38 around year 33. Throughout the volatile mid-simulation phase spanning approximately years 25 to 37, the metric fluctuates between 0.35 and 0.38, indicating persistent instability in collective uncertainty perception as households respond to environmental feedback under the significant rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100) driving the ecological dynamics.

Following this peak period, the trend reverses sharply into a prolonged decline from year 37 through approximately year 65. Over this interval, the average risk disposition decreases significantly from its maximum values near 0.38 down to roughly 0.19. This downward movement stabilizes after year 65, with the metric converging to approximately 0.191 by the conclusion of the 100-year simulation. The final 35 years exhibit no statistically significant directional trend, suggesting that the three distinct learning strategy types—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning with single-loop adaptation—collectively settle into an equilibrium state of moderate risk disposition around 0.19 following the initial volatile adjustment period.

The overall trajectory is characterized by three distinct phases: an early period of rapidly escalating risk tolerance or uncertainty perception, a mid-simulation plateau of maximum volatility near 0.38, and a late-simulation convergence to a stable moderate level. The mean risk disposition across the entire simulation remains moderate at 0.23, reflecting the balance between the initial near-zero values, the mid-century peak representing maximum deviation from initial risk parameters, and the eventual steady-state adjustment to long-term pasture management under incomplete information.

The system exhibits a distinct pattern of initial degradation followed by sustained recovery over the century-long simulation. Initially, reserve biomass begins at approximately 90,000 units and undergoes a pronounced decline during the first three decades. This downward trajectory is steepest in the earliest years, with significant decreases continuing until reaching a minimum value of approximately 84,115 units at year 26.

This minimum represents a critical inflection point in the system's dynamics. Following year 26, the trajectory reverses entirely, initiating a monotonic increase that persists for the remaining 74 years of the simulation. The recovery phase features distinct sub-periods: an initial acceleration of biomass accumulation from years 33 to 54, followed by a prolonged deceleration phase from years 55 to 100. By the conclusion of the simulation at year 100, reserve biomass reaches approximately 121,073 units, representing a substantial net gain of roughly 34% above initial conditions despite the early depletion.

The trajectory is marked by two notable inflection points in the rate of change. The first occurs around year 8, during the initial decline, while the second emerges near year 48, corresponding to a structural change point at year 50 that divides the recovery phase into distinct acceleration and stabilization periods. The oscillation patterns suggest cyclic variations with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, indicating that fluctuations in biomass condition operate on roughly half the temporal scale of the full simulation.

Interpreting these dynamics through the lens of adaptive grazing management reveals important behavioral patterns. The initial decline to year 26 suggests that stationary households, beginning with 90 animals each and employing diverse learning strategies—including baseline rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—initially struggle to calibrate stocking rates against highly variable rainfall conditions. The absence of livestock reproduction means biomass changes reflect solely the interaction between grazing pressure and vegetation growth dynamics.

The sustained recovery after year 26 indicates that the three learning mechanisms eventually facilitate effective adaptation to local conditions. Households appear to reduce grazing pressure sufficiently to allow vegetation recovery, with the steepest biomass gains occurring between years 44 and 54, suggesting a period of rapidly improving pasture management practices. The gradual slowing of accumulation rates in later decades implies the system is converging toward a sustainable equilibrium state where household stocking decisions balance biomass production with grazing demand, even without formal resting protocols or strategy switching capabilities. The final biomass levels substantially exceed initial conditions, suggesting that effective learning within this private property regime ultimately enhances long-term pasture productivity compared to the starting configuration.

Available green biomass initiates at approximately 45,000 units and follows a U-shaped trajectory with superimposed multi-decadal cyclical variations. During the first four years, the system exhibits minor fluctuation, reaching approximately 45,261 units before entering a pronounced decline. This depletion phase intensifies through year 9, marking an inflection point where the rate of loss begins moderating, and continues until year 27 when biomass reaches its minimum value of approximately 42,057 units.

Following this nadir, the system enters a recovery phase. Between years 27 and 33, the decline stabilizes into a period of no significant trend. Thereafter, a sustained increasing trajectory commences, accelerating notably between years 44 and 54 where the rate of biomass accumulation reaches its maximum velocity. A second inflection point at year 49 signals a transition to more gradual—but persistent—growth rates. By year 50, the system has crossed a critical threshold into a steady accumulation phase.

From year 50 through year 100, biomass increases monotonically across all measurement windows, though at progressively decelerating rates, ultimately reaching approximately 60,535 units. This terminal value represents a substantial increase of roughly 34% above initial conditions and 44% above the minimum recorded at year 27. Throughout the entire series, oscillations with a periodicity of approximately 49 years are evident, particularly during intervals spanning years 10-20 and 41-49.

These dynamics suggest that initial stocking levels of 90 animals per household exceed sustainable carrying capacity under the variable rainfall regime (mean 200 units, standard deviation 100), driving the early biomass depletion. As farmers employ learning-by-doing and social learning strategies—observing neighbors within the one-patch knowledge radius—they progressively reduce herd sizes to alleviate grazing pressure. Because livestock reproduction is inactive, these reductions permanently decrease animal numbers, allowing vegetation to recover and accumulate biomass beyond initial levels once conservative management strategies diffuse through the population. The sustained accumulation throughout the latter 50 years indicates that adaptive learning successfully facilitates stocking decisions that enable long-term pasture rehabilitation despite stochastic environmental conditions.

The simulation begins with each household maintaining 90 livestock. This downward trajectory reflects the system's adjustment from initial stocking levels toward sustainable carrying capacity under variable rainfall conditions and the cognitive constraints of the three learning strategies employed.

Following this minimum, the system enters a transitional period between approximately years 37 and 46 where livestock numbers stabilize without significant directional trends, indicating a temporary equilibrium as households process environmental feedback. From year 46 onward through the conclusion of the simulation at year 100, the trend reverses to a gradual increase, with livestock rising from the trough to approximately 47.5 animals per household. This recovery phase exhibits a pattern of initially moderate growth rates that progressively diminish over time, suggesting convergence toward a new equilibrium below initial stocking levels.

Throughout the simulation, oscillatory dynamics are superimposed on these broader trends, with cyclical adjustments occurring particularly during the initial decline phase (years 5-20) and near the transition point (years 39-42). The final livestock levels represent a substantial reduction from initial conditions—approximately 47% below starting values—yet indicate a modest recovery of roughly 16% from the minimum levels experienced during the adjustment period. The system ultimately stabilizes at levels consistent with sustainable pasture management under the combined influence of experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning strategies, with the mean livestock level across the entire simulation approaching 48.8 animals per household.

The average number of livestock destocked initiates at zero, reflecting initial stocking levels of ninety animals per household prior to environmental feedback. Within the first three years, destocking rates escalate sharply to a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals per household, indicating immediate resource pressure as initial stocking decisions confront variable rainfall conditions (mean 200 units, standard deviation 100). This early period exhibits pronounced volatility, with secondary peaks occurring around years 9 and 12 at values of roughly 3.4 and 2.7 animals respectively, followed by a series of decaying oscillations through approximately year 27.

Throughout the first three decades, the series demonstrates a statistically significant decreasing trend, with the rate of decline attenuating over successive ten-year windows. The slope moderates from steep initial drops to gentler reductions, transitioning around year 30—identified as a primary change point—into a regime of relative stabilization. During this adaptation phase, the amplitude of fluctuations diminishes substantially as households adjust stocking decisions based on experiential and observational learning (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 strategies).

Beyond year 30, destocking rates persist at low levels, oscillating around a median of approximately 0.31 animals, though exhibiting a long-term cyclical pattern with a dominant period of 49 years. This sustained oscillation suggests persistent ecological or climatic forcing despite learning-based adaptation. Notably, between years 66 and 76, the series exhibits a significant increasing trend (slope +0.001), temporarily reversing the long-term decline while remaining below the historical mean of 0.96 animals. By the centennial mark, destocking converges to approximately 0.31 animals per household, representing a reduction of over 95% from the initial peak and indicating that the aggregate learning dynamics eventually buffer households against the most severe resource shortfalls, though minor destocking persists due to continued rainfall variability and the absence of livestock reproduction in the system.

The simulation reveals a consistent long-term trend toward increasing inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock among the 60 households, beginning from a state of near-perfect equality and reaching a Gini coefficient of approximately 0.073 by year 100. This trajectory indicates that despite identical starting conditions—with each household initially holding 90 animals and equivalent risk dispositions—the combination of distinct learning strategies and environmental variability drives households toward divergent outcomes over the century-long period.

The accumulation of inequality proceeds through distinct phases marked by changing rates of divergence. During the initial 50 years, inequality grows gradually from effectively zero, with the pace of divergence characterized by multiple inflection points occurring at years 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, and 37. By the conclusion of the simulation at year 100, the distribution of healthy livestock remains relatively equitable despite the statistically significant upward trend, with the final inequality level of 0.073 indicating that no single learning strategy achieves complete dominance or drives widespread household failure under the modeled conditions of private pasture management and restricted social learning.

The trajectory of households engaged in social learning follows a sigmoid pattern characterized by rapid initial adoption, progressive deceleration, and eventual stabilization. At initialization (year 0), no households exhibit active social learning engagement. During the first two decades (years 0–21), the count increases steeply with a strongly positive trend, indicating rapid uptake of social learning behaviors among the population. This phase transitions around year 21 into a period of decelerating growth (years 22–40), where the rate of new adoption diminishes substantially, as indicated by reduced trend slopes. A change point at year 40 marks the transition to a plateau phase (years 40–63), during which the metric increases minimally until reaching an asymptote of approximately 32 households by year 63. From year 63 through the conclusion of the simulation at year 100, the count remains stable at roughly 32 households, representing a persistent subset of the total population that maintains social learning engagement. Across the full 100-year period, the average number of households engaged in social learning is approximately 27.4, with values ranging from the initial state of 0 to the maximum plateau of 32.

The cumulative number of destocked livestock per household exhibits a monotonic increasing trajectory throughout the 100-year simulation, beginning at zero and accumulating to approximately 97 animals by the final timestep. This progression occurs through three distinct phases demarcated by change points at years 25 and 60, reflecting evolving rates of herd reduction as households adapt to environmental conditions.  

During the initial phase (years 0–25), destocking occurs most rapidly, with accumulation rates exceeding 4.9 animals per year in the first decade before decelerating to roughly 1.5 animals per year by year 21, and further to approximately 0.7 animals per year by year 32. This early rapid reduction reflects sharp initial adjustments from the starting herd size of 90 animals as households respond to pasture degradation and uncertainty under high rainfall variability.  

The middle phase (years 25–60) is characterized by continued moderation in destocking rates, with the accumulation slope stabilizing around 0.32–0.36 animals per year. This period contains multiple inflection points concentrated around years 45, 47, and 53–57, indicating subtle shifts in the curvature of the cumulative trend as learning processes—whether through individual experience or social observation—refine household decision-making.  

In the final phase (years 60–100), the trend stabilizes into a consistent, gradual increase with rates approximating 0.31 animals per year, suggesting that households have reached a steady-state pattern of destocking adjustments.  

Superimposed on this long-term trend is a cyclical oscillation with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, manifesting at indices clustered around years 22–26, 49–61, and 79–80. These periodic fluctuations align with the configured rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200), indicating that destocking decisions pulse in response to recurring drought or recovery cycles, even as the overall cumulative trend continues upward. The sustained accumulation throughout the century—totaling more than the initial herd endowment—implies that households engage in multiple rounds of destocking and potential restocking as they navigate the uncertain productivity of their private pastures through their respective learning strategies.
