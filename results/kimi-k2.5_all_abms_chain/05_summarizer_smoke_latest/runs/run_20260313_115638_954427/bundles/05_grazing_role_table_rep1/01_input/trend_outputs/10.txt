Over the 100-year simulation period, the population of households employing the non-learning E-RO strategy undergoes a characteristic trajectory of initial stability followed by sustained attrition and eventual stabilization. A pronounced downward trend emerges shortly thereafter, persisting with statistical significance from approximately year 11 through year 65. During this period, the number of E-RO households declines steadily from roughly 20 to about 15, with the most rapid decreases occurring between years 20 and 45. This phase corresponds to the period of most intense selective pressure against non-learning strategies.

Following year 65, the rate of attrition attenuates substantially, with the trajectory exhibiting no statistically significant trend through the remainder of the simulation. The population stabilizes around 14.7 households, reaching a minimum of approximately 14.7 at year 92 before concluding at roughly 14.7 households at year 100. This terminal plateau suggests that the remaining non-learning households have reached a persistent equilibrium, having either occupied resilient niches or persisted through favorable environmental stochasticity despite their lack of adaptive capacity.

The stabilization in later decades implies that the selective advantage of learning strategies diminishes once the vulnerable non-learning households have exited, leaving a residual population of E-RO agents that can persist under the prevailing conditions.
