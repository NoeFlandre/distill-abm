Simulations of the extension RAGE model reveal distinct temporal dynamics in household risk attitude (r-parameter) across the 100-step time horizon. The aggregate behavior of the 60-household system initiates at a near-neutral risk attitude of approximately 0.007, remaining stable through the first seven steps before entering a phase of rapid escalation. Between steps 7 and 33, the mean risk attitude increases significantly, reaching a system-wide maximum value of 0.3814 at step 33. This upward trajectory indicates a collective shift toward more conservative uncertainty management during the initial learning period, coinciding with the establishment of social learning networks and early adaptation to pasture conditions.

Following this peak, the aggregate series undergoes a pronounced decline from step 33 through approximately step 65, decreasing to values around 0.19. This middle-phase reduction suggests adaptive adjustments as households refine their risk assessments based on observed outcomes and neighbor performance. From step 65 onward, the system stabilizes with minimal directional trend, maintaining a terminal value of 0.1911 at step 100. Statistical inflection points at steps 39, 46, and 51 demarcate the transition from peak risk aversion to the stabilization phase, while the absence of significant trends in rolling windows after step 66 confirms convergence to steady-state behaviors.

Beneath this aggregate pattern lies substantial household-level heterogeneity. Individual trajectories diverge rapidly between steps 10 and 30, with distinct subpopulations emerging: approximately one-third of households stabilize at high risk-attitude levels (0.6 to 0.8), indicating strong uncertainty aversion; another cluster maintains moderate values (0.2 to 0.4); while a significant minority develops negative risk attitudes (declining to -0.4 by step 60), suggesting risk-seeking behaviors relative to recommended stocking rates. This polarization becomes entrenched after step 60, with individual trajectories exhibiting reduced volatility but persistent stratification through the terminal steps.

The bimodal distribution of terminal risk attitudes—ranging from -0.4 to +0.8—reflects the path-dependent nature of social learning and learning-by-doing processes in private property grazing systems. The initial 33 steps represent a critical bifurcation window where early learning experiences and social network influences determine long-term risk management strategies. The subsequent stabilization suggests that initial heterogeneity in pasture conditions or social learning outcomes becomes locked in, with limited switching between behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, E-RO-SL1) after the mid-simulation transition period.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The emergence of persistent risk-attitude clusters within the same agro-ecological conditions suggests that agricultural extension services should abandon uniform risk-management recommendations in favor of targeted interventions. Households exhibiting negative risk attitudes by step 40 may require monitoring for overstocking behaviors that threaten ecological resilience, while highly risk-averse households may benefit from support to optimize rather than minimize exposure. The critical learning window during the first 35 steps indicates that early intervention programs for new entrants or during system perturbations could have disproportionate long-term impacts on risk management cultures. Finally, the stabilization of attitudes after step 65 suggests that policy assessments should focus on the first three simulation cycles (years), as behavioral trajectories become increasingly resistant to external influence thereafter.
