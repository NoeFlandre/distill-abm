 Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change . Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances . Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores .  The number of active human forager agents remains constant at a value of 20 throughout the entire temporal span of the simulations, beginning at step 0 and continuing uninterrupted through the final recorded step of approximately 2200 . The cumulative number of male ungulates killed by human foragers exhibits a consistent pattern of rapid initial accumulation followed by deceleration and eventual stabilization across all simulation runs .  The cumulative mortality of postpartum females across replicate simulations exhibits a saturation pattern characterized by rapid initial accumulation followed by asymptotic stabilization . The maximum cumulative kills of approximately 120-125 individuals represents roughly 48-50% of the initial ungulate population, though accounting for natural mortality, starvation, and the sex-specific hunting pressure .  Several replicates demonstrate continued but sporadic accumulation of births at discrete intervals, with cumulative births reaching terminal values between 5 and 10 by steps 1500 to 2000 . Surviving females in these populations completed multiple reproductive cycles despite pressures from human hunting and seasonal resource variability . This limited reproductive output represents fewer than 10 total additions to the population from an initial cohort of 250 animals over nearly 2000 timesteps .  The simulations consistently begin with a total prey population of 250 individuals . The per-capita mortality rate from hunting exceeds the maximum reproductive rate permitted by the 730-tick interbirth interval . The variation in persistence times reflects spatial positioning of hunters and prey on the 211×211 landscape .  Some populations experience near-total collapse, approaching zero energy . Others maintain residual energy pools fluctuating between 500 and 2,000 units, while others approach zero . This variability suggests stochastic differences in herd survival strategies and resource acquisition success despite identical parameterization across runs .  Despite strong seasonal forcing (seasonality = 0.8) and intense initial grazing pressure, the rapid vegetation regrowth enables the system to stabilize near the initial 33% cover . Some trajectories display minor fluctuations and step-like discontinuities while others maintain more constant values .
