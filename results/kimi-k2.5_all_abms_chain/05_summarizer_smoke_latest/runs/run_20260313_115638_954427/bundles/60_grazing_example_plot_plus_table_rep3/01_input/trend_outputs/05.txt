The average total livestock holdings per household, including destocked animals, begins at 90 animals. Over the first 37 time steps, this value declines rapidly and significantly, reaching a minimum of approximately 41 animals. This steep initial decrease reflects the adjustment period as households adapt to private property constraints and ecological limits, with high initial stocking rates leading to forced destocking as pastures face pressure.

Between approximately steps 33 and 43, the decline stabilizes, showing no significant trend as the system reaches a temporary equilibrium at the minimum level. This plateau represents the nadir of the crisis where livestock numbers cannot sustain further reduction without compromising household viability.

From step 44 onward, a recovery phase initiates and continues through the end of the simulation, with livestock holdings increasing significantly through step 100. The rate of increase is steep initially (steps 44-55) and then gradually slows, though remaining positive throughout the later period. By the conclusion at step 100, the average reaches approximately 47.5 animals, though outcomes vary substantially across simulation runs, with final values ranging from roughly 41 to 62 animals.

This divergence in final outcomes reflects the heterogeneous performance of different behavioral types. The recovery trajectory demonstrates that learning capabilities enable some households to rebuild herds beyond the minimum crisis level, while baseline behaviors without learning remain constrained at lower stocking rates. The sustained variance across runs indicates that initial conditions and stochastic factors create persistent differences in household resilience, with learning-enabled agents achieving higher long-term livestock holdings compared to non-learning baseline behaviors.
