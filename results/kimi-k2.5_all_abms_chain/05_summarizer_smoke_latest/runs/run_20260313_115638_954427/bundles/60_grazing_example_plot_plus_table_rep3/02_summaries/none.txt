This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. These operate through a yearly sequence where green biomass updates, households with E-RO-SL1 types update their r-parameters, households update behavioral strategies (if switching is enabled), make livestock placement decisions, livestock feeds on biomass, households observe neighbor performance, reserve biomass updates, and households observe and remember reserve biomass states.

The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. It seeks to evaluate how learning capabilities affect household resilience and pasture conditions in private property grazing systems. Specific objectives include analyzing the impact of social learning double-loop processes through strategy switching in heterogeneous populations, observing the diffusion and competition between different learning behaviors in mixed populations, and assessing how demographic settings and initial herd sizes influence system outcomes. The model allows for the evaluation of outcomes across social, ecological, and economic dimensions including average herd size, pasture biomass condition, and surviving number of households.

Following the peak at step 33, the deviation undergoes a significant decline through step 65, decreasing to approximately 0.19. From step 65 through the end of the simulation at step 100, the metric stabilizes around 0.19 with no statistically significant trend, suggesting that the system has reached a steady state of heterogeneous risk attitudes. The dominant oscillation period of 49 time steps suggests that the social-ecological system exhibits cyclic adjustment patterns, possibly corresponding to the yearly vegetation dynamics and learning cycles, where agents periodically reassess strategies in response to biomass conditions. The stabilization of the aggregate mean at approximately 0.23, with a median of 0.20, indicates that across the population, agents settle into persistently diverse but bounded deviations from optimal stocking densities, reflecting the balance between exploitation and learning in private property grazing systems.

Following the trough at step 27, the system undergoes a sustained and statistically significant recovery. Between steps 33 and 54, biomass increases at an accelerating rate, with the most rapid accumulation occurring during the interval from step 44 to 54. The expansion continues through the remainder of the simulation, though at progressively decelerating rates from steps 55 to 100. This heterogeneity in outcomes suggests that while early system behavior is largely determined by initial stocking densities and uniform vegetation parameters, long-term pasture productivity becomes increasingly influenced by the specific adaptive decisions and heterogeneous learning processes—including learning-by-doing and social learning of risk attitudes—employed by the mixed population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types.

The overall pattern of initial degradation followed by robust recovery exceeding baseline conditions indicates that the combination of vegetation dynamics and adaptive household behaviors eventually enables sustainable biomass management, allowing pastures to recover and stabilize at higher productivity levels despite continuous grazing activity.

Across the population of 60 households in the private property grazing system, final engagement levels vary considerably among simulation runs, ranging from approximately 18 to 54 households. This high variance (standard deviation of 8.92) indicates path-dependent diffusion trajectories, with some simulations achieving near-universal adoption of social learning behaviors while others stabilize with only partial penetration. The step-wise nature of individual adoption trajectories suggests discrete behavioral switching events, consistent with social contagion mechanisms where households adopt social learning strategies upon observing neighbor performance within the defined knowledge radius.

The rapid initial uptake reflects effective social transmission among the heterogeneous population containing baseline, learning-by-doing, and social learning-capable agents. The subsequent slowing and eventual plateau around timestep 63, maintaining approximately 32 engaged households (roughly 53% of the population), indicates that social learning diffuses to about half the households on average, though stochastic variation leads to divergent outcomes across runs ranging from 30% to 90% adoption rates. The persistence of values near 32 from timestep 63 to 100 suggests the system reaches a stable equilibrium where remaining households maintain their original behavioral types despite ongoing social learning opportunities.
