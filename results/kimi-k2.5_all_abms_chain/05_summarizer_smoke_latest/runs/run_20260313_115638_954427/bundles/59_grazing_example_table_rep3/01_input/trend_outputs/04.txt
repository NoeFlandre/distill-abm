The average available green biomass initiates at 45,000 units and remains relatively stable through the first ten time steps, exhibiting no significant directional trend. Subsequently, the system experiences a pronounced decline between years 11 and 21, reaching a minimum value of approximately 42,057 units around year 27. This initial depletion likely corresponds to the immediate grazing pressure exerted by the initial livestock populations across the private property patches, temporarily exceeding the vegetation's regenerative capacity before household adaptation strategies take effect. The biomass stabilizes at this depressed level through approximately year 32, indicating a temporary equilibrium where grazing pressure matches limited grass growth.  

A reversal commences around year 33, initiating a sustained recovery that persists throughout the remainder of the 100-year sequence. The growth accelerates significantly between years 33 and 54, characterized by a steep positive slope, suggesting that the interaction between learning-by-doing and social learning processes enables households to refine their grazing strategies effectively. During this phase, the heterogeneity in risk attitudes among the mixed behavioral types appears to facilitate exploratory management approaches that enhance pasture recovery beyond initial levels.  

Following year 54, the biomass continues to increase but at progressively diminishing rates, ultimately reaching 60,535 units by the final time step. This decelerating growth pattern indicates the system is converging toward a higher equilibrium state, constrained by the ecological limits of the vegetation dynamics and climatic variability. The transition around year 50 delineates the shift from rapid recovery to gradual stabilization, reflecting the long-term success of adaptive co-management in maintaining elevated biomass levels well above the starting conditions, despite the early period of resource drawdown.
