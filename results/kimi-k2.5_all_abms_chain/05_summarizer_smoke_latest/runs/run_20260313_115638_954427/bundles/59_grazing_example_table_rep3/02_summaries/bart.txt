This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes. The average degree of deviation from the recommended number of livestock begins at a minimal value of approximately 0.007. During the first three time steps, the deviation dips slightly to its minimum value before entering a phase of rapid escalation. The average available green biomass initiates at 45,000 units and remains relatively stable through the first ten time steps. After year 65, the trend stabilizes with no significant directional movement. The average number of livestock destocked per household initiates at zero, reflecting initial stocking levels of 90 animals per household prior to resource pressure. The system exhibits oscillatory dynamics with a dominant period of approximately 49 time steps. The inequality in healthy livestock distribution, measured by the Giniini coefficient, reflects the uniform initial allocation of livestock units to each household. The number of households engaged in social learning begins at zero and undergoes rapid initial growth during the first seven time steps. The system reaches its maximum engagement level of approximately 32 households by step 63. The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across households begins at 0 and increases throughout the 100 time steps, reaching approximately 97 by the simulation's end. The trajectory exhibits three distinct phases. During the first decade, the population displays volatility with minor fluctuations between 20 and 21 households. Subsequently, from approximately year 11 through year 65, the E-RO household count experiences a steady and significant decrease. After year 66, the reduction slows considerably, and the series stabilizes, fluctuating narrowly between roughly 14.7 and 15.6 households.
