The mean reserve biomass across all simulation runs begins at approximately 90,000 units, then undergoes a sustained decrease during the initial phase of the simulation, reaching a minimum value of 84,114 at step 26. This early decline, which is statistically significant with negative slopes exceeding -195 units per step between steps 0 and 21, suggests an initial period of resource depletion as livestock grazing pressure impacts vegetation before effective adaptation occurs.

Following this trough, the system enters a recovery phase characterized by a rapid upward trajectory. Between steps 33 and 54, the increasing trend intensifies dramatically, with rates of change peaking at approximately 1,909 units per step during the 44-54 step window. This steep recovery coincides with significant inflection points around step 48 and a major change point at step 50, indicating a fundamental shift in system dynamics where learning processes likely begin to dominate agent decision-making.

After step 50, the reserve biomass continues to increase but at progressively diminishing rates, with slope coefficients declining from 643 units per step (steps 55-65) to merely 5.4 units per step (steps 88-98). This pattern indicates the system is approaching a new equilibrium or carrying capacity. By the conclusion of the simulation at step 100, the metric stabilizes at approximately 121,073 units—roughly 34% higher than the initial value and 44% above the minimum threshold observed at step 26. The oscillation patterns detected between steps 10-20 and 41-49 suggest periods of experimentation or strategy adjustment as the mixed population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types adapt their grazing practices.

For decision makers, these trends demonstrate that while private-property grazing systems with learning-capable agents may experience short-term ecological degradation during the initial adaptation period, the integration of learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms can drive substantial long-term biomass recovery. The system requires approximately 25-30 time steps to transition from depletion to growth, suggesting that policy interventions supporting behavioral adaptation should anticipate an initial lag period before ecological benefits materialize. The final stabilization above initial conditions indicates that adaptive co-management strategies can outperform static approaches, though the recovery rate is front-loaded within the 30-60 step window.
