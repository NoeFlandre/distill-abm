The time series data reveals a consistent pattern across simulation runs characterized by an initial crisis phase followed by adaptive stabilization. During the first 5 steps, the system experiences a sharp transient spike where mean destocking rapidly increases from 0 to peak values between approximately 6.0 and 7.5 livestock per household, indicating an initial resource bottleneck as the population adjusts to starting conditions. This peak reflects immediate forced liquidation due to insufficient pasture biomass relative to the initial herd size of 90 animals under variable rainfall conditions.

Following this initial shock, steps 5 through 20 exhibit a steep monotonic decline, with mean destocking decreasing to levels between 0.5 and 2.0, though considerable heterogeneity emerges across runs. Between steps 20 and 40, the decline continues but with marked oscillations ranging between 0 and 2.0, reflecting the interplay of stochastic rainfall variability (with mean 200 and standard deviation 100) and household adaptation processes as agents adjust their risk parameters and grazing strategies.

After approximately step 40, the system enters a steady-state phase where mean destocking stabilizes at low baseline levels persistently maintained through step 100. By the terminal period, values converge to a range between 0 and approximately 1.0, with most runs stabilizing below 0.5 while a minority maintain persistent destocking rates near 1.0. This final distribution suggests that while learning mechanisms (learning-by-doing and social learning) enable most households to achieve sustainable herd management with minimal forced liquidation, persistent heterogeneity in outcomes remains due to the fixed behavioral type composition (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) and stochastic ecological variability. The long-term convergence to low destocking levels indicates successful adaptation to private property grazing constraints, though the non-zero floor in some runs reflects ongoing climatic risk that cannot be fully eliminated through behavioral adaptation alone.
