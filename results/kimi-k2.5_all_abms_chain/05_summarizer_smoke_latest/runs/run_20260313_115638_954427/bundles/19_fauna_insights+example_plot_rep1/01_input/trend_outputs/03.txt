The pronounced slowing suggests that male prey availability is declining, reducing encounter frequencies between hunters and target animals.

After step 1000, the majority of runs stabilize with negligible further accumulation, maintaining final cumulative counts between approximately 110 and 125. Individual runs reach these plateaus at varying times—some as early as step 700, others continuing to accumulate sporadically until step 1500 or beyond, with the latest extension reaching approximately step 2000. The persistent variation in both timing and final magnitude across runs indicates substantial stochastic influence from spatial dynamics and encounter probabilities.

The consistent upper asymptote around 120 kills suggests a fundamental limit imposed by either prey population dynamics or hunter foraging constraints under the specified parameter configuration.

**Implications:** The self-limiting nature of the accumulation curve—rapid initial harvest followed by deceleration and stabilization—indicates that hunting pressure on males generates negative feedback through prey depletion. For decision makers testing extinction hypotheses, this pattern suggests that male megaherbivore populations are vulnerable to rapid depletion under the modeled hunting intensity, with the majority of mortality concentrated in the early simulation period. The high variability in depletion trajectories across runs emphasizes that stochastic factors may advance or delay effective extinction outcomes by substantial margins, necessitating large sample sizes for reliable predictions of population persistence.
