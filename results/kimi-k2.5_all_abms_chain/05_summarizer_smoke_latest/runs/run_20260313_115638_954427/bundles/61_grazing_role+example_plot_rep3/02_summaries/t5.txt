Model focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making. The average initial risk attitude of social learning agents remains constant throughout the 100-year simulation period. The range of values, spanning from negative dispositions around -0.11 to positive values around 0.18, indicates the repeated simulations explore diverse baseline risk attitudes. Biomass at step 100 stabilizes at values ranging from 111,000 to 128,000 units. The final biomass levels substantially exceed the initial starting values. Variation emerges across simulation runs during the recovery phase and persists through the final plateau. Trajectories converge toward low destock rates by step 50, but some maintain elevated destocking of 1-2 animals until approximately step 40 before declining. By step 60, the system approaches steady-state with destock rate stabilizing predominantly between 0 and 1 animal per household. This persistent variation reflects differential effectiveness of the three behavioral strategies in achieving long-term stock-pasture equilibrium. The average cumulative number of livestock destocked per household begins at zero and increases rapidly during the initial 20 time steps, reaching approximately 55 to 80 animals across all simulation runs. Trajectories diverge markedly into two distinct behavioral patterns following this initial adjustment period.
