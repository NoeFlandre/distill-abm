Across the 100-year simulation horizon, the trajectories of households engaged in social learning exhibit a consistent three-phase pattern with substantial variation in final outcomes. At initialization (step 0), no households have activated social learning. During the initial adoption phase (approximately steps 0-20), the count increases rapidly across all simulation runs. By step 10, most trajectories show between 12-24 households with social learning, and by step 20, counts range from approximately 20-50 households depending on the simulation run.

The middle transition phase (steps 20-40) shows continued but decelerating growth, with trajectories rising to final values between approximately 30-54 households. After step 40, virtually all trajectories stabilize, maintaining constant counts through step 100 with no further net changes in the number of social learning households.

The ensemble of simulation runs reveals a wide dispersion in equilibrium outcomes. At the lower bound, several trajectories stabilize at approximately 18-20 households, which corresponds closely to the 20 households pre-designated as E-RO-SL1 (social learning) type. At the upper bound, several trajectories reach approximately 48-54 households, indicating that up to 80-90% of the 60-household population ultimately adopted social learning. Intermediate outcomes cluster around 30, 36, 42, and 45 households.

Interpreted in the context of the three learning strategies deployed (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 with equal representation of 20 households each), the results suggest that the E-RO-SL1 households reliably activate social learning early in all simulations. The variation in final counts reflects differential success in the diffusion of social learning to the remaining 40 households (E-RO and E-LBD types). Runs stabilizing near 20 indicate containment of social learning within the pre-designated group, while runs reaching 40-54 households demonstrate substantial crossover adoption by experiential rule-of-thumb and learning-by-doing households. The rapid initial rise suggests that decisions to adopt social learning occur primarily in the first two decades of the simulation, after which the community settles into stable behavioral configurations with no further strategy switching detected through the century-long horizon.
