The mean number of livestock plus destocked animals per household begins at approximately 90 units at step 0. Over the first 40 steps, the system undergoes a rapid and consistent decline across all simulation runs, reaching a minimum of approximately 40 units by step 40. This initial collapse represents a sharp reduction in herd sizes, likely driven by initial destocking decisions and vegetation constraints as the system responds to starting conditions.

After step 40, the trajectories diverge significantly, indicating high variability in recovery outcomes across different simulation runs. By step 100, the ensemble of runs spans a broad range from approximately 40 to 62 units. A subset of trajectories shows strong recovery, climbing steadily from the minimum to reach 60–62 units by the final time step. These runs demonstrate successful adaptation and herd rebuilding following the initial shock. Another subset exhibits moderate recovery, stabilizing in the 45–50 unit range, suggesting partial adaptation or persistent environmental constraints. A smaller group shows minimal recovery, remaining near 40–42 units throughout the latter half of the simulation, indicating persistent low-level equilibria or continued destocking pressure.

The divergence after step 40 reflects the interaction between stochastic environmental variability (such as rainfall fluctuations) and heterogeneous household behaviors, including different learning strategies and risk attitudes. For decision-makers, the results suggest that while initial destocking phases are highly predictable, long-term recovery outcomes are uncertain and highly sensitive to both environmental conditions and the specific behavioral adaptations employed by households. Interventions aimed at supporting recovery should account for this variability, focusing on enhancing adaptive capacity during the critical 40–60 step window when recovery trajectories become established.
