The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock distribution begins at zero, reflecting an initially equal allocation of livestock across all 60 households. During the first 20 time steps, inequality remains minimal, rising only slightly to values between 0.005 and 0.015 as households initiate their distinct grazing and learning strategies on private patches.

Between steps 20 and 60, the metric increases at a moderate, roughly linear pace, reaching values ranging from 0.020 to 0.045 across different simulation runs. This divergence reflects the cumulative effects of heterogeneous risk attitudes, stochastic rainfall variability with a standard deviation of 100, and differential success in learning-by-doing and social learning processes among the three behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1).

After step 60, the trajectory steepens considerably in most runs, with inequality accelerating rapidly through positive feedback mechanisms where households with healthier herds leverage superior pasture management knowledge to maintain livestock advantages. By step 100, the Gini coefficient spans a wide range from approximately 0.030 to 0.095, indicating that some simulation outcomes maintain relatively equitable distributions of healthy livestock while others exhibit significant concentration among a subset of households. This late-phase acceleration and cross-run variation demonstrates that inequality emerges endogenously from the interaction of ecological stochasticity and adaptive learning processes in stationary private-property grazing systems.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that livestock health inequality is not a static initial condition but an emergent property that intensifies over time, particularly after the first 60 time steps. The sensitivity of final inequality levels to stochastic factors suggests that early intervention strategies—such as targeted support for lagging households or modulation of social learning networks—should be implemented before positive feedback loops consolidate livestock advantages, as redistribution becomes progressively more difficult once learning-based inequalities have entrenched.
