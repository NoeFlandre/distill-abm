The count of households following the non-learning E-RO strategy begins at 20 across all simulation runs. During the initial 20 time steps, the number diverges rapidly, with different trajectories spanning from near 0 to approximately 40 households. Several runs exhibit sharp increases in this period, peaking between 35 and 42 households around time steps 15 to 35, indicating that in these scenarios, households originally using learning-based strategies switched to the baseline non-learning approach. Other trajectories show steep declines within the first 30 steps, with some reaching counts as low as 2 to 5 households, reflecting rapid adoption of learning strategies in those runs.

Between time steps 40 and 60, most trajectories transition from rapid change to quasi-stable oscillations around distinct equilibrium levels. By time step 100, the final counts cluster into several distinct groups: approximately 25-27 households in some runs, 23-24 in others, 16-18 in a third group, and near-zero levels (0-5) in several trajectories. One particular run reaches complete extinction of the E-RO strategy by step 65, maintaining zero non-learning households through the remainder of the simulation.

The sustained separation between high-count and low-count trajectories after step 60 suggests strong path dependency in strategy adoption. The early divergence indicates that stochastic variations in the initial learning processes and ecological feedbacks create tipping points that determine whether non-learning behaviors persist or learning strategies dominate the population.

These dynamics imply that the diffusion of learning-based management practices in smallholder grazing systems is highly unpredictable and sensitive to early conditions. Decision makers should recognize that intervention windows may be narrow, as the first 20 time steps appear critical for determining long-term strategy distributions. Policies aimed at promoting adaptive learning should prioritize early engagement with households, as delays may result in system lock-in to non-learning equilibria that prove resistant to later change. The coexistence of trajectories with high and low E-RO counts also suggests that uniform policy approaches may be ineffective, and interventions might need to account for local heterogeneity in social learning networks and initial risk attitudes.
