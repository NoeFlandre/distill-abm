The mean reserve biomass exhibits a characteristic four-phase trajectory over the 100-step simulation horizon. Initially, the metric commences at approximately 90,000 units and undergoes a brief transient increase, reaching roughly 91,000 units within the first 5 steps. Following this initial adjustment, the system enters a degradation phase persisting from approximately step 5 through step 40, during which reserve biomass declines progressively to minimum values ranging between 80,000 and 88,000 units. This represents a reduction of 2% to 11% from initial levels, with the timing and depth of the trough varying stochastically across simulation runs.

Subsequently, a rapid recovery phase dominates the period from step 40 to step 70, characterized by logistic growth dynamics where biomass increases steeply from the minima toward asymptotic levels. The rate of recovery substantially exceeds the preceding rate of decline, suggesting accelerated vegetation regrowth coupled with reduced grazing pressure. From approximately step 70 through step 100, the metric stabilizes in an equilibrium phase, converging to steady-state values between 111,000 and 128,000 units. These terminal values exceed initial conditions by 23% to 42%, indicating a net ecological improvement over the simulation duration despite the intermediate depletion period.

The substantial variation in final equilibrium states across runs—spanning a range of approximately 17,000 units—reflects the heterogeneity introduced by mixed behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) operating under stochastic rainfall and learning dynamics. The consistent overshoot of initial biomass levels in all trajectories suggests that adaptive learning mechanisms enable the system to identify and maintain grazing strategies superior to initial behavioral configurations. The divergence in recovery pathways, particularly the variation in trough depth and final plateau height, indicates that stochastic factors and social learning interactions significantly influence long-term pasture productivity.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The U-shaped trajectory demonstrates that short-term resource depletion during adaptation phases may be an unavoidable consequence of initial learning processes in grazing systems. However, the consistent recovery to levels substantially exceeding initial conditions indicates that promoting adaptive capacity—specifically learning-by-doing and social learning—yields significant long-term ecological benefits. The wide variation in final biomass outcomes (111,000–128,000) suggests that specific social-ecological configurations strongly influence productivity; thus, policies should prioritize facilitating rapid transitions from degradation to recovery phases, potentially by accelerating information sharing among smallholders. The resilience demonstrated after step 40 implies that once effective strategies diffuse through the population via social learning, the system can sustain higher biomass levels than historically observed, supporting both livestock productivity and ecological health in private property grazing contexts.
