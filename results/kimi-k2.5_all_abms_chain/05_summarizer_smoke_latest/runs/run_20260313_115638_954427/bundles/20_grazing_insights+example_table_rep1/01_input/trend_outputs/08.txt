This early phase reflects the initial diffusion of social learning behaviors as households first begin observing and responding to neighbor performance, particularly among those with higher susceptibility to social cues or lower initial uncertainty parameters. By step 28, the growth rate begins to decelerate markedly, transitioning from an initial rapid ascent to a more gradual accumulation as the pool of potential adopters begins to saturate.

Between steps 30 and 60, the system exhibits continued but slowing growth punctuated by minor fluctuations, with inflection points around steps 33, 38, and 57 indicating periodic adjustments in the adoption rate. These oscillations likely correspond to the annual vegetation dynamics and feedback loops between pasture conditions and household decision-making, where poor years may temporarily slow adoption while good years accelerate it. The increase slows to a near-zero slope by step 60, stabilizing at approximately 32 households—representing roughly 53% of the total population—where it remains through step 100.

This plateau suggests that social learning reaches an equilibrium where it coexists with other behavioral strategies (baseline and learning-by-doing), rather than displacing them entirely. The persistent heterogeneity implies that distinct niches exist for different uncertainty management approaches within the same resource system, with approximately half the households maintaining alternative strategies despite the demonstrated success of social learning neighbors.

For decision makers, this trajectory indicates that promoting social learning in smallholder grazing systems requires patience, as full diffusion takes approximately 60 time periods to reach equilibrium. The incomplete adoption ceiling suggests that policy interventions should not expect universal uptake of social learning practices and should instead account for persistent diversity in management approaches. The early rapid adoption phase (first 20–30 steps) represents a critical window for intervention, as households are most receptive to behavioral influence during this period before strategies crystallize.
