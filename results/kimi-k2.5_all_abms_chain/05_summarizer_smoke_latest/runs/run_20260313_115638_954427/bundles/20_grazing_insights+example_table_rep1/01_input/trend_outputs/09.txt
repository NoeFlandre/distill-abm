The cumulative number of destocked livestock begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the simulation period, reaching approximately 97 animals per household by the final timestep. During the initial phase, from timestep 0 to roughly timestep 25, the accumulation occurs most rapidly with a steep decelerating slope, indicating frequent or large-scale destocking events as households respond to initial pasture conditions, climatic variability, and the establishment of private property management regimes. Following the first change point around timestep 25, the rate of accumulation decreases substantially to less than one animal per timestep, suggesting an adaptation phase where households—comprising a heterogeneous mix of baseline, learning-by-doing, and social learning behavioral types—refine their grazing management and livestock placement decisions based on observed biomass conditions and neighbor performance. A second change point near timestep 60 marks a further transition to a slower, steady accumulation rate of approximately 0.31 animals per timestep, which persists through the remainder of the simulation. This continued but decelerating accumulation implies that while destocking remains a continual management necessity in response to stochastic rainfall and ecological constraints, the severity of required stock reductions diminishes over time as the population gradually approaches an equilibrium between herd size and carrying capacity. The absence of significant oscillations or declines in the cumulative measure indicates that destocking events are permanent adjustments rather than temporary fluctuations, reflecting sustained pressure on livestock populations despite the presence of learning mechanisms.

For decision makers, this pattern suggests that support mechanisms for livestock retention—such as emergency feed provision or financial safety nets—are most critical during the initial establishment phase when destocking pressure is highest. The long-term trend indicates that while adaptive learning capabilities eventually reduce the rate of destocking, they do not eliminate the need for ongoing herd size adjustments in private property grazing systems facing climatic uncertainty, pointing to the necessity of sustained institutional support for climate adaptation rather than one-time interventions.
