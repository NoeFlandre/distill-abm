The average number of livestock destocked per household begins at zero, then surges to approximately 6.6 animals by the third yearly time step. This immediate spike reflects an initial resource crisis where the starting herd size of 90 animals per household exceeds the private pasture carrying capacity under high rainfall variability, forcing rapid liquidation before adaptive learning mechanisms can take effect.

Following this initial shock, destocking rates decline precipitously over the subsequent 25 to 30 time steps, falling from the peak of roughly 6.6 to below 0.7 animals per household by step 27. This sharp decrease indicates that households—comprising a heterogeneous mix of baseline (E-RO), learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1) types—rapidly adjust their management strategies. The E-LBD agents refine their decisions through individual experience with biomass feedbacks on their private patches, while E-RO-SL1 agents leverage observational learning from neighbors' destocking outcomes and reserve biomass observations, collectively reducing the need for forced animal removal as they calibrate to ecological limits.

Between approximately steps 30 and 65, the destocking rate stabilizes at minimal levels, oscillating between roughly 0.3 and 0.9 animals per household with no clear directional trend. This plateau suggests the system reaches a quasi-equilibrium where the combination of fixed property boundaries, stationary household positioning, and established learning routines buffers against minor environmental fluctuations. However, persistent low-level destocking indicates that even with learning capabilities, households face ongoing pressure from climatic stochasticity, particularly given the significant rainfall standard deviation relative to mean precipitation.

After step 66, a modest but significant increasing trend emerges, with destocking levels rising from minimal values toward approximately 0.31 animals per household by the simulation's conclusion at step 100. This late-stage uptick may reflect accumulated pasture degradation over the century-long sequence or the manifestation of rare drought events in the stochastic rainfall pattern, challenging the long-term sustainability of static behavioral configurations since strategy switching between types is disabled.

For decision makers, these dynamics underscore that initial stocking rates of 90 animals per household create immediate welfare losses through forced destocking, suggesting that lower initial allocations or pre-implementation carrying capacity assessments could prevent early asset depletion. While learning mechanisms prove effective at stabilizing destocking at low levels during the middle phase, the slight resurgence toward the end of the simulation indicates that fixed behavioral strategies may struggle with long-term climatic variability, highlighting potential benefits from enabling dynamic strategy switching or enhancing social learning networks to address emerging resource scarcity.
