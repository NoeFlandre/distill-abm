Model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making. The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. The average degree of deviation from recommended livestock levels begins at approximately 0.007, indicating near-complete adherence to guidelines. Over the first 33 time steps, this deviation increases rapidly and significantly, reaching a maximum value of approximately 0.38. This steep ascent suggests that agents quickly diverge from recommended stocking rates. Pronounced declining trend emerges between steps 11 and 21, with biomass continuing to decrease until it reaches a minimum value of approximately 42,057 units at step 27. Biomass begins increasing significantly around step 33, with the rate of growth accelerating notably between steps 44 and 54 where the steepest upward slope occurs. After step 54, the growth rate moderates progressively but remains consistently positive through the remainder of the simulation. Destocking levels rise from minimal values toward approximately 0.31 animals per household. This late-stage uptick may reflect accumulated pasture degradation over the century-long sequence or the manifestation of rare drought events in the stochastic rainfall pattern. The increase slows to a near-zero slope by step 60, stabilizing at approximately 32 households. This plateau suggests that social learning reaches an equilibrium where it coexists with other behavioral strategies (baseline and learning-by-doing) behaviors do not occur within the simulated timeframe. The oscillatory patterns observed throughout the series imply cyclical adjustments in strategy adoption rates. Fluctuations likely reflect feedback loops between pasture biomass conditions, rainfall variability, and household decision-making.
