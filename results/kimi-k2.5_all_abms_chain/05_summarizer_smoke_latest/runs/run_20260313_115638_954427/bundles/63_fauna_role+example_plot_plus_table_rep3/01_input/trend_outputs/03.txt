The cumulative number of male ungulates harvested by human foragers initiates at zero and follows a strongly saturating trajectory, ultimately stabilizing near 117.5 individuals. During the initial phase extending approximately through tick 850, the metric increases rapidly, with rolling trend analyses indicating significantly positive accumulation rates initially as steep as 0.173 per tick, gradually decelerating to 0.008 by tick 1159. This early phase is punctuated by change points at ticks 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850, reflecting discrete transitions in hunting efficiency as forager bands encounter and harvest male prey. The presence of an inflection point near tick 362 marks a shift from accelerating to decelerating accumulation, corresponding to cumulative kill counts approaching roughly 40-50 individuals.

Between ticks 1160 and 1855, the series enters a protracted plateau phase characterized by minimal or statistically insignificant trends, with slopes approaching zero and intermittent windows showing no discernible trend. During this interval, the cumulative count oscillates narrowly around a median of 116.5, peaking at approximately 120.17 individuals near tick 1876. The stabilization at this asymptotic level—representing approximately 48% of the initial prey population of 250, assuming roughly equal sex ratios—suggests effective depletion of the male cohort or a strategic shift by hunters toward alternative prey categories as the energetic return per unit effort for male ungulates diminishes.

In the terminal phase beyond tick 1856, the series exhibits a slightly decreasing trend with periodic oscillations having a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, potentially reflecting seasonal hunting patterns given the model's high seasonality parameter. This late-stage fluctuation around the plateau value of 117-120, combined with the absence of further significant accumulation, indicates that predation pressure on male ungulates effectively ceases once the population is reduced to critically low densities, despite the continued presence of forager agents in the landscape. The high variance observed in early time steps (standard deviation of 32.5 across the series) converges toward zero as all simulation runs approach similar saturation levels, demonstrating consistent long-term predation outcomes despite stochastic variation in individual encounter rates.
