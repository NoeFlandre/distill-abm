Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change. Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances. Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation period of 2,312 time steps. The total energy available to all forager bands initiates at 2000 units and experiences a sustained monotonic decline throughout the simulation. This trajectory reflects a persistent energetic deficit wherein cumulative movement costs and processing expenditures consistently exceed caloric returns from hunting, driving the aggregate forager energy balance into increasingly negative territory. The cumulative number of postpartum female ungulates hunted begins at zero and exhibits a sigmoid growth pattern characterized by three distinct phases. During this interval, the cumulative count oscillates narrowly around a median of 116.5, peaking at approximately 120.17 individuals near tick 1876. The stabilization at this asymptotic level represents 48% of the initial prey population of 250, assuming roughly equal sex ratios. The model's strongly seasonal environment (seasonality parameter 0.8), creating regular pulses in postpartum female availability that align with reproductive cycling approximately every two months within the annual cycle. The observed saturation around 117 killed post-partum females suggests that hunters exhaust the available supply of reproductive females with dependent offspring. The trajectory was characterized by three distinct phases: an initial rapid collapse, an intermediate period of decelerated decline with persistent oscillations, and a terminal phase of asymptotic approach to extinction. During the initial phase (0–475), the population experiences rapid reduction from 125 to values below 20, marked by inflection points at 305 that indicate shifts in the rate of decline. This phase represents the initial impact of hunting pressure from 20 human foragers on the naive population. The total energy stored in the male ungulate population begins at 12,500 units and exhibits a prolonged, monotonic decline toward extinction, reaching zero by tick 2,284. The decline follows a nonlinear pattern characterized by distinct phases of differential vulnerability to hunting pressure and environmental forcing. The median population collapses to 1.47 individuals, suggesting functionally extinct conditions for most of the simulation duration. The aggregate energy contained within the megaherbivore population initiates at approximately 16,667 units and undergoes a precipitous decline throughout the simulation, ultimately reaching zero at tick 2,311. This trajectory indicates a complete depletion of the prey energy base over approximately 6.3 years (given the temporal calibration of 365 ticks per year)
