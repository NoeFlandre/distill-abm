The cumulative number of livestock destocked across households initiates at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation period, reaching a mean terminal value of approximately 96.8 animals per household. This accumulation follows a decelerating trajectory characterized by distinct temporal phases. During the initial 25 years, the system exhibits rapid adjustment dynamics with slope values declining sharply from 4.96 animals per year in the first decade to 1.47 and 0.69 in subsequent intervals, indicating an initial phase of intensive destocking as households adapt from the initial stocking level of 90 animals to prevailing pasture conditions under significant rainfall variability (coefficient of variation of 50%).

Significant structural changes in the accumulation rate occur at years 25 and 60, marking transitions between distinct behavioral regimes. After year 25, the rate of destocking stabilizes considerably, with slopes ranging between 0.31 and 0.36 animals per year for the remainder of the simulation, suggesting households have approached equilibrium stocking decisions relative to their learning strategies and local ecological conditions. The series exhibits multiple inflection points concentrated around years 45-47, 53, 56-57, 62, and 67-75, superimposed on an oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, reflecting long-term cyclical adjustments potentially linked to vegetation recovery dynamics or adaptation cycles among the three behavioral types (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning).

Substantial heterogeneity persists across the population throughout the simulation, with terminal values ranging from approximately 58 to 173 animals, indicating that learning strategy significantly determines long-term destocking intensity despite identical starting conditions and ecological exposure. The standard deviation of 20.3 around the mean of 75.5 (median 81.2) demonstrates divergent adaptation pathways, where some households maintain relatively stable herds with minimal destocking accumulation while others experience continual high turnover.

**Implications for decision makers:** The pronounced initial destocking phase (years 0-25) suggests that initial stocking rates exceed sustainable carrying capacity under variable rainfall, indicating need for conservative starting policies or early adjustment mechanisms. The identification of distinct performance trajectories across learning strategies by year 60, with some households stabilizing at low cumulative destocking (approximately 58) while others continue accumulating losses (exceeding 140), demonstrates that institutional support for specific learning approaches—particularly those facilitating social learning or experiential adaptation—may significantly influence long-term livelihood resilience. The 49-year oscillatory pattern indicates that evaluation of grazing management policies should extend beyond short-term windows to capture full ecological and social adaptation cycles.
