The number of households engaged in social learning exhibits a characteristic sigmoid growth pattern over the 100-year simulation horizon. Initially, at timestep 0, no households have activated social learning behavior. During the first approximately 20 timesteps, the system undergoes a phase of rapid acceleration, with adoption rates increasing steeply from zero toward a mean value of roughly 27 households. Statistical analysis identifies significant upward trends throughout this initial period (steps 0–21), with the steepest increases occurring between steps 0 and 10 (slope of 1.430) and steps 11 and 21 (slope of 1.139), indicating rapid diffusion of social learning mechanisms among the 60 farming families.

Between steps 21 and 40, the rate of increase decelerates considerably, though remaining statistically significantly positive. The mean trajectory exhibits multiple inflection points during this interval (particularly around steps 28, 30, 33, and 38), indicating stepwise adoption events consistent with discrete annual decisions by farmers. By step 40, a change point occurs where the growth trajectory transitions from active accumulation to relative stabilization.

From approximately step 40 onward, and settling definitively by step 60, the count stabilizes at a mean plateau of roughly 32 households (representing approximately 53% of the total population). This terminal value varies substantially across individual simulation runs, with final outcomes ranging from roughly 18 to 54 households ultimately engaged in social learning. The mean series reaches its maximum value of 32.07 at step 63 and maintains this approximate level through step 100, though with minor fluctuations detected by oscillation indices clustered around steps 22–32 and 51–59. The standard deviation of 8.92 across the time series reflects considerable divergence in both adoption timing and final penetration rates between runs, likely driven by the high rainfall variability (σ=100) and localized knowledge sharing within the restricted knowledge radius of 1 patch.

Given that only 20 households were initially designated as social learning types (E-RO-SL1), the final mean count of 32 suggests that households initially employing experiential rule-of-thumb or learning-by-doing strategies transition to social learning over time as they encounter uncertainty in pasture conditions. The rapid early adoption phase indicates that social learning spreads quickly once the simulation begins, while the subsequent plateau suggests saturation of the susceptible population or equilibrium conditions where the costs and benefits of social learning balance out.

**Implications for decision makers:** The concentration of social learning adoption in the first 20–40 years suggests that interventions to promote knowledge sharing should target nascent stages of system development, as this is when farmers are most susceptible to adopting new decision-making strategies. The substantial variability in final adoption rates (ranging over 36 households between minimum and maximum outcomes) indicates that local environmental shocks and neighborhood network effects play crucial roles in determining whether social learning becomes widespread or remains limited. Expect approximately half of farming households to ultimately engage in social learning, though this figure is highly sensitive to stochastic rainfall events and initial risk dispositions.
