The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the simulation duration from step 0 through step 2312. This value exhibits zero variance with no detectable peaks, valleys, or directional trends. For decision makers, this fixed hunter density functions as a critical experimental control that isolates hunting pressure from population dynamics. Between steps 500 and 1200, the accumulation saturates, with most simulation runs asymptoting at values between 110 and 125 individuals. The median cumulative kill count reaches approximately 116.5, while the aggregate mean across all 80 simulation runs stabilizes near 117.5 by the conclusion of the observation period at step 2312. Cumulative number of postpartum females killed begins at zero and undergoes rapid accumulation during the initial 475 time steps. Most simulation runs have accumulated between 100 and 120 kills, representing the majority of the total hunting mortality observed across the full simulation period. The peak reproductive output around step 1700 defines the maximum sustainable population growth under current conditions. Decision makers should intervene before step 1600 by reducing hunting pressure, increasing resource availability, or expanding habitat area. Predictable oscillatory pattern suggests timed interventions synchronized with the 50-step reproductive cycle may be more effective than continuous management approaches. The population of female prey animals begins at 125 individuals. Over the course of approximately 2,312 time steps, the population undergoes a sustained decline to extinction. The decline is most rapid during the initial phase, with significant decreases occurring within the first 475 time steps. Ulate population undergoes a severe, deterministic decline toward zero, reaching complete depletion by step 2,284. The trajectory exhibits distinct kinetic phases: an initial modest reduction to approximately 11,608 units by step 65 is followed by a catastrophic collapse between steps 65 and 376. The combined energy available in all prey animals initiates at approximately 16,667 units and declines deterministically to extinction by time step 2,311. The depletion follows a nonlinear trajectory characterized by distinct phases of rapid collapse, moderate fluctuation, and terminal decline. Nnfrom time step 500 to 875, the system enters a phase of continued decline marked by increased volatility and substantially reduced energy levels. Despite these perturbations, the mean coverage remains stable at 32.66%. Trend analysis reveals generally increasing trajectories within most rolling windows. Decision makers must account for predictable, severe annual resource bottlenecks where available forage drops by nearly 25%.
