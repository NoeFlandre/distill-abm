The percentage of vegetated landscape patches initiates at approximately 33%, consistent with the parameterized initial grass coverage, and immediately experiences a modest decline to roughly 32% within the first 50 time steps as herbivores begin grazing. Over the subsequent 250 steps, the vegetation percentage exhibits a gradual recovery, climbing to approximately 32.5-32.8% as grass regrowth dynamics begin to balance consumption.

Around step 300-350, the system encounters a severe collapse in vegetation cover, with the percentage of green patches plummeting dramatically from the pre-crash level of ~32.8%. The severity of this crash varies considerably across simulation runs, with values falling to between 23% and 29%, representing a decline of 4-10 percentage points. This initial collapse represents the most extreme vegetation depletion event in the time series.

Following this nadir, rapid recovery occurs within approximately 50 steps, with vegetation returning to 32.5-33% by step 400, facilitated by the fast regrowth time of 3 ticks. The system then exhibits a pattern of seasonal oscillations superimposed on a generally stable baseline near 33% vegetation cover. Subsequent crashes occur at approximately step 720, step 1100, and step 1450, corresponding to successive annual minima in the seasonal cycle. Each successive decline becomes progressively less severe: the second crash reaches approximately 29-30%, the third drops to 31.5-32%, and the fourth to around 32.5%. This attenuation in crash severity suggests a gradual reduction in grazing pressure over the first four annual cycles.

After approximately step 1500, most simulation trajectories stabilize at or near 33% vegetation cover with minimal fluctuation, though one extended run continues to exhibit low-amplitude oscillations through step 2200, maintaining values between 32.5% and 33.5%.

The variance among simulation runs is highest during the first vegetation crash (steps 300-350), where some runs experience catastrophic declines to 23% while others only fall to 29%. This divergence indicates high sensitivity to stochastic grazing patterns and movement dynamics during the initial year. After step 400, the runs converge closely, with the exception of one trajectory that maintains higher variability through the end of the observation period.

**Implications for decision makers:**
The model demonstrates that grassland ecosystems with high seasonal variability (seasonality = 0.8) and rapid regrowth capacity can sustain herbivore populations, but are vulnerable to severe overgrazing events during the first seasonal minimum when initial herbivore densities are set at 250 individuals. The progressive attenuation of vegetation crashes suggests that the herbivore population either self-regulates or experiences demographic decline after the first year. For conservation or management purposes, this indicates that initial stocking rates near carrying capacity may trigger boom-bust dynamics, and that interventions to reduce herbivore density before the first seasonal minimum (around step 350) could prevent the severe vegetation depletion that occurs in the first annual cycle. The rapid recovery capacity (3-day regrowth) provides ecosystem resilience, but cannot prevent the initial overgrazing crisis when populations start near maximum density.
