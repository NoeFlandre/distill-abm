The cumulative mortality of female prey animals initiates at zero and follows a saturating trajectory across all simulation replicates. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, the metric exhibits rapid, steep ascension, with most runs accumulating between 100 and 120 female kills. This swift accumulation reflects intensive encounter rates and successful hunting events when the initial female population, estimated at roughly 125 individuals based on the initialization parameters, is abundant and accessible to forager bands operating within a foraging radius of 6 patches.

Between approximately steps 500 and 1000, the rate of increase decelerates markedly across all trajectories, transitioning from steep growth to gradual, stepwise increments. This deceleration coincides with the depletion of the female prey population, as cumulative mortality approaches the theoretical maximum of available females, constraining future hunting opportunities and forcing foragers to encounter progressively scarcer individuals.

By approximately step 1000 to 1500, the majority of simulations stabilize at terminal values ranging between 108 and 125 females killed, with some trajectories plateauing as early as step 600 and others extending accumulation until step 2000. The asymptotic convergence near 125 kills—the expected initial female population size given the starting conditions—indicates near-complete extirpation of the female demographic cohort in most runs. The persistent variance in plateau timing and final counts, with some runs terminating at approximately 108 kills while others reach 122 or higher, suggests stochastic variation in search efficiency, herd movement patterns, and the spatial distribution of surviving females during the terminal hunting phases.

For decision makers: The rapid accumulation of 80 to 90 percent of total possible female mortality within the first 500 to 700 time steps demonstrates that the reproductive capacity of the megaherbivore population collapses quickly under current hunting pressures, severely limiting the window for intervention. The consistent approach toward effective elimination of the female breeding stock indicates that the existing forager population of 20 agents, combined with the specified energetic returns and processing costs, generates unsustainable harvest rates. Management strategies must prioritize immediate reduction of hunting pressure or enhancement of female refugia within the first 400 to 500 time steps to prevent irreversible loss of reproductive potential and total population collapse.
