The aggregate green biomass exhibits a pronounced U-shaped trajectory over the century-long simulation. Initially positioned at approximately 45,000 biomass units, the system undergoes a significant decline during the first three decades, reaching a minimum of roughly 42,057 units around year 27. This early degradation phase reflects the initial pressure of 90 livestock units per household on private pastures before adaptive learning mechanisms take effect.

Following the trough at year 27, the system enters a sustained recovery period that continues through the remainder of the simulation. Between approximately years 33 and 54, biomass increases at an accelerating rate, with the steepest gains occurring in the decade following year 44. After year 55, the recovery continues but at gradually diminishing rates of increase, though remaining statistically significant through year 98. By the terminal year, biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units—markedly exceeding initial conditions by over 15,000 units.

The trajectory suggests distinct behavioral phases: an initial period of resource depletion as stationary households apply baseline stocking decisions, followed by a transition period where learning-by-doing and social learning from neighbors within the knowledge radius enable adaptive destocking or management adjustments. The pronounced oscillation patterns detected between years 10–20 and years 41–49 likely reflect cyclical adjustments as heterogeneous learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, individual learning, and social observation) interact with high rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 units around a 200-unit mean).

For decision makers, these dynamics indicate that transitions to learning-based grazing management on private lands may involve an initial "valley of degradation" lasting nearly three decades before recovery manifests. The eventual overshoot of initial biomass conditions suggests that adaptive learning strategies successfully counterbalance environmental stochasticity and initial overstocking tendencies, but patience and potentially supplemental support during the vulnerable first 30 years appear critical for achieving long-term pasture health improvements. The slowing recovery rate in final decades implies approaching a new equilibrium, though the upward trend at year 100 suggests continued potential for further gains beyond the simulation horizon.
