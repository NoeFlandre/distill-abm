**Context and Goals**

This agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Unlike the original RAGE model which examined common property scenarios where herders move livestock across shared rangelands, this extension focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods.

The simulation tracks the interaction between vegetation condition and household decisions about livestock numbers over time. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors.

The central purpose is understanding how these different ways of adapting to changing conditions influence pasture health, herd sizes, and the persistence of households. The model particularly examines how farmers handle uncertainty and incomplete information when making stocking decisions, represented through individual risk dispositions that affect how they perceive and react to environmental feedback.

**Key Parameters**

*Population and Spatial Setup:*
The **number-households** parameter establishes how many farming families occupy the landscape, which can range from 1 to 100 families, with this study using 60 households. Each household begins with **livestock-init** set at 90 animals. The simulation runs for **timesteps** set at 100 years. Farmers begin the simulation positioned on their designated land parcels, with **start-on-homepatch?** set to True. Each farmer can observe conditions within a **knowledge-radius** that ranges from 0 to 5 patches, set here at 1 patch distance.

*Behavioral Type Configuration:*
The study examines three specific learning strategies with equal representation: **number-E-RO** (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb), **number-E-LBD** (learning-by-doing), and **number-E-RO-SL1** (social learning with single-loop adaptation), each set to 20 households. The **homog-behav-types?** parameter set to True ensures each type is evaluated in uniform groups. The original RAGE model behavioral types are inactive in this extension: **number-SAT**, **number-TRAD**, **number-MAX**, and **number-random** are all set to 0. The default reference **behavioral-type** is set to E-RO.

*Grazing Management Practices:*
Formal pasture resting is disabled with **resting?** set to False. The threshold for initiating rest periods, **resting-threshold**, ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0.1. The **intrinsic-preference** for livestock relative to other values ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0. Similarly, **social-susceptibility** to neighbor influence ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0, indicating decisions rely on other factors.

*Original Model Parameters:*
Several parameters from the foundational RAGE model are retained. The **satisficing-threshold** for satisfied decision-making ranges from 0 to 100 and is set at 0. The **satisficing-trials** parameter, ranging from 0 to 100, is set at 21. These control how the original satisficer type would search for acceptable solutions, though this type is not active in the current setup.

*Ecological Conditions:*
Rainfall is applied uniformly across the landscape with **global-rain?** set to True. The average rainfall **rain-mean** is set at 200 units annually. Year-to-year variation in precipitation is controlled by **rain-std** set at 100, indicating significant variability in annual rainfall totals. Vegetation growth responds through two rates: **gr1** set at 0.5 and **gr2** set at 0.1. The parameter **w** from the original vegetation model is set at 0.8, influencing how pastures recover from grazing. Livestock reproduction is not active in this extension, with **b** set to 0.

*Learning and Risk Mechanisms:*
The extension module is activated with **extension-model?** set to True. Uncertainty handling is engaged with **risk-mode?** set to True. Each agent begins with an initial risk disposition **risk-att-init** set at 0.5, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance. The ability to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False, confining agents to single-loop learning within their assigned type.

The average initial risk attitude among social learning agents remains constant at approximately 0.0074 throughout the entire 100-year simulation period, from the initial timestep through year 100. This value exhibits no variation, trend, or oscillation across any observed interval, maintaining identical magnitude at all recorded points in the simulation horizon.

This stability reflects the static nature of initial conditions in the model design, where risk disposition is assigned as a fixed attribute at the outset and remains unchanged, consistent with the disabled double-loop learning mechanism that prevents agents from fundamentally altering their uncertainty preferences over time.

Implications for decision makers: The invariant nature of initial risk attitudes indicates that differential outcomes between learning strategies arise from how fixed baseline dispositions interact with environmental feedback and social information, rather than from evolving risk perceptions. This suggests that extension services and policy interventions targeting grazing management should prioritize understanding and appropriately matching initial farmer risk profiles, as these baseline attitudes permanently govern how farmers interpret pasture conditions and neighbor behaviors when making stocking decisions under uncertainty.

The trajectory of average deviation from recommended livestock levels over the 100-year simulation reveals a three-phase adaptive process characterized by an initial escalation, a subsequent correction, and final stabilization.

**Phase 1: Initial Escalation (Years 0–33)**
The metric begins at effectively zero (approximately 0.007 in year 3), indicating near-complete adherence to recommendations initially. A rapid increase ensues, with the most pronounced escalation occurring between years 11 and 21, where the deviation increases steeply. By year 33, the metric reaches its maximum value of approximately 0.38. This pattern suggests that as farmers engage in learning-by-doing and social observation, their stocking decisions initially diverge significantly from recommended levels, possibly due to experimentation, localized adaptation to variable rainfall conditions, or initial overconfidence in private pasture management.

**Phase 2: Correction (Years 33–65)**
Following the peak at year 33, the deviation enters a sustained decline lasting approximately 32 years. The metric decreases from 0.38 to roughly 0.19 by year 65, with significant downward trends persisting through year 43 and again between years 55 and 65. This correction phase indicates that learning mechanisms eventually moderate the initial extreme deviations, as farmers adjust their strategies based on feedback from pasture conditions and neighbor outcomes, moving back toward recommendations while accounting for local realities.

**Phase 3: Stabilization (Years 65–100)**
After year 65, the metric stabilizes around 0.19, exhibiting no significant trend through the remainder of the simulation. This plateau suggests that the system converges on a persistent, moderate level of deviation from recommendations, representing an equilibrium where farmers balance adherence to guidelines with adaptive management necessary for variable ecological conditions.

**Cyclical Dynamics**
Underlying these phases is a cyclical pattern with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, aligning closely with the observed peak-to-trough structure of the deviation trajectory.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The trajectory indicates that when introducing private pasture management with learning-based decision strategies, policymakers should anticipate an initial decades-long period where farmer behavior diverges from recommendations, peaking around year 30 before self-correcting. Interventions targeting stocking rates may be most critical during the escalation phase (years 10–30) to prevent excessive deviation. The persistent stabilization at approximately 0.19 suggests that perfect compliance with centralized recommendations is neither achievable nor necessarily desirable under variable rainfall conditions; instead, moderate deviation represents successful local adaptation. Finally, the long oscillation period (49 years) implies that evaluation of policy effectiveness requires multi-decadal observation windows rather than short-term assessments.

The oscillation patterns detected throughout the series, particularly concentrated around years 10-20 and 40-50, align with the substantial rainfall variability (coefficient of variation of 50%) built into the ecological parameters. However, the long-term trajectory indicates that the learning mechanisms employed by households—whether experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning—collectively enable adaptive stocking decisions that progressively alleviate grazing pressure and allow vegetation recovery.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The initial resource depletion phase suggests that early intervention programs may be necessary to prevent pasture degradation during the first 25 years as farmers calibrate to local conditions. However, the eventual overshoot of initial biomass levels demonstrates that adaptive learning strategies can achieve superior long-term ecological outcomes compared to static management approaches. The approximately 25-year lag before recovery initiates represents a critical vulnerability window requiring policy buffers or temporary stocking restrictions. The slowing growth rate toward the end of the simulation suggests the system approaches a new equilibrium under adapted management, indicating that sustained learning mechanisms eventually stabilize at higher biomass levels than initial conditions, potentially supporting larger sustainable herd sizes over time.

For decision makers, these dynamics indicate that transitions to learning-based grazing management on private lands may involve an initial "valley of degradation" lasting nearly three decades before recovery manifests. The eventual overshoot of initial biomass conditions suggests that adaptive learning strategies successfully counterbalance environmental stochasticity and initial overstocking tendencies, but patience and potentially supplemental support during the vulnerable first 30 years appear critical for achieving long-term pasture health improvements. The slowing recovery rate in final decades implies approaching a new equilibrium, though the upward trend at year 100 suggests continued potential for further gains beyond the simulation horizon.

The grazing system exhibits a pronounced three-phase trajectory over the century-long simulation. Initially, aggregate livestock holdings begin at 90 animals per household but undergo a steep, sustained decline throughout the first 37 years, reaching a minimum of approximately 41 animals. This represents a reduction of more than half the initial herd size, indicating that the initial stocking rate substantially exceeds the long-term carrying capacity given the rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100) and vegetation growth parameters.

Following this crash, the system enters a stabilization period between roughly years 33 and 43, where the decline halts and values hover near the minimum without significant directional trend. This plateau suggests that farmers across all three learning strategies—baseline rule-of-thumb (E-RO), learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1)—converge on conservative stocking decisions as they process environmental feedback and adjust their risk dispositions from the initial 0.5 baseline.

After year 44, the dynamics shift to a gradual recovery phase that continues through year 100. During this 56-year period, aggregate livestock holdings slowly increase from the trough of 41 to approximately 48 animals, with the trend remaining consistently positive but decelerating over time (slope decreasing from 0.313 to 0.002). By the final timestep, the system settles near 47.5 animals, establishing a new equilibrium roughly 47% below initial levels but significantly above the crisis minimum. This long oscillation period of approximately 49 years suggests that full adaptation to sustainable stocking rates requires multiple decades of iterative learning and vegetation recovery cycles.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The initial destocking phase demonstrates that static, high stocking rates are unsustainable under variable rainfall conditions, requiring policymakers to anticipate significant herd reductions (potentially 50% or more) during the first four decades of system development. The 37-year minimum represents a critical vulnerability window where household livelihoods face maximum stress; support mechanisms should target this period. The eventual stabilization above the minimum suggests that integrating experiential and social learning mechanisms enables farmers to find sustainable equilibria, though final herd sizes remain well below initial expectations, indicating that long-term carrying capacity assessments must account for climate variability and learning lag times.

Destocking rates commence at zero but immediately escalate, reaching a pronounced maximum average of approximately 6.6 animals per household by year three. This initial spike indicates severe resource pressure as the initial allocation of 90 livestock per household exceeds the carrying capacity under highly variable rainfall conditions (mean 200 units with standard deviation of 100). Following this peak, destocking declines markedly over the first three decades, transitioning through progressively lower peaks—3.4 animals at year 9, 2.7 at year 12, and below 1.5 animals by year 17—before stabilizing.

By year 30, the system reaches a critical transition point where destocking shifts from high volatility to relative stability. The contrast between the century-long mean of approximately 0.96 animals and the median of 0.31 animals reflects the heavy influence of these early high destocking events; after year 30, typical destocking rates remain minimal, oscillating at low amplitude around values close to zero. Between years 33 and 65, no significant directional trend prevails, indicating that the three learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning) have successfully enabled households to converge on sustainable stocking levels that account for their risk dispositions and localized pasture observations within the one-patch knowledge radius.

A modest resurgence occurs between years 66 and 76, where destocking rates increase slightly before declining again to approximately 0.31 animals per household by year 100. This late-century fluctuation, superimposed on a dominant oscillatory cycle of roughly 49 years, suggests ongoing sensitivity to rainfall variability even after long-term adaptation. Because livestock reproduction is disabled in this system, destocking represents the sole mechanism for herd reduction, making the trajectory from high initial forced removals to near-zero destocking particularly significant for household persistence.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The three-decade adjustment period required to stabilize destocking rates suggests that even with active learning mechanisms, grazing systems experience substantial time lags before achieving sustainable equilibrium. Extension services should anticipate high early destocking rates and consider temporary support mechanisms to prevent severe resource degradation during initial adaptation phases. The effectiveness of diverse learning strategies—particularly the combination of individual experience and social observation—indicates that policies promoting knowledge sharing among neighboring households can accelerate convergence toward sustainable management. However, the persistent low-level volatility and late-century increase in destocking demonstrate that private pasture management under high rainfall variability remains vulnerable to periodic resource shortfalls, suggesting that risk management tools beyond single-loop learning may be necessary for long-term resilience.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The gradual emergence of inequality (reaching only ~7% after 100 years) suggests that heterogeneous learning strategies within private tenure systems maintain relative equity while permitting adaptive specialization to local conditions. Development interventions should recognize that disparities begin solidifying significantly after the 50-year mark; therefore, early support mechanisms for struggling households may prevent long-term stratification. The modest magnitude of inequality indicates that policies promoting diverse adaptive approaches—rather than enforcing uniform management rules—may effectively balance equity with ecological resilience in stochastic environments.

Over the 100-year simulation period, engagement in social learning exhibits an S-curve diffusion pattern, beginning at zero households and asymptotically approaching approximately 32 households by the final years. The trajectory reveals three distinct phases of behavioral adoption across the farming population.

The initial phase (years 0–10) demonstrates rapid acceleration, with the number of households engaging in social learning increasing sharply from zero to roughly 14–15 households. This represents the steepest adoption rate in the entire series, suggesting that early exposure to environmental variability and neighbor observations quickly demonstrates the value of information-sharing when pastures begin showing stress.

During the middle phase (years 10–40), growth continues but decelerates progressively. By year 40, approximately 30–31 households have adopted social learning behaviors. This inflection point marks a critical transition where the most receptive households have already adopted the practice, leaving a residual population that relies more heavily on private experience or rule-of-thumb approaches.

The final phase (years 40–63) shows greatly diminished growth rates, eventually plateauing at 32 households by year 63. From year 63 through year 100, the count remains stable at this level, indicating that the system has reached equilibrium with roughly 53% of the total farming community regularly engaging in social learning. Notably, this saturation level exceeds the 20 households initially designated as social-learning types, indicating that approximately 12 additional households from the experiential and rule-of-thumb cohorts eventually incorporated neighbor observation into their decision-making repertoires.

The plateau at 32 households—well below the total population of 60—suggests persistent heterogeneity in learning strategies. Despite 100 years of environmental fluctuation and opportunities for information exchange, nearly half the farming population maintains reliance on personal experience or static rules rather than social observation. This equilibrium implies that local pasture conditions, individual risk dispositions, or the costs of information acquisition create niches where private decision-making remains competitive with socially-informed strategies.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The diffusion ceiling at approximately half the population indicates that universal adoption of social learning should not be expected, even with decades of exposure. Interventions promoting information-sharing networks will likely achieve maximum impact within the first decade, with diminishing returns thereafter. Policymakers should recognize that a substantial minority of farmers will continue relying on individualized approaches, suggesting that support systems must accommodate diverse learning styles rather than assuming homogeneous adoption of social information networks. The stability of the final plateau implies that this mixed-learning equilibrium is robust to the high rainfall variability characterizing the system, indicating that both social and private learning strategies can coexist as viable long-term adaptations to environmental uncertainty.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The cyclical patterns indicate that even after stabilization, maintaining flexibility for periodic destocking in response to environmental variability remains essential for sustainable private pasture management. Policies supporting early-stage adaptation and risk management during this transition phase could improve household resilience during the necessary adjustment period.
