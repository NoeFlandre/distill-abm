The cumulative mortality of prey populations initiates at zero and undergoes rapid accumulation during the initial decades of the simulation. Within the first approximately 11 years, the rate of death accumulation is steepest (slope 0.346 per observation period), reflecting immediate predation pressure on the initial population of 250 individuals by 20 foragers operating under optimal foraging constraints. By year 22, this initial surge begins to decelerate significantly, with slope coefficients dropping from 0.438 to 0.170, suggesting that prey populations undergo initial demographic contraction or spatial redistribution that reduces encounter rates with hunters.

The accumulation continues to slow through the first century, with the rate declining to approximately 0.017 deaths per observation period by year 110, indicating that the system is approaching a demographic equilibrium where recruitment offsets mortality despite sustained hunting pressure. Between approximately years 110 and 140, the cumulative mortality stabilizes into a plateau with no significant net directional trend, suggesting that predator-prey dynamics have temporarily balanced, possibly due to hunting selectivity shifting toward higher-value targets or prey herd fragmentation reducing hunting efficiency.

A brief resumption of mortality accumulation occurs between years 140 and 160 (slope 0.003), potentially corresponding to demographic transitions as initial cohorts reach senescence at age 20 (7,300 ticks), increasing natural mortality that compounds with ongoing predation. The metric reaches its maximum value of approximately 240 cumulative deaths around year 162, after which it declines slightly to 235 by the conclusion of the 200-year simulation period. This late-stage reduction, occurring despite the cumulative nature of the metric, reflects heterogeneity in extinction outcomes across simulation runs, wherein replicates experiencing earlier prey extinction cease contributing to the cumulative count, while those with stable populations maintain mortality rates that do not offset the loss of high-mortality runs from the mean calculation. The substantial variance across runs (standard deviation 65.0) and the divergence between the mean (200.8) and median (233.0) further indicate that while most simulations experience high cumulative mortality near the maximum observed values, a significant minority experience earlier population crashes that suppress their total death counts.

Superimposed upon these long-term trends are cyclical fluctuations with a dominant period of approximately 5.5 years, consistent with the model's high seasonality parameter (0.8) affecting grass regrowth cycles. These oscillations indicate that mortality pulses track seasonal resource bottlenecks, with prey becoming more vulnerable to predation during periods of environmental stress despite the overall trend toward equilibrium.

**Implications:** The rapid early accumulation and subsequent deceleration suggest that hunting pressure exerts its strongest demographic impact immediately upon contact, but prey populations exhibit resilience mechanisms that buffer against continuous linear decline. However, the peak at year 162 and late-stage variability indicate that extinction remains a latent risk that materializes after prolonged pressure, particularly as populations age into senescence. The presence of multi-annual mortality cycles highlights the critical role of seasonal resource availability in mediating hunting efficiency, suggesting that environmental stochasticity determines whether populations stabilize or proceed to extinction under chronic hunting pressure.
