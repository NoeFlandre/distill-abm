The reproductive dynamics exhibit a pronounced three-phase trajectory characterized by initial quiescence, rapid expansion, and terminal decline. From initialization through approximately tick 700, breeding activity remains effectively absent, with aggregate mean values holding near zero. This latent period transitions sharply around tick 725 into a sustained growth phase spanning ticks 700-1670, during which mean cumulative births increase monotonically from near-zero to a maximum value of 5.600 at tick 1673. 

The expansion phase contains significant structural heterogeneity, marked by inflection points at ticks 613, 733, 903, 1121, and 1227 that denote shifts in the rate of reproductive accumulation. Short-term oscillations with a periodicity of 50 ticks are superimposed on the long-term trend, consistent with the strong seasonal forcing (seasonality = 0.8) driving vegetation availability and synchronized breeding pulses. Rolling trend analysis confirms statistically significant increasing trajectories (p < 0.001) across four consecutive windows covering ticks 696-1623, with Kendall's tau values ranging from 0.392 to 0.851.

Following the peak at tick 1673, the trajectory reverses into a decline phase extending to tick 2312, terminating at a mean value of 1.000. This counterintuitive decrease in cumulative metrics reflects selective simulation termination: populations achieving higher reproductive output (8-10 births) experience earlier extinction and exit the sample pool, while demographically stagnant populations (1-2 births) persist to the temporal limit. Rolling Mann-Kendall windows 5-6 (ticks 1624-2087) capture this attrition as significantly decreasing trends (tau = -0.495 and -0.453, p < 0.001). Additional regime shifts at change points 1325, 1850, and 2050 demarcate transitions between establishment, expansion, and collapse dynamics.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The inverse relationship between reproductive success and population persistence indicates that high recruitment rates under current parameter configurations (20 hunters, high seasonality) accelerate rather than prevent extinction. The 700-tick reproductive lag suggests populations require nearly two years to establish breeding capacity, after which rapid growth triggers overexploitation. Conservation interventions must be implemented before tick 700 or sustained throughout the expansion phase (ticks 700-1600) to decouple reproductive success from early extinction. The 50-tick oscillatory component suggests that management strategies timed to seasonal resource pulses could leverage predictable windows of vulnerability.
