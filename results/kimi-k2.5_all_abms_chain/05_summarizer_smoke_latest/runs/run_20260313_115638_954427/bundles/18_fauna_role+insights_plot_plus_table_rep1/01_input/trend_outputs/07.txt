The cumulative mortality trajectory across 40 simulation replicates reveals a characteristic saturation pattern with three distinct phases. During the initial phase (approximately steps 0–500), deaths accumulate rapidly with steep monotonic increases, rising from zero to approximately 200–230 deaths. This represents the most critical mortality window, where 80–90% of total mortality occurs. The middle transition phase (steps 500–1000) shows a marked deceleration in mortality rates as the curves begin flattening, with cumulative deaths reaching 230–240. The final plateau phase (steps 1000–2300) exhibits minimal additional mortality, with counts stabilizing between 235 and 250 deaths across replicates.

Statistical analysis confirms these visual patterns: rolling trend tests show highly significant increasing trends (tau > 0.95, p < 0.001) through step 1159, followed by periods of no significant trend interspersed with slight decreasing tendencies in later windows. The aggregate mean across all series reaches 235 deaths by termination, representing approximately 94% of the initial population of 250 animals. Change point analysis identifies critical transitions at steps 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850, corresponding to shifts in mortality regime.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The narrow intervention window (approximately the first 500 steps) suggests that conservation measures must be implemented rapidly and early in the population decline trajectory to be effective. The high terminal mortality rate (~94%) indicates that current parameter configurations (including 20 human foragers with the specified hunting efficiency and environmental seasonality of 0.8) lead to population collapse rather than sustainable coexistence. The plateauing of mortality curves after step 1000 suggests that once populations crash below critical thresholds, recovery becomes unlikely without active intervention, as the system stabilizes in a low-density or extinct equilibrium state.
