 Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based simulation designed to explore how megaherbivore populations respond to combined pressures of human hunting and environmental change . Model treats individual animals and human hunters as autonomous agents making decisions based on their immediate circumstances . Model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape, where each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for herbivores .  The number of active human foragers remains absolutely constant at 20 individuals across all 2,312 time steps of the simulation . The total energy available to forager bands exhibits a consistent monotonic decline throughout the entire simulation period . Decision makers can interpret resulting megaherbivore population trends as direct responses to consistent hunting pressure .  Trend analysis between ticks 1624-1855 shows no significant change (p = 0.127), while the final window (ticks 1856-2087) exhibits a slight decreasing trend (p < 0.001), reflecting the convergence of simulations toward terminal values and the dropout of high-accumulation runs . The rapid saturation of cumulative male mortality indicates that male megaherbivore populations face swift depletion under the modeled hunting pressure of 20 foragers .  The data indicate a critical tipping point around year 4-5 where populations transition from growth to decline . Management strategies must address hunting pressure thresholds that trigger post-peak collapse . The pronounced seasonality creates predictable windows of reproductive vulnerability that may require targeted protection periods to prevent the observed 80% reduction in birth rates .  The number of male animals initiates at 125 individuals and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction, reaching zero by step 2284 (approximately 6.26 years in the temporal calibration of the model) The decline exhibits three distinct phases demarcated by critical inflection points . Management interventions must prioritize reducing hunter numbers or foraging radii before step 390 (the first inflection point)  The simulated megaherbivore populations exhibit deterministic collapse under the specified hunting pressure, declining from an initial mean of 166.7 animals to complete extinction at tick 2311 . By tick 1000, most populations have declined to fewer than 5 individuals, and by tick 1500, the majority of runs have reached extinction . The timing of final extinction varies substantially across replicates .  The parameter configuration produces inevitable population extinction, but the extended persistence at low densities (ticks 500–2000) creates a "extinction debt" period where populations appear stable but are demographically nonviable . The 63-tick oscillation cycle suggests that seasonal resource bottlenecks exacerbate hunting pressure, particularly when grass availability is minimal .  Female megaherbivore populations exhibit unsustainable trajectories toward extinction within approximately 2,300 time steps . The current configuration of 20 human foragers and environmental parameters produces unsustainable hunting pressure . The rapid depletion within the first 350 steps—representing less than one year given the daily time-step calibration—suggests intervention strategies must be implemented within extremely narrow temporal windows . Management strategies would require significant reduction in forager numbers .  The mean vegetation coverage across the entire simulation period averages 32.66 percent, slightly below the initial allocation, while the median stabilizes at 32.97 percent . The 63-step oscillation cycle implies that herbivore populations must regularly adapt to pulsed resource shortages approximately six times per annual cycle .
