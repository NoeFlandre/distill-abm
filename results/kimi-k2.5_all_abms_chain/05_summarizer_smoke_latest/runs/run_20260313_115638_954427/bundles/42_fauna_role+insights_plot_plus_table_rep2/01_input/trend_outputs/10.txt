The simulated megaherbivore populations exhibit deterministic collapse under the specified hunting pressure, declining from an initial mean of 166.7 animals to complete extinction at tick 2311. The trajectory follows a nonlinear decay pattern characterized by three distinct phases and substantial cross-run variability in persistence times.

**Phase 1: Rapid Decline (Ticks 0–875)**
The populations undergo immediate and severe reduction. During the initial 231 ticks, the decline is steep and monotonic, with statistical tests confirming a significantly decreasing trend (Mann-Kendall tau = -1.0, slope = -0.251). The rate of loss accelerates slightly through tick 463 (slope = -0.320), then moderates but remains strongly negative through tick 695 (slope = -0.117). Critical transition points occur at ticks 125, 475, and 625, marking shifts in the velocity of population contraction. By tick 875, the mean population has collapsed to approximately 7–9 individuals, representing a 95% reduction from starting densities.

**Phase 2: Low-Density Oscillation (Ticks 876–1400)**
Below the threshold of approximately 10 animals, the population enters a prolonged period of unstable persistence. The rate of decline slows dramatically (slopes approaching -0.001 to -0.010), with superimposed oscillations having a dominant period of 63 ticks—consistent with the model's strong seasonality parameter (0.8) and 365-tick annual cycle. During this interval, the population fluctuates between peaks of 11.4 animals (tick 730) and 2.3 animals (tick 1030), with corresponding valleys showing minimal separation from peaks, indicating demographic volatility at low numbers. A temporary reversal occurs between ticks 1392–1623, where the trend shows slight increase (tau = 0.203), likely representing brief reproductive recovery or reduced encounter rates with hunters when prey become scarce.

**Phase 3: Terminal Fluctuation and Extinction (Ticks 1401–2311)**
The final phase exhibits erratic dynamics alternating between minuscule increases and decreases. Windows between ticks 1624–1855 show renewed decline, followed by slight increase from 1856–2087, but the overall trajectory remains bounded below 3 animals after tick 1030. The population mean drops below 1.5 animals (median = 1.48) for the majority of this extended terminal period. Final extinction occurs at tick 2311, though individual simulation runs terminate at variable times, with some populations persisting past tick 2000 while others crash by tick 500.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The parameter configuration produces inevitable population extinction, but the extended persistence at low densities (ticks 500–2000) creates a "extinction debt" period where populations appear stable but are demographically nonviable. The 63-tick oscillation cycle suggests that seasonal resource bottlenecks exacerbate hunting pressure, particularly when grass availability is minimal. The high variability in time-to-extinction across runs indicates that stochastic factors dominate at low population sizes, meaning management interventions would need to target the initial rapid decline phase (first 500 ticks) to be effective. Once populations drop below approximately 50 individuals, deterministic extinction becomes unavoidable regardless of subsequent hunting moratoriums, as the remaining individuals are insufficient to sustain genetic or demographic recovery against the combined pressures of predation, senescence, and environmental stochasticity.
