The simulation initiates with a total energy reserve of 12,500 units distributed among male ungulate agents. This aggregate metric follows a trajectory of deterministic collapse, ultimately declining to zero by tick 2,284 and remaining at that level through the termination of the experiment at tick 2,312.

During the initial phase spanning ticks 0 through 500, the male population energy exhibits a steep monotonic decrease characterized by pronounced seasonal oscillations at a dominant periodicity of 63 ticks. From the starting value of 12,500, the energy drops to approximately 11,608 by tick 65, followed by a rapid descent to roughly 4,000 units by tick 377. Throughout this interval, rolling Mann-Kendall tests confirm strongly significant decreasing trends with steep negative slopes ranging from -14.8 to -28.1 units per tick.

Between ticks 500 and 1,000, the depletion continues across all replicates, though with considerable inter-run variation. While some trajectories maintain residual energy pools between 500 and 2,000 units during this middle phase, the aggregate mean declines steadily. Statistical analysis identifies significant change points at ticks 125, 350, 500, 625, and 875, marking transitions in the rate of energy extraction.

From tick 1,000 through tick 1,500, most replicate trajectories converge toward complete exhaustion. However, a transient stabilization occurs between ticks 1,392 and 1,623, during which the rolling trend briefly shifts to a weak but statistically significant increase with a slope of +0.091, representing a temporary equilibrium at low energy levels rather than a genuine recovery. This is followed by a final decline to extinction.

The statistical distribution of values across the full 2,312-tick series reveals a median of 134.5 and a mean of 1,984, indicating that the male population spends the majority of the simulation duration at severely depleted energy states despite the high initial values. The prolonged terminal phase from tick 1,500 to 2,312 is characterized by near-universal absence of energy in the male subpopulation, with individual replicates exhibiting stochastic fluctuations at negligible levels (below 100 units) before final depletion to zero.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The parameter configuration tested—featuring 20 human foragers with food storage capabilities operating at a foraging radius of 6 patches on a landscape with 0.8 seasonality—generates unsustainable hunting pressure that drives the male megaherbivore subpopulation to functional extinction within approximately 2,300 time steps (equivalent to roughly 6.3 years under the 365 ticks-per-year calibration). The brief stabilization observed around tick 1,400 suggests that hunting efficiency may temporarily decline as prey become scarce, but this does not prevent ultimate collapse. Resource managers or conservation planners should note that hunting pressures at this intensity threshold result in deterministic extinction of breeding males, which would compromise population viability through mate limitation even if female populations persisted.
