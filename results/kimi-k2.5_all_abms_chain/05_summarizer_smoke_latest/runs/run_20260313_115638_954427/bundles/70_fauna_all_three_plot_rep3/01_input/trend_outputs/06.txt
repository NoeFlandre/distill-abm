.The cumulative number of births across the prey population exhibits a pronounced initial lag phase, remaining at zero for approximately the first 700 to 750 time steps. This delay corresponds to the maturation period required for the initial cohort of 250 animals to reach reproductive age, consistent with the interbirth interval parameter of 730 ticks that governs the minimum time between reproductive events.

Following this latency period, all simulation runs simultaneously exhibit discrete stepwise increases, indicating the first wave of reproductive events. The trajectories subsequently diverge across runs, with cumulative births plateauing at various levels ranging from approximately 1 to 10 births by the conclusion of the simulations at around 2,000 time steps. Several runs stabilize at intermediate values (2, 3, 4, 5, 6, and 8 births) for extended periods, reflecting the discrete nature of reproductive timing combined with stochastic variation in individual survival and reproductive success.

The maximum cumulative birth count observed reaches 10 births in one trajectory by approximately step 1,500, while other runs terminate with substantially lower reproductive output, including one run achieving only a single birth. This high variance across simulations indicates that despite identical initial conditions and parameter settings, stochastic processes—including individual hunting encounters, energy allocation decisions by foragers, and demographic stochasticity in the initial population—generate divergent reproductive outcomes. The stepwise pattern of accumulation, with plateaus lasting hundreds of time steps between increments, reflects the model's birth-spacing constraint of 730 ticks, creating distinct reproductive cohorts rather than continuous recruitment.

The relatively low absolute number of cumulative births (maximum 10) from an initial population of 250 animals over approximately 5.5 simulated years suggests that mortality pressures—including the constant presence of 20 human foragers operating with optimal foraging strategies, external predation, and potential resource limitations from seasonal vegetation fluctuations—effectively suppress population growth despite the absence of hunter mortality. The asynchrony in stepwise increases across runs after the initial reproductive wave indicates that individual-level variation in survival and energetic status drives heterogeneity in reproductive timing. For decision makers, these results suggest that even moderate hunting pressure (20 foragers) combined with environmental seasonality can constrain megaherbivore population recovery, with stochastic effects creating high uncertainty in population trajectories that complicates predictive management for conservation or hunting regulation.
