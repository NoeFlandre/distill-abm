The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The population of human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 individuals throughout the simulation duration of 2,312 time steps. The population functions as a fixed parameter rather than a dynamic variable, providing unwavering hunting pressure on the megaherbivore populations. The maximum observed value of 120.17 occurs at step 1876, after which the cumulative count remains effectively constant. The consistent upper bound near 120 males across runs suggests a hard demographic limit imposed by the initial prey population of 250 animals. Some simulations reach the plateau as early as step 500 while others continue accumulating fatalities beyond step 2000. The late phase beyond step 1500 displays minor oscillations superimposed on the plateau, potentially corresponding to seasonal hunting cycles or sporadic encounters with the remaining elusive individuals. At approximately 1876 time steps, the cumulative death count stabilizes within a narrow range of 237 to 240, exhibiting negligible net growth for extended periods. This plateau indicates that active mortality events have become infrequent, potentially representing a quasi-equilibrium where remnant prey populations persist at densities too low to support significant hunting returns. Population commences at 125 individuals and declines continuously to extinction by step 2312. Rapid decline from 125 to fewer than 20 individuals represents a loss of over 80 percent of the initial population. This rapid collapse suggests intense hunting pressure acting upon the initially abundant and spatially distributed prey. The total energy available in the male animal population begins at 12,500 units and declines progressively toward zero over the course of approximately 2,300 simulation steps. The initial phase is characterized by a precipitous decrease, with the population losing energy at a rate of roughly 14.8 to 28.1 units per step. This rapid depletion reduces the total male energy from the initial 12,500 to approximately 4,000 by step 377. The dominant oscillation period of 63 steps suggests demographic cycles in herd reproduction create modest energetic pulses that hunters partially exploit. A second inflection occurs around step 500, after which total energy drops from approximately 2,000 units to below 500 by step 625. This accelerated secondary decline corresponds with the onset of environmental constraints. Icks correspond to small-scale prey population recoveries, potentially driven by birth spacing intervals (set at 730 steps, or approximately two years) allowing limited recruitment in isolated herds, or by temporary satiation of hunter populations. The dominant trend resumes its decline.
