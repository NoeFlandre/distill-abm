The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The population of human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 individuals throughout the simulation duration of 2,312 time steps. The population functions as a fixed parameter rather than a dynamic variable, providing unwavering hunting pressure on the megaherbivore populations. The maximum observed value of 120.17 occurs at step 1876, after which the cumulative count remains effectively constant. The consistent upper bound near 120 males across runs suggests a hard demographic limit imposed by the initial prey population of 250 animals. The system reaches a saturation point at approximately step 1,826, achieving a maximum cumulative kill count of 123.7 females. The rapid depletion of reproductive females demonstrates hunting pressure generates a demographic collapse that outpaces general population reduction. Management strategies must prioritize the protection of nursing females specifically, as their loss creates an extinction vortex that is not immediately visible in total population counts. Decision makers should focus on reducing early-stage mortality during this initial 725-step period. The male megaherbivore population initiates at 125 individuals and undergoes a pronounced decline throughout the simulation. During the first 500 time steps, the population experiences a steep collapse, plummeting from 125 to roughly 10 individuals. The population enters a terminal phase, dropping below 5 individuals and continuing to erode toward zero. The megaherbivore population begins at approximately 167 individuals and follows a deterministic trajectory toward complete extinction, reaching zero by step 2311. By step 700, the population has collapsed to fewer than 10 individuals on average, entering a prolonged phase of low-density persistence characterized by high volatility. Management interventions must occur before populations drop below approximately 10 individuals, as recovery probability becomes negligible once the system enters the low density oscillatory phase. The system exhibits high variance across the 40 simulation runs (standard deviation of 3,478), indicating substantial heterogeneity in extinction trajectories. The high variance between simulation runs implies that extinction outcomes are sensitive to stochastic demographic and hunting events, meaning extinction can occur within a wide time window even under identical initial conditions. The multi-phase decline pattern indicates that male megafauna populations under sustained hunting pressure experience an initial rapid collapse followed by a prolonged terminal decline. The model demonstrates that sustained hunting pressure from a non-declining hunter population inevitably drives megaherbivore populations to extinction. The grassland ecosystem demonstrates substantial resilience under current parameters, consistently recovering from severe depletion events within 100–150 steps. The recurring 63-step oscillation cycle and significant annual depletion event observed at step 365 indicate that the system operates with limited buffer capacity during seasonal constraints. Management strategies should account for the potential for rapid resource depletion during critical periods.
