The female megaherbivore population initiates at 125 individuals and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction over the course of 2,312 time steps. During the initial phase from step 0 to approximately step 475, the population undergoes a rapid and steep decline, with an inflection point in the trend dynamics occurring around step 305. By step 475, the population has typically fallen below 20 individuals across the majority of simulation trajectories, representing a loss of over 80 percent of the initial population.

Between steps 475 and 875, the rate of decline moderates but remains consistently negative, with notable change points in the trend structure occurring at steps 600 and 875. During this transitional period, the population drifts from approximately 20 individuals down to single digits. The median population size drops to 1 individual, indicating that in the majority of simulation runs, the female population has been reduced to the minimum viable level, while the mean remains elevated at 16.48 due to a subset of trajectories maintaining small populations longer than others.

From step 875 through the conclusion at step 2,312, the population enters a final extinction phase, gradually declining from low single digits to zero. This phase is characterized by high variability and brief, localized deviations from the downward trend. Specifically, between steps 1,160 and 1,391, the rolling trend analysis detects a statistically significant but weak increasing tendency (tau=0.093), likely representing temporary stabilization or minor reproductive pulses, before the resumption of the decreasing trend. Small oscillatory fluctuations with a dominant period of approximately 63 time steps are superimposed on the decline, with minor peaks occurring around steps 710-730, 800-820, and 900-1000, though these are insufficient to reverse the overall trajectory. By step 2,310, the final change point is reached, and the population achieves complete extinction at step 2,312.

For decision makers, these results demonstrate that the current configuration of 20 human foragers generates sufficient predation pressure to drive the female megaherbivore population to extinction within approximately 6.3 years, given the temporal scaling of 365 ticks per year. The rapid initial decline indicates that hunting pressure immediately exceeds the population's reproductive capacity, while the lack of recovery from low numbers suggests that demographic stochasticity and potential Allee effects prevent population persistence once numbers fall below critical thresholds. This implies that sustainable coexistence would require reducing hunter numbers below the current threshold of 20, modifying hunter foraging behavior to reduce encounter rates with females, or enhancing habitat productivity to increase the population's intrinsic growth rate and compensate for hunting mortality.
