The cumulative mortality of male prey organisms demonstrates a consistent pattern of rapid initial accumulation followed by asymptotic stabilization across replicated simulation runs. Beginning from zero at initialization, kill counts increase steeply during the first 400 to 600 time steps, with most runs accumulating between 80 and 100 male deaths within this interval. This active hunting phase transitions into a saturation period where accumulation rates decelerate markedly between steps 600 and 1000, with individual trajectories displaying irregular stepwise increments of decreasing frequency.

Asymptotic levels vary across replicates, with terminal cumulative counts ranging from approximately 85 to 125 individuals, concentrated primarily between 105 and 120 kills. The time required to reach these maximum values exhibits substantial heterogeneity, with some simulations achieving terminal counts by step 700 while others continue accumulating kills through step 2000 and beyond. Notably, runs demonstrating earlier saturation tend to stabilize at lower asymptotic values, whereas those sustaining accumulation longer generally achieve higher final counts approaching the estimated initial male population size.

Within the context of the MHPM's optimal foraging framework, these dynamics indicate that forager bands, operating under the specified energy storage and processing cost parameters (males: food value 30, processing cost 6; females: food value 27, processing cost 3), initially prioritize male prey despite higher handling costs, exhausting this demographic subset through sustained predation pressure. The asymptotic behavior suggests functional extinction of male prey within the foraging radius, forcing hunters to either switch to alternative prey categories or face energetic constraints as encounter rates with males decline to zero. The inter-run variability in extinction timing reflects stochastic fluctuations in herd movement patterns, hunter-prey encounter probabilities, and the spatial distribution of remaining prey.

Implications for decision makers: The inevitable convergence toward male population depletion across all replicates demonstrates that moderate processing penalties do not sufficiently buffer high-value prey against extinction when hunters employ energy-maximizing strategies. Resource managers should expect sexually dimorphic prey populations to experience male-biased mortality leading to effective unisexual extinction within 700–2000 time steps under comparable hunting pressures, necessitating intervention timelines that account for this sex-specific vulnerability. The unpredictability in exact extinction timing suggests that reliance on average projections carries substantial risk, requiring conservative buffers when establishing hunting quotas or protected area designations.
