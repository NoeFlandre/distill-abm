The cumulative mortality of male ungulates inflicted by human foragers exhibits a saturating trajectory characterized by three distinct phases across the temporal progression of the simulation. Initially, from the onset through approximately the first 850 time steps, the accumulated kill count increases rapidly from zero but with progressively diminishing marginal returns, transitioning through distinct behavioral regimes marked by change points at roughly 125-step intervals (125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850). During this establishment phase, the rate of accumulation declines systematically from an initial steep slope of 0.173 to 0.008 kills per time step, reflecting the efficient initial exploitation of the naive prey population by foragers operating under optimal foraging strategies, followed by increasing difficulty in locating remaining males as the population structure adjusts.

Following this initial accumulation, the system enters an extended plateau phase spanning roughly from step 850 to 1850, where the cumulative kill count stabilizes with high variability around a median value of approximately 116.5 individuals. This saturation reflects an equilibrium between hunting pressure and prey population dynamics, where the continued removal of males is partially offset by population recruitment and the energetic constraints of foragers. Minor resurgences in accumulation rates occur within this plateau (notably around steps 1392-1623), potentially corresponding to seasonal windows of increased male vulnerability or demographic pulses in prey availability given the high environmental seasonality configured in the model.

The trajectory reaches its zenith at approximately step 1876 with a maximum cumulative kill count of 120.2 males, after which the metric exhibits a declining trend through the terminal phase of the simulation (steps 1856-2087 and beyond), concluding at a final value of 117.5. This late-phase decrease suggests that as prey populations become depleted in individual simulation runs, the cross-simulation average accumulation rate drops below maintenance levels, or that selective pressure on males has reduced their availability to the point where hunting success diminishes significantly. Superimposed on these broad trends is a regular oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of 63 time steps, particularly pronounced in the final 150 steps of the simulation, likely driven by strong seasonal resource fluctuations that synchronize forager hunting success with annual cycles of prey aggregation and dispersal.

The distribution of outcomes across the 80 simulation runs shows substantial variance (standard deviation = 32.5), with the mean (100.4) falling below the median (116.5), indicating that while most simulations resulted in high cumulative male mortality, a subset of runs experienced significantly lower hunting success, possibly due to stochastic variation in initial forager-prey spatial distributions or demographic stochasticity in the small initial prey population.
