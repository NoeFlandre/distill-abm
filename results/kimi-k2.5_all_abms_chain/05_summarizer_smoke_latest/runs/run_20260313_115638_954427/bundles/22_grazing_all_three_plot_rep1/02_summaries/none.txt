**Context and Goals**

This agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Unlike the original RAGE model which examined common property scenarios where herders move livestock across shared rangelands, this extension focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods.

The simulation tracks the interaction between vegetation condition and household decisions about livestock numbers over time. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors.

The central purpose is understanding how these different ways of adapting to changing conditions influence pasture health, herd sizes, and the persistence of households. The model particularly examines how farmers handle uncertainty and incomplete information when making stocking decisions, represented through individual risk dispositions that affect how they perceive and react to environmental feedback.

**Key Parameters**

*Population and Spatial Setup:*
The **number-households** parameter establishes how many farming families occupy the landscape, which can range from 1 to 100 families, with this study using 60 households. Each household begins with **livestock-init** set at 90 animals. The simulation runs for **timesteps** set at 100 years. Farmers begin the simulation positioned on their designated land parcels, with **start-on-homepatch?** set to True. Each farmer can observe conditions within a **knowledge-radius** that ranges from 0 to 5 patches, set here at 1 patch distance.

*Behavioral Type Configuration:*
The study examines three specific learning strategies with equal representation: **number-E-RO** (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb), **number-E-LBD** (learning-by-doing), and **number-E-RO-SL1** (social learning with single-loop adaptation), each set to 20 households. The **homog-behav-types?** parameter set to True ensures each type is evaluated in uniform groups. The original RAGE model behavioral types are inactive in this extension: **number-SAT**, **number-TRAD**, **number-MAX**, and **number-random** are all set to 0. The default reference **behavioral-type** is set to E-RO.

*Grazing Management Practices:*
Formal pasture resting is disabled with **resting?** set to False. The threshold for initiating rest periods, **resting-threshold**, ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0.1. The **intrinsic-preference** for livestock relative to other values ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0. Similarly, **social-susceptibility** to neighbor influence ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0, indicating decisions rely on other factors.

*Original Model Parameters:*
Several parameters from the foundational RAGE model are retained. The **satisficing-threshold** for satisfied decision-making ranges from 0 to 100 and is set at 0. The **satisficing-trials** parameter, ranging from 0 to 100, is set at 21. These control how the original satisficer type would search for acceptable solutions, though this type is not active in the current setup.

*Ecological Conditions:*
Rainfall is applied uniformly across the landscape with **global-rain?** set to True. The average rainfall **rain-mean** is set at 200 units annually. Year-to-year variation in precipitation is controlled by **rain-std** set at 100, indicating significant variability in annual rainfall totals. Vegetation growth responds through two rates: **gr1** set at 0.5 and **gr2** set at 0.1. The parameter **w** from the original vegetation model is set at 0.8, influencing how pastures recover from grazing. Livestock reproduction is not active in this extension, with **b** set to 0.

*Learning and Risk Mechanisms:*
The extension module is activated with **extension-model?** set to True. Uncertainty handling is engaged with **risk-mode?** set to True. Each agent begins with an initial risk disposition **risk-att-init** set at 0.5, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance. The ability to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False, confining agents to single-loop learning within their assigned type.

The mean household initial risk attitude remains constant throughout the 100-step simulation period, with values fixed at their starting levels from step 0 through step 100. Individual household values are distributed across a range spanning approximately -0.11 to +0.18, with multiple distinct constant levels observed across the population. These invariant trajectories confirm that the metric captures a static baseline characteristic assigned at model initialization, representing each social learning agent's predetermined disposition to deviate from recommended livestock numbers when confronting uncertainty about pasture conditions.

The observed spread from negative to positive values indicates substantial heterogeneity in risk preferences among the simulated farming households. Households with negative initial risk attitudes demonstrate inherent conservatism in their decision-making frameworks, predisposing them toward cautious stocking strategies that adhere closely to ecological recommendations. Conversely, households with positive initial values possess baseline propensities for greater experimentation, rendering them more likely to maintain livestock numbers that substantially deviate from advised levels when interpreting environmental feedback and neighbor behavior. This diversity reflects realistic variation in how smallholder farmers psychologically approach uncertainty regarding land management under the social learning regime.

For decision makers, the stability of these initial risk dispositions throughout the simulation suggests that baseline risk profiles act as persistent filters through which all learning and adaptation occur. Since risk attitudes do not shift over time in this model, agricultural extension services and policy interventions should prioritize early identification of farmer risk types and tailor communication strategies accordingly. Risk-averse farmers may require explicit demonstration of safety margins and gradual adjustment protocols before modifying stocking rates, while risk-tolerant farmers might benefit from boundary-setting mechanisms to prevent overstocking. Furthermore, the wide dispersion in initial attitudes implies that community-level pasture management outcomes will vary significantly based on the specific distribution of risk types within local farming populations, necessitating community-specific rather than uniform policy approaches.

The mean household deviation from recommended livestock levels initiates at values near zero, with most households clustered between -0.1 and 0.2 during the first 10 steps. During this initial period, the system exhibits high volatility as households begin processing environmental feedback and neighbor observations within their defined knowledge radius.

Between steps 15 and 35, the population undergoes rapid divergence, with deviation values spreading across a wide spectrum from -0.2 to 0.8. A substantial portion of households increases their livestock levels well above recommendations, reaching peak deviations of approximately 0.75 to 0.8 by step 25-30. Conversely, other households maintain moderate deviations between 0.2 and 0.6, while a smaller subset transitions toward negative deviations, indicating conservative stocking below recommended levels.

From approximately step 40 onward, the population segregates into distinct stable clusters characterized by persistent deviation levels. By step 60, the system stabilizes into multiple distinct behavioral regimes: a high-deviation cluster sustaining levels of 0.7 to 0.8, several intermediate clusters maintaining deviations between 0.2 and 0.6, and a conservative cluster stabilizing at negative deviations between -0.3 and -0.1. Notably, a subset of households exhibits sharp declines in deviation around steps 40-50, transitioning from moderate positive values to sustained negative deviations of approximately -0.35.

The long-term dynamics (steps 60-100) demonstrate remarkable persistence of these clustered patterns, with minimal convergence or drift between groups. This suggests that early learning experiences create path-dependent trajectories that lock households into distinct risk-taking behaviors regarding stocking decisions. The presence of sustained high-deviation clusters indicates that without external intervention, a significant portion of farmers will consistently exceed recommended stocking rates, potentially accelerating pasture degradation. Conversely, the emergence of a conservatively managed cluster demonstrates that under certain learning trajectories, households voluntarily adopt under-stocking strategies that may enhance long-term pasture resilience despite reduced immediate livestock productivity.

The mean reserve biomass begins at approximately 90,000 units at initialization. Over the initial 10 to 15 years, it declines rapidly, reaching a trough between 80,000 and 88,000 units around years 15 to 30. This initial degradation suggests that households, despite their varied learning strategies, initially maintain livestock levels that exceed the carrying capacity of their private pastures, leading to a drawdown of vegetation reserves as the system adjusts from its starting configuration.

Following this decline, a sharp recovery phase emerges between years 40 and 60, with biomass increasing steeply from approximately 85,000 units to levels between 110,000 and 125,000 units. This rapid accrual indicates that learning mechanisms—whether through personal experience (learning-by-doing) or observation of neighbors (social learning)—successfully trigger reductions in stocking rates or other adaptive management changes that allow vegetation recovery. The inflection point around year 40 to 50 marks the transition from resource depletion to regeneration, as accumulated experiential knowledge enables households to better align their decisions with ecological feedback.

After year 70, the trajectory stabilizes, though outcomes diverge considerably across simulation runs, with long-term equilibria ranging from approximately 111,000 to 128,000 units by year 100. This divergence, despite identical initial conditions and equal representation of behavioral types, indicates that stochastic elements—particularly the high annual rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200) and individual risk dispositions—generate path-dependent outcomes where historical sequences of weather events and idiosyncratic learning experiences create persistent differences in vegetation conditions. The highest equilibrium values suggest scenarios where effective learning coincided with favorable rainfall sequences, while lower plateaus indicate instances where conservative stocking decisions or adverse climatic conditions limited biomass accumulation.

The wide range of final outcomes carries significant implications for decision makers. First, the 10- to 15-year lag between implementation of learning strategies and measurable ecosystem recovery suggests that policy interventions in grazing systems require extended evaluation periods, as short-term assessments may capture only the initial degradation phase. Second, the substantial variance in long-term biomass levels—spanning roughly 17,000 units or approximately 15% of the mean—demonstrates that uniform policy approaches may yield heterogeneous results across similarly structured farming communities, necessitating flexible adaptive management frameworks that account for local context and historical contingencies. Finally, the fact that all learning strategies eventually achieve higher biomass than initial conditions (even accounting for variability) suggests that active experimentation and social observation contribute to improved resource outcomes, though the specific trajectory and final equilibrium remain sensitive to environmental stochasticity and individual risk preferences.

The mean green biomass initiates at approximately 45,000 units, exhibiting relative stability through the first 10 steps with a minor peak near 45,500. This reduction indicates that initial livestock levels exceed sustainable carrying capacities before adaptive learning mechanisms take effect.

A rapid recovery ensues between steps 40 and 70, characterized by a steep sigmoid increase as biomass climbs toward values between 55,000 and 64,000. This acceleration suggests that farmers successfully adjust stocking rates in response to observed pasture deterioration, allowing vegetation to regenerate.

Beyond step 70, growth rates decelerate markedly, with trajectories diverging to stabilize at distinct equilibrium levels by step 100. Final values span a considerable range from roughly 55,000 to 64,000, representing increases of 22% to 42% above initial conditions. The majority of simulations converge between 58,000 and 63,000, though several persist at the lower end of the spectrum.

The substantial variation in long-term outcomes implies that interactions between learning strategies and stochastic rainfall variability produce heterogeneous sustainability results. Decision makers should note that while all adaptive approaches eventually prevent terminal degradation, the specific learning mechanism employed and the management of climate uncertainty critically determine whether the system achieves high productivity (near 64,000) or merely moderate recovery (near 55,000). Furthermore, the consistent initial decline across all scenarios suggests that anticipatory stocking restrictions may be necessary to prevent the early degradation phase, regardless of subsequent learning efficacy.

The mean livestock holdings across households, including animals designated for destocking, begins at 90 animals per household and undergoes a rapid decline over the first 20 time steps, falling to approximately 40-45 animals. This initial collapse reflects a system-wide adjustment from initial overstocking levels as pastures face heavy grazing pressure and households are forced to reduce herd sizes substantially to prevent total degradation.

Between time steps 20 and 40, the system enters a consolidation phase where livestock levels stabilize at the reduced baseline of roughly 40-45 animals, representing the minimum viable herd size under degraded pasture conditions. During this period, variation across simulation runs remains relatively constrained, indicating that initial ecological pressures dominate decision-making regardless of learning strategy.

From time step 40 onward, the trajectories diverge markedly. A subset of outcomes maintains livestock levels at the reduced baseline, stabilizing between 40 and 43 animals through the end of the simulation. The majority of trajectories recover partially, settling into a middle range of 45-50 animals by step 100. Notably, select trajectories demonstrate substantial recovery, reaching approximately 58-60 animals and 61-62 animals respectively by the conclusion of the 100-step period.

This divergence indicates that while all households face identical initial ecological forcing that mandates destocking, the capacity for herd recovery and subsequent growth varies systematically with decision-making approach after the initial crisis. The clustering of outcomes around 40-43, 45-50, and 58-62 animals suggests the three learning strategies produce categorically different long-term stocking equilibria, with the most successful strategies supporting herd sizes approximately 50% larger than the least successful approaches by year 100.

The low variance in outcomes during the initial decline (steps 0-20) followed by increasing divergence (steps 40-100) suggests that learning mechanisms become critical determinants of system performance only after households have experienced sufficient feedback from their management decisions. Conservative rule-based approaches appear to lock households into low-level equilibria around 40 animals, while adaptive learning mechanisms enable identification and maintenance of higher sustainable stocking rates between 58-62 animals despite environmental variability.

For decision makers in grazing system management, these findings indicate that promoting active learning strategies among herders—whether through peer observation or experiential adaptation—can significantly improve long-term livelihood outcomes compared to static decision rules. However, the persistence of low-equilibrium outcomes in some simulations highlights that without effective learning mechanisms, households may remain trapped in poverty trajectories following initial environmental shocks. Extension services should prioritize facilitating social learning networks or structured experimentation, as these approaches demonstrate the capacity to rebuild herd assets to levels 20-50% higher than non-adaptive strategies within a 100-year planning horizon.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The systematic increase in inequality across all learning strategies suggests that policy interventions targeting equitable livestock health outcomes should focus on early-stage support mechanisms before disparities become entrenched. The wide variation in final inequality levels (0.030 to 0.095) indicates that stochastic environmental factors play a significant role in determining which households succeed, implying that risk-sharing mechanisms or insurance products could help buffer households against early adverse events that trigger downward spirals. Since social learning does not appear to reduce inequality compared to individual learning strategies, extension services should be cautious about assuming that information diffusion alone will lead to equitable outcomes; targeted support for struggling households may be necessary regardless of their access to neighbor knowledge. The persistence of inequality even with learning mechanisms suggests that structural factors such as initial pasture quality or location may be more determinative than adaptive capacity, warranting investigation into spatial targeting of soil and water conservation investments.

Implications for decision makers: The rapid initial uptake suggests that social learning mechanisms can disseminate quickly through smallholder communities when communication channels exist, potentially facilitating rapid spread of sustainable grazing practices. However, the substantial variance in final adoption rates indicates that reliance on organic social diffusion may leave 30–70% of households without exposure to socially transmitted strategies, depending on local stochastic conditions. Policymakers should anticipate this heterogeneity and consider complementary outreach mechanisms to reach spatially isolated or less socially connected households that fail to adopt through natural network dynamics.
