The cumulative count of male prey killed initiates at zero and exhibits a saturating growth pattern across all simulation runs. This early acceleration reflects high encounter probabilities between the fixed cohort of 20 human foragers and the initial ungulate population of 250 animals, during which hunters target males preferentially due to their marginally higher nutritional return (30 energy units versus 27 for females) despite higher processing costs.

Between steps 500 and 800, the accumulation rate decelerates substantially as the cumulative totals approach asymptotic values. By step 800, most simulations have recorded between 100 and 120 male kills, representing the transition from active hunting to population depletion. The final saturation phase stabilizes at values ranging from approximately 110 to 125 males killed, with individual runs reaching these plateaus anywhere from step 800 to step 2,000. Given the initial ungulate population parameters, these terminal values approximate the expected total initial male population, indicating near-complete demographic removal of breeding males within the simulation timeframe.

Variability across simulation runs remains minimal during the initial 300 steps, with cumulative kill counts tracking within narrow bands, but increases markedly during the approach to saturation. Early-plateauing runs stabilize around 110 kills by step 800, while late-plateauing runs continue accumulating kills until approximately step 1,500 or beyond, reaching maximum values of 122 to 125. This divergence reflects stochastic variation in herd movement patterns across the 211 by 211 patch landscape, localized vegetation dynamics under strong seasonal forcing (seasonality parameter 0.8), and the specific spatial distribution of hunter encounters as male density declines.

The universal trend toward saturation suggests that the hunting pressure generated by 20 immortal foragers utilizing food storage and operating with a 6-patch foraging radius is sufficient to functionally extirpate the male subpopulation. The processing cost differential (6 units for males versus 3 for females) does not sufficiently offset the nutritional gain to prevent preferential male harvest, while the immortality of foragers ensures sustained predation pressure regardless of prey availability.

For decision makers: The consistent depletion of males within approximately 800 to 2,000 daily time steps (2.2 to 5.5 years) across all stochastic realizations indicates that current hunting parameters create a high-risk scenario for megaherbivore population viability. The rapid removal of the entire breeding male cohort would eliminate reproductive capacity in polygynous species, potentially triggering population collapse even if female survival remains high. Conservation strategies should prioritize reducing hunter density below the critical threshold of 20 individuals per comparable landscape area, or implementing seasonal restrictions that interrupt the continuous foraging pattern enabled by food storage mechanisms. The model suggests that without intervention, anthropogenic hunting pressure alone can demographically eliminate male megaherbivores within a few years, independent of environmental stochasticity variations.
