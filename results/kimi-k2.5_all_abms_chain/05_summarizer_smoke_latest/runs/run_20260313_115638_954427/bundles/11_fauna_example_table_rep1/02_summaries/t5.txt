The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation period. The constancy reflects the model configuration where foragers are immortal and do not reproduce, maintaining the initial population of 20 hunters established at the start of the simulation. The cumulative mortality of female prey initiates at zero and undergoes rapid accumulation during the first 375 ticks, reaching approximately 85 individuals. This initial surge corresponds to the deployment of 20 forager agents exploiting the founding prey population of 250 ungulates. The trajectory exhibits inflection points at 1204, 1336, and 1487, suggesting progressive slowing in hunting success. Around tick 613, the first inflection point marks the onset of measurable reproductive accumulation. The metric exhibits sustained growth punctuated by regular oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 50 ticks. This trajectory reverses dramatically, with statistically significant decreasing trends dominating from tick 1624 through 2087. Moderates between ticks 600 and 875 entering a prolonged phase of gradual reduction characterized by high volatility. Superimposed upon this trend are cyclical fluctuations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks. These oscillations generate periodic peaks and troughs as recruitment attempts align with resource availability cycles. Temporary demographic recoveries interrupt the decline at two intervals: first between approximately ticks 1160 and 1390. The final phase from tick 1,624 to 2,284 exhibits terminal decline to zero, representing population collapse and functional extinction of males. The eventual disappearance by tick 2,284, just 28 ticks before the simulation termination indicates that hunting pressure ultimately overwhelmed all demographic compensatory mechanisms. The combination of sustained hunter presence, optimal foraging behavior, and environmental seasonality ultimately overwhelms the megaherbivore population's replacement capacity.
