This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence. The simulation outcomes reveal a continuous accumulation of whole milk selections across the 1974–2005 transition period. The trajectory of adoption follows a consistently positive but decelerating growth pattern. Decision makers should recognize that initial health and environmental messaging drives rapid early adoption, but the middle plateau indicates that habit persistence and social network structures create substantial resistance. The simulation demonstrates a perfectly linear accumulation of milk consumption choices from 1974 through 2005, beginning at zero and increasing by exactly 1,000 decisions per annual time step to reach a total of 32,000 choices by the end of the simulation period. The trajectory reflects a classic innovation diffusion process within the agent population driven by the interaction of social influence, habit formation, and perception mechanisms embedded in the Watts-Strogatz network topology. Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk began the simulation period at approximately 5.3 units per person per week. During the first nine time steps, the metric underwent sharp, statistically significant increases, with weekly consumption rising rapidly to approach 1265 units by time step 11. Consumption resumed a significant upward trajectory, ultimately reaching approximately 1456.6 units. The habit-driven weighting factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption begins at a neutral baseline of 1.0 in 1974, indicating no initial habitual preference for alternative varieties over whole milk. From roughly 1979 through the late 1980s, the rate of habit formation accelerates significantly, with sharp increases in the habitual weighting applied to alternative milk choices.
