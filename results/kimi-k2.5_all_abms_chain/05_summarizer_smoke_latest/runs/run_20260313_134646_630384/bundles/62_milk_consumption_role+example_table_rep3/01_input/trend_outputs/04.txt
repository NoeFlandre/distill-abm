The total number of selections of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at 0 in 1974 and increases continuously throughout the 31-year period, reaching approximately 25,644 by the final time step. This trajectory reflects a strictly monotonic adoption pattern with no periods of decline or fluctuation, indicating a unidirectional transition away from whole milk dominance.

The adoption dynamics exhibit two distinct phases. During the initial phase, spanning roughly the first ten to fifteen time steps (1974 through the late 1980s), the rate of increase accelerates steadily. The slope of adoption grows from approximately 201 selections per time step in the earliest intervals to over 900 selections per time step by the midpoint of the series. This acceleration suggests an initial period of slow diffusion followed by rapid uptake as health and environmental perceptions gain traction within the consumer population.

In the second phase, from approximately the mid-1990s through 2005, the growth rate stabilizes at roughly 935 selections per time step. This constant linear increase indicates that the adoption process has entered a steady-state regime where the forces driving change—social influence, evolving perceptions, and habit formation—have achieved a temporary equilibrium with the residual inertia of incumbent consumption patterns. The absence of saturation effects within the observed timeframe suggests that the market transition remained incomplete by 2005, with potential for continued growth in alternative milk selections beyond the simulation horizon.
