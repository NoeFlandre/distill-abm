The model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. It captures how individual decision-making factors collectively drive macro-level market shifts. Success is measured by the model's ability to reproduce the historical crossover point where skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption surpassed whole milk consumption. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent initiates at a minimal level of 5.29 units, reflecting the 1974 baseline where whole milk maintained dominant habitual preference. During the initial ten time steps, consumption decreases rapidly, with particularly steep drops occurring between steps 0–4 and 5–9. The trajectory contains eight distinct inflection points, particularly notable at steps 5, 13, 16, 19, 24, and 25. The total number of milk choices accumulates linearly throughout the simulation, beginning at zero and increasing at a constant rate of 1,000 choices per time step to reach 32,000 by the final time step. This trajectory reflects a strictly monotonic adoption pattern with no periods of decline or fluctuation, indicating a unidirectional transition away from whole milk dominance. The mean quantity of whole milk consumed begins at approximately 2,655 units at the outset of the simulation. The mean habit factor applied to whole milk consumption decisions initializes at 1.0000 and exhibits a consistent, monotonic upward trajectory across the 33 time steps. The convergence toward high consumption levels by the final time step suggests that social norms have fully shifted, with social norms consolidating the new consumption pattern as the established behavioral standard. The mean deviation between agents' held health values and their actual milk choices begins at 0.0000 and exhibits a statistically significant monotonic increase throughout the entire simulation period, reaching 0.7251 by the final time step. This trajectory indicates a steadily widening gap between consumers' health-oriented preferences and their consumption behaviors over the 31-year period. The complete absence of detected valleys or downward trends confirms that the value-action gap never systematically closes during the simulation period. This suggests that the psychological mechanisms governing habit maintenance and justification successfully buffer agents from full behavioral alignment with their evolving health perceptions until beyond the final time step.
