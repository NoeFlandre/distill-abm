model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005 . individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions . each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers . the trajectory reflects the progressive erosion of whole milk's market dominance as consumers transition toward skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives . a gentle resumption of decline occurs between steps 20 and 24, though at a markedly slower pace than observed in the earliest phase . the accumulation curve exhibits distinct inflection points at time steps 1, 7 and 31, indicating subtle shifts in the rate of whole milk adoption . the early inflexion at step 1 likely corresponds to the transition from initialization to active decision-making, while the influence at step 7 suggests a modest acceleration or deceleration in accumulation as agents begin processing health and environmental attributes . consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at a minimal level of approximately 5.3 units at the initial time step, reflecting the historical dominance of whole milk in 1974 . over the subsequent 32 time steps representing the period through 2005, consumption increases dramatically to reach approximately 1456.6 units . the 15-step oscillatory pattern suggests cyclical renewals of habit reinforcement . these cycles imply that individual agents may transition to alternative milk varieties . the aggregate habitual commitment among remaining whole milk consumers experiences rhythmic intensification . despite growing health consciousness throughout the population, the deviation approaches its maximum value of 0.7251 . the high justification parameter (0.61) likely enables these agents to rationalize their habitual choices without behavioral change .
