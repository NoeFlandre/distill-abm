The simulation of the heterogeneous population with E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types reveals a characteristic pattern of initial decline followed by sustained recovery and growth in reserve biomass over the 100-step period. Beginning at 90,000 units, reserve biomass decreases during the first 26 steps, reaching a minimum of 84,114 units. This early reduction reflects the initial grazing pressure from livestock with starting herd sizes of 90 animals per household on private pasture patches.

Following the minimum at step 26, the system stabilizes for approximately six steps with no clear directional trend. A recovery phase initiates around step 33, marked by an inflection point at step 48 that signals a fundamental shift in system dynamics. The recovery accelerates significantly between steps 44 and 54, during which reserve biomass increases at a rate of approximately 1,909 units per step—the fastest growth rate observed in the simulation.

While the rate of increase gradually decelerates in subsequent periods (dropping to approximately 643 units per step during steps 55-65, then 93 units per step during steps 66-76, and continuing to slow to roughly 5-20 units per step in the final intervals), the upward trajectory persists throughout the remainder of the simulation. By step 100, reserve biomass reaches 121,073 units, representing an increase of 34% above the initial value and 44% above the minimum level. The average reserve biomass across the full period is 103,366 units.

This trajectory indicates that the adaptive learning mechanisms embedded in the behavioral types—particularly learning-by-doing and social learning processes—enable households to adjust their grazing strategies over time. The system demonstrates resilience by not only recovering from initial degradation but actually achieving reserve biomass levels substantially higher than the starting condition, suggesting that adaptive co-management approaches in private property grazing systems can improve long-term pasture conditions beyond baseline states.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The results suggest that encouraging learning-based behavioral adaptation among smallholder livestock keepers can lead to improved pasture conditions over time, even when initial grazing pressure causes degradation. The recovery pattern indicates that patience is required during the initial adaptation phase (approximately 25-30 steps), after which the benefits of learning become apparent. The sustained growth beyond initial levels demonstrates that investment in social learning infrastructure and adaptive management capacity can yield long-term ecological benefits that exceed static management approaches. Heterogeneous populations mixing baseline, learning-by-doing, and social learning behaviors appear capable of self-organizing toward improved resource conditions without external intervention.
