Model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. Individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers. Over the 31-year period, consumption undergoes a substantial monotonic decline, terminating near 253 units by the final time step. The trajectory exhibits distinct phases of deceleration punctuated by temporary plateaus. This early decline accelerates further between time steps five and nine. The series enters a prolonged deceleration phase lasting through approximately time step 31. Whole milk selections continue to accumulate but at progressively diminishing rates, declining from roughly 71 selections per time step in the middle windows (time steps 10–14). The cumulative count exhibits oscillatory fluctuations superimposed on the underlying growth trend, with detectable periodicity around 15 time steps. Between steps ten and fourteen, the rate of decline moderates to approximately 38 units per step. This reflects the model's justification mechanisms (0.61) enabling agents to temporarily rationalize continued whole milk consumption despite shifting social norms. A distinct plateau emerges between steps fifteen and nineteen, where consumption stabilizes in the 760–774 unit range without significant monotonic trend. Significant variance (401 units) relative to the mean throughout the simulation indicates heterogeneous adoption speeds across the agent population. The mean factor applied to the whole milk choice function due to habit begins at a neutral value of 1.0000. This represents a cumulative increase of roughly 5% in the habitual influence on whole milk selection over the 33 time steps. An inflection occurs around 1981, after which the rate of divergence moderates but continues climbing through the mid-1980s. This deceleration suggests that while additional agents continue adopting alternative milk varieties, the marginal rate at which consumer evaluations diverge slows as the market approaches a stable configuration of mixed preferences. By the end of 2002, the increase becomes very gradual, approaching the terminal value of 0.7508. Selections initiate at zero, indicating complete alignment between personal environmental valuations and consumption choices at the baseline corresponding to 1974. This abrupt departure from zero reflects the early phase of the milk transition, where powerful habitual forces and emerging health perceptions begin compelling consumers toward alternative varieties that generate increasing tension with their underlying environmental values.
