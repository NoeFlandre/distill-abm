This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The simulation integrates theoretical frameworks with empirical data, employing calibration against observed macro-level consumption patterns to validate outcomes. The pattern fits classic product lifecycle or demand adoption curves seen in consumer goods. The final value of 1456 suggests sustained, growing demand for skimmed/semi-skimmed milk products. The total number of milk choices begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 32 time steps, reaching approximately 25,644 total choices. The mean weekly consumption of whole milk per person begins at approximately 2655 units and undergoes a pronounced decline to roughly 253 units by the final time step. This trajectory reflects the modeled transition away from whole milk dominance toward alternative varieties characteristic of the 1974 to 2005 period. The metric reaches 16,000 at the midpoint of the simulation (step 16), maintaining perfect symmetry in its trajectory. The mean habit factor for whole milk begins at a baseline value of 1.0000 and exhibits a sustained monotonic increase throughout the 33-step simulation period. This represents an overall growth of roughly 5% in the habit-driven reinforcement applied to whole milk choice functions. The pattern suggests that as agents transition from whole milk to alternatives, the accumulation of repeated consumption decisions generates positive feedback in habit formation. The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the health values implied by their milk choices initiates at zero and increases monotonically throughout the simulation period.
