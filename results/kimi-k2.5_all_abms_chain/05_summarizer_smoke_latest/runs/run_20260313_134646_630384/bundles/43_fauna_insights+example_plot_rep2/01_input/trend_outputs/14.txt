The percentage of vegetated landscape patches begins at approximately 33% and undergoes a rapid decline to roughly 32% within the first 100 time steps as initial grass stocks are depleted by prey consumption. The system subsequently exhibits pronounced cyclical dynamics synchronized with seasonal regrowth patterns. Around step 350, vegetated coverage crashes dramatically, reaching minima between 23% and 30% across simulation runs depending on stochastic prey movement patterns, before recovering rapidly to approximately 33% by step 400.

This seasonal collapse-and-recovery pattern repeats at approximately 365-step intervals, with subsequent minima occurring around steps 700, 1050, and 1400. However, the severity of these crashes attenuates progressively: the second minimum reaches only 29-31%, the third stabilizes around 31-32.5%, and by the fourth cycle, declines are barely perceptible above the baseline of 32.5-33%. After step 1500, the percentage remains stable between 32.5% and 33% with only minor seasonal fluctuations.

This trajectory indicates that the grass-prey system undergoes a convergence process toward dynamic equilibrium. The initial extreme crashes reflect a lag between high prey density and the seasonal limitations on grass regrowth (regrowth-time: 3, seasonality: 0.8), creating temporary but severe fodder bottlenecks. As prey herds adjust demographically or distribute grazing pressure more evenly across the toroidal landscape, the vegetation coverage stabilizes despite continued seasonal constraints.

For researchers testing megafauna extinction hypotheses, these dynamics imply that simulations must extend beyond 1500 steps (approximately four seasonal cycles) to observe equilibrium fodder conditions rather than transient grazing pressure effects. The early severe crashes represent periods of acute nutritional stress that could artificially inflate prey vulnerability to hunting pressure or trigger demographic bottlenecks, potentially confounding extinction dynamics if interpreted as steady-state conditions.
