 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction . It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges . The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics .  The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the simulation period from initialization through approximately step 2200 . This indicates that any observed extinction dynamics or demographic shifts in the prey populations arise solely from the interactions between environmental variables, prey life history traits, and the constant hunting pressure .  The data demonstrates that male megaherbivore populations face extremely rapid collapse under hunting pressure, with effective extinction occurring within 2–5 years . Given the sexual dimorphism in food value (males valued at 30 versus females at 27) and the higher processing cost for males (6 versus 3 for females), hunters exhibit clear preference for males .  The model demonstrates that megaherbivore populations of 250 individuals face high extinction risk within approximately 1000 to 1500 time steps (roughly 3 to 4 years) under the specified hunting pressure of 20 foragers . The mandatory 750-step reproductive lag creates a critical vulnerability window where prey populations cannot offset mortality through births .  The population undergoes a steep decline during the first 500 steps, with most trajectories falling to between 20 and 40 individuals, though some runs exhibit faster collapse reaching near-zero levels by step 300 . Between steps 500 and 1000, the decline continues but at a reduced rate, with populations typically falling below 20 individuals as the trajectories converge toward extinction .
