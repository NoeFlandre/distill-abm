The cumulative mortality of postpartum female ungulates exhibits a sigmoid trajectory over the 2312-tick simulation horizon. Initiating from zero accumulation, the metric undergoes rapid exponential-phase growth during the initial 475 ticks, achieving a mean inflection rate of 0.213 kills per tick with perfect monotonicity (τ=1.000), indicating intense initial harvesting pressure on nursing females coinciding with the establishment of hunter-forager bands in the landscape.

This acceleration phase contains multiple dynamic inflections between ticks 245 and 285, suggesting rapid adjustment of hunting strategies toward reproductive females before transitioning into a pronounced deceleration phase lasting approximately until tick 850. During this interval (ticks 475–850), the accumulation rate drops precipitously from 0.226 to 0.015 kills per tick, reflecting either declining encounter rates as vulnerable individuals are removed from the population or energetic constraints on hunter-foragers processing high-cost reproductive females relative to other prey categories.

Between ticks 850 and 1391, the system enters a near-linear accumulation phase with slopes approaching 0.001, followed by a statistically negligible plateau (τ=-0.078, p>0.05) from ticks 1392–1623 where cumulative mortality stabilizes near 117 individuals. This asymptotic behavior, oscillating narrowly between 116.9 and 119.0 individuals with a dominant periodicity of 63 ticks, suggests the effective extirpation of accessible postpartum females from the hunting catchment area or a radical shift in forager optimal foraging strategies away from reproductive-age females burdened with offspring.

Minor fluctuations characterize the terminal phase (ticks >1856), including a statistically significant but ecologically marginal decreasing trend (τ=-0.324) likely representing simulation boundary effects rather than biological recovery. The final saturation value of 117 cumulative postpartum female deaths, achieved by tick 1826 and maintained through 2312, represents approximately 47% of the initial ungulate population (250) and likely constitutes a critical threshold for population viability given the reproductive value of this demographic (birth-spacing intervals of 730 ticks imply each lost nursing female represents substantial multi-tick reproductive potential loss).

**Decision-Making Implications:**

The initial 475-tick window (approximately 1.3 simulated years) represents a critical period where hunting pressure on reproductive females accumulates irreversibly; intervention strategies targeting hunter mobility restrictions or vegetation management to reduce encounter rates must be implemented before tick 850 to prevent the asymptotic mortality accumulation that characterizes population collapse trajectories. The 63-tick oscillation cycle superimposed on the saturation plateau suggests predictable seasonal vulnerability windows in forager success rates that could inform temporally-pulsed conservation measures.
