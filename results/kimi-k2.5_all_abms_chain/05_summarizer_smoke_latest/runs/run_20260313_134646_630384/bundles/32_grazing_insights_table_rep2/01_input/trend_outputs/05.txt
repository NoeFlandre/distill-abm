The average total livestock per household commences at 90 units and follows a trajectory of initial depletion followed by partial recovery over the 100-timestep simulation. During the first 20 timesteps, the metric exhibits a steep decreasing trend, losing approximately 25% of its initial value as the system adjusts to ecological constraints. This decline continues at a decelerating rate between timesteps 20 and 37, reaching a minimum value of approximately 41 units at timestep 37. This trough represents the lowest point of livestock holdings, indicating the nadir of the system's productive capacity under the initial stress conditions.

Following this minimum, the metric enters a brief transitional plateau between timesteps 37 and 44, where no significant directional trend is detected, suggesting a temporary stabilization of household herds. From timestep 44 onward, the system exhibits a statistically significant increasing trend that persists through the conclusion of the simulation. However, the recovery occurs at a decelerating rate: the initial recovery phase (timesteps 44–54) shows relatively robust growth with a slope of 0.313 units per timestep, which gradually diminishes to 0.002 units per timestep by the final interval (timesteps 88–98).

The simulation concludes with average total livestock stabilizing at approximately 47.5 units per household, roughly 53% of the initial stocking level but 16% above the minimum trough observed at timestep 37. Key inflection points at timesteps 3 and 46 mark transitions between the steep decline phase and the moderating decline phase, and between the plateau and recovery phases, respectively.

In the context of mixed behavioral populations employing learning-by-doing (E-LBD) and social learning (E-RO-SL1) alongside baseline behaviors (E-RO), these dynamics suggest that initial stocking rates significantly exceeded the sustainable carrying capacity of the private property grazing system under the specified climatic variability (rainfall mean 200, standard deviation 100). The 37-timestep decline period represents the time required for adaptive learning processes and resource feedbacks to force a reduction to sustainable levels, while the subsequent slow recovery indicates that learning-enabled agents can gradually rebuild herds but cannot restore initial levels within the 100-timestep horizon.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

Initial livestock allocations of 90 units per household appear unsustainable, leading to inevitable herd reductions of over 50% before system stabilization. Decision makers should consider conservative initial stocking rates near 45–50 units to avoid the severe destocking and lost productivity observed during the first 37 timesteps. The slow recovery rate following the crash (averaging less than 0.2 units per timestep after year 55) indicates that rebuilding herds after overstocking requires substantial time even with learning-based adaptation mechanisms. In private property grazing systems with high rainfall variability, maintaining initial herds below 50 units prevents the sharp livestock losses and extended recovery periods documented in the first half of the simulation, while still allowing for gradual growth toward the system's approximate equilibrium capacity of 48 units.
