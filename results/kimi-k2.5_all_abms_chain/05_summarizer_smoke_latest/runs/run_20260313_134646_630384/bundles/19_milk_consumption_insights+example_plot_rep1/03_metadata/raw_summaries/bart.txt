 This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005, replicating individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence .  The mean weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent initiates at approximately zero during the first time step . Consumption undergoes rapid exponential-like growth, accelerating from near-zero values to approximately 1,200–1,280 units . Following this initial surge, consumption enters a prolonged plateau phase spanning roughly steps 11 through 27 .  The cumulative count of milk choices made by the entire agent population begins at zero and increases linearly at a constant rate of approximately 1,000 choices per step throughout the simulation period . By step 32, the total reaches roughly 32,000 accumulated decisions . The consistent linear trajectory suggests that the model assumes continuous consumption behavior where no agents exit the simulation .  The mean weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately zero units at the initial time step . Over the first ten steps, the quantity increases rapidly and steeply, reaching approximately 1,200 to 1,250 units . This sharp upward trajectory indicates a swift initial adoption phase where agents rapidly transition from whole milk consumption to lower-fat alternatives .  The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 32-step simulation period representing 1974 to 2005 . The persistent increase in health deviation throughout the entire simulation implies that social influence, environmental considerations, and habit persistence drive consumer choices increasingly away from pure health-value optimization .  The trajectory suggests that the initial phase of the dietary transition generates substantial and rapid misalignment between environmental values and consumption habits . The rapid emergence of deviation within the first five time steps highlights the critical importance of early intervention strategies, as dissonant patterns crystallize quickly and become resistant to change . The persistent high plateau suggests that interventions targeting habit disruption may prove more effective than value-based messaging alone .
