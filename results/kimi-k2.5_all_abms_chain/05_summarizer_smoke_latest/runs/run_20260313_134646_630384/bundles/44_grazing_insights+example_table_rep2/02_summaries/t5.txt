Model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making. The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. The initial risk disposition of social learning households remains fixed at its initialized value. The absence of variation suggests that social learning agents may update other behavioral parameters or strategies during the simulation. However, their foundational risk attitudes established at model initialization persist unchanged, serving as a static reference point. The average green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units. Following an initial period of relative stability through the first 10 time steps, the system experiences a marked decline, reaching a minimum of roughly 42,057 units by time step 27. This downward trajectory reflects initial grazing pressures and adaptation challenges. The average number of livestock destocked per household begins at zero and surges sharply to approximately 6.6 animals by the third year. This initial spike represents the most severe liquidation event, after which destocking rates decline rapidly but non-monotonically over the following two decades. The primary transition in adoption dynamics occurs near time step forty. The accumulation of engaged households shifts from active growth to the stable equilibrium observed in the final thirty-seven time steps. Early-adopter programs can achieve rapid uptake within the first decade of simulation time. Decline from the initial 20 households to approximately 15 suggests that fixed risk attitudes and absence of adaptive learning place these households at a competitive disadvantage relative to E-LBD (learning-by-doing) and E-RO-SL1 (social learning) counterparts.
