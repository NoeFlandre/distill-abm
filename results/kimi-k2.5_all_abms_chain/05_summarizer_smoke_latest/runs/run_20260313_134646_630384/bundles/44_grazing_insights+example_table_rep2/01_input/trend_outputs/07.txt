The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households begins at effectively zero, indicating perfect equality among all households at the outset. Over the 100-year period, this inequality rises steadily and monotonically, reaching approximately 0.073 by the final timestep. The average inequality level across the entire period remains relatively moderate at around 0.03, though the consistent upward trajectory indicates that divergence in household fortunes accelerates over time.

This trend reflects a gradual stratification where certain households—likely those leveraging more effective learning mechanisms such as learning-by-doing or social learning, or those with more favorable risk management parameters—accumulate healthier livestock at faster rates than others. The absence of any plateau or reversal suggests that in private property grazing systems with heterogeneous learning capabilities, inequality in livestock health becomes increasingly entrenched without external intervention.

For decision makers, the steady increase in inequality signals that early-stage support for vulnerable households is critical. Monitoring livestock health distribution metrics during the first 20-30 years could serve as an early warning system for long-term disparity, enabling targeted interventions before gaps become structurally embedded. Policies promoting knowledge sharing, risk-pooling mechanisms, or equitable access to learning opportunities may help mitigate the divergence observed between more adaptive and less adaptive households.
