The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods. The metric spans a range from approximately -0.11 to +0.18 across the population of social learning agents, with individual households maintaining distinct, static risk disposition values throughout the duration. The distribution indicates substantial heterogeneity in initial risk profiles among these agents. Some simulation runs converge to states where the farming population maintains herds substantially above recommended levels. The simulations initiate with all households holding 90 livestock animals per household. During the first 20 time steps, the metric undergoes a rapid and substantial decline, falling from 90 to approximately 40-50 animals per house. The subsequent recovery indicates learning mechanisms activating as farmers adjust stocking decisions based on observed pasture conditions. The mean destock of households exhibits distinct temporal dynamics across the 100-step simulation period. At initial point, the metric begins at zero, then immediately spikes to peak values between 7.0 and 7.5 animals per household within the first two steps. At step 20, destocking rates decline rapidly and monotonically across all simulation runs, falling from the 6-7 range down to values between 0 and 2. Social learning engagement is an absorbing state, once a household engages in social learning, it remains in that state for the duration of the simulation. Social learning spreads to or is adopted by households of other behavioral types (E-RO and E-LBD) during the simulation period, with the extent of this adoption varying stochastically. Several trajectories maintain elevated counts of 35–40 non-learning households, exhibiting fluctuations of ±5 households around a high plateau. These paths suggest environmental conditions where non-adaptive strategies temporarily avoid negative consequences despite poor pasture conditions. Other trajectories continue monotonic declines, stabilizing temporarily at intermediate values of 15–25 households.
