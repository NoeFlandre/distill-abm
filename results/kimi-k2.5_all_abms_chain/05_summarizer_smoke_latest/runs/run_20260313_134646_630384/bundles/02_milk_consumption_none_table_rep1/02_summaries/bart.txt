This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence, and post-choice evaluation. Average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent rises from 5.29 to a terminal value of 1456.60. This trajectory reflects the modeled historical transition away from whole milk, which initially maintained dominance through strong incumbent habit strength set at 10.15. The simulation of milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 begins with minimal consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties, starting at approximately 5.3 units per person per week. During the initial phase spanning roughly the first nine time steps, consumption undergoes rapid exponential growth, with statistically significant increasing trends as early adoption drives the metric sharply upward. Consumption resumes a significant increasing trend, climbing from the plateau levels to reach a final saturation point of approximately 1457 units. Habit on whole milk selection slightly reinforces over the simulation period despite the historical transition toward lower-fat alternatives. The habit weighting of whole milk consumption increases marginally, suggesting that established consumption patterns maintain or slightly strengthen their grip on consumer decision-making throughout the three-decade simulation window. The mean deviation between agents' held environmental values and their milk choice behaviors exhibits a pronounced trajectory characterized by an initial phase of rapid divergence followed by prolonged stabilization. After approximately 1987, the deviation saturates, indicating that the population settled into a persistent state where milk choices consistently diverged from environmental valuations. This pattern suggests that as the milk market transitioned from whole milk dominance toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties, consumers increasingly maintained consumption habits that conflicted with personal environmental values.
