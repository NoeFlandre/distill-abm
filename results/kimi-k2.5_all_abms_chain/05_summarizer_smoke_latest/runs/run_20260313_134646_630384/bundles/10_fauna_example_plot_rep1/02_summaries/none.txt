The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The spatial scale is unitless but can be conceptualized relative to daily movement distances of megaherbivores. The model integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Agents include human hunters and sexually dimorphic ungulate herds. Hunters operate under logic approximating dynamic optimal foraging theory, making pursuit decisions based on their current energy status, prey food value, prey density in the landscape, and the costs of pursuit, hunting, and processing. Hunters do not reproduce, expend energy when moving, can die from energy depletion, and can store energy surpluses. Ungulates move as herds with environmental feedback, age over time, reproduce with user-controlled birth spacing intervals, and exhibit increased mortality risk after reaching senescence. Nursing offspring add to the food value of females. The environment consists of grass patches that can be static or dynamic, with optional seasonality limiting regrowth to specific seasonal cycles.

The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. It seeks to determine whether these interactions accelerate extinction, buffer against extinction, or create equilibrium conditions that prevent extinction events. The model allows users to isolate and test specific hypotheses by manipulating variables related to fodder availability, seasonality, relative animal food value, animal fecundity, lifespans, relative hunting danger, and movement energy costs. Experimental protocols involve establishing baselines without hunting or environmental change, then iteratively adding variables such as environmental degradation, hunter population density, sexual dimorphism ( differential food values and processing costs), and life history constraints (interbirth intervals, lifespan) to observe their individual and combined effects on extinction probability, herd demography, herd movement patterns, and hunting selectivity.

The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the observation period, from the initial step through approximately 2,200 time steps. This stability reflects the parameter configuration where foragers are immortal and do not reproduce, maintaining the initial population of 20 established at the start of the simulation. No fluctuations or directional trends are observed in the metric across any of the simulation runs, indicating that under these specific conditions, hunter mortality and recruitment dynamics do not influence population size regardless of environmental changes, prey availability, or energy expenditure patterns.

The total energy available to all forager bands begins at approximately 2000 units across all simulation runs. Over the first 500 time steps, the energy declines rapidly in a non-linear fashion, reaching values between -4000 and -6000. This initial decline reflects the transient phase where foragers expend movement costs while hunting returns diminish as prey populations are depleted or become harder to locate.

After approximately 500 time steps, the energy trajectory transitions to a steady linear decline that persists until the simulation ends. During this phase, the energy decreases at a constant rate of roughly 15-20 units per step, with all simulation runs converging to follow nearly identical trajectories. By step 2000, the total energy reaches approximately -30,000 to -32,000 units in most runs, with one run continuing to step 2200 where the energy falls to approximately -38,000.

The convergence of all runs after step 500 suggests that despite initial stochastic variation in foraging success and movement patterns, the system reaches a consistent energetic equilibrium where hunting gains fail to offset movement expenditures. The continuous negative trend indicates that immortal foragers continue accumulating energy deficits (or drawing down stored energy) without lethal consequences, maintaining constant activity levels despite deteriorating foraging returns. The linearity of the long-term decline implies stable per-capita energy expenditure relative to acquisition rates once the prey population has been suppressed to a low-density equilibrium.

This plateau indicates that hunting of male prey effectively ceases, likely due to the depletion of males from the system, extremely low population densities making pursuit energetically unprofitable, or a transition in hunter selectivity toward females and calves. Variation across runs shows some simulations reaching plateau states earlier (around step 800) and others continuing growth until step 1400, with final cumulative totals varying by up to 20 individuals, reflecting stochastic differences in hunter-prey encounters and demographic fluctuations across replicate simulations.
