Over the 31-year simulation period representing 1974 to 2005, the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent exhibits a distinct three-phase trajectory characterized by rapid early adoption, an extended plateau of stagnation, and final consolidation.

**Phase 1: Initial Rapid Adoption (Time Steps 0–9)**

The simulation begins at time step 0 with minimal alternative milk consumption at approximately 5.3 units per agent, reflecting the historical dominance of whole milk in 1974. During the first decade of the simulation, consumption increases sharply and monotonically, reaching approximately 1,256 units by time step 9. This initial growth phase represents the period when early adopter agents, less constrained by habitual attachment to whole milk and more responsive to emerging health and environmental perceptions, begin switching to alternative varieties. The steep slope indicates that once agents overcome initial habit thresholds, adoption occurs rapidly within local network clusters.

**Phase 2: Stagnation and Oscillation (Time Steps 10–24)**

Following the initial growth spurt, the simulation enters a prolonged plateau lasting approximately 15 time steps, during which consumption oscillates around a stable mean of roughly 1,220 units without significant directional trend. During this middle period, values fluctuate between peaks of approximately 1,265 units (observed at time step 11) and valleys of approximately 1,183 units (observed at time step 18), with minor peaks recurring at time steps 13, 19, and 21. 

This stagnation reflects the model's representation of social and habitual resistance mechanisms. During this phase, the majority of remaining whole milk consumers possess strong habitual attachments (initial habit strength of 10.0) that require substantial accumulated motivation to break. Social influence creates equilibrium rather than change, as agents observe mixed behaviors within their networks and conform to local norms that stabilize at intermediate adoption levels. The oscillations likely represent yearly fluctuations in the balance between habit maintenance forces and gradual shifts in global perceptions regarding health and environmental attributes.

**Phase 3: Final Consolidation (Time Steps 25–32)**

Beginning around time step 25, the simulation exhibits renewed significant growth, breaking the previous plateau and advancing toward complete market transformation. Consumption increases from approximately 1,210 units at time step 23 to a terminal value of 1,456.6 units at time step 32. This final growth phase, while significant, proceeds at a more moderate rate compared to the initial adoption period, with an average increase of approximately 48.5 units per time step during the late growth window (time steps 25–29).

This consolidation phase represents the tipping point where alternative milk consumption becomes the dominant social norm. As the proportion of alternative milk consumers in the network reaches critical mass, social influence shifts from inhibiting to facilitating change, creating positive feedback loops that overcome remaining habitual resistance. The slower rate compared to initial adoption reflects the diminishing pool of remaining whole milk consumers and the increasing difficulty of converting the most habitually entrenched agents.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The three-phase trajectory reveals that consumer transitions in habit-driven markets follow non-linear patterns with distinct inflection points. Decision makers should anticipate extended plateau periods where adoption appears to stall despite initial momentum, requiring sustained intervention rather than short-term campaigns. The 15-time-step stagnation phase suggests that mid-transition strategies must specifically target habitual resistance and social norm stabilization, potentially through network-specific interventions that prevent equilibrium at intermediate adoption levels. Finally, the model indicates that early investment in rapid initial adoption creates the foundation for eventual tipping points, but patience is required as the final consolidation phase, while inevitable once critical mass is achieved, proceeds at a more gradual pace than initial uptake.
