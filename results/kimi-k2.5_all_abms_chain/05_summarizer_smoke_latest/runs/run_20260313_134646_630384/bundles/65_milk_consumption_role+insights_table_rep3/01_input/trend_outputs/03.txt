## Trend Analysis: Cumulative Whole Milk Selection Dynamics

The simulation reveals a monotonic accumulation of whole milk consumption choices across the agent population, beginning from an initial baseline of zero and reaching approximately 2,195 total selection events by the conclusion of the 33 time-step period. This trajectory reflects the persistent—though decelerating—role of incumbent consumption habits despite the broader market transition toward alternative milk varieties. The aggregate cumulative count of approximately 2,195 events across 1,000 agents indicates that, on average, each agent selected whole milk roughly 2.2 times over the simulation duration, suggesting that while whole milk lost its dominant market position historically, it maintained residual appeal throughout the transition period.

The rate of accumulation exhibits distinct phases characterized by changing growth velocities. During the initial phase (time steps 0–7), the system demonstrates rapid acceleration, with the steepest accumulation rates occurring between steps 5 and 9, where the slope reaches approximately 76.2 additional choices per time step. This early surge reflects the strong initial habit strength (parameterized at 10.0) that agents hold toward whole milk, creating substantial inertia in consumer behavior at the simulation's outset. A critical inflection point emerges at step 7, marking the transition from accelerating to decelerating growth; following this point, the accumulation rate gradually declines through subsequent windows (steps 10–14, 15–19, 20–24, and 25–29), with slopes decreasing sequentially from approximately 71.4 to 64.1. This deceleration pattern aligns with the threshold-based psychological mechanism where agents require accumulating motivation to break established habits, resulting in a slowdown rather than abrupt cessation of incumbent choices as alternative perceptions improve.

Later-stage dynamics introduce complexity through oscillatory behavior observed between steps 27 and 30, manifesting a dominant periodicity of approximately 15 steps. These fluctuations suggest temporary volatility or cyclical reconsideration of whole milk choices in the mature phase of the market transition, possibly driven by network effects and social conformity pressures (parameterized at 0.28) interacting with residual habit persistence. The trajectory culminates in a change point at step 32, where the series approaches its terminal value of 2,194.6, indicating stabilization of cumulative consumption as the simulation nears its 2005 endpoint. Notably, the absence of detected peaks or valleys in the cumulative series confirms that whole milk consumption never fully ceased but rather approached a saturation point where new adoption events became increasingly rare.

### Implications for Decision Makers

The persistence of cumulative whole milk selections throughout the 31-year simulation period underscores that market transitions in habitual consumer goods involve prolonged residual demand rather than immediate displacement, suggesting that incumbent product categories retain economic relevance even after losing majority market share. The identification of peak accumulation rates during steps 5–9 establishes a critical temporal window where interventions targeting consumer perceptions or social norms may achieve maximum leverage in accelerating transition trajectories. Furthermore, the late-stage oscillations (steps 27–30) and gradual deceleration imply that mature markets may exhibit unpredictable, cyclical returns to incumbent products driven by social network dynamics; consequently, stakeholders should anticipate and plan for persistent niche markets rather than assuming complete category abandonment. The threshold-based nature of habit disruption evident in the data suggests that public health or environmental campaigns must sustain pressure over extended periods to overcome high initial habit thresholds, as consumption reductions occur gradually rather than precipitously.
