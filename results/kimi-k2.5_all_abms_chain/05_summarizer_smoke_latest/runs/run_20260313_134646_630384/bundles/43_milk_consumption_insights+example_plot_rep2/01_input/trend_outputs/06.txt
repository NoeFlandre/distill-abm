Mean weekly whole milk consumption per person begins at approximately 2,650 milliliters at the start of the simulation period, reflecting the market dominance of whole milk in 1974. The quantity declines rapidly over the first ten steps, falling to roughly 950-1,000 milliliters by step 10. This steep initial reduction corresponds to the early phase of the milk transition, wherein health-conscious consumers and those most susceptible to social influence begin adopting skimmed and semi-skimmied alternatives, disrupting established consumption habits within the agent population.

Following this initial period, the rate of decline moderates considerably between steps 10 and 20, with consumption decreasing gradually from approximately 1,000 milliliters to around 750 milliliters. This slowdown suggests that remaining whole milk consumers possess stronger habit persistence or lower susceptibility to health and environmental messaging, requiring more sustained social pressure or repeated exposure to alternative choice characteristics to trigger disposition toward behavior change.

From step 20 onward, the downward trend continues with some fluctuation, reaching approximately 600-650 milliliters by step 25 before experiencing sharper reductions between steps 26 and 28, where consumption drops from roughly 600 milliliters to approximately 300-350 milliliters. This late-stage acceleration may reflect the maturation of social norms favoring low-fat alternatives and the cumulative effect of network influences propagating through the Watts-Strogatz topology. By the final step (step 32), mean whole milk consumption stabilizes at approximately 200-300 milliliters per person per week, indicating near-complete market transition with only a residual segment of the population maintaining whole milk preferences.

The trajectories demonstrate relatively low variance among simulation runs during the initial and final phases (steps 0-5 and steps 28-32), suggesting robust convergence in model outcomes when whole milk dominates or when alternatives have achieved market saturation. Greater divergence appears during the middle transition phase (steps 5-15), particularly around step 10 where values range from approximately 950 to 1,100 milliliters, indicating sensitivity to the specific disposition mechanism employed—whether threshold-based or probability-based—and variations in parameter settings affecting habit disruption and social influence transmission.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The pronounced nonlinear dynamics, characterized by an initial rapid decline, subsequent moderation, and final accelerated drop, suggest that public health interventions targeting milk consumption should anticipate variable rates of behavioral change across different population segments and time periods. The steep decline in the first third of the simulation period indicates that initial messaging campaigns focusing on health benefits can achieve rapid impact among receptive segments, but sustained effort lasting two decades or more is required for habituated consumers, as evidenced by the slower decline in the middle phase. The sharper reductions in the final decade suggest that network effects and normative pressures become particularly potent once alternative consumption reaches critical mass, implying that early investment in social influence mechanisms—such as community-based programs or visible endorsement strategies—may yield disproportionate returns in later periods. For the dairy industry, the model predicts that whole milk will retain only approximately 10-15% of its original market volume, necessitating strategic diversification into lower-fat alternatives well before the final decline phase, and emulating the social influence processes observed in the simulation to accelerate adoption of new product lines.
