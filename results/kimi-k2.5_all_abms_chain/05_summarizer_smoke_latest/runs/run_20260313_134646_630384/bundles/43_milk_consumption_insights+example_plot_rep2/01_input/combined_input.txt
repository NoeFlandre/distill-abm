This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005, replicating individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The simulation integrates theoretical frameworks with empirical data, employing calibration against observed macro-level consumption patterns to validate outcomes. Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment, each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology, where they can sense and influence other agents' choice functions through interaction probabilities.

The primary objective is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence interact within individual decision-making processes regarding milk choice, with the main outcome metric being average weekly consumption per person for each milk type. The study compares two model variants employing different disposition mechanisms for triggering consideration of alternatives: a threshold-based approach versus a probability-based approach. Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence, and post-choice evaluation where agents may adjust future decisions. Social norms operate globally to impact choice function weightings, while habit formation and cognitive dissonance thresholds modify consumption patterns over time.

Between steps 10 and 20, the rate of decline moderates considerably, with consumption decreasing gradually to approximately 750 units. This phase reflects the persistence of strong habitual behaviors among remaining whole milk consumers, who require more prolonged exposure to dissonance-inducing information or accumulated social proof before triggering reconsideration of their established consumption patterns.

From step 20 to step 25, the downward trajectory continues but at a diminished pace, settling near 600 units with observable minor fluctuations. During this period, the remaining consumer base represents agents with high habit thresholds or limited social connectivity, maintaining their preferences despite the widespread shift to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties that has permeated the broader network.

A marked discontinuity emerges around steps 26 and 27, where consumption plummets sharply from approximately 600 units to below 400. This sudden drop suggests a critical transition point in the simulation wherein cumulative social influence and conformist pressures reach a tipping point sufficient to overcome the cognitive thresholds of previously resistant agents, resulting in synchronized reconsideration events.

Throughout the final sequence from step 28 to step 32, consumption stabilizes at low levels between 200 and 300 units, indicating a persistent but small segment of the population that maintains whole milk consumption regardless of prevailing social norms. The consistency across simulation runs remains high throughout, though slightly elevated variance during the middle phase (steps 5 through 15) reflects stochastic differences in the timing of individual agent interactions and disposition triggers.

Implications for decision makers: The pronounced initial decline suggests that leveraging early adopters and social network influencers can effectively catalyze rapid dietary shifts during transition periods.
