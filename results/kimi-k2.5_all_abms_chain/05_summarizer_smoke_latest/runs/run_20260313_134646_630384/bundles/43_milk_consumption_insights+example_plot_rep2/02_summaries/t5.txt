Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. Social network influencers can effectively catalyze rapid dietary shifts during transition periods. The extended plateau preceding the late-stage sharp drop indicates that traditional persuasion tactics may face resistance from habit-entrenched consumers until social norms achieve critical mass. Accumulated choices remain below 1,500, indicating limited early adoption. Lag phase reflects model's initialization with high incumbent habit strength (10.0) for whole milk and initial health and environmental perception means of 1.69 and 1.12 respectively. Between steps 5 and 15, adoption rate accelerates substantially, with cumulative choices increasing from approximately 1,500 to 10,000. Trajectories show relatively low variance in model outcomes when whole milk dominates or when alternatives have achieved market saturation. Greater divergence appears during the middle transition phase (steps 5-15), particularly around step 10 where values range from approximately 950 to 1,100 milliliters. Variance across simulation runs progressively widens over time. The spread between the maximum and minimum values expanding to approximately 0.01 by the end of the simulation. This consistent upward trend indicates that agents progressively strengthen their habitual reliance on whole milk consumption. The metric accelerates again, climbing rapidly to 0.70 or higher by the conclusion of the simulation. The initial rapid growth phase (steps 0-10) represents a critical window for early education and awareness campaigns before habits become entrenched. This early period suggests an early period of significant misalignment.
