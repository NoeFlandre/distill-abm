This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence. Means weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent initiates at approximately zero, consistent with the 1974 baseline where whole milk dominated the market. During the first ten steps, consumption undergoes rapid, approximately exponential growth, surging from negligible levels to between 1,200 and 1,280 units. The total number of milk choices made by all agents combined begins at zero and increases linearly throughout the simulation period, reaching approximately 32,000 choices by step 32. By step 20, the cumulative total reaches approximately 15,000, and by step 30, it approaches 25,000. By the final step (step 32), mean whole milk consumption stabilizes approximately 200-300 milliliters per week. The mean weekly consumption of skimmed and whole milk begins at approximately zero units. Consumption undergoes rapid acceleration, steeply rising to values between 1,200 and 1,280 units. For the dairy industry, the model predicts that whole milk will retain only approximately 10-15% of its original market volume. The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at 1.0 at step 0, indicating a neutral baseline where habits exert no modifying influence on the choice function. Campaigns targeting milk choice modification should therefore prioritize early intervention strategies that interrupt the initial habit reinforcement phase before the cumulative weight of habitual behavior creates substantial resistance to alternative products. The mean value environmental deviation initiates at approximately zero, indicating that agents begin with milk choices fully aligned with their held environmental values. Over the initial two to three steps, the deviation undergoes a rapid escalation, surging to roughly 10 to 12 units. The stabilization period (steps 10-25) suggests that incremental nudges and social norming interventions may be most effective when the system is in equilibrium.
