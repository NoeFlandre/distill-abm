Model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. Individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers. The most pronounced decrease occurs during the initial 10 steps. Consumption plummets by approximately 1,700 units to reach 950. This deceleration aligns with the expectation that consumers remaining after the initial wave possess habit strengths exceeding the threshold of 2.0 or exhibit higher social blindness. The majority of trajectories cluster between 2,100 and 2,250. The trajectory follows a strictly linear pattern throughout the entire period. However, the dispersion between the highest and lowest values increases slightly in the latter half of the simulation. The constant volume of choices per period provides a reliable foundation for assessing how health perceptions, habit strength, and social network effects drive market share transitions when the underlying decision-making population remains fixed. The metric exhibits a steep monotonic decline throughout the first ten time steps, reaching roughly 950 to 1,000 units. The variance across simulation runs remains remarkably low throughout the entire 32-step period. The distinct two-phase growth pattern reveals that market transitions in habit-driven food categories often exhibit prolonged plateau periods between initial rapid adoption and final market saturation. This initial jump suggests a rapid consolidation of habitual behavior toward whole milk. From step 10 through the conclusion at step 32, the habit factor exhibits sustained, roughly linear growth, ascending from 1.08 to approximately 1.58-1.60. This extended growth phase indicates continuous habit reinforcement across the consumer population as adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk becomes increasingly prevalent. The six-step lag before habit acceleration suggests a critical intervention window during which marketing efforts or policy measures could most effectively influence long-term consumption patterns. The habit threshold (set at 2.0) slows the divergence as agents attempt to reconcile emerging environmental awareness with deep-seated consumption routines. After step 10, the deviation stabilizes and remains nearly constant at approximately 13.5 through the remainder of the simulation period (step 32), exhibiting only negligible fluctuation. The variance across simulation replications is minimal throughout the entire temporal sequence.
