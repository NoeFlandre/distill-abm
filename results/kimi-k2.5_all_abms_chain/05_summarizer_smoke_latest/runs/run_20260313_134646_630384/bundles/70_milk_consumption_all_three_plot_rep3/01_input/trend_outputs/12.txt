The mean deviation between agents' environmental values and their milk choices initiates at approximately zero. It undergoes an extremely rapid ascent during the first five time steps, surging to approximately 13.0. This initial explosive growth reflects the immediate tension between agents' low baseline environmental perceptions of alternative milk options (alt-env-mean-initial: 1.12) and their dominant habitual attachment to whole milk (incumbent-initial-habit: 10.0), creating a substantial and widening gap as environmental information enters the system while behavioral inertia remains strong.

The growth rate decelerates considerably between steps 5 and 10, with the metric climbing more gradually to approximately 13.5. During this transition phase, the moderating effects of social influence mechanisms (operating at p-interact: 0.3 and social-susceptibility: 0.2) and the habit threshold (set at 2.0) slow the divergence as agents attempt to reconcile emerging environmental awareness with deep-seated consumption routines, though insufficiently to close the value-choice gap.

After step 10, the deviation stabilizes and remains nearly constant at approximately 13.5 through the remainder of the simulation period (step 32), exhibiting only negligible fluctuation. This plateau indicates that the system reaches a psychological equilibrium where justification mechanisms (operating at 0.61) and cognitive dissonance tolerance (0.15) enable agents to sustain choices that substantially diverge from their environmental values without generating sufficient internal pressure to drive behavioral realignment.

The variance across simulation replications is minimal throughout the entire temporal sequence, with all runs producing virtually identical values at each time step. This high consistency suggests that the parameter configuration generates a deterministic outcome where the interplay of strong incumbent habits, moderate social blindness (0.51), and the Watts-Strogatz network structure (network-parameter: 8.0) inevitably produces this stable value-choice divergence regardless of stochastic variation.

The rapid emergence and persistence of this substantial deviation imply that decision makers face a critical window of opportunity during the initial ten time steps of market transition. Interventions targeting habit disruption or environmental value consolidation must occur early, as the system becomes increasingly resistant to change once the deviation crystallizes, indicating that early-stage educational campaigns or behavioral nudges may prove more cost-effective than efforts to shift established consumption patterns after the equilibrium sets in.
