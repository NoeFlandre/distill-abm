Despite the historical transition toward skimmed varieties, the persistent upward trend shows that habit formation continues to reinforce whole milk consumption among population segments maintaining incumbent choices, with memory lifetime and post-choice evaluation mechanisms sustaining incremental habit growth throughout the simulation period.

Implications for decision makers include recognizing that consumption habits exhibit strong path dependency and inertia, continuing to strengthen even as alternatives become available. The initial rapid habit formation suggests early intervention is critical to prevent entrenched behaviors. The increasing variance across scenarios indicates that social network effects create unpredictable trajectories, implying that targeted interventions at critical network nodes may prove more effective than broad uniform campaigns. Furthermore, the sustained growth in habit factors despite market transitions suggests that shifting established consumption patterns requires overcoming substantial accumulated habit strength, necessitating either prolonged promotion of alternatives or disruptive policy changes to break self-reinforcing behavioral cycles.
