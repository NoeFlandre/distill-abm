The mean value health deviation initiates at 0 and undergoes a rapid ascent during the first ten steps, reaching values between 0.5 and 0.55. This steep initial increase reflects the swift emergence of cognitive dissonance as agents begin processing information about alternative milk varieties while maintaining whole milk consumption patterns due to strong incumbent habits established at the simulation's outset.

Between steps 10 and 27, the metric transitions to a phase of gradual growth, climbing slowly from approximately 0.55 to 0.6. This deceleration suggests the establishment of a temporary equilibrium wherein the rate at which agents adjust their health perceptions through social influence and information diffusion roughly balances the stabilizing force of habit persistence and social conformity mechanisms.

Following step 27, the deviation accelerates markedly through the conclusion of the simulation at step 32, ultimately achieving values ranging from 0.7 to 0.75. This late-stage divergence indicates that the gap between agents' held health values and their actual consumption choices continues widening as the system approaches 2005, with health perception evolution outpacing behavioral adaptation.

Across repeated simulation runs, the trajectories maintain tight clustering throughout all phases, indicating robust consistency in the model dynamics despite stochastic variations in agent interactions within the Watts-Strogatz network topology.

The persistent elevation and ultimate growth of this deviation metric, with sustained values above 0.5 and final measures approaching 0.75, demonstrate that significant misalignment between health perceptions and consumption behavior becomes deeply entrenched in the population over the thirty-year period. For public health decision makers, these trends suggest that information dissemination alone creates substantial cognitive dissonance without necessarily driving behavioral change; interventions targeting habit disruption, reducing cognitive dissonance tolerance thresholds, or increasing reconsideration frequency may prove more effective than awareness campaigns at aligning milk consumption with health perceptions. Furthermore, the widening gap in later years indicates that without complementary behavioral interventions, continued health messaging may increasingly frustrate consumers by heightening awareness of healthier alternatives while structural habits and social norms prevent adoption, potentially eroding public trust in health recommendations over time.
