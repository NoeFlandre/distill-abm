The total number of whole milk choices begins at zero and accumulates steadily across all 32 time steps, reaching values between approximately 2,000 and 2,300 by the conclusion of the simulation period. This progression follows a roughly linear trajectory, with agents selecting whole milk at a consistent rate throughout the entire timeframe without evidence of saturation or decline.

While the trajectories remain tightly clustered during the initial stages, divergence between simulation runs becomes increasingly pronounced after approximately step 10, with final values spreading across a range of roughly 300 units by step 32. This growing variance suggests that while initial consumption behaviors remain highly constrained by the calibrated initial conditions—specifically the strong incumbent habits assigned to all agents—stochastic elements in social interactions and disposition triggers generate increasingly divergent accumulation patterns as the simulation progresses.

The persistent linear growth in total whole milk choices indicates that high initial habit strength, combined with the specific thresholds for cognitive dissonance and social susceptibility employed in this model variant, effectively maintains whole milk consumption at baseline levels despite the presence of alternative options. Rather than exhibiting the rapid early adoption of alternatives followed by a decline in whole milk selection that would characterize a successful dietary transition, the system demonstrates significant behavioral inertia. The unidirectional social network structure and modest social conformity parameters appear insufficient to overcome the strong incumbent habits established at initialization, resulting in continued whole milk dominance throughout the simulation period.

For decision makers, these results suggest that interventions targeting the British milk market between 1974 and 2005 would require mechanisms stronger than passive social diffusion to overcome entrenched consumption habits. The simulation indicates that without explicit habit disruption—whether through policy changes, health messaging that exceeds current cognitive dissonance thresholds, or structural changes to availability—population-level dietary transitions toward skimmed varieties proceed slowly when initial preferences are strongly established. Public health strategies should prioritize direct challenges to existing habits rather than relying solely on gradual social influence within fixed network topologies.
