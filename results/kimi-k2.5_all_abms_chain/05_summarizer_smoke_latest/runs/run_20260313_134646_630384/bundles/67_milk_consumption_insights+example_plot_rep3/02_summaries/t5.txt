Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5 milliliters during the initial time step. Between steps 1 and 11, consumption increases rapidly in an approximately linear fashion. This sharp acceleration indicates a period of rapid adoption driven by initial disposition triggers and social influence propagation through the network topology. Between time steps 5 and 10, the rate of accumulation accelerates markedly. This acceleration phase corresponds to the critical mass dynamics of innovation diffusion through the Watts-Strogatz network topology. Early adopting agents leverage interaction probabilities (p-interact = 0.3) and social susceptibility (0.2) to influence neighbors. The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed starts near zero at the initial time step. It increases rapidly and approximately linearly over the first ten time steps. Between time steps 10 and 25, the consumption quantity enters a plateau phase, fluctuating narrowly around 1,220 to 1,250 with minor oscillations. This stabilization suggests that the system has reached a temporary equilibrium where the rate of new adopters slows as habit strength and incumbent preferences create resistance to further switching. The metric enters a transitional phase of gradual acceleration, rising from approximately 1.02 to roughly 1.08 or 1.09. This uptick signals the onset of behavioral adjustment as agents begin incorporating lower-fat milk varieties into their regular consumption patterns. The sustained linear growth after step 10 suggests that interventions targeting milk consumption habits require approximately a decade. Alignment between health perceptions and consumption behavior becomes deeply entrenched in the population over the thirty-year period. The widening gap in later years indicates that without complementary behavioral interventions, continued health messaging may increasingly frustrate consumers by heightening awareness of healthier alternatives.
