This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The primary objective is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence interact within individual decision-making processes regarding milk choice. The S-curve adoption pattern with an extended plateau indicates that dietary behavior change involves distinct phases of rapid early adoption, middle-period resistance, and eventual saturation. The total number of whole milk choices begins at zero and accumulates steadily across all 32 time steps, reaching values between approximately 2,000 and 2,300. The total number of choices made begins at zero and increases linearly at a constant rate of 1,000 choices per step. The linearity suggests that post-adoption habit formation has locked in the new behaviors. This suggests the model captures regular, repeated milk consumption decisions where each agent evaluates options at every discrete interval. The mean habit factor for whole milk begins at a baseline value of 1.00 at step 0. Within the first two steps, the metric undergoes a rapid escalation, converging toward approximately 1.02 across all simulation instances. From step 10 onward, the habit factor resumes a steady, approximately linear ascent, reaching values between 1.044 and 1.055 by step 32. The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at approximately zero and undergoes a rapid increase during the initial five time steps. This initial surge reflects a swift diversification in how agents evaluate milk options as they process health and environmental information and engage in social interactions within the Watts-Strogatz network topology. Policy strategies should address habit disruption and social norm shifts simultaneously rather than relying solely on environmental value messaging, authors say. The critical window for intervention appears to be within the first 10 time steps, before misalignment stabilizes. The persistent deviation suggests that interventions targeting environmental awareness alone may be insufficient to drive consumption changes.
