The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The number of active foragers remained constant at 20 agents throughout the entire simulation duration of 2,312 steps. The metric maintained the initial value of 20 at every recorded time step from step 0 through step 2312, showing zero deviation or variability across the complete temporal sequence. A constant predator population design isolates the effects of prey life history traits and environmental parameters. The cumulative count rises steeply, reaching values between 80 and 110. The aggregate mean across 80 simulation runs reaches its highest value of approximately 120.2 around step 1876. Following step 1850, statistical analyses reveal a decreasing trend in the accumulation rate, with some windows showing no significant trend. Change points occurring around steps 625 and 850 indicating transitions in the killing dynamics. The cumulative count stabilizes into a prolonged plateau phase extending through step 2312 and beyond. Some simulations stabilizing by step 600 while others continue gradual accumulation until step 2000 or later. Combined with the minimal variance in peak prominences, individual simulation runs are converging on maximum cumulative death counts between approximately 225 and 250 individuals. The parameterized hunting pressure is unsustainable and drives the prey population toward functional extinction within the simulated timeframe. Foragers are immortal and operate under optimal foraging constraints with energy storage. The population experiences a statistically significant monotonic decrease (= -1.000, p 0.001), dropping from 125 to approximately 5 individuals by step 710. This phase exhibits oscillatory dynamics with a dominant period of 63 steps, corresponding to seasonal cycling (approximately 5.8 cycles per year given the 365-step annual parameterization) the final change point at step 2310 marks the cessation of the last remaining individuals. Peaks and valleys at indices such as 730, 709, and 802 represent noise in the low-density tail rather than sustainable recovery. The aggregate mean energy across the simulation series is 1,569 units. The median value of 100 indicates substantial right-skewness. An initial rapid descent dominates steps 0 through approximately 300. This steep decline moderates at inflection point 299. Rapid initial collapse within the first year (365 ticks) suggests that hunting pressure immediately exceeds maximum sustainable yield, while the oscillatory pattern indicates failed seasonal recovery attempts. Managers must substantially reduce hunter density, implement hunting moratoria during breeding seasons, or decrease birth-spacing intervals below the current 730-tick (2-year) threshold.
