The male prey population consistently exhibited an extinction trajectory across all 40 simulation replicates, initiating at 125 individuals and declining to zero by approximately 2,284 time steps, where it remained through the end of the observation period at 2,312 steps. The population underwent a rapid initial decline phase, dropping substantially within the first 390 time steps—the first major inflection point—before continuing a decelerating descent punctuated by oscillatory fluctuations.

Throughout the first approximately 1,400 time steps, statistically significant decreasing trends dominated (p < 0.001), with the population falling below 10 individuals by time step 730. During this middle phase, the system displayed oscillatory dynamics with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 time steps, producing local recruitment peaks at time steps 709 (8.8 individuals), 730 (10.5 individuals), 803 (6.8 individuals), and 820 (6.2 individuals), followed by progressively diminishing peaks below 5 individuals after time step 893. These fluctuations occurred within the context of severe population depression, as evidenced by a median population size of only 1.3 individuals across the full simulation duration, compared to a mean of 21.0 that reflects the heavy weighting of the initial high-density phase.

A notable deviation from the declining trend occurred between time steps 1,392 and 1,623, during which the population exhibited a statistically significant increasing trend (tau = 0.435, p < 0.001), suggesting a brief demographic recovery. However, this was followed by periods of no detectable trend (time steps 1,624–2,087) as the population fluctuated near extinction thresholds. Additional inflection points at time steps 755, 817, 1,262, 1,336, and 1,466 marked further transitions in the rate of decline, indicating complex interactions between hunting pressure, herd demography, and environmental constraints.

**Insights for Decision Makers:** The simulation results demonstrate that hunting pressure with the specified parameters (20 hunters with differential processing costs and food values) inexorably drives male megaherbivore populations to extinction, with irreversible decline typically occurring within the first 1,000 time steps. The brief recovery period observed in mid-simulation (approximately time steps 1,400–1,600) was insufficient to prevent ultimate extinction, indicating that conservation interventions must be implemented extremely early in the decline phase—likely within the first 500 time steps—to be effective. The 63-time-step oscillation cycle suggests predictable windows of slight population increase; however, sustainable coexistence would require substantial parameter adjustments to reduce hunting pressure, enhance prey fecundity, or extend lifespan thresholds, as the current configuration produces deterministic extinction outcomes.
