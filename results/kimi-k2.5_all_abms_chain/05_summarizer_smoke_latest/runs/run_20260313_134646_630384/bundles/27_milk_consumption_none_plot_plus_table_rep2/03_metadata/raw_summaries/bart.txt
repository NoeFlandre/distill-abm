 This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005 . It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence .  The simulation demonstrates a consistent monotonic increase in the cumulative adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk throughout the 32-step period representing 1974 to 2005 . The total accumulation of approximately 2,200 choices across 1,000 agents over 32 time steps reflects the declining but persistent market presence of whole milk during this period of dietary transition .  The mean habit factor applied to the whole milk choice function begins at a baseline value of 1.0000 at the initial time step . Following an immediate sharp increase to approximately 1.020 within the first time step, the metric continues to rise gradually and monotonically throughout the subsequent 31 time steps, ultimately reaching 1.0502 at step 32 .  The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices exhibits a monotonically increasing trend throughout the 32-step simulation period representing 1974 to 2005 . The trajectory follows a non-linear growth pattern characterized by distinct phases .  Statistical analysis confirms monotonic increasing trends across all examined windows . The oscillation analysis reveals a dominant period of 15 steps, suggesting underlying cyclical dynamics in the alignment between health values and consumption choices . The average mean value environmental deviation is 12.6015, with a median of 13.4302 and a standard deviation of 2.5933 .
