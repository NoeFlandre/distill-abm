During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 20 steps, the number of households adopting social learning increases rapidly and monotonically across runs, with statistically significant positive trends (p<0.001) and high rates of change exceeding 1.1 additional households per step through step 21. 

Between approximately steps 22 and 40, the adoption rate decelerates considerably, though remaining positive. Inflection points detected at steps 7, 28, 30, 33, and 38 mark transitions where growth slows, with the rate of increase dropping to approximately 0.18 households per step during the window spanning steps 33-43. The trajectory exhibits minor oscillations during this deceleration phase, particularly between steps 22-32, reflecting discrete step-wise increments in adoption counts.

After approximately step 40, the diffusion process approaches saturation, with most runs stabilizing at run-specific plateau values. The aggregate mean across 30 simulations reaches its maximum value of approximately 32 households by step 63 and maintains this level through step 100, indicating that the mean diffusion process largely completes within the first two-thirds of the simulation timeframe. In the final phase from steps 55-65, the rate of increase diminishes to nearly zero (slope approximately 0.008), with change points at steps 40 and 100 demarcating these distinct behavioral regimes.

Substantial heterogeneity exists across stochastic realizations regarding the ultimate extent of social learning adoption. While the aggregate mean stabilizes near 32 households, individual simulation runs exhibit final counts at step 100 ranging from approximately 18 to 54 households. This variation suggests that while the temporal pattern of diffusion remains consistent—characterized by rapid early adoption followed by saturation—the final penetration rate of social learning behaviors depends on stochastic elements or initial conditions that vary between runs.

The observed trajectory aligns with the model's design to evaluate social-ecological feedbacks in heterogeneous populations, where social learning capabilities diffuse rapidly through neighbor interactions and observation mechanisms during the initial 40 steps before reaching equilibrium in the stationary household configuration of the private property grazing system.
