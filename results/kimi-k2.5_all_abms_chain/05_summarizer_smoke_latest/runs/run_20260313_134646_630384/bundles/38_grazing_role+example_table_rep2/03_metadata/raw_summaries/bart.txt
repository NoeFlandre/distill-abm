 The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system . Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods .  The average deviation from recommended livestock stocking levels begins at a minimal value of 0.0074 during the third year of simulation . The average available green biomass across all private pastures begins at approximately 45,000 units and follows a distinct trajectory of initial decline followed by sustained recovery .  The average number of livestock destocked begins at zero and spikes sharply to approximately 6.6 animals per household by year three . Livestock numbers begin increasing around year 44, with the rate of growth peaking between years 44 and 55 before gradually decelerating . The researchers say the dynamics reveal three distinct phases in the adaptation process .  The number of households engaged in social learning begins at zero at the outset of the simulation . Over the first approximately seven time steps, the metric undergoes a period of initial acceleration . Between time steps 7 and 40, the count increases rapidly, rising with steep initial slopes that gradually moderate . By time step 40,  the growth rate decelerates significantly as the system approaches a saturation threshold .  The decline continues through a second phase spanning roughly years 20 to 45, though at a moderated rate compared to the initial drop . From year 45 through the end of the simulation at year 100, the number of persisting E-RO households fluctuates narrowly around a minimum value of approximately 14.7 .
