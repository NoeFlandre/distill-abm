The model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005, specifically capturing the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Within this virtual marketplace, individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions, when whole milk dominated consumer choices. These agents exist within a social network structure where they continuously make milk selections based on their perceptions of product attributes, existing habits, and social pressures from network connections. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers in their network. __________________________________________The primary objective is to replicate the observed historical adoption patterns of milk consumption in Britain, capturing how individual decision-making factors collectively drive macro-level market shifts. The model specifically examines three core influences on consumer choice: perception formation regarding health and environmental attributes, the role of habitual behavior in maintaining existing choices, and social influence mechanisms that facilitate or inhibit change. The simulation compares two distinct psychological mechanisms for when consumers become willing to reconsider their habitual choices: a threshold-based approach where agents must accumulate sufficient motivation to break habits, and a probability-based approach where openness to change occurs stochastically.
