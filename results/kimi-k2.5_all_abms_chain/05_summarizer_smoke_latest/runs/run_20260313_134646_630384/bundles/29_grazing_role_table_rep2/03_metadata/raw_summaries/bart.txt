 The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system . Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods .  The average degree of deviation from recommended livestock numbers follows a distinct non-monotonic trajectory over the 100-year simulation horizon . Deviation reaches its maximum extent around year 33, peaking at approximately 0.38 . Reserve biomass follows a pronounced U-shaped trajectory, marked by an initial degradation phase, a critical minimum, and subsequent sustained recovery .  The initial decline from 90 to 41 animals represents destocking responses to pasture degradation caused by initially excessive grazing pressure . By year 100, holdings reach 47.5472 animals per household, converging toward a long-term mean of 48.7946 animals . This trajectory reflects the adaptation of stocking decisions to ecological constraints under variable rainfall conditions .  A Gini coefficient of effectively zero, the metric rises progressively to reach 0.0726 by year 100, indicating a modest but consistent divergence in livestock health outcomes among households . The trajectory is characterized by ten consecutive decades of statistically significant increasing trends, though the rate of inequality accumulation varies across distinct phases .  The simulation initializes with 20 households employing the E-RO (experiential rule-of-thumb) strategy . During the initial decade, the number of households following this approach exhibits a modest increase, peaking at approximately 21 households around year 8 .
