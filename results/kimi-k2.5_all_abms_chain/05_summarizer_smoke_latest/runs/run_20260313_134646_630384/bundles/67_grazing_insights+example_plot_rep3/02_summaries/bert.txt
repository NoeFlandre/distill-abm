Between steps 40 and 60, a subset of trajectories experiences sharp declines, with some dropping precipitously from moderate positive values to negative territory, reaching as low as - 0. This initial depletion corresponds to the grazing pressure exerted by the initial livestock herds, which are set at 90 animals per household, as the system adjusts to the combined biomass consumption before vegetation recovery processes and adaptive management responses take full effect. This recovery trajectory suggests that the learning mechanisms implemented by the different behavioral types — whether through individual learning - by - doing, social learning, or baseline optimization — enable households to adjust their grazing strategies effectively, allowing vegetation reserves to regenerate beyond initial levels. The persistence of this variance suggests that while all learning mechanisms facilitate ecological recovery above baseline levels, their specific efficiency in optimizing long - term reserve accumulation varies significantly, likely influenced by the interaction between learning strategies, local ecological conditions, and the private property structure of the grazing system. However, the variability in final outcomes suggests that the specific mix of behavioral types in a community significantly influences long - term pasture health, implying that interventions promoting specific learning mechanisms may yield different ecological dividends. This variation likely stems from the interaction of stochastic rainfall patterns (mean 200, standard deviation 100) with the three distinct learning mechanisms, where some runs achieve higher sustainable biomass levels than others. The variance across runs remains moderate during the initial decline but increases substantially during the recovery phase and persists into the steady state, suggesting that small differences in early learning trajectories or climatic realizations compound to produce significantly different pasture conditions over the long term. The wide variation in final biomass levels (spanning 8, 000 units) suggests that relying solely on decentralized learning produces inconsistent ecological outcomes, and supplementary governance mechanisms may be necessary to ensure systems reach higher sustainable states rather than remaining at lower biomass equilibria. Policies targeting the support of social learning and learning - by - doing processes may help shift households toward the higher equilibrium outcomes (58 – 62 units) rather than allowing them to settle into the lower stability traps (41 – 45 units). The mean number of livestock destocked per household initiates at zero and undergoes a rapid escalation during the first five time steps, attaining peak values ranging between approximately 6 and 7. This decrease occurs with substantial oscillations ranging between 1 and 4 animals, reflecting the interaction of high inter - annual rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100) with household learning processes. The high variance across simulation runs during the initial 40 steps gradually diminishes but never fully disappears, indicating that stochastic rainfall variability continues to influence household destocking decisions even after behavioral strategies have stabilized. The high initial destocking rates suggest that initial livestock endowments may exceed sustainable carrying capacities before adaptation occurs, indicating that restocking programs or initial herd size regulations should account for learning curves and adaptation periods to prevent early asset depletion. Finally, the continued oscillations in destocking rates throughout the simulation highlight that climatic variability remains a dominant driver of household vulnerability regardless of behavioral adaptation, underscoring the need for climate - resilient safety nets and insurance mechanisms that operate independently of behavioral learning processes. During this transitional phase, incremental gains occur as the system approaches equilibrium, with some simulations stabilizing near 18 households while others continue ascending toward higher plateaus. This pattern reveals that while social learning capabilities spread rapidly through stationary household networks in private property grazing systems, long - term adoption remains highly contingent on stochastic ecological factors such as rainfall variability and initial spatial configurations of agent types. For decision makers, the rapid initial uptake suggests that social learning interventions can achieve quick early traction within communities, but the persistent variance in final adoption rates underscores the necessity of targeted support mechanisms ; without addressing local barriers to information exchange, a significant proportion of households — potentially up to 70 % — may remain permanently disengaged from social learning processes even after extended periods of exposure. The trajectories that continue rising steeply likely reflect households engaging in proactive, adaptive destocking strategies to maintain pasture health and livelihood resilience, while those that flatten may indicate either successful early optimization requiring minimal subsequent adjustment or conversely, constrained adaptive capacity. The existence of divergent trajectory groups suggests that policies should be designed to accommodate diverse household responses and foster adaptive capacities, as static intervention strategies may be insufficient given the path - dependent nature of outcomes. During the initial 10 to 20 steps, trajectories diverge sharply : approximately half of the simulation runs exhibit a rapid increase in non - learning households, peaking between 38 and 41, while the remainder decline to values between 0 and 10. In household risk attitudes create path dependencies that lock the system into divergent social - ecological states. Insights for decision makers : the unpredictability of behavioral adoption suggests that promoting learning - based grazing management requires targeted intervention early in the process when trajectories are most malleable. Once the system stabilizes, the high persistence of non - learning households in approximately half of the scenarios indicates that relying solely on endogenous social learning may be insufficient for universal adoption of improved practices. External support mechanisms may be necessary to shift systems trapped in high non - learning equilibria toward more adaptive management configurations.
