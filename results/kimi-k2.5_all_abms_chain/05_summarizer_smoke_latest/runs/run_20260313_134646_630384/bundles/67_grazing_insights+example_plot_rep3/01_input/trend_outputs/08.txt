The number of households engaged in social learning initiates at zero across all simulation runs. During the first 20 steps, the count undergoes a rapid increase, rising to values ranging between approximately 12 and 48 households. This swift initial diffusion reflects the early activation of information-sharing mechanisms within the knowledge radius as households begin observing neighbor performance and updating their uncertainty parameters.

Between steps 20 and 40, the growth rate decelerates considerably, with trajectories diverging into distinct saturation levels. During this transitional phase, incremental gains occur as the system approaches equilibrium, with some simulations stabilizing near 18 households while others continue ascending toward higher plateaus.

After approximately step 40, the adoption process largely concludes, and counts remain constant through step 100. Final values demonstrate substantial heterogeneity across stochastic runs, stabilizing at levels ranging from roughly 18 to 54 households. This indicates that ultimately between 30% and 90% of the 60 households engage in social learning behaviors, suggesting the system settles into multiple distinct equilibria regarding the prevalence of adaptive co-management practices.

This pattern reveals that while social learning capabilities spread rapidly through stationary household networks in private property grazing systems, long-term adoption remains highly contingent on stochastic ecological factors such as rainfall variability and initial spatial configurations of agent types. For decision makers, the rapid initial uptake suggests that social learning interventions can achieve quick early traction within communities, but the persistent variance in final adoption rates underscores the necessity of targeted support mechanisms; without addressing local barriers to information exchange, a significant proportion of households—potentially up to 70%—may remain permanently disengaged from social learning processes even after extended periods of exposure.
