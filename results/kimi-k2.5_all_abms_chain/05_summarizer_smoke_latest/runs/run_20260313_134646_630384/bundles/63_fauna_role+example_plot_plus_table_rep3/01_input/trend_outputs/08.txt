The male megaherbivore population initiated at 125 individuals and underwent a deterministic decline toward extinction, reaching zero individuals by approximately tick 2284. The trajectory was characterized by three distinct phases: an initial rapid collapse, an intermediate period of decelerated decline with persistent oscillations, and a terminal phase of asymptotic approach to extinction.

During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 ticks, the population experienced a steep reduction from 125 to roughly 25 individuals. This rapid decrease suggests intense hunting pressure immediately impacting the male cohort, which, given the sexual dimorphism parameters where males offer higher food value (30 vs. 27) but higher processing costs (6 vs. 3), likely made them preferential yet more energetically costly targets for human foragers.

Between ticks 500 and 1500, the decline continued but at a markedly reduced rate, with the population fluctuating between approximately 20 and 2 individuals. This period exhibited oscillatory dynamics with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, likely reflecting seasonal variations in resource availability (given the high seasonality setting of 0.8) interacting with the two-year interbirth interval (730 ticks) of the species. The oscillations suggest that remnant populations experienced temporary recoveries during favorable seasonal conditions before continued hunting pressure drove numbers downward.

After tick 1500, the population entered a terminal decline phase, oscillating at very low abundances (below 5 individuals) before finally reaching extinction at tick 2284. The extended persistence at low densities, followed by eventual elimination, indicates that Allee effects or demographic stochasticity may have operated in these small populations, where the difficulty of finding mates at low densities combined with continued hunting pressure ultimately prevented recovery.

Throughout the simulation, the median population size of 1.35 individuals, contrasted with the mean of 21.03, reflects the strongly right-skewed distribution characteristic of declining populations with intermittent crashes and temporary plateaus.
