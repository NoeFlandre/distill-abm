Over the 100-year simulation period, engagement in social learning exhibits an S-curve diffusion pattern, beginning at zero households and asymptotically approaching approximately 32 households by the final years. The trajectory reveals three distinct phases of behavioral adoption across the farming population.

The initial phase (years 0–10) demonstrates rapid acceleration, with the number of households engaging in social learning increasing sharply from zero to roughly 14–15 households. This represents the steepest adoption rate in the entire series, suggesting that early exposure to environmental variability and neighbor observations quickly demonstrates the value of information-sharing when pastures begin showing stress.

During the middle phase (years 10–40), growth continues but decelerates progressively. By year 40, approximately 30–31 households have adopted social learning behaviors. This inflection point marks a critical transition where the most receptive households have already adopted the practice, leaving a residual population that relies more heavily on private experience or rule-of-thumb approaches.

The final phase (years 40–63) shows greatly diminished growth rates, eventually plateauing at 32 households by year 63. From year 63 through year 100, the count remains stable at this level, indicating that the system has reached equilibrium with roughly 53% of the total farming community regularly engaging in social learning. Notably, this saturation level exceeds the 20 households initially designated as social-learning types, indicating that approximately 12 additional households from the experiential and rule-of-thumb cohorts eventually incorporated neighbor observation into their decision-making repertoires.

The plateau at 32 households—well below the total population of 60—suggests persistent heterogeneity in learning strategies. Despite 100 years of environmental fluctuation and opportunities for information exchange, nearly half the farming population maintains reliance on personal experience or static rules rather than social observation. This equilibrium implies that local pasture conditions, individual risk dispositions, or the costs of information acquisition create niches where private decision-making remains competitive with socially-informed strategies.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The diffusion ceiling at approximately half the population indicates that universal adoption of social learning should not be expected, even with decades of exposure. Interventions promoting information-sharing networks will likely achieve maximum impact within the first decade, with diminishing returns thereafter. Policymakers should recognize that a substantial minority of farmers will continue relying on individualized approaches, suggesting that support systems must accommodate diverse learning styles rather than assuming homogeneous adoption of social information networks. The stability of the final plateau implies that this mixed-learning equilibrium is robust to the high rainfall variability characterizing the system, indicating that both social and private learning strategies can coexist as viable long-term adaptations to environmental uncertainty.
