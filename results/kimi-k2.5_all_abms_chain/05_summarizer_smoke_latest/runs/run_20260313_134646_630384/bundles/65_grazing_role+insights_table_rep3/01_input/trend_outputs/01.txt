The average initial risk attitude among social learning agents remains constant at approximately 0.0074 throughout the entire 100-year simulation period, from the initial timestep through year 100. This value exhibits no variation, trend, or oscillation across any observed interval, maintaining identical magnitude at all recorded points in the simulation horizon.

This stability reflects the static nature of initial conditions in the model design, where risk disposition is assigned as a fixed attribute at the outset and remains unchanged, consistent with the disabled double-loop learning mechanism that prevents agents from fundamentally altering their uncertainty preferences over time.

Implications for decision makers: The invariant nature of initial risk attitudes indicates that differential outcomes between learning strategies arise from how fixed baseline dispositions interact with environmental feedback and social information, rather than from evolving risk perceptions. This suggests that extension services and policy interventions targeting grazing management should prioritize understanding and appropriately matching initial farmer risk profiles, as these baseline attitudes permanently govern how farmers interpret pasture conditions and neighbor behaviors when making stocking decisions under uncertainty.
