 The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system . Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods .  The trajectory of average deviation from recommended livestock levels over the 100-year simulation reveals a three-phase adaptive process characterized by an initial escalation, a subsequent correction, and final stabilization . Implications for decision makers: The trajectory indicates that when introducing private pasture management with learning-based decision strategies, policymakers should anticipate an initial decades-long period where farmer behavior diverges from recommendations .  The grazing system exhibits a three-phase trajectory over the century-long simulation . Initially, aggregate livestock holdings begin at 90 animals per household but undergo a steep decline throughout the first 37 years . The trajectory suggests distinct behavioral phases: an initial period of resource depletion as stationary households apply baseline stocking decisions, followed by a transition period where learning-by-doing and social learning from neighbors within the knowledge radius enable adaptive destocking or management adjustments .  The distribution of healthy livestock among the 60 households began at effectively zero (indicating identical holdings of 90 animals per household) Over the century-long simulation, inequality exhibits a steady monotonic increase, culminating in a Gini coefficient of approximately 0.073 by year 100 .  The trajectory reveals three distinct phases of behavioral adoption across farming population . Nearly half the farming population maintains reliance on personal experience or static rules rather than social observation . Policymakers should recognize that a substantial minority of farmers will continue relying on individualized approaches .  The cyclical patterns indicate that even after stabilization, maintaining flexibility for periodic destocking in response to environmental variability remains essential for sustainable private pasture management . Policy interventions supporting early-stage adaptation and risk management during this transition phase could improve household resilience . The stabilization observed after year 60 suggests that learning and adaptation eventually lead to sustainable management regimes .
