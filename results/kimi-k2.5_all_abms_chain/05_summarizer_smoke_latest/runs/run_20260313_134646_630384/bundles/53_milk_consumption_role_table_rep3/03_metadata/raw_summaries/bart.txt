 The model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005 . It captures how individual decision-making factors collectively drive macro-level market shifts . Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers in their network .  The simulated trajectory of alternative milk consumption follows a pronounced non-linear growth pattern spanning 33 time steps . This represents a massive behavioral shift from negligible initial adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties toward near-dominant market penetration . The trajectory unfolds through three analytically distinct phases marked by specific inflection points and local extrema .  The metric measuring cumulative incumbent product product selections exhibits a monotonic increasing trajectory across the 33 time steps, beginning at zero and accumulating to approximately 2,195 total selections by the final period . The progression demonstrates statistically significant increases throughout all observed intervals, with six consecutive analytical windows confirming sustained growth at high confidence levels .  The simulation reveals a dramatic secular decline in whole milk consumption over the 31-year period . Consumption fell from approximately 2655 units at the 1974 baseline (tick 0) to roughly 253 units by 2005 (tick 32), representing a 90% reduction that captures the historical transition from whole milk dominance to the supremacy of skimmed milk varieties .  The mean habit factor applied to whole milk choice begins at a baseline value of 1.0000 and exhibits a modest, statistically significant monotonic increase throughout the 33 time steps . By the terminal time step representing 2005, consumption reaches 1456.60 units, indicating that skimmed and semi-skimmed milk varieties have achieved dominant market position, completing the historical transition from whole milk dominance .  The simulation reveals a trajectory of increasing heterogeneity in consumer choice evaluation functions across the virtual population spanning the 1974–2005 transition period . The metric measuring deviation between agents' held health values and the values implied by their milk choices exhibits a consistent monotonic increase throughout the simulation period . By 2005, the consumer base has fragmented into highly diverse psychological profiles regarding milk consumption .  The simulation reveals a trajectory of environmental value-behavior divergence that initiates at zero alignment and escalates toward a persistent high-deviation equilibrium . The steady accumulation of deviation rather than abrupt shifts suggests a gradual decoupling of health attitudes from behavioral outcomes throughout the three-decade simulation period .
