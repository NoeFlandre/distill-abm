The study simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. The primary objective is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence interact within individual decision-making processes regarding milk choice. Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment, each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology Average weekly consumption per agent begins at approximately 5.3 units in 1974, representing negligible market presence. During the first decade (1974–1983), consumption exhibits steep, consistent linear growth. Consumption fluctuates between valley points of approximately 1,183 units (observed around 1992) and peak values near 1,265 units (recurring around 1985, 1987, 1993, and 1995), suggesting a 15-year cyclical pattern. The British public maintains constant engagement in milk consumption decision-making throughout the historical transition period, with no evidence of seasonal fluctuations, decision fatigue, or attrition affecting aggregate choice frequency. The simulation output reveals a deterministic linear accumulation of total milk choices across the thirty-two year period from 1974 to 2005. The simulation reveals a transformative shift in consumption patterns over the 32-year period, characterized by an S-curve adoption trajectory with distinct phases of acceleration, oscillatory equilibrium, and late-stage growth. During the first decade of simulation (roughly corresponding to the 1970s through early 1980s), consumption experienced rapid exponential growth, increasing by over two orders of magnitude. This progression indicates a gradual strengthening of habitual consumption patterns toward alternative milk varieties. The British public's decision-making frameworks became increasingly individualized over the three-decade period. The trajectory toward increasing heterogeneity implies that public health campaigns and market strategies must account for highly segmented consumer profiles. Public health campaigns promoting dietary transitions should anticipate and address the cognitive dissonance that arises when health messaging conflicts with habitual consumption patterns. Misalignment between environmental values and actual milk choices widens substantially during the initial decade of dietary transition, then crystallizes into a persistent high-level discrepancy for the remaining two decades. The stability of the plateau after time step 13 suggests that consumption patterns become locked in early, preventing later realignment with environmental values even as social norms and susceptibility continue operating within the network.
