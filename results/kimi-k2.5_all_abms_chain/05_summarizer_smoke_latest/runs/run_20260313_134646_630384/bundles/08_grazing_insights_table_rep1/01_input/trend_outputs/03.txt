The reserve biomass indicator commenced at approximately 90,000 units and followed a distinct U-shaped trajectory over the 100-timestep simulation period. During the initial phase spanning timesteps 0 through 26, the metric exhibited a pronounced declining trend, with the rate of decrease being steepest in the earliest interval (timesteps 0–10) before moderating slightly through timestep 21. This descent culminated in a minimum value of approximately 84,114 units at timestep 26, representing the nadir of pasture conditions.

Following this low point, the system entered a brief transitional phase between timesteps 22 and 32 where the metric stabilized without significant directional movement, indicating a turning point in the grazing system's dynamics. From timestep 33 onward, the reserve biomass entered a sustained period of recovery and growth that persisted through the end of the simulation. This upward trajectory began with moderate increases (timesteps 33–43), accelerated substantially through the middle period—reaching peak growth rates around timesteps 44 to 54—and subsequently continued to rise but at progressively decelerating rates through timestep 100.

By the conclusion of the simulation at timestep 100, the metric reached approximately 121,073 units, substantially exceeding initial conditions. Notable inflection points in the recovery pattern occurred around timesteps 8 and 48, with a significant structural shift in growth dynamics observed near timestep 50. The oscillation patterns superimposed on the overall trend exhibited a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 timesteps, with notable fluctuations clustering around years 10–20 and 41–49, though these did not interrupt the fundamental recovery trajectory.

Implications for decision makers include the recognition that private-property grazing systems employing heterogeneous learning strategies—combining baseline economic rationality with learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms—may experience an initial period of ecological stress lasting roughly 25–30 timesteps before adaptive management practices generate measurable biomass recovery. The eventual overshoot of initial biomass levels suggests that stationary households can achieve long-term pasture improvement without mobility, provided sufficient time for learning processes to optimize grazing pressure. The decelerating growth rate in the final decades implies the system is converging toward a new equilibrium state, indicating that the observed recovery represents a sustainable structural improvement rather than transient fluctuation. Policymakers should therefore account for lag effects in ecological outcomes when evaluating the success of learning-based grazing management interventions, as significant benefits may only materialize after an initial period of resource depletion.
