 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model . It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes .  The average household risk attitude (r-parameter) initiates at minimal values near 0.007 and undergoes a pronounced three-phase trajectory over the 100-timestep sequence . The value near zero indicates that social learning agents began the simulation with minimal inherent tendency to deviate from recommended stocking rates .  The average available green biomass commenced at approximately 45,000 units and concluded at roughly 60,535 units after 100 time steps . Pronounced fluctuations occurred during two distinct intervals: the initial degradation phase (steps 10-20) and the subsequent rapid recovery period (steps 41-49) During the final phase, biomass continued increasing but at progressively diminishing rates .  The dynamics of livestock destocking exhibit a pronounced initial crisis followed by sustained adaptation and long-term stabilization . In the initial phase, destocking spikes dramatically to over 6.6 livestock units by year 3, indicating severe resource constraints as households confront the mismatch between initial herd densities and pasture carrying capacity under variable climatic conditions . The successful transition from collapse to recovery suggests that maintaining diverse learning capabilities among households enhances resilience in smallholder grazing systems .  The number of households engaged in social learning exhibits a sigmoidal diffusion pattern over the 100-timestep simulation period . Some degree of inequality in livestock health outcomes is structurally inevitable in smallholder private-property grazing systems . However, the gradual and bounded nature of this inequality suggests that policy interventions supporting knowledge diffusion and adaptive management can prevent extreme disparities .  The population of households adhering to the non-learning E-RO behavioral strategy exhibits a complex trajectory characterized by an initial ascent followed by a sustained decline and eventual stabilization . Despite the presence of learning mechanisms, the cumulative destocking approaches nearly 97 animals per household by timestep 100, with a median accumulation of approximately 81 animals across the full time series .
