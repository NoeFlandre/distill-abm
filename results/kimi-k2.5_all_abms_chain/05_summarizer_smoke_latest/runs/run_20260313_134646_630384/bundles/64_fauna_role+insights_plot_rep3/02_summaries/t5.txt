The model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape. Each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for the herbivores. The model specifically incorporates sexual dimorphism. Animals-gain-from-food ranges from 0 to 10, set at 1.5, representing energy obtained from grazing. Threshold for starvation mortality, animals-starvation-threshold, ranges between 0 and 100, set at 1. The distance hunters can perceive and pursue prey is determined by foraging-radius. A forager group of 20 individuals may be unsustainable given prey densities of 250 animals and the specified foraging radius of 6 patches. The linear nature of the decline after tick 500 indicates that the system has reached a stable but energetically impoverished equilibrium that will persist indefinitely without intervention. An initial establishment phase spanning roughly 0 to 200 steps records minimal mortality. A deceleration phase ensues as cumulative approaches asymptotic limits. Some forager bands achieve cumulative kills exceeding 100 females by step 400. Others require 600 to 800 steps to reach comparable thresholds. The model predicts near-total loss of breeding females under status quo conditions, effectively eliminating recruitment capacity and ensuring population collapse. By step 1500, cumulative births range from 2 to 10, and by step 2000, final cumulative counts span from 0 to 10 births. The final mortality counts slightly below 250 in some replicates may reflect prey individuals that survived until the simulation termination or died from baseline mortality mechanisms. For conservation applications, this configuration demonstrates no sustainable equilibrium—stochastic variation only delays the inevitable extinction rather than preventing it. Populations typically fall from initial values of 120–125 to ranges of 80–100 females. A minority of simulation runs exhibit extended persistence, with total female energy hovering between 0–500 units until steps 1,500–2,200. However, even these outliers eventually reach zero energy, suggesting complete female population extinction across all parameters tested. The longest surviving trajectory persists until approximately step 2,200, suggesting that small female cohorts might survive 1.5–2 years (550–850 days beyond the initial collapse phase) before ultimate elimination. Population recovery becomes probabilistically improbable. The heterogeneity in collapse timing confirms local ecological stochasticity may delay but cannot prevent extinction under sustained hunting pressure.
