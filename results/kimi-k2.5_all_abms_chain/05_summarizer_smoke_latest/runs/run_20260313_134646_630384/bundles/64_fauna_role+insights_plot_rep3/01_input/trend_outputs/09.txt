Across all simulation replicates, the female megaherbivore population exhibits a deterministic trajectory of decline from initial abundances of approximately 120–125 individuals toward extinction. The populations demonstrate substantial variability in persistence time, with the most rapid collapses occurring within 300–400 time steps, while the most gradual declines endure until approximately 2000–2200 steps before reaching zero females.

The decline dynamics are characterized by stepwise, monotonic decreases rather than smooth exponential decay. During early time steps (0–200), populations typically fall from initial values of 120–125 to ranges of 80–100 females. By approximately 500 steps, most populations have declined below 50 females, with several trajectories approaching zero. Between 500 and 1000 steps, the majority of remaining populations undergo accelerated declines from 20–40 females to extinction, though some trajectories exhibit temporary stabilization at low densities (5–20 females) lasting several hundred steps before final collapse.

These extinction dynamics emerge from the interaction of continuous hunting pressure by 20 immortal foragers utilizing food storage, high environmental seasonality (0.8), and demographic constraints including the 730-tick interbirth interval. The stepwise pattern reflects discrete mortality events likely associated with hunting encounters and seasonal resource bottlenecks, where grass availability fluctuates severely on a landscape limited to 33% initial vegetation cover with 3-tick regrowth periods. The consistent eventual extinction across all stochastic replicates indicates that the per-capita mortality rate from human hunting exceeds the maximum sustainable reproductive capacity of the ungulate population, particularly given movement costs of 1 energy unit per tick and starvation mortality thresholds of 25 units.

For decision makers, these results demonstrate that the current configuration of hunting pressure and environmental seasonality creates a non-viable population scenario where megaherbivore extinction is deterministic rather than stochastic. Sustainable management requires either substantial reduction in forager numbers, elimination of immortal hunter status to create feedback between prey scarcity and hunter mortality, or significant enhancement of habitat productivity to offset the 0.8 seasonality intensity. The high variability in time to extinction suggests that while population collapse is inevitable under these parameters, the exact timing remains subject to demographic stochasticity and environmental fluctuations, necessitating precautionary management approaches rather than reactive measures based on population monitoring.
