Across all simulation replicates, the cumulative number of postpartum females killed exhibits a saturating growth pattern characterized by three distinct temporal phases. During the initial approximately 100 to 200 time steps, accumulation proceeds at highly variable rates between replicates, with cumulative counts ranging from fewer than 5 to approximately 40 individuals killed. This variability suggests stochastic initial spatial distributions of hunters and prey or differences in early encounter probabilities.  

Between roughly 200 and 600 time steps, all trajectories demonstrate rapid, sustained increases, typically adding between 60 and 100 individuals to reach cumulative totals of approximately 100 to 120 by step 600. The slopes during this phase remain steep and relatively consistent, indicating efficient encounter and pursuit dynamics once hunting pressure establishes spatial overlap with breeding herds.  

Following this growth phase, accumulation rates decelerate markedly. Most replicates reach asymptotic stability between 800 and 1,500 steps, with final cumulative totals converging between approximately 110 and 125 individuals. However, substantial variation persists in the timing of saturation: some replicates plateau as early as step 600, while others continue showing sporadic increments until approximately step 2,000. The stepwise nature of the trajectories reflects discrete killing events consistent with agent-based decision making, while the consistent saturation ceiling across all runs suggests the effective depletion of the postpartum female subpopulation from the initial cohort of 250 ungulates.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The consistency of saturation across stochastic replicates indicates that current hunting parameters generate inevitable removal of the reproductive core within a short temporal window, threatening population viability regardless of random variation in spatial dynamics. Management interventions should prioritize reducing encounter rates with postpartum females or altering the energetic calculus that prioritizes them as targets, as the model predicts near-total loss of breeding females under status quo conditions, effectively eliminating recruitment capacity and ensuring population collapse.
