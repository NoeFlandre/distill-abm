Model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. Individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers. Over the first ten time steps, consumption declines precipitously to roughly 960 milliliters, representing a reduction of nearly 64%. This rapid initial decrease suggests that the strong habitual attachment to whole milk proves insufficient to withstand the accumulating health and environmental perceptions agents develop. Per step and perfect positive correlation (tau=1.000) indicates that social network effects eventually overcome habit barriers. The transition from whole to skimmed/semi-skimmed milk achieves substantial penetration early, but the complete market transformation requires sustained social diffusion processes operating over the full temporal horizon. Choices reach approximately 25,644, representing roughly eighty percent of total possible selections across the simulated population and timeframe. The deterministic accumulation confirms that the simulation maintains complete and constant agent participation throughout the entire 1974-2005 time frame. Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk initiates at approximately 5 units at time step 0, reflecting the market dominance of whole milk at the simulation's outset. A second growth phase emerges around step 25, with consumption increasing from approximately 1,220 units to 1,456 units by step 32. The maximum observed value of 1.0502 at step 32 coincides with the simulation terminus, suggesting the habit formation process had not reached saturation within the modeled timeframe. The rapid initial spike to 1.0200 within the first two steps suggests that habitual patterns establish quickly after the simulation begins. The metric reaches approximately 0.67 by the twelfth time step. A third inflection point at step twenty-eight precedes the final phase. The expansion exhibits consistent statistical significance but with monotonically decreasing slopes. The deviation initiates at zero and undergoes a rapid escalation during the initial phase, reaching approximately 13.5 by step 13. This swift emergence of misalignment suggests that as the consumer population begins transitioning from whole milk to alternative varieties, the gap between environmental values and the environmental implications of their actual choices widens dramatically.
