The model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. It captures how individual decision-making factors collectively drive macro-level market shifts. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers in their network. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent initiates at 5.29 units, indicating minimal initial adoption reflective of 1974 market conditions when whole milk dominated consumer preferences. By the end of the simulation period, whole milk has become a niche choice rather than the dominant option. The trajectory features notable oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 15 steps. The median consumption of 1,220.20 units, significantly higher than the mean 1,051.59, indicates that the distribution of outcomes skews toward higher consumption values in the later periods. Successful market transformation requires distinct strategies for different adoption stages. Decision makers should anticipate this stagnation and deploy targeted interventions. The mean whole milk quantity begins at 2,654.81 units, representing the dominant market position of whole milk at the start of the simulation period corresponding to 1974. The decline accelerates between steps 5 and 9, exhibiting the steepest negative trajectory with slopes exceeding -220 units per step, driving consumption down to approximately 1,000 units. During this phase, social influence mechanisms intensify the transition as the 30% interaction probability and network connectivity effects amplify the visibility of alternative milk choices. The mean habit factor for whole milk commences at a baseline value of 1.0000 and follows a consistently increasing trajectory across all 33 simulation steps. The average habitual strength associated with whole milk consumption gradually intensifies rather than diminishes over the 31-year period. Marketing strategists targeting the skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives must account for this hardening of incumbent preferences. The study suggests that habit formation for alternative milk varieties follows a classic diffusion curve with three distinct phases: an initial lag period where incumbent habits suppress new pattern formation, a middle phase of explosive growth, and a late phase of maturing consolidation where growth continues but approaches saturation. The misalignment between British consumers' health values and their actual milk consumption choices begins at perfect alignment (zero deviation) and increases monotonically throughout the 31-year period, reaching approximately 0.725 by 2005. The substantial terminal divergence indicates a permanently fragmented marketplace requiring differentiated product positioning and targeted messaging to address the heterogeneous segments. Market strategists say shifting market share toward alternative products does not automatically resolve value-action gaps. Targeting interventions addressing the psychological mechanisms of habit formation and post-choice justification may be necessary to align consumption patterns with environmental preferences during and after market transitions.
