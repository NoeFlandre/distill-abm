The misalignment between British consumers' health values and their actual milk consumption choices begins at perfect alignment (zero deviation) and increases monotonically throughout the 31-year period, reaching approximately 0.725 by 2005. This expanding value-action gap follows an average trajectory of 0.497, though the median value of 0.558 indicates that deviation levels cluster higher in the latter half of the transition period.

The early phase (1974-1984, approximately steps 0-10) exhibits the most rapid divergence, with deviation increasing at a rate of 0.063 per time step. This initial surge reflects the immediate tension created as health information about skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins penetrating the consumer population, causing health perceptions to shift away from whole milk while strong habitual attachments (initial habit strength of 10.0) maintain existing consumption patterns.

A significant deceleration occurs during the middle phase (1984-1999, approximately steps 10-25), where the growth rate drops precipitously to 0.003-0.004 per time step, with inflection points marking this transition at steps 16 and 19. This plateau suggests a temporary stabilization where social influence mechanisms and accumulated health information successfully prompt a portion of the population to break habitual whole milk consumption, partially reconciling values with behaviors and slowing the expansion of the deviation gap.

However, the final phase (1999-2005, steps 25-32) shows renewed acceleration with the growth rate increasing to 0.019 per time step, culminating at the maximum deviation of 0.725. This late-stage intensification indicates that among the remaining whole milk consumers, the gap between their increasingly favorable health perceptions of alternatives and their persistent habitual choices continues to widen rather than contract.

The trajectory exhibits low variability across population segments during the initial decade but displays oscillatory behavior with a dominant cycle of approximately 15 years in the later stages, particularly around steps 18-20 and 27-31. These fluctuations suggest periodic waves of attempted habit disruption and social influence effects as the population struggles to achieve alignment between evolving health values and behavioral inertia.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The persistent expansion of the value-action gap throughout the entire transition period indicates that health education campaigns successfully shifted consumer perceptions but failed to overcome habitual behavior sufficiently to align actions with values. Public health officials should recognize that information dissemination alone creates psychological tension (cognitive dissonance) rather than behavioral change, as evidenced by the deviation continuing to grow even three decades into the transition. The late-stage acceleration among resistant consumers suggests that targeted interventions specifically designed to disrupt habit formation—such as point-of-purchase environmental changes or intensive social norm marketing—may be necessary to bridge the gap for the remaining incumbent users, rather than relying on general health messaging.
