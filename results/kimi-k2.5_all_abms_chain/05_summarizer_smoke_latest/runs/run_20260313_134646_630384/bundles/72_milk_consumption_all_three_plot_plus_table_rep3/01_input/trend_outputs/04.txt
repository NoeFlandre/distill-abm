The cumulative adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at effectively zero, reflecting the 1974 market conditions when whole milk maintained maximum incumbent habit strength and consumer perceptions of alternative health and environmental benefits remained underdeveloped. Over the initial five time steps, accumulation proceeds at a modest rate of approximately 200 choices per step, as the high habit threshold for whole milk and low baseline health perceptions (1.69) create substantial behavioral inertia resisting early transitions.

Between steps five and ten, the adoption trajectory undergoes marked acceleration, with the rate of increase more than tripling to roughly 737 choices per step. This inflection represents the critical threshold where early adopters—having accumulated sufficient positive perceptions through memory and learning mechanisms—begin breaking established habits, thereby triggering social influence cascades through the network structure. The statistically significant increasing trends detected across all rolling Mann-Kendall windows (p=0.027) confirm that this growth is systematic rather than stochastic.

From step ten through step thirty, the adoption enters a phase of rapid, sustained expansion with the slope stabilizing near 930 to 935 choices per step. During this prolonged middle period, social conformity mechanisms and reduced social blindness facilitate mainstream diffusion as alternative milk choices transition from minority to majority behavior. The consistency of the slope across consecutive five-step windows (ranging from 927 to 936) indicates that once the habit-breaking threshold is breached for a critical mass of the population, social learning and justification mechanisms sustain momentum without requiring continued external forcing.

By step thirty-two, cumulative choices reach approximately 25,644, representing roughly eighty percent of total possible selections across the simulated population and timeframe. The trajectory maintains this steep positive slope through the final observations, suggesting that while adoption has achieved dominant market share, the transition momentum generated by social influence and shifting norms continues to drive growth. The absence of detected peaks or valleys throughout the series confirms the irreversible nature of the dietary transition, while the increasing variance observed across simulation runs toward the latter steps indicates that individual-level timing of adoption remains subject to stochastic social interaction patterns despite the deterministic macro-level trajectory.

For decision makers, the pronounced acceleration between steps five and ten indicates that interventions targeting health perception formation and social network activation during the initial decade are disproportionately critical for triggering self-sustaining adoption cascades. The stabilization of growth rates after step ten suggests that once a critical mass is achieved, social norm mechanisms can maintain transition momentum with minimal additional policy support. Furthermore, the high terminal adoption level approaching market saturation implies that models incorporating habit-breaking thresholds and moderate social susceptibility successfully capture the mechanisms necessary for complete market transformation, suggesting that policies emphasizing social norm visibility may prove more effective than purely informational campaigns once initial habit barriers are overcome.
