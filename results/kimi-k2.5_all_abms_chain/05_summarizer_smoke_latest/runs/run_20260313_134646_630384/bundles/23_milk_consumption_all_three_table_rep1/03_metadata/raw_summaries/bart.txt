 The model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005 . It captures how individual decision-making factors collectively drive macro-level market shifts . Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers in their network .  Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at minimal levels, approximately 5.3 units per agent per week, and follows a pronounced S-curve trajectory over the 31-year period . The trajectory of decline exhibits distinct phases of varying intensity . Consumption increases rapidly with slopes exceeding 139 units per period . This steep ascent reflects the initial breakdown of incumbent habits .  The cumulative count of whole milk selections begins at zero and rises monotonically throughout the simulation period, reaching approximately 2,195 total selection events by the final time step . The series exhibits an S-shaped growth pattern characterized by three distinct inflection points . This trajectory reflects a complete market transition from initial whole milk dominance to widespread alternative milk adoption .  Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at 5.29 units in 1974, representing minimal market presence at the simulation's outset . By 2005, whole milk consumption has declined to roughly one-tenth of its initial level, indicating that the incumbent product has transitioned from a dominant market position to a niche consumption choice .  The mean habit factor applied to whole milk choice begins at a neutral baseline of 1.0 and exhibits a steady, monotonic increase throughout the 33 time steps, reaching approximately 1.05 by the conclusion of the simulation period . The increasing habit factor suggests that continued consumption of whole milk strengthens behavioral inertia .  The deviation between consumers' held health values and their actual milk choices begins at zero, indicating perfect alignment between health perceptions and consumption behavior at the start of the simulation period . By 2005, consumers were choosing milk types that substantially diverged from their personal health valuations, likely maintaining whole milk consumption through habit persistence .  This trajectory suggests that the value-action gap in environmental consumption emerges rapidly during the early transition period (1974-1987) as consumers develop heightened environmental awareness regarding alternative milk varieties . Superimposed on this plateau is an oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of fifteen time steps, manifesting in regular fluctuations around the elevated equilibrium level .
