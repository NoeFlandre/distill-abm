The number of households employing the E-RO (non-learning) behavioral strategy begins at 20 and exhibits an initial, transient increase, reaching a peak of approximately 21.1 households around the eighth timestep. This early expansion likely reflects random initial variation before the comparative advantages of adaptive learning mechanisms become manifest in the system.

Following this peak, the population of E-RO households undergoes a sustained decline characterized by distinct phases. Between timesteps 8 and 20, the number decreases rapidly, passing a critical transition point around timestep 20. This steep initial drop suggests that as learning-capable agents (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1) begin to optimize their decision-making under variable climatic conditions and private property constraints, non-learning households face mounting competitive pressure or higher failure rates.

The decline continues through timestep 45 but at a markedly decelerating rate compared to the initial phase. By this point, the population has fallen substantially below its starting value, operating within a range of approximately 16 to 17 households. From timestep 45 to 92, the decrease progresses gradually, reaching a minimum value of roughly 14.7 households near timestep 92.

In the final phase from timestep 92 to 100, the series stabilizes, maintaining values near 14.7 without significant directional movement. This plateau suggests the system has reached a quasi-equilibrium where a reduced core of E-RO households persists despite the dominance of learning-based strategies, possibly representing agents whose initial risk parameters align sufficiently with environmental variability to avoid failure without adaptation.

For decision makers, the gradual displacement of over 26 percent of the non-learning population over the simulation horizon indicates that in heterogeneous grazing systems under variable rainfall, static behavioral strategies confer significant vulnerability. This suggests that extension programs promoting learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms could enhance long-term household persistence and system resilience in smallholder contexts.
