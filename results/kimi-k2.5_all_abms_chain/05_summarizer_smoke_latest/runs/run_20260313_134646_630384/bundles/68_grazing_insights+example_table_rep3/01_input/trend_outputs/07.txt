The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households begins at effectively zero, indicating perfect equality in initial livestock holdings among all households. Over the course of the 100-timestep simulation, this metric exhibits a consistent monotonic increase, reaching approximately 0.073 by the final timestep. This gradual accumulation of inequality suggests that despite identical starting conditions, heterogeneous household characteristics—specifically variations in the r-parameter representing risk attitudes and sensing uncertainty, combined with differential learning capabilities across the E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types—generate divergent outcomes in livestock health management.

The trajectory is punctuated by several inflection points at timesteps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, 37, and 57, where the rate of inequality growth shifts. These junctures likely correspond to critical periods in the adaptive learning process—such as the consolidation of learning-by-doing experiences or the diffusion of socially learned strategies through the knowledge network—or to feedback cycles between household decisions and pasture biomass conditions. Notably, the system exhibits change points at timesteps 51 and 71, suggesting regime-like transitions in how inequality accumulates, potentially reflecting the saturation of learning effects or the emergence of persistent differentiation between high and low-performing households.

For decision makers, the gradual nature of inequality development—culminating in a Gini coefficient of only 0.073—indicates that the system maintains relative social cohesion even after 100 timesteps, suggesting that private property arrangements combined with adaptive learning capabilities provide buffers against extreme polarization. However, the consistent upward trend across all measurement windows implies that inequality is structurally embedded in the heterogeneity of risk attitudes and learning processes. Policymakers should consider targeted interventions—such as knowledge-sharing platforms to accelerate social learning diffusion or safety-net mechanisms for risk-averse households—before timestep 50, after which inequality appears to accelerate and may become more entrenched and difficult to reverse.
