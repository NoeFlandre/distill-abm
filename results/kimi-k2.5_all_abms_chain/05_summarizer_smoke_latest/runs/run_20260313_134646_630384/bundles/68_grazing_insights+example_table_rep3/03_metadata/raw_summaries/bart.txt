 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model . It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes .  The mean household risk attitude begins at a minimal level of approximately 0.007 during the initial time steps, indicating that households initially operate with very low values of the uncertainty management parameter . This steep ascent suggests an initial phase of active social learning where E-RO-SL1 agents rapidly recalibrate their r-parameters in response to early ecological feedbacks and neighbor observations .  The average livestock holdings per household, inclusive of destocked animals, begins at 90 units and undergoes a pronounced reduction during the initial phase of the simulation . Livestock numbers increase steadily from approximately step 44 through step 100, though the rate of increase decelerates over time . The system culminates at approximately 60,535 units by the hundredth time step, establishing a new equilibrium above initial conditions .  The average destocking rate stabilizes at approximately 0.31 animals per household, maintaining the low-level equilibrium established after the initial adaptation period . Policymakers should consider targeted interventions—such as knowledge-sharing platforms to accelerate social learning diffusion or safety-net mechanisms for risk-averse households .  The average cumulative number of livestock destocked per household begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation . During the initial phase (steps 0–25), destocking occurs most aggressively, with the rate of accumulation exceeding five animals per step in the first ten steps . By step 25, households have typically destocked the majority of their eventual total, suggesting an adjustment to initial pasture conditions .  Between timesteps 8 and 20, the number decreases rapidly, passing a critical transition point around timestep 20 . This steep initial drop suggests that as learning-capable agents (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1) begin to optimize their decision-making under variable climatic conditions . Non-learning households face mounting competitive pressure or higher failure rates .
