Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. The oscillation period of 15 steps and the decreasing amplitude of these fluctuations in later stages indicate that social conformity pressures (0.28) and habit formation dynamics eventually overcome residual resistance among remaining whole milk consumers. The pronounced initial decline indicates that consumers are highly responsive to health and environmental messaging during early campaign phases. Directional trend away from whole milk is robust, precise market share predictions remain sensitive to specific network topologies and social influence parameters. The total number of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices commences at zero and exhibits a monotonic increase throughout the 33-step simulation period, terminating at approximately 25,644 cumulative choices. Accelerated abandonment of whole milk reflects the amplification of health and environmental perceptions through social network interactions among agents. From time step ten to twenty, consumption continues its downward trajectory, ultimately reaching approximately 253 units. Rapid initial adoption curve suggests that public health interventions targeting milk consumption can achieve swift behavioral change when aligned with disposition triggers and social influence mechanisms. However, the subsequent fifteen-year plateau indicates that significant market segments resist further change once habits consolidate, requiring sustained normative pressure rather than one-time informational campaigns. The deviation begins at 0.000, representing perfect alignment between consumers' held health values and the health implications of their milk choices. Between steps 10 and 25, the rate of increase slows considerably, entering a plateau phase where the deviation gradually rises from approximately 0.55 to 0.60. This deceleration reflects the tension between emerging health awareness and the persistent influence of incumbent habits and social norms that moderate the pace of behavioral transition.
