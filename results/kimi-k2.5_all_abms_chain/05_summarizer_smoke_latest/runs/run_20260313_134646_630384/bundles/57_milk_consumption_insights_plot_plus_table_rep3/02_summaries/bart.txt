This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence, and post-choice evaluation where agents may adjust future decisions. Average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent exhibited a clear upward trajectory, beginning at approximately 5.29 units and culminating at 1456.60 units by the final step. Public health officials and dairy industry stakeholders should anticipate logistic-like adoption curves when promoting dietary transitions. Simulation results show a cumulative adoption trajectory of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices rising monotonically from zero to approximately 25,644 total selections over 33 time steps. The dynamics unfold through three distinct phases reflecting the interplay of habit strength, social influence, and health perception mechanisms in the agent population. Simulation of milk consumption adoption from 1974 to 2005 reveals a three-phase trajectory in the aggregate consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The mean habit factor for whole milk initiates at a value of 1.0000 at time step 0 and follows a monotonically increasing trajectory throughout the simulation period. The S-curve pattern suggests that once agents begin consuming skimmed or semi-skimmed milk regularly, the psychological mechanisms of habit formation create compounding momentum that progressively strengthens preference for these alternatives. Policymakers can leverage this compounding effect by maintaining consistent messaging through the transition period. The study quantifies the misalignment between agents' held health values and the health implications of their actual milk choices. The average discrepancy increased substantially over the simulation timeframe. Public health strategists should recognize that successful behavioral change campaigns may create growing misalignments between personal values and behaviors. Once the deviation stabilizes around step 13-15, misalignment between environmental values and consumption patterns becomes resistant to natural market correction. Passive adoption models may perpetuate environmentally suboptimal choices. Proactive policies targeting the early phase of market transition are essential to prevent the system from locking into a persistent high-deviation state.
