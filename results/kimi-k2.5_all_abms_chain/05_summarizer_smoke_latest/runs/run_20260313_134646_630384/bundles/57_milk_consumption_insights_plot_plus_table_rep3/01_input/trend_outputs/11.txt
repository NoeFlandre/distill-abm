**Implications for Decision Makers:**

These trends suggest that the historical transition from whole milk to lower-fat varieties was accompanied by growing cognitive dissonance among consumers. The initial rapid rise in deviation indicates that early adopters of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk often made these choices despite holding health values that would not naturally support such selections, possibly driven by emerging health information or early social signaling. The extended plateau during the middle period suggests a temporary equilibrium where behavioral change and value alignment briefly stabilized, representing a potential window where interventions could have effectively aligned beliefs with behaviors.

The pronounced acceleration in the final years implies that late-stage adoption was increasingly driven by social influence and conformity pressures rather than genuine health consciousness, as the deviation grew substantially while approaching 0.7251. Public health strategists should recognize that successful behavioral change campaigns may create growing misalignments between personal values and behaviors, particularly when social norms become the primary drivers of consumption patterns. The sustained divergence indicates that many consumers adopted lower-fat milk varieties for reasons other than personal health valuation, suggesting that maintenance of behavioral change may depend heavily on continued social norm reinforcement rather than intrinsic motivation. Policymakers might consider either educational interventions to align consumer values with their choices or alternatively, leverage the demonstrated power of social influence mechanisms to sustain the transition even when intrinsic health motivation lags behind actual behavior.
