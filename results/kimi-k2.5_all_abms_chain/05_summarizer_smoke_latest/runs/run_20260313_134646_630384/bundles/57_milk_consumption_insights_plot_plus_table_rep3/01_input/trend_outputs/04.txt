The simulation results show a cumulative adoption trajectory of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices rising monotonically from zero to approximately 25,644 total selections over 33 time steps, representing the period from 1974 to 2005. The dynamics unfold through three distinct phases reflecting the interplay of habit strength, social influence, and health perception mechanisms in the agent population.

During the initial phase spanning steps 0 through 5, cumulative alternative milk choices remain minimal, accumulating to approximately 1,000-1,500 selections. This protracted lag corresponds to the maximum initial habit strength parameter (10.0) binding agents to whole milk consumption, creating substantial inertia against the adoption of lower-fat alternatives despite initial health perception values averaging 1.69 and environmental perceptions at 1.12.

The transition phase between steps 5 and 15 exhibits rapid acceleration in adoption rates. The rate of increase escalates from approximately 201 choices per step in the earliest interval to 737 choices per step during steps 5-9, then further to 927 choices per step during steps 10-14. By step 10, cumulative choices reach approximately 5,000, expanding to nearly 15,000 by step 20. This acceleration reflects the activation of disposition mechanisms—whether threshold-based or probability-based—as agents' cognitive dissonance thresholds (0.15) are exceeded, triggering reconsideration of entrenched habits. The Watts-Strogatz network topology facilitates behavioral contagion through interaction probabilities of 0.3 and social susceptibility parameters of 0.2, enabling social norms to progressively override initial habit dominance.

From step 15 onward, the adoption trajectory stabilizes into sustained linear growth with a consistent rate of approximately 935 additional choices per step, continuing through step 32. This linear regime indicates that the system has reached a steady-state transition rate where the combined effects of memory lifetime (5.0), social conformity (0.28), and justification mechanisms (0.61) maintain a constant flow of adoption decisions without the saturation dynamics typical of standard diffusion models. The variance across the 30 simulation replications remains constrained throughout, suggesting robust behavioral dynamics under the specified parameterization.

**Implications for decision makers**: The pronounced five-step lag phase demonstrates that dietary transitions face substantial inertia from entrenched consumption habits, requiring sustained intervention efforts before measurable behavioral shifts emerge. The subsequent acceleration phase indicates that investments in health messaging and social network activation yield increasing marginal returns once critical mass is achieved, with adoption rates quadrupling within ten steps. The stabilization of growth at approximately 935 choices per step in later periods suggests that the modeled transition mechanisms successfully capture the historical British shift toward lower-fat milk varieties, providing a predictable framework for forecasting market evolution during food system transitions. Policy instruments should prioritize overcoming initial habit thresholds through targeted early interventions, then leverage social influence dynamics to maintain momentum, as the linear late-stage growth implies that behavioral change becomes self-sustaining through network effects and norm formation without requiring escalating intervention intensity.
