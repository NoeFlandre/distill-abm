The simulation reveals a pronounced long-term decline in average weekly whole milk consumption per person across the 33 time steps representing the period from 1974 to 2005. Initial consumption levels begin at 2654.81 units, reflecting the market dominance of whole milk in 1974, and ultimately decrease to 253.10 units by the final time step, indicating a substantial shift away from whole milk over the simulation period.

The trajectory follows a nonlinear pattern characterized by distinct phases of varying velocity. During the initial phase (approximately time steps 0-10), consumption declines steeply from 2654.81 to roughly 900-1100 units, representing the most rapid period of transition. This sharp decrease suggests that early adopters quickly respond to emerging health and environmental perceptions regarding alternative milk varieties.

Between approximately time steps 10 and 25, the rate of decline moderates considerably. This middle phase exhibits localized volatility, with consumption reaching a temporary minimum of 760.00 units at step 18 before rebounding to 773.96 units at step 19, then resuming decline. Similar oscillations appear later in the series: a valley of 594.35 units at step 26 followed by a peak of 620.98 units at step 27, and another valley of 323.46 units at step 29 followed by a peak of 335.18 units at step 30. These fluctuations suggest temporary stabilization or minor resurgences in consumption among certain agent subgroups before continued downward movement.

Inflection points occurring at steps 5, 13, 16, 19, 24, 25, 27, and 30 indicate multiple shifts in the acceleration of behavioral change throughout the transition. The most pronounced structural change occurs at the final time step (32), where consumption reaches its minimum value of 253.10 units.

Statistical testing confirms significant decreasing trends during the early rapid transition (steps 0-14), with particularly strong negative slopes in the initial windows. However, periods of statistical indeterminacy emerge between steps 15-19 and 25-29, corresponding to the observed volatility and suggesting that the decline temporarily loses momentum during these intervals.

Implications for decision makers: The steep early decline indicates that initial awareness campaigns targeting health and environmental perceptions can achieve rapid behavioral shifts among receptive populations, but the subsequent volatility demonstrates that sustained transition requires persistent intervention rather than one-time efforts. The repeated pattern of valleys followed by peaks (steps 18-19, 26-27, 29-30) suggests that habit formation and social network effects create measurable resistance periods where consumption temporarily stabilizes or rebounds, indicating that policymakers should anticipate non-linear adoption curves with potential temporary reversals. The substantial residual consumption remaining at the end of the simulation (253.10 units, approximately 9.5% of initial levels) suggests that a persistent minority maintains whole milk preferences despite prolonged exposure to alternative social norms, implying that complete market transformation may be unrealistic and resources might be better allocated to incremental reduction strategies or targeting specific demographic segments rather than universal conversion goals.
