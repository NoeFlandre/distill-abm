The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock begins at approximately zero, indicating that all 60 households started with perfectly equal endowments of 90 animals each. During the initial 20-year period, inequality remained minimal, with values rising only slightly to between 0.005 and 0.015. This early phase reflects the initial buffering capacity of the system as all households operated under similar starting conditions before the effects of decision-making strategies and environmental variability became apparent.

Between years 20 and 60, the metric exhibited accelerating divergence across simulation runs. By year 40, values spread across a range of approximately 0.01 to 0.03, and by year 60, the divergence intensified to span roughly 0.025 to 0.05. This mid-period acceleration coincides with the cumulative impact of annual rainfall variability (with standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200) interacting with the three distinct learning strategies employed by the household groups.

From year 60 to 100, the trajectories separated into distinct tiers, resulting in a wide dispersion of inequality outcomes by the final timestep. Terminal values at year 100 ranged from approximately 0.03 in the most equitable outcomes to nearly 0.095 in the most unequal scenarios. The lower bound of this range suggests certain learning configurations maintained relatively moderate inequality despite environmental uncertainty, while the upper bound indicates conditions where livestock health became highly concentrated among a subset of households.

These trends demonstrate that under conditions of significant rainfall stochasticity and fixed behavioral strategies, inequality in livestock health endowments increases monotonically over time. The substantial variation in terminal Gini coefficients across the 100-year horizon suggests that the interaction between specific learning approaches—whether baseline rule-of-thumb, individual learning-by-doing, or social learning from neighbors—and stochastic environmental feedbacks generates fundamentally different distributional outcomes. The absence of livestock reproduction (b=0) indicates that these inequalities arise solely from differential success in preserving existing animal stocks under varying pasture conditions, mediated by how each strategy processes information about pasture health within the restricted knowledge radius of one patch.
