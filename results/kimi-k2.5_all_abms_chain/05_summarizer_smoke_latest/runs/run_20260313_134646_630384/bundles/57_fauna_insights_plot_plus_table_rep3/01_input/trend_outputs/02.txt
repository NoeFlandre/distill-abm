The total energy available to forager bands exhibits a pronounced and statistically significant decreasing trend across 40 simulation replicates spanning 2312 ticks. Initializing at 2000 energy units, the aggregate energy plummets to approximately -38,229 by tick 2312, with an absolute minimum of -38,233 recorded at tick 2310. The mean energy across all series averages -15,983.7, while the median reaches -15,095.9, indicating a consistent downward trajectory with substantial cumulative deficits.

The rate of energy depletion evolves through distinct phases. During the initial approximately 650 ticks, the decline proceeds at a variable rate, initially decreasing by roughly 11.1 units per tick, then moderating to approximately 8.8 units per tick between ticks 232 and 463. A marked acceleration occurs between ticks 650 and 1160, where depletion rates intensify from -15.7 to -19.6 units per tick. After tick 1160, the descent stabilizes at a consistent rate of approximately -20.0 energy units per tick for the remainder of the simulation, as confirmed by significant Mann-Kendall test results (p < 0.001) across all rolling windows.

Superimposed upon this steady decline, oscillatory behavior emerges prominently in the final simulation stages (post-tick 2170), characterized by a dominant periodicity of 63 ticks. These oscillations likely reflect interactions between seasonal environmental limitations (seasonality parameter set to 0.8), grass regrowth cycles (3 ticks), and forager movement patterns.

Within the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model context, these dynamics indicate that forager populations consistently operate under severe metabolic deficits. Despite immortal status preventing mortality from starvation and the activation of energy storage capabilities, hunting returns cumulatively fail to offset movement expenditures (set at 1 unit per movement) and processing costs (3-6 units depending on prey sex). The linear accumulation of energy debt suggests that prey encounter rates and food values (27-30 units) are insufficient to sustain the 20 initial foragers under current environmental productivity (grass proportion 0.33 with seasonality effects). The persistent negative slope implies that foragers increasingly deplete their energy reserves—or accumulate debt—regardless of prey population dynamics, while late-stage oscillations hint at seasonal hunting efficiency variations or cyclic prey vulnerability windows.

For decision makers, these results demonstrate that current parameter configurations generate unsustainable foraging economics, yielding deteriorating energy balances that would incapacitate mortal forager populations. To achieve energy equilibrium or surplus, adjustments should prioritize reducing forager movement costs, increasing prey food values or abundance, enhancing hunting success rates, or mitigating seasonal resource bottlenecks. The consistent -20 unit per tick deficit rate observed after tick 1160 provides a quantitative benchmark for calibrating necessary intervention magnitudes to prevent cumulative energy exhaustion.
