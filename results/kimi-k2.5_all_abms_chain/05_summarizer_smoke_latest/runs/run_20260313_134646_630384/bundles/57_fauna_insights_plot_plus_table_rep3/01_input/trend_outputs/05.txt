The cumulative mortality of postpartum female prey initiates at zero and undergoes rapid accumulation during the initial approximately 500 time steps, reaching values between 60 and 100 individuals. Inflection points detected at steps 245, 262, and 285 mark the transition from accelerating to decelerating growth rates during this early phase, characterized by statistically significant increasing trends.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the accumulation rate diminishes substantially, with structural change points identified at steps 475, 625, and 850 indicating shifts in hunting dynamics. During this transitional period, the cumulative count rises from approximately 80–100 to 110–115 individuals. The Mann-Kendall trend analysis shows weakening but persistent increasing patterns through step 1391, with slopes decreasing from 0.213 to 0.001.

After approximately 1000 to 1500 steps, individual trajectories stabilize at asymptotic values ranging from 105 to 125, with the aggregate mean series reaching a maximum value of 119 around step 1826. By step 2312, the mean stabilizes at 117, though individual realizations plateau at different times between approximately step 1000 and step 2000. The period between steps 1392 and 1623 exhibits no significant trend, followed by a decreasing trend in the aggregate mean after step 1856 as individual simulation runs reach their respective saturation points. Superimposed on this saturation pattern is a periodic oscillation with a 63-step cycle.

Implications for decision makers: The hunting mortality of reproductive females is heavily front-loaded, with approximately 85–90 percent of the total cumulative mortality occurring within the first 1000 steps (approximately 2.7 years). The rapid saturation suggests that hunting pressure quickly depletes the pool of postpartum females or drives prey populations to levels where encountering breeding females becomes rare. This timeline indicates that megaherbivore populations face acute risk of reproductive collapse within the first few years of sustained hunting pressure targeting females with offspring. Conservation and management strategies must prioritize immediate protection of breeding females during initial contact periods, as delayed intervention would occur after the demographic damage has largely been sustained. The consistent asymptotic values across 40 stochastic realizations demonstrate robustness in the extinction dynamics, suggesting that outcomes are deterministic regarding reproductive female depletion despite stochastic variation in individual hunting events.
