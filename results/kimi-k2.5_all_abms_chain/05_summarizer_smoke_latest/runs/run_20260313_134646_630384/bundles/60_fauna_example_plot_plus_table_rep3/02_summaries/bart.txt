The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The population of human hunter-gatherer bands remains fixed at 20 throughout the entire simulation duration, from the initial time step through approximately 2,300 time steps. The stability arises from the model configuration where foragers are immortal and do not reproduce, maintaining the initial population size of 20 bands regardless of energy levels, hunting success or environmental conditions. The cumulative number of female prey killed begins at zero and increases throughout the simulation, ultimately reaching approximately 123 individuals on average across simulation runs, with individual runs terminating between roughly 108 and 124 kills. The cumulative mortality of postpartum female prey increases rapidly during the initial approximately 475 time steps, with the mean value reaching approximately 119 by this point.
