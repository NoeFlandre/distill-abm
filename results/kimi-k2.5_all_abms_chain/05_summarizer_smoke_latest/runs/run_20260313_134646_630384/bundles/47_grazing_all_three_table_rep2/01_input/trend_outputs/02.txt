The average deviation from recommended livestock levels begins near zero at approximately 0.007 during the initial years, indicating that households initially maintain stocking rates very close to ecological recommendations. This alignment rapidly deteriorates over the first three decades, with deviation increasing significantly to reach a maximum of 0.38 around year 33. This sharp rise suggests that initial learning processes—whether through individual experimentation or social observation—initially drive farmers away from optimal stocking densities as they respond to short-term productivity signals or neighbor behaviors without fully accounting for long-term carrying capacity.

Following this peak, the system enters a prolonged correction phase lasting approximately until year 65, during which deviation steadily declines from 0.38 to roughly 0.19. This downward trend likely reflects the activation of negative feedback mechanisms as degraded pasture conditions force households to adjust expectations and reduce stock numbers toward more sustainable levels. The decline is not uniform but exhibits significant oscillatory behavior throughout this period, with a dominant cyclical pattern of approximately 49 years suggesting that learning and adjustment occur through boom-bust cycles of overstocking followed by corrective destocking.

After year 65, the deviation stabilizes around 0.19 through the end of the simulation at year 100, showing no significant further trend. This persistent plateau indicates that while the learning mechanisms eventually prevent catastrophic divergence from recommendations, they converge on a suboptimal equilibrium rather than returning to the initial low-deviation state. The system settles into a state where households consistently maintain livestock levels approximately 19% above or below optimal recommendations, suggesting that incomplete information, heterogeneous learning strategies among the three behavioral types, or risk aversion prevents perfect optimization even after decades of adaptation.

Key transition points occur around years 12, 39, and 50, where the trajectory of deviation shifts significantly, marking transitions from rapid learning to correction, and finally to stabilization.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The early simulation period (years 0-33) represents a critical vulnerability window where learning-induced overstocking peaks, suggesting that external extension services or temporary stocking limits may be most valuable during the first three decades of system operation. The eventual stabilization at 19% deviation implies that autonomous learning among private pastoralists may be insufficient to achieve optimal resource use, warranting continued institutional support for sustainable grazing education. Furthermore, the 49-year oscillation cycle indicates that short-term assessments of grazing system performance may miss important long-term fluctuations, requiring monitoring programs that span multiple decades to accurately evaluate management outcomes.
