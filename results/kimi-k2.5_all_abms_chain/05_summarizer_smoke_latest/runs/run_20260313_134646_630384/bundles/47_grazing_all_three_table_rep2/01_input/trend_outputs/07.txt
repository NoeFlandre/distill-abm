The distribution of healthy livestock among farming households begins in a state of near-perfect equality, with all agents possessing identical initial herd sizes of 90 animals. Over the century-long simulation, inequality gradually emerges, culminating in a Gini coefficient of approximately 0.073 by year 100. This represents a transition from complete parity to modest but measurable differentiation in productive assets across the farming population.

The initial eight years mark the beginning of divergence, as the three distinct learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—begin processing environmental feedback differently under variable rainfall conditions. By years 13 and 15, the rate of differentiation accelerates, suggesting that early adaptive decisions create compounding advantages or disadvantages. The first two decades witness the establishment of initial stratification patterns as households with more effective learning mechanisms begin preserving livestock health more successfully than those relying on static heuristics.

The period between years 20 and 50 shows continued increases in inequality, with notable inflections around years 25 and 37 indicating shifts in the velocity of differentiation. The transition point at year 51 marks a significant threshold where the gap between well-performing and struggling households begins to widen more distinctly. This mid-simulation inflection likely reflects the point where cumulative effects of stocking decisions under stochastic rainfall have created persistent heterogeneity in pasture conditions and herd health across the landscape.

Following the change point at year 71, the system enters a phase characterized by oscillatory behavior between years 78 and 93, superimposed on the underlying upward trend. These fluctuations suggest cyclical patterns of adjustment as households with depleted herds attempt recovery while successful households experience temporary setbacks due to rainfall variability or social learning transmission effects. Despite these oscillations, the fundamental trajectory toward greater inequality persists through to year 100.

The emergence of inequality stems from the differential effectiveness of learning strategies in responding to environmental uncertainty. The baseline rule-of-thumb approach likely fails to adjust adequately to rainfall variability, while learning-by-doing and social learning enable some households to maintain healthier herds. The relatively modest final inequality level (0.073) suggests that risk disposition parameters and knowledge exchange within the defined radius of one patch provide some buffering against extreme asset concentration, preventing the collapse of vulnerable households while allowing successful strategies to yield modest advantages.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The consistent increase in livestock inequality indicates that in private pasture systems with heterogeneous learning capacities, uniform starting conditions do not ensure equitable long-term outcomes. Interventions targeting information sharing—particularly during the first two decades when inequality trajectories crystallize—could mitigate divergence. The oscillatory patterns in later years suggest natural windows of opportunity for lagging households to recover, indicating that temporary assistance programs timed with these fluctuations may prove more effective than continuous support. The persistence of inequality despite single-loop learning suggests that enabling households to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) might be necessary to prevent long-term asset stratification in smallholder communities.
