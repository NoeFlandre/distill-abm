This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. These operate through a yearly sequence where green biomass updates, households with E-RO-SL1 types update their r-parameters, households update behavioral strategies (if switching is enabled), make livestock placement decisions, livestock feeds on biomass, households observe neighbor performance, reserve biomass updates, and households observe and remember reserve biomass states.

The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. It seeks to evaluate how learning capabilities affect household resilience and pasture conditions in private property grazing systems. Specific objectives include analyzing the impact of social learning double-loop processes through strategy switching in heterogeneous populations, observing the diffusion and competition between different learning behaviors in mixed populations, and assessing how demographic settings and initial herd sizes influence system outcomes. The model allows for the evaluation of outcomes across social, ecological, and economic dimensions including average herd size, pasture biomass condition, and surviving number of households.

The average initial risk attitude of social learning agents remains constant throughout the simulation period from step 0 to step 100, maintaining values distributed across a range of approximately -0.11 to 0.18. Each trajectory holds its specific initial value without temporal variation, reflecting that this metric captures fixed baseline attributes assigned to agents at initialization rather than dynamic variables that evolve over time. The dispersion across simulation runs indicates heterogeneity in the initial assignment of risk attitudes, with some populations exhibiting negative initial dispositions (approximately -0.11 to -0.03), others near neutral (approximately 0.00 to 0.05), and others with positive dispositions (approximately 0.07 to 0.18) regarding deviation from recommended livestock numbers. This stability confirms that while agents may update their current risk parameters through social learning processes during the simulation, their initial reference values remain unchanged as static system properties.

The mean household risk attitude starts at step 0 with values distributed approximately between -0.1 and 0.2 across different simulation runs. During the first 20 time steps, the system undergoes rapid divergence characterized by sharp increases in risk attitude in many runs, with values rising rapidly to ranges between 0.4 and 0.8, while other runs remain at lower levels or experience modest declines. This initial phase represents the activation of learning processes as households adjust their uncertainty management parameters based on early experiences and observations.

Between steps 20 and 40, most trajectories stabilize into distinct equilibrium levels. Several runs converge to high risk attitudes of approximately 0.75 to 0.8, indicating strong uncertainty aversion. Other simulation runs stabilize at intermediate levels of roughly 0.6 to 0.7, 0.5, 0.3 to 0.4, or remain at low positive values near 0.1 to 0.2. A subset of trajectories maintains values near zero or slightly negative.

Around steps 50 to 60, abrupt transitions occur in several runs, where risk attitudes drop sharply by approximately 0.2 to 0.5 units, shifting from moderate positive values to near-zero or negative ranges, with some reaching approximately -0.35. By step 100, the system exhibits a broad distribution of stable mean risk attitudes spanning from approximately -0.35 to 0.78.

The high variance across simulation runs indicates strong path dependency in the learning dynamics, where stochastic elements during the initial adaptation phase lead to divergent long-term behavioral configurations. The existence of multiple stable equilibria suggests that the combination of learning-by-doing and social learning processes in the three behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) can result in persistent differences in household risk management strategies, with some populations converging to highly risk-averse postures while others adopt risk-neutral or risk-seeking behaviors. The late transitions observed around steps 50-60 suggest that strategy switching or social learning interactions can occasionally trigger regime shifts even after extended periods of stability.

The mean reserve biomass begins at approximately 90,000 units. During the initial 5 to 10 steps, the metric remains relatively stable with a marginal increase to roughly 90,500 to 91,000, after which it enters a decline phase persisting for approximately 25 to 35 steps. This decline reaches minimum values ranging between 80,000 and 88,000 across different simulation instances, with the lowest points occurring at varying times—some instances reaching their nadir around step 30 while others continue declining until approximately step 35 to 40.

Following this minimum, the biomass undergoes a rapid, sigmoid-patterned increase over approximately 25 to 30 steps, with the most pronounced growth occurring between steps 45 and 65. The rate of accumulation accelerates sharply during the interval from step 50 to step 60, then gradually decelerates as the system approaches equilibrium.

By approximately step 70 to 80, the biomass stabilizes at values ranging from roughly 111,000 to 128,000, substantially exceeding the initial conditions. These final equilibrium levels persist consistently through step 100, with considerable variation across simulation instances—the highest stabilized values reach approximately 128,000 while the lowest plateau around 111,000 to 113,000, representing a spread of roughly 15,000 to 17,000 units.

The initial stability followed by decline reflects the impact of livestock grazing pressure as households utilize their private pastures, temporarily extracting biomass at rates exceeding natural regeneration. The subsequent recovery and elevation above initial levels indicate that adaptive learning processes—encompassing both learning-by-doing and social learning—enable households to optimize grazing strategies, allowing vegetation to regenerate and accumulate to higher equilibrium states. The variation in final biomass levels across instances suggests that stochastic climatic factors, specifically the high rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 around a mean of 200), combined with heterogeneity in household risk attitudes and learning outcomes, generate divergent long-term ecological states. Systems achieving the highest equilibrium levels likely represent configurations where effective adaptive co-management successfully balances grazing pressure with vegetation recovery dynamics, while lower equilibrium instances may reflect less optimal adaptation pathways or adverse stochastic rainfall sequences.

The mean green biomass starts at approximately 45,000 units at step 0. Over the first 10 to 20 steps, it declines rapidly to a minimum of roughly 40,000 to 41,000. This initial reduction likely corresponds to the immediate grazing pressure exerted by the initial livestock population on the available vegetation.

Following this decline, the biomass enters a recovery phase beginning around step 20. From step 20 to approximately step 45, the biomass increases gradually from 41,000 to around 45,000. Subsequently, between steps 45 and 60, the system undergoes a period of rapid growth where the biomass escalates steeply from 45,000 to values between 60,000 and 64,000. This growth trajectory reflects the vegetation's response to rainfall inputs and the balance between biomass production and consumption as households manage their herds.

After step 60 to 70, the growth rate decelerates markedly as the system approaches equilibrium. From step 70 through step 100, the biomass stabilizes into a plateau phase, with final values ranging approximately from 55,000 to 64,000 across different simulation runs. This plateau represents the long-term carrying capacity of the system where green biomass production balances grazing consumption under the prevailing climatic and management conditions.

The substantial variation in final biomass values across runs indicates the influence of heterogeneous household behaviors, learning mechanisms, and stochastic environmental factors on system outcomes.

The average total livestock inventory, inclusive of destocked animals, commences at 90 units per household at the initiation of the simulation. Over the initial 20 steps, this metric undergoes a precipitous decline, falling to approximately 45–50 units, reflecting immediate destocking responses as households adjust to initial pasture biomass constraints and ecological feedbacks. The decline continues at a decelerated rate through step 40, reaching minimum threshold values of roughly 40–43 units, representing the nadir of livestock holdings across the majority of simulation trajectories.

Beyond step 40, the system exhibits substantial divergence in outcomes. A significant subset of trajectories stabilizes at lower equilibrium levels, maintaining approximately 41–43 units through step 100, suggesting persistent low-level equilibria where destocking pressures balance with reproduction or acquisition. The majority of pathways demonstrate moderate recovery from the minimum, gradually increasing to stabilize between 45 and 52 units by step 60–80, indicating successful adaptation to grazing pressures through behavioral adjustments.

Two distinct trajectories exhibit markedly superior performance: one recovers to approximately 59 units by step 100, while another achieves the highest observed level of approximately 62 units. These elevated pathways suggest that certain configurations of learning mechanisms—likely those leveraging learning-by-doing or social learning processes enabled by the E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types—enable households to maintain substantially larger herds while managing vegetation dynamics sustainably. By step 80, all trajectories stabilize, indicating the attainment of distinct long-term equilibria that vary by approximately 21 units (from 41 to 62) depending on the effectiveness of the adaptive decision-making strategies employed.

The mean number of livestock destocked per household initiates at zero, then rises sharply within the first 5 time steps to reach peak values between 6 and 7.5. This initial surge reflects immediate resource pressure following system initialization, where households begin with 90 livestock units under variable rainfall conditions (mean 200, standard deviation 100), creating an initial mismatch between animal density and pasture carrying capacity in the stationary private property system.

Following this peak, the metric declines rapidly through approximately time step 20, dropping to values ranging between 0.5 and 2.0 across different simulation runs. This steep reduction indicates active destocking responses as the three behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) adapt to biomass constraints, with households reducing herd sizes to align available forage with animal needs.

Between time steps 20 and 40, the decline continues but moderates, exhibiting pronounced fluctuations while converging toward values below 1.5. By time step 40, distinct trajectories emerge, with most runs stabilizing at low destocking levels while maintaining heterogeneous outcomes. Through the remainder of the simulation period to step 100, the majority of runs converge toward zero destocking, indicating successful adaptation to sustainable stocking rates. However, several trajectories persist with low-level destocking between 0.5 and 1.0 animals per household, with one particular run maintaining values near 1.0–1.2 throughout the final 40 steps, suggesting differential effectiveness of learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms in managing persistent uncertainty.

The variance across simulation runs is highest during the initial 20 steps, then gradually diminishes, though distinct clusters of behavioral outcomes remain evident through the conclusion of the simulation. This pattern reflects the divergent adaptive pathways of the heterogeneous agent population as they navigate the dual-loop learning processes, climatic variability, and fixed spatial constraints characteristic of private property grazing systems without mobility options.

The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock begins at approximately zero across all simulation runs, reflecting an initially equal distribution of healthy animals among households. During the first 15 to 20 steps, values remain near zero, rising only marginally to between 0.005 and 0.015. This early period of parity corresponds to the initial phase where households operate with similar herd sizes and vegetation biomass has not yet differentiated significantly across private property patches.

Between steps 20 and 50, the metric increases at a moderate pace, reaching values ranging from approximately 0.010 to 0.030 by step 40, and between 0.025 and 0.045 by step 50. During this intermediate phase, divergence across simulation runs becomes noticeable as differences in household decision-making strategies, local pasture conditions, and learning trajectories begin to generate heterogeneity in livestock health outcomes.

After step 50, the rate of increase accelerates substantially, with the Gini coefficient rising steeply through step 100. By the simulation's conclusion, values span a broad range from approximately 0.030 to 0.095. This accelerating trajectory indicates that inequality in healthy livestock distribution compounds over time as the feedback loops between pasture quality, livestock health, and adaptive decision-making amplify initial small disparities. The wide spread of outcomes at step 100, covering a range of approximately 0.065, suggests that the interaction of heterogeneous risk attitudes, distinct behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), and stochastic vegetation dynamics under private property regimes produces significantly different inequality endpoints depending on the specific paths of household learning and ecological variation. The pattern demonstrates that even with initially equivalent conditions, the combination of private property boundaries, learning processes, and climatic variability inevitably generates increasing concentration of healthy livestock among subsets of households, with the pace of inequality generation intensifying markedly in the latter half of the simulation period.

The count of households with social learning initiates at zero across all simulation runs. During the first 20 steps, the metric exhibits rapid growth, with the number of households engaging in social learning increasing to between approximately 24 and 48. This initial surge corresponds to the early simulation phase where households begin observing neighbor performance and activating social learning mechanisms within the private property grazing system.

Between steps 20 and 40, the adoption trajectory diverges across runs. Some simulations reach stable plateaus, maintaining constant counts of households with household-SL set to true, while others continue accumulating adopters at a decelerated rate. By step 40, the majority of runs have stabilized at their terminal values.

Final counts range broadly from approximately 18 to 54 households by step 100, representing varying degrees of social learning penetration across stochastic realizations. The upper extreme approaches the total population size, indicating scenarios where social learning achieves near-universal adoption, while the lower extreme suggests persistent heterogeneity where roughly one-third of households never engage in social learning behaviors.

The discrete, stepwise increases observable throughout the adoption phase reflect the yearly decision-making schedule of the model, where households update their behavioral strategies based on observations of reserve biomass and neighbor outcomes. The variability in final saturation levels likely emerges from stochastic differences in rainfall patterns, initial livestock distributions, and the specific network pathways through which social information diffuses among stationary households in the private property context.
