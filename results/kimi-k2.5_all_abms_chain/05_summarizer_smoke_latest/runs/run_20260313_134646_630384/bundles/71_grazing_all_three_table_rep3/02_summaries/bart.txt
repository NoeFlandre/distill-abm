The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods. The average deviation from recommended livestock numbers begins at approximately 0.007 during the third year, indicating farmers initially maintain stocking rates very close to the theoretically optimal levels. Over the subsequent three decades, this deviation increases significantly and substantially, reaching a maximum of about 0.38 around year 33. This downward trend reflects the gradual adaptation and convergence of learning strategies. An initial degradation phase persists through the first 27 years, driving biomass to 42,057 units. By year 50, a distinct inflection point marks the transition from degradation to sustained recovery. The eventual biomass levels exceeding initial conditions by over 34% demonstrate that decentralized learning strategies can successfully manage grazing lands under high climatic variability without centralized coordination. The average number of livestock destocked per household commences at zero and immediately surges to approximately 6.6 animals by the third year. The failure to recover initial capital levels even after 100 years suggests that current learning mechanisms may be too conservative or slow to capitalize on pasture recovery. The prevalence of social learning among households begins at zero and undergoes a sustained diffusion process throughout the century-long simulation. For decision makers concerned with grazing management policy, this diffusion pattern suggests that social learning mechanisms can spread rapidly through farming communities. The number of households employing the baseline experiential rule-of-thumb strategy without active learning begins at 20, representing the initial cohort assigned to this behavioral type. During the first approximately 8 years, this count exhibits a slight increase, reaching a peak of roughly 21 households. After approximately year 70, the decline attenuates and the count stabilizes near 15 households.
