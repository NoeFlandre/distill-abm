This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. These operate through a yearly sequence where green biomass updates, households with E-RO-SL1 types update their r-parameters, households update behavioral strategies (if switching is enabled), make livestock placement decisions, livestock feeds on biomass, households observe neighbor performance, reserve biomass updates, and households observe and remember reserve biomass states.

The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. It seeks to evaluate how learning capabilities affect household resilience and pasture conditions in private property grazing systems. Specific objectives include analyzing the impact of social learning double-loop processes through strategy switching in heterogeneous populations, observing the diffusion and competition between different learning behaviors in mixed populations, and assessing how demographic settings and initial herd sizes influence system outcomes. The model allows for the evaluation of outcomes across social, ecological, and economic dimensions including average herd size, pasture biomass condition, and surviving number of households.

The initial risk attitude values for social learning agents remain constant throughout the 100 time steps at fixed levels ranging from approximately -0.11 to +0.18. These constant values represent the baseline disposition of E-RO-SL1 agents to deviate from recommended livestock numbers, established at initialization and preserved as reference points. The heterogeneity in these initial values indicates that agents begin the simulation with diverse risk attitudes. Time step 0 establishes these baseline values, which persist unchanged through time step 100.

**Implications for decision makers:** The presence of heterogeneous initial risk attitudes among social learning agents suggests that adaptive management interventions must account for varying baseline dispositions within the population. Since these initial attitudes serve as reference points for the social learning process, the observed spread indicates that some agents start with conservative predispositions (negative values) while others begin with more risk-tolerant attitudes (positive values). This baseline diversity may influence how social learning propagates through the community and how quickly the population converges on effective grazing strategies. Policymakers should consider that uniform interventions may affect agents differently depending on their initial risk disposition, and that the initialization of risk parameters is a critical design consideration for models of adaptive co-management in grazing systems.

Across the 100 time steps, simulations exhibit substantial divergence in average livestock deviation from recommended levels, with outcomes fragmenting into distinct stable regimes. Initially, all runs start with deviations near zero (ranging from approximately -0.1 to 0.2), indicating stocking rates close to recommendations. During the first 20 time steps, rapid divergence occurs as most trajectories exhibit sharp increases in positive deviation, with values climbing to between 0.2 and 0.8.

Between steps 20 and 40, trajectories continue along distinct adjustment paths. Several simulations stabilize at high positive deviation levels (0.7-0.8), while others moderate to 0.4-0.6 or exhibit continued volatility with moderate fluctuations.

A critical bifurcation phase occurs between steps 40 and 60, where multiple trajectories that had maintained moderate positive deviations undergo steep transitions. Several runs drop precipitously from values around 0.2-0.4 to negative deviations reaching -0.3 to -0.4 by step 60, indicating shifts from overstocking to substantial understocking.

By the final phase (steps 60-100), most trajectories stabilize into persistent equilibrium states with minimal fluctuation. Terminal values at step 100 span a wide spectrum: a significant portion stabilize at high positive deviations of 0.6-0.8 (60-80% over recommended levels), another group stabilizes in the 0.2-0.5 range, some converge near zero (±0.1), and several runs settle at sustained negative deviations of -0.3 to -0.4 (30-40% below recommendations).

For decision makers, the persistent bifurcation into overstocking and understocking regimes suggests that uniform stocking recommendations are insufficient under adaptive learning dynamics. The prevalence of high positive deviations indicates a systemic tendency toward overstocking that requires active monitoring, while the subset of runs exhibiting severe understocking suggests that some learning trajectories lead to overly conservative strategies. Interventions should detect early trajectory divergence and implement corrective measures before systems lock into unsustainable stable states at either extreme.

**Implications for decision makers:** The U-shaped trajectory indicates that grazing systems with mixed behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) experience an initial degradation phase lasting approximately 20 time steps before learning mechanisms enable recovery to levels exceeding initial conditions. The recovery to 115,000–128,000 units—substantially above the starting 90,000—suggests that learning-by-doing and social learning processes enhance long-term pasture productivity in private property systems. However, the 20-time-step vulnerable window, where biomass drops to 80,000–88,000 units, represents a critical period requiring risk management strategies to prevent system collapse. The considerable variation in final outcomes (111,000–128,000) indicates that climatic stochasticity significantly impacts long-term ecological conditions despite the presence of learning mechanisms, suggesting that decision makers should account for high uncertainty in projected pasture availability and maintain adaptive capacity to respond to variable recovery trajectories.

At the start of the simulation, mean destocking across households begins at zero but immediately spikes to peak values ranging from 6 to nearly 8 within the first 5 steps. This indicates a severe initial destocking crisis affecting all household behavioral types. Following this peak, mean destocking undergoes a rapid decline between steps 5 and 20, falling from approximately 7 down to ranges between 0.5 and 2.5. The trajectories during this phase exhibit high volatility with substantial oscillations as households adjust to resource constraints. Between steps 20 and 40, the rate of decrease slows considerably, though destocking continues to trend downward. During this transition period, significant variability persists across simulation runs, with some trajectories approaching zero while others maintain levels between 1.0 and 2.0. From approximately step 40 through step 100, the system stabilizes at low destocking levels, with most trajectories converging to steady-state values between 0 and 1.0. By the final steps, the mean destocking levels have settled into distinct equilibria, with the majority stabilizing below 0.8 and a few outliers maintaining values near 1.0.

These trends indicate that the initial livestock allocation of 90 animals per household substantially exceeds the carrying capacity of private pastures under the specified rainfall regime (mean 200, standard deviation 100), forcing immediate and drastic destocking. The rapid decline during steps 5-20 suggests that the adaptive mechanisms—learning-by-doing for E-LBD agents, social learning for E-RO-SL1 agents, and baseline optimization for E-RO agents—enable households to quickly adjust herd sizes to sustainable levels. The long-term stabilization at low destocking rates (0-1.0 animals per household) after step 40 demonstrates that the combination of adaptive decision-making and private property incentives successfully reduces destocking pressure once the system reaches equilibrium. The persistent low-level destocking activity in the steady state reflects ongoing adjustments to stochastic rainfall variability and ecological fluctuations rather than systematic overstocking.

For decision makers, these findings emphasize that initial stocking rates significantly exceeding carrying capacity trigger immediate destocking crises regardless of learning capabilities. Interventions should prioritize ensuring initial herd sizes align with estimated pasture productivity to avoid the severe livestock losses observed in the first 5 steps. However, the rapid convergence to low destocking levels indicates that learning-based adaptation is effective at stabilizing household livelihoods once the initial shock subsides. The variation in long-term destocking levels across simulations suggests that promoting diverse adaptive strategies among farmers may lead to more resilient outcomes, though some destocking will likely remain necessary to manage climatic variability. Policy support should focus on providing early warning systems and destocking assistance during the initial adaptation period (first 40 steps), while recognizing that the system naturally stabilizes thereafter with minimal ongoing destocking requirements.

**Implications for decision makers:** The emergence of inequality appears inevitable in this system due to climatic stochasticity and behavioral heterogeneity, but the extent of inequality (ranging from 0.03 to 0.095) is highly sensitive to early-phase dynamics. The data tracks the diffusion of social learning behaviors across 60 households over 100 time steps across multiple simulation runs. 

Initially, at step 0, no households have engaged in social learning (count = 0). Between approximately steps 5 and 25, all simulation runs exhibit rapid, step-like increases in the count of households with social learning, characterized by discrete jumps followed by brief plateaus. This staircase pattern indicates that adoption occurs in discrete events rather than continuously.

The rate of diffusion varies substantially across simulations. Some runs reach saturation as early as step 30, while others continue accumulating adopting households until approximately step 60. By step 60, nearly all simulation runs have stabilized, maintaining constant counts through step 100.

Final adoption levels at step 100 vary widely across the simulation ensemble, ranging from approximately 18 households (30% of the population) to approximately 54 households (90% of the population). The most common terminal values cluster between 42 and 48 households, though significant outliers exist at both the lower and upper extremes.

**Implications for decision makers:** The rapid initial diffusion (within 25 steps) suggests that social learning interventions can spread quickly through smallholder communities, but the wide variation in final adoption rates (30-90%) indicates that network structure, spatial configuration, or initial conditions significantly influence whether behaviors become community-wide or remain limited to sub-populations. The persistence of plateaus after step 60 suggests that once diffusion stabilizes, the behavior is durable, making early intervention critical for maximizing adoption.

All scenarios commence with zero cumulative destocking at initialization. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 20 steps, households across all simulation runs rapidly accumulate destocked livestock, reaching mean totals between 60 and 80 head. This initial period represents a transient adjustment phase where households respond to initial pasture conditions and establish their management strategies.

Following step 20, the trajectories diverge considerably, revealing heterogeneous long-term outcomes. By step 40, mean destock totals range from approximately 58 to 110 head across different runs. This divergence continues to amplify through the end of the simulation period. At step 100, the spread extends from roughly 58 head to 173 head, indicating substantially different cumulative offtake outcomes despite identical starting conditions of 90 head per household.

A distinct subset of scenarios exhibits plateau behavior, stabilizing around 58 to 60 head shortly after step 40 and maintaining this level through step 100. In contrast, the majority of trajectories demonstrate sustained, approximately linear accumulation of destocked animals throughout the entire 100-step period, with slopes varying from moderate increases to steep escalations.

This pattern suggests that while initial destocking behavior converges across different configurations during the establishment phase, long-term cumulative outcomes depend critically on the specific behavioral dynamics and ecological feedbacks emerging from the interaction between learning types and vegetation dynamics. The existence of both plateau and continuously rising trajectories indicates that some household configurations achieve equilibrium states that stabilize livestock holdings, while others engage in ongoing destocking cycles.

For decision makers, these results highlight that adaptive learning behaviors produce highly variable long-term livelihood outcomes, with cumulative destocking differing by nearly a factor of three between the most conservative and most active configurations. The plateau scenarios suggest that certain combinations of learning-by-doing and social learning may enable households to stabilize their asset base early, while configurations with sustained destocking trajectories may indicate either successful commercial optimization or ongoing vulnerability to ecological shocks requiring repeated liquidation. Policies should account for this wide outcome dispersion when assessing the resilience of smallholder grazing systems.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The multiplicity of stable equilibria suggests that smallholder grazing systems can become locked into divergent behavioral regimes based on early stochastic events or initial conditions, creating path dependency. The existence of high-count equilibria (25+ non-learning households) indicates that reliance on traditional non-adaptive behaviors can persist indefinitely in some contexts, potentially undermining social-ecological resilience. Conversely, the presence of trajectories achieving complete or near-complete adoption of learning behaviors (0-5 non-learning households) suggests that under favorable conditions, learning-by-doing and social learning can achieve full diffusion. The pronounced volatility during steps 10-40 represents a critical window where interventions or disturbances could tip the system toward either learning dominance or non-learning entrenchment. Policy designs should account for this tipping-point dynamic and consider mechanisms to prevent early lock-in to non-learning equilibria, particularly given the upper-band stability around 25 households that resists further conversion.
