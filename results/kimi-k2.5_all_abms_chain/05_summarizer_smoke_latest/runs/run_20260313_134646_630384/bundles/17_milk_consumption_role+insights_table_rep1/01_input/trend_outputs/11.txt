The simulation reveals a sustained monotonic increase in the mean deviation between agents' held health values and the health values implied by their milk consumption choices, beginning at 0.0000 and culminating at 0.7251 over the 31-year period (32 time steps). This trajectory indicates a progressive decoupling of consumer health perceptions from actual behavioral patterns. The median deviation value of 0.5584 indicates that for the majority of the simulation timeframe, agents exhibited substantial misalignment between their health valuations and consumption choices.

The temporal dynamics exhibit distinct phases characterized by varying rates of divergence. An initial rapid ascent occurs between steps 0 and 4, establishing early deviation momentum. Subsequent inflection points at steps 6, 16, 19, 22, 28, and 31 mark transitions in the acceleration of growing dissonance, suggesting periods where underlying psychological mechanisms—such as habit persistence, social influence, or perception updating—shifted in relative dominance. Statistical validation across all rolling windows confirms significant increasing trends (p=0.027) throughout the entire series, indicating this drift represents a structural transformation rather than transient fluctuation.

Interpreted within the context of British milk market transition, this pattern reflects the psychological tension between evolving health information favoring skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties and the inertial force of habitual whole milk consumption. The zero-deviation starting point represents initial harmony between health beliefs and choices under the 1974 market regime. As health information permeated the social network over subsequent decades, agents updated their health valuations toward alternative milk options, yet habitual behavior and social conformity pressures prevented immediate behavioral alignment. The resulting deviation represents accumulated cognitive dissonance—agents choosing whole milk while holding health valuations that would rationally imply choosing skimmed varieties, or later, agents choosing skimmed milk while potentially maintaining residual health valuations from earlier periods.

For decision makers in public health and consumer policy, these trends suggest that information-based interventions alone may generate psychological discomfort without achieving proportional behavioral change. The persistent upward trajectory implies that campaigns focusing solely on health education risk creating populations with high cognitive dissonance—knowledgeable about health benefits yet unable to overcome habitual patterns. Policymakers should prioritize habit-disruption strategies, such as environmental restructuring, default-option modifications, or timing interventions to coincide with detected inflection points where psychological structures are in flux. Additionally, the substantial median deviation value suggests that by mid-transition, the majority of consumers operated under significant psychological tension, indicating potential opportunities for interventions targeting specific behavioral barriers at critical temporal windows identified by the inflection points.
