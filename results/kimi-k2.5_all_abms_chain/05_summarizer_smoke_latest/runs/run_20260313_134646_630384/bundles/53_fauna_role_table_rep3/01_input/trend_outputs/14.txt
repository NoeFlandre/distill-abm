The vegetation coverage exhibits remarkable long-term stationarity, initiating at precisely 33.0% and terminating at 33.0% after approximately 2,312 ticks (roughly 6.3 years), with a mean value of 32.7% maintained throughout the observation period. This stability indicates that grass consumption by the ungulate population and regrowth dynamics (governed by a rapid 3-tick recovery time) achieved a dynamic equilibrium, keeping available fodder consistently near the initialized one-third landscape coverage despite continuous grazing pressure.

A pronounced seasonal signature emerges as an initial transient decline, reaching a minimum value of approximately 24.9% at tick 365—the exact completion of the first annual cycle—before recovering. This nadir reflects the first seasonal resource bottleneck under the high seasonality parameter (0.8), where reduced grass availability coincides with maximum cumulative grazing impact.

Superimposed on this baseline stability is a dominant oscillatory pattern with a period of approximately 63 ticks (roughly two months), generating regular, low-amplitude fluctuations throughout the series. The tight variance (standard deviation of 0.95 percentage points) and the near-zero prominence of detected peaks and valleys confirm that these oscillations represent smooth, cyclical vegetation dynamics rather than abrupt regime shifts. These cycles likely reflect feedback loops between localized herd grazing patterns and the rapid regrowth restricted to initially vegetated patches.

Temporal trend analysis reveals distinct phases: the first approximately 1,800 ticks (five years) show statistically significant but slight increasing trends (slopes of 0.001–0.002), suggesting a slow accretion of vegetation cover as the system adjusts from initial conditions. This transitions to weak, statistically significant decreasing trends (slopes approximating -0.000) in the latter portion of the sequence (ticks 1,624–2,087), though these linear trends are ecologically negligible compared to the dominant 63-tick cyclic variation.

The system demonstrates robust resilience, maintaining vegetation coverage within a narrow band (24.9% to 33.0%) despite strong environmental forcing (seasonality 0.8) and herbivore population pressure, with the fast regrowth rate preventing the degradation of the grassland resource base below the starvation threshold for megaherbivores.
