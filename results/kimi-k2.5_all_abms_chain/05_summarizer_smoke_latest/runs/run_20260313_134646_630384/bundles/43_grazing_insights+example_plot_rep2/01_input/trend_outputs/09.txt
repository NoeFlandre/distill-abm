The cumulative destocking of livestock across households initiates at zero and exhibits immediate accumulation during the first 20 steps across all simulation runs, with values rapidly ascending to between 40 and 80 animals per household. This initial surge reflects the immediate pressure to reduce initial herd sizes from the starting value of 90 livestock as households apply their baseline decision rules and uncertainty parameters to the private property grazing context, adjusting to initial vegetation conditions and rainfall variability.

Following this initial phase, the trajectories diverge substantially after step 20, revealing four distinct behavioral outcomes that persist through the 100-step period. Approximately one-third of simulation runs stabilize completely by steps 30–40, maintaining cumulative destocking totals between 58 and 70 for the remaining duration, suggesting these configurations rapidly identified sustainable stocking rates and required no further destocking interventions. Other trajectories exhibit continued moderate increase, reaching values of 85–105 by step 60 and 100–130 by step 100, indicating persistent but manageable pressure to adjust herd sizes in response to seasonal vegetation dynamics.

A distinct subset of runs demonstrates aggressive sustained destocking throughout the entire period, exceeding cumulative totals of 140 by step 80 and reaching maxima near 173 by step 100. The variance between minimum and maximum observed outcomes expands monotonically from roughly 20 units at step 20 to over 110 units by step 100, reflecting the differential effectiveness of the three behavioral algorithms (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) in adapting to stochastic rainfall conditions (rain-mean 200, rain-std 100) and the heterogeneity in uncertainty management captured by the r-parameter distribution.

By the conclusion of the simulations at step 100, the cumulative destocking metric clusters into three quasi-stable equilibria: a low-destocking range (58–75) representing successful early adaptation and stable herd management, a mid-range cluster (80–110) indicating ongoing adjustment pressure, and a high-destocking cluster (120–173) reflecting either highly conservative stocking strategies or continued ecological degradation forcing repeated destocking events across the century-scale simulation.

**Insights for Decision Makers:**
The pronounced divergence in destocking trajectories after step 20 suggests that early intervention strategies and the specific configuration of learning mechanisms critically determine long-term livestock management outcomes. Decision makers should prioritize identifying which behavioral configurations enable the rapid stabilization observed in the low-destocking cluster (58–70), as these represent sustainable equilibria that minimize transaction costs and destocking burdens over time. Additionally, the existence of high-destocking trajectories (exceeding 130 by step 100) indicates that without effective learning mechanisms, households may face continuous pressure to reduce herds, suggesting that policies supporting social learning or learning-by-doing processes could prevent the accumulation of excessive destocking requirements in smallholder grazing systems.
