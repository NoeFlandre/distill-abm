The mean number of livestock destocked per household begins at approximately zero, then rises sharply within the first five time steps to reach peak values between 7.0 and 7.5. This initial surge indicates a period of acute resource pressure where households are forced to rapidly reduce herd sizes due to insufficient pasture biomass relative to livestock demand. Following this peak, the metric declines steadily from step 5 through approximately step 40, though this descent is characterized by considerable oscillation with secondary peaks occurring at irregular intervals. These fluctuations suggest that households are engaging in iterative adjustments as they respond to variable rainfall conditions and learn to manage their stocking rates. From roughly step 40 to step 60, the rate of decline slows and the metric begins to stabilize. By step 60 and continuing through step 100, the values plateau at levels ranging from near zero to approximately 1.0, with individual simulation runs converging to distinct steady-state values. This divergence in long-term outcomes, where some trajectories stabilize at minimal destocking levels while others remain elevated, indicates strong path dependency in the system. The high variance across simulation runs, particularly pronounced in the first 20 steps and again in the final 40 steps, suggests that stochastic elements such as rainfall variability and initial household conditions significantly influence the ability of households to achieve resilient grazing management. For decision makers, these trends highlight that early intervention may be critical to prevent the system from locking into high-destock trajectories, and that the potential for achieving low-destock equilibrium states exists but depends heavily on initial conditions and the specific realization of environmental variability.
