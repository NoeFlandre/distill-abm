 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model . It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management .  The mean household risk attitude, representing the average deviation from recommended livestock levels across the population of extension agents, begins the simulation tightly clustered between 0.0 and 0.1 . Between steps 40 and 50, specific simulation runs experience abrupt drops, with values falling from moderate highs of approximately 0.4–0.8 down to negative levels near -0.4 .  Initial biomass depletion and subsequent recovery pattern indicates that the grazing system experiences a transient period of resource stress before adaptive mechanisms enable regeneration . The convergence of trajectories during the recovery phase suggests robust system dynamics in the medium term . The divergence in final outcomes highlights the importance of early intervention and management strategies to ensure favorable long-term biomass conditions .  The Gini coefficient for livestock distribution begins at zero, reflecting an initially equal allocation of livestock across all 60 households . During the first 20 time steps, inequality remains minimal, rising only slightly to values between 0.005 and 0.015 . Between steps 20 and 60, the metric increases at a moderate, roughly linear pace, reaching values ranging from 0.020 to 0.045 .  The count of households following the non-learning E-RO strategy begins at 20 across all simulation runs . During the initial 20 time steps, the number diverges rapidly, with different trajectories spanning from near 0 to approximately 40 households . A distinct subset of runs demonstrates aggressive sustained destocking throughout the entire period, exceeding cumulative totals of 140 by step 80 .
