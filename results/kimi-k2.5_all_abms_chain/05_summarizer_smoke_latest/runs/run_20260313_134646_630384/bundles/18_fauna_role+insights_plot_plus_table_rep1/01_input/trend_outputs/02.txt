Across 40 simulation replicates spanning 2,312 time steps, the aggregate energy of human forager populations exhibits a consistent monotonic decline from an initial value of 2,000 units to a terminal value of approximately -38,229 units. 

The trajectory divides into two distinct phases. During the initial transient period (approximately steps 0 to 650), the rate of energy decline fluctuates, with an initial slope of -11.07 units per step moderating to -8.82 units per step before accelerating again. This early phase displays considerable variability between simulation runs, with divergent energy states emerging as different forager bands experience heterogeneous hunting success. 

Following the first identified change point at step 650, the system enters a phase of sustained, uniform decline characterized by greater consistency across replicates. From step 696 onward, the rate of energy loss stabilizes at approximately -18.5 to -20.0 units per step, exhibiting remarkable regularity through subsequent windows (steps 928–1159, 1160–1391, 1392–1623, 1624–1855, and 1856–2087). Throughout this period, Mann-Kendall tests confirm a significantly decreasing trend (p < 0.001) with tau values near -1.0, indicating deterministic decline. Additional change points at steps 1075, 1500, 1925, and 2310 mark subtle adjustments in decline rates but do not alter the fundamental downward trajectory.

The negative terminal values indicate accumulated energy deficits rather than instantaneous starvation, permitted by the model's configuration allowing energy storage and immortality for foragers. The convergence of replicate trajectories after step 650 suggests that despite initial stochastic variation in hunting success, the system rapidly equilibrates to a consistent rate of energy extraction insufficient to offset forager metabolic costs and movement expenditures. 

For decision makers, this pattern indicates that under the specified parameterization—particularly with 20 initial foragers, high environmental seasonality (0.8), and rapid vegetation regrowth (3 ticks)—human hunting pressure generates unsustainable energetic returns. The constant negative slope implies that prey populations cannot support the energetic demands of persistent forager presence, leading to cumulative resource deficits even without forager mortality from starvation. This suggests that sustainable coexistence between human foragers and ungulate prey populations may require reducing hunter numbers, lowering movement costs, or enhancing prey nutritional value to achieve energetic balance.
