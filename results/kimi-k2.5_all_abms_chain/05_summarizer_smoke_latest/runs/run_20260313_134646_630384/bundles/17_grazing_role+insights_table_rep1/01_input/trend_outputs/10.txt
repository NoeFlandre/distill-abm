The population of households employing the baseline non-learning strategy (E-RO) undergoes a characteristic trajectory of initial growth, sustained attrition, and eventual stabilization over the century-long simulation. Beginning at 20 households, the population increases during the establishment phase, peaking at approximately 21 households around year 8. This represents a transient expansion where non-learning agents briefly constitute a growing proportion of the farming community.

Following this peak, a pronounced decline commences around year 11 and persists with statistical significance through year 65. During this middle phase, the population decreases steadily from roughly 20 to 15 households, with the rate of attrition gradually diminishing over time. The most rapid reduction occurs between years 11 and 21, after which the decline continues at moderated rates through successive decades. By year 45, the series reaches a change point where the consistent downward trajectory transitions toward stability.

In the final third of the simulation, from approximately year 66 onward, the population exhibits no significant directional trend, fluctuating around a mean of roughly 15 households. The minimum value of approximately 14.7 households occurs near year 92, with the simulation concluding at 14.7 households—representing a net loss of over 25% of the initial non-learning population.

This pattern indicates that under conditions of significant rainfall variability, fixed decision-making rules without adaptive learning capacity expose smallholder farmers to elevated risks of business failure over multi-decade periods. However, the persistence of approximately 75% of non-learning households suggests that a substantial minority can maintain viability without active adaptation, possibly through conservative stocking strategies or favorable spatial positioning. For agricultural development practitioners, these dynamics emphasize that promoting learning-based decision strategies—whether through personal experience or social observation—could substantially improve long-term farmer persistence, as non-adaptive approaches face a gradual but significant erosion of population viability under stochastic environmental conditions.
