The mean cumulative destock total of households initiates at zero livestock units and follows a strictly increasing trajectory throughout the 100-step simulation, culminating at approximately 97 units. This accumulation exhibits pronounced non-linearity characterized by rapidly diminishing growth rates over time, with distinct phases marking different adaptive regimes.

During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 25 steps, households undertake aggressive destocking at steep rates averaging 4.96 units per step in the earliest interval (steps 0–10), moderating to roughly 1.47 units per step by steps 11–21, and further declining to 0.69 units per step through step 32. This early rapid accumulation reflects immediate adjustment of livestock holdings from initial densities of 90 head per household, as agents respond to initial pasture conditions and activate risk-management strategies under private property constraints. The first detected change point at step 25 marks a transition from rapid acceleration to a deceleration phase, indicating that households have largely completed initial stock adjustments.

Between steps 25 and 60, the system enters a middle phase where growth rates decline progressively from approximately 0.36 to 0.31 units per step. Multiple inflection points clustered between steps 45 and 57, along with oscillation indices concentrated around steps 49–61 and 22–26, suggest periodic recalibration of destocking decisions. These dynamics align with annual vegetation cycles and the updating of behavioral strategies through learning-by-doing and social learning processes, as households observe neighbor performance and adjust their uncertainty management parameters.

Following the second change point at step 60, the trajectory enters a late phase characterized by sustained but modest accumulation at approximately 0.31 units per step through step 100. This stable, near-linear growth suggests the attainment of quasi-equilibrium management patterns where destocking continues primarily for livelihood maintenance rather than systemic adjustment. Oscillation indices detected around steps 79–80 indicate continued periodic reassessment even in this stabilized regime.

Statistical analysis confirms statistically significant increasing trends across all rolling windows (p<0.000), despite the declining marginal rates. The temporal mean of 75.48 and median of 81.17 across the series reflect the heavy influence of early low values during the rapid accumulation phase, while substantial heterogeneity emerges across simulation runs. This divergence—ranging from approximately 58 to over 170 units by step 100—indicates divergent adaptive pathways among the three behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) as they respond to stochastic rainfall variability and social-ecological feedbacks through their respective learning mechanisms.
