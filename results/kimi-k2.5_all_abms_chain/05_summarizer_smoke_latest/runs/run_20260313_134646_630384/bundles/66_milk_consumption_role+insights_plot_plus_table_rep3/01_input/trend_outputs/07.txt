The simulation of British milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 reveals a three-phase adoption pattern for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. At initialization (step 0), consumption of alternative milk varieties begins at a minimal level of approximately 5.3 units. 

The first phase (steps 0-11) demonstrates rapid exponential growth, with consumption accelerating from near-zero to approximately 1,265 units by step 11. Statistical testing confirms significant monotonic increases during this initial period (steps 0-4: slope=112.8, p=0.027; steps 5-9: slope=139.0, p=0.027), indicating robust early adoption dynamics.

The second phase (steps 11-25) exhibits a pronounced plateau characterized by oscillatory behavior around a mean of approximately 1,220 units, with values fluctuating between local minima near 1,183 (step 18) and local maxima near 1,266 (step 11). During this middle period, statistical tests reveal no significant trend (steps 10-14, 15-19, and 20-24 all show non-significant slopes), suggesting a temporary equilibrium where adoption rates stall. The oscillation analysis indicates a dominant periodicity of 15 steps, consistent with cyclical fluctuations during this saturation phase.

The third phase (steps 25-32) resumes significant growth, with consumption increasing from approximately 1,220 units to a terminal value of 1,456.6 units. Statistical confirmation of this late-stage acceleration appears in the significant increasing trend for steps 25-29 (slope=48.5, p=0.027).

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The three-phase pattern suggests that market transitions in food consumption exhibit distinct adoption regimes: an initial period of rapid uptake among early adopters, a protracted middle period of market saturation where social influence and habit formation create resistance among remaining consumers, and a final consolidation phase where accumulated social pressure and shifting perceptions eventually overcome habitual barriers. The 15-step oscillation cycle during the plateau phase indicates that temporary setbacks in adoption are systematic rather than random, suggesting that interventions targeting the plateau period may require sustained effort to overcome cyclical resistance patterns. The terminal growth spurt (steps 25-32) demonstrates that even after prolonged stagnation, markets can achieve complete transition when underlying preference parameters reach critical thresholds.
