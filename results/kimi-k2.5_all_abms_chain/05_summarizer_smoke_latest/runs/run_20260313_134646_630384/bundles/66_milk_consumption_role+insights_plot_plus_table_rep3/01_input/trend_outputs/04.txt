The cumulative adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk demonstrates a consistent upward trajectory across the entire simulation period, beginning from zero adoption and reaching approximately 25,644 total choices by the final time step. The dynamics unfold through three distinct phases characterized by markedly different rates of accumulation.

During the initial phase spanning time steps 0 through 4, adoption proceeds at a modest pace with an accumulation rate of approximately 201 choices per step. This early inertia reflects the dominance of established habits, as agents begin with maximum habitual attachment to whole milk (strength of 10.0) while holding only moderately favorable initial perceptions of alternative milk's health (1.69) and environmental (1.12) attributes. The strong incumbent habit combined with initially low social proof of alternative choices creates significant resistance to early switching.

Between time steps 5 and 14, the system enters a rapid acceleration phase where the accumulation rate more than triples, increasing from approximately 737 to over 927 choices per step. This inflection represents the critical threshold where social influence mechanisms begin overcoming habitual inertia. As early adopters establish visible patterns of alternative milk consumption within the Watts-Strogatz network topology (connectivity parameter 8.0), social learning and conformity pressures (social-conformity weight of 0.28, social-susceptibility of 0.2) compound with evolving health perceptions. The justification mechanism (0.61) and cognitive dissonance threshold (0.15) enable agents to rationalize departures from long-standing habits as social norms shift.

From time step 15 through the conclusion at step 32, the adoption rate stabilizes at approximately 933-936 choices per step, indicating a mature diffusion state. This linear growth phase suggests that alternative milk has achieved normative status, with social blindness (0.51) and habit thresholds (2.0) no longer presenting significant barriers. The oscillation patterns detected in the final indices (27-31) indicate minor fluctuations around this high-adoption equilibrium, consistent with stochastic decision-making processes where agents maintain a probability-based openness to change.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The pronounced S-curve acceleration between steps 5-14 highlights the critical importance of the early adopter phase in driving macro-level transitions. Interventions targeting network hubs during the initial slow period (steps 0-4) could potentially accelerate the inflection point, as the model demonstrates that social proof accumulation follows a non-linear threshold effect.

The stabilization of adoption rates in the final phase suggests that market saturation occurs gradually rather than abruptly, leaving room for sustained marketing efforts throughout the transition period. The parameter configuration—with moderate social susceptibility (0.2) but high network connectivity (8.0)—indicates that structural network position may be more influential than individual receptivity in determining adoption speed.

For product positioning, the model confirms that health and environmental attribute perceptions drive adoption even when initial evaluations are modest (starting below 2.0 on a 3.0 scale), provided social reinforcement mechanisms are active. This suggests that emphasizing social proof and normative messaging may be as critical as direct attribute advertising, particularly during the acceleration phase when collective behavior patterns crystallize.
