The repeated simulations demonstrate highly consistent trajectories for mean weekly whole milk consumption per agent. At the initial time step, consumption begins at approximately 2,650–2,700 milliliters. Over the first ten time steps, consumption undergoes a precipitous decline of approximately 64%, falling to roughly 950–1,000 milliliters by step 10.

From step 10 to step 25, the rate of decline moderates substantially. During this fifteen-step interval, consumption decreases gradually from approximately 950 milliliters to roughly 600–650 milliliters, representing a slower linear decay with minor temporary fluctuations around steps 18–20 where consumption briefly stabilizes near 750 milliliters before continuing downward.

A renewed acceleration occurs from step 25 to the final observation at step 32. Over these seven steps, consumption drops sharply from approximately 650 milliliters to roughly 200–250 milliliters, achieving a total reduction of approximately 92% from initial levels. The simulation runs exhibit minimal variance throughout, with trajectories remaining tightly clustered particularly after step 12.

Interpreted within the behavioral model context, the three-phase pattern reflects distinct dynamics of the disposition mechanisms and habit formation parameters. The steep initial decline corresponds to the period when agents with lower habit thresholds and higher health perception values rapidly switch from whole milk to alternatives. The protracted middle phase represents the "stubborn" segment of the population whose high incumbent habit strength (parameter value 10) and social blindness (0.51) temporarily resist disposition triggers despite accumulating social influence. The final accelerated decline suggests that cumulative network effects operating through the Watts-Strogatz topology eventually overcome these barriers as social norms intensify and agents with prolonged exposure to alternative choices finally trigger disposition evaluation.

For decision makers: The non-linear trajectory suggests that dietary transitions require sustained intervention beyond the initial rapid adoption phase. The extended plateau between steps 10–25 indicates that roughly 25–30% of the population remains resistant to change for a considerable period, suggesting that targeted strategies addressing high habit strength or cognitive dissonance thresholds may be necessary to accelerate the transition. The late-stage convergence of all simulation runs implies that once social influence reaches a critical threshold, the transition becomes self-reinforcing and inevitable, highlighting the importance of early investment in network-embedded information dissemination to compress the middle plateau phase.
