 The model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005 . It captures how individual decision-making factors collectively drive macro-level market shifts . Success is measured by the model's ability to reproduce the historical crossover point where skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption surpassed whole milk consumption .  Between steps 1 and 10, consumption undergoes rapid non-linear growth, accelerating from near-zero levels to approximately 1,200–1,250 units . During this period, the rate of adoption slows considerably as many agents remain locked in strong habitual patterns of whole milk consumption, reflecting the high incumbent initial habit strength of 10.0 and the habit threshold of 2.0 .  The cumulative count of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices begins at essentially zero and follows a trajectory of initially slow growth that accelerates through the middle period before transitioning to sustained linear accumulation . This acceleration phase corresponds to the period where social influence diffusing through the Watts-Strogatz network structure, combined with memory-weighted learning about product attributes, begins to overcome habitual barriers .  The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed initiates at negligible levels near zero . Consumption undergoes a rapid, non-linear acceleration, surging from near zero to approximately 1,200–1,280 units . By the final steps, whole milk consumption reaches a low baseline level, representing the successful historical transition where skimmed milk has largely displaced whole milk in the virtual marketplace .  The mean value environmental deviation begins at zero at the start of the simulation . During the first five time steps, the metric undergoes a rapid increase, rising steeply from zero to approximately 13.4 . The trajectories exhibit very low variance across simulation runs throughout the entire 32-step period, with multiple runs following nearly identical paths .
