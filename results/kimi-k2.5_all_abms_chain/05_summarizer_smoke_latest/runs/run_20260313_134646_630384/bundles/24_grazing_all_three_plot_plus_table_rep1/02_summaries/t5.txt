Model focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making. The mean initial risk attitude across the social learning agent population remains constant at 0.0074 throughout the entire 100-step simulation period. This value holds steady from step 0 through step 100, exhibiting zero variance and no statistically significant temporal trends in any analysis window. Between steps 26 and 48, the system enters a transition period characterized by statistical stabilization with no clear directional trend. This plateau suggests that learning mechanisms begin counteracting the degradation pressure, halting further biomass loss despite ongoing rainfall variability. Initial decline represents a critical vulnerability window where pasture degradation could compromise household livelihoods before learning mechanisms effectively reduce grazing pressure. The subsequent recovery to levels significantly exceeding initial conditions demonstrates that adaptive learning strategies eventually improve pasture health. Divergence intensifies, and by year 71, the system enters a phase of rapid polarization. Inequality increasing at its fastest rate in the final decades. Trajectories tend toward greater inequality, but the specific magnitude and timing depend on particular sequences of decisions and environmental conditions. The presence of multiple inflection points between years 45 and 75 suggests complex feedback dynamics in the learning and adaptation processes. The substantial variance across simulation runs, with a standard deviation of approximately 20 animals and a range exceeding 100 animals between minimum and maximum trajectories, indicates high sensitivity to stochastic factors.
