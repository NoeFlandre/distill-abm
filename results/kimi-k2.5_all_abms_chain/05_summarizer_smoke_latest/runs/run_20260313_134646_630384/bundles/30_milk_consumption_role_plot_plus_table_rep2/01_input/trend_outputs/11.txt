The mean deviation between consumers' held health values and the health values implied by their actual milk choices exhibits a monotonic increase throughout the 33 time steps of the simulation, beginning at 0.0000 and terminating at 0.7251. This trajectory indicates a progressively widening gap between what consumers believe about health attributes and the health signals their actual consumption choices convey to the market.

The dynamics unfold through three distinct phases characterized by varying rates of divergence. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first ten time steps, the deviation accelerates rapidly, surging from 0 to approximately 0.50-0.55. Statistical analysis confirms this period exhibits the steepest positive trend, with an initial slope of 0.063 that moderates slightly to 0.041 in the second window (steps 5-9), yet remains highly significant throughout.

A pronounced inflection occurs around time steps 15-19, where the growth rate decelerates dramatically to a slope of merely 0.003. This plateau phase extends roughly from step 10 to step 25, during which the deviation increases only marginally from approximately 0.55 to 0.58-0.60. The median value of 0.5584 falls within this middle range, indicating that for half of the simulation duration, the attitude-behavior gap stabilized at moderate levels despite ongoing market transition.

In the final phase from time step 25 to 32, the divergence resumes with renewed momentum, achieving a slope of 0.019 and ultimately reaching the maximum value of 0.7251. This late-stage acceleration suggests that the misalignment between health perceptions and consumption behaviors intensifies rather than resolves as the simulation approaches its terminal point.

From a consumer behavior perspective, this pattern reflects the psychological tension inherent in the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The increasing deviation suggests that as information about the superior health attributes of alternative milk options diffuses through the social network, consumers' held health valuations shift toward favoring low-fat options, yet their actual consumption choices—constrained by strong habitual tendencies (initial habit strength of 10), social influence mechanisms, and network effects—fail to keep pace with these attitudinal changes. The mid-simulation plateau likely corresponds to a period where social conformity pressures and habit maintenance temporarily stabilize the attitude-behavior gap, while the final uptick indicates that persistent exposure to health information and shifting social norms ultimately drives health perceptions even further from entrenched consumption patterns, creating substantial cognitive dissonance among agents who continue to consume whole milk despite increasingly positive health perceptions of alternatives.
