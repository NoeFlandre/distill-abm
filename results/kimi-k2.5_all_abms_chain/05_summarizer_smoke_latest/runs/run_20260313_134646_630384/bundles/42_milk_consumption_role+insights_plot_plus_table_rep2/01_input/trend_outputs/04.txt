The simulation of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005 reveals a monotonic increase in cumulative skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices across 33 time steps, beginning at zero and terminating at approximately 25,644 total selections. This trajectory manifests distinct behavioral phases reflective of the underlying psychological and social mechanisms governing consumer transition from whole milk dominance.

During the initial period (steps 0–5), adoption progresses slowly with an effective slope of approximately 201 choices per step, reflecting the constraining influence of high initial habit strength (10.0) for whole milk combined with modest initial health (1.69) and environmental (1.12) perceptions of alternatives. By step 5, cumulative choices reach roughly 1,000–1,500, indicating limited early penetration as agents largely maintain incumbent behaviors governed by the habit threshold of 2.0.

A transition occurs between steps 5 and 10, where the adoption rate accelerates sharply to a slope of approximately 737 choices per step. During this phase, cumulative selections increase from roughly 1,500 to 5,000, suggesting that social influence mechanisms—operating through a Watts-Strogatz network structure with 30% interaction probability and moderate social susceptibility (0.2)—begin overcoming habitual inertia. The acceleration continues through steps 10–15, with the slope stabilizing around 927–935 choices per step, where cumulative totals rise from 5,000 to approximately 10,000.

From step 15 through the conclusion at step 32, the adoption maintains a statistically significant linear growth pattern (tau = 1.000, p = 0.027 across all rolling windows) with a consistent slope near 935 choices per step. This sustained linear increase carries cumulative selections from roughly 10,000 at step 15, through approximately 15,000 at step 20, 19,000 at step 25, and ultimately to 25,644 at step 32. The absence of detected peaks or valleys confirms irreversible adoption momentum once established, while the inflection point at step 31 suggests approaching market saturation constraints within the agent population of 1,000 consumers.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The dynamics indicate that transitioning consumer food preferences requires accounting for substantial initial inertia periods where habitual behavior dominates despite awareness campaigns. Investment in shifting health and environmental perceptions proves most critical during early stages (steps 0–10), as these factors must accumulate sufficiently to trigger the threshold-based habit-breaking mechanisms. Once adoption accelerates beyond step 10, social network effects become self-sustaining, suggesting that resources should pivot from individual education to community-level influence amplification during middle phases. The linear late-stage growth (steps 15–32) demonstrates that consumer behavioral shifts, once established, maintain predictable momentum, allowing for long-term supply chain and market planning with confidence in continued alternative milk demand growth through the simulation horizon.
