The cumulative count of female prey killed begins at zero and increases rapidly during the initial approximately 500 time steps, with values at step 500 ranging from approximately 60 to 120 across different simulation runs. This early acceleration reflects high encounter rates between hunters and female herds when prey populations remain abundant and spatially accessible.

Between approximately 500 and 1000 time steps, the accumulation rate decelerates markedly, with trajectories converging toward values between approximately 110 and 125. During this interval, individual runs exhibit step-like plateaus indicating periods without successful female kills interspersed with occasional successful hunts, suggesting diminishing returns as the female prey population becomes depleted.

After approximately 1000 to 1500 time steps, nearly all trajectories stabilize at terminal values between approximately 118 and 125 cumulative female kills, maintaining these levels with minimal change through the remainder of the observation period extending beyond 2000 steps. This saturation indicates the effective depletion of the female prey population to levels where further hunting encounters become statistically rare, effectively reaching a functional extinction threshold for females within the prey population.

Notable variation exists across simulation runs during the initial 500 steps, with one trajectory exhibiting substantially delayed accumulation reaching only approximately 60 kills by step 500 before rapidly converging toward the typical saturation range by step 1000. This variability reflects the stochastic nature of hunter-prey encounters and potential differences in hunting selectivity or spatial distribution patterns across runs.

**Implications for decision makers:** The consistent saturation of cumulative kills at approximately 120 individuals across nearly all runs suggests that the initial female prey population is being driven to effective extinction under the current parameter configuration. The rapid accumulation during the first 500 steps indicates that hunting pressure is highly effective initially, while the subsequent plateau confirms that the prey population lacks sufficient resilience or recruitment to sustain the female subpopulation against persistent hunting pressure. Decision makers should note that the system exhibits low variability in final outcomes despite stochastic intermediate dynamics, suggesting that extinction of the female subpopulation is a robust outcome under these conditions.
