The total energy available to forager bands begins at a slightly positive value, approximately 1,500 energy units, and declines continuously throughout the simulation duration. During the initial 500 steps, the metric drops rapidly to approximately -5,000 units, displaying considerable variation across simulation runs with deviations spanning roughly 2,000 units. This early divergence indicates sensitivity to stochastic initial conditions, such as variable hunting success rates or the spatial distribution of prey patches during the establishment phase.

Following step 500, the trajectories converge and exhibit a steady, approximately linear decline through step 2,000 and beyond. During this period, energy decreases at a consistent rate of approximately 15 to 17 units per step, with minimal variation between runs. By step 2,000, values range between -32,000 and -35,000 units, with some runs continuing to approximately -38,000 units by step 2,200.

The persistent negative trend indicates that forager energy expenditure consistently exceeds energy acquisition from hunting activities. Given that foragers expend energy during movement while acquiring energy only through successful hunts, the sustained linear decline after step 500 suggests that hunting returns become insufficient to offset movement costs, likely due to prey depletion or increasing search effort as prey populations decline. The accumulation of deeply negative values—reaching nearly -40,000 units—demonstrates that under the current parameter configuration where foragers cannot die from energy depletion, the population accumulates severe energy debt rather than achieving equilibrium.

For researchers examining sustainable hunting pressures, these results indicate that the current combination of 20 foragers, 250 initial prey herds, and the specified movement and processing costs creates an unsustainable energetic trajectory. The convergence of all simulation runs onto the same declining path after step 500 suggests that regardless of initial hunting success, the foraging economy inevitably becomes energetically deficient under the tested conditions. This implies that adaptive foraging behavior based on optimal foraging theory is insufficient to prevent progressive energy decline, suggesting that parameter adjustments—such as reducing forager density, increasing prey food values, or decreasing movement costs—would be necessary to establish sustainable energy equilibrium in the model.
