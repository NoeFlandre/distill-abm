 The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system . Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods .  The average deviation from recommended livestock levels begins at approximately 0.007, indicating that farmers initially operate very close to recommended stocking rates . During the first decade, this deviation increases significantly as farmers adjust their strategies in response to local pasture conditions and rainfall variability . The system exhibits cyclical behavior with a dominant period of approximately 49 years .  The average available green biomass across all pasture patches begins at approximately 45,000 units and follows a trajectory of initial decline followed by sustained long-term recovery . This decline intensifies through approximately year 27, when the system reaches its minimum value of roughly 42,057 units . From year 33 onward, the biomass exhibits a consistently increasing trend .  The average number of livestock destocked per household initiates at zero but surges to approximately 6.6 animals by year 3, indicating severe resource scarcity during the initial adaptation phase . This early spike reflects the immediate pressure on vegetation when 60 households commence with 90 animals each on fixed private parcels under highly variable rainfall conditions .  The number of households engaging in social learning begins at zero and follows a logistic diffusion trajectory . The persistence of low final inequality despite high environmental variability suggests that private property frameworks combined with adaptive learning can support both ecological sustainability and reasonable distributional outcomes in grazing systems .  The number of E-RO households undergoes a sharp decline between years 11 and 21, losing nearly one-third of the peak value by year 20 . The decline continues through year 45, though at a moderated rate between years 22 and 65 . After approximately year 66, the number stabilizes near 14 to 15 households .
