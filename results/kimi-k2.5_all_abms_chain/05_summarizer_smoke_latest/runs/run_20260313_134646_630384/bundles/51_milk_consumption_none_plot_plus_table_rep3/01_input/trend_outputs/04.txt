The simulation tracks the cumulative count of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk selections across 1,000 agents over 33 time steps (representing the period 1974–2005). The metric begins at zero and exhibits a strictly monotonic increase throughout the entire observation period, terminating at approximately 25,644 total choices by the final time step. The overall mean value across all time steps is approximately 11,157, with a median of 10,679, indicating that the distribution of cumulative choices is slightly right-skewed due to the accelerating nature of the adoption curve.

The growth trajectory follows a distinct three-phase pattern characterized by evolving rates of increase. In the initial phase spanning steps 0–4, the accumulation occurs at a relatively modest rate of approximately 201 choices per step. During the transitional phase covering steps 5–9, the adoption rate accelerates sharply to roughly 737 choices per step. From step 10 onward through step 29, the growth stabilizes into a consistent linear pattern with a sustained rate of approximately 935 choices per step.

Statistical trend analysis confirms that each five-step window exhibits a significantly increasing pattern (Mann-Kendall tau = 1.000, p = 0.027 across all windows), with no detected peaks or valleys interrupting the upward trajectory. The absence of significant oscillatory deviations from the trend line, despite the identification of oscillation indices in the later steps (27–31), indicates that the underlying adoption dynamics remain dominated by persistent growth rather than cyclical fluctuations. The change point detected at step 32 corresponds to the series termination point where the cumulative count reaches its maximum observed value of 25,644.

This pattern reflects the gradual erosion of incumbent whole milk habits (initially set at maximum strength) through agent interactions within the Watts-Strogatz network topology, combined with social influence mechanisms and disposition triggering. The transition from slow initial adoption to stable linear growth suggests that once a critical mass of agents begins considering alternatives—driven by perception of health characteristics (mean initial perception 1.69) and environmental attributes (mean initial perception 1.12)—the social influence effect (susceptibility 0.2, conformity 0.28) propagates alternative milk choices at a constant aggregate rate across the population.
