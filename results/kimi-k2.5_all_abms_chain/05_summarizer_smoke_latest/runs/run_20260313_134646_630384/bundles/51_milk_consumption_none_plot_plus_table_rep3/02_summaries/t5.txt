Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk initiates at 5.29 units at time step 0, representing the 1974 baseline when whole milk dominated market preferences. A phase of rapid adoption ensues, with consumption increasing significantly through time step 11, where it reaches approximately 1265.47 units. The simulation tracks the cumulative count of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk selections across 1,000 agents over 33 time steps. The metric begins at zero and exhibits a strictly monotonic increase throughout the entire observation period, terminating approximately 25,644 total choices by the final time step. Statistical analysis confirms significant monotonic decreasing trends in five of six examined intervals. Only the periods covering steps 15-19 and 25-29 showed no statistically significant trend, consistent with the observed plateau phases. The overall trajectory demonstrates an initial rapid adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at a baseline value of 1.0000, indicating a neutral habit effect on choice functions. From time step 5 through step 32, the factor exhibits a statistically significant monotonic increase. The rate of increase is most pronounced between time steps 10 and 14, where the factor rises with an average slope of 0.030 per step. Metric shows rapid acceleration characterized by a Mann-Kendall slope of 0.063, rising sharply to approximately 0.25–0.30. This corresponds to the initial disposition triggering period where agents begin considering low-fat alternatives despite high initial habit strength. In the subsequent middle phase from roughly time steps 10 through 24, the rate of increase diminishes substantially.
