This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence. The dynamics replicate the historical shift from whole milk dominance toward alternative varieties, with social influence and habit formation mechanisms generating the observed non-linear adoption curve. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent initiates at approximately 5.29 units at time step 0, representing the 1974 baseline when whole milk dominated market preferences. Trend analysis confirms that each five-step window exhibits a significantly increasing pattern (Mann-Kendall tau = 1,000, p = 0.027 across all windows), with no detected peaks or valleys interrupting the upward trajectory. The social influence effect propagates alternative milk choices at a constant aggregate rate across the population. The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed per agent begins at approximately 5.3 units at step 0 and increases to approximately 1457 units by step 32. This period marks a transition where alternative milk varieties intensifies among agents. By the end of the simulation period, habitual behavior substantially amplifies the choice function for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk varieties. The mean choice function deviation exhibits a pronounced monotonic increasing trend across the 33 time steps. The temporal evolution is characterized by three distinct phases of growth with markedly different rates of change.
