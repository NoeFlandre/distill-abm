Model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. Individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers. This trajectory manifests three distinct phases characterizing the historical transition from incumbent whole milk dominance to alternative milk adoption. The initial phase spanning roughly steps 0 through 10, consumption undergoes a steep decline from the starting value of approximately 2,650 down to around 950–1,000 units. This rapid decrease reflects the initial erosion of strong habitual preferences as agents begin processing emerging health and environmental information. The model produces a stable equilibrium in which whole milk maintains a consistent minority selection probability of approximately 7% per agent per time step throughout the entire 1974-2005 period. The increasing variance across simulation runs reflects the accumulation of stochastic effects from social network interactions. The metric begins at approximately zero at step 0, representing the initial state of minimal skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption in 1974. Between steps 0 and 10, the quantity exhibits rapid exponential-like growth, ascending sharply from near zero to reach values ranging between 1200 and 1280 by step 10. This period corresponds to the initial rapid adoption phase during the late 1970s and early 1980s. The deviation continues to rise gradually, reaching terminal values of approximately 0.74 to 0.75 by step 32. The pace of divergence decreases as the population approaches a stable level of heterogeneity. Despite emerging positive health perceptions, the extremely strong incumbent habit strength of 10.0 for whole milk creates a substantial disconnection between evolving health values. From step 3 to step 10, the metric stabilizes and plateaus, maintaining a relatively constant value between approximately 13.3 and 13.5 through step 32. This stabilization indicates that the system reaches a steady state where the average misalignment between consumers' environmental values and their milk choices remains persistent and substantial.
