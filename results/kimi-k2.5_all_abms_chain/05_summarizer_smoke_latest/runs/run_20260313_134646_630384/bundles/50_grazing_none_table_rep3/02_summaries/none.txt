This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. These operate through a yearly sequence where green biomass updates, households with E-RO-SL1 types update their r-parameters, households update behavioral strategies (if switching is enabled), make livestock placement decisions, livestock feeds on biomass, households observe neighbor performance, reserve biomass updates, and households observe and remember reserve biomass states.

The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. It seeks to evaluate how learning capabilities affect household resilience and pasture conditions in private property grazing systems. Specific objectives include analyzing the impact of social learning double-loop processes through strategy switching in heterogeneous populations, observing the diffusion and competition between different learning behaviors in mixed populations, and assessing how demographic settings and initial herd sizes influence system outcomes. The model allows for the evaluation of outcomes across social, ecological, and economic dimensions including average herd size, pasture biomass condition, and surviving number of households.

The average initial risk attitude among social learning agents (E-RO-SL1 type) remains invariant at approximately 0.0074 throughout the entire simulation duration from initialization (timestep 0) through timestep 100. This metric exhibits no temporal variation, maintaining an identical value across all 101 time steps with zero standard deviation and variance.

In the context of the extended RAGE model, this constancy indicates that the metric represents a fixed baseline attribute assigned to agents at the start of the simulation rather than a dynamically evolving variable. As a measure of initial disposition to deviate from recommended livestock numbers, the stability reflects the permanent record of starting heterogeneity in uncertainty management among the social learning population. Unlike current risk attitudes that may update through learning-by-doing or social learning processes during the yearly decision-making sequence, this initial value remains unchanged, serving as a constant reference point for evaluating how learning behaviors develop from their original baseline conditions in the private property grazing system.

The mean household risk attitude (r-parameter) across the population begins at a minimal value near 0.007 during the initial timesteps, substantially below the initial parameter setting of 0.5. During the first 21 timesteps, the metric undergoes a significant monotonic increase, rising rapidly to approximately 0.36 as the mixed population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 agents begins updating their uncertainty management strategies.

Between timesteps 21 and 37, the trajectory enters a highly oscillatory phase characterized by multiple peaks and valleys. Values fluctuate between approximately 0.36 and 0.38, reaching a maximum of 0.3814 around timestep 33. This oscillatory behavior coincides with the period when E-RO-SL1 agents actively engage in social learning to update their r-parameters based on neighbor observations, creating dynamic feedback loops within the heterogeneous population.

Following the peak at timestep 33, the metric enters a sustained decline through approximately timestep 64, dropping from 0.38 to around 0.19. This decreasing trend is statistically significant across multiple consecutive windows spanning timesteps 33 to 65, indicating a systematic relaxation of the population's uncertainty management or risk aversion levels after the initial peak.

From timestep 64 through the conclusion of the simulation at timestep 100, the metric stabilizes at approximately 0.19, with no statistically significant trend detected in the final windows. The oscillation dominant period of 49 timesteps aligns with the transition from the initial adaptive phase to this stabilized state.

Throughout the simulation, the trajectory reflects the collective adaptation process where households adjust their uncertainty management parameters through learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms. The early rapid increase suggests initial over-correction or heightened sensitivity to uncertainty, followed by a calibration phase that settles into a moderate, stable level of risk attitude in the long term.

Subsequent to this plateau, a sustained recovery phase commences around year 33 and continues through the conclusion of the simulation at year 100. This ascending trajectory exhibits markedly variable rates of increase across distinct sub-periods. After this mid-simulation acceleration, the rate of increase progressively decelerates through years 55 to 100, with increments becoming gradually smaller though remaining consistently positive, culminating in a final value of approximately 121,073 units.

Superimposed upon these directional trends, the system exhibits oscillatory dynamics with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 years, with notable oscillatory activity concentrated around years 10 through 20 and again near years 41 through 49. In the context of this extension to the RAGE model—featuring heterogeneous populations of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types managing private pastures with stationary households—the initial decline corresponds to immediate pressure from the substantial initial livestock endowment (90 animals per household) prior to the activation of learning mechanisms. This dynamic reflects the interplay between substantial initial livestock endowments (90 animals per household across 60 stationary private-property households) and the model's learning mechanisms. The early decline suggests that initial stocking rates exceeded immediate sustainable carrying capacity, or that agents required time to calibrate their uncertainty management parameters (initially set to 0.5) to local conditions. The pronounced recovery from timestep 27 onward indicates that the heterogenous population of E-RO (baseline), E-LBD (learning-by-doing), and E-RO-SL1 (social learning) behavioral types successfully adapted their grazing strategies. The simulation begins with an initial average livestock holding of 90 animals per household at year 0. A sharp decline occurs during the first 37 years, with livestock numbers falling rapidly to reach a minimum of approximately 41 animals by year 37. This period represents a crash phase where initial overstocking leads to significant destocking and pasture degradation under private property constraints. Following this decline, the system enters a stabilization phase between years 37 and 55, where livestock numbers fluctuate narrowly around 41 to 47 animals as households adjust to sustainable stocking levels. From year 55 onward, a gradual recovery trend emerges, with livestock numbers slowly increasing through the remaining simulation period. By year 100, the average livestock holding reaches approximately 47.5 animals. This late-stage recovery reflects the influence of learning processes—particularly learning-by-doing among E-LBD agents and social learning among E-RO-SL1 agents—enabling households to adapt their risk management strategies and improve livestock retention compared to the baseline E-RO behavioral type. The final equilibrium remains well below initial stocking levels, indicating that learning-based adaptation facilitates partial recovery but cannot fully overcome the ecological constraints of the private property grazing system within the simulated timeframe.

At initialization, no destocking occurs. Immediately thereafter, at timestep 3, the average number of destocked livestock reaches a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals per household. This initial shock represents a period of severe resource constraint where households are forced to rapidly reduce herd sizes.

Following this peak, the system enters a phase of high volatility but declining amplitude. Local maxima occur at timesteps 9 (approximately 3.4 animals), 12 (approximately 2.7 animals), 17 (approximately 1.5 animals), and 22 (approximately 1.2 animals), with each successive peak representing lower destocking pressure than the previous. Intervening troughs show corresponding decreases, falling below 1.0 animal by timestep 18 and continuing to diminish.

After approximately timestep 30, the dynamics stabilize significantly. The system converges toward a low steady state, with values fluctuating narrowly around 0.3 animals by timestep 100. This represents a 95% reduction from the initial crisis peak.

Statistically, the early period (timesteps 0-32) exhibits significant decreasing trends across multiple consecutive windows, indicating consistent improvement in resource management. The middle period shows no significant trend, while late periods demonstrate slight variability but maintain near-zero destocking levels.

Interpreted within the context of the RAGE extension model, these dynamics reflect the efficacy of adaptive learning mechanisms in private property grazing systems. The initial destocking spike corresponds to the adjustment period where stationary households face initial resource scarcity. The subsequent decline illustrates how learning-by-doing (E-LBD) and social learning (E-RO-SL1) behaviors, combined with baseline E-RO strategies, enable households to progressively optimize stocking rates and reduce forced destocking. The final stabilization at low levels indicates that after approximately 30 timesteps, the mixed population of learning agents achieves a sustainable equilibrium where resource constraints rarely necessitate emergency herd reductions.

The distribution of healthy livestock across households begins at perfect equality, with the inequality metric effectively at zero, reflecting the homogeneous initial condition where all 60 households commence with identical livestock endowments of 90 units each. Over the century-long simulation horizon, inequality emerges through a persistent monotonic upward trajectory, culminating in a terminal value of approximately 0.073. This modest final level indicates that while disparities in livestock health develop over time, the system maintains relatively equitable distributional outcomes despite heterogeneous agent behaviors and environmental variability.

The accumulation of inequality proceeds through distinct temporal phases characterized by shifts in growth rates. During the initial establishment period spanning approximately the first 25 timesteps, the system undergoes rapid structural differentiation, with inflection points occurring at timesteps 8, 13, 15, 20, and 25 marking successive transitions in the velocity of divergence. These early adjustments reflect the initial differentiation between the three fixed behavioral cohorts—baseline expectation-based rotation (E-RO), learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1)—as their distinct approaches to uncertainty management and decision-making under private property constraints begin generating differential livestock health outcomes.

Between timesteps 25 and 57, inequality continues mounting through a prolonged phase of steady accumulation, punctuated by an additional inflection at timestep 37. This middle period corresponds to the operational maturation of learning processes, where households with E-RO-SL1 capabilities update their risk parameters through social observation within a knowledge radius of one patch, while E-LBD agents refine decisions through individual experience and E-RO agents maintain static strategies. A significant structural break occurs at timestep 51, where the slope of inequality growth steepens perceptibly, transitioning from negligible incremental gains to a sustained increase of approximately 0.001 per timestep.

The final phase from timestep 57 onward exhibits continued divergence interrupted by cyclical dynamics. Following a second major transition at timestep 71, the trajectory enters an oscillatory regime with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 timesteps, manifesting prominently between timesteps 78 and 93. These fluctuations likely emerge from feedback interactions between climatic variability—with rainfall exhibiting substantial standard deviation around the mean—and the adaptive grazing decisions of heterogeneous agents managing stationary private pastures. Throughout this late period, inequality approaches its maximum value of 0.0726 at timestep 100, having maintained consistent upward momentum across all analytical windows despite the superimposed oscillations.

The modest magnitude of terminal inequality, combined with the absence of extreme divergence, suggests that the fixed behavioral heterogeneity and adaptive learning mechanisms operate within constraints imposed by private property institutions and environmental uncertainty, preventing the emergence of severe stratification in household livestock wealth while permitting the gradual differentiation of outcomes across the mixed population of learning agents.

The number of households engaged in social learning initiates at zero and follows a saturation trajectory over the 100-timestep simulation period, ultimately stabilizing at approximately 32 households. This progression exhibits distinct phases of adoption characteristic of diffusion processes within the stationary, private-property grazing context.

Following this initiation lag, a rapid acceleration phase occurs between timesteps 7 and 28, characterized by steep monotonic increases where the number of participating households rises sharply from near-zero to approximately 20-25 households. A deceleration phase follows from approximately timestep 28 to timestep 63, where the rate of new adoption diminishes considerably though the absolute number continues to increase gradually from roughly 25 to the maximum value of approximately 32.07 households observed at timestep 63. This slowing reflects the saturation of the adoption curve as the pool of remaining non-adopting households diminishes within the fixed population of 60 households.

From timestep 63 through the conclusion at timestep 100, the system enters a stabilization phase where engagement maintains approximately 32 households with minimal variation, ending at 32.07. This plateau represents an equilibrium where roughly 53% of the household population maintains active social learning engagement, suggesting that under the specified parameter configuration with heterogeneous behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), social learning processes diffuse beyond the initially designated E-RO-SL1 agents to encompass a substantial portion of the farming community, despite the absence of strategy switching mechanisms.

The substantial cumulative destocking (exceeding the initial herd size of 90 animals per household when accounting for the entire simulation) indicates repeated destocking events driven by ecological pressures, yet the flattening curve implies progressive adaptive capacity in managing climatic uncertainty without addressing whether this represents optimal or suboptimal system performance.
