The simulation commenced with a population of 125 female prey animals. Over the course of approximately 2,312 time steps, this population underwent a complete extinction, declining to zero individuals by the conclusion of the run.

The trajectory of decline was characterized by several distinct phases. An initial rapid decrease occurred during the first 475 time steps, after which the rate of decline shifted at approximately time step 600. A subsequent change in dynamics was observed around time step 875, where the descent continued but with notable periodic fluctuations. Throughout the entire sequence, the population exhibited regular oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 time steps, superimposed over the general downward trend.

During the latter portion of the simulation, the population persisted at very low levels for an extended duration, with the median population across all time steps being approximately 1 individual. This prolonged persistence at low numbers preceded the final extinction event at time step 2,311.

Within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these dynamics suggest that the initial population of 125 female prey, representing a subset of the 250 total initial ungulates, faced unsustainable predation pressure from the 20 human foragers operating under optimal foraging constraints. The 63-tick oscillation period likely reflects seasonal environmental cycles or demographic pulses related to the two-year birth spacing interval and annual tick cycle. The change points at 475, 600, and 875 time steps indicate thresholds where hunting efficiency or prey vulnerability shifted, possibly corresponding to population densities where encounter rates changed or where the age structure of the remaining herds altered hunting selectivity. The prolonged final phase with median population near 1 represents an extinction vortex where demographic stochasticity and Allee effects, combined with continued hunting pressure, prevented recovery despite the presence of nursing calves that temporarily increased food value for predators.
