The male megaherbivore population initiated at 125 individuals and underwent a progressive decline to extinction approximately 6.26 years (2,284 ticks) into the simulation. The trajectory exhibited distinct phases of decelerating decline punctuated by temporary demographic recovery and superimposed oscillatory dynamics.

During the initial approximately two-year period (ticks 0–730), the population experienced rapid attrition, falling from 125 to roughly 10 individuals. This phase featured the steepest rates of decline, with negative slopes indicating continuous substantial losses as hunting pressure impacted the initial cohort.

Between years 2 and 4 (ticks approximately 700–1,400), the rate of decline decelerated markedly. While remaining negative, the slope flattened by an order of magnitude compared to the initial phase, allowing the population to persist at low densities, typically fewer than 10 individuals. Throughout this interval, the decline exhibited superimposed oscillations with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks (roughly bi-monthly cycles), creating regular fluctuations between local peaks and valleys even as the overall trend remained downward.

Notably, during years 3.8 to 4.4 (ticks 1,392–1,623), the population temporarily reversed its long-term trajectory, exhibiting a slight increasing trend that elevated counts marginally before subsiding. This brief recovery interrupted the otherwise persistent decline.

From approximately year 4.4 until extinction at year 6.3 (ticks 1,624–2,284), the population fluctuated without clear directional trend at extremely low densities—frequently below 2 individuals—while maintaining the approximately 63-tick oscillatory pattern. During this final phase, values oscillated between near-zero and approximately 2.5 individuals until the population reached zero at tick 2,284.

The substantial disparity between the arithmetic mean of 21.0 and the median of 1.35 indicates that the population spent the majority of the simulation duration at very low densities, with the higher early-phase values and temporary recoveries skewing the average upward. The oscillatory dynamics, consistent with the model's seasonal parameters (seasonality set to 0.8) and reproductive intervals (birth spacing of 730 ticks), modulated the decline throughout all phases, suggesting regular feedback between hunter foraging behavior, environmental seasonality, and prey demography even as the population approached extinction.
