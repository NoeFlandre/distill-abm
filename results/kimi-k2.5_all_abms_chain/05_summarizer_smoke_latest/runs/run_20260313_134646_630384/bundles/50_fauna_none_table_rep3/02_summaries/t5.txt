The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation duration. The total energy available to forager bands exhibits a pronounced monotonic decrease throughout the simulation. This trajectory represents a transition from positive collective energy reserves to a state of substantial energy debt. Cumulative mortality of female megaherbivores exhibits a logistic-like trajectory. The metric initiates at zero and demonstrates strong positive acceleration through the first 125 ticks. Inflection points at tick 463 mark the transition from exponential-like growth to decelerating gains. Peak, and subsequent decline over the 200-year simulation duration (73,000 ticks) growth period is non-linear, featuring significant oscillatory behavior. Indices 1073 (value 2.875), 1214 (value 3.45) and 1392 (value 4.45) demarcate shifts in growth rates. The male megaherbivore population initiated at 125 individuals and underwent a progressive decline to extinction approximately 6.26 years (2,284 ticks) into the simulation ensemble. During the initial approximately two-year period (ticks 0–730), the population experienced rapid attrition, falling from 125 to roughly 10 individuals. The prey population initiated at approximately 167 individuals and underwent deterministic extinction by tick 2311. Local maxima emerged at tick 730 (11 individuals), tick 803 (7 individuals), and tick 982 (3 individuals), demonstrating successively lower recovery peaks as the population diminished. These oscillations likely reflect seasonal environmental fluctuations interacting with the herds' demographic cycles. Dynamics shift at key junctures, notably around ticks 500, 625, and 875. Trend temporarily reverses, showing a significant increase in total energy before resuming decline. Similar recovery occurs between ticks 1856 and 2087. Vegetation coverage returns to approximately 33.0%, effectively matching initial conditions. Grass regrowth mechanisms successfully balance herbivore consumption, maintaining constant baseline fodder availability despite continuous grazing pressure.
