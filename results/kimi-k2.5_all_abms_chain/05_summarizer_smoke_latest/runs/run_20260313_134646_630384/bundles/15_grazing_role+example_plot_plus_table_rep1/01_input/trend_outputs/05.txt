The average livestock holdings per household, inclusive of destocked animals, begins at 90 animals. Over the first 37 years, this value declines rapidly and consistently, reaching a minimum of approximately 41 animals. This steep initial reduction reflects the pressure of high stocking rates on private pastures, where households facing deteriorating vegetation conditions are compelled to destock significantly to prevent total resource collapse. The inflection point observed around year 3 marks the transition from the most acute phase of destocking to a more gradual decline as the system approaches its lowest equilibrium.

Following the minimum at year 37, the trajectory reverses and enters a prolonged recovery phase lasting through year 100. By year 55, a significant change point occurs where the accumulation of livestock accelerates notably, with the average holdings increasing from approximately 42 animals to nearly 48 by the end of the simulation. This upward trend, which becomes progressively shallower in slope from years 66 through 100, indicates that households successfully rebuild their herds as they accumulate experiential knowledge about sustainable stocking levels and observe neighbor outcomes.

However, the recovery is not uniform across the population. After approximately year 40, the trajectories diverge substantially, with final values at year 100 ranging from roughly 41 animals in the lowest-performing group to over 60 animals in the highest-performing cohort. This divergence reflects the differential effectiveness of the three learning strategies: households employing learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms achieve significantly higher stable herd sizes compared to those relying on static rule-of-thumb approaches. The oscillation patterns detected between years 5 and 20, and again between years 39 and 42, suggest temporary instabilities as households adjust their strategies in response to feedback from pasture conditions.

By the final decades of the simulation, the system approaches a new equilibrium around 47-48 animals per household on average, though the persistent variance between learning strategy outcomes indicates that long-term livelihood security remains contingent on the specific adaptive approach employed by individual households.
