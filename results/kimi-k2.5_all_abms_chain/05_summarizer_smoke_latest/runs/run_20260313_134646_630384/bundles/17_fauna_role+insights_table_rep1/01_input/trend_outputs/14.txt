The vegetation coverage exhibits rapid stabilization to an equilibrium state of approximately 33% of the landscape following initial establishment, with distinct phase transitions occurring at time steps 150, 300, 475, and 725 during the first two simulated years. Thereafter, the system maintains long-term stability around a mean of 32.7%, terminating at essentially the same level (33.0%) after 73,000 time steps (200 simulated years).

Superimposed on this stable equilibrium is a pronounced oscillatory regime characterized by a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 time steps, creating regular pulses in resource availability. An absolute minimum coverage of 24.9% occurs precisely at time step 365, marking an annual resource bottleneck where vegetation availability drops roughly eight percentage points below mean levels before seasonal recovery.

Temporal trend analysis reveals predominantly increasing trajectories through the first 1,600 time steps across sequential observation windows, reflecting establishment dynamics as the vegetation pattern adjusts to initial conditions and herbivore grazing pressures. Subsequently, localized decreasing trends emerge between time steps 1,624 and 2,088, representing phase adjustments within the stable oscillatory cycle rather than indicating long-term degradation.

For decision makers, these findings indicate that despite strong seasonality driving significant intra-annual variation (nearly 25% reduction in available forage at seasonal nadirs), the grassland vegetation demonstrates robust resilience under current megaherbivore grazing and hunter foraging pressures, maintaining consistent baseline coverage over centennial timescales. However, the severe seasonal depletion suggests that megaherbivore populations face periodic nutritional stress that may drive seasonal demographic bottlenecks or range-shift behaviors, while the early transition period (first 725 time steps) represents a critical establishment phase where system dynamics stabilize and should be excluded from short-term trend assessments.
