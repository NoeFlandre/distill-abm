Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk initiates at approximately 5.3 units, consistent with the 1974 baseline where whole milk maintained market dominance. During the initial phase spanning roughly the first ten time steps, consumption increases rapidly and significantly, exceeding 1200 units by step 10 and approaching 1265 units at step 11. From time step 15 through time step 29, adoption rate stabilizes at 933 to 936 selections per period across successive intervals. Cumulative count rises monotonically without regression, reflecting irreversible learning and justification mechanisms with a threshold of 0.61 that prevented reversion to whole milk. Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5.3 units per agent at the initial time step, reflecting the minimal market penetration of low-fat milk varieties in 1974. Over the first nine time steps, the metric rises sharply and significantly, exceeding 400 units by time step 9. Growth rate in this final phase suggests habit formation is approaching saturation as the population converges on stable new consumption patterns. This trajectory reflects the historical transition from whole milk dominance to widespread adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties between 1974 and 2005. Agents are disrupted by social influence and habit persistence. This prevents the system from resolving the value-behavior gap. Persistent misalignment characterizes the population despite sustained environmental norms.
