 This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005 . It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence .  Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent initiates at approximately 5.3 units, consistent with the 1974 baseline where whole milk maintained market dominance . During the initial phase spanning roughly the first ten time steps, consumption increases rapidly and significantly, exceeding 1200 units by step 10 and approaching 1265 units at step 11 . The trajectory stabilizes into an oscillatory pattern with values fluctuating between approximately 1183 and 1265 .  The average weekly consumption of whole milk per person begins at approximately 2,655 units in 1974, declining substantially to roughly 253 units by 2005 . The total number of milk choices accumulates linearly from zero at the initial time step to 32,000 at the final time step (index 32) The trajectory culminates at approximately 25,644 total alternative milk selections by time step 32, representing substantial adoption saturation approaching the theoretical maximum possible selections .  The consumption level represents a nearly 275-fold increase from the initial state, quantifying the substantial historical shift from whole milk to low-fat alternatives over the simulated 31-year period . The trajectory of whole milk consumption reflects the historical transition between whole milk dominance to widespread adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties .  This pattern corresponds to the modeled transition from whole milk dominance to varied adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties between 1974 and 2005 . Initial uniformity reflects the 1974 baseline where whole milk predominated and agents shared similar traditional consumption habits . The progressive divergence captures the differentiation of consumer preferences as agents developed heterogeneous health perceptions .
