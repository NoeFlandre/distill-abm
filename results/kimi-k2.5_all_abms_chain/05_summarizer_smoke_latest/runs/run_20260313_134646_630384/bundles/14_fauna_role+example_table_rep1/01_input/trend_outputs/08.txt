The male megaherbivore population initiates at 125 individuals and follows a deterministic extinction trajectory, reaching zero abundance by tick 2284. The decline proceeds through four distinct demographic phases characterized by changing rates of decrease and temporary population reversals.

During the initial 1,391 ticks, the cohort experiences profound and statistically significant decrease across consecutive temporal windows. The steepest decline occurs between ticks 232 and 463, with a slope of -0.244 individuals per tick, indicating rapid depletion of the male population through hunting pressure. This early collapse reflects the vulnerability of the founding population to encounter rates with optimal foragers, compounded by the higher nutritional value assigned to males relative to females, which incentivizes selective harvesting of the larger, more energetically profitable sex.

Superimposed upon this downward trend are persistent oscillations with a dominant period of 63 ticks, generating recurring local maxima that diminish in amplitude over time. Early peaks reach approximately 10.5 individuals at tick 730, while oscillations after tick 1000 rarely exceed 2-3 individuals. These fluctuations likely correspond to seasonal resource pulses and breeding cycles governed by the high seasonality parameter, where temporary grass abundance enables brief demographic rebounds that are subsequently dampened by renewed hunting effort.

Between ticks 1392 and 1623, the trajectory deviates markedly from the preceding decline, exhibiting a significant increasing trend. This temporary recovery, elevating abundance briefly to approximately 6-8 individuals, suggests a density-dependent release phenomenon. Reduced population density likely decreases encounter rates with hunters below sustainable harvest thresholds, allowing transient net recruitment before stochastic extinction processes resume dominance.

Following this brief increase, the population enters a stagnation phase from tick 1624 to 2087 characterized by no significant directional trend, with values hovering between 2 and 4 individuals. This represents an unstable quasi-equilibrium where sporadic recruitment events balance mortality, yet insufficiently to drive genuine recovery. Final extinction occurs rapidly after tick 2087, with the last individuals disappearing by tick 2284—approximately 6.25 years into the simulation assuming daily time steps. The substantial disparity between mean abundance (21.0) and median abundance (1.3) reflects the heavily skewed distribution typical of exploited populations, where the temporal record consists predominantly of sparse survivors persisting long after the initial population collapse.
