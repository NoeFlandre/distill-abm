The cumulative number of livestock destocked exhibits a monotonic increasing trend over the 100-year simulation period, beginning at zero and reaching an average terminal value of approximately 97 animals per household, with substantial variation across simulation runs ranging from roughly 58 to 173 animals.

The accumulation rate follows a distinct multi-phase pattern. During the initial approximately 25 years, destocking proceeds most rapidly with an average rate exceeding 4.9 animals per year in the earliest decade, subsequently moderating to roughly 1.5 animals per year through year 21. This early phase accounts for the majority of total destocking accumulation, representing the primary adjustment period where households reduce initial stocking levels in response to localized pasture conditions and learning experiences.

Following the first structural transition around year 25, the rate of increase declines substantially to approximately 0.36 to 0.69 animals per year through year 60, indicating a shift from initial crisis-driven destocking to more gradual adaptive management. After year 60, the accumulation rate stabilizes at roughly 0.31 animals per year through the terminal period, suggesting the approach of a quasi-equilibrium where destocking events become less frequent but continue unabated across all behavioral strategies.

Notably, the trajectories diverge significantly over time, with a spread of approximately 15 animals at year 20 expanding to over 115 animals by year 100. This divergence reflects fundamental differences in the efficacy of the learning strategies employed: some households achieve relative stabilization with cumulative totals plateauing near 58-70 animals, while others experience persistent livestock turnover exceeding 140-170 animals over the century. Periodic fluctuations in accumulation rates detectable around years 45-75 and 79-80 correspond to feedback cycles between vegetation recovery and stocking decisions under the high rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 units) configured in the model.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The persistent upward trend across all scenarios indicates that complete stabilization of herd sizes remains elusive under high rainfall variability, suggesting that risk management strategies must account for continuous, albeit diminishing, asset liquidation over decades. The critical window for intervention occurs within the first 25 years, where accumulation rates are highest; support mechanisms introduced during this initial phase could prevent the lock-in of high-destocking trajectories. Furthermore, the wide variation in outcomes demonstrates that institutional support for learning strategies—particularly those enabling effective social learning or experiential adaptation—significantly influences long-term livelihood security, with suboptimal decision-making approaches resulting in cumulative livestock losses nearly triple those of effective strategies. Policy should prioritize facilitating information exchange and adaptive capacity during the early establishment period to prevent chronic overstocking cycles.
