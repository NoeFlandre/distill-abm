The cumulative mortality of prey populations initiates at zero and exhibits a complex, multi-phasic accumulation pattern over the simulation duration. During the initial approximately 850 time steps, deaths accumulate rapidly with strongly monotonic increases across consecutive analytical windows, rising steeply from zero toward roughly 150 deaths as the naive initial population of 250 animals encounters the full force of 20 human foragers operating under optimal foraging constraints, combined with baseline senescence and external predation pressures. This early phase is punctuated by multiple regime changes at regular intervals (approximately every 125 steps), reflecting successive waves of mortality as distinct demographic cohorts are subjected to hunting pressure.

The dynamics transition sharply around step 850 into an extended plateau phase lasting through step 1159, where the mortality accumulation rate decelerates significantly. Between steps 1160 and 1391, the series exhibits no statistically significant trend for over 230 consecutive steps, indicating the system has achieved a demographic equilibrium where recruitment temporarily balances losses from hunting and natural causes. This plateau suggests the prey population has declined to a density where hunter encounter rates diminish and energy constraints limit foraging efficiency, creating a stable coexistence interval.

Following this equilibrium, mortality accumulation resumes a modest but significant increasing trend between steps 1392 and 1623, possibly reflecting hunter adaptation or demographic stochasticity reducing prey numbers below sustainable thresholds. The series reaches its maximum value of approximately 240 cumulative deaths around step 1876, after which it enters a gradual decline to 235 by the conclusion of the simulation at step 2312. This late-phase decrease, occurring alongside pronounced oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of 63 steps, suggests that replicate populations experiencing higher cumulative mortality collapsed and were removed from the mean calculation, while surviving populations maintained lower terminal mortality profiles. The embedded 63-step oscillation cycles, likely driven by the strong seasonality parameter (0.8) modulating grass availability and prey vulnerability, imprint regular pulses on the mortality curve throughout the latter half of the simulation.

For decision makers: The four-decade persistence of prey populations under continuous hunting pressure, evidenced by the prolonged plateau in cumulative mortality between approximately years 27 and 39, demonstrates that megaherbivore populations possess substantial demographic resilience that can mask vulnerability to exploitation. The pronounced oscillatory patterns indicate that mortality risk varies predictably with seasonal resource availability, suggesting that management interventions or hunting moratoriums applied during high-vulnerability seasonal windows could significantly alter population trajectories. The divergent terminal outcomes—where mean cumulative deaths actually decline in the final phase due to selective removal of high-mortality replicates—highlight that stochastic factors rather than deterministic hunting pressure alone govern final extinction probabilities, indicating that early warning signals during apparent equilibrium phases may be unreliable predictors of eventual population collapse.
