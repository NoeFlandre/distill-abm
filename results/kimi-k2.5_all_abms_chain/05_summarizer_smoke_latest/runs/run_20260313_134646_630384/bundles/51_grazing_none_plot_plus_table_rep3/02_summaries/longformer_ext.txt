This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. At later stages indicates sensitivity to stochastic ecological conditions or initial random variations in household attributes, resulting in some populations maintaining relatively egalitarian distributions while others develop highly stratified livestock holdings. Additionally, there is a temporal pattern of behavioral diffusion in the model, which is characterized by a gradual increase in the number of households employing the E-RO (non-learning) behavioral strategy. This temporal pattern suggests that while the baseline non-learning strategy experiences transient growth during the initial adaptation phase, learning capabilities become dominant in the adaptive landscape of the private property grazing system. The stabilization of the non-learning population after step 65 indicates that the system reaches a quasi-equilibrium where approximately 73% of the original non-learning households retain their initial strategy, while the remainder have successfully adopted alternative learning-based approaches to managing livestock and pasture resources under variable climatic conditions.
