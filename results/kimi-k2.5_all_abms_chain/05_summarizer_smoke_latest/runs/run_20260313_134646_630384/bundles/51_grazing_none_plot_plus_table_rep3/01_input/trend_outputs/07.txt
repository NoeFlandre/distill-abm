The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock begins at effectively zero at initialization, indicating perfect equality in livestock holdings across all 60 households. Over the 100-step simulation period, inequality increases monotonically, reaching a mean value of approximately 0.073 by the final step, though individual simulation runs exhibit considerable heterogeneity in terminal values ranging from roughly 0.030 to 0.095.  

The trajectory of inequality accumulation is characterized by distinct phases. During the initial 20 steps, the metric rises slowly from zero with multiple inflection points at steps 8, 13, 15, and 20, suggesting gradual differentiation in household livestock health outcomes as agents begin applying their respective decision-making heuristics (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1). Between steps 20 and 57, the rate of inequality growth accelerates, marked by inflection points at steps 25 and 37, and a significant change point at step 51 where the slope increases distinctly. Throughout this middle phase, rolling trend analysis confirms statistically significant monotonic increases with slopes escalating from near zero to 0.001.  

After step 60, inequality continues to rise but the divergence between simulation runs becomes more pronounced, with some trajectories exhibiting steep linear increases while others plateau at moderate levels. A second change point occurs at step 71, followed by another at step 100. Notably, oscillatory patterns emerge between steps 78 and 93 with a dominant period of 49 steps, indicating cyclical dynamics in the latter phase of the simulation despite the overall upward trend.  

In the context of private-property grazing systems with heterogeneous learning behaviors, these dynamics suggest that initial equality in livestock endowments (90 head per household) gives way to cumulative differentiation as households with varying risk attitudes (r-parameters) and learning capabilities (learning-by-doing versus social learning) experience divergent success in maintaining healthy herds under stochastic rainfall conditions (mean 200, std 100). The accelerating inequality implies positive feedback mechanisms where early advantages in pasture management or livestock health compound over time, even without strategy switching enabled. The substantial variation across simulation runs at later stages indicates sensitivity to stochastic ecological conditions or initial random variations in household attributes, resulting in some populations maintaining relatively egalitarian distributions while others develop highly stratified livestock holdings.
