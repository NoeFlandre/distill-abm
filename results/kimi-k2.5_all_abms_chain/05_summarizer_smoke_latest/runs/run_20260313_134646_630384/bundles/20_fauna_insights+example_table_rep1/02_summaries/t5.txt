The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. Population of active human hunter-gatherer bands maintains a constant value of 20 across all 2,312 observed time steps. No peaks, declines, or fluctuations occur in the hunter population throughout the simulation period. The cumulative mortality of female prey initiates at zero and accumulates to approximately 123 individuals by the conclusion of the observation period at step 2312. The temporal trajectory reveals distinct phases of accumulation dynamics rather than uniform growth. An initial establishment phase spans steps 0 through 463, characterized by rapid acceleration with accumulation rates exceeding 0.21 individuals per step. Between approximately time step 850 and 1826, the cumulative count approaches an asymptotic maximum of 119 individuals, with the rate of increase slowing to negligible levels. Superimposed on this plateau are regular fluctuations with a periodicity of approximately 63 time steps, reflecting seasonal breeding cycles and the two-year birth spacing interval. Cumulative mortality of prey populations initiates at zero and undergoes rapid accumulation during the initial decades of the simulation. The accumulation continues to slow through the first century, with the rate declining to approximately 0.017 deaths per observation period by year 110. This suggests that predator-prey dynamics have temporarily balanced, possibly due to hunting selectivity shifting toward higher-value targets. Population of female prey initiates at 125 individuals and undergoes precipitous decline throughout the simulation duration of 2,311 ticks. From tick 1,624 through 2,284, the population exhibits no significant directional trend while fluctuating at critically low levels. Despite statistically detectable but demographically negligible increases occurring between ticks 1,160–1,391 and again between 1,856–2,087, the population median remains at 1 individual. Prolonged low-density phase represents an extinction vortex where stochastic processes dominate. 63-tick oscillation periodicity suggests that seasonal protection measures timed with reproductive cycles could theoretically extend population persistence. The total energy stored within male prey populations initiates at 12,500 units, representing the full demographic capacity of the initial cohort. Over the course of the simulation, this total energy exhibits a deterministic decline toward complete depletion, reaching zero by tick 2,311. These early fluctuations correspond to seasonal variations in foraging success, grass availability, or reproductive phenology. The population crosses irreversible extinction thresholds near time step 2,311, where aggregate female energy reserves reach zero. Initial drop reflects the intersection of strong seasonal constraints and immediate grazing pressure from the initial herbivore population. The metric recovers and stabilizes around a long-term mean of approximately 32.7%, oscillating with a dominant period of 63 time steps superimposed on annual fluctuations.
