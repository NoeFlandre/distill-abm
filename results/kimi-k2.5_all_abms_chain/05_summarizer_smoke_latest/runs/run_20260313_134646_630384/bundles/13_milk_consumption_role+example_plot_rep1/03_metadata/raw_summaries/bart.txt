 The model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005 . It captures how individual decision-making factors collectively drive macro-level market shifts . Success is measured by the model's ability to reproduce the historical crossover point where skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption surpassed whole milk consumption .  Consumption declines sharply to approximately 950–1000 units, representing a reduction of roughly 62–64% over the first ten steps . The steep trajectory suggests that initial skepticism toward alternatives erodes as agents process health and environmental information within their social networks . The slower rate of change reflects the stochastic nature of social influence (p-interact at 0.3, social-susceptibility at  0.2) and social-blindness .  The aggregate count of milk selections begins at zero and accumulates at a constant rate of approximately 1,000 decisions per time step throughout the simulation period . By the conclusion of the 31-year timeframe, the total number of choices made reaches roughly 32,000 . The mean whole milk quantity begins at approximately 2,650 units, reflecting the 1974 market conditions .  The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at a baseline value of 1.0 at the start of the simulation period . By step 10, the value reaches approximately 1.08, indicating that habitual tendencies toward skimmed milk have begun to strengthen measurably . The trajectory exhibits remarkably low variance across simulation runs throughout the entire 32-step period .  The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the health values implied by their milk choices begins at 0.0 and increases monotonically throughout the 32 time steps of the simulation . The system appears to be converging toward a stable level of heterogeneity characteristic of the mature market structure observed by 2005 .
