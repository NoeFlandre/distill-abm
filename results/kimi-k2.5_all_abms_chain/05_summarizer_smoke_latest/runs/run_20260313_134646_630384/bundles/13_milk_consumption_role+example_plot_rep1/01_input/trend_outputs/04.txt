The cumulative adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk follows a characteristic S-shaped trajectory across the 32-step simulation period. Initially, during steps 0 through approximately 5, the total count of low-fat milk choices remains minimal, accumulating to fewer than 2,000 selections. This lag phase reflects the initial resistance to change when whole milk dominated consumer habits and awareness of low-fat alternatives remained limited.

Between steps 5 and 25, the adoption curve enters a phase of rapid acceleration, with cumulative choices increasing steeply from approximately 2,000 to nearly 20,000. This period represents the mainstream diffusion of low-fat milk, where social influence mechanisms and shifting health perceptions drive widespread behavioral change. The trajectory maintains a consistent upward momentum through this middle period, indicating sustained momentum in the transition away from whole milk consumption.

During the final phase from steps 25 to 32, the growth rate decelerates markedly as the system approaches saturation. Cumulative choices rise from roughly 20,000 to approximately 25,500–26,000 by the conclusion of the simulation. This slowing reflects the natural ceiling of market adoption as the majority of consumers have already transitioned to low-fat alternatives, leaving fewer individuals available to convert.

Across all simulation runs, the trajectories demonstrate remarkably low variance, with multiple replications following nearly identical paths. This consistency suggests that the underlying social influence and habit formation mechanisms produce robust and predictable patterns of innovation diffusion, capturing the historical transition in British milk consumption with high fidelity.
