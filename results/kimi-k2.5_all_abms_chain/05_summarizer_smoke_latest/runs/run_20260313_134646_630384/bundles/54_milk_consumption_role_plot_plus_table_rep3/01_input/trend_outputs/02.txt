The mean consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits a three-phase trajectory across the 33-step simulation period, representing the years 1974 to 2005. 

In the initial acceleration phase (steps 0–11), consumption rises sharply from a near-baseline value of 5.29 to approximately 1265.47. This represents the breakthrough period where emerging health and environmental perceptions, combined with initial social influence effects, begin to overcome the strong incumbent habits favoring whole milk that characterized the 1974 starting conditions.

The subsequent stabilization phase (steps 11–27) displays a plateau pattern with oscillations between approximately 1183 and 1240. During this middle period, the mean consumption fluctuates around a median value of roughly 1220, with minor peaks at steps 13 (1240.35), 19 (1221.57), and 21 (1226.86), interspersed with valleys at steps 12 (1233.62), 18 (1183.39), and 20 (1214.46). This oscillatory behavior suggests a temporary equilibrium where social influence mechanisms and habit maintenance forces reach a balance, with the probability-based and threshold-based psychological mechanisms for habit-breaking creating variable adoption rates across the agent population.

The final expansion phase (steps 27–32) demonstrates renewed growth, with consumption accelerating from approximately 1240 to a terminal value of 1456.60. This late-stage increase indicates a secondary wave of adoption, potentially driven by accumulated social proof effects, the erosion of remaining habit barriers among late adopters, and the strengthening of social norms favoring skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The trajectory concludes with the alternative milk types achieving dominant market position, successfully replicating the historical crossover pattern observed in British dairy consumption between 1974 and 2005.

Across the full simulation, the mean consumption averages 1051.59, with substantial variance (405.15 standard deviation) reflecting the heterogeneous agent behaviors and the non-linear diffusion process characteristic of social influence-driven market transitions.
