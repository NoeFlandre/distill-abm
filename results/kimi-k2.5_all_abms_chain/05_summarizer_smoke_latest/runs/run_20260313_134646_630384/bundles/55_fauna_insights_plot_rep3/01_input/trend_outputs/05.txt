The cumulative number of postpartum female prey killed begins at zero and increases rapidly during the initial phase of the simulation, rising steeply within the first 500 time steps to reach values between approximately 100 and 120 individuals. Across different simulation runs, the metric accumulates at varying rates, with most trajectories reaching terminal values ranging from 110 to 125 by the time the rapid growth phase concludes.

Following this initial period of active accumulation, the cumulative counts stabilize and plateau across all simulation runs, persisting at these terminal values for the remainder of the simulation duration, which extends to approximately 2000 time steps. The plateau indicates a cessation in the hunting of postpartum females, with no further additions to the cumulative count despite continued simulation runtime.

Variation exists across simulation runs regarding the specific timing of plateau onset and the final cumulative values achieved. Some trajectories stabilize earlier, around time step 500 to 700, at lower cumulative values near 100 to 105, while others continue accumulating until approximately time step 1000 to 1200, reaching higher terminal values of 120 to 125.

Within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these trends suggest that postpartum females—who possess enhanced food value due to nursing offspring—are actively targeted during the initial phases of human-prey interaction when prey populations are abundant and accessible. The subsequent plateau indicates either the successful depletion of postpartum females from the prey population, a shift in hunter foraging strategy toward alternative prey types as postpartum females become scarce, or demographic collapse within prey herds preventing further encounters with reproductive females.

For decision makers analyzing megafaunal extinction hypotheses, these results demonstrate that hunting pressure specifically targeting reproductive females with dependent offspring can rapidly accumulate significant mortality within a few hundred time steps, potentially representing a critical demographic bottleneck. The consistent plateau across runs suggests that this hunting strategy may be self-limiting—either through prey depletion or behavioral switching—but only after substantial damage to the reproductive capacity of prey populations has occurred. Conservation strategies or management interventions would need to address such targeted harvesting of reproductive individuals early, as the window for effective intervention appears limited to the initial phase of interaction before cumulative mortality reaches critical thresholds.
