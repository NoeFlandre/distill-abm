The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It integrates environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions and human hunting pressure influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands maintains a constant value of 20 throughout the simulation sequence. Hunters are immortal (preventing mortality from energy depletion), do not reproduce, and therefore maintain the initial population size of 20 bands indefinitely without response to environmental conditions or prey availability. The inter-run variability in extinction timing reflects stochastic fluctuations in herd movement patterns, hunter-prey encounter probabilities, and the spatial distribution of remaining prey. Reliance on average projections carries substantial risk, requiring conservative buffers when setting hunting quotas or protected area designations. The consistent plateau across runs suggests that hunting pressure specifically targeting reproductive females with dependent offspring can rapidly accumulate significant mortality within a few hundred time steps. Trajectories accumulate births rapidly, reaching ten births by step 1600, while others exhibit minimal accumulation, recording only one or two births. Population collapse is highly probable under the specified parameter configuration. The combination of 20 initial foragers with the specified food values generates hunting pressure sufficient to drive the prey population to extinction with high consistency. This warns against using short-term population persistence as an indicator of system sustainability. Population decline is monotonic but characterized by substantial variation in the rate of decrease across different runs. The most rapid declines reduce populations from 250 to fewer than 10 individuals within 400 to 500 steps, while the most gradual decline extend the persistence of small populations (1–50 individuals) for 2000 or more steps. The current parameter configuration produces deterministic male extinction within approximately 2-3 years (given 365 ticks per year), with the most rapid collapses occurring within the first year. This suggests that hunting intensity may be too high to observe nuanced interactions between environmental change and hunting pressure, or that the sexual dimorphism parameters create excessive targeting. The system enters a phase characterized by subsequent disturbances of decreasing magnitude. Around step 700, coverage declines to approximately 29% before recovering to baseline levels by step 800. A smaller fluctuation occurs near step 1050, with values briefly dipping to approximately 32.3%.
