The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The number of active hunter agents remains fixed at 20 individuals throughout the entire simulation, from the initial step through all recorded time steps. The static population level directly corresponds to the parameter configuration establishing an initial forager count of 20 combined with immortality. The total energy available to forager bands begins at 2000 units and undergoes a continuous decline throughout the simulation. The trajectory reveals distinct phases of accumulation that reflect the interaction between foraging efficiency and prey availability in the grassland environment. The model demonstrates that a cohort of 20 immortal foragers can reliably eliminate approximately 120 male ungulates (representing near-total depletion of the initial male population from the starting herd of 250) within the equivalent of roughly 3-6 years. The cumulative number of births across the prey population begins at zero and remains at zero for approximately the first 600 to 700 time steps. The population enters a sustained growth phase lasting from approximately step 900 through step 1700, during which the mean cumulative births increase monotonically with statistical significance. The data indicate that postpartum females face acute vulnerability to hunting pressure immediately upon forager introduction, with cumulative mortality reaching near maximum levels within the first 850 steps. The cumulative mortality of megaherbivore herds begins at zero and undergoes a rapid, sustained increase throughout the initial phase of the simulation. Within the first 125 to 250 time steps, the death count escalates sharply from zero to approximately 50 to 100 individuals. By step 500, the majority of simulation runs have effectively reached zero or maintain only residual populations of fewer than 5 individuals. A precipitous decline dominates the first phase, spanning approximately 475 to 600 steps, during which populations plummet from the initial density to values averaging below 20 individuals. By step 2310, all populations have reached zero, indicating complete extinction across all forty simulation runs. Intervention strategies must either substantially reduce forager numbers (currently 20) or significantly enhance forage numbers. The total energy of male animals begins at 12,500 units, representing the initial aggregated biomass of the male ungulate population. This metric undergoes a precipitous decline throughout the simulation, ultimately reaching zero by step 2,284. Conservation strategies must prioritize preventing the initial rapid decline phase rather than managing small remnant populations. The total energy of all animals begins at approximately 16,667 units, representing the initial aggregated biomass available across the prey population. Over the first 350 steps, energy undergoes a precipitous decline, dropping sharply as hunting pressure from the initial cohort of 20 foragers rapidly depletes accessible herds. After step 875, the system enters a terminal depletion phase characterized by a monotonic decline toward extinction. Conservation strategies must account for high variability in persistence times. The percentage of vegetated patches initiates at approximately 33.0%, consistent with the initial grass proportion parameterization. At step 365, the metric undergoes a severe collapse to approximately 24.9%—the lowest recorded value representing an 8-percentage-point loss in available fodder. This dramatic contraction indicates a seasonal bottleneck where grass availability is critically constrained.
