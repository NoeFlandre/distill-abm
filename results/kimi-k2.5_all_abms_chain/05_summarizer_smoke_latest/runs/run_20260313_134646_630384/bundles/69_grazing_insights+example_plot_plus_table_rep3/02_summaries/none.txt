This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. These operate through a yearly sequence where green biomass updates, households with E-RO-SL1 types update their r-parameters, households update behavioral strategies (if switching is enabled), make livestock placement decisions, livestock feeds on biomass, households observe neighbor performance, reserve biomass updates, and households observe and remember reserve biomass states.

The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. It seeks to evaluate how learning capabilities affect household resilience and pasture conditions in private property grazing systems. Specific objectives include analyzing the impact of social learning double-loop processes through strategy switching in heterogeneous populations, observing the diffusion and competition between different learning behaviors in mixed populations, and assessing how demographic settings and initial herd sizes influence system outcomes. The model allows for the evaluation of outcomes across social, ecological, and economic dimensions including average herd size, pasture biomass condition, and surviving number of households.

Following the peak at step 33, the metric enters a sustained correction phase through step 65, decreasing significantly at rates between -0.004 and -0.010 per step to approximately 0.19. This downward adjustment represents a self-organizing stabilization process wherein households moderate their initial exploratory behavior as they accumulate experiential knowledge of private pasture conditions and livestock outcomes, with the oscillation dynamics suggesting underlying cyclical reassessments tied to vegetation cycles or annual decision-making periodicities.

After step 65, the system converges to a relatively stable equilibrium, maintaining a terminal value of 0.19 through step 100 with no statistically significant directional trend, despite continued micro-adjustments indicated by multiple inflection points between steps 67 and 90. The persistence of this moderate deviation, combined with the initial volatility and subsequent stabilization, demonstrates that mixed populations of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types naturally explore risk space before settling into persistent heterogeneous risk attitudes that balance exploration against sustainable grazing pressures.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that while the introduction of adaptive learning capabilities in private property grazing systems may temporarily increase deviation from recommended livestock levels during the initial 20-35 step exploration phase, the system possesses robust self-correcting properties that drive convergence toward moderate, stable risk parameters within approximately 65 steps. The persistent terminal deviation of 0.19 suggests that strict adherence to centralized stocking rate recommendations may be neither achievable nor optimal under heterogeneous adaptive management regimes, implying that policy interventions should focus on constraining deviations within acceptable bounds—likely between 0.1 and 0.4 units—rather than enforcing rigid compliance, thereby accommodating the necessary exploration space for effective social learning and learning-by-doing processes to enhance long-term resilience.

The average household livestock holdings begin at 90 animals. Over the first 37 time steps, the system experiences a severe decline, with average livestock falling to approximately 41. This represents a reduction of over 54% from initial levels, indicating that the initial stocking density substantially exceeds the sustainable carrying capacity of the private pasture system under the given climatic variability.

Following the minimum at step 37, the system enters a prolonged recovery phase characterized by significant divergence across the population. By step 100, average livestock levels stabilize across a wide range of outcomes, with some households or behavioral groups maintaining approximately 40–45 animals, others stabilizing near 48–50, and the most successful groups achieving levels of 60–62 animals. The aggregate mean across all simulations stabilizes near 47.5 animals by the end of the period, approximately 47% below initial levels but 16% above the minimum point.

The trajectory reveals three distinct phases: an initial rapid destocking period (steps 0–20), a continued decline to the system nadir (steps 20–37), and a heterogeneous recovery period (steps 37–100). The increasing variance after step 40 suggests that behavioral differences—specifically the learning mechanisms embedded in the E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 agent types—generate divergent long-term outcomes despite identical initial conditions and shared environmental stochasticity.

For decision makers, these results indicate that initial herd sizes of 90 animals per household are unsustainable and inevitably lead to severe destocking crises. The wide variation in recovery outcomes suggests that supporting adaptive learning mechanisms—whether through individual learning-by-doing or social learning processes—may be critical for determining which households achieve viable long-term livelihoods versus those that remain at subsistence levels. The system demonstrates path dependency, where early losses and behavioral responses during the crisis period (steps 20–40) strongly constrain long-term productive capacity.

The distribution of healthy livestock across households begins in a state of perfect equality, with the Gini coefficient initially at approximately zero. Over the 100-year simulation period, inequality exhibits a persistent and statistically significant increasing trend, reaching a mean final value of 0.0726. This trajectory represents a transition from complete equality to moderate inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock.

The increase follows a non-linear pattern characterized by distinct phases. During the initial approximately 50 time steps, inequality accumulates at a moderate pace. Subsequently, between time steps 51 and 71, the rate of increase accelerates markedly. After time step 71, the growth rate moderates slightly but continues upward through the remainder of the simulation. Throughout all observed intervals, the trend remains strictly monotonic with no periods of decline or stabilization.

Individual simulation runs exhibit substantial variation in the magnitude of inequality by the end of the simulation, with final Gini coefficients ranging from approximately 0.03 to 0.095 across the 30 replicates. Despite this variation, all trajectories maintain the consistent upward trend, indicating that increasing inequality is a robust feature of the system dynamics.

The progressive divergence in livestock holdings reflects the interplay between heterogeneous decision-making strategies—specifically the coexistence of baseline (E-RO), learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1) behavioral types—and stochastic environmental conditions. The stationary nature of households on private property patches allows initial advantages, whether derived from favorable pasture conditions, superior learning outcomes, or stochastic rainfall patterns, to compound over time. The presence of learning mechanisms does not prevent inequality accumulation; rather, the differential effectiveness of learning across households appears to contribute to the widening gap between high and low performers. The acceleration of inequality after time step 50 suggests that learning effects and environmental feedbacks become more pronounced as households accumulate experience and as pasture conditions diverge across the landscape.

**Implications for decision makers:** The inevitable rise in inequality indicates that market or natural dynamics in private property grazing systems will lead to wealth concentration without intervention. The acceleration phase around time step 50-70 suggests a critical window for policy intervention before disparities become structurally entrenched. The variation across simulation runs implies that local contextual factors significantly influence inequality levels, requiring tailored rather than uniform interventions. Strengthening social learning mechanisms or facilitating knowledge transfer between households may help moderate inequality growth, though the current model suggests learning alone is insufficient to prevent concentration.

The adoption of social learning capabilities among households commences at zero and undergoes a rapid, stair-step diffusion process throughout the simulation period. During the initial phase, extending roughly from step 0 to step 10, the count of households engaging in social learning increases sharply with a significant positive trend (slope approximately 1.43), indicative of swift early adoption as initial observers access neighbor performance data and update uncertainty parameters. This rapid ascent continues, albeit at a decelerating rate, through step 28 to step 40, where trend slopes diminish to approximately 0.18 and lower inflection points signal transitions toward saturation dynamics.

A critical change point occurs near step 40, marking a shift from active diffusion to a stabilization phase. By step 63, the aggregate mean adoption level reaches its maximum of approximately 32 households (from a total population of 60), plateauing at this value through the remainder of the 100-step horizon. However, substantial heterogeneity characterizes individual simulation trajectories, with final adoption counts ranging widely from roughly 18 to 54 households across different runs. This variance persists despite uniformly significant increasing trends throughout all observed windows, suggesting that stochastic network effects, initial herd size distributions, or climatic variability create divergent diffusion pathways even as the overarching adoption pattern remains consistent.

The step-like progression observed in the series reflects discrete adoption events, consistent with households switching to social learning behaviors upon satisfying observational criteria defined by the knowledge radius and neighbor performance thresholds. Once established, these learning behaviors persist, resulting in non-decreasing accumulation curves that stabilize once the reachable population segment saturates.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The concentration of adoption growth within the first 40 steps indicates a narrow policy window for accelerating social learning diffusion; interventions such as targeted information campaigns or network facilitation should prioritize early-stage engagement before the system locks into saturation equilibrium. The considerable variance in final adoption rates—where optimal runs achieve nearly complete penetration (54 of 60 households) while others stall near 30% adoption—implies that uniform policy prescriptions may fail; decision-makers should assess local demographic and network conditions to identify contexts vulnerable to low diffusion. Furthermore, since the mean saturation level captures only about half the population, complementary mechanisms beyond peer-to-peer social learning may be required to engage the remaining households, particularly in scenarios where initial adopter concentrations are suboptimal.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The consistent early rapid destocking during the initial 25 steps suggests that households initially adjust aggressively to pasture conditions or learning signals, potentially depleting herds to sustainable levels quickly. This critical window requires close monitoring as it determines baseline stocking rates for subsequent periods.

The substantial divergence in cumulative destocking after step 25 indicates that learning behavior types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) and risk attitudes lead to markedly different long-term livestock management strategies. Some households maintain conservative destocking regimes while others accumulate minimal destocking obligations, suggesting that learning mechanisms significantly affect resilience outcomes. Extension programs should prioritize supporting social learning and learning-by-doing processes that appear to drive this divergence in sustainable stocking rates.

The stabilization of destocking rates after step 60 suggests that households reach steady-state management patterns where learning and adaptation have largely converged to stable strategies. Interventions targeting behavioral change may be most effective if implemented before this convergence occurs, as patterns become increasingly rigid thereafter.

Policymakers should consider that while some households may survive without learning, promoting adaptive capacity could prevent the early attrition of approximately 5-6 households per 20 in similar pastoral communities.
