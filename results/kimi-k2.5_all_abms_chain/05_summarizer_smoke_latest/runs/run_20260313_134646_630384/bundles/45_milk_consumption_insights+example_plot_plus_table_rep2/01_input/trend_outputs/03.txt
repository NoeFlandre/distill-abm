The cumulative count of incumbent whole milk selections begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the thirty-three step simulation period, terminating at a mean value of 2,194.6 across the thirty simulation series. This accumulation follows a near-linear trajectory, with the rate of increase averaging approximately sixty-five to seventy-six choices per step, though exhibiting modest deceleration in later periods as indicated by progressively declining slope estimates from 76.2 in early observation windows to 64.1 in final windows.

Initially, during the first seven steps, the series demonstrate minimal dispersion, converging tightly around the mean accumulation path of roughly 1,159 selections at the median step. Subsequently, variance increases substantially, with individual runs diverging to form distinct trajectories separated by several hundred choices by the series conclusion. While the central tendency approaches 2,195 total selections, terminal values range between approximately 1,990 and 2,350 across the thirty runs, reflecting the compounding effects of stochastic social influence and network interactions within the Watts-Strogatz topology over the simulated 1974-2005 timeframe.

Statistical validation confirms significant monotonic increases across all six rolling observation windows, each maintaining a Kendall tau of 1.000 with p-values of 0.027. Inflection points in the aggregate trajectory occur at steps 1, 7, and 31, corresponding to transitions in the rate of social diffusion and habit formation among the 1,000-agent population. The change point at step 32 marks the final acceleration divergence before stabilization.

The persistent linear accumulation indicates that whole milk retains substantial market share throughout the entire transition period, with agents continuing to select the incumbent variety at a relatively constant baseline rate despite the availability of skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives. For public health decision makers, this suggests that campaigns aiming to reduce saturated fat consumption through milk substitution should anticipate prolonged resistance and plan for multi-decade intervention horizons rather than rapid market shifts. The widening variance between simulations highlights the unpredictable nature of social contagion effects in consumption choices, indicating that agricultural policy makers should develop robust, adaptive strategies capable of responding to divergent market evolution scenarios rather than relying on single-point consumption forecasts.
