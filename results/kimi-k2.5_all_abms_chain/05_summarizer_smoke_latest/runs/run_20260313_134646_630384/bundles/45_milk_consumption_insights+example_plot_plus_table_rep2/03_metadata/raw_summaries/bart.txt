 This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005 . It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . The primary objective is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence interact within individual decision-making processes regarding milk choice .  The three-phase pattern suggests that public health campaigns promoting skimmed and semi-skimmed milk achieve maximum penetration efficiency during initial implementation stages when social networks amplify perception changes . The widening variance between simulations highlights the unpredictable nature of social contagion effects in consumption choices .  The authors say that dietary transitions in food systems follow predictable S-curve trajectories when social network structures facilitate peer influence . The authors suggest that early intervention strategies must account for strong incumbent habits requiring sustained exposure to alternative product attributes before network effects activate . For decision makers, this indicates that early market penetration of alternative milk varieties can occur rapidly .  The mean habit factor for whole milk begins at a neutral value of 1,000 and exhibits a consistent, monotonic increase throughout the 32-step simulation period . Public health strategies should prioritize early disruption of initial consumption patterns before the habit factor exceeds 1.02, after which the rate of habit consolidation becomes deeply embedded in the agent decision-making architecture .  The trajectory reveals an S-curve pattern of behavioral habit formation, characterized by an initial lag phase with minimal change, followed by acceleration beginning at step 5, and concluding with sustained reinforcement . The predictable trajectory allows for forecasting future consumption patterns based on early adoption rates . Implications for decision makers: The pronounced early divergence suggests that the period immediately following market introduction of alternative milk varieties represents a critical window where consumer preferences are most malleable .  Public health campaigns promoting skimmed milk between 1974 and 2005 created growing cognitive dissonance among consumers, with the gap between stated health values and actual choices widening most dramatically in the final years of the transition . For decision makers, the steep early escalation demonstrates that windows for influencing consumption patterns close rapidly as social norms and habits crystallize within the first 1015 time steps .
