The cumulative mortality of postpartum females initiates at zero and undergoes rapid acceleration during the first approximately 300 time steps, with inflection points at steps 245, 262, and 285 marking phases of increasing hunting efficiency. This early period delivers the steepest increases in cumulative kills, with values rising sharply from single digits to exceed 80 individuals by step 500. The rate of accumulation decelerates significantly around change points at steps 475 and 625, transitioning from exponential-like growth to a more gradual linear increase as the exploitable population of nursing females diminishes.

By step 850, the series approaches an asymptotic ceiling, with individual simulation runs plateauing between 100 and 125 total deaths. The aggregate mean stabilizes near 117 individuals, though the median of 116.86 and mean of 102.44 indicate distributional skew, as stochastic variation in hunting success and spatial encounter rates causes some trajectories to saturate at lower levels near 100 while others peak near 119. After approximately step 1000, most trajectories exhibit minimal net change, maintaining stable plateaus with only minor oscillations (period ~63 steps) reflecting demographic stochasticity. Rolling trend analysis confirms this stabilization, with no significant trend detected between steps 1392 and 1623, followed by slight decreases in the aggregate mean during final windows due to asynchronous termination of runs that had reached higher cumulative counts.

This sigmoidal pattern reflects the optimal foraging dynamics of hunter agents, who target postpartum females intensively early in the simulation due to their favorable energy return ratio (food value 27 relative to processing cost 3). The rapid saturation of cumulative mortality within roughly 1000 steps—representing less than three years given the 365-tick annual calibration—indicates that postpartum females function as a non-renewable resource on this timescale, as the 730-tick interbirth interval prevents sufficient replacement to offset hunting losses. For resource managers and conservation planners, this implies that protective measures for nursing females must be implemented within the first 500 steps (approximately 1.4 years) to be effective; delayed intervention fails because the demographic damage occurs too rapidly for natural reproductive cycles to compensate, leading to persistent deficits in the breeding population that compromise long-term herd viability.
