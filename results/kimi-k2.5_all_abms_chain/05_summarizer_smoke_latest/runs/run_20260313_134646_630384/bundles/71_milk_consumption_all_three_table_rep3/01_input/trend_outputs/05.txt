The total volume of milk selections accumulates in a perfectly linear progression throughout the 1974-2005 observation period. Beginning at zero decisions at the outset of 1974, the aggregate count increases by precisely 1,000 choices at each successive annual interval, reaching approximately 16,000 total selections by the midpoint around 1989-1990 and culminating at 32,000 cumulative choices by 2005. This consistent accumulation rate, maintained without deviation across all observation points, indicates that the consumer population remains fully engaged in active decision-making throughout the entire duration of the market transition.

This pattern reflects continuous participation by the complete cohort of adult consumers, with each individual contributing approximately one milk selection per time period on average. The stability of this linear growth persists despite substantial shifts in the underlying market composition, including the erosion of whole milk's dominance and the rising adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Notably, the accumulation rate shows no inflection even during periods when threshold-based habit-breaking mechanisms and social influence pressures likely accelerated the transition between milk types, suggesting that agents maintained consistent purchase frequencies regardless of which specific variety they selected or their initial perceptions of health and environmental attributes.

For decision makers, this trend indicates that consumer attention to milk categories remained stable and predictable throughout the historical transition period. Public health initiatives and marketing strategies targeting behavioral change could rely on sustained consumer engagement without needing to account for market dropout or declining participation rates that might otherwise complicate intervention timing. The consistent volume of decision-making activity suggests that the marketplace offered sufficient variety and accessibility to maintain consumer involvement even as preferences shifted from incumbent whole milk options to alternative varieties.
