The average weekly whole milk consumption per agent begins at approximately 2,655 units. It declines rapidly over the first nine time steps, with the rate of descent steepening around step 5 as indicated by the increasing negative slope in the rolling trend analysis. This initial rapid reduction corresponds to the period when agents, initially configured with high incumbent habit strength reflecting 1974 market conditions, encounter sufficient social influence and health information triggers to overcome initial behavioral inertia and consider skimmed or semi-skimmed alternatives.

The decline moderates considerably between steps 10 and 19, decreasing from roughly 1,000 units to approximately 760 units. Inflection points at steps 13, 16, and 19 mark transitions in this decelerated phase, where temporary plateaus and minor oscillations occur, including a brief increase at step 19 to approximately 774 units. These patterns reflect the persistence of habits among the majority population and the operation of cognitive dissonance thresholds that slow the transition as agents with moderate social susceptibility resist change until surrounding social norms sufficiently shift.

From step 20 to the conclusion at step 32, consumption continues a gradual descent with intermittent oscillations, exhibiting small peaks at steps 27 and 30 reaching approximately 621 and 335 units respectively, and valleys at steps 26 and 29, ultimately reaching approximately 253 units. These late-stage fluctuations represent the behavior of resistant agents within the Watts-Strogatz network topology, where local clustering and varying interaction probabilities create temporary reversions to incumbent consumption among late adopters before the final stabilization at low levels.

The trajectory demonstrates minimal variance across the 30 simulation runs, indicating that the integrated effects of the disposition mechanisms, habit formation parameters, and global social norms generate consistent macro-level outcomes despite individual stochasticity in agent interactions.

Implications for decision makers suggest that public health interventions targeting initial health perceptions generate immediate behavioral shifts during the early rapid decline phase, while the persistent residual consumption of approximately 253 units indicates that a resistant subpopulation requires differentiated, network-specific strategies rather than broad messaging to achieve further reduction. The oscillatory pattern in later steps further implies that maintaining low consumption levels requires continuous reinforcement of social norms to prevent behavioral relapses among marginally committed agents.
