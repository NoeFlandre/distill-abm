Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5.3 units at the initial time step, reflecting the 1974 baseline where whole milk dominated the market. From this starting point, consumption undergoes a rapid, statistically significant increase through the first eleven steps, surging to roughly 1,265 units by step 11. Semi-skimmed milk initiates at zero and follows a continuous monotonic increase throughout the 32-step simulation period. The growth stabilizes into a consistent linear pattern with rates exceeding 935 selections per step across multiple consecutive windows. This sustained momentum indicates that once social norms shift and habit barriers are breached, the diffusion process becomes self-reinforcing. The final phase from step 20 to step 32 shows a gradual but steady decline from approximately 750 to 253 units, with occasional minor peaks at step 30 reaching around 335 units. The overall trajectory demonstrates the progressive abandonment of whole milk consumption as agents adopt alternatives, driven by shifting health perceptions and evolving social norms. Habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at a neutral baseline value of 1.0000 at step 0, reflecting the initial market dominance of whole milk among agents in 1974. By step 32, the metric reaches 1.5879, marking an inflection point where habit accumulation accelerates, and again at step 15. By step 32, the metric stabilizes near 0.7508, representing a substantial and persistent differentiation in agent decision-making functions. Despite the stochastic nature of agent interactions and network positioning, the series demonstrates minimal variance across all thirty simulation runs at each time step.
