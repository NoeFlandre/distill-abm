This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence. Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5.3 units at the initial time step, reflecting the 1974 baseline where whole milk dominated the market. Consumption undergoes a rapid, statistically significant increase through the first eleven steps, surging to roughly 1,265 units by step 11. Following this rapid ascent, the system enters a prolonged plateau phase spanning approximately steps 12 through 26, during which consumption oscillates around a median value of 1,220 units. The study examined milk consumption in the British population from 1974 to 2005. It found that the transition toward low-fat alternatives follows a unidirectional adoption curve without behavioral regression. Each of the 1,000 agents makes exactly one choice per step throughout the entire timeframe, representing consistent annual reconsideration of milk consumption. The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed per agent begins at 5.29 units at step 0 and undergoes a statistically significant increase over the first 11 steps. The steep initial decline suggests that public health interventions targeting early adopters can rapidly shift consumption patterns. Decision makers should prioritize intensive early campaigns to capitalize on the high susceptibility period, then transition to targeted approaches for resistant subpopulations during later phases. The habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at a neutral baseline value of 1.0000 at step 0, reflecting the initial market dominance of whole milk among agents in 1974. The rate of growth intensifies around step 12, marking an inflection point where habit accumulation accelerates, and again at step 15, which constitutes a change point in the series dynamics. The growth rate decelerates considerably while remaining significantly positive, carrying the series toward a plateau. The metric reaches its maximum value of 13-point-four-eight at step 13. The series demonstrates minimal variance across all thirty simulation runs. Policy measures targeting habit disruption or preference alignment implemented during this formative period may prove substantially more effective than later efforts.
