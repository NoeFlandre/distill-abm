During the initial phase spanning approximately steps 0 to 20, all simulation runs exhibit synchronous rapid accumulation of mean household destock totals, rising uniformly from initial values of 0 to between 60 and 80 animals per household. This consistent pattern reflects the universal adjustment from the initial livestock endowment of 90 animals toward levels constrained by available pasture productivity and biomass regeneration rates.

Subsequent to step 20, the trajectories diverge into three distinct accumulation regimes that persist through step 100. A subset of runs stabilizes between steps 25 and 35 at cumulative destock values of approximately 58 to 70, maintaining these levels with minimal additional accumulation thereafter. These cases represent systems achieving sustainable ecological-economic equilibrium where households successfully balance livestock numbers with private pasture carrying capacity.

A second group demonstrates steady linear growth from step 20 through step 100, ending with cumulative destock totals between 80 and 130 animals. This moderate trajectory indicates systems experiencing ongoing but manageable grazing pressure, where households must periodically destock to cope with seasonal biomass fluctuations but maintain viable herd cores.

A third subset exhibits steep continuous growth throughout the simulation period, reaching values between 140 and 173 by step 100—exceeding the initial herd endowment by 55 to 92 percent. These trajectories indicate chronic system stress where households face repeated drought conditions or pasture degradation that prevents herd recovery, forcing serial liquidations.

The divergence establishes rapidly after the initial adjustment phase. By step 30, the spread between minimum stable values and maximum stressed values reaches approximately 50 animals. By step 60, this differential expands to roughly 70-75 animals, and by step 100, the gap between the most stable systems (approximately 58) and most stressed systems (approximately 173) exceeds 110 animals per household. Notably, the lowest trajectories plateau by step 35, suggesting that system resilience is determined relatively early in the simulation sequence.

This pattern reveals that while initial destocking of roughly 65-75 percent of initial stock is inevitable across all behavioral configurations, climate realizations, and learning strategies, long-term stability depends critically on stochastic rainfall sequences and the effectiveness of adaptive decision-making processes. The existence of stable trajectories demonstrates that sustainable private property grazing is achievable with learning-based management, but the prevalence of rising trajectories indicates that without appropriate support, many households face chronic vulnerability requiring repeated asset liquidation that depletes productive capital.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

Stakeholders should anticipate universal herd reduction of approximately two-thirds during the first 20 periods across all household behavioral types. The wide divergence in outcomes emerging after step 20 indicates that early warning systems are critical, as post-adjustment stability cannot be predicted from initial destocking levels alone. Interventions should target the critical transition window between steps 20 and 35, when systems differentiate into stable versus vulnerable trajectories. Support mechanisms should prioritize maintaining minimum viable herd sizes during this period to prevent households from entering the high-destocking trajectory, which exhibits irreversible cumulative losses. Policy designs should account for the fact that social learning and learning-by-doing capabilities produce highly heterogeneous outcomes depending on environmental stochasticity, suggesting that blanket recommendations are less effective than adaptive, responsive support frameworks.
