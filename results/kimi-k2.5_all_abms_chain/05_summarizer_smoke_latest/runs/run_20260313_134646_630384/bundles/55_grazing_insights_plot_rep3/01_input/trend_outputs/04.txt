Across the 100-step simulation period, mean green biomass exhibits a characteristic three-phase trajectory marked by initial decline, steep recovery, and heterogeneous stabilization. At initialization (Step 0), the metric commences at approximately 45,000 units. During the first phase (Steps 0–35), biomass undergoes a pronounced decline, reaching minimum values between 40,000 and 42,000 units around Steps 25–35. This trough represents the lowest availability of forage resources across the simulation period.

The second phase (Steps 35–65) features a steep, sigmoid-shaped recovery where biomass increases rapidly from the minimum values to reach approximately 55,000–62,000 units by Step 65. The rate of increase is particularly pronounced between Steps 45 and 60, with annual increments often exceeding 1,000 units per step.

In the final phase (Steps 65–100), the growth rate decelerates markedly as the system approaches equilibrium. By Step 100, biomass values stabilize within a broad range of approximately 56,000 to 64,000 units across different simulation trajectories. Notable inter-run heterogeneity characterizes the entire sequence: while most trajectories follow the described decline-and-recovery pattern, at least one trajectory exhibits earlier recovery initiation (around Step 20) and maintains consistently higher biomass levels throughout the decline phase, reaching only approximately 44,000 at its minimum. Additionally, the dispersion of final values—spanning roughly 8,000 units between the lowest and highest trajectories at Step 100—indicates substantial path dependency in long-term ecological outcomes.

**Implications for Decision-Makers:**
The initial 35-step decline period represents a critical transient phase where agricultural pressures temporarily exceed sustainable grazing thresholds, suggesting that early-stage biomass reductions should be anticipated as part of system adjustment rather than interpreted as permanent degradation. The subsequent steep recovery demonstrates that adaptive learning mechanisms (learning-by-doing and social learning) can effectively facilitate vegetation rehabilitation within approximately 30 steps, but the wide variation in final equilibrium states (56,000–64,000 units) indicates that stochastic factors and initial conditions significantly influence long-term pasture productivity. Decision-makers should prepare for this variability by implementing flexible stocking rate policies that accommodate the 20% variation in final carrying capacity observed across similar management contexts, and prioritize interventions that accelerate the transition from the decline phase to recovery, as extended periods at minimum biomass levels (below 42,000 units) may threaten household resilience before learning effects manifest.
