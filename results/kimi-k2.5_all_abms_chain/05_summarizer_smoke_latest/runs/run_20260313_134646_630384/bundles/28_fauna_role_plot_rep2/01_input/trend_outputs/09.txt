Across all simulation runs, the female megaherbivore population initiated at approximately 125 individuals, consistent with the initialization of 250 total ungulates and expected sex ratios. Every replicate exhibited a deterministic trajectory toward population collapse, though the temporal dynamics of decline varied substantially among runs. The most rapid collapses occurred within 300 to 400 time steps, where female counts dropped from initial levels to fewer than 10 individuals, effectively reaching demographic extinction. More gradual declines persisted in other replicates, with some populations maintaining 20 to 40 females until approximately 600 to 800 time steps before entering terminal decline. By 1,000 time steps, the majority of populations had been reduced to fewer than 20 females, with several runs exhibiting temporary stabilization at low abundances (between 2 and 15 individuals) for extended periods of 200 to 400 steps before final extinction. Terminal extinction or near-zero abundances (0–2 females) was achieved in all runs by 2,000 time steps, with the final populations persisting until approximately 1,500 to 1,800 steps in the most resilient replicates.

Interpreted within the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model framework, these dynamics indicate that the parameter combination of 20 immortal foragers with food storage capabilities, high environmental seasonality (0.8), and the specified energetic costs and reproductive biology creates unsustainable hunting pressure. The inter-run variation in extinction timing reflects the stochastic nature of agent-based interactions—specifically the probabilistic encounters between foragers with a foraging radius of 6 and female ungulates (food value 27, processing cost 3), compounded by demographic stochasticity in reproduction (birth spacing of 730 ticks) and mortality. The absence of any stable coexistence trajectories suggests that under these specific parameter settings, the mortality inflicted by human hunting exceeds the population's maximum growth capacity, even accounting for vegetation regrowth cycles (3 ticks) and grass availability across 33% of the landscape. The stepwise nature of the decline aligns with discrete mortality events and the discrete-time architecture of the model, where individual agent deaths cause abrupt rather than continuous population changes.
