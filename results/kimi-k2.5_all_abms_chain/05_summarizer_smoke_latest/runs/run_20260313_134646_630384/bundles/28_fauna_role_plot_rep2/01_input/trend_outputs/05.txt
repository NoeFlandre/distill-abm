The cumulative mortality of postpartum female ungulates follows a saturating trajectory across all simulation replicates, characterized by three distinct phases: rapid initial accumulation, transitional deceleration, and asymptotic stabilization.

During the initialization period (step 0), cumulative mortality begins at zero across all runs. Within the first 400 to 600 time steps, the majority of trajectories exhibit steep increases, rising rapidly to cumulative counts between 80 and 115 postpartum females killed. This phase represents the period of maximum hunting pressure on nursing females, where forager agents encounter and process these high-value targets at peak efficiency.

Between approximately steps 500 and 1000, the rate of accumulation declines markedly. The trajectories transition from steep ascents to shallow slopes as the incremental addition of new kills diminishes. By step 1000, most runs have accumulated between 100 and 125 total postpartum female mortalities.

The terminal phase exhibits persistent plateaus extending from roughly step 800 through step 2100, with final cumulative values clustering tightly between 110 and 125 killed individuals. However, substantial stochastic variation exists in the timing of saturation: some replicates plateau earlier, achieving maximum counts near 105 by step 700, while others continue gradual accumulation until steps 1500-1800, ultimately reaching 115-120. One notable outlier exhibits delayed onset dynamics, with negligible mortality until approximately step 300, followed by sustained accumulation and final saturation around step 1700 at a cumulative value near 115.

Given the temporal calibration (365 ticks per year), these dynamics unfold entirely within the first 5.7 years of the simulation. Within the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model framework, the rapid saturation of cumulative mortality indicates that postpartum females constitute a finite resource pool that becomes depleted or inaccessible within this initial temporal window. The initial accumulation rate reflects optimal foraging behavior targeting reproductive females (food-value-females = 27) and their associated calves. The subsequent plateauing suggests either the functional extinction of postpartum females from the landscape, the collapse of the breeding population below sustainable recruitment levels given the 730-tick birth spacing constraint, or the elimination of the initial adult female cohort (approximately 125 individuals assuming equal sex distribution in the initial population of 250). The asymptotic levels (100-125 individuals) represent intense predation pressure specifically directed at the reproductive demographic, with variation across runs reflecting stochasticity in herd movement patterns, forager encounter rates, and individual energy dynamics.
