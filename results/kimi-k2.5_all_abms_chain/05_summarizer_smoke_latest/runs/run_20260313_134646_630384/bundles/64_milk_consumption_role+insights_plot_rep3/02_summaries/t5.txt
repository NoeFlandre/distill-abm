Model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. Individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers. Over the 32-step simulation period corresponding to 1974-2005, the metric follows a decreasing trajectory characterized by three distinct phases of decline. Consumption undergoes a steep reduction, falling to approximately 950-1,100 units. This represents a rapid initial transition away from whole milk as agents begin overcoming high initial habit strengths (10.0) some runs dipping to 1,180 while others maintain 1,240-1,260 levels. This plateau suggests a temporary equilibrium where habit maintenance mechanisms (habit threshold: 2.0) and social blindness effects (0.51) create resistance to further adoption. Cumulative count of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk selections demonstrates a characteristic innovation diffusion pattern over the 32-step simulation period corresponding to 1974 through 2005. The series begins at levels near zero, consistent with initial market conditions where whole milk dominated consumer preferences. Through the first five time steps, accumulation remains minimal, totaling fewer than 1,500 selections across the full population of 1,000 agents. Between steps 10 and 20, the rate of decline moderates substantially to approximately 25 units per step, with consumption falling from 1,000 units to 750 units. This deceleration suggests the emergence of a resistant core of consumers whose habit strength exceeds the threshold for change (habit-threshold: 2.0) the final values represent approximately 95–98% of the maximum observed values across all simulation runs. The between-simulation variation remains relatively constrained throughout all phases, with the coefficient of variation typically below 5%, indicating robustness in the emergent patterns. Growth pattern transitions to a roughly linear increase from 1.37 to approximately 1.58–1.60. This suggests the emergence of a self-sustaining cycle where established habits for alternative milk types perpetuate further consumption. The deviation increases sharply from 0.0 at step 0 to approximately 0.72-0.75. This represents the most aggressive growth phase, with the metric gaining roughly 0.05 per time step. The growing gap reflects emerging cognitive dissonance as consumers recognize alternatives as healthier yet maintain their whole milk consumption patterns due to ingrained habits. Decision makers should consider that current parameter configuration produces consistent, reproducible outcomes across simulation runs. Modifying habit-threshold or incumbent-initial-habit parameters may be necessary to achieve closer value-behavior alignment.
