This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence. The cumulative adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at zero in 1974, reflecting the initial market dominance of whole milk at the simulation's outset. Over the subsequent 31-year period, this metric exhibits a continuous monotonic increase, reaching approximately 25,644 total adoption events by 2005. The trajectory follows a classic diffusion pattern characterized by three distinct phases. The cumulative number of milk consumption decisions recorded across the agent population exhibits a perfectly linear increase from zero choices at the 1974 starting point to 32,000 total choices by 2005. The average weekly consumption of whole milk begins at approximately 2,655 milliliters per person at the outset of the 1974–2005 period. Over the subsequent 32 time steps, consumption declines monotonically though non-linearly. The trajectory exhibits minimal volatility, with values remaining tightly clustered around a mean of 1.0359. The mean habit factor applied to the skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choice function begins at 1.0000 and increases monotonically to 1.5879 by the final observation period. The British milk market transitioned from a state of high consensus, where whole milk dominated due to uniform habitual preferences, to a fragmented market characterized by distinct consumer segments with highly heterogeneous valuation structures. Dairy industry stakeholders should recognize that the early simulation years represent a critical window for establishing product positioning before preferences solidify.
