The simulations consistently initialized with approximately 125 female animals, representing roughly half of the initial ungulate population of 250 individuals. Across all stochastic replicates, the number of females exhibited a monotonic decline throughout the temporal sequence, ultimately approaching zero in most instances.

The most pronounced reduction occurred within the first 300 to 500 time steps, during which populations typically decreased from the initial ~125 individuals to fewer than 20 females. This rapid collapse phase was followed by a prolonged period of low abundance, with many populations persisting at fewer than 10 individuals for several hundred additional steps before final extinction.

Substantial variability characterized the timing of population collapse across simulation replicates. While some populations declined to zero within approximately 300 steps, others maintained small numbers of females—ranging from 1 to 20 individuals—for 1,000 steps or more, with rare instances persisting beyond 2,000 steps. However, by the conclusion of the extended simulation period (approaching 2,200 steps), nearly all replicates had converged on zero or one remaining females.

Interpretation in context: The consistent downward trend indicates that the combination of 20 human foragers operating under optimal foraging strategies, sexual dimorphism favoring only marginally higher hunting returns for males (27 energy units for females versus 30 for males), and environmental seasonality (0.8 intensity) with rapid grass regrowth (3 ticks) created unsustainable mortality pressure. The rapid initial decline suggests that hunting mortality, potentially compounded by starvation and external predation (0.0025 baseline rate), exceeded the reproductive capacity governed by the 730-tick interbirth interval. The prolonged persistence of some small populations reflects demographic stochasticity inherent in agent-based systems, where individual reproductive events and hunting encounters create variability in extinction timing despite identical initial parameters.
