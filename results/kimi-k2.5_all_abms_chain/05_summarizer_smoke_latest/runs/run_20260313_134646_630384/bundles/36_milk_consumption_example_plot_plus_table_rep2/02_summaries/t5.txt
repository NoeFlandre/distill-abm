Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. Average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5.3 units per agent. Over the first ten to eleven time steps, consumption increases rapidly and significantly, reaching approximately 1,265 units by step 11. This initial surge indicates a swift disposition triggering phase where agents begin considering alternatives, driven by perception of health characteristics and early social influence effects. The aggregate series demonstrates low variance across the 30 simulation runs. All realizations following nearly identical trajectories from the initial null state to the final value. The absence of oscillations or declines throughout the series suggests a monotonic, irreversible transition in aggregate consumption behavior toward skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The habit-based disposition factor for whole milk consumption initiates at a baseline value of 1.0000 and undergoes a pronounced initial surge to approximately 1.020 within the first time step. This is followed by a sustained ascending trajectory throughout the temporal domain, culminating at 1.0502 by step 32. The gradual amplification of the habit factor suggests that agents who continue consuming whole milk experience progressively stronger habitual anchoring to this choice over the 1974-2005 timeframe. The trajectory culminates at 0.7508 by step 32 (2005), representing a 75.08 percentage point difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores across the population. This substantial terminal value indicates that by the end of the simulation period, the British milk market exhibits pronounced heterogeneity in underlying consumer preferences. The tension between environmental values and behavioral habits remains constant. While agents continue to hold environmental preferences, their consumption behaviors adjust only partially or stabilize at a consistent level of deviation from those values.
