Across simulation runs, the cumulative mortality of male prey exhibits a characteristic pattern of rapid initial increase followed by deceleration and eventual stabilization. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 200 steps, kill counts rise synchronously from zero to between 40 and 60 individuals across all runs, reflecting consistent initial encounter rates between forager bands and the male ungulate population. 

Subsequently, runs enter a transitional phase lasting from roughly step 200 to step 800, during which accumulation rates diverge considerably. Some runs reach cumulative totals exceeding 100 males killed by step 600, while others achieve only 70 to 80 kills by this time point. This variability suggests stochastic differences in hunter-prey spatial dynamics and encounter probabilities despite identical initial conditions.

Eventually, all trajectories asymptotically approach maximum values ranging between approximately 105 and 125 males killed. Most simulations stabilize within this range between steps 800 and 1200, though several runs exhibit continued gradual accumulation until steps 1400–1700, with at least one run extending the accumulation period beyond step 2000 without reaching a definitive plateau. The termination of male mortality accumulation likely reflects the functional depletion of the initial male cohort, consistent with the starting prey population of 250 individuals and the significant food value differential favoring male harvests.

The data reveal that while the total hunting pressure on males converges to a consistent ceiling reflecting population constraints, the temporal dynamics of accumulation are highly sensitive to stochastic factors. This pattern indicates that under the specified parameters—20 immortal foragers, high male food value relative to processing costs, and seasonal grassland productivity—the male subpopulation faces near-complete depletion regardless of variable encounter rates, though the timing of this depletion remains unpredictable.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The consistency of terminal male mortality values across stochastic runs suggests that total male depletion is structurally determined by initial population parameters rather than by variability in hunting efficiency or movement patterns. However, the substantial variation in timing—ranging from 800 to over 2000 steps to reach comparable mortality levels—indicates that short-term observational data may provide unreliable indicators of long-term extinction risk. For management or experimental design, this implies that interventions based on observed hunting rates during early or middle phases may fail to account for the inevitable depletion trajectory, and buffer strategies should account for the full range of temporal possibilities rather than average-case scenarios.
