 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction . It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges . The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics .  The number of foragers remains constant at the initial value of 20 across all simulation runs throughout the observed period from step 0 through approximately step 2200 . The constancy reflects the model configuration where foragers are designated as immortal and do not reproduce, thereby maintaining the initial population count established by the initialization parameter .  The consistency of terminal male mortality values across stochastic runs suggests that total male depletion is structurally determined by initial population parameters rather than by variability in hunting efficiency or movement patterns . The substantial variation in timing—ranging from 800 to over 2000 steps to reach comparable mortality levels—indicates that short-term observational data may provide unreliable indicators of long-term extinction risk .  The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model suggests that under current hunting pressure and life history constraints, the population experiences severe demographic collapse or is at imminent risk of extinction within the first 5.5 years . The rate of mortality subsequently declines as cumulative death counts approach asymptotic levels between 200 and 250 .  By step 500, approximately 75% of simulation runs have declined to fewer than 10 females . By step 1000, nearly all populations have reached zero or are maintained at 1–2 individuals . The decline exhibits discrete stepwise reductions consistent with discrete hunting events rather than smooth exponential decay .  Some simulations experience steep monotonic decreases reaching near-zero levels by step 500-600 . Others show more gradual declines with temporary plateaus and fluctuations between steps 300-800 before continuing downward . Decision makers should recognize that under these conditions, male prey populations cannot persist, and the system lacks regulatory feedback to prevent extinction .  The percentage of vegetated landscape patches initiates at approximately 33% and exhibits a pattern of periodic collapses and recoveries with progressively decreasing amplitude over the simulation timeframe . Decision makers should recognize that under these conditions, no equilibrium state is achieved; the system lacks the demographic resilience (through reproduction or reduced mortality) to offset hunting losses .
