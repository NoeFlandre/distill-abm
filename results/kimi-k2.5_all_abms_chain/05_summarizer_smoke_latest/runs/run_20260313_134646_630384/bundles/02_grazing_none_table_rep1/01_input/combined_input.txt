This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. These operate through a yearly sequence where green biomass updates, households with E-RO-SL1 types update their r-parameters, households update behavioral strategies (if switching is enabled), make livestock placement decisions, livestock feeds on biomass, households observe neighbor performance, reserve biomass updates, and households observe and remember reserve biomass states.

The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. It seeks to evaluate how learning capabilities affect household resilience and pasture conditions in private property grazing systems. Specific objectives include analyzing the impact of social learning double-loop processes through strategy switching in heterogeneous populations, observing the diffusion and competition between different learning behaviors in mixed populations, and assessing how demographic settings and initial herd sizes influence system outcomes. The model allows for the evaluation of outcomes across social, ecological, and economic dimensions including average herd size, pasture biomass condition, and surviving number of households.

The initial risk attitude of social learning agents remains constant at approximately 0.0074 throughout the entire 100-timestep simulation period. This metric exhibits no temporal variation, maintaining identical values at the initial timestep (0) and final timestep (100), with zero variance across all intermediate timesteps. The complete absence of trend indicates that the initial risk attitude functions as a static attribute assigned to agents at initialization that does not undergo modification during the simulation runtime. This stability reflects the predetermined disposition of social learning agents regarding deviation from recommended livestock numbers, which remains unaltered regardless of ecological dynamics, social interactions, or learning processes occurring within the system.

Over the 100-timestep simulation period, the average deviation from recommended livestock levels among extension agents exhibits a distinct three-phase trajectory: an initial rapid escalation, a subsequent prolonged decline, and final stabilization at a moderate level.

The series begins at near-zero deviation (0.0074 at timestep 0), indicating initial compliance with recommended stocking rates. Following a brief minimum of 0.0074 observed at timestep 3, the metric enters a significant expansionary phase. Between timesteps 0 and 10, deviation increases significantly (slope = 0.018), accelerating through timestep 21. This growth continues through timestep 32, though at a decelerated rate (slope = 0.001), culminating in a maximum deviation of 0.3814 at timestep 33. This peak represents a nearly 50-fold increase from initial conditions, suggesting a period of substantial experimentation or stress-induced departure from recommended grazing pressures. Notable local maxima cluster between timesteps 21 and 37, with sustained high deviation (>0.35) indicating persistent overstocking or understocking relative to ecological carrying capacity before corrective feedbacks activate.

A sharp reversal initiates at timestep 33, with deviation declining significantly through timestep 65 (slopes ranging from -0.010 to -0.004). By timestep 46, values fall to approximately 0.27, and by timestep 64 reach 0.1913. This contraction likely reflects adaptive responses to degraded pasture conditions or accumulated learning outcomes, as households adjust herd sizes back toward recommended levels.

From timestep 66 onward, the series stabilizes, exhibiting no significant trend through the simulation end. Values fluctuate around 0.19, concluding at 0.1911 at timestep 100. This persistent deviation of approximately 19% from recommendations—while substantially lower than the peak—suggests that heterogeneous households settle into a steady-state where optimal risk management or livelihood strategies inherently diverge from theoretical recommendations, possibly due to the r-parameter heterogeneity and learning processes (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1) embedded in the model. The dominant oscillation period of 49 timesteps indicates long-term cyclical adjustments in household stocking strategies relative to ecological feedbacks, consistent with adaptive co-management dynamics in private property grazing systems.

The reserve biomass exhibits a pronounced U-shaped trajectory over the 100-timestep simulation horizon. Initially, the metric begins at approximately 90,000 biomass units and undergoes a steep decline during the first 26 timesteps, reaching a minimum value of roughly 84,114 units at timestep 26. This early depletion phase reflects the initial grazing pressure exerted by the livestock herds across the 60 stationary households managing private pasture patches.

Following this nadir, the system enters a transitional stabilization period extending from approximately timestep 22 through 32, during which the biomass exhibits no statistically significant trend and maintains values in the low 84,000-unit range. At timestep 48, an inflection point marks the beginning of a sustained recovery phase that continues through the simulation's conclusion.

The recovery demonstrates distinct acceleration and deceleration phases. Between timesteps 33 and 54, the biomass increases rapidly, with the steepest growth occurring around timesteps 44-54 at a rate exceeding 1,900 units per timestep. Subsequently, the rate of increase gradually diminishes: from approximately 643 units per timestep during timesteps 55-65, slowing to roughly 93 units during timesteps 66-76, then to 20 units during timesteps 77-87, and finally approaching 5 units per timestep during the closing periods. By timestep 100, the reserve biomass reaches approximately 121,073 units, substantially exceeding initial conditions.

Superimposed on these broad trends are oscillatory dynamics with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 timesteps, with notable fluctuation clusters appearing between timesteps 10-20 and again between timesteps 41-49. These oscillations likely reflect the interaction between seasonal vegetation growth cycles and the adaptive decision-making processes of the heterogeneous household population comprising baseline (E-RO), learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1) behavioral types.

The overall trajectory suggests that despite initial resource depletion under the combined grazing pressure of 20 households per behavioral type, the integration of learning capabilities enables a recovery that ultimately enhances pasture conditions beyond baseline levels. The decelerating growth rate in latter timesteps indicates the system is approaching a new equilibrium characterized by improved reserve biomass stocks under private property management with adaptive co-management behaviors.

The average livestock holdings per household, encompassing both active stock and destocked animals, follow a distinct trajectory of sharp initial decline, trough stabilization, and modest recovery over the 100-timestep simulation. This rapid destocking phase begins moderating around timestep 3, as indicated by an inflection point where the rate of decline slows considerably.

Between timesteps 10 and 20, the downward trend continues but at decelerating slopes (from -0.963 to -0.368), leading to a system minimum of approximately 41 animals per household at timestep 37. From roughly timestep 33 to 43, the series exhibits no significant directional trend, plateauing with minor fluctuations around a median value of 47.3 animals, suggesting a temporary equilibrium where destocking pressures balance against retention decisions.

A fundamental shift occurs around timestep 46, corresponding to change point 55, where the trend reverses to a gradual increase. By timestep 100, the metric reaches approximately 47.5 animals per household, stabilizing at roughly 53% of the initial value.

This pattern suggests that the heterogeneous population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 households initially overstocked relative to private pasture carrying capacity, necessitating heavy destocking, but subsequently entered a recovery phase as learning behaviors and adaptive co-management processes enabled improved matching of livestock numbers to biomass availability under variable climatic conditions.

The long-term trajectory indicates a transition from an initial crisis phase characterized by high resource pressure and forced animal removal, toward a stabilized system where destocking becomes rare.

In the context of the mixed behavioral population—comprising baseline (E-RO), learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1) agents—the initial destocking likely reflects the mismatch between initial herd sizes and pasture carrying capacity under variable climatic conditions.
