This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model. It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes. The aggregate risk attitude of the household population stabilizes immediately at the start of the simulation and persists without adjustment throughout the yearly cycles of vegetation dynamics and decision-making processes. The mean household risk attitude initiates at a value near zero (approximately 0.007) and remains at minimal levels through the first three time steps. During the initial phase from step 0 to step 26, the system exhibits a significant decreasing trend, reaching a minimum value of 84,114 units at step 26. The average number of livestock destocked per household begins at 0.0 at the initial time step, then rises sharply to reach a maximum value of approximately 6.6 by time step 3.6. By step 100, the average livestock count increases significantly, but the recovery trajectories diverge considerably across the 30 replicate simulations. The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock begins at approximately zero, indicating an initially equal distribution of healthy livestock across households. Over the course of 100 steps, this inequality metric exhibits a consistent upward trajectory, with the aggregate mean increasing monotonically from near zero to approximately 0.073 by the final step. The count of households following the E-RO (non-learning) behavioral strategy begins at 20 households at the initiation of the simulation. During the initial phase, specifically through approximately step 8, the count exhibits a brief increasing trend. The count exhibits moderate variability around a mean of approximately 16.37 households, with oscillatory behavior showing a dominant period of approximately 49 time steps.
