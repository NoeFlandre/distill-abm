The mean deviation between consumers' held health values and the health values implied by their milk choices begins at zero and follows a sustained upward trajectory across all 33 time steps, reaching approximately 0.725 by the conclusion of the simulation period. This progression indicates a monotonically increasing misalignment between health-related beliefs and actual consumption behaviors throughout the historical transition from whole milk to alternative varieties.

The dynamics unfold through three distinct phases characterized by varying rates of change. During the initial period spanning approximately steps 0-9, the deviation increases rapidly with steep slopes (0.063 and 0.041 in successive windows), rising from 0 to roughly 0.5. This early acceleration reflects the initial formation of positive health perceptions regarding skimmed and semi-skimmed milk while strong incumbent habits (initial habit strength of 10.0 for whole milk) prevent immediate behavioral adaptation. Consumers recognize health benefits of alternatives but remain locked into traditional consumption patterns, generating immediate cognitive dissonance.

The trajectory then enters a prolonged plateau phase from approximately steps 10-24, where the rate of increase diminishes substantially (slopes of 0.012, 0.003, and 0.004). During this middle period, the deviation stabilizes around 0.55-0.6 as gradual behavioral change occurs. Agents begin overcoming habit thresholds and switching to alternative milk types, partially alleviating the gap between beliefs and behaviors. The inflection points at steps 16 and 19 mark subtle transitions within this plateau, suggesting oscillating adjustments as social influence mechanisms (operating at 30% interaction probability and 20% susceptibility) facilitate incremental adoption.

In the final phase from steps 25-32, the deviation accelerates again with a renewed slope of approximately 0.019, climbing from roughly 0.6 to the terminal value of 0.725. This late-stage divergence coincides with the change point at step 32 and likely reflects the consumption patterns of residual whole milk drinkers who maintain strong habitual attachments despite three decades of accumulated health information and social learning. The oscillation patterns (dominant period of 15 steps) evident throughout the series suggest cyclical psychological adjustments, potentially corresponding to periodic cognitive dissonance threshold breaches (set at 0.15) or justification mechanisms (0.61) as consumers rationalize persistent choice-behavior inconsistencies.

Statistical evidence confirms significant monotonic increases across all observation windows (tau=1.000, p=0.027), indicating that cognitive dissonance between health beliefs and consumption choices intensifies predictably throughout the transition period without reversal.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The persistent growth in health deviation to nearly 0.73 suggests that information campaigns alone are insufficient to align consumer behavior with health perceptions, resulting in decades of suboptimal choices due to habit persistence. The three-phase pattern identifies critical intervention windows: the initial high-growth phase (steps 0-9) represents the optimal period for habit-disruption strategies before misalignment solidifies, while the late-stage acceleration (steps 25-32) indicates a resistant subpopulation requiring targeted interventions. The 15-step oscillation cycles suggest that consumer receptivity to behavioral change fluctuates predictably, offering strategic timing for policy deployment. To reduce the value-action gap, decision makers should synchronize health information dissemination with structural interventions that lower habit thresholds or increase social conformity pressures beyond the current 0.28 weighting, particularly during the plateau phase where deviation temporarily stabilizes.
