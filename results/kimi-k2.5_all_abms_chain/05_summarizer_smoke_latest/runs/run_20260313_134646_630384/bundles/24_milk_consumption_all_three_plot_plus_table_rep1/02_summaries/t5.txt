Model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. Individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers. Over the first ten time steps, consumption declines rapidly and steeply, falling to roughly 1,000 units. This precipitous drop suggests that the combined mechanisms of shifting health and environmental perceptions, coupled with social influence effects, rapidly eroded the incumbent habit strength of whole milk once the transition process initiated. The accumulation pattern reflects the persistence of established consumption habits within the agent population. The strong initial habit strength assigned to whole milk enables the incumbent choice to sustain substantial selection frequency throughout the simulation window. Cumulative count of milk consumption decisions demonstrates a perfectly linear increase throughout the simulation. By time step 16, the system records 16,000 total choices, reaching 32,000 by the final time step at index 32. Absence of curvature or variation in the accumulation rate indicates stable consumer engagement without dropout. A 5% increase in the habit factor over the simulation period suggests that the psychological "stickiness" of whole milk choices intensifies substantially over time, creating mounting inertia against the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives. The heterogeneity in consumer choice functions across the population initiates at 0.0000, reflecting complete uniformity in decision-making frameworks. This metric rises sharply during the first 4–5 time steps, reaching approximately 0.57–0.58, indicating rapid differentiation as agents begin overcoming their initial habit strength of 10.0 and processing divergent information about health and environmental attributes of alternative milk options. Persistent growth in health deviation to nearly 0.73 suggests that information campaigns alone are insufficient to align consumer behavior with health perceptions. Decision makers should synchronize health information dissemination with structural interventions that lower habit thresholds or increase social conformity pressures beyond the current 0.28 weighting.
