The population of households employing the non-learning E-RO strategy begins at 20 and initially increases to a peak of approximately 21 households around year 8. This is followed by a prolonged decline lasting until about year 65, during which the population decreases significantly to roughly 15 households, with strong statistical evidence of negative trends (p < 0.01) and reduction rates exceeding 0.2 households per year in the early phase. From year 65 to year 100, the count stabilizes, ending at approximately 14.7 households.

Individual simulation trajectories exhibit substantial divergence, with outcomes ranging from complete disappearance of non-learning households to proliferation exceeding 40 households, indicating extreme sensitivity of fixed-rule strategies to environmental variability and initial conditions. The aggregate pattern reveals that despite short-term stability or growth, non-learning households face a 26% average reduction over the century.

This high volatility and long-term decline suggest that purely rule-based grazing management without adaptive learning mechanisms poses significant sustainability risks for smallholder households in variable rainfall environments. Decision-makers should recognize that while simple heuristics may suffice temporarily, they are associated with higher long-term attrition and unpredictable outcomes, supporting the value of incorporating learning mechanisms into pastoral risk management strategies.
