The data exhibit a consistent trajectory across simulation replicates, initiating at zero cumulative male mortality and progressing through distinct temporal phases before reaching terminal plateaus. During the initial period spanning approximately the first 600 time steps, the cumulative count increases steeply, with most simulations recording between 80 and 100 males killed by step 700, reflecting high initial hunting efficiency upon a relatively dense prey population. 

Subsequently, between roughly steps 600 and 1200, the rate of accumulation decelerates markedly as the trajectories approach asymptotic limits. Terminal cumulative values cluster between approximately 110 and 125 males killed, with individual simulations achieving these maxima at highly variable times ranging from as early as step 900 to beyond step 2000. The discrete, incremental increases observed throughout the time series indicate individual hunting events, while the eventual flattening of all trajectories signifies the cessation of successful male predation. This pattern indicates that hunting pressure effectively removes nearly the entire available male prey population, consistent with prey depletion dynamics given the initial abundance parameters, as the asymptotic values approach the theoretical maximum based on initial herd counts and sexual dimorphism ratios.
