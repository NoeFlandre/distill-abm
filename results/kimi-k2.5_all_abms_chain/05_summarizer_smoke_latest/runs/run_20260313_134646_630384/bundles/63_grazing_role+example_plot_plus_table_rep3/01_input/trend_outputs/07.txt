The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock holdings initiates at effectively zero, reflecting the uniform initial allocation of 90 animals per household across the 60 farming families. Throughout the 100-year simulation period, inequality exhibits a sustained monotonic increase across all scenarios, with aggregate mean values rising to approximately 0.073 by the final timestep, though individual simulation outcomes diverge to range between roughly 0.030 and 0.095.  

During the initial establishment phase (approximately years 0-25), inequality accumulates at a gradual pace, reaching values of roughly 0.010 to 0.015 by year 20. This early differentiation emerges as households begin applying their distinct learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—to manage private pastures under variable rainfall conditions. The rate of inequality growth undergoes multiple inflections during this period, particularly around years 8, 13, 15, and 20-25, indicating episodic accelerations in the divergence of herd health outcomes as management effectiveness begins to separate households into performance tiers.  

The middle phase (approximately years 25-60) witnesses a moderate acceleration in inequality generation, with the Gini coefficient typically reaching 0.030 to 0.045 by year 60. A notable change point occurs around year 51, marking a transition where vegetation degradation dynamics and differential stocking decisions begin to compound disparities more rapidly. The inflection around year 37 and the structural break at year 57 further segment this phase into periods of varying inequality accumulation rates, reflecting the complex interaction between ecological feedback loops and adaptive learning behaviors.  

In the final phase (years 60-100), particularly following change points at years 71 and 100, the trajectories exhibit substantial divergence across simulation runs. While all scenarios maintain increasing inequality (as confirmed by significant positive Mann-Kendall trends across all rolling windows with tau values of 1.000), the rate of increase varies dramatically. Some trajectories accelerate steeply to reach Gini coefficients exceeding 0.090 by year 100, while others plateau at more modest levels of approximately 0.045 to 0.060. This divergence suggests that stochastic rainfall variability (operating with a standard deviation of 100 units around a 200-unit mean) and the spatial arrangement of learning types generate path-dependent outcomes where initial small differences in pasture condition compound into substantial wealth disparities.  

The absence of detected peaks or valleys throughout the time series indicates that inequality, once established, is not corrected by market forces or ecological feedback within the observed timeframe. By year 100, the system exhibits high variance across runs, with oscillation patterns in the final decades (indices 78-93) overlaying the dominant increasing trend, suggesting that while cyclical environmental fluctuations continue to influence short-term outcomes, the long-term trajectory toward resource concentration among successful learners remains consistent across all scenarios.
