model focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land . each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . the model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making . the mean household risk attitude begins at approximately 0.007, indicating near-neutral risk disposition at the initiation of the simulation . over the first 33 time steps, this metric increases rapidly and significantly, reaching a maximum value of 0.381 . this initial escalation suggests that households initially deviate increasingly from recommended livestock numbers as they commence pasture management . this early collapse reflects the immediate pressure of high initial stocking rates combined with significant rainfall variability . livestock numbers increase significantly with a slope of 0.31, after which the growth rate gradually diminishes across subsequent decades . by the final timestep, the average stabilizes at approximately 47.5 animals per household . the middle phase (approximately years 25-60) witnesses a moderate acceleration in inequality generation, with the Gini coefficient typically reaching 0.030 to 0.045 by year 60 . the inflection around year 37 and the structural break at year 57 further segment this phase into periods of varying inequality accumulation rates . in the final phase (years 60-100), particularly following change points at years 71 and 100, the trajectories exhibit substantial divergence across simulation runs . the first change point at year 25 marks a transition from reactive destocking to strategy-stabilized management . experiential rule-of-thumb households may approach equilibrium stocking levels while learning-by-doing and social learning households continue adaptive adjustments . the system exhibits multiple inflection dynamics around years 45, 47, 53, and 56 .
