 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction . It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges . The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics .  The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands maintains a constant value of 20 across all 2,312 time steps of the simulation . The aggregate energy available to forager bands exhibits a persistent, monotonic decline throughout the simulation period . This trajectory indicates a systematic failure of hunting to offset energetic expenditures across the forager population .  The trajectory demonstrates that with 20 initial foragers operating under optimal foraging constraints and prey initialization of 250 herds, hunting pressure on females follows a classic exploitation curve: rapid initial harvest followed by diminishing returns as the population depletes . This early phase features sharply decelerating growth rates marked by inflection points around ticks 245, 262, and 285, indicating an initial surge in the hunting of lactating females, followed by rapid moderation as the most accessible individuals are depleted .  Reproductive dynamics of the prey population exhibit distinct phased behavior across the simulation span . Birth counts remain at zero during the earliest time steps, reflecting a demographic lag as the founding population of 250 individuals matures toward reproductive capability . During this interval, birth counts rise from near zero values to a maximum of approximately 5.6 births per sampling interval, achieved around indices 1673-1702 .  The male megaherbivore population initiated at 125 individuals and experienced immediate steep decline, falling below 11 individuals by approximately tick 730 and continuing to decrease throughout the first 1,200 ticks . During this interval, population values typically ranged between 2 and 10 individuals, representing more than ninety percent decline from initial abundance . The population ultimately reached zero individuals at tick 2284 .  The prey population exhibits a deterministic trajectory of exponential decline from an initial abundance of approximately 167 individuals to complete extinction by tick 2,311 . This collapse unfolds markedly faster than the species' programmed biological potential, as the parameterized maximum lifespan of 10,220 ticks (approximately 28 years) and senescence threshold of 7,300 ticks (20 years) would theoretically permit multidecadal population persistence .  The initial phase (ticks 0–375) exhibited the steepest decline, with the metric decreasing rapidly from the initial maximum . During this period, a regular oscillatory pattern emerged with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks (roughly bi-monthly cycles) The aggregate prey energy exhibits a deterministic trajectory toward extinction over 2,311 ticks, opening at approximately 16,667 energy units and declining monotonically to complete exhaustion (0 units)  The percentage of vegetated landscape patches exhibited stable long-term dynamics centered around 33%, consistent with the model's high seasonality setting . The dominant periodicity of approximately 63 sample intervals indicates recurring environmental cycles . Subsequent oscillations correspond to hunting-mediated demographic disruptions interacting with seasonal resource cycles .
