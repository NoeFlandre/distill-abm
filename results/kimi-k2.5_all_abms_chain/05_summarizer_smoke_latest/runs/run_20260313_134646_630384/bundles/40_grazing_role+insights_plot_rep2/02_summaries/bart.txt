The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods. The simulation tracks the degree to which households deviate from recommended livestock numbers across 100 annual time steps. The mean reserve biomass across simulation runs exhibits a consistent four-phase trajectory over the 100-year period, though with notable variation in magnitude across replicates. The emergence of persistent overstocking among the majority of households suggests that the implemented learning mechanisms reinforce short-term optimization at the expense of long-term sustainability. The U-shaped or "recovery" pattern reflects the dynamics of a system transitioning from initial overstocking to adaptive equilibrium. Initial biomass decline occurs as 60 households graze their initial livestock allocations of 90 animals each on private pastures. The subsequent recovery (Steps 30–60) indicates successful adaptation through the three learning strategies (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning) The Gini coefficient measuring inequality in livestock distribution begins at approximately zero across all simulation runs, consistent with the uniform initial allocation of 90 animals per household. Over the 100-year simulation period, inequality increases monotonically in all stochastic realizations, though the rate of increase and terminal values diverge substantially. The spread between the lowest and highest cumulative destocking values spans roughly 114 units (58 to 172), indicating vastly different long-term outcomes across the parameters or behavioral strategies tested. The wide outcome variability implies that learning strategy selection and risk disposition calibration are critical determinants of household economic resilience.
