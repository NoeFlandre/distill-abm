The cumulative destocking of livestock initiates at zero and follows a monotonically increasing trajectory throughout the 100-timestep simulation, ultimately reaching approximately 97 livestock units. The temporal dynamics exhibit three distinct phases of accumulation. During the initial phase spanning timesteps 0 to 10, destocking accumulates rapidly at a rate of roughly 5 units per timestep, indicating immediate pressure on household herds as agents adapt to initial pasture conditions and climatic variability. This acute phase transitions into a deceleration period between timesteps 11 and 25, where the accumulation rate declines to approximately 1.5 units per timestep, suggesting an adjustment period as heterogeneous behavioral types—including baseline E-RO, learning-by-doing E-LBD, and socially learning E-RO-SL1 agents—adapt their strategies within the private property constraints. A notable change point occurs at timestep 25, marking the transition to a prolonged phase of gradual accumulation extending through timestep 100. During this final phase, destocking continues at a nearly constant rate of approximately 0.3 units per timestep, indicating persistent, chronic pressure on livestock holdings despite the presence of adaptive learning mechanisms. The trajectory features multiple inflection points clustered between timesteps 45 and 75, reflecting subtle adjustments in destocking rates as agents respond to oscillatory ecological dynamics with a dominant period of approximately 49 timesteps, likely corresponding to yearly vegetation cycles driven by rainfall variability. The distribution of cumulative destocking values across the simulation series yields a mean of approximately 75.5 units and a median of 81.2 units, with the median exceeding the mean, indicating that while most households experience substantial destocking, the distribution is influenced by periods of higher accumulation rates in later timesteps. For decision makers, the continuous accumulation of destocked livestock throughout the simulation—transitioning from acute initial shocks to chronic long-term stress—suggests that adaptive co-management strategies involving social learning and learning-by-doing, while potentially moderating the rate of destocking, do not prevent cumulative livestock loss in private property grazing systems under the tested climatic and demographic conditions. The persistent gradual accumulation after timestep 25 indicates that interventions targeting early-stage crisis management may stabilize immediate losses but require complementary long-term support mechanisms to address ongoing resource constraints and prevent gradual erosion of household capital. The oscillatory patterns suggest opportunities for timed policy interventions aligned with ecological cycles to minimize forced livestock sales during critical vegetation periods.
