The simulation reveals a trajectory of gradually increasing inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock among households, beginning from perfect equality (coefficient ~0.000) at initialization and rising to approximately 0.073 by the 100th year. Throughout the entire period, the metric demonstrates a statistically robust increasing trend, indicating consistent divergence in household outcomes despite identical initial livestock endowments and environmental starting conditions.

The progression unfolds through several distinct phases. During the initial 25 years, inequality accumulates rapidly, marked by inflection points at years 8, 13, 15, 20, and 25 that suggest accelerating differentiation as the three behavioral types—E-RO (baseline), E-LBD (learning-by-doing), and E-RO-SL1 (social learning)—begin experiencing divergent success in managing their private pastures under variable climatic conditions. Between years 25 and 57, the rate of inequality growth moderates somewhat but continues its upward march, with notable shifts in dynamics occurring around years 37 and 57, potentially reflecting the lag time required for learning processes to compound into measurable differences in livestock health.

Two critical change points emerge at years 51 and 71, where the trajectory of divergence adjusts, coinciding with the transition from early to mature phases of adaptive learning. During the final decades (years 71–100), the baseline upward trend persists but becomes superimposed with oscillatory dynamics, particularly between years 78 and 93, indicating that inequality does not stabilize but continues fluctuating as households with different learning capabilities and risk attitudes respond to environmental stochasticity.

Over the full 100-year period, the average inequality level remains relatively modest (mean ~0.030), suggesting that while learning heterogeneity and private property management drive differential outcomes, the system avoids extreme concentration of healthy livestock assets.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The emergence of measurable inequality even from identical starting conditions indicates that differential adaptation capabilities—specifically the comparative advantages of learning-by-doing versus social learning—create persistent disparities in household resilience. Decision makers should anticipate that initial equity in resource distribution will naturally erode over time as households with more effective learning strategies accumulate advantages. The oscillatory patterns observed in later decades suggest that inequality is not a static outcome but a dynamic condition requiring ongoing monitoring, particularly around the 50- and 70-year marks where systemic shifts in household fortunes accelerate. Finally, the low absolute magnitude of inequality (remaining below 0.1) implies that private property frameworks combined with adaptive learning mechanisms provide sufficient buffers to prevent catastrophic divergence in livelihood outcomes, though targeted support for slower-adapting households may be necessary to maintain social cohesion in long-term grazing management.
