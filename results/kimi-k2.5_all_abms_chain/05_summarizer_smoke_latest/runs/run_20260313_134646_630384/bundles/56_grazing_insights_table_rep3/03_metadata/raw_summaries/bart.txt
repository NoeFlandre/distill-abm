 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management .  The average household risk attitude parameter begins at a near-neutral value of approximately 0.007 . Between timesteps 3 and 33, the system undergoes a rapid and substantial increase, reaching a pronounced peak of 0.3814 at timestep 33 . This sharp escalation indicates a significant adjustment in agent risk parameters, likely reflecting intensive learning-by-doing and social learning processes .  Adaptive learning mechanisms in private property grazing systems require substantial time lags before yielding measurable ecological benefits . The eventual accumulation of reserve biomass approximately 34% above initial levels indicates that heterogeneous populations combining individual experiential learning and social observation can significantly enhance long-term pasture resilience compared to baseline conditions, provided managers endure the initial "adaptation debt"  The dynamics of livestock destocking exhibit an initial shock followed by gradual stabilization with late-stage fluctuations . These dynamics reflect the adaptation processes of a heterogeneous population comprising 60 households with equal representation of three behavioral types . The persistence of low-level destocking (0.3 animals/household) at equilibrium suggests that complete elimination of stock losses is unrealistic in high-variability rainfall environments .  The average inequality level over the full 100-year period remains relatively modest (mean ~0.030), suggesting that while learning heterogeneity and private property management drive differential outcomes, the system avoids extreme concentration of healthy livestock assets . The rapid initial uptake of social learning within the first 30 timesteps indicates that early interventions promoting social learning mechanisms can achieve quick penetration in smallholder grazing communities .  The number of households employing the E-RO (non-learning) strategy begins at 20 households . During the initial period through approximately time step 8, the count exhibits a modest increase, reaching a maximum value of approximately 21.1 households . Following this initial peak, a consistent downward trajectory dominates the dynamics .
