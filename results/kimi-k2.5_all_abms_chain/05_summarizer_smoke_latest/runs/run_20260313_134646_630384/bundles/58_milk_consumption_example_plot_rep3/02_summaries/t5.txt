Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. Between steps 25 and 32, consumption increases from approximately 1225 to values ranging between 1400 and 1480 milliliters. This late-stage increase indicates a secondary wave of adoption or intensification of consumption among existing adopters, potentially driven by cumulative social influence. Despite the modeled transition toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties, whole milk consumption persists at consistent levels within the population. The mean whole milk quantity begins at approximately 2,650 units at step 0, representing the initial dominance of whole milk consumption in 1974. It declines sharply over the first 10 steps, dropping to approximately 1,000 units by step 10. This decelerated decline suggests that while the initial shift away from whole milk was rapid, the remaining consumers exhibited stronger habit persistence or lower susceptibility to social influence. Variance across runs suggests that stochastic variations in Watts-Strogatz network realizations and social interaction probabilities generate divergent collective habit formation trajectories. The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk initiates at a value of 1.0 at step 0, remaining relatively stable throughout the first 8 to 10 steps with values hovering near 1.02. Variance across repeated simulation runs remains minimal during the initial ten steps, indicating robust consistency in early-stage cognitive dissonance development. However, variability increases progressively throughout the middle phase and becomes particularly pronounced after step 25. The deviation between agents' environmental values and the implied values by their milk choices initiates at approximately zero.
