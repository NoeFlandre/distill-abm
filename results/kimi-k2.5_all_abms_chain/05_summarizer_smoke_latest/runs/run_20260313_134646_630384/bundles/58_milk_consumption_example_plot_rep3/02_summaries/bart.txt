This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence. The total number of whole milk choices begins at approximately zero at the start of the simulation. Over the 32-step period, this cumulative metric increases monotonically and roughly linearly, reaching values between 2,000 and 2,300 by the final step. The temporal pattern corresponds to the historical period from 1974 to 2005, with each step representing approximately one year of the transition in British milk consumption patterns. The mean weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per person begins at approximately zero at the initial time step. Consumption increases rapidly and nonlinearly, rising from near zero to approximately 1,200 units by step 10. The consumption trajectory enters a plateau phase characterized by relative stability and minor fluctuations. By step 32, the habit factor reaches values between 1.58 and 1.59, representing a nearly 60% increase from the baseline. This progressive escalation indicates continuous reinforcement of consumption behaviors. The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices initiates at approximately zero. The system is approaching a saturation point in the misalignment between internal values and external behaviors. After step 10 and continuing through step 32, the deviation stabilizes at approximately 13.5, maintaining this level with negligible variation. The minimal variance across simulation runs throughout the trajectory indicates that this value-behavior divergence pattern is highly robust under the specified parameter configuration.
