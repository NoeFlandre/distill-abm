Model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. Individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers. Decline proceeds non-linearly across several distinct phases marked by changing velocities and temporary oscillations. This early period marks the initial disruption of automatic behavior. Time steps 12 (1233.62), 20 (1214.46), and 23 (1209.70) oscillations occur with a dominant period of 15 time steps and inflection points at indices 12, 13, 16, 19, and 22. The plateau suggests that early adopters transitioned quickly during the initial phase. A period of rapid acceleration follows (approximately indices 5 through 15), wherein the rate of cumulative increase jumps substantially from roughly 737 to 927 choices per time step. This acceleration coincides with the activation of social influence mechanisms—specifically, interactions occurring with probability 0.3 within a Watts-Strogatz network structure. Peaks or valleys throughout the series confirm that neither alternative milk attributes nor social influence mechanisms generated temporary adoption reversals. The transition proceeded as irreversible, constant-velocity substitution consistent with the model's threshold-based preference updating and memory lifetime constraints (5.0 steps) these dynamics collectively reproduce the historical pattern of British dairy transition. The final phase resumes the downward trajectory, though at a more moderate pace than the initial period, ultimately converging toward the terminal value of 253 units. This late-stage decline likely represents the cumulative effect of sustained social influence and shifting social norms. Despite high initial habit strength for whole milk, the combined influence of health and environmental attribute perceptions successfully motivated a substantial segment of the population to overcome habitual barriers and adopt alternative milk varieties. A subtle oscillatory component with a dominant period of approximately 15 time steps is superimposed on the linear trend, potentially reflecting cyclical consumption patterns or periodic reassessment cycles in agent decision-making. However, these fluctuations remain secondary to the dominant long-term strengthening of whole milk habits. A second inflection indicates further structural reorganization, potentially corresponding to the critical mass formation. Periodic oscillations characterize the middle and late phases, with pronounced fluctuation clusters appearing around steps 6-8, 16-19, and 26-30. The simulation terminates at time step 32 with a value of 0.7508, representing substantial but incomplete polarization.
