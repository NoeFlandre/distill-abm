This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. These operate through a yearly sequence where green biomass updates, households with E-RO-SL1 types update their r-parameters, households update behavioral strategies (if switching is enabled), make livestock placement decisions, livestock feeds on biomass, households observe neighbor performance, reserve biomass updates, and households observe and remember reserve biomass states.

The model aims to compare the long-term social-ecological performance of different behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) under various ecological and climatic conditions. It seeks to evaluate how learning capabilities affect household resilience and pasture conditions in private property grazing systems. Specific objectives include analyzing the impact of social learning double-loop processes through strategy switching in heterogeneous populations, observing the diffusion and competition between different learning behaviors in mixed populations, and assessing how demographic settings and initial herd sizes influence system outcomes. The model allows for the evaluation of outcomes across social, ecological, and economic dimensions including average herd size, pasture biomass condition, and surviving number of households.

The average initial risk attitude of social learning agents remains constant at approximately 0.0074 across all 100 time steps of the simulation. This value shows no variation from the initial timestep through to the end, indicating that the initial disposition of E-RO-SL1 agents to deviate from recommended livestock numbers remains fixed as a baseline reference throughout the simulation period, even as these agents may update their current risk parameters through social learning processes.

The average household risk attitude across the population begins at approximately 0.007, reflecting minimal initial uncertainty management among agents. During the first 33 time steps, this metric increases rapidly, ascending to a maximum value of approximately 0.38. This steep upward trajectory corresponds to the initial learning phase where agents—particularly those employing social learning mechanisms—encounter environmental variability and rapidly adjust their risk parameters upward in response to early experiences with pasture conditions and livestock performance dynamics.

Following this peak at step 33, the average risk attitude enters a prolonged decline, decreasing steadily through approximately step 65 to values around 0.27. This downward trend suggests a calibration process where households moderate their initially high uncertainty levels or risk aversion as they accumulate experiential knowledge and assimilate performance information from social networks. The reduction indicates that initial overestimation of environmental uncertainty gives way to more balanced risk management as the mixed population of behavioral types refines its strategies.

After step 65, the dynamics stabilize, with the metric gradually settling near 0.19 by the end of the simulation. This plateau reflects the system's convergence toward a steady-state level of uncertainty management, where the interplay between learning-by-doing, social learning processes, and ecological feedbacks reaches equilibrium. The final value, substantially elevated above the initial near-zero state but well below the mid-simulation peak, indicates that persistent environmental variability sustains moderate risk awareness among households in this private property grazing system.

The reserve biomass begins at 90,000 units and undergoes a marked decline during the initial quarter of the simulation period, reaching its minimum value of approximately 84,114 units by time step 26. This initial reduction reflects the immediate pressure exerted by the initial livestock herd sizes on pasture resources before substantial adaptation occurs.

Following this decline, the system exhibits a brief stabilization period between time steps 26 and 32, during which values hover near the minimum threshold and no significant directional trend is detected. 

Subsequently, commencing around time step 33, the reserve biomass enters a sustained recovery phase characterized by consistent increase through the remainder of the simulation. This upward trajectory continues through time step 100, where the metric reaches its maximum value of approximately 121,073 units. The rate of increase is notably steeper during the middle portion of this recovery phase (roughly time steps 33 to 54) before gradually decelerating in later periods; the growth rate diminishes from nearly 1,909 units per time step in the mid-range intervals to approximately 5 units per time step by the final periods.

This pattern suggests that while initial grazing pressures temporarily degrade pasture conditions, the combined learning mechanisms—learning-by-doing and social learning among the heterogeneous agent population—enable households to progressively improve their biomass management strategies. The decelerating growth rate toward the end of the simulation indicates the system is approaching a new equilibrium state with substantially higher reserve biomass than the initial conditions, implying that adaptive co-management processes successfully enhance the ecological resilience of the private property grazing system over the century-long timeframe.

The average available green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units, declining to a minimum of roughly 42,057 units by time step 27. This initial reduction corresponds to the immediate grazing pressure exerted by the 60 stationary households managing livestock on their private patches, as the initial herd sizes consume biomass faster than the vegetation dynamics can replenish it in the early simulation years.

Following this trough, biomass enters a sustained recovery phase. Between time steps 33 and 54, the increase is particularly pronounced, suggesting that the heterogeneous decision-making processes—including learning-by-doing and social learning among the mixed population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types—enable households to adapt their grazing strategies to allow pasture recovery. During this period, the system transitions from resource depletion to active regeneration as adaptive co-management practices take effect.

After time step 50, the rate of increase gradually decelerates, though the upward trajectory continues through to the end of the simulation. This slowing growth indicates the system is approaching an equilibrium state where green biomass production increasingly balances with consumption levels. By the conclusion at time step 100, biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units, representing a substantial recovery that exceeds initial conditions by over 34%.

The trajectory suggests that despite initial overgrazing pressures characteristic of smallholder systems with high initial livestock endowments, the combination of private property rights, stationary household management, and learning-based behavioral adaptation fosters long-term ecological improvement. The system demonstrates resilience by not only recovering from the early biomass depression but achieving higher sustainable pasture conditions over the century-long period, with the oscillatory dynamics typical of grazing systems giving way to stable accumulation in the latter half of the simulation.

The average total livestock per household begins at 90 animals, representing the initial stocking level across the heterogeneous population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types. Over the first 20 time steps, the population undergoes a sharp decline, dropping rapidly as households destock in response to pasture constraints and the feedback between biomass availability and livestock carrying capacity. This initial phase reflects the pressure exerted on private pasture patches when stocking rates exceed sustainable levels, forcing households to reduce herd sizes aggressively to avoid complete degradation of their stationary patches.

The decline continues through approximately time step 37, reaching a minimum value of roughly 41 livestock per household. This nadir represents the point of maximum system stress, where the combination of vegetation dynamics and household decision-making under uncertainty has driven herd sizes to their lowest sustainable threshold. The trajectory suggests that without the mobility mechanisms present in common property systems, stationary households face severe constraints that necessitate dramatic herd reductions during the initial adaptation period.

Following this minimum, the system enters a transition phase between steps 37 and 44, where the rate of destocking slows and stabilizes. Subsequently, from step 44 onward through the remainder of the simulation, a gradual recovery phase emerges. The livestock population increases slowly but steadily, climbing from approximately 41 to about 47.5 by the final time step. Notably, the rate of recovery diminishes over time—initially rising more rapidly between steps 44 and 55, then flattening considerably in the later stages with only marginal gains per step. This asymptotic approach suggests that while learning mechanisms (both individual learning-by-doing and social learning) enable households to partially rebuild their herds and improve resource management, the system stabilizes at a new equilibrium substantially below the initial stocking rate.

The oscillatory dynamics with a dominant period of approximately 49 steps indicate cyclical adjustments in the social-ecological system, likely corresponding to vegetation recovery cycles or rainfall variability interacting with household adaptation strategies. The final value of roughly 48 livestock per household, while higher than the critical low point at step 37, remains significantly below the starting population, indicating that sustainable carrying capacity in this private property context with risk-heterogeneous agents settles at approximately half the initial stocking density.

The average level of livestock destocking begins at zero, then surges to a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals per household by year 3. This initial spike suggests that households enter the simulation with initial stocking levels that temporarily exceed the immediate carrying capacity of their private pastures, necessitating rapid removal of excess animals to prevent complete biomass depletion.

Following this initial shock, destocking declines precipitously through the first decade, dropping sharply from the peak to roughly 3.3 by year 8, with secondary fluctuations occurring around years 9 and 12 at values of approximately 3.4 and 2.7 respectively. These early oscillations reflect the adjustment period as the mixed population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types respond to resource scarcity through their respective learning and decision-making frameworks.

From approximately year 20 through year 32, destocking continues a statistically significant decreasing trend, falling gradually from around 1.2 at year 22 to less than 0.7 by year 27. This pattern indicates progressive stabilization as households refine their stocking strategies through learning-by-doing and social learning processes, reducing the frequency of crisis-driven animal removals.

During the middle period spanning years 33 to 65, destocking rates fluctuate at low baseline levels between approximately 0.5 and 0.9 animals per household without exhibiting significant directional trends. This plateau suggests the system has reached a quasi-equilibrium where private property boundaries and adaptive behaviors have stabilized herd sizes near sustainable levels, though minor destocking continues due to stochastic rainfall variability.

A modest increasing trend emerges between years 66 and 76, with destocking rising slightly though remaining below 1.0 animals per household, potentially reflecting delayed demographic pressures or the cumulative effects of climatic stochasticity on household reserves. By the conclusion of the simulation at year 100, destocking stabilizes near 0.3 animals per household, representing a 95% reduction from the initial peak. This convergence toward minimal destocking indicates that the combination of private property tenure and heterogeneous learning behaviors enables households to achieve relatively stable livelihood strategies aligned with long-term pasture productivity, though small residual destocking persists as an adaptive buffer against environmental uncertainty.

The inequality in healthy livestock distribution, as measured by the Gini coefficient, initiates at effectively zero, reflecting the homogeneous starting conditions where all households possess identical initial herd sizes. Over the 100-step simulation period, this metric exhibits a consistent upward trajectory, culminating at approximately 0.073, indicating a measurable but bounded emergence of disparity across the heterogeneous population.

The accumulation of inequality progresses through several phases marked by subtle shifts in the rate of divergence. Initial increases occur gradually, with inflection points appearing at steps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, and 37, suggesting early adjustments as the three behavioral types—E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1—begin differentiating their livestock management outcomes. More pronounced transitions occur around steps 51 and 71, where the pace of inequality accumulation intensifies slightly and maintains a steady upward trajectory through the conclusion of the simulation.

This pattern reflects the differential performance of stationary households operating under private property regimes with distinct adaptive strategies. While all households begin with equivalent livestock endowments, the divergence in outcomes indicates that baseline reactive optimization, learning-by-doing processes, and social learning mechanisms produce heterogeneous success in maintaining livestock health over time. The relatively modest final Gini value of 0.073 implies that despite the presence of distinct behavioral adaptations and heterogeneous uncertainty management, the system preserves a substantially equitable distribution of healthy livestock even after extended operation, suggesting that localized learning and private property boundaries constrain the degree of inequality that emerges between differently performing strategies.

The number of households engaging in social learning processes begins at zero and undergoes rapid expansion during the initial phase of the simulation, rising sharply through approximately the first twenty time steps. This early surge likely reflects the activation of social learning capabilities among the twenty agents initially designated as E-RO-SL1 type, combined with immediate diffusion to proximate households within the constrained knowledge radius of one patch, as stationary households on private property observe and emulate successful neighbor strategies.

Following this initial acceleration, the rate of new adoption decelerates markedly between approximately time steps twenty and forty, with multiple inflection points indicating shifting dynamics in how agents perceive and integrate social information. This transition from rapid to gradual growth suggests the exhaustion of immediate early adopters and increasing resistance among the remaining population, as households following E-RO baseline behaviors or E-LBD learning-by-doing strategies maintain their initial uncertainty management approaches or find existing strategies sufficiently effective given the specific rainfall variability and initial livestock endowments.

The engagement reaches its maximum level around time step sixty-three at approximately thirty-two households, or roughly fifty-three percent of the total population of sixty, before stabilizing for the remainder of the simulation. By the conclusion at time step one hundred, the count settles at approximately thirty-two point one households. This saturation plateau indicates that roughly twelve households beyond the initial twenty E-RO-SL1 agents have adopted social learning behaviors, while the remaining twenty-eight persist with alternative strategies. The stabilization suggests that under the current configuration—with strategy switching disabled and heterogeneous behavioral types distributed equally—the social learning practices have diffused to their maximum extent, constrained by spatial interaction limits and diverse risk attitudes among households managing private pastures.

Mild oscillatory patterns observed during the middle periods, particularly around time steps twenty to thirty-two and fifty to fifty-nine, likely reflect annual fluctuations in the timing of information exchange, potentially correlating with seasonal cycles of biomass observation and livestock placement decisions that influence when households update their r-parameters based on neighbor performance.

The cumulative number of livestock destocked begins at zero and increases rapidly throughout the first 25 time steps, rising steeply as households immediately adjust their herds to initial pasture conditions and uncertainty levels. This sharp initial accumulation, occurring across all three behavioral types—E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1—likely reflects rapid destocking in response to early ecological constraints and initial risk-management strategies as stationary households calibrate their livestock placement on private pastures.

Between approximately time steps 25 and 60, the rate of cumulative destocking decelerates considerably while remaining positive, slowing to roughly a third of its initial velocity. This transition marks a shift from rapid equilibration to a more moderated adjustment phase, suggesting that learning-by-doing and social learning processes are refining household decision-making and reducing the frequency of necessary destocking interventions.

From step 60 through the conclusion at step 100, the cumulative total approaches approximately 97 destocked livestock per household with minimal further acceleration, settling into a nearly linear but gradual increase. By this final phase, the accumulation indicates that households have converged on stable long-term herd sizes relative to their private resource bases, with destocking continuing at maintenance rates under the stationary household configuration with heterogeneous uncertainty management parameters.
