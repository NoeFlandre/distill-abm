 The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system . Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep . The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods .  The model structure does not permit risk attitude evolution through experiential or social learning processes . The mean reserve biomass exhibits a pronounced U-shaped pattern characterized by an initial decline phase followed by a sustained recovery and growth trajectory over the 100-year simulation period .  Initial decline reflects pressure of initial stocking decisions where farmers begin with 90 animals each but have not yet adapted their management to local pasture conditions . By year 100, biomass levels exceeding initial conditions suggest that learning mechanisms facilitate the emergence of sustainable management practices that enhance long-term pasture productivity beyond the starting state .  The recovery progresses through three distinct phases delineated by change points at steps 55 and 100 . The majority of populations stabilize between 40 and 50 animals, yet a distinct subset achieves equilibria of 58 to 62 animals . Extension services and policy mechanisms that facilitate effective experiential learning or social information-sharing may substantially enhance pastoral system resilience .  Policy interventions should prioritize rapid diagnostic capacity and early destocking incentives to prevent vegetation degradation during this critical window . The inevitable emergence of asset inequality in autonomous private grazing systems—even among equally endowed households employing diverse adaptive strategies—indicates that long-term equity requires explicit policy attention .  The average number of livestock destocked across households initiates at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation period . After year 25, the rate of destocking stabilizes considerably, with slopes ranging between 0.31 and 0.36 animals per year for the remainder of the simulation . The rapid early adoption phase indicates that social learning spreads quickly once the simulation begins .  The series exhibits oscillatory characteristics with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 steps . The overall trajectory indicates a net reduction of roughly 25% in the non-learning population over the simulation period . Despite aggregate stability, between-run variability remains pronounced, with terminal values at step 100 spanning approximately 0 to 26 households .
