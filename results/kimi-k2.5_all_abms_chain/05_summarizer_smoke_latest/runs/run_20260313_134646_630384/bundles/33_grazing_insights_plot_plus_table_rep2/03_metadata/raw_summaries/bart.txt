 This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model . It is designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems . The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes .  The aggregate behavior of the 60-household system initiates at a near-neutral risk attitude of approximately 0.007 . Between steps 7 and 33, the mean risk attitude increases significantly, reaching a system-wide maximum value of 0.3814 at step 33 . Individual trajectories diverge rapidly between steps 10 and 30, with distinct subpopulations emerging . The deceleration of growth in later steps indicates the system approaches a new equilibrium, implying that long-term planning should account for eventual stabilization of biomass conditions .  At the onset of the simulation, mean household destocking begins at zero and immediately spikes to a maximum value of approximately 6.6 within the first three steps . Following this peak, the metric undergoes a pronounced and statistically significant decline through step 32, with the strongest negative trend occurring between steps 11 and 21 . The median destocking level of 0.31 across all timesteps indicates that for the majority of the . simulation period, households experience minimal forced livestock removal .  The number of households engaged in social learning initiates at zero and increases monotonically across all simulation runs, with aggregate trajectories exhibiting rapid initial growth followed by stabilization at heterogeneous equilibrium levels . The rate of adoption declines significantly over time, from an initial slope exceeding 1.4 households per step to less than 0.01 in the final phase .  The population of non-learning households (E-RO behavioral type) undergoes distinct evolutionary phases over the 100-step simulation horizon . Early rapid accumulation followed by slower but continued growth implies immediate destocking pressures are intense but extend throughout the simulation period . The wide variation across scenarios suggests behavioral diversity and initial conditions significantly impact destocking outcomes .
