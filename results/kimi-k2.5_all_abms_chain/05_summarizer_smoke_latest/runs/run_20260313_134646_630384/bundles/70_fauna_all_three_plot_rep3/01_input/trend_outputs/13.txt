The combined total energy available in all prey animals begins at approximately 25,000 units across all simulation runs. This initial energy pool undergoes a dramatic and sustained decline throughout the first 400 steps, dropping by roughly 80% to reach values near 5,000. This rapid depletion suggests that the initial encounter rates between human foragers and ungulate herds are extremely high, with hunters efficiently exploiting the naive, high-density prey population before spatial avoidance behaviors or population reductions take effect.

Between steps 400 and 700, the decline continues but at a reduced rate, with energy levels fluctuating between approximately 5,000 and 8,000. This plateau reflects a transitional phase where remaining prey aggregates in harder-to-access areas or where vegetation constraints limit both predator and prey movements, creating temporary refugia. The high seasonality parameter (0.8) likely contributes to these fluctuations, as seasonal pulses in grass availability create brief windows where prey energy recovery temporarily offsets hunting losses.

After step 700, the total energy enters a final decline phase that continues through approximately step 1,200, at which point values approach zero. This terminal collapse indicates that the combination of persistent hunting pressure from 20 immortal foragers with food storage capability, combined with environmental constraints on prey reproduction and energy recovery, drives the system toward prey elimination. The presence of food storage allows hunters to maintain pressure even during periods of low prey availability, preventing population recovery.

From step 1,200 through the simulation end at 2,200 steps, total energy remains near zero with minimal variation across runs. Some trajectories show brief, small resurgences between steps 800 and 1,000, reaching maximum values of approximately 3,500, likely corresponding to seasonal recovery periods or stochastic reproductive events. However, these recoveries are insufficient to prevent ultimate collapse.

The substantial variation across simulation runs after step 300 suggests that stochastic factors—particularly herd movement patterns, vegetation regrowth timing, and the distribution of reproductive females—create divergent pathways to collapse. Some populations persist longer due to favorable spatial configurations that delay hunter encounter rates, but no simulation achieves stable coexistence.

For decision makers, these results indicate that moderate hunter populations with energy storage capabilities and high environmental seasonality create conditions where prey energy collapse is essentially deterministic. Management strategies seeking to prevent megaherbivore population collapse would need to either substantially reduce hunter numbers, eliminate energy storage advantages, or implement spatial refugia that permanently separate hunters from breeding populations. The irreversibility of the decline once initiated suggests that early intervention thresholds are critical, as recovery windows close rapidly after initial energy depletion. The parameter combination tested here—with immortal hunters, food storage enabled, and high seasonality—represents a worst-case scenario for prey persistence.
