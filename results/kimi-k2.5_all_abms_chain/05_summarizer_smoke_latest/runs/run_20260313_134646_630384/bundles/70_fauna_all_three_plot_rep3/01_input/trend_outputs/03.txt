The cumulative number of male ungulates harvested by human foragers follows a saturating trajectory across all simulation runs. From time step 0 to approximately 400–500, the cumulative kill count increases rapidly and approximately linearly, rising from 0 to values ranging between 70 and 110 depending on the specific stochastic realization. This initial phase represents high-efficiency hunting when male prey remain abundant and provide optimal energetic returns relative to their processing costs under the dynamic optimal foraging rules.

Between time steps 500 and 1000, the rate of accumulation decelerates markedly as the totals approach asymptotic limits. During this transition phase, additional kills occur at reduced frequency, with cumulative totals converging toward values between 100 and 125 males killed. After approximately 1000–1200 time steps, the cumulative counts stabilize at plateaus ranging from roughly 105 to 130 individuals, with minimal to no additional accumulation observed through the end of the recorded period at 2000 time steps. Some runs exhibit earlier saturation around step 600–700 at lower asymptotes (approximately 105–115), while others show gradual accumulation extending to step 1500 or beyond, reaching maximum values near 125–130.

This pattern indicates functional depletion of the male prey demographic within the first 3–4 years of the simulation (given the calibration of 365 ticks per year). The saturation suggests either local extirpation of adult males from the herbivore population or a behavioral switch by hunter agents to alternative prey categories (females and calves) as male encounter rates decline below profitability thresholds defined by the model’s sexual dimorphism parameters. The consistent plateau across runs, despite variation in the timing of saturation, implies that once male abundance drops below critical thresholds, hunting pressure shifts permanently, preventing further male harvests.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

These results demonstrate that selective hunting pressure targeting high-value male megafauna can exhaust specific demographic subsets rapidly, creating severe sex-ratio imbalances within ecologically brief timeframes (hundreds of days). Management strategies must account for the asymmetric vulnerability of sexually dimorphic species to hunting pressure, as the higher nutritional value of males (30 versus 27 for females) drives disproportionate extraction that can collapse the male subpopulation before overall population limits are reached. The high variance in depletion timing across runs suggests that environmental stochasticity and spatial factors introduce significant unpredictability in extinction trajectories, requiring conservative buffer zones and continuous demographic monitoring rather than aggregate population metrics alone. Once male depletion occurs, the system stabilizes into a state of extreme demographic skew that may compromise breeding viability and long-term population persistence, even when total population counts remain above critical thresholds.
