Model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. Individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers. Over the first ten steps, the metric undergoes a precipitous decline to roughly 950 units, representing a reduction of nearly 64%. This rapid early decrease suggests that agents rapidly incorporate health and environmental perception updates into their decision-making, triggering widespread abandonment of whole milk once the psychological threshold for habit disruption is crossed. Variance emphasizes the critical importance of initial marketing intensity to reliably trigger the exponential growth phase by step 5. Whole milk maintains a relatively constant selection rate of approximately 65 to 70 choices per time step across the entire simulation period, rather than exhibiting the expected declining trajectory. Mean whole milk consumption begins at approximately 2,650 units at Step 0, representing the 1974 baseline when incumbent habit strength was maximized at 10.0. The quantity declines sharply over the first 10 steps, falling to roughly 950 to 1,100 units by Step 10. This rapid initial decrease suggests that despite strong habitual attachment to whole milk, the combination of social influence mechanisms (with interaction probability 0.3 and susceptibility 0.2) and initial positive perceptions of alternative milk options drove significant consumer switching behavior. The three-phase pattern reveals that market transitions in habitual consumption goods follow a predictable S-curve where early growth is followed by a significant stagnation period before final consolidation. Trajectories demonstrate relatively low variance across simulation runs during the initial and final phases. The habit factor reaches approximately 1.6, representing a 60% increase in habitual tendency toward low-fat milk options compared to the 1974 baseline. A rapid acceleration phase where threshold effects trigger mass habit restructuring, and a mature growth phase characterized by linear consolidation of new behavioral patterns. Value-behavior deviation indicates that health-oriented messaging alone may generate psychological tension without immediate behavioral outcomes. Sharp acceleration suggests that market tipping points create significant psychological resistance among remaining traditional consumers. Deviation undergoes rapid escalation between steps 0 and 5, surging from near zero to approximately 12.5 to 13.0.
