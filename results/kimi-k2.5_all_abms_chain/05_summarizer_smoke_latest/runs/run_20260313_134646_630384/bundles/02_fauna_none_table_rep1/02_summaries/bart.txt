The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments testing hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It simulates a simplified grassland environment represented as an abstract 211×211 patch matrix forming a square universe that wraps at the edges. The model aims to examine how interactions between environmental conditions, human hunting pressure, and animal life history traits influence extinction dynamics. The number of active human hunter-gatherer bands remains constant at 20 throughout the entire simulation duration of 2312 ticks. The aggregate energy available to forager bands exhibits a persistent decline throughout the simulation. This trajectory indicates a sustained energy deficit across the hunter population, with foragers expending substantially more energy in movement and pursuit than they recover through successful predation. Cumulative mortality of female megaherbivores initiates at zero and undergoes a pronounced accumulation phase during the first 475 ticks. During this early period, the cumulative kills rise sharply toward approximately 115, reflecting intensive targeting of reproductive females who offer high net energetic returns due to combined adult and offspring food value relative to processing costs. The cumulative death count among prey populations exhibits a complex, non-monotonic trajectory over the approximately 6.3-year simulation span (7,312 ticks), progressing through distinct phases of rapid accumulation, decelerating growth, plateauing, and eventual decline. This pattern suggests an initial colonization phase where the founding prey population establishes reproductive momentum, overcoming initial demographic instability and hunting pressure. The female prey population initiated at 125 individuals and underwent a protracted decline toward extinction over 2,311 time steps. During the first approximately 475 time steps, the population decreased rapidly from 125 toward intermediate abundance levels. The initial rapid decline corresponds to heavy predation on the abundant male population, while the subsequent moderation and temporary recovery align with dynamic optimal foraging behavior, wherein hunters may reduce pursuit intensity.
