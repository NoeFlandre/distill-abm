The cumulative death count among prey populations exhibits a complex, non-monotonic trajectory over the approximately 6.3-year simulation span (7,312 ticks), progressing through distinct phases of rapid accumulation, decelerating growth, plateauing, and eventual decline. Initially, from tick 0 through approximately tick 850 (year 2.3), mortality accumulates steeply as high encounter rates between the initial forager and prey populations drive intensive predation. This phase is characterized by progressively decelerating slopes; the most rapid increases occur between ticks 0–463, with growth rates diminishing significantly by tick 850 as prey depletion or spatial dispersion reduces hunting efficiency. Regular structural transitions every 125 ticks during this early period suggest discrete shifts in predation dynamics, possibly corresponding to initial prey herd fragmentation or the exhaustion of high-vulnerability individuals.

Between tick 850 and approximately tick 1850 (years 2.3–5.1), the system enters a fluctuating equilibrium state. The accumulation of deaths slows dramatically, with extended intervals—notably tick 1160 to 1391 and tick 1624 to 1855—exhibiting no statistically significant trend, suggesting demographic balancing where prey recruitment offsets mortality, or where optimal foraging constraints limit hunter activity due to low prey density. Brief resurgences in mortality accumulation (e.g., tick 1392–1623) interrupt these plateaus, potentially reflecting seasonal resource pulses or temporary aggregations of prey driven by patchy grass regrowth. Throughout this middle phase, the metric oscillates with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks (roughly 63 days), consistent with the model's high seasonality parameter (0.8) and grass regrowth cycles, indicating that environmental rhythms strongly modulate mortality rates.

The trajectory reaches its maximum value of approximately 240 deaths around tick 1876 (year 5.1). Following this peak, the metric exhibits a statistically significant declining trend through tick 2087, ultimately settling at 235 deaths by the conclusion of the recorded period at tick 2312 (year 6.3). This decrease suggests that the metric tracks standing carcass availability or active mortality stocks rather than a strictly historical cumulative sum, implying that processing costs, forager consumption, or carcass decay remove deceased individuals from the count faster than new deaths accumulate as prey populations become severely depleted or spatially inaccessible. The persistent 63-tick oscillatory pattern continues even during this decline phase (evident between ticks 2170–2265), underscoring the enduring influence of seasonal environmental constraints on mortality dynamics despite reduced overall prey numbers.
