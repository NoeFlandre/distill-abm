This agent-based model is an extension of the RAGE (Resilience of Agent-based Grazing Systems) model, designed to simulate feedbacks between pastures, livestock, and household livelihoods in grazing systems. While the original RAGE model focused on common property grazing with mobile households, this extension applies to smallholder farming contexts where pastures are private property and households remain stationary on their patches. The model introduces learning capabilities to agent decision-making, specifically incorporating learning-by-doing and social learning processes central to adaptive co-management. It implements three new behavioral types: E-RO (baseline behavior), E-RO-SL1 (social learning), and E-LBD (learning-by-doing). Agents are characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management, represented by an r-parameter (household-risk-att) that captures factors such as sensing uncertainty, information noise, and risk attitude. The model comprises two main sub-models: Vegetation dynamics and Household decision-making. During the initial phase, from timestep 0 to roughly timestep 25, the accumulation occurs most rapidly with a steep decelerating slope, indicating frequent or large-scale destocking events as households respond to initial pasture conditions, climatic variability, and the establishment of private property management regimes. Following the first change point around timestep 25, the rate of accumulation decreases substantially to less than one animal per timestep, suggesting an adaptation phase where households—comprising a heterogeneous mix of baseline, learning-by-doing, and social learning behavioral types—refine their grazing management and livestock placement decisions based on observed biomass conditions and neighbor performance. A second change point near timestep 60 marks a further transition to a slower, steady accumulation rate of approximately 0.31 animals per timestep, which persists through the remainder of the simulation. This continued but decelerating accumulation implies that while destocking remains a continual management necessity in response to stochastic rainfall and ecological constraints, the severity of required stock reductions diminishes over time as the population gradually approaches an equilibrium between herd size and carrying capacity.
