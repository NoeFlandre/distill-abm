The agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods. The average degree of deviation from recommended livestock levels begins at a near-optimal state of approximately 0.007 units at year 3. The initial risk attitude serves as a time-invariant characteristic for the social learning cohort. This stability reflects the static nature of the initial condition parameter, which establishes a permanent reference point for how these agents perceive uncertainty when evaluating pasture conditions. The average available green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units. The average livestock holdings per household begin at 90 animals and undergo a pronounced decline over the first four decades. The oscillation patterns observed throughout the time series, with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, likely reflect the interaction between variable rainfall conditions and the adaptive responses of the grazing system. The distribution of healthy livestock across households begins in a state of perfect equality at approximately zero. Over the subsequent century, inequality increases steadily and monotonically, reaching approximately 0.073 by year 100. The trajectory illustrates a transition from acute resource crises during initial adaptation to resilient, low-level perturbation management as learning mechanisms mature. The population of households employing the non-learning baseline strategy initiates at 20 families. During the first eight years, this count undergoes a modest expansion, reaching a maximum of approximately 21 households near year 8. The attrition continues at a moderated but steady rate, falling from approximately 17.5 to 15.5 households.
