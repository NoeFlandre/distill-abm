**Context and Goals**

This agent-based model represents smallholder farmers managing private pastures in a grazing system. Unlike the original RAGE model which examined common property scenarios where herders move livestock across shared rangelands, this extension focuses on stationary households each owning a specific patch of land. The model explores how different learning strategies affect long-term outcomes for both the land and the farmers' livelihoods.

The simulation tracks the interaction between vegetation condition and household decisions about livestock numbers over time. Each year, farmers observe their pasture condition and neighbors' situations to decide how many animals to keep. The model specifically investigates three distinct approaches to decision-making: a baseline approach without active learning, learning-by-doing through personal experience, and social learning from observing neighbors.

The central purpose is understanding how these different ways of adapting to changing conditions influence pasture health, herd sizes, and the persistence of households. The model particularly examines how farmers handle uncertainty and incomplete information when making stocking decisions, represented through individual risk dispositions that affect how they perceive and react to environmental feedback.

**Key Parameters**

*Population and Spatial Setup:*
The **number-households** parameter establishes how many farming families occupy the landscape, which can range from 1 to 100 families, with this study using 60 households. Each household begins with **livestock-init** set at 90 animals. The simulation runs for **timesteps** set at 100 years. Farmers begin the simulation positioned on their designated land parcels, with **start-on-homepatch?** set to True. Each farmer can observe conditions within a **knowledge-radius** that ranges from 0 to 5 patches, set here at 1 patch distance.

*Behavioral Type Configuration:*
The study examines three specific learning strategies with equal representation: **number-E-RO** (baseline experiential rule-of-thumb), **number-E-LBD** (learning-by-doing), and **number-E-RO-SL1** (social learning with single-loop adaptation), each set to 20 households. The **homog-behav-types?** parameter set to True ensures each type is evaluated in uniform groups. The original RAGE model behavioral types are inactive in this extension: **number-SAT**, **number-TRAD**, **number-MAX**, and **number-random** are all set to 0. The default reference **behavioral-type** is set to E-RO.

*Grazing Management Practices:*
Formal pasture resting is disabled with **resting?** set to False. The threshold for initiating rest periods, **resting-threshold**, ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0.1. The **intrinsic-preference** for livestock relative to other values ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0. Similarly, **social-susceptibility** to neighbor influence ranges from 0 to 1 and is set at 0, indicating decisions rely on other factors.

*Original Model Parameters:*
Several parameters from the foundational RAGE model are retained. The **satisficing-threshold** for satisfied decision-making ranges from 0 to 100 and is set at 0. The **satisficing-trials** parameter, ranging from 0 to 100, is set at 21. These control how the original satisficer type would search for acceptable solutions, though this type is not active in the current setup.

*Ecological Conditions:*
Rainfall is applied uniformly across the landscape with **global-rain?** set to True. The average rainfall **rain-mean** is set at 200 units annually. Year-to-year variation in precipitation is controlled by **rain-std** set at 100, indicating significant variability in annual rainfall totals. Vegetation growth responds through two rates: **gr1** set at 0.5 and **gr2** set at 0.1. The parameter **w** from the original vegetation model is set at 0.8, influencing how pastures recover from grazing. Livestock reproduction is not active in this extension, with **b** set to 0.

*Learning and Risk Mechanisms:*
The extension module is activated with **extension-model?** set to True. Uncertainty handling is engaged with **risk-mode?** set to True. Each agent begins with an initial risk disposition **risk-att-init** set at 0.5, representing their baseline attitude toward uncertainty when interpreting pasture conditions and neighbor performance. The ability to switch fundamental behavioral strategies (double-loop learning) is disabled with **SL2-strategy-switch?** set to False, confining agents to single-loop learning within their assigned type.

The average initial risk attitude among social learning agents remains fixed at 0.0074 throughout the entire simulation, persisting unchanged from the initial timestep through year 100. This constant value exhibits zero variance across all time steps, indicating that the baseline risk disposition assigned to social learning households at the outset does not fluctuate or evolve during the simulation period. This stability reflects the static nature of the initial condition parameter, which establishes a permanent reference point for how these agents perceive uncertainty when evaluating pasture conditions and determining deviations from recommended livestock levels. The absence of any trend or oscillation confirms that the initial risk attitude serves as a time-invariant characteristic for the social learning cohort, providing a consistent behavioral foundation against which their adaptive decisions are calibrated across the full temporal span of the grazing system simulation.

The average degree of deviation from recommended livestock levels begins at a near-optimal state of approximately 0.007 units at year 3, indicating that households initially maintain stocking rates very close to theoretical recommendations. This minimal deviation is short-lived, as the metric rises sharply throughout the early simulation period, reaching a maximum value of 0.3814 around year 33. This substantial increase suggests that as farmers apply their respective learning strategies—whether baseline rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning—they initially diverge significantly from optimal stocking densities, potentially due to overestimation of carrying capacity during favorable rainfall years or delayed responses to pasture degradation.

Following this peak, the deviation enters a sustained decline from year 33 through approximately year 50, decreasing to values around 0.27 by year 48 before continuing to fall. This reduction likely reflects the effectiveness of experiential and social learning mechanisms in correcting earlier misjudgments, as households adjust their risk perceptions and stocking decisions based on accumulated feedback from pasture conditions and neighbor outcomes. The decline is particularly pronounced between years 33 and 50, corresponding to the period when agents refine their single-loop adaptations within their fixed behavioral strategies.

After year 50, the deviation stabilizes considerably, fluctuating without significant directional trend around a mean of approximately 0.23, ultimately settling at 0.1911 by the end of the 100-year simulation. While this represents a substantial improvement from the peak deviation observed at year 33, the final value remains significantly elevated above the initial near-zero baseline. This persistence suggests that despite continuous learning and adaptation, the combination of high rainfall variability and restricted knowledge radius (limited to neighboring patches) prevents households from fully converging on recommended stocking rates.

The system exhibits a dominant oscillatory pattern with a period of approximately 49 years, indicating cyclical adjustments in stocking decisions that likely correspond to multi-decadal vegetation recovery cycles or recurrent patterns of drought and recovery. These oscillations reflect the ongoing tension between livestock accumulation pressures and pasture regeneration rates, mediated by farmers' evolving risk attitudes under uncertainty. The high variance in deviation during the middle years (approximately years 20-40) compared to the final decades suggests that the learning process introduces significant variability in outcomes before settling into more consistent, albeit imperfect, management patterns.

The average reserve biomass across the landscape begins at 90,000 units and exhibits a pronounced U-shaped trajectory over the 100-year simulation period. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 26 years, biomass undergoes a significant and steady decline, reaching a minimum value of roughly 84,114 units. This early depletion reflects the immediate grazing pressure exerted by the initial livestock endowments—90 animals per household across 60 farms—which temporarily exceeds the vegetation's regenerative capacity under the regime of high rainfall variability (mean 200 units annually with standard deviation of 100).

Following this nadir at year 26, the system enters a transitional recovery phase lasting until approximately year 48. Notable inflection points in the trend dynamics occur around years 8 and 48, signaling shifts in the rate of change as the ecological and behavioral components of the system interact. By year 50, a definitive structural change occurs, marking the onset of a sustained accumulation phase. From this midpoint onward, reserve biomass increases monotonically through the remainder of the simulation, ultimately reaching approximately 121,073 units by year 100.

The trajectory exhibits oscillatory characteristics, particularly clustered around years 10–20 and 41–49, with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 years. These cycles likely reflect the interaction between stochastic rainfall events and the adaptive responses of farmers employing distinct learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning). The recovery pattern suggests that as farmers utilize their knowledge radius to observe pasture conditions and neighbor outcomes, they adjust stocking decisions to reduce grazing pressure below carrying capacity. 

Statistical evidence indicates highly significant decreasing trends in the earliest windows (p < 0.01), transitioning through a brief period of no significant trend around years 22–32, followed by consistently significant increasing trends from year 33 onward (p < 0.001). Notably, the rate of biomass accumulation decelerates over time—from slopes exceeding 1,900 units per year in the mid-recovery phase to merely 5 units per year in the final decade—suggesting the system asymptotically approaches a higher biomass equilibrium as learning-mediated stocking rates stabilize under conditions of ecological uncertainty and initial risk disposition of 0.5.

The average available green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units. Over the first 27 years, it declines to a minimum of roughly 42,057 units. This initial decrease suggests that the initial stocking levels exert pressure on the vegetation, temporarily degrading pasture condition as the system adjusts to grazing pressure.

Following this low point at year 27, the biomass enters a sustained recovery phase. Between years 33 and 54, the vegetation increases rapidly, indicating that farmers' adaptive learning strategies—whether through personal experience or observation of neighbors—enable them to adjust stocking decisions in ways that facilitate pasture recovery. The rate of increase moderates after year 54 but continues steadily through year 100, when biomass reaches approximately 60,535 units.

This trajectory suggests that the learning mechanisms implemented by the farmers eventually overcome the initial grazing pressure, allowing the system to stabilize at a higher biomass state than its initial condition. The oscillation patterns observed throughout the time series, with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, likely reflect the interaction between variable rainfall conditions and the adaptive responses of the grazing system.

The average livestock holdings per household begin at 90 animals and undergo a pronounced decline over the first four decades. This initial reduction occurs most rapidly during the first ten years, reflecting the immediate pressure of initial stocking levels on pasture vegetation that exceeds sustainable carrying capacity given the variable rainfall regime. The decline continues through year 20, though at a moderating rate, as farmers gradually adjust their herds in response to deteriorating pasture conditions and accumulated experience with ecological feedback.

The trajectory reaches its minimum around year 37, when holdings fall to approximately 41 animals per household. This nadir represents the low point of productive capacity, where stocking levels have compressed to roughly 45 percent of their initial value under severe resource constraints. Following this trough, the system enters a brief stabilization period lasting until approximately year 44, where livestock numbers plateau as farmers assimilate information about the degraded baseline productivity and the limits imposed by private pasture boundaries.

A gradual recovery phase commences around year 44 and continues through the remainder of the century. Holdings increase moderately from the minimum, reaching approximately 48 animals by year 55, then continue a slow ascent to terminate near 47.5 animals at year 100. This upward trend becomes progressively more gradual over time, with the rate of increase slowing considerably after year 55 and diminishing further in the final decades. This asymptotic approach suggests that farmers are cautiously rebuilding herds within constrained limits, likely reflecting conservative risk dispositions developed through the earlier crash experience, while vegetation stabilizes at a lower productivity equilibrium.

Across the full simulation period, the system transitions from an initial overstocked condition through a correction phase of substantial destocking, ultimately settling at an equilibrium roughly 47 percent below initial levels. The extended recovery never approaches the original stocking rate, indicating that the learning strategies—whether experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social observation—enable adaptation to environmental feedback but result in a permanently reduced but presumably more sustainable stocking density that accounts for rainfall variability and the finite carrying capacity of privately managed pastures.

The average number of livestock destocked per household begins at 0 animals in year 0, reflecting full initial resource availability. By year 3, the value spikes sharply to approximately 6.6 animals, representing the maximum destocking pressure observed throughout the simulation. This early peak indicates that the initial herd size of 90 animals per household substantially exceeds pasture carrying capacity under variable rainfall conditions, where precipitation fluctuates with a standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200 units annually.

Following the initial crisis, destocking rates exhibit a pronounced decreasing trend through approximately year 30. Secondary peaks occur at years 9 and 12 with values near 3.4 and 2.7 animals respectively, followed by progressively smaller oscillations ranging between 0.8 and 1.5 animals through year 27. This systematic decline suggests effective adaptation across the three learning strategies, as experiential learning and social observation enable households to adjust stocking decisions proactively, reducing reliance on forced destocking as a primary management tool.

Between years 30 and 65, destocking stabilizes at chronically low levels, generally remaining below 0.5 animals per household with no significant directional trend. This plateau reflects a management equilibrium where destocking events arise primarily from environmental stochasticity rather than systematic overgrazing, indicating that farmers have converged on sustainable stocking rates relative to their private pasture conditions.

From years 66 through 76, the series exhibits a modest but statistically significant increasing trend, with values rising slightly before stabilizing again. This late-period fluctuation may relate to the dominant oscillation cycle of approximately 49 years interacting with rainfall variability, temporarily increasing pressure on pasture resources even under established management routines.

By year 100, destocking levels decline to approximately 0.31 animals per household, a value nearly identical to the median of 0.31 but substantially below the overall mean of 0.96. This distribution indicates right-skewed dynamics where the majority of forced destocking concentrates in the first two decades of the simulation, while the latter half operates under relatively stable conditions with minimal livestock removal. The trajectory illustrates a transition from acute resource crises during initial adaptation to resilient, low-level perturbation management as learning mechanisms mature.

The distribution of healthy livestock across households begins in a state of perfect equality at approximately zero, reflecting the uniform initial endowment of animals across all farming families. Over the subsequent century, inequality increases steadily and monotonically, reaching approximately 0.073 by year 100, indicating a modest divergence in livestock health outcomes despite remaining at relatively low levels throughout the simulation.

The initial establishment of inequality proceeds rapidly within the first decade, with the rate of accumulation exhibiting multiple inflections during the early and middle phases around years 8, 13, 15, 20, and 25. These shifts reflect the differential effectiveness of the three learning strategies—baseline experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—in responding to highly variable rainfall conditions, as households begin to accumulate distinct portfolios of healthy livestock based on their adaptive approaches. The trajectory continues to evolve through the middle period, with further adjustments to the accumulation rate around years 37 and 57, suggesting ongoing differentiation as learning effects compound over time.

Significant transitions in the dynamics of inequality accumulation occur around years 51 and 71, marking change points where the system enters new regimes of divergence, likely corresponding to the manifestation of cumulative learning advantages over multi-decadal timescales. Superimposed on this increasing trend, the system exhibits cyclical fluctuations with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 years, particularly evident in the later decades. This oscillatory component likely emerges from the interaction between inter-annual rainfall variability and the adaptive responses of heterogeneous learner types, creating temporary convergences and divergences in outcomes as environmental conditions fluctuate.

By the conclusion of the simulation, the final value of approximately 0.073 indicates that while measurable disparities exist in the distribution of healthy livestock across households employing different cognitive strategies, the overall system maintains a relatively equitable distribution of animal assets. This suggests that the combination of local observation networks within the restricted knowledge radius and adaptive decision-making constraints prevents extreme polarization of livestock holdings, despite the divergent long-term outcomes generated by distinct approaches to learning and risk management.

The engagement of households in social learning commences at zero and follows a trajectory of rapid initial growth followed by progressive deceleration and eventual stabilization. During the first seven years, the number of engaged households increases sharply, reflecting immediate adoption of information-seeking behaviors as farmers confront initial environmental uncertainty and incomplete information about pasture conditions. By year 28, the growth rate moderates considerably as the system transitions from acute establishment to sustained accumulation, with the rate of new engagement diminishing through years 30, 33, and 38.  

The count approaches an asymptote near 32 households around year 63, maintaining this plateau through the conclusion of the simulation at year 100. This saturation level suggests that approximately half of the farming population ultimately participates in social learning, indicating a stable equilibrium where observation of neighbors becomes a persistent strategy for a substantial minority of households despite the presence of alternative decision-making approaches.  

Superimposed on this general upward trend are periodic oscillations with a dominant cycle of approximately 49 years, particularly evident between years 22-32 and again between years 51-59. These fluctuations likely reflect the influence of high inter-annual rainfall variability on decision-making cycles, where deviations in precipitation trigger temporary variations in the propensity to seek social information. The oscillations diminish in amplitude as the system approaches its plateau, suggesting that long-term engagement patterns become less sensitive to environmental shocks once social learning networks are established.  

This pattern indicates that social learning diffuses rapidly when initial pasture assessments prove ambiguous under uncertainty, but reaches a ceiling well below universal adoption. The partial penetration may reflect the model's constraint against strategy-switching, which preserves distinct learning typologies across the population. The cyclic variations align with the vegetation recovery dynamics and stochastic rainfall, suggesting that households adjust their reliance on neighbor observation according to transient visibility of environmental feedback signals.

The cumulative number of livestock destocked per household initiates at zero and accumulates monotonically throughout the century-long simulation, ultimately reaching approximately 97 animals. The trajectory exhibits a markedly concave form, characterized by rapid initial accumulation that decelerates substantially over time.

During the first decade, destocking proceeds at an accelerated pace, accumulating nearly 5 animals annually as households undertake immediate reductions from their initial herds of 90 animals. This rapid phase suggests acute adjustments to pasture conditions or initial calibration of stocking levels under uncertainty. By approximately year 25, the accumulation rate diminishes significantly to less than one animal per year, transitioning the system into a phase of managed, gradual destocking.

The period between years 25 and 60 shows continued but slower accumulation, with periodic adjustments indicated by fluctuating inflection points around years 45 through 60. These dynamics likely reflect ongoing adaptive responses to the high inter-annual rainfall variability, where households periodically remove animals during drought episodes. A secondary modulation in the accumulation rate occurs around year 60, after which the system enters a sustained low-rate destocking phase through year 100.

Superimposed on the long-term increasing trend, oscillatory patterns with a dominant period of approximately 49 years suggest cyclical pressures on herd management, potentially corresponding to multi-decadal climatic variations or vegetation recovery cycles. The persistence of positive accumulation throughout the simulation, even in later decades, indicates that destocking remains a recurrent management tool under variable ecological conditions, rather than a single corrective event. The final cumulative value exceeding the initial herd size implies that households engage in restocking activities between destocking events to maintain livelihoods while navigating the trade-offs between herd size and pasture sustainability.

The population of households employing the non-learning baseline strategy initiates at 20 families. During the first eight years, this count undergoes a modest expansion, reaching a maximum of approximately 21 households near year 8. This brief increase suggests an initial period of demographic stability before the full impact of environmental variability manifests.

Following this peak, the system enters a sustained decline commencing around year 12. The reduction accelerates significantly through year 20, dropping from roughly 20 to below 19 households, indicating the onset of household exits as fixed rule-of-thumb decision-making encounters the realities of high rainfall variability (annual standard deviation of 100 units) and dynamic pasture feedbacks. Between years 22 and 45, the attrition continues at a moderated but steady rate, falling from approximately 17.5 to 15.5 households, reflecting ongoing vulnerability of non-adaptive agents to ecological stochasticity and competitive pressure from learning-capable neighbors.

After approximately year 60, the rate of exit diminishes substantially, and the series stabilizes near 15 households. From years 66 through 100, no significant directional trend persists, with values fluctuating narrowly between approximately 14.7 and 15.0 households, reaching a minimum of roughly 14.7 near year 92 before concluding at 14.7. This late-stage plateau suggests that the surviving E-RO households have adjusted their risk dispositions through single-loop learning to viable levels despite lacking access to experiential or social learning mechanisms, achieving a persistent equilibrium with their local ecological conditions.

The overall trajectory reveals a net decline of approximately 26.5% over the century, demonstrating that in environments characterized by substantial rainfall uncertainty and vegetation dynamics, stationary households relying solely on static decision rules face elevated attrition rates, though a subset achieves long-term persistence through within-strategy risk adjustment.
