Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. Cyclical variation suggests social influence mechanisms operating through the Watts-Strogatz network topology create temporary reversals or pauses in the abandonment of whole milk consumption. The median consumption level of approximately 817 units indicates that for roughly half the simulation duration, weekly consumption remained above this value. The rate of increase more than triples to roughly 737 selections per period. From 1984 through 2005, the system enters a sustained high-growth regime. This nineteen-year period of consistent velocity reflects the maturation of social influence mechanisms operating through the Watts-Strogatz network topology. The trajectory unfolds across three distinct phases. Spanning time steps 0 through 9, consumption exhibits rapid exponential-like growth. The middle phase, encompassing time steps 10 through 24, demonstrates a pronounced plateau characterized by oscillation around a stable mean of roughly 1200-1250 units. Habit factor reaches 1.5879, indicating that by the end of the simulation period, habitual disposition has become a substantial positive driver for alternative milk selection. At the outset in 1974, all agents exhibited identical choice function scores, reflecting the initial market condition where whole milk dominated consumption patterns uniformly across the population. Avior misalignment as habit persistence, social blindness and justification mechanisms inhibited immediate behavioral responses to updated health perceptions. Cognitive dissonance threshold (0.15) likely contributed to this delay, as agents required substantial accumulated inconsistency before adjusting choices.
