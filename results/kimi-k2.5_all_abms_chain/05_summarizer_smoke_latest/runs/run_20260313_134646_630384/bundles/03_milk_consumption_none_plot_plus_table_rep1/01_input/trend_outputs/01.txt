Mean whole milk consumption exhibits a pronounced decreasing trend across the simulation period, declining from approximately 2655 milliliters per agent at initialization (step 0) to roughly 253 milliliters by the final observation (step 32), representing a reduction of nearly 90% over the 33 time steps representing the 1974–2005 timeframe.

The trajectory follows a markedly non-linear path characterized by four distinct phases. Initially, a rapid decline occurs during steps 0 through 10, with consumption falling sharply from 2655 to approximately 1000 milliliters. This phase features the steepest rate of decrease, particularly between steps 5 and 9, where statistical analysis confirms a significantly decreasing trend with the maximum negative slope.

Following this precipitous drop, the rate of decline moderates substantially between steps 10 and 18 as consumption decreases gradually toward the 760–800 milliliter range. By step 18, values reach approximately 760 milliliters before a minor fluctuation creates a temporary peak of roughly 774 milliliters at step 19.

From approximately step 19 through step 25, the series enters a plateau phase characterized by relative stabilization and minor oscillations. During this interval, values fluctuate between approximately 594 and 775 milliliters, with a local minimum occurring at step 26 (594 milliliters) followed by a secondary peak at step 27 (621 milliliters). Statistical tests indicate no significant monotonic trend during portions of this middle period, suggesting a temporary equilibrium where strong incumbent habits—initialized at maximum strength (parameter value 10)—and social blindness effects partially counteract the disposition to adopt alternative milk varieties.

After step 27, the decline resumes with varying intensity, accelerating through steps 28 and 29 to reach approximately 323 milliliters, followed by a minor oscillation showing a temporary rise to 335 milliliters at step 30, before concluding with a final decrease to the terminal value of 253 milliliters at step 32.

In the context of the modeled British public transitioning from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties, this trajectory reflects the interaction between individual disposition mechanisms—operating through both threshold-based and probability-based triggers—and the persistence of consumption habits. The intermediate plateau represents a resistant subpopulation maintaining whole milk consumption due to high habit strength and cognitive dissonance thresholds (0.15), before continued normative pressure and cumulative social influence within the agent network ultimately drive consumption down to minimal levels by the simulation's end.
