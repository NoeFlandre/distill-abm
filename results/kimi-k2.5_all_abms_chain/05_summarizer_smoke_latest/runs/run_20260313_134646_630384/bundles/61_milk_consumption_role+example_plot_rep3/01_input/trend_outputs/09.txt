The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk initiates at a baseline value of 1.0, representing the absence of established habitual tendencies toward these alternative options at the simulation's outset. Throughout the first approximately five time steps, the factor remains effectively constant, fluctuating negligibly between 1.0 and 1.02. This stagnation corresponds to the period where whole milk maintains its dominance due to the high initial habit strength assigned to the incumbent choice, limiting the frequency with which agents select skimmed or semi-skimmed varieties.

Between steps 5 and 10, the metric undergoes a gradual inflection, accelerating from roughly 1.02 to approximately 1.08. This modest upturn signals the beginning of habit crystallization as early adopters transition to lower-fat alternatives and begin accumulating repeated consumption experiences within their memory windows.

Subsequently, from step 10 through the conclusion of the simulation at step 32, the habit factor increases in an approximately linear fashion at a rate of about 0.02 to 0.025 per time step. By step 20, the value reaches approximately 1.35, and by step 30, it approaches 1.55. The trajectory terminates at values between 1.58 and 1.60. This sustained, steady growth indicates that as the population progressively adopts skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, each repetition of the behavior reinforces neural and cognitive associations, systematically strengthening the habitual pull toward these alternatives in the choice function.

The consistency across simulation runs is notably high, with minimal dispersion observed at all time steps. This reliability suggests that the habit formation process, governed by the interaction of the 5.0-step memory lifetime and the threshold-based habit mechanics, produces deterministic macro-level patterns despite stochastic elements in individual agent decision-making. The monotonic increase from 1.0 to nearly 1.6 over 32 steps reflects the temporal scaling of habitual strength as the alternative milk transitions from a novel option to a default behavior for the majority of the simulated consumer population.
