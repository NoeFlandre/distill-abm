The average weekly whole milk consumption per agent commences at approximately 2,650 milliliters at time step zero. After a brief initial plateau lasting roughly one time step, the metric enters a pronounced phase of decline. Between time steps 1 and 10, consumption drops steeply from approximately 2,650 to 950, representing a reduction of nearly two-thirds. This rapid descent indicates that despite the initially high habit strength of 10.0 assigned to whole milk, the combined effects of social influence mechanisms—with a 30% interaction probability and substantial network connectivity—and evolving perceptions of alternative milk options quickly overcome habitual barriers for a significant portion of the population.

From time step 10 through approximately time step 25, the rate of decline moderates substantially. During this intermediate period, consumption decreases gradually from roughly 950 to approximately 600-650, exhibiting minor fluctuations around time steps 18-20 where the trajectory briefly stabilizes. This deceleration reflects the persistence of entrenched habitual behavior among remaining consumers, as the population segment with the strongest habitual attachments to whole milk resists the social and perceptual pressures that drove earlier adopters to switch.

In the terminal phase spanning time steps 25 to 32, the decline accelerates once more, falling from approximately 600-650 to roughly 250-300 by the simulation's conclusion. This final drop suggests that even resistant consumers eventually yield to accumulated social conformity pressures and the increasing salience of health and environmental attributes, though a residual consumption level of 250-300 persists, indicating that a small segment of the population maintains whole milk consumption throughout the 31-year period.

Across repeated simulation runs, variance remains minimal during the initial and final time steps but increases moderately during the rapid transition phase between time steps 5 and 15. This pattern suggests that while the starting conditions and ultimate convergence are deterministic, the stochastic timing of individual habit-breaking events introduces variability in the intermediate dynamics, consistent with the probabilistic nature of social interactions and threshold-based psychological mechanisms governing the model.
