model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005 . individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions . each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers . between time steps 1 and 10, consumption drops steeply from 2,650 to 950 . this represents a reduction of nearly two-thirds . despite the initially high habit strength of 10.0 assigned to whole milk, the combined effects of social influence mechanisms quickly overcome habitual barriers . the variance across simulation runs remains minimal during the first 10 steps, with all trajectories tightly clustered, but diverges progressively thereafter, reaching a spread of approximately 400 total choices by step 32 . the total number of milk choices made by the population of agents accumulates in a strictly linear fashion throughout the simulation period . trajectories exhibit moderate variance during rapid growth phase of steps 5 through 10, where different runs diverge by up to 130 units . variance diminishes significantly during the plateau phase and converges tightly by the conclusion, suggesting that random seeds affect timing of initial market penetration . metric increases sharply, ascending rapidly to approximately 0.58 . rapid escalation indicates swift differentiation in how agents evaluate milk options as they begin processing health and environmental information, forming new habits, and responding to social networks .
