The mean weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent initiates at approximately 5.3 units at time step zero, representing the 1974 baseline when whole milk dominated the British market and alternative varieties held minimal market share. During the initial nine time steps, consumption exhibits a steep monotonic increase, rising rapidly from near-negligible levels to approximately 1,200 units by time step 9, and reaching a local maximum of roughly 1,265 units at time step 11. This sharp upward trajectory corresponds to the initial adoption phase where health perceptions and social influence mechanisms begin overcoming incumbent habits and whole milk preferences among the agent population.

Following this rapid ascent, the consumption series enters an extended plateau phase spanning approximately time steps 12 through 23, during which values oscillate moderately between roughly 1,180 and 1,240 units without establishing a consistent directional trend. This middle period reflects a temporary market equilibrium where habit formation strength and social network structures create resistance to further rapid transitions, despite continued exposure to alternative milk preferences and health information within the social system.

In the final phase from time step 24 through 32, consumption resumes a significant upward trajectory, climbing from approximately 1,210 units to a terminal value of roughly 1,457 units. This late-stage increase represents the consolidation of market dominance by skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties, successfully capturing the historical crossover point where alternative milk consumption surpasses whole milk levels by 2005. The consistency of this three-phase pattern across multiple simulation runs indicates robust model behavior in capturing the historical British milk transition dynamics, with minimal variance in initial and terminal states suggesting stable boundary conditions and strong habit persistence mechanisms at both the introduction and maturation stages of the product substitution process.
