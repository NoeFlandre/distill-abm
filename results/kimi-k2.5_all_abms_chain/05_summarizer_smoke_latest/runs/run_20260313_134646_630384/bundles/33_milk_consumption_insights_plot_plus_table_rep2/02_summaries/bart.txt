This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The primary objective is to explore how perception, habits, and social influence interact within individual decision-making processes regarding milk choice. The simulation of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk adoption reveals a distinct three-phase trajectory characterized by rapid early growth, an extended plateau period, and late-stage resurgence. The overall pattern demonstrates a 275-fold increase in alternative milk consumption, with the most statistically significant growth occurring in windows 0-4 and 5-9. Statistical testing confirms significantly increasing trends across all rolling windows, with Kendall's tau values of 1,000 and p-values of 0.027. The dynamics suggest an initial establishment phase during the first five steps characterized by accelerating adoption, followed by a prolonged period of roughly linear growth. This pattern reflects the historical shift from whole milk dominance to reduced-fat alternatives in British consumption habits. The mean habit factor for whole milk initiates at a baseline value of 1,000 and undergoes an immediate increase to approximately 1.020 within the first time step. Over the subsequent 31 time steps, the metric exhibits a steady, monotonic upward trajectory. The trajectory exhibits subtle inflection points around time steps 7 and 12, where the rate of habit strengthening modulates slightly, though the overall ascending progression remains consistent and statistically significant. At first, the choice function deviation registers at 0.0000, indicating homogeneity among agents in their evaluation of milk options. By step 5, agents begin developing distinct evaluation criteria based on health perceptions, environmental considerations, and social influences. The rate of divergence then moderates significantly, with the metric reaching approximately 0.67 by step 12 and continuing a gradual ascent to 0.7508 by the final step at step 32. The British public transitioned from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties between 1974 and 2005. Consumers increasingly experienced misalignment between their personal health valuations and their actual consumption choices. Public health interventions might effectively address emerging cognitive dissonance before it becomes entrenched.
