The inequality in healthy livestock distribution begins at complete equality with a Gini coefficient of 0.0000, reflecting the homogeneous initial condition where all 60 households start with identical herd sizes of 90 animals. Over the 100-year simulation period, this inequality rises consistently to reach approximately 0.0726 by the final time step, indicating the emergence of moderate differentiation in household livestock holdings despite identical starting resources.

The trajectory of inequality accumulation follows a non-linear pattern characterized by distinct phases of acceleration. During the initial phase from years 1 to approximately 50, inequality builds gradually with notable inflection points occurring around years 8, 13, 15, 20, and 25, suggesting periods where the rate of differentiation intensifies. This early divergence likely stems from the heterogeneity in behavioral types—specifically the coexistence of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 decision-making strategies—and variations in household risk attitudes (r-parameters), which generate divergent grazing and livestock management decisions even under identical ecological conditions.

A significant acceleration occurs around year 51, marking a change point where the slope of inequality growth steepens. This acceleration continues through subsequent change points at years 71 and 100, with the most pronounced increases occurring in the latter half of the simulation. By years 78 through 93, the system exhibits oscillatory behavior superimposed on the upward trend, suggesting that while the overall direction toward greater inequality remains consistent, temporary fluctuations in relative household performance create short-term variations in the distribution pattern. These oscillations likely reflect the feedback dynamics between vegetation conditions, learning processes, and adaptive decision-making as households with different learning capabilities respond to environmental stochasticity and neighbor observations.

The statistical evidence confirms a monotonic increasing trend across all analyzed windows, with highly significant positive trends (tau values of 1.000) throughout the entire simulation period. This consistent upward trajectory indicates that differentiation is not transient but structurally embedded in the system dynamics, driven by the interplay between private property boundaries, heterogeneous learning behaviors, and ecological feedbacks.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The inevitable emergence of livestock inequality even under optimal starting conditions suggests that policy interventions should anticipate and manage distributional outcomes rather than assume equitable outcomes will persist. The acceleration of inequality after year 50 indicates that early-stage equality may mask underlying divergence processes, warranting monitoring systems that track not just aggregate productivity but distributional equity. The moderate final Gini coefficient (0.07) suggests that while differentiation occurs, it remains within manageable bounds in this mixed behavioral context, implying that promoting diverse learning strategies may help constrain inequality compared to homogeneous populations. However, the consistent upward trend across all simulation runs indicates that without active redistribution mechanisms or collective action frameworks, private property grazing systems naturally trend toward increased stratification over multi-decadal timescales.
