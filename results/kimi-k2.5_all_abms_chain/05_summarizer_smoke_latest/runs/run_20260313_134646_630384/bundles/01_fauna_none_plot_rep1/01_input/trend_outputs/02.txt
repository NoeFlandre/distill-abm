The total energy available to forager bands exhibits a consistent downward trajectory across all simulation runs, declining from an initial value of approximately 2000 energy units at step zero to values between -32,000 and -38,000 energy units by the conclusion of the simulations at approximately 2100 to 2200 steps.

During the initial phase spanning roughly the first 500 steps, the energy decreases rapidly to approximately -5000 units, though this phase is characterized by considerable variation in the rate of decline across different runs. The spread between runs reaches several thousand units, indicating that stochastic differences in initial positioning or early hunting success create divergent energy states during the transient period.

From approximately step 500 to step 2000, the dynamics shift to a highly regular pattern. All simulations follow nearly identical linear trajectories, decreasing at a constant rate of approximately 15 to 20 energy units per step. During this period, the total energy falls from approximately -5000 to -32,000. The tight convergence of values across all runs indicates that initial stochastic differences become irrelevant as the system settles into a consistent state of energy depletion.

In the final phase beyond step 2000, the trajectories diverge slightly. While most simulations terminate or plateau around -32,000 to -33,000 energy units at approximately step 2100, at least one simulation continues the linear decline to approximately -38,000 energy units by step 2200.

The persistent negative trend throughout all phases indicates that forager bands are operating at a net energy loss, with energy expended through movement and hunting activities exceeding energy gained from successfully processed prey. The linearity of the decline during the middle phase suggests a steady depletion of prey resources or a constant search effort applied to an increasingly scarce prey population, resulting in predictable rates of energy deficit accumulation. The convergence across runs demonstrates that regardless of initial stochastic variation, the interaction between hunting costs, processing costs, and prey availability drives all forager populations toward similar energy states over time.
