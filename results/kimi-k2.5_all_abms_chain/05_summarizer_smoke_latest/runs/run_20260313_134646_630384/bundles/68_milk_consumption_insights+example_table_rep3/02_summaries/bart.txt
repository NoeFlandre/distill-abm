The study simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005, replicating individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence, and scores where agents may adjust future decisions. The cumulative count of whole milk selections begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the simulation, reaching approximately 2,195 total choices by the conclusion of the 2005 period. The trajectory exhibits distinct phases of growth velocity. During the initial quinquennial period (1974–1978), accumulation proceeds at roughly 200 choices per annum, gradually gradually increasing. The cumulative accumulation proceeds gradually to reach approximately 25,644 total choice events by 2005. The average weekly consumption of whole milk per person begins at approximately 2,655 units in 1974. Consumption declines substantially to roughly 253 units by 2005, reflecting the historical transition toward skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives driven by shifting health perceptions within the agent population. The non-linear trajectory suggests that dietary transition policies should account for distinct phases of adoption, with intervention resources most efficiently deployed during the initial rapid decline period (steps 5-13) when social influence spreads most virally through the network. For decision makers, this pattern suggests that public health interventions may face initial rapid uptake followed by unexpected resistance periods as social norms stabilize. The habit-driven multiplier for alternative milk selection begins at unity, indicating a neutral baseline where habitual considerations initially exert no net positive influence on skimmed or semi-skimmed varieties. Between approximately 1980 and the early 1990s, the metric climbs steadily from roughly 1.02 to approximately 1.4, indicating that agents progressively develop stronger habitual attachments to lower-fat milk varieties. The most rapid expansion of the deviation occurs during the earliest stage, with the metric climbing steeply through the first four time steps before moderating into a more gradual but persistent ascent. As social influence mechanisms and disposition triggers activate within the Watts-Strogatz network, agents adopt alternative milk varieties due to shifting social norms and peer effects rather than purely personal health optimization.
