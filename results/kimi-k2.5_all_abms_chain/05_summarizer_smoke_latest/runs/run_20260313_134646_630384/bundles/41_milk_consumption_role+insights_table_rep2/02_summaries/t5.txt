Model simulates the shifting landscape of British milk consumption between 1974 and 2005. Individual agents represent adult consumers who begin with preferences reflective of 1974 market conditions. Each agent processes information about health and environmental characteristics of milk options, weighs these against their habitual tendencies, and responds to social influence from other consumers. Per-agent weekly values falling from approximately 2655 units at time step 0 to roughly 253 units by time step 32 (representing 2005) this trajectory represents a reduction of nearly 90 percent over the three-decade period. The descent moderated around time step 5, marking the first inflection. Approximately 71 selections per step, then falls to roughly 67 selections during time steps 15-19 (1989-1993) the deceleration continues through the late 1990s and early 2000s. The accumulation curve exhibits inflection points at time steps 1, 7 and 31, indicating shifts in the curvature of the adoption trajectory. Step indicates consistent agent participation without attrition, variation in decision frequency, or interruption in the simulation sequence. The simulation reveals a pronounced secular decline in whole milk consumption over the 33 time steps, with aggregate quantities falling from an initial level of approximately 2655 units to a terminal value of roughly 253 units. This 15-step cyclical dominance suggests periodic resistance mechanisms where social influence and habit strength create temporary equilibria. The absence of significant monotonic trends during windows 10-14 and 15-19 indicates a contested market phase. Beginning at time step 5, the factor reaches approximately 1.59, representing nearly a 59% increase from the baseline. The accumulation of habit creates self-reinforcing behavioral momentum that becomes difficult to reverse as the simulation progresses. Consumers begin updating health perceptions regarding alternative varieties while maintaining incumbent consumption patterns due to high habit thresholds. This plateau suggests a temporary stabilization of dissonance, potentially occurring as early adopters transition to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties, thereby reducing their personal deviation. A substantial portion of the population continues to consume whole milk despite holding environmental values that would favor alternatives. The oscillatory pattern superimposed on this plateau implies cyclical adjustments within the population as agents periodically reassess their choices. Persistence of a 13-unit deviation gap after three decades suggests that environmental education and awareness campaigns alone are insufficient to align consumption with environmental values.
