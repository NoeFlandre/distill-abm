The cumulative number of female prey animals killed by human foragers exhibits a characteristic saturation pattern across all simulation runs, beginning at zero and ultimately converging to values between approximately 118 and 125 individuals by the termination of the simulations at 2,200 time steps.

During the initial phase from 0 to 500 time steps, the cumulative kill counts increase rapidly but display substantial stochastic variation between runs. By step 500, most trajectories reach values between 100 and 120, though one simulation run lags significantly at approximately 80 females killed. This early variability suggests that chance encounters between forager agents and female prey dominate the hunting dynamics during the establishment phase of the model, with some hunter bands experiencing delayed success in locating and capturing female animals.

Between 500 and 1,000 time steps, the rate of increase decelerates markedly across all runs. During this interval, the previously lagging simulation exhibits catch-up dynamics, reaching approximately 100 kills by step 800 and converging with the other trajectories. By step 1,000, the range narrows to approximately 110–125 cumulative kills, indicating that differential early hunting success becomes less relevant as the prey population structure stabilizes or as hunting pressure saturates the available female prey.

After approximately 1,000–1,500 time steps, the cumulative kill counts plateau, with minimal to no additional females being hunted across all simulation runs. This saturation at roughly 120–125 females killed suggests that the hunting of females effectively ceases, likely due to the depletion of accessible female prey from the population, changes in herd structure or movement patterns that reduce encounter rates, or a shift in forager targeting behavior toward other prey categories. The consistency of the plateau level across runs indicates that the model converges to a similar terminal hunting pressure on females regardless of early stochastic variation in forager success.
