 This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005 . It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence .  The cumulative count of whole milk selections begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the simulation period . This consistent upward trajectory indicates that agents continue to choose whole milk throughout the modeled period from 1974 to 2005 . As adoption spreads through the network, global social norm effects progressively diminish the influence of incumbent habits, allowing the weighted choice functions to favor alternative milk varieties .  The trajectory reflects the continuous operation of the decision sequence, from perception and disposition to choice evaluation . The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5.3 units per person at the start of the simulation period . The decline exhibits distinct phases characterized by varying rates of change .  The mean percentage difference between maximum and minimum choice function scores begins at 0.0000, indicating that at the start of the simulation period corresponding to 1974, all agents evaluated milk options with effectively identical choice functions . By step 4, values reach approximately 0.64, suggesting that disposition mechanisms quickly triggered consideration of options beyond the incumbent whole milk, driven by varying individual perceptions of health and environmental characteristics .  The Watts-Strogatz network topology is the activation of disposition mechanisms and the initial propagation of health information . The absence of detected peaks or valleys throughout the entire series indicates an irreversible directional drift, consistent with a one-way societal transition where historical health perceptions increasingly diverge from actual milk consumption choices .
