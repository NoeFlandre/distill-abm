The model operates on a 211 by 211 patch grid representing a grassland landscape. Each patch can contain vegetation that serves as food for the herbivores. The model specifically incorporates sexual dimorphism. Animals-gain-from-food ranges from 0 to 100, set at 1.5, representing energy obtained from grazing. Threshold for starvation mortality, animals-starvation-threshold, ranges between 0 and 100. Movement costs energy, with animals-movement-cost ranging from 1 to 10. The acceleration of energy loss around tick 650 suggests a critical threshold where prey depletion renders hunting increasingly inefficient. The current parameter configuration produces unsustainable forager energetics. Prey populations initialized at 250 individuals with 20 foragers appear inadequate to support positive energy balance under the specified hunting and movement costs. Majority of runs converge toward the upper range of 120-124 deaths. The mortality rate is steepest, with slopes of 0.2-0.23 female deaths per step and near-perfect monotonic increase. A deceleration phase follows (steps 500-1000, approximately 1.4-2.7 years), where the rate of accumulation drops precipitously to slopes 0.02-0.06 per step. The asymptote suggests that foragers effectively remove nearly the entire reproductive cohort of adult females. The initial steep accumulation corresponds to the harvesting of the starting population's postpartum females, while the subsequent flattening indicates either the successful elimination of all nursing females followed by a reproductive collapse. The system enters a low-mortality steady state characterized by small surviving subpopulations or refugia effects. The rapid accumulation of mortality during the initial phase demonstrates that delayed conservation action may result in irreversible population declines. Individual simulation trajectories display considerable heterogeneity in their temporal dynamics. Some populations collapse completely by step 500, while others persist in fluctuating declines until beyond step 2,000. Superimposed on the long-term decline are periodic oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks. Once densities drop below 10 individuals, recovery becomes statistically improbable. The consistent extinction outcome across all 120 replicates suggests that parameter adjustments to hunter numbers, foraging radius, or environmental productivity would be necessary to achieve coexistence. 3.2–3.8 years into the simulation, the trend reversed to a weak but statistically significant increase. The population resumed decline with increasing momentum (slopes ranging from -0.024 to -0.167 units per tick), eventually reaching zero at tick 2,311. The 63-time-step oscillation cycle corresponds to seasonal vegetation pulsing (seasonality parameter 0.8), creating regular energetic bottlenecks that accelerate when superimposed on hunting mortality. Hunter densities of 20 individuals per 211211 patch landscape exceed the sustainable carrying capacity for megaherbivore populations of 250 animals.
