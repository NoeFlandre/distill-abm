**Trends in Total Energy of Male Ungulate Populations**

The trajectories of combined male energy across multiple simulation runs reveal a consistent pattern of rapid depletion from initial conditions. At the outset (step 0), the total energy stored in male animals ranges between approximately 12,000 and 12,500 units. This metric undergoes a steep decline within the first 300 to 500 steps, with most simulations showing reductions of 60-80% by step 500.

The rate of decline varies substantially across simulation runs during the initial phase (steps 0-300), with some trajectories dropping precipitously to near-zero levels by step 300-400, while others maintain values between 4,000 and 5,000 through step 500. This divergence suggests that stochastic factors in hunter-forager movement patterns or grassland distribution create variability in hunting success rates across different landscape configurations.

Between steps 500 and 1,000, the decline continues but at a decelerating rate for those populations that persist. By step 1,000, approximately half of the simulation runs have reached total male energy levels below 500, with many approaching zero. The remaining active simulations display intermittent plateaus and stepwise reductions, consistent with episodic hunting events and temporary refuge periods.

By step 1,500, nearly all trajectories have converged to zero or near-zero values (0-200 units), indicating that the male segment of the ungulate population has been effectively depleted across virtually all simulation scenarios. A small number of runs show persistence of minimal male energy (under 500 units) extending to approximately step 2,000-2,200 before final depletion.

**Interpretation and Implications**

Given that male ungulates possess higher nutritional value to hunters (30 energy units versus 27 for females) but require greater processing costs (6 versus 3), the rapid depletion of male energy reserves indicates that hunters are efficiently targeting this demographic despite the higher handling costs. The consistent trajectory toward zero male energy by step 1,500 (approximately 4.1 years in model time) suggests that the combination of 20 initial hunters, high food value of males, and the foraging radius of 6 patches creates sufficient predation pressure to functionally eliminate the male subpopulation.

**Decision-Maker Insights**

The data indicate that under current parameter settings, male megaherbivores face extremely high extinction risk within 4-5 years of simulation time. The high variability in depletion rates during the first 500 steps suggests that management interventions or policy decisions have a narrow window of approximately 1-1.5 years to be effective. The consistent terminal outcome across nearly all stochastic variations implies that minor adjustments to hunter numbers or foraging efficiency will likely be insufficient to prevent male population collapse; substantial reductions in hunting pressure or significant landscape modifications would be required to achieve sustainable male population levels. The sexual dimorphism parameters, which assign males 11% higher food value, are evidently sufficient to drive preferential exploitation patterns that eliminate this demographic segment before the female population is comparably affected.
