The aggregate total energy stored in the megaherbivore population initiated at approximately 16,667 units across all simulation replicates and underwent a complete collapse to zero by the termination of the simulation series at 2,312 time steps. This trajectory represents a deterministic decline toward population extinction under the combined pressures of human hunting and seasonal environmental constraints.

The initial phase (time steps 0–500) exhibited the most precipitous reduction, with mean aggregate energy declining by approximately 75% to values around 4,000 units by time step 385–440. During this interval, the system displayed pronounced oscillatory behavior superimposed on the declining trend, characterized by a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 time steps. These oscillations likely reflect the strong seasonal environment (seasonality parameter of 0.8) modulating vegetation availability and consequently herbivore energy acquisition, creating rhythmic pulses of energy increase followed by depletion. Local peaks in aggregate energy during this phase reached values of approximately 4,100 units at time step 385, declining to subsequent peaks of roughly 3,700 units by time step 440.

Between time steps 500 and 875, the rate of decline attenuated considerably, with aggregate energy fluctuating between approximately 800 and 1,100 units. Statistical change point analysis identifies transitions at time steps 500, 625, and 875, marking shifts in the trajectory of depletion. During the interval from time steps 696–927, the Mann-Kendall trend analysis reveals a statistically significant decreasing pattern with a slope of -3.139 energy units per time step, substantially less steep than the initial decline of -24.950 units per time step observed in the first window.

A notable transient dynamic emerged between time steps 1,392 and 1,623, where the system exhibited a statistically significant increasing trend (tau = 0.206, slope = +0.034), suggesting a temporary demographic recovery or reduction in hunting pressure effectiveness. However, this respite proved ephemeral, as the subsequent window (time steps 1,624–1,855) reverted to a significant decreasing trend (tau = -0.565). A second minor recovery occurred between time steps 1,856 and 2,087 with a positive trend (tau = 0.445), though with negligible slope magnitude.

The terminal phase (beyond time step 2,000) approached the extinction threshold, with aggregate energy values declining to near-zero levels. By time step 2,310, the final change point occurred, leading to the complete exhaustion of energy stores by time step 2,312. The median aggregate energy across the entire simulation series was 146.4 units, substantially lower than the mean of 2,368.8 units, indicating a right-skewed distribution where the majority of time steps operated at severely depleted population levels following the initial crash.

The coefficient of variation and high standard deviation (4,360.9) relative to the mean reflect the considerable variability across simulation replicates during the collapse phase, with some populations persisting longer than others due to stochastic factors in hunter-prey encounters and seasonal resource fluctuations. Ultimately, all replicates converged to zero energy storage, indicating deterministic extinction of the megaherbivore population under the parameterized hunting pressure of 20 immortal foragers with energy storage capabilities operating in a seasonal grassland environment with 33% initial vegetation cover.
