The total energetic reserves of female megaherbivores initiate at 12,500 units across the 40 simulation replicates and follow a deterministic trajectory toward extinction, reaching zero by step 2,312. The temporal dynamics exhibit distinct phases demarcated by change points at steps 375, 500, 625, 875, and 2,310, with the aggregate mean energy averaging 1,569 units but displaying extreme variance (standard deviation 3,083) throughout the decline.

An initial phase of rapid depletion dominates steps 0 through 375, characterized by steep negative slopes exceeding -22 units per step. During this interval, total female energy plummets by over 80 percent, dropping from 12,500 to approximately 2,230 units. This precipitous decline reflects the immediate impact of hunting pressure from the initial cohort of 20 human foragers operating under dynamic optimal foraging strategies, compounded by movement costs and baseline mortality in the seasonal environment.

Between steps 375 and 875, the system enters a transition phase where the rate of energetic loss attenuates substantially to slopes ranging from -1.9 to -5.7 units per step. This interval exhibits pronounced oscillatory behavior with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 steps, generating localized peaks around 2,200 units at step 376 and subsequent fluctuations between 1,000 and 2,500 units. These oscillations correspond to inflection points in the decline curve at steps 299, 472, and 506, suggesting periodic resistance to energy loss potentially driven by the high seasonality parameter (0.8) creating seasonal pulses in vegetation regrowth, demographic recovery following birth spacing intervals (730 steps), or stochastic variation in hunting efficiency across replicates.

Beyond step 875, the population enters a terminal phase of protracted low-energy persistence. Aggregate levels fall below the median threshold of 100 units, yet high variance persists due to asynchronous extinction times across individual simulation runs. While the mean trajectory trends toward zero with gentle slopes (-0.02 to -0.17 units per step), transient increases occur in specific windows, notably steps 1,160 through 1,391 and 1,856 through 2,087, representing temporary energy accumulation in surviving subpopulations within particular replicates. By step 2,310, all replicates converge to zero energetic biomass, indicating complete population extinction.

The demographic collapse demonstrates substantial heterogeneity in persistence duration. Individual replicates exhibit divergent extinction trajectories, with some populations exhausting energy reserves by step 500 while others persist beyond step 2,000. This variation arises despite identical initial conditions (250 ungulates, 20 foragers) and reflects stochastic interactions between human hunting behavior—where females present lower food value (27) but reduced processing costs (3) compared to males—and fluctuating resource availability under strong seasonal forcing. The ultimate convergence to zero across all replicates indicates that under the specified parameter configuration, the combined pressures of anthropogenic hunting, environmental seasonality, and reproductive costs (birth spacing of 730 steps) generate deterministic extinction dynamics, though the timing of collapse remains probabilistic.
