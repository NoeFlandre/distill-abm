The simulation reveals a trajectory of increasing heterogeneity in agent choice function evaluations over the 32-period timeframe. At initialization (period 0), choice function scores exhibit perfect uniformity across the population with zero dispersion between the highest and lowest evaluations. This homogeneity reflects the initial market conditions of 1974, where dominant incumbent habits for whole milk and standardized health perceptions created uniform preference structures among agents.

A rapid phase of differentiation characterizes the early periods (0–7), during which the divergence between maximum and minimum choice function scores accelerates sharply, achieving the steepest growth rates observed in the entire series. By period 7, the trajectory undergoes its first inflection, transitioning from rapid expansion to a more moderated growth rate. This early fragmentation corresponds to the initial activation of disposition mechanisms, where heterogeneous network positions and varying interaction sequences begin decoupling agents' evaluation frameworks from their initial uniform state.

Between periods 7 and 28, the dispersion continues to increase but at substantially reduced and relatively stable increments, with growth rates approximately an order of magnitude slower than the initial phase. Secondary inflections at periods 12 and 28 mark subtle adjustments in the rate of divergence, potentially reflecting the temporal dynamics of habit formation cycles and social influence diffusion through the Watts-Strogatz network topology. Throughout this middle phase, periodic oscillations emerge around periods 6–8, 16–19, and 26–30, suggesting waves of reconsideration or social contagion that temporarily modulate the differentiation process.

By the terminal period (32), the mean percentage difference between extreme choice function scores reaches approximately 0.75, indicating substantial and persistent heterogeneity in how agents evaluate milk options. This end-state divergence implies that the population fragments into distinct preference clusters, with some agents maintaining strong incumbent evaluations while others develop markedly divergent scores for alternative milk varieties.

Interpretation within the behavioral framework suggests that the interplay between disposition triggers, habit persistence (operating at threshold 2.0), and moderate social susceptibility (0.2) generates sufficient path dependence to sustain heterogeneous evaluation profiles despite global social norms. The decreasing rate of divergence over time indicates that while initial consideration of alternatives spreads rapidly through the network, subsequent consolidation of distinct preference profiles occurs more gradually as agents' choice functions stabilize under the influence of accumulated habits and social reinforcement.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
Market segmentation emerges rapidly during the initial transition phase, requiring early differentiation of product positioning strategies. The sustained high heterogeneity (approximately 75%) at equilibrium suggests that uniform marketing approaches become increasingly ineffective after the initial periods, necessitating targeted interventions for distinct consumer segments. The periodic oscillations in divergence rates reveal strategic windows of opportunity where consumers may be more receptive to reassessment, particularly around the identified inflection periods.
