Average weekly whole milk consumption per agent exhibits a dramatic secular decline throughout the 1974–2005 simulation period, falling from approximately 2,655 units in 1974 to roughly 253 units by 2005—representing a reduction of nearly 90 percent. The decline unfolds unevenly across three distinct phases. During the initial phase, spanning approximately the first five to six years (1974–1980), consumption plummets steeply from the initial 2,655 units to roughly half that level, reflecting rapid initial shifts in agent dispositions as health and environmental perceptions begin to alter choice functions. This sharp early descent suggests that threshold-based or probability-based disposition mechanisms trigger substantial reconsideration of incumbent whole milk habits when new information enters the system. Following this initial drop, the middle phase (approximately 1980–1995) exhibits a more gradual, though consistently downward, trajectory punctuated by minor oscillations. Consumption drifts from roughly 1,300 units toward the 600–800 unit range. These fluctuations align with the model’s social influence mechanisms operating through the Watts-Strogatz network topology, wherein agents adjust choice functions based on peer behaviors, creating temporary stabilization points or slight reversals as social norms evolve incrementally rather than continuously. In the final phase (approximately 1995–2005), the decline decelerates but continues steadily toward the terminal value of 253 units. By this stage, whole milk has become a niche choice as alternative milk varieties dominate agent preferences. The persistent, though slower, decline reflects the durability of habit formation and cognitive dissonance thresholds—even as social norms strongly favor alternatives, some agents maintain incumbent consumption patterns until late-stage disposition triggers or network effects finally convert the remaining holdouts. For decision makers, these trends indicate that public health interventions targeting perception shifts can achieve rapid initial behavioral change, but sustained transitions require accounting for social network effects and the long time horizons needed to overcome entrenched habits. The extended tail of whole milk consumption suggests that policies should anticipate gradual rather than immediate complete adoption of alternative behaviors, necessitating persistent engagement strategies rather than one-time information campaigns.
