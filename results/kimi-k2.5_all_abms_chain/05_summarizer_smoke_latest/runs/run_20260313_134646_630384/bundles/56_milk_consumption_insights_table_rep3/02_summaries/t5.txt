Agents represent adult consumers positioned randomly within an information environment. Each starting with milk choices reflecting the 1974 market dominance of whole milk versus skimmed varieties. All 1,000 agents exist within a unidirectional social network based on the Watts-Strogatz topology. Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits a trajectory characterized by initial rapid acceleration followed by a prolonged period of oscillatory fluctuation. This early expansion reflects the initial influence of health perceptions and social network effects overcoming incumbent habits favoring whole milk. However, consumption increases sharply and significantly, reaching values exceeding 1,200 units by approximately step 11. Early-to-middle phases of behavioral transition may yield disproportionate impact compared to later periods. Early-stage oscillations suggest that milk choice behaviors respond to periodic cyclical factors—potentially seasonal consumption patterns or economic fluctuations—offering opportunities. The simulation depicts a substantial reduction in mean weekly whole milk consumption per person over the 1974–2005 period, declining from approximately 2,655 units at the outset in 1974 to roughly 253 units by 2005. The trajectory exhibits distinct phases of behavioral transition rather than a uniform linear decline. The 15-step oscillation period suggests that habit reinforcement operates through quasi-regular cycles. A change point at step 32 marks the maximum observed value, suggesting a saturation point or equilibrium in habit strength development by the conclusion of the 1974–2005 timeframe. Choice function scores exhibit perfect uniformity across the population with zero dispersion between the highest and lowest evaluations. This homogeneity reflects the initial market conditions of 1974, where dominant incumbent habits for whole milk and standardized health perceptions created uniform preference structures. The late period from steps 20 to 29 exhibits renewed acceleration, with growth rates increasing to 0.004 and subsequently to 0.019 per step, driving the final value to 0.7251. For decision makers, these dynamics indicate that health communication strategies face substantial structural barriers when competing against entrenched social norms and habitual consumption patterns.
