This agent-based model simulates the adoption behaviors of milk consumption among the British public from 1974 to 2005. It replicates individual preferences and decision factors underlying the historical transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decision-making follows a structured sequence involving perception of health and environmental choice characteristics, disposition triggering, scored choice functions weighted by habit strength and social influence. Average weekly consumption per person begins at approximately 5.3 units, representing the historical baseline of minimal alternative milk adoption in 1974. The metric tracking whole milk selection begins at a value of zero and accumulates monotonically throughout the 33 time steps of the simulation period. Whole milk consumption persists as a behavioral pattern across the entire timeframe of the British milk transition, though the dynamics of accumulation shift significantly over the decades. The simulation depicts a substantial reduction in mean weekly whole milk consumption per person over the 1974–2005 period. Each of the 1,000 agents makes one consumption choice annually throughout the 32-year simulation period. The model operationalizes decision-making as a regular, recurring process where every agent reevaluates their milk consumption preferences each year without fail. The simulation of milk consumption adoption reveals a trajectory beginning with minimal skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption at approximately 5.3 units per person weekly. During the initial time steps, consumption undergoes rapid growth, increasing substantially through the early period.
