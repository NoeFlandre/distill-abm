The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the implied health values of their milk choices exhibits a monotonic increase across all 32 time steps of the simulation period, rising from an initial value of 0.0 to approximately 0.73 by the final step, with the trajectory displaying three distinct phases of growth dynamics.

During the initial phase spanning steps 0 to 10, the deviation undergoes rapid acceleration, reaching approximately 0.5. This steep early ascent reflects the initial learning period in which agents begin receiving health information about alternative milk varieties through social networks and individual experience, rapidly updating their internal health valuations. However, the extremely high initial habit strength for whole milk (parameterized at 10.0) combined with active psychological justification mechanisms (justification = 0.61) enables consumers to maintain their incumbent choices despite growing awareness that skimmed and semi-skimmed options offer superior health benefits, creating a substantial and rapidly widening gap between what agents value and what they consume.

The second phase, encompassing steps 10 through 27, demonstrates markedly slower growth, with the deviation increasing gradually from approximately 0.5 to 0.6. This attenuated growth rate corresponds to the middle period of the historical transition where substantial market share begins shifting from whole to low-fat milk varieties. During this interval, the combined influence of social interaction (p-interact = 0.3), accumulating health information within the memory lifetime (5.0 steps), and cognitive dissonance effects (threshold = 0.15) facilitates partial behavioral adaptation that modestly narrows the rate at which the value-choice gap expands, though the gap continues to grow due to ongoing increases in held health valuations.

The third phase, occurring between steps 27 and 32, shows renewed acceleration as the deviation climbs from approximately 0.6 to 0.73. This late-stage surge corresponds to the historical crossover period where skimmed and semi-skimmed consumption surpasses whole milk consumption. The acceleration suggests that as social norms shift toward low-fat milk adoption (social-conformity = 0.28, norms = True) and health information becomes more prevalent in the network, agents' held health valuations continue to rise sharply. However, the persistence of social blindness (0.51), remaining habitual attachments, and the specific structural properties of the Watts-Strogatz network topology create conditions where behavioral choices increasingly lag behind burgeoning health consciousness in the final years of the transition.

Across all simulation runs, the tightening clustering of trajectories in later steps (coefficient of variation decreasing toward step 32) indicates robust consistency in the model's generation of growing value-behavior misalignment despite stochastic variation in agent interactions. The final deviation value of approximately 0.73 suggests that even after three decades of market transition, a substantial disconnect persists between consumers' health aspirations and their realized consumption patterns.

**Insights for Decision Makers:**

The persistent growth in health deviation throughout the simulation suggests that public health campaigns focused solely on increasing health awareness may inadvertently generate psychological discomfort without achieving proportional behavioral change, particularly when strong habitual mechanisms and social network structures perpetuate incumbent consumption patterns. The three-phase pattern indicates that early informational interventions (years 0-10) produce the largest value-behavior gaps, suggesting that health communication strategies should be paired simultaneously with habit-disruption interventions rather than deployed sequentially. Furthermore, the late-stage acceleration (steps 27-32) demonstrates that consumer health consciousness continues evolving even after market dominance has shifted, implying that policy interventions should anticipate and address residual whole milk consumption even during periods when alternatives have achieved majority market share. Decision makers should prioritize reducing the "social blindness" parameter and increasing actual interaction probabilities rather than merely increasing health information availability, as the bottleneck appears to be behavioral implementation rather than knowledge acquisition.
