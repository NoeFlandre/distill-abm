This early surge suggests an initial diffusion process where social learning behaviors quickly spread among the population, likely driven by interactions with the initially present E-RO-SL1 agents and early observations of neighbor performance regarding pasture management and livestock outcomes.

Following this initial period, the rate of adoption decelerates markedly, with inflections in the growth trajectory occurring around steps 7, 28, and 38. While the count of participating households continues to rise, the slope diminishes progressively through step 40—a key change point indicating a transition in system dynamics. This deceleration reflects a saturation effect as the pool of households susceptible to adopting social learning practices diminishes, while the remaining agents, characterized by heterogeneity in uncertainty management and fixed behavioral types, maintain their baseline or learning-by-doing strategies.

The metric reaches its maximum value of approximately 32 households by step 63, after which it stabilizes for the remainder of the simulation, concluding at roughly 32 households at step 100. This plateau suggests that under the current configuration—with strategy switching disabled—the population achieves an equilibrium where approximately 53% of the 60 total households maintain active social learning engagement. The stability of this value from step 63 onward indicates that the system has reached a persistent state of partial adoption, where the complement of learning-by-doing and baseline E-RO agents constitutes the remaining portion of the community.

Minor fluctuations are evident during the middle growth phase, particularly between steps 22 and 32, and again during the plateau phase around steps 51 to 59. These oscillations suggest ongoing dynamic micro-adjustments in household learning states, potentially reflecting temporary variations in r-parameter updates or neighbor observation outcomes, even after the aggregate population-level trend has stabilized.
