The metric measuring mean deviation between agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices exhibits a monotonic increase over the 32 time steps, following a distinct three-phase trajectory. 

**Phase 1: Rapid Escalation (Steps 0–10)**
The deviation accelerates sharply from approximately 0.0 at step 0 to roughly 0.52 by step 10. This steep initial rise reflects the rapid formation and dissemination of health perceptions regarding alternative milk varieties—starting from the initial mean perception of 1.69—while the majority of agents remain consumption-locked to whole milk due to the high initial habit strength of 10.0. During this period, agents increasingly recognize skimmed and semi-skimmed options as healthier choices, yet their actual consumption patterns remain anchored by habitual mechanisms (habit-threshold = 2.0), creating expanding cognitive dissonance.

**Phase 2: Decelerated Growth (Steps 10–26)**
Between steps 10 and 26, the trajectory shifts to a gradual, near-linear increase from approximately 0.52 to 0.60. This plateau phase represents a period of market transition where social influence mechanisms (operating at p-interact = 0.3 and social-susceptibility = 0.2) begin facilitating gradual adoption among network-connected agents. However, the persistent upward trend indicates that the rate of habit dissolution lags behind the continued evolution of health perceptions, sustaining a growing value-action gap across the population.

**Phase 3: Late-Stage Acceleration (Steps 26–32)**
From step 26 through step 32, the deviation exhibits renewed acceleration, climbing from approximately 0.60 to terminal values ranging between 0.70 and 0.75. This sharp uptick suggests that remaining whole milk consumers experience intensified psychological tension as health information accumulates in the social network (network-parameter = 8.0) and normative pressures activate (norms = True), yet their habitual barriers persist. The pronounced inflection around step 26 indicates a critical threshold where cumulative motivation to change approaches the breaking point for the final holdout segment.

Across all simulation runs, the trajectories display high consistency with minimal variance, suggesting robust emergence of this dissonance pattern under the specified parameter configuration of moderate social conformity (0.28) and high justification capability (0.61).

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The sustained increase in health deviation throughout the transition reveals that consumer psychological discomfort intensifies even as markets shift, creating a protracted period of cognitive dissonance. The middle plateau (steps 10–26) represents a critical intervention window where targeted behavioral nudges could accelerate transition, as the population already holds favorable health perceptions but remains habitually constrained. The late-stage acceleration suggests that the final consumer segments to convert may require intensive support, as they face the greatest misalignment between their evolving health values and entrenched consumption habits. Public health strategies should account for this growing deviation by providing not just information, but concrete habit-disruption mechanisms, as information alone appears insufficient to prevent escalating psychological tension during extended transition periods.
