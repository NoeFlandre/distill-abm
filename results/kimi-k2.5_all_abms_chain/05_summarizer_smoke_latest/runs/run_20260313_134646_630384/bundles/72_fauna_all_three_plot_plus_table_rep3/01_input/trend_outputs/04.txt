The cumulative number of female megaherbivores killed by human foragers begins at zero and undergoes a rapid accumulation phase during the initial 500 time steps, reaching approximately 100 to 120 individuals. This initial surge represents the period when forager bands encounter naive prey populations and exploit the readily available female animals, which offer favorable energetic returns relative to processing costs.

Following this rapid accumulation, the rate of increase decelerates markedly between steps 500 and 1500, with the cumulative count approaching an asymptotic value near 120 to 124 individuals. This deceleration indicates that the easily accessible female prey have been depleted, forcing foragers to expend greater effort to locate remaining animals or switch to alternative prey types. The plateauing dynamics suggest that the female segment of the megaherbivore population has been hunted to near-extinction or has declined below the threshold of viable encounter rates for forager bands.

Between approximately step 1500 and the simulation termination at step 2312, the cumulative kill count stabilizes around 123 individuals, with minimal net increase. Statistical analysis reveals a subtle oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of approximately 63 time steps superimposed on this plateau, likely reflecting seasonal variations in foraging success or reproductive cycles affecting female vulnerability. A slight decline in the mean cumulative count is observed in the final phase of the simulation, suggesting that some forager bands ceased hunting operations or that the remaining female population became effectively inaccessible.

The trajectory demonstrates high consistency across simulation runs during the initial accumulation phase, with increasing variability in the timing of plateau attainment reflecting stochastic differences in forager-prey encounters and landscape-scale depletion patterns. The ultimate convergence near 123 cumulative female kills represents approximately 98% of the estimated initial female population, indicating that sustained foraging pressure under these parameter configurations results in functional extirpation of female megaherbivores within approximately four years of simulated time.

For decision makers, these results indicate that megaherbivore populations face acute risk of gender-biased collapse when exposed to persistent human foraging pressure, with female animals—which are critical for population recovery through reproduction—being depleted rapidly within the first 1.4 years of contact. Conservation or management strategies would require intervention within this narrow initial window to prevent irreversible demographic collapse, as the model indicates no natural stabilization of hunting mortality at sustainable levels once forager bands are established in the landscape.
