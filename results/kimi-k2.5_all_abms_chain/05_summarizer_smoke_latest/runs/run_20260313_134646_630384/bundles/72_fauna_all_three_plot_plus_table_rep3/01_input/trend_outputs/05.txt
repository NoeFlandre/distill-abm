This early phase reflects intensive hunting targeting reproductive females, driven by the energetic profitability of nursing individuals (moderate food value combined with low processing costs) and high initial encounter rates within the grassland landscape.

Following the initial surge, the rate of increase decelerates markedly around step 625, with a significant transition toward saturation evident by step 850. The cumulative count stabilizes asymptotically near 117 individuals on average, reaching a maximum observed value of 119, while individual simulation trajectories plateau at values ranging from approximately 105 to 125. This saturation pattern indicates the progressive depletion of the postpartum female cohort or the collapse of the prey population's reproductive capacity under sustained predation pressure from immortal forager agents utilizing energy storage.

Substantial variability characterizes the temporal dynamics across stochastic realizations. Some trajectories reach maximum cumulative mortality exceeding 115 individuals by approximately 1,000 steps and remain static thereafter, while others exhibit continued gradual accumulation extending beyond 2,000 steps. This divergence reflects sensitivity to grassland productivity fluctuations governed by high seasonality (0.8), stochastic herd movement patterns, and the dynamic decisions of human foragers operating under optimal foraging theory.

Statistical evidence reveals distinct oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of 63 steps superimposed on the growth trajectory, particularly noticeable in later phases around steps 2,170 to 2,265. While early intervals demonstrate consistently increasing trends, the rate of accumulation plateaus significantly after step 1,160, with later windows showing no significant trend or weak decreases as the system approaches demographic equilibrium.

Implications for decision makers: The rapid saturation of postpartum female mortality within the first 500–850 time steps indicates that nursing females face disproportionate extinction risk under sustained hunting pressure, potentially triggering reproductive collapse or Allee effects in megaherbivore populations. Conservation interventions must prioritize protection of breeding females during initial phases of human-wildlife interaction, as the window for effective action narrows significantly once cumulative mortality exceeds approximately 100 individuals. The observed variability across simulations further suggests that landscape management strategies, such as maintaining refuge areas with stable vegetation, could extend population persistence timelines even under continuous hunting pressure by moderating the encounter rates between foragers and vulnerable postpartum herds.
