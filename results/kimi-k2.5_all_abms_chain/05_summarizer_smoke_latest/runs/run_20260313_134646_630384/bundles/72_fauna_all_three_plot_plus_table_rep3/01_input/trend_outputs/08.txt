The male ungulate population initiated at 125 individuals and followed a deterministic trajectory toward extinction across all 40 simulation replicates. An immediate and precipitous decline characterized the first 500 time steps, with aggregate measures decreasing rapidly from the initial carrying capacity. This early phase represents the period of highest predation efficiency, where the fixed cohort of 20 human foragers encountered abundant male prey exhibiting high nutritional value (30 energy units) relative to processing costs.

Change point analysis identifies step 500 as a critical transition where the rate of population decline attenuated significantly. Between steps 500 and 875, the system entered a prolonged period of low-density persistence, with populations fluctuating between 0 and 20 individuals across different runs. During this interval, the median population fell to approximately 1.3 individuals while the mean remained at 21, indicating positive skew driven by a few highly persistent simulation runs maintaining small remnant groups while the majority had already reached extinction.

A notable demographic anomaly emerged between steps 1392 and 1623, where aggregate statistical analysis detected a significant increasing trend (Mann-Kendall tau = 0.435, p < 0.001) in male numbers. This temporary reversal likely reflects adaptive foraging behavior, as hunters switched to alternative prey (females) once male availability declined below viable encounter rates, permitting remnant male populations brief demographic respite through relaxed predation pressure. However, this recovery proved ephemeral, as subsequent periods through step 2310 showed no significant directional trend, with populations stabilized at effectively zero.

Individual simulation trajectories diverged substantially during the terminal phase, with final extinction events occurring anywhere between steps 700 and 2284. This high variance in persistence time suggests that stochastic environmental factors including grass regrowth dynamics (3-tick recovery cycles), strong seasonal resource pulses (seasonality parameter 0.8), and random hunter movement within the 211×211 landscape created unpredictable survival windows for the final individuals, despite the deterministic outcome of total population collapse.

Oscillatory patterns with a dominant period of approximately 63 steps permeated the decline trajectory, corresponding to seasonal hunting cycles or vegetation recovery pulses that modulated predation pressure throughout the multi-year simulation.

**Insights for decision makers:** The inevitability of male population collapse across all runs indicates that the current configuration of 20 hunters with a foraging radius of 6 patches creates fundamentally unsustainable predation pressure. The brief statistical recovery observed in the 1392-1623 step window demonstrates that hunting effort rapidly redistributes to females once male densities drop, preventing population stabilization through prey switching. Conservation interventions must target the initial 500-step window when decline rates are steepest and deterministic; after step 875, stochastic extinction dynamics dominate, making outcomes unpredictable and largely irreversible. Reducing initial forager numbers, shortening foraging radius, or modifying the sexual dimorphism in nutritional value would be necessary to prevent the immediate overharvesting evident in the first phase of the trajectory.
