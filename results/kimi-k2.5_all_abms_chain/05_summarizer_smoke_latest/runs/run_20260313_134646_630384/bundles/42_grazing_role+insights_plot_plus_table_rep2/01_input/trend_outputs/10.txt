The number of households following the non-learning E-RO strategy exhibits high variability across simulation replicates, with aggregate trends showing an initial brief increase followed by sustained decline. Starting from 20 households at initialization, the mean population reaches a maximum of approximately 21 households around year 8, then enters a statistically significant decreasing phase from year 11 through year 65. During this period, the attrition follows a consistent downward trajectory with change points occurring around years 20 and 45 that mark transitions in the rate of decline. After year 65, the trend stabilizes, showing no significant directional movement through the remainder of the simulation, with the population fluctuating around 15 households. The minimum value of approximately 14.7 households occurs near year 92, concluding at 14.7 households by year 100.

The temporal average across the century maintains approximately 16.4 households, with values typically ranging between 14.7 and 21.1 and moderate variability around the trend (standard deviation of 1.9). The overall reduction represents a 25-27% decrease from initial levels, indicating that while non-learning households face higher exit rates, approximately 73-75% demonstrate long-term persistence. Individual simulation runs demonstrate substantially wider variation than the mean trajectory, with outcomes ranging from scenarios where non-learning households dominate the landscape to scenarios where they nearly vanish from the system.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The gradual attrition of non-learning households suggests that adaptive learning capabilities provide measurable survival advantages in variable rainfall environments, with the divergence between learning and non-learning strategies becoming pronounced within the first two decades. However, the persistence of a substantial resilient core of non-learning households (approximately three-quarters of the initial population) and the high variability across runs indicate that simple rule-of-thumb decision-making remains viable for certain participants, likely those with favorable spatial locations, risk dispositions, or initial land conditions. The stabilization of attrition rates after year 65 suggests that surviving non-learning households either occupy ecological niches that buffer them from environmental variability or have successfully adapted their stocking decisions through single-loop learning within their fixed behavioral type. For agricultural extension services, these findings imply that while promoting adaptive learning strategies may improve overall household persistence, blanket recommendations may be inappropriate, as some farmers can achieve long-term viability through traditional non-learning approaches under specific circumstances.
