The system exhibits an acute initial destocking crisis followed by rapid stabilization and long-term divergence across learning strategies. At initialization, destocking is absent, but by year 3, households face severe resource constraints necessitating the removal of approximately 6.6 animals per household on average, with some experiencing destocking pressures exceeding 7 animals. This reflects a fundamental mismatch between initial herd sizes of 90 animals and pasture carrying capacity under high rainfall variability.

Following this peak, destocking rates decline significantly through year 30. By year 9, average destocking falls to approximately 3.4 animals, and by year 30, it reaches roughly 0.3 animals. Statistical analysis confirms significant decreasing trends throughout this adjustment period, indicating active adaptation as households reduce herds to sustainable levels.

After year 30, the system undergoes a structural transition marked by stabilization at divergent levels. By year 100, average destocking stabilizes at 0.31 animals per household, but individual simulation runs separate into distinct performance bands: some households maintain minimal destocking rates below 0.2 animals, while others stabilize at intermediate levels between 0.5 and 1.0 animals. This persistent divergence suggests that different learning strategies—baseline rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—yield varying long-term resilience outcomes.

The system exhibits low-amplitude oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, reflecting multi-decadal cycles in vegetation productivity and rainfall variability. Short-term fluctuations continue throughout the simulation but with diminishing magnitude after the initial adjustment phase.

**Implications for decision makers:** The severe initial destocking spike indicates that starting herds of 90 animals significantly exceed sustainable carrying capacity, suggesting that initial stocking rate guidelines or early destocking interventions could prevent resource crises. The persistent performance gaps between learning strategies after year 30 demonstrate that adaptive learning mechanisms—whether through personal experience or social observation—produce better long-term outcomes than static decision rules. Policies supporting farmer-to-farmer learning networks and adaptive management training could reduce chronic destocking needs and improve pasture sustainability across the landscape.
