In the simulated private-property grazing system with heterogeneous learning behaviors, the mean household risk attitude (r-parameter) begins at approximately 0.0074 and follows a distinct three-phase trajectory over the 100-timestep period. During the initial phase from timestep 0 to 33, the metric increases rapidly and significantly, reaching a maximum value of 0.3814 around timestep 33, with individual simulation runs showing increases to values ranging between 0.6 and 0.8. This early acceleration reflects the initial social learning and adaptation processes among E-RO-SL1 agents as they adjust their uncertainty management strategies based on local information.

Following this peak, the system enters a decline phase from approximately timestep 33 to 65, during which the aggregate mean decreases significantly to around 0.19. Individual simulation trajectories diverge substantially during this period: approximately half stabilize at moderate-to-high risk attitude values (0.4 to 0.8), while others experience sharp decreases, with some declining to negative values as low as -0.4 by timestep 60. This divergence indicates the emergence of path-dependent outcomes where different simulation runs settle into distinct risk management regimes.

From timestep 65 onward, the system stabilizes into a steady-state phase with no significant trend, ending at a value of 0.1911. The long-term average across the full simulation is 0.2333 with a median of 0.2013, indicating that final values cluster in the low-to-moderate range despite substantial early volatility. The oscillation patterns between timesteps 20-35 and the stabilization of individual trajectories after timestep 60-70 suggest that learning-by-doing and social learning processes lead to persistent heterogeneity in risk attitudes rather than convergence toward uniform behavior.

For decision makers, these dynamics imply that smallholder grazing systems undergoing adaptive learning may experience an initial period of inflated risk tolerance that corrects over time, but policy interventions must account for the enduring divergence in household risk attitudes. The emergence of both highly risk-averse and risk-tolerant sub-populations suggests that blanket recommendations regarding livestock management will be less effective than targeted approaches that accommodate locally-evolved uncertainty management strategies.
