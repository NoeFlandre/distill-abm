The prey population initiated at an average of approximately 167 animals and declined continuously to complete extinction by step 2311. The trajectory exhibited three distinct phases: an initial rapid collapse, an extended period of low-density fluctuation, and final extinction.

During the first 357 steps, the population underwent steep decline with multiple inflection points indicating changing rates of decrease. By step 475, a key change point, the population had fallen below 50 animals in most simulation runs. The decline maintained statistically significant negative trends through step 1391, with rolling Mann-Kendall slopes moderating from -0.251 per step in the initial window (steps 0-231) to -0.001 per step by steps 1160-1391, yet remaining significantly decreasing throughout.

Between steps 500 and 1500, the population persisted at low densities with high volatility, exhibiting oscillatory dynamics with a dominant period of 63 steps. During this phase, the population fluctuated around a median of approximately 1.5 animals, with transient peaks reaching 11.4 animals at step 730 and 9.3 animals at step 709, separated by valleys dropping to 9.1 and 8.4 animals respectively. Additional minor peaks occurred at steps 803 (7.4 animals), 820 (6.9 animals), and 893 (4.6 animals), followed by progressively smaller oscillations below 3 animals after step 980.

Brief demographic recoveries emerged during intervals 1392-1623 and 1856-2087, where weak positive growth trends (tau = 0.203 and 0.445 respectively) temporarily interrupted the decline. However, these proved insufficient to alter the overall trajectory, as subsequent periods resumed significant downward trends. The population reached zero animals at step 2311, representing complete extinction across all 120 simulation runs.

This pattern indicates that constant hunting pressure from 20 immortal foragers operating under dynamic optimal foraging theory overwhelmed the prey's demographic capacity, which was constrained by a birth spacing of 730 steps (two years), senescence effects after 7300 steps (twenty years), and grass seasonality of 0.8. The oscillations at low density represent demographic stochasticity and potential seasonal effects interacting with density-dependent prey movement rather than recovery potential.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The simulation demonstrates that a forager density of 20 immortal hunters creates deterministic extinction pressure for prey populations of this life history type, even with initial populations of 250 individuals. Management interventions must either reduce hunter density, remove immortality constraints on hunters, or substantially increase prey fecundity (reduce birth spacing below 730 steps) or environmental carrying capacity to achieve sustainability. The absence of stable low-density equilibria below approximately 50 animals suggests that recovery efforts must intervene before populations reach critically low thresholds, as temporary increases at low densities (such as the peaks observed at steps 730 and 893) do not indicate viable recovery potential but rather represent demographic noise prior to final collapse. The 63-step oscillation period suggests that seasonal environmental effects may create brief windows of lower predation pressure, but these are insufficient to offset constant hunting mortality.
