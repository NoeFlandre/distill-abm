Between years 20 and 37, the rate of decrease slows considerably, eventually reaching a minimum average of approximately 41 animals per household around year 37. The most substantial gains occur between years 44 and 55, after which the growth rate attenuates progressively through the remainder of the century. By year 100, holdings stabilize at approximately 47.5 animals per household—substantially below the initial endowment but above the crisis minimum. These dynamics reveal that initial stocking levels exceeded the sustainable carrying capacity of the private pastures, triggering ecological degradation that necessitated severe destocking. The subsequent recovery demonstrates that learning-by-doing and social learning strategies enable farmers to adapt their management practices, gradually rebuilding herds once pasture conditions begin to improve. However, the failure to return to initial stocking levels suggests either permanent ecological degradation or the adoption of more conservative, risk-averse strategies that prioritize system stability over maximum productivity.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The trajectory indicates that initial stocking rates were approximately double the long-term sustainable carrying capacity, highlighting the critical need for conservative initial stocking guidelines or early warning systems to prevent severe destocking crises. The 60-year recovery period from the crisis minimum to stabilization underscores the long time horizons required for pasture rehabilitation under private management, suggesting that short-term productivity metrics may undermine long-term livelihood security. Finally, the partial recovery demonstrates that while experiential and social learning can prevent total system collapse, they cannot fully compensate for initial overstocking, implying that policy interventions should focus on preventing the degradation cycle rather than managing its aftermath.
