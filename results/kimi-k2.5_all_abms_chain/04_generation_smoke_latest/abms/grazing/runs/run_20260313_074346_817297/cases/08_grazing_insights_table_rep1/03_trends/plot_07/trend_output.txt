The distribution of healthy livestock across households begins with perfect equality, registering a Gini coefficient of zero at initialization when all 60 households possess identical livestock endowments. Over the subsequent 100 time steps, inequality exhibits a steady, statistically significant monotonic increase, concluding at approximately 0.073. This trajectory indicates that despite starting from homogeneous conditions, divergence in household livestock health outcomes emerges progressively within this private-property grazing system.

The temporal dynamics reveal distinct phases in the accumulation of inequality. During the initial 50 time steps, the Gini coefficient rises gradually, reaching approximately 0.04 by the midpoint. Around time step 51, the rate of inequality accumulation accelerates modestly, suggesting a shift in the underlying differentiation processes among the three behavioral types—baseline E-RO, learning-by-doing E-LBD, and social learning E-RO-SL1. A second inflection occurs near time step 71, after which the increase continues but with altered dynamics through the remainder of the simulation.

Throughout the latter half of the simulation, specifically between time steps 78 and 93, the inequality metric exhibits oscillatory behavior superimposed on the increasing trend, with fluctuations occurring around a dominant period of approximately 49 time steps. These oscillations suggest that while the overall direction favors increasing differentiation in household outcomes, temporary convergences and divergences occur as households adapt to ecological variability and neighbor performance.

In the context of heterogeneous learning behaviors operating within stationary, private-property patches, the emergence of inequality reflects the differential effectiveness of distinct adaptive strategies in maintaining herd health under varying climatic conditions. The relatively modest final inequality level (Gini < 0.08) indicates that while some households outperform others, the system avoids extreme concentration of livestock wealth, suggesting that the combination of learning mechanisms and private property rights moderates disparities even as differentiation inevitably emerges.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that in smallholder private-property grazing systems, some degree of inequality in livestock health outcomes is structurally inevitable despite equal initial endowments and access to learning mechanisms. However, the gradual and bounded nature of this inequality suggests that policy interventions supporting knowledge diffusion and adaptive management can prevent extreme disparities. The oscillatory patterns in later periods highlight the importance of designing flexible safety nets that account for cyclical fluctuations, as households may experience temporary setbacks even after achieving stable herd health, and inequality levels may vary significantly within short timeframes despite long-term stability.
