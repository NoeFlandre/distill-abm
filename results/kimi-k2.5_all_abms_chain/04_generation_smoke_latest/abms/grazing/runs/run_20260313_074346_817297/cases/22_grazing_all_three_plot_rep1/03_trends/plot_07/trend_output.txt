The Gini coefficient measuring inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households begins at approximately 0.000, indicating perfect equality in initial conditions where all households possess similar numbers of healthy animals. Over the first 20 time steps, the coefficient increases rapidly to values between 0.010 and 0.015, reflecting the emergence of initial disparities as households begin experiencing differential outcomes based on local pasture conditions and individual decision-making. Between time steps 20 and 50, the coefficient continues rising but with increasing divergence across simulation runs, reaching values ranging from approximately 0.030 to 0.050 by step 50. This divergence indicates that stochastic environmental factors, particularly rainfall variability with a standard deviation of 100 units, interact with the three distinct learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning) to produce increasingly heterogeneous outcomes across the farming population. From time step 50 to 100, the separation between simulation trajectories accelerates dramatically, with final Gini coefficients at step 100 spanning a wide range from approximately 0.030 to 0.095. This substantial divergence suggests path-dependent dynamics where early stochastic events and local pasture conditions create self-reinforcing cycles of advantage or disadvantage. The learning-by-doing and social learning strategies appear to amplify initial differences rather than converge toward equitable outcomes, as households with early success accumulate more experience or become models for neighbors, while struggling households fall further behind. The consistently upward trend across all simulations indicates that inequality in livestock health is an emergent property of this private pasture system under variable rainfall conditions, regardless of the specific learning strategy employed.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The systematic increase in inequality across all learning strategies suggests that policy interventions targeting equitable livestock health outcomes should focus on early-stage support mechanisms before disparities become entrenched. The wide variation in final inequality levels (0.030 to 0.095) indicates that stochastic environmental factors play a significant role in determining which households succeed, implying that risk-sharing mechanisms or insurance products could help buffer households against early adverse events that trigger downward spirals. Since social learning does not appear to reduce inequality compared to individual learning strategies, extension services should be cautious about assuming that information diffusion alone will lead to equitable outcomes; targeted support for struggling households may be necessary regardless of their access to neighbor knowledge. The persistence of inequality even with learning mechanisms suggests that structural factors such as initial pasture quality or location may be more determinative than adaptive capacity, warranting investigation into spatial targeting of soil and water conservation investments.
