Over the course of the 100-year simulation period, the cumulative number of destocked livestock exhibits a monotonically increasing pattern, beginning at zero and accumulating to approximately 96.8 livestock units by the final time step. The trajectory is characterized by three distinct phases of accumulation rates. During the initial decade (time steps 0–10), the system exhibits rapid accumulation with an average rate of approximately 4.96 livestock units per time step, reflecting immediate adjustment pressures from the initial livestock endowment of 90 units per household. This initial phase transitions around time step 25 into a moderating period (time steps 11–44) where the accumulation rate declines sequentially from approximately 1.47 to 0.36 livestock units per time step, indicating that households across all behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) are adapting their destocking strategies as learning processes stabilize. From time step 44 onward, the system enters a steady-state accumulation phase with a nearly constant rate of approximately 0.31 livestock units per time step, continuing through to time step 100. This stabilization suggests that in the private property context with stationary households, where spatial mobility is not available as an adaptation strategy, learning-by-doing and social learning processes eventually converge toward conservative destocking rates that balance immediate livelihood needs against long-term pasture sustainability. The median cumulative destocking of approximately 81.2 livestock units observed around the midpoint of the simulation (time steps 50–60) indicates that by the halfway point, households had typically destocked the equivalent of nearly their entire initial herd, reflecting the intensive management pressure characteristic of smallholder systems operating under climatic variability with a rainfall mean of 200 and standard deviation of 100.
