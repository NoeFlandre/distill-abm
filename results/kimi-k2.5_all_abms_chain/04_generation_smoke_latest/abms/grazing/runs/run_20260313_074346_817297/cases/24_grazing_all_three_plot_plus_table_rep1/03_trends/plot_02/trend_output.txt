The mean household risk attitude across the population begins at approximately zero (0.0074), indicating that initial stocking decisions align closely with formal recommendations. During the first thirty-three steps, the system undergoes a rapid and statistically significant increase, reaching a maximum value of 0.3814. This upward trend suggests that households initially adopt increasingly conservative strategies, potentially understocking their pastures as they encounter environmental variability or process early-warning signals from pasture conditions and neighbor observations.

Following this peak at step thirty-three, the trajectory reverses significantly through step sixty-five, declining to approximately 0.19. This correction phase indicates that learning mechanisms—whether through individual experience or social observation—enable households to recognize and moderate their initial over-conservatism, adjusting stocking rates back toward recommended levels while maintaining a positive safety margin.

After step sixty-five, the system stabilizes with no statistically significant trend through the remainder of the simulation, settling at a terminal value of 0.1911. This persistent positive deviation suggests that long-term learning outcomes converge on a moderately conservative equilibrium rather than returning to baseline recommendations or continuing to decline.

However, substantial heterogeneity characterizes individual household trajectories within this aggregate pattern. While the population mean stabilizes near 0.2, individual simulation runs exhibit terminal values spanning from highly conservative positions (0.8) to risk-seeking orientations (-0.4). This divergence emerges primarily between steps ten and forty, after which individual households largely maintain their relative risk positions with minor oscillations. Multiple inflection points between steps seven and ninety indicate that the learning process involves continuous micro-adjustments rather than simple linear progression, with notable oscillations occurring between steps twenty and thirty-five coinciding with the period of maximum strategic divergence.

For decision makers, these trends suggest that smallholder learning systems naturally produce an initial period of excessive caution that may temporarily reduce livestock productivity below optimal levels. The high variance in outcomes indicates that uniform extension recommendations may prove insufficient; instead, differentiated support is needed for households converging toward risky overstocking behaviors, while conservative households may require encouragement to utilize pasture capacity more fully. The persistent moderate conservatism in the population mean suggests that risk-averse behaviors become embedded in the social-ecological system, potentially providing resilience against extreme environmental shocks but also indicating systematic underutilization of grazing resources.
