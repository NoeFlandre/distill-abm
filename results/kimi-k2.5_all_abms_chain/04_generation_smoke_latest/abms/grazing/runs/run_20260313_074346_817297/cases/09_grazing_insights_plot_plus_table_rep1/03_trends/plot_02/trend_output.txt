During the 100-step simulation, mean household risk attitude exhibits a dynamic trajectory characterized by an initial rapid ascent, a mid-period peak and decline, and eventual stabilization at moderate levels, while underlying individual household trajectories diverge substantially into distinct high, medium, and low risk-attitude sub-populations.

In the initial adaptation phase spanning steps 0 through 33, aggregate risk attitude values increase significantly from a starting value of 0.0074 to a maximum value of 0.3814 reached at step 33. This ascent is statistically robust, with significant monotonic increasing trends detected across three consecutive rolling windows covering steps 0-10 (slope=0.018, p=0.008), 11-21 (slope=0.016, p<0.001), and 22-32 (slope=0.001, p=0.008). Inflection points at steps 7, 12, and 13 mark transitions in the rate of increase during this learning period. Secondary peaks occur at steps 25 (0.3757), 27 (0.3754), 29 (0.3793), and 37 (0.3802), indicating sustained high values around 0.37-0.38 throughout the late 20s and late 30s.

A pronounced reversal occurs during the correction phase from steps 33 to 65, wherein aggregate values decline significantly from the peak of 0.3814 to approximately 0.1913 by step 64. Rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms significant decreasing trends across windows 33-43 (slope=-0.010, p<0.001), 44-54 (slope=-0.005, p=0.001), and 55-65 (slope=-0.004, p<0.001), with inflection points at steps 39, 46, and 51 marking shifts in the rate of decline. A local peak of 0.2723 occurs at step 48 during this descent, followed by continued reduction.

The final stabilization phase from steps 66 to 100 shows no statistically significant trend across windows 66-76, 77-87, and 88-98 (all p>0.05), with values settling around the median of 0.2013 and ending at 0.1911. The overall mean across the entire simulation is 0.2333 with a standard deviation of 0.0936, indicating that final values hover slightly below the long-term average.

While aggregate metrics suggest a single peaked trajectory, individual household risk attitudes exhibit extreme heterogeneity, diverging markedly after approximately step 40. By the conclusion at step 100, household risk attitudes span a range from approximately -0.35 to +0.8. Distinct clusters emerge: one group maintains persistently high risk attitudes around 0.7-0.8, a second cluster stabilizes at intermediate levels of 0.2-0.6, and a third group declines to negative values around -0.3 to -0.4. This divergence reflects differential outcomes among the E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types, with some households maintaining aggressive uncertainty management while others adopt highly conservative or negative risk postures.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The initial 30-step period of rising risk tolerance suggests that households initially increase their appetite for risk as they learn and adapt to the private property grazing context, indicating that early intervention policies should account for this natural trend toward risk-taking. The subsequent correction phase (steps 30-60) represents a critical window where households adjust their strategies based on observed outcomes, suggesting that monitoring and support systems should be particularly active during this transition to prevent excessive risk accumulation. 

The long-term divergence into distinct risk-attitude clusters implies that uniform policy interventions become ineffective after step 60; instead, differentiated approaches are required to address the heterogeneous risk profiles that crystallize, ranging from highly conservative (negative values) to aggressive (0.8) strategies. Decision makers should recognize that risk attitudes stabilize relatively early in the simulation lifecycle, with the final 40 steps showing persistent stratification, indicating that early experiences and learning processes have lasting impacts on household decision-making behavior in grazing systems.
