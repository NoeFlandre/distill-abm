The population of households adhering to the non-learning E-RO behavioral strategy exhibits a complex trajectory characterized by an initial transient ascent followed by a sustained decline and eventual stabilization. At the outset, the system contains exactly 20 households employing this baseline strategy. During the first eight timesteps, this count rises to a maximum of approximately 21.1 households, indicating a brief net influx of agents adopting non-learning behaviors. This peak represents a temporary expansion beyond the initial allocation, suggesting early simulation dynamics favoring baseline uncertainty management approaches.

Following this apex at timestep 8, the E-RO population enters a prolonged contraction phase extending through approximately timestep 45. During this interval, the count decreases steadily through multiple regimes, crossing a significant change point near timestep 20 where the rate of decline intensifies, and continuing through a second change point near timestep 45. By this middle phase, the population has fallen to roughly 15–16 households. Statistical trend analysis confirms this decline is highly significant across consecutive windows from timesteps 11 through 65, with slopes indicating consistent net outflows from the E-RO category.

In the latter portion of the simulation (timesteps 45 to 100), the downward trajectory attenuates substantially. The series stabilizes, oscillating within a narrow range and reaching a minimum value of approximately 14.7 households near timestep 92 before concluding at 14.7. From timestep 66 onward, statistical tests reveal no significant directional trends, indicating the system has settled into a quasi-equilibrium where the E-RO population fluctuates mildly around a mean of roughly 15 households—approximately 75% of its initial size but substantially below its peak.

This trajectory suggests dynamic strategy mobility within the heterogeneous population, wherein households initially experiment with or temporarily revert to non-learning behaviors before systematically transitioning toward learning-capable strategies (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1). The incomplete convergence—stabilizing at roughly 15 rather than zero E-RO households—indicates persistent ecological or economic niches where baseline non-learning approaches remain viable despite the general competitiveness of learning behaviors. The distinct change points at timesteps 20 and 45 likely reflect threshold effects in the diffusion of learning behaviors or feedback mechanisms between vegetation conditions and household decision-making.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The gradual but incomplete abandonment of non-learning strategies suggests that while adaptive learning capabilities provide competitive advantages in private property grazing systems, baseline approaches retain viability for a substantial minority of households—roughly one-quarter of the E-RO population switches strategies while three-quarters persist. Policymakers should anticipate persistent behavioral heterogeneity rather than universal convergence toward learning behaviors, and should design interventions robust to this diversity. Additionally, the initial spike in E-RO adoption followed by decline warns against overinterpreting short-term behavioral trends; early adoption patterns may reverse as households gain experience with learning alternatives, indicating a need for patient, long-term evaluation of behavioral change programs.
