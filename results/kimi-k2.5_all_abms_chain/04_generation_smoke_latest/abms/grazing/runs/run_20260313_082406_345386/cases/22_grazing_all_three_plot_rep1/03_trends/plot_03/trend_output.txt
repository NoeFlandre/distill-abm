The mean reserve biomass begins at approximately 90,000 units at initialization. Over the initial 10 to 15 years, it declines rapidly, reaching a trough between 80,000 and 88,000 units around years 15 to 30. This initial degradation suggests that households, despite their varied learning strategies, initially maintain livestock levels that exceed the carrying capacity of their private pastures, leading to a drawdown of vegetation reserves as the system adjusts from its starting configuration.

Following this decline, a sharp recovery phase emerges between years 40 and 60, with biomass increasing steeply from approximately 85,000 units to levels between 110,000 and 125,000 units. This rapid accrual indicates that learning mechanisms—whether through personal experience (learning-by-doing) or observation of neighbors (social learning)—successfully trigger reductions in stocking rates or other adaptive management changes that allow vegetation recovery. The inflection point around year 40 to 50 marks the transition from resource depletion to regeneration, as accumulated experiential knowledge enables households to better align their decisions with ecological feedback.

After year 70, the trajectory stabilizes, though outcomes diverge considerably across simulation runs, with long-term equilibria ranging from approximately 111,000 to 128,000 units by year 100. This divergence, despite identical initial conditions and equal representation of behavioral types, indicates that stochastic elements—particularly the high annual rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200) and individual risk dispositions—generate path-dependent outcomes where historical sequences of weather events and idiosyncratic learning experiences create persistent differences in vegetation conditions. The highest equilibrium values suggest scenarios where effective learning coincided with favorable rainfall sequences, while lower plateaus indicate instances where conservative stocking decisions or adverse climatic conditions limited biomass accumulation.

The wide range of final outcomes carries significant implications for decision makers. First, the 10- to 15-year lag between implementation of learning strategies and measurable ecosystem recovery suggests that policy interventions in grazing systems require extended evaluation periods, as short-term assessments may capture only the initial degradation phase. Second, the substantial variance in long-term biomass levels—spanning roughly 17,000 units or approximately 15% of the mean—demonstrates that uniform policy approaches may yield heterogeneous results across similarly structured farming communities, necessitating flexible adaptive management frameworks that account for local context and historical contingencies. Finally, the fact that all learning strategies eventually achieve higher biomass than initial conditions (even accounting for variability) suggests that active experimentation and social observation contribute to improved resource outcomes, though the specific trajectory and final equilibrium remain sensitive to environmental stochasticity and individual risk preferences.
