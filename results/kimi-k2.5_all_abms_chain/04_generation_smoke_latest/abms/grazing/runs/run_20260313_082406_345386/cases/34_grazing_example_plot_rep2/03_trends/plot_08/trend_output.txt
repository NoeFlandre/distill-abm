The count of households with social learning begins at zero across all simulation runs at the initial time step. During the first 20 steps, this metric increases rapidly and steeply, with most trajectories rising from 0 to between 12 and 48 households engaged in social learning by step 20. This swift early diffusion suggests that social learning behaviors spread quickly through the household network as agents observe neighbor performance and update their r-parameters during the annual decision-making cycle.

Between steps 20 and 40, the rate of increase slows considerably, though continued incremental growth occurs. The trajectories exhibit a step-like pattern during this phase, indicating discrete annual adoption events where additional households switch to or activate social learning mechanisms at specific yearly intervals aligned with vegetation and household sub-model updates.

After approximately step 40, the majority of trajectories stabilize and plateau, maintaining constant values through step 100. Final equilibrium values vary substantially across simulation runs, ranging from approximately 18 to 54 households, representing between 30 and 90 percent of the total 60-household population. Some runs reach maximum saturation near 54 households by step 40, while others stabilize at intermediate levels such as 30, 36, 42, or 48 households, with the lowest equilibrium levels around 18 households.

The persistence of these plateaus without subsequent decline indicates that once households adopt social learning, they retain these behaviors throughout the remaining simulation period. The high variability in final saturation levels suggests that the diffusion and adoption of social learning is highly sensitive to stochastic interactions, initial conditions, and specific historical sequences of neighbor observations and strategy updates within the private property grazing context.
