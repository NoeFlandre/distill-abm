The simulation data exhibit substantial variability in the trajectory of E-RO household counts across repeated runs. All simulations initiate with exactly 20 E-RO households. Within the first 10-15 time steps, distinct divergence patterns emerge: approximately one-third of simulations show rapid growth, with counts increasing to 30-40 households by step 15 and maintaining elevated levels between 35-40 until approximately step 40, while the remaining simulations show declines to varying degrees, with some stabilizing between 15-25 households and others falling to 10 or fewer by step 20.

Between steps 20 and 50, the high-count cluster undergoes transitions, with most declining to the range of 23-28 households, while simulations with intermediate counts exhibit fluctuations but generally maintain their relative positions. The low-count group continues to decrease, with several simulations reaching 5 or fewer households by step 40 and at least one instance declining to zero by step 65.

From steps 60-100, most trajectories stabilize, though with continued minor oscillations of 2-3 households. By step 100, final outcomes span a wide distribution: several simulations stabilize at approximately 25-27 E-RO households, others at 16-19, some at 8-14, and a subset at or below 5 households. The data suggest clustering around three approximate equilibrium levels: high (24-27), medium (16-19), and low (0-14).

In the context of the RAGE extension model, these patterns indicate that non-learning behavior can either persist as a dominant strategy, coexist as a minority, or nearly disappear depending on stochastic variation and interaction dynamics with learning-capable households. The sharp divergence within the first 20 steps suggests that early learning outcomes and social network effects create strong path dependencies that lock in distinct long-term distributions of behavioral types.

For decision makers, these findings imply that efforts to promote adaptive learning in smallholder grazing systems should prepare for heterogeneous adoption outcomes rather than uniform transitions. The persistence of non-learning households in many scenarios suggests that traditional behaviors remain resilient, potentially requiring targeted incentives or structural changes to facilitate adoption of adaptive co-management practices. The strong path dependency observed indicates that timely support during initial implementation phases may be critical for influencing long-term behavioral distributions.
