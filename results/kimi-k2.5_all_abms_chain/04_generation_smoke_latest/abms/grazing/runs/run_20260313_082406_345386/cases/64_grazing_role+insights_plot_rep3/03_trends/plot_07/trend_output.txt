The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock begins at perfect equality (approximately 0.0) across all households at initialization, reflecting the uniform starting conditions where each of the 60 households begins with 90 animals. Over the 100-year simulation period, inequality consistently increases, though the trajectories diverge significantly across stochastic replications.

During the initial phase (steps 0-20), the metric rises rapidly to values between approximately 0.005 and 0.015, indicating the immediate onset of differentiation in livestock health outcomes as households begin responding to variable rainfall conditions (mean 200, standard deviation 100) and implementing their respective learning strategies. By step 40, values range from roughly 0.01 to 0.03, with some simulation runs showing steeper divergence than others.

A notable convergence occurs around steps 50-60, where many trajectories temporarily cluster between approximately 0.03 and 0.04, suggesting a transient synchronization of inequality levels despite different random rainfall histories. However, following this midpoint, the simulations diverge sharply into distinct stratification patterns during steps 60-100.

By the final timestep, the Gini coefficient spans a wide range from approximately 0.03 to 0.095 across different simulation runs. This indicates that under identical learning strategy configurations (equal representation of experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning types), inherent ecological stochasticity produces highly variable distributional outcomes. The upper bound approaches 0.10, representing substantial concentration of healthy livestock among a subset of households, while the lower bound suggests relatively more equitable distributions remained possible but increasingly unlikely.

The consistent upward trend across all trajectories demonstrates that inequality in livestock health is an emergent property of the system rather than an anomaly. The widening spread after step 60 indicates path-dependent accumulation of advantages or disadvantages, where early stochastic differences compound through the learning and decision-making processes. For decision makers, this implies that even with structured learning mechanisms and private property rights, rainfall variability inevitably generates growing inequality in productive assets over multi-decade timescales, requiring proactive equity-mitigation policies or risk-sharing mechanisms to counteract these divergent trajectories.
