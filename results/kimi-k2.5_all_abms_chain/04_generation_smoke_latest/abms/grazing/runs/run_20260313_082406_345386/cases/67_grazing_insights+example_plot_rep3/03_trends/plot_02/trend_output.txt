The mean household risk attitude initializes at approximately 0.5 across all simulation runs. During the first 20 steps, the metric undergoes rapid divergence, with trajectories spreading across the range of 0.0 to 0.8. By step 40, distinct clusters emerge: a high-risk-attitude cluster stabilizing between 0.6 and 0.8, a medium cluster ranging from 0.4 to 0.6, and a lower cluster between 0.2 and 0.4.

Between steps 40 and 60, a subset of trajectories experiences sharp declines, with some dropping precipitously from moderate positive values to negative territory, reaching as low as -0.4. This critical transition affects only a portion of the simulated households, while others maintain their previously established levels. From step 60 through the conclusion at step 100, the risk attitudes stabilize into persistent distinct profiles, maintaining high variance across the population with values spanning from approximately -0.35 to +0.78.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that learning mechanisms in grazing systems naturally produce heterogeneous risk strategies rather than converging toward uniform behavior. The emergence of strongly negative risk attitudes in certain households suggests potential vulnerability or maladaptation in specific behavioral types, warranting targeted support mechanisms. The stability of divergent attitudes after the initial transient phase implies that early interventions during the learning period may have lasting effects on household risk management strategies.
