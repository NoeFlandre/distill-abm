The average number of livestock destocked per household begins at zero and surges sharply to approximately 6.6 animals by the third year, reflecting an immediate ecological correction as the initial herd size of ninety animals exceeds the carrying capacity of private pastures under variable rainfall regimes. This initial spike represents the most severe liquidation event, after which destocking rates decline rapidly but non-monotonically over the following two decades.

By year nine, destocking falls to roughly 3.4 animals, with subsequent oscillations showing secondary peaks of approximately 2.7 animals at year twelve, 1.5 at year seventeen, and 1.4 at year nineteen. These diminishing waves suggest adaptive adjustments as the mixed population of baseline, learning-by-doing, and social-learning households recalibrates stocking densities in response to annual biomass fluctuations. The decline continues through year twenty-six, where destocking drops below one animal per household for the first time.

Between years thirty and one hundred, the system stabilizes at low levels with values converging toward approximately 0.3 animals by the terminal period. Throughout this latter phase, small-amplitude oscillations persist with a dominant periodicity of forty-nine years, likely tracking long-term rainfall cycles, while the overall trend flattens after year thirty, with brief periods of slight increase in the seventh decade followed by continued gradual decline.

This trajectory indicates that heterogeneous learning behaviors facilitate a rapid transition from initial overstocking to sustainable livestock management, with the sharpest ecological pressures concentrated in the establishment phase. The convergence toward minimal destocking suggests that private property rights combined with adaptive learning enable stationary households to maintain viable herds that track long-term pasture productivity despite significant climatic variability.

For decision makers, the pronounced initial destocking spike highlights the need for conservative stocking rates or temporary destocking support when establishing private grazing systems, as households require several years to learn local ecological constraints. The long-term stabilization demonstrates that promoting diverse learning strategies—including learning-by-doing and social learning—can effectively reduce livestock liquidations and build resilience in smallholder grazing systems facing uncertain rainfall.
