The mean reserve biomass begins the simulation at approximately 90,000 units. During the initial 5 steps, the metric exhibits minor fluctuations, peaking briefly around 90,500 to 91,000 before commencing a pronounced downward trajectory. From approximately step 5 through step 30, biomass declines steadily across all runs, reaching minimum values ranging between 80,000 and 88,000. The timing of this minimum varies slightly across stochastic realizations, generally occurring between steps 20 and 35.

Following this trough, the system enters a rapid recovery phase characterized by a sigmoidal increase from approximately step 40 through step 60. During this interval, biomass climbs steeply from the depleted minimum to levels between 110,000 and 125,000. After step 60, the growth rate decelerates markedly as the metric approaches equilibrium, stabilizing between steps 70 and 100 at final values spanning from approximately 111,000 to 128,000.

Across the repeated simulations, variance remains relatively constrained during the initial decline and minimum phases, with trajectories tracking closely together, but diverges substantially during the recovery and equilibrium phases. This divergence results in a spread of final biomass levels exceeding 15,000 units by the conclusion of the 100-step period.

The initial decline reflects the immediate grazing pressure exerted by the initial livestock populations (90 animals per household across 60 households) on vegetation resources before experiential and social learning mechanisms substantially modify farmer stocking decisions. The minimum represents a period of maximum vegetation stress where grazing pressure temporarily exceeds sustainable carrying capacity, depleting reserve biomass to its lowest point. The subsequent sigmoidal recovery indicates that adaptive learning strategies eventually reduce stocking rates sufficiently to allow vegetation regeneration, with the accelerating growth phase suggesting positive ecological feedback mechanisms—such as improved ground cover and soil moisture retention—once biomass exceeds critical recovery thresholds. The wide variation in final equilibrium values suggests that stochastic rainfall variability (with standard deviation set at 100 units around a mean of 200) generates multiple potential stable states; sequences of favorable or unfavorable precipitation during the critical recovery window (steps 40-70) lead to substantially divergent long-term pasture conditions despite identical initial parameters, learning strategies, and behavioral configurations.
