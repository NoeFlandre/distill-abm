The mean reserve biomass follows a distinct U-shaped trajectory characterized by an initial depletion phase, a critical minimum, and a sustained recovery culminating in high-level equilibrium. Beginning at 90,000 units, biomass experiences a transient peak near 90,523 at step 3 before declining sharply over the subsequent twenty-three steps. This early reduction reflects initial grazing pressure from livestock herds before adaptive management strategies effectively modulate resource extraction rates.\n\nThe system reaches a critical minimum of 84,114 units at step 26, representing the point of maximum vegetation stress where initial biomass stocks are most depleted. This trough coincides with the calibration period where agents with heterogeneous risk parameters are learning optimal grazing strategies through trial-and-error and social observation. Following this nadir, biomass enters a prolonged recovery phase that persists throughout the remainder of the simulation.\n\nBetween steps 26 and 48, biomass accumulates gradually with decelerating decline giving way to modest increases as learning-by-doing and social learning processes enable households to refine their grazing decisions. The recovery accelerates significantly after step 33, with the most rapid accumulation occurring between steps 44 and 54, coinciding with the critical transition point at step 50. During this interval, biomass increases at rates exceeding 1,900 units per step as optimized livestock management and vegetation regrowth dynamics generate positive feedbacks.\n\nAfter step 50, while biomass continues to increase monotonically toward higher equilibrium values, the rate of growth progressively diminishes. The slope of increase moderates from over 600 units per step during the 55–65 interval to approximately 20 units per step between steps 77 and 87, eventually slowing to roughly 5 units per step in the final periods. By step 100, biomass approaches an equilibrium value near 121,073 units, representing a 34% net increase from initial conditions and a 44% increase from the step 26 minimum.\n\nAcross the 30 simulation runs, variance remains relatively contained during the initial decline phase but exhibits considerable divergence during the recovery period (steps 40–70) before converging toward similar equilibrium values exceeding 120,000 units. This convergence suggests that despite initial heterogeneity in household risk attitudes and strategic choices, the adaptive learning mechanisms eventually guide the system toward consistent high-biomass outcomes regardless of specific behavioral composition (E-RO, E-LBD, or E-RO-SL1).\n\n**Implications for decision makers:**\nThe pronounced initial decline lasting approximately 26 steps indicates that new grazing management implementations inevitably face a 'valley of vulnerability' where pasture conditions deteriorate before improving. Policy support during this critical early phase—such as supplemental feed provision or temporary grazing restrictions—could prevent irreversible degradation before learning effects manifest. The subsequent rapid recovery demonstrates that adaptive learning capabilities are effective but require 40–50 steps to generate significant ecological benefits, emphasizing the necessity of long-term commitment to adaptive co-management frameworks rather than expecting immediate improvements. The final equilibrium level, substantially higher than initial conditions, validates investment in learning-based grazing strategies, though the 70+ step timeline to reach this state necessitates multi-year planning horizons. The convergence of outcomes across diverse behavioral types suggests that mixed populations of learning agents can reliably achieve sustainable biomass levels, supporting policies that encourage heterogeneous management approaches rather than mandating uniform strategies across all households.
