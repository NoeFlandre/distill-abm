The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households begins at effectively zero, indicating that all households start with nearly identical livestock health conditions. Over the course of the simulation, this inequality increases steadily and consistently across all scenarios, reaching an average value of approximately 0.073 by the final time step, with individual simulation runs ranging from roughly 0.03 to 0.095.

In the initial phase spanning approximately the first 20 time steps, inequality rises gradually from zero, with the first notable shift in the rate of increase occurring around time step 8. During this period, the different behavioral types—baseline E-RO, learning-by-doing E-LBD, and social learning E-RO-SL1—begin to exhibit slight differences in their ability to maintain healthy livestock, though the divergence remains modest.

Between time steps 20 and 60, the rate of increase in inequality becomes more pronounced, with inflection points in the trend occurring around steps 37 and 57. The spread between simulation runs widens considerably during this middle phase, suggesting that stochastic factors such as rainfall variability interact with the different learning mechanisms to produce increasingly divergent outcomes. By time step 51, a structural shift in the dynamics is evident, with the rate of divergence accelerating.

In the final phase from time step 60 to 100, inequality continues to rise, with another structural change occurring around step 71. The gap between the most and least successful households in terms of healthy livestock holdings reaches its maximum, with final Gini coefficients varying by a factor of more than three across different simulation runs. This late-stage divergence indicates that the compounding effects of learning capabilities—or the lack thereof—create persistent and growing disparities in household resilience and livestock management effectiveness over time.

The consistent upward trend across all windows of the Mann-Kendall analysis confirms that the increase in inequality is monotonic and statistically significant throughout the simulation, with no periods of decline or stabilization. This suggests that in this private property grazing system with heterogeneous learning behaviors, initial equality in livestock health gives way to structural inequality as households with more effective learning strategies accumulate advantages in maintaining healthy herds.
