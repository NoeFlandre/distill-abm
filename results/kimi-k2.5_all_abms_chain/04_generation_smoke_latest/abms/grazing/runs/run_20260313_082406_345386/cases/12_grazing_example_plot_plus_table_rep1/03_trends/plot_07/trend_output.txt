The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock commences at effectively zero, reflecting perfect equality in livestock health distribution across all 60 households at initialization. This initial condition represents the standardized starting state where each household possesses identical initial livestock endowments and operates under homogeneous behavioral parameters.  

Throughout the 100-step simulation, the metric exhibits a monotonic upward trajectory, terminating at approximately 0.073 across the aggregate mean of 30 simulation runs, though individual runs diverge substantially to reach values as high as 0.095 or as low as 0.030 by the final step. The progression follows a positively accelerating curve rather than linear growth, characterized by distinct phases of inequality generation.  

During the initial 20 steps, the coefficient increases gradually to roughly 0.01, displaying minimal variance across runs and multiple inflection points at steps 8, 13, and 15 that indicate initial volatility in the rate of divergence. This early phase suggests that heterogeneous learning strategies—encompassing baseline E-RO behavior, learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1)—have limited immediate impact on livestock health disparities as households begin adapting to private pasture conditions.  

Between steps 20 and 50, the rate of increase intensifies markedly, with values approaching 0.03 by the change point at step 51. This acceleration coincides with inflection points at steps 37 and 57, indicating periods where differences in household performance begin to compound significantly. The transition at step 71 marks a further shift toward rapid inequality generation, with values climbing steeply from approximately 0.05 to the terminal mean of 0.073 during the final 30 steps.  

Substantial heterogeneity emerges across simulation runs during the late phase (steps 71–100), with the spread between minimum and maximum outcomes widening to nearly 0.065 units. Oscillation indices detected between steps 78 and 93 suggest secondary fluctuations superimposed on the dominant upward trend, potentially corresponding to vegetation recovery cycles or periodic adjustments in social learning networks.  

The sustained increasing trend across all observational windows demonstrates that inequality in healthy livestock accumulates progressively without reversal throughout the simulation. By step 100, the considerable variance across runs indicates that minor initial variations in learning efficacy, risk attitude heterogeneity, and local ecological conditions on private property patches generate vastly divergent long-term outcomes for household livestock wealth, with some households maintaining significantly healthier herds than others despite starting from identical initial conditions.
