The average total livestock per household begins at 90 animals, representing the initial stocking level across the heterogeneous population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types. Over the first 20 time steps, the population undergoes a sharp decline, dropping rapidly as households destock in response to pasture constraints and the feedback between biomass availability and livestock carrying capacity. This initial phase reflects the pressure exerted on private pasture patches when stocking rates exceed sustainable levels, forcing households to reduce herd sizes aggressively to avoid complete degradation of their stationary patches.

The decline continues through approximately time step 37, reaching a minimum value of roughly 41 livestock per household. This nadir represents the point of maximum system stress, where the combination of vegetation dynamics and household decision-making under uncertainty has driven herd sizes to their lowest sustainable threshold. The trajectory suggests that without the mobility mechanisms present in common property systems, stationary households face severe constraints that necessitate dramatic herd reductions during the initial adaptation period.

Following this minimum, the system enters a transition phase between steps 37 and 44, where the rate of destocking slows and stabilizes. Subsequently, from step 44 onward through the remainder of the simulation, a gradual recovery phase emerges. The livestock population increases slowly but steadily, climbing from approximately 41 to about 47.5 by the final time step. Notably, the rate of recovery diminishes over time—initially rising more rapidly between steps 44 and 55, then flattening considerably in the later stages with only marginal gains per step. This asymptotic approach suggests that while learning mechanisms (both individual learning-by-doing and social learning) enable households to partially rebuild their herds and improve resource management, the system stabilizes at a new equilibrium substantially below the initial stocking rate.

The oscillatory dynamics with a dominant period of approximately 49 steps indicate cyclical adjustments in the social-ecological system, likely corresponding to vegetation recovery cycles or rainfall variability interacting with household adaptation strategies. The final value of roughly 48 livestock per household, while higher than the critical low point at step 37, remains significantly below the starting population, indicating that sustainable carrying capacity in this private property context with risk-heterogeneous agents settles at approximately half the initial stocking density.
