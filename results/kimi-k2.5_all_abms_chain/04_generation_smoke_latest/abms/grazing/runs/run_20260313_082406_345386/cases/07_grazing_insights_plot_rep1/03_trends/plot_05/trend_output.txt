The agent-based simulations consistently initialize with an average livestock holding of 90 animals per household. During the first 40 time steps, the system undergoes a severe contraction phase, with the metric declining rapidly to minimum values ranging between approximately 40 and 45 animals around steps 35 to 40, representing a reduction of roughly 50 to 55 percent from initial levels.

Following this period of minimum herd sizes, the trajectories diverge significantly across simulation runs. Between steps 40 and 80, approximately one-third of the simulations exhibit strong recovery dynamics, increasing steeply to reach steady-state values between 58 and 62 animals by step 100. Another subset demonstrates moderate recovery, stabilizing between 45 and 50 animals from approximately step 60 onward, while remaining simulations persist at minimal levels near 40 to 42 animals throughout the final 60 time steps. By step 80, most trajectories have stabilized into distinct equilibrium clusters, with final values at step 100 spanning a range of approximately 20 animals between the lowest and highest outcomes.

Implications for decision makers: The system exhibits high sensitivity to initial environmental stochasticity, with early shocks potentially locking households into low-livestock equilibrium states despite the presence of learning mechanisms. The wide variance in final outcomes indicates that adaptation strategies alone may be insufficient to guarantee recovery under adverse conditions, suggesting that targeted interventions during the critical recovery window (steps 40-60) could be necessary to prevent entrapment in low-productivity states. Conversely, the achievement of higher equilibrium levels (near 60 animals) in some simulations demonstrates the potential for substantial resilience when environmental conditions and learning processes align favorably.
