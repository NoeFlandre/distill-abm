The reserve biomass exhibits a pronounced U-shaped trajectory over the century-long simulation. Initially, the system experiences a sustained decline from approximately 90,000 units at the outset, reaching a minimum of roughly 84,000 units around year 26. This early reduction suggests that initial stocking levels and decision-making patterns exert substantial pressure on vegetation resources, depleting reserves below starting conditions despite the significant inter-annual rainfall variability characterizing the environment.

Following this nadir, the system undergoes a transition period between approximately years 22 and 32 where biomass stabilizes without clear directional trend, indicating a temporary equilibrium where vegetation pressure and recovery forces balance. Subsequently, from year 33 onward, reserve biomass embarks on a sustained recovery and growth phase that continues through the remainder of the simulation. This recovery accelerates notably between years 44 and 54, with biomass increasing at its fastest rate, before the growth velocity gradually decelerates in later decades while remaining positive. By the conclusion of the simulation at year 100, reserve biomass reaches approximately 121,000 units, substantially exceeding initial conditions by over 34%.

The oscillation patterns detected throughout the series, particularly concentrated around years 10-20 and 40-50, align with the substantial rainfall variability (coefficient of variation of 50%) built into the ecological parameters. However, the long-term trajectory indicates that the learning mechanisms employed by households—whether experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning—collectively enable adaptive stocking decisions that progressively alleviate grazing pressure and allow vegetation recovery.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The initial resource depletion phase suggests that early intervention programs may be necessary to prevent pasture degradation during the first 25 years as farmers calibrate to local conditions. However, the eventual overshoot of initial biomass levels demonstrates that adaptive learning strategies can achieve superior long-term ecological outcomes compared to static management approaches. The approximately 25-year lag before recovery initiates represents a critical vulnerability window requiring policy buffers or temporary stocking restrictions. The slowing growth rate toward the end of the simulation suggests the system approaches a new equilibrium under adapted management, indicating that sustained learning mechanisms eventually stabilize at higher biomass levels than initial conditions, potentially supporting larger sustainable herd sizes over time.
