**Interpretation and Implications:**

Given that 20 of the 60 households are initially designated as the social learning type (E-RO-SL1), the expansion to an average of 32 households (approximately 53% of the population) and maximum values exceeding 50 households in some simulation runs indicates that households assigned to other learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb and learning-by-doing) adopt social learning behaviors over time. The rapid initial spread suggests high contagion or attractiveness of social learning when uncertainty is prevalent and neighbor information provides valuable signals for stocking decisions. However, the saturation well below the full population of 60 households indicates persistent strategy heterogeneity, with approximately half the households maintaining reliance on individual experiential learning rather than social observation.

For decision makers and extension service providers, the critical window for promoting social learning occurs within the first 40 timesteps (years), during which adoption rates are highest and interventions can most effectively accelerate the spread of information-sharing practices. The long-term stabilization at roughly 32 households suggests that universal adoption of social learning is neither inevitable nor necessarily optimal, as nearly half the population maintains alternative learning strategies suited to their specific risk dispositions or local conditions. This persistent heterogeneity implies that supporting diverse learning approaches—rather than mandating uniform social learning—may better serve smallholder farming communities operating under variable rainfall conditions and incomplete information.
