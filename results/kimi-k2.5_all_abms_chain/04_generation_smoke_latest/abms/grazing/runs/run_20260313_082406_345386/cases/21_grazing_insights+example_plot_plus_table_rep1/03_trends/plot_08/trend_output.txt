The count of households engaging in social learning initiates at zero and experiences rapid growth during the first seven steps, reflecting the initial diffusion phase where early adopters begin employing social observation mechanisms within their knowledge radius. Between steps 7 and 38, the adoption rate continues to increase but decelerates progressively, with notable inflections in the growth curve occurring at steps 28, 30, 33, and 38. This deceleration indicates a transition from explosive early adoption to a saturation phase as the pool of households susceptible to social influence gradually exhausts and the system approaches equilibrium.

By step 40, identified as a critical change point, the growth rate diminishes to marginal levels, with the rolling trend slope declining precipitously from 1.430 in the initial window to approximately 0.029 by steps 44-54, and further to 0.008 by steps 55-65. The aggregate count reaches its maximum value of 32.07 households by step 63 and stabilizes at this level through the remainder of the simulation, ending at 32.07 at step 100. This plateau suggests that under the specified parameters, social learning penetrates approximately 53% of the total household population on average, with the system achieving a stable equilibrium state within the first two-thirds of the temporal sequence.

Considerable variability exists across the thirty simulation runs, with final adoption counts ranging from roughly eighteen to fifty-four households. This dispersion indicates that stochastic ecological and demographic factors—such as rainfall variability, initial herd configurations, and spatial arrangement of private property patches—generate significantly different diffusion trajectories even as aggregate behavior stabilizes at similar temporal benchmarks. The oscillation patterns observed between steps 22-32 and 51-59 further reveal step-wise adoption dynamics where brief periods of stagnation alternate with rapid uptake as localized learning clusters form and propagate through the stationary household network.

For decision makers, these patterns indicate that interventions designed to promote social learning in private grazing systems should prioritize the initial forty steps when adoption dynamics are most responsive, as the system becomes largely locked into its equilibrium state thereafter. The high variance across simulations suggests that social learning success depends heavily on initial conditions and stochastic environmental factors, implying that policymakers should prepare for heterogeneous adoption outcomes across different grazing communities even when structural parameters are identical. Additionally, the consistent plateau at roughly half the population suggests that complementary mechanisms beyond pure social observation may be necessary to achieve universal adoption of adaptive grazing strategies in smallholder contexts.
