The mean number of livestock destocked per household initiates at zero and immediately surges to a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals by step 3, indicating acute initial pressure to reduce herd sizes as households confront resource constraints on their private pasture patches. This peak represents the most severe destocking event across the simulation timeframe.

Following this initial spike, the metric undergoes a rapid and statistically significant decline through approximately step 30, characterized by a series of dampening oscillations. Secondary peaks occur at steps 9, 12, 17, 19, and 22 with progressively diminishing magnitudes—declining from roughly 3.4 to 1.4—and valleys interspersed at steps 8, 11, 16, 18, and 21, creating a sawtooth descent pattern. This phase reflects continued adjustment as heterogeneous households with varying risk parameters and learning capabilities (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 types) iteratively adapt their stocking strategies to observed pasture conditions.

At step 30, the system exhibits a structural change point, transitioning into a stabilized regime where destocking rates remain consistently below 1.0 for most simulation trajectories. During this extended equilibrium phase from steps 30 to 100, volatility diminishes substantially, though fluctuations persist with decreasing amplitude. While the majority of household cohorts converge toward minimal destocking values approaching zero, significant heterogeneity persists across the population throughout this period, with some cohorts maintaining destocking rates approximately three to four times higher than others through the terminal steps.

The final phase, particularly from steps 66 through 76, exhibits a slight but significant increasing trend in destocking rates before stabilizing again. By step 100, the mean destocking rate settles near 0.3 animals per household, indicating that learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms successfully reduced forced livestock removal to minimal levels after the initial crisis period. The trajectory suggests that stationary households managing private pastures initially destock aggressively to align livestock with carrying capacity, then gradually refine their strategies through adaptive management to maintain herds near sustainable levels, though a subset of households continues to experience higher vulnerability to resource shortages.
