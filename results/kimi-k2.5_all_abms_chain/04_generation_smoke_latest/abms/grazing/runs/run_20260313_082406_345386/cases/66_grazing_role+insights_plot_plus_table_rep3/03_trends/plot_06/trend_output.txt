The mean destocking of households exhibits a pronounced initial surge followed by a gradual decline toward a low-level equilibrium over the 100-year simulation horizon. This initial crisis phase, spanning approximately the first 30 years, features a series of declining oscillations with secondary peaks occurring at steps 9 (3.4 animals), 12 (2.7), 17 (1.5), and 19 (1.4), interspersed with troughs that progressively diminish in magnitude. Statistical analysis confirms significant decreasing trends throughout this early period, with change point detection identifying step 30 as the critical transition to a stabilized regime. The overall mean destocking across the entire time series stands at 0.96 animals, while the median value of 0.31 indicates that the system spends the majority of the simulation period at low destocking levels once the initial adjustment concludes.

Following the transition at step 30, destocking rates converge to minimal levels, averaging below 0.5 animals per household by step 40 and ultimately reaching 0.31 by step 100. While the dominant trajectory remains decreasing, the period between steps 66 and 76 exhibits a slight but statistically significant increasing trend, though absolute values remain negligible compared to the initial volatility. The variance of 2.05 and standard deviation of 1.43 reflect the high dispersion driven primarily by the early-phase volatility rather than late-period fluctuations.

Across the 30 simulation replications, substantial heterogeneity characterizes the early adaptation phase, with individual runs displaying varying amplitudes of destocking spikes between steps 0 and 30. Some trajectories show more pronounced oscillations and slower rates of decline, potentially corresponding to different learning strategy formulations (experiential rule-of-thumb versus learning-by-doing versus social learning) or stochastic variation in rainfall sequences. Despite these divergent initial trajectories, all runs stabilize toward similarly low values by the century's end, with terminal values clustering around 0.3 animals, suggesting convergence in long-term pasture management outcomes regardless of the specific adaptive pathway.

For decision makers, these dynamics reveal that private pasture systems face an inevitable adjustment period of approximately three decades when transitioning to stationary management regimes, during which significant livestock losses occur as farmers calibrate stocking rates to actual carrying capacity under uncertainty. However, the eventual stabilization at minimal destocking levels (roughly 0.3 animals by year 100) suggests that private property arrangements, combined with experiential and social learning mechanisms, facilitate successful long-term adaptation to highly variable rainfall conditions. The persistent low-level destocking even after stabilization indicates that complete elimination of climate-induced livestock losses remains unattainable, necessitating contingency planning for minor but recurring resource shortfalls rather than catastrophic events, and suggesting that policies should focus on supporting households through the initial 30-year transition period rather than attempting to eliminate all destocking shocks entirely. The sustained high variability across runs during the adjustment phase further implies that initial conditions and early learning experiences critically determine short-term livelihood outcomes, even when long-term equilibria converge.
