The data indicate a distinct three-phase pattern in mean livestock destocking across the 100-year simulation period. Initially, destocking remains negligible at step 0, then rises precipitously to peak values between approximately 7.0 and 7.5 livestock units per household by step 5-7. This represents the maximum resource stress period where insufficient pasture biomass forces households to remove substantial portions of their herds.

Following this peak, a sustained decline phase occurs from approximately step 7 through step 40. During this period, destocking rates decrease steeply initially, falling to values between 1.0 and 3.0 by step 20, then continuing a more gradual descent. By step 40, most trajectories have diminished to levels between 0 and 1.0 livestock units per household.

The final phase, spanning steps 40 to 100, exhibits stabilization with persistent low-level destocking. End-of-period values range from approximately 0 to 1.0 across different simulation runs, with the majority clustering between 0.2 and 0.8, though some trajectories approach zero while others maintain slightly elevated levels around 1.0. Throughout this stabilization phase, minor fluctuations occur but the overall magnitude remains consistently low relative to the initial peak.

Interpreted within the context of heterogeneous behavioral types operating on private property pastures, the pattern suggests that the mixed population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 agents faces severe initial resource constraints when starting with herd sizes of 90 animals, leading to mandatory herd reduction. Over time, learning-by-doing and social learning processes enable households to better align stocking rates with carrying capacity, reducing destocking needs. However, the variability in long-term outcomes across simulation runs—with some maintaining persistent low-level destocking while others approach zero—indicates that stochastic rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100) and heterogeneous risk attitudes continue to produce divergent household outcomes even after adaptation.
