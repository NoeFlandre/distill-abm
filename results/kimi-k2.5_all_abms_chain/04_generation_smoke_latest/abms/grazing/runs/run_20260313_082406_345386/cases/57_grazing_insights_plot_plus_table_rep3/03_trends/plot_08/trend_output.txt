The simulation of social learning adoption among 60 households begins with zero households engaged in social learning and exhibits a characteristic S-curve diffusion pattern over 100 time steps. The number of households practicing social learning increases to an average of approximately 32 households (53% of the population) by the conclusion of the simulation, with individual runs showing substantial heterogeneity in final adoption levels ranging from roughly 18 to 54 households.

The adoption trajectory proceeds through three distinct phases with markedly different growth rates. During the initial phase (time steps 0–20), social learning spreads rapidly with strong acceleration, increasing at approximately 1.4 households per time step in the earliest period (0–10) and maintaining a robust growth rate of about 1.1 households per time step through time step 21. An initial inflection occurs around time step 7 as the growth rate begins to moderate slightly, though the trend remains strongly increasing.

A transition phase emerges between approximately time steps 20 and 40, during which the adoption rate decelerates substantially to roughly 0.18 households per time step. This period is characterized by multiple inflection points at time steps 28, 30, 33, and 38, indicating step-wise adoption dynamics and temporary plateaus. A structural change point at time step 40 delineates the boundary between this transition and the subsequent saturation phase.

Following time step 40, the system enters a saturation phase where the adoption rate slows dramatically, declining to approximately 0.03 households per time step between time steps 44–54 and eventually to 0.008 by time steps 55–65. The aggregate mean series reaches its maximum value of 32.07 households at time step 63 and stabilizes at this level through the remainder of the simulation. Oscillation patterns detected around time steps 22–32 and 51–59 suggest periodic fluctuations or discrete adoption events in the underlying simulation dynamics.

The considerable variation across simulation runs—with some trajectories plateauing near 18 households while others reach 54—indicates that stochastic factors and initial conditions significantly influence the diffusion outcome. The gap between the median (31.9) and mean (27.4) values across the time series reflects the extended early period of low adoption pulling the mean downward, while the majority of later time steps cluster near the maximum value.

Implications for decision makers: The pronounced S-curve pattern demonstrates that the window for accelerating social learning diffusion is concentrated in the first 20–40 time steps, after which adoption becomes progressively slower and more difficult to stimulate. The high variability in final adoption levels (ranging from 30% to 90% of households) suggests that relying solely on organic diffusion may result in socially suboptimal coverage, potentially warranting targeted interventions to achieve universal adoption. The critical early momentum indicated by the steep initial slope suggests that resources should be prioritized toward establishing social learning mechanisms early in the process, as the marginal returns to such investments diminish substantially after approximately 40 time steps.
