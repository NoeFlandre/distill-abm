The cumulative number of destocked livestock exhibits a persistent increasing trend across the 100-year simulation period, beginning at zero and reaching an average of approximately 97 animals per household by year 100, with final values ranging from roughly 58 to 172 animals across different simulation realizations.

The accumulation follows a decelerating trajectory characterized by three distinct phases. During the initial phase (years 0-20), destocking accumulates rapidly with the steepest rates of increase—approximately 5 animals per year in the first decade, slowing to about 1.5 animals per year by year 20. This early period accounts for roughly 40-50% of total cumulative destocking in most realizations. A transition occurs around year 25, after which the middle phase (years 25-60) exhibits continued but markedly slower accumulation, with rates declining to approximately 0.3-0.4 animals per year. A second change point around year 60 initiates the late phase (years 60-100), where accumulation further decelerates to roughly 0.3 animals per year yet continues to rise without plateauing completely.

This pattern reflects the interaction between high inter-annual rainfall variability and the three learning strategies employed by households. The steep early destocking suggests that initial stocking levels (90 animals) exceed carrying capacity during dry years, forcing rapid adjustment regardless of learning type. The persistent but slowing accumulation throughout the century indicates that households continue to experience periodic destocking events as they adapt to stochastic rainfall, with learning-by-doing and social learning strategies potentially enabling more gradual adjustments compared to the baseline rule-of-thumb approach. The wide variation in final cumulative destocking values across realizations suggests that stochastic rainfall sequences generate substantially different household fates despite identical learning strategies, highlighting the role of chance in determining long-term outcomes in variable environments.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

These trends indicate that destocking is an ongoing necessity throughout the planning horizon rather than a one-time adjustment, requiring sustained institutional support for livestock offtake markets. The early rapid accumulation suggests that initial stocking recommendations should be conservative to avoid forced destocking, while the persistent late-period accumulation indicates that even experienced households require contingency planning for drought-induced destocking after decades of operation. The high variability across realizations underscores the importance of safety nets and insurance mechanisms that protect households from destocking costs during unfavorable rainfall sequences.
