Across the 100 time steps, simulations exhibit substantial divergence in average livestock deviation from recommended levels, with outcomes fragmenting into distinct stable regimes. During the first 20 time steps, rapid divergence occurs as most trajectories exhibit sharp increases in positive deviation, with values climbing to between 0.2 and 0.8.

Between steps 20 and 40, trajectories continue along distinct adjustment paths. Several simulations stabilize at high positive deviation levels (0.7-0.8), while others moderate to 0.4-0.6 or exhibit continued volatility with moderate fluctuations.

A critical bifurcation phase occurs between steps 40 and 60, where multiple trajectories that had maintained moderate positive deviations undergo steep transitions. Several runs drop precipitously from values around 0.2-0.4 to negative deviations reaching -0.3 to -0.4 by step 60, indicating shifts from overstocking to substantial understocking.

By the final phase (steps 60-100), most trajectories stabilize into persistent equilibrium states with minimal fluctuation. Terminal values at step 100 span a wide spectrum: a significant portion stabilize at high positive deviations of 0.6-0.8 (60-80% over recommended levels), another group stabilizes in the 0.2-0.5 range, some converge near zero (±0.1), and several runs settle at sustained negative deviations of -0.3 to -0.4 (30-40% below recommendations).

For decision makers, the persistent bifurcation into overstocking and understocking regimes suggests that uniform stocking recommendations are insufficient under adaptive learning dynamics. The prevalence of high positive deviations indicates a systemic tendency toward overstocking that requires active monitoring, while the subset of runs exhibiting severe understocking suggests that some learning trajectories lead to overly conservative strategies. Interventions should detect early trajectory divergence and implement corrective measures before systems lock into unsustainable stable states at either extreme.
