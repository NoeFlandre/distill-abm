Following this initial peak, the system enters a pronounced declining phase characterized by a sequence of diminishing local maxima at timesteps 9 and 12, with destocking levels falling to approximately 3.4 and 2.7 animals respectively. The trajectory exhibits a significant decreasing trend through timestep 30, with the rate of decline moderating from steep initial drops (slope of -0.38 in the first window) to more gradual reductions (slope of -0.06 by timestep 32). A structural change point at timestep 30 marks the transition from active decline to a stabilization phase where destocking rates remain low and exhibit no significant trend through approximately timestep 60. This plateau suggests convergence toward sustainable stocking strategies, with households having adapted their r-parameters (risk attitudes) to accommodate the stochastic rainfall regime (mean 200mm, standard deviation 100mm) and private property constraints. The system displays an oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of 49 timesteps, indicating multi-decadal cycles where destocking pressures ebb and flow in response to accumulated biomass dynamics and climatic variability.

Between timesteps 66 and 76, a modest increasing trend emerges, with destocking rates rising slightly before subsiding again. This late-stage fluctuation likely reflects transient ecological stress or stochastic rainfall events testing the resilience of established grazing strategies. By the conclusion of the simulation at timestep 100, destocking stabilizes at approximately 0.31 animals per household—near the median value observed across the 100-year horizon and substantially below the long-term mean of 0.96. This terminal low level indicates that the combination of stationary private property tenure, heterogeneous learning capabilities across the mixed population (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), and adaptive co-management processes ultimately enabled households to approximate equilibrium stocking rates that minimize forced animal removal despite persistent rainfall uncertainty.
