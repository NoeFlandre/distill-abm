The upper bound reflects scenarios of persistent destocking throughout the century-long simulation, where mean household destocking accumulates linearly at rates approaching 1.7 units per year, suggesting ongoing resource degradation that forces continuous herd reduction.  

Intermediate trajectories cluster between 70 and 130 by step 100, displaying concave patterns where the rate of destocking decelerates over time but never fully stabilizes. This suggests these configurations partially adapt to declining conditions but cannot fully arrest the need for ongoing divestment.  

The wide variance in outcomes—spanning nearly 115 units between the most and least intensive destocking scenarios—indicates that the specific learning mechanism employed (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning) profoundly influences long-term livestock retention and household viability. Decision-makers should note that strategic configurations exist that can limit cumulative destocking to around 60 units over the century, while suboptimal approaches may result in nearly three times that amount, representing a substantial difference in livelihood security and capital retention.
