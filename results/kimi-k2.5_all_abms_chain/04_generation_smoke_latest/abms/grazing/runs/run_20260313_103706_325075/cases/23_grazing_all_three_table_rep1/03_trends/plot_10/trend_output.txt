The population of households adhering to the experiential rule-of-thumb strategy begins at 20 and experiences a modest initial increase during the first decade, peaking at approximately 21 households around year 8. This initial rise may reflect temporary stability as households operate under favorable initial conditions before fully experiencing the consequences of their static decision-making approach in a variable environment.

Following this peak, the number of E-RO households undergoes a sharp decline between years 11 and 21, losing nearly one-third of the peak value by year 20. This steep reduction represents the onset of household failures as non-learning agents encounter environmental variability without the capacity to adjust stocking decisions in response to deteriorating pasture conditions. The inability to learn from feedback or observe neighbors renders these households particularly vulnerable during this early-to-middle phase, leading to rapid attrition.

The decline continues through year 45, though at a moderated rate between years 22 and 65, suggesting gradual attrition as the remaining E-RO households face continued pressure from rainfall variability and potential overgrazing. After approximately year 66, the number stabilizes near 14 to 15 households, maintaining this level through the end of the simulation period with minimal further change. This plateau indicates that a subset of rule-of-thumb households manages to persist, likely those experiencing locally favorable conditions that coincidentally align with their fixed behavioral parameters.

By the conclusion of the 100-year period, the number of non-learning households has fallen to approximately 14.7, representing an overall attrition of roughly one-quarter to one-third of the initial cohort. This persistent decline throughout the majority of the simulation suggests that static rule-of-thumb approaches are unsustainable for a significant portion of farmers facing stochastic environmental conditions.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that purely experiential strategies lacking active learning mechanisms pose substantial risks to long-term household persistence in variable grazing systems. The pronounced vulnerability of non-learning agents during the first two decades suggests that interventions promoting adaptive capacity—whether through individual learning-by-doing or social observation—may be critical for preventing early household exit. Policymakers should consider that without such adaptive mechanisms, a notable fraction of farming households employing rigid stocking rules may fail to persist over multi-decade timescales, potentially undermining rural community stability and land management outcomes.
