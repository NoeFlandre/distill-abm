Mean household risk attitude begins the simulation clustered within a narrow band between approximately -0.1 and +0.15, indicating relatively homogeneous initial dispositions toward uncertainty despite the parameter initialization. Within the first 20 time steps, households undergo rapid divergence as learning mechanisms activate, with values diverging sharply across the population. By step 20, distinct risk-attitude clusters emerge: approximately one-third of trajectories ascend rapidly to high-risk tolerance levels between 0.70 and 0.80, another segment stabilizes in a moderate range of 0.40 to 0.65, and the remainder either plateau at low positive values near 0.10 or begin declining toward negative risk attitudes.

Between steps 20 and 40, the high-risk cluster maintains stable positions near 0.75-0.80 with minimal fluctuation, suggesting early lock-in of risk-seeking behavior. The moderate cluster exhibits greater volatility during this period, with several trajectories peaking near 0.70 around step 25 before declining to approximately 0.45 by step 50. A notable discontinuity occurs between steps 40 and 55, where multiple household trajectories experience sharp crashes, plummeting from moderate positive values (0.20 to 0.40) to negative risk attitudes ranging from -0.10 down to -0.35. These crashes coincide with the period when ecological feedback from earlier stocking decisions would become apparent.

After step 60, nearly all household risk attitudes stabilize into steady-state values that persist through the remainder of the 100-step simulation. The final distribution at step 100 spans a wide range from approximately -0.35 to +0.78, indicating substantial and persistent heterogeneity in risk dispositions. The upper bound remains fixed near 0.78, while the lower bound stabilizes near -0.35. Very few trajectories exhibit values between 0.00 and 0.20 at the conclusion, suggesting a polarization outcome where households either maintain cautious negative attitudes or settled into moderate-to-high risk tolerance, with the middle range largely depopulated.

The rapid divergence within the first 20 steps followed by long-term stabilization implies that initial learning experiences create path-dependent risk dispositions that are resistant to subsequent modification. The emergence of negative risk attitudes in a subset of households suggests that adverse ecological or economic shocks during the establishment phase drive some agents toward excessively conservative decision-making frameworks. For policymakers and extension services, this pattern indicates that intervention windows are narrow and early; support provided during the initial 20-step adaptation period could substantially influence whether households develop sustainable moderate-risk attitudes versus extreme positions (either high-risk or ultra-conservative) that persist for the long term. The wide final dispersion also suggests that uniform stocking recommendations will be interpreted heterogeneously, with high-risk households systematically deviating upward from guidelines while negative-risk households potentially understock relative to optimal levels.
