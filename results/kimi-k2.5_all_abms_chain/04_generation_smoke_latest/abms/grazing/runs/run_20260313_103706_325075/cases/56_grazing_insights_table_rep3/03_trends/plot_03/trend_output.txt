Reserve biomass exhibits a pronounced U-shaped trajectory over the 100-year simulation period, characterized by an initial degradation phase, a transitional plateau, and subsequent sustained recovery that culminates in substantial accumulation beyond initial levels.

During the opening phase spanning timesteps 0 through 26, reserve biomass declines steadily from an initial value of approximately 90,000 units to a minimum of roughly 84,114 units at timestep 26. This early reduction indicates that initial livestock placement and grazing pressures initially exceed the regenerative capacity of the private property patches, depleting stored vegetation buffers. An inflection occurs around timestep 8, suggesting a shift in the rate of decline as household adaptation mechanisms begin partially offsetting continued pressure, though not yet reversing the overall depletion trend.

Between approximately timesteps 22 and 32, the system enters a transitional plateau where biomass levels stabilize near their nadir. This interval marks the turning point where the cumulative effects of learning-by-doing and social learning processes among the mixed population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types begin to balance grazing impacts, halting further degradation.

A sustained recovery commences around timestep 33 and continues through the simulation's conclusion. This growth phase features distinct acceleration and deceleration patterns: between timesteps 44 and 54, biomass accumulation reaches its maximum velocity, representing a period of rapid ecological improvement as adaptive management strategies diffuse through the heterogeneous stationary households. Following timestep 55, the rate of increase gradually decelerates—exhibiting a second inflection around timestep 48 where growth transitions from rapid acceleration to moderating gains—though remaining consistently positive through timestep 100. By the final timestep, reserve biomass reaches approximately 121,073 units, representing a substantial surplus above both the initial conditions and the minimum values observed during the degradation phase.

Superimposed upon this overall recovery trajectory, the system exhibits oscillatory dynamics with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 timesteps, particularly evident during the early transitional period (timesteps 10–20) and mid-recovery phase (timesteps 41–49), suggesting cyclical interactions between seasonal vegetation feedbacks and yearly household decision-making cycles.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The 26-year initial degradation period demonstrates that adaptive learning mechanisms in private property grazing systems require substantial time lags before yielding measurable ecological benefits, necessitating policy patience and potential safety net provisions during the transition phase. The eventual accumulation of reserve biomass approximately 34% above initial levels indicates that heterogeneous populations combining individual experiential learning and social observation can significantly enhance long-term pasture resilience compared to baseline conditions, provided managers endure the initial "adaptation debt." The nonlinear recovery pattern—particularly the rapid acceleration between years 44 and 54—suggests that social-ecological systems may exhibit tipping point dynamics where knowledge diffusion crosses critical thresholds, enabling accelerated restoration that could be targeted for monitoring and supportive intervention around the third decade of management implementation.
