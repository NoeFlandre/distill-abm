The average cumulative number of destocked livestock increases monotonically throughout the 100-timestep simulation, beginning at 0 and reaching approximately 96.8 animals per household by the final timestep. This trajectory reflects continuous livestock removal across the mixed population of 20 E-RO, 20 E-LBD, and 20 E-RO-SL1 agents operating under stationary private property conditions with global rainfall variability and an initial herd size of 90 animals.

The accumulation occurs in three distinct phases defined by markedly different rates. During the initial 25 timesteps, destocking accumulates most rapidly, with an average rate of approximately 4.96 animals per timestep in the first 10 timesteps, decelerating to roughly 1.47 animals per timestep between timesteps 11 and 21, and further to about 0.69 animals per timestep from timesteps 22 to 32. This early phase represents intensive adjustment from initial conditions as heterogeneous households engage in learning-by-doing and social learning processes to adapt to pasture conditions.

Following the change point at timestep 25, the system enters a transitional phase lasting until approximately timestep 60, where accumulation continues but at substantially reduced rates averaging between 0.32 and 0.36 animals per timestep. Multiple inflection points occur between timesteps 45 and 75, indicating gradual curvature changes in the accumulation trajectory as behavioral strategies stabilize and households optimize their uncertainty management through the r-parameter adjustments characteristic of the E-RO-SL1 and E-LBD behavioral types.

In the final phase from timestep 60 to 100, the accumulation stabilizes at a consistent rate of approximately 0.31 animals per timestep, converging toward the terminal value of 96.8. This sustained linear accumulation indicates that cumulative destocking eventually exceeds the initial herd size, implying complete herd turnover occurs over the simulation period with continued offtake thereafter.

For decision makers, these trends demonstrate that adaptive co-management through learning-based behavioral strategies generates substantial cumulative livestock offtake in smallholder private property systems. The high turnover rate, with cumulative destocking exceeding initial endowments, necessitates robust policies for livestock replacement, financial liquidity for restocking, and pasture regeneration to maintain long-term livelihood resilience. The deceleration of destocking rates over time suggests that learning mechanisms enable optimization of stocking strategies, but the persistent accumulation throughout all 100 timesteps indicates that destocking represents a permanent management feature rather than a transient crisis response, underscoring the critical importance of balancing immediate offtake with long-term pasture sustainability.
