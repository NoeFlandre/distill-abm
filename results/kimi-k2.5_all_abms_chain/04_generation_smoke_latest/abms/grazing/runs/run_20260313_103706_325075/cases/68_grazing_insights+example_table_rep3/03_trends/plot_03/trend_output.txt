The average reserve biomass across the grazing system initiates at approximately 90,000 units and declines significantly over the first 26 time steps, reaching a minimum value of roughly 84,100 units. This initial depletion likely stems from immediate grazing pressures exerted by the established livestock populations before households fully adapt their management strategies to local conditions, representing a transient overutilization phase common in newly initialized systems.

Subsequently, the reserve biomass undergoes a sustained recovery and growth phase lasting from time step 26 through the conclusion of the simulation. The recovery initially accelerates, with particularly rapid accumulation occurring between approximately time steps 33 and 54, before the rate of increase progressively diminishes through time steps 55 to 100. By time step 100, reserve biomass reaches approximately 121,000 units, marking a substantial improvement of roughly 34 percent above initial levels. This trajectory indicates that learning processes—encompassing both individual learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms—enable households to progressively refine their grazing decisions, facilitating vegetation regeneration that exceeds baseline ecological conditions.

The transition from decline to growth around time step 26 suggests a critical threshold where adaptive management begins to outweigh initial resource depletion pressures. The decelerating growth rates observed in the final third of the simulation imply the system is asymptotically approaching a new equilibrium or ecological carrying capacity, where continued biomass accumulation becomes increasingly constrained despite ongoing behavioral adaptation.

For decision makers, these trends demonstrate that private property grazing systems experiencing initial resource degradation can achieve significant ecological recovery through learning-based adaptive management. However, the plateauing growth suggests inherent limits to biomass productivity under current climatic and demographic settings, indicating that sustainable long-term management may require additional interventions—such as controlled stocking rates or pasture enhancement—once learning-optimized carrying capacity is reached.
