The number of households engaged in social learning begins at zero and increases throughout the 100-step simulation period, reaching an aggregate mean of approximately 32 households by the terminal step. Individual simulation trajectories, however, exhibit substantial heterogeneity, with final counts ranging from roughly 18 to 54 households, indicating that stochastic or initial condition variations significantly influence adoption patterns across different realizations.

The most pronounced increase occurs during the initial phase between steps 0 and 20, where the aggregate count rises steeply with a Mann-Kendall slope of 1.430. This rapid early adoption suggests that once triggering conditions emerge within the grazing system, social learning mechanisms diffuse quickly through the farmer network. Notable inflection points at steps 7, 28, 30, 33, and 38 indicate discrete waves or cohorts of households adopting social learning behaviors during this transitional period.

Between approximately steps 20 and 60, the rate of increase moderates considerably while remaining statistically significant, with sequential rolling window slopes declining from 1.139 to 0.183 and further to 0.029. A significant change point at step 40 marks a structural shift in the diffusion dynamics, after which the marginal rate of new adoption diminishes substantially. The aggregate series reaches its maximum value of approximately 32.07 at step 63, with the final observation window (steps 55-65) showing a reduced slope of 0.008, indicating the system approaches saturation.

From step 60 onward, most individual trajectories stabilize at plateaus, though some continue gradual increases until step 100. The data exhibits a dominant oscillation period of approximately 49 steps, with clustering of oscillation indices between steps 22-32 and 51-60, suggesting cyclical patterns in the timing of adoption decisions that may correspond to seasonal or inter-annual rainfall variability (rain-mean 200, rain-std 100) affecting pasture conditions and consequent learning opportunities.

**Implications for decision makers:** The S-curve pattern of adoption—with an initial lag, rapid growth phase, and eventual saturation—suggests that extension programs promoting social learning in grazing systems should concentrate resources during the early simulation period (first 40 steps) when adoption rates are most malleable. The considerable variation across runs, with some simulations failing to achieve widespread social learning despite identical initial parameters, indicates that relying solely on endogenous neighbor-to-neighbor diffusion may leave 30-70% of farming households (approximately 18-42 of 60 households) unengaged. Given the knowledge-radius constraint of 1 patch, spatial proximity limitations appear to create structural barriers to full adoption, suggesting that complementary extension approaches targeting isolated or peripheral households remain necessary even after 100 time steps to achieve universal coverage of sustainable grazing management practices.
