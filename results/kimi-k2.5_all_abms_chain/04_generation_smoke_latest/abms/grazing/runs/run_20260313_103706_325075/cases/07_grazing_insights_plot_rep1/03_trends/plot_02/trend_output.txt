The metric measures the average deviation from recommended livestock numbers across extension agents, with positive values indicating risk-seeking behavior (stocking above recommendations) and negative values indicating risk-averse behavior (stocking below recommendations).

**Temporal Dynamics:**

**Initial Phase (Steps 0-5):** All households begin with deviations clustered narrowly between approximately -0.1 and +0.15, indicating near-adherence to recommendations at the outset.

**Rapid Divergence (Steps 5-25):** Households experience swift differentiation in risk attitudes. By step 25, values spread across nearly the full eventual range, with approximately 30% of households reaching high positive deviations (0.6-0.8), 40% attaining moderate positive deviations (0.3-0.6), and the remainder distributed between 0.0 and 0.3. Several households begin declining toward negative values during this period.

**Stabilization and Adjustment (Steps 25-45):** A distinct bifurcation emerges. Approximately one-quarter of households stabilize at high deviation levels (0.75-0.8), maintaining aggressive stocking strategies. Another group stabilizes in the 0.5-0.7 range. Meanwhile, roughly 15% of households that had reached 0.3-0.5 begin downward trajectories.

**Critical Transition Period (Steps 45-60):** This phase features abrupt strategy reversals for multiple households. Several agents exhibiting previously high positive deviations (0.7-0.8) crash to strongly negative values (approximately -0.35). Others fall from moderate positive levels (0.4-0.5) to negative deviations (-0.3 to -0.15). These transitions occur rapidly, typically over 5-10 step intervals, suggesting threshold effects in learning or feedback mechanisms.

**Long-Term Equilibrium (Steps 60-100):** After step 60, volatility diminishes substantially and values stabilize into persistent steady states. The final distribution at step 100 spans from approximately -0.35 to +0.78, with five discernible clusters: (1) high-risk stable households maintaining 0.75-0.8 deviations (approximately 15-20% of households), (2) medium-high stable at 0.6-0.7 (roughly 25%), (3) moderate stable at 0.4-0.55 (approximately 20%), (4) low positive stable at 0.1-0.35 (roughly 25%), and (5) negative deviation households at -0.35 to -0.1 (approximately 10-15%).

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The emergence of persistent heterogeneity in risk attitudes, even after 100 steps with identical initial conditions and shared learning mechanisms, suggests that uniform policy prescriptions may be ineffective for grazing system management. The mid-simulation crashes demonstrate that households can and do radically revise strategies based on accumulated experience, particularly shifting from aggressive to conservative stocking when feedback indicates unsustainability. The presence of households maintaining high positive deviations (0.8) alongside others with negative deviations (-0.35) indicates that a one-size-fits-all approach to livestock recommendations will face systematic non-compliance from substantial population segments. Interventions should accommodate diverse risk-management strategies rather than attempting to enforce uniform adherence to recommendations, and monitoring systems should particularly watch for the threshold dynamics observed around steps 45-60 that signal major strategy shifts.
