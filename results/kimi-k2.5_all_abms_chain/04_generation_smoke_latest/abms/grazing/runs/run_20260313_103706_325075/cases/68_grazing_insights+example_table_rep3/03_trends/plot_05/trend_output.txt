The average livestock holdings per household, inclusive of destocked animals, begins at 90 units and undergoes a pronounced reduction during the initial phase of the simulation. A sharp decline occurs over the first 10 to 20 time steps, with the rate of decrease moderating slightly after approximately step 3. This initial contraction reflects the adjustment from the initial stocking density toward levels more commensurate with the pasture's carrying capacity, as heterogeneous households employing baseline optimization, learning-by-doing, and social learning strategies encounter ecological constraints and engage in destocking.

The downward trajectory continues with diminishing intensity through approximately step 37, where livestock holdings reach a minimum of roughly 41 units per household. This nadir represents the point of maximum contraction, likely corresponding to the period where initial overgrazing pressures peak and biomass reserves are most depleted across the private property patches.

Following this minimum, the system enters a recovery phase beginning around step 46. Livestock numbers increase steadily from approximately step 44 through step 100, though the rate of increase decelerates progressively over time. The early recovery (steps 44–54) exhibits the strongest growth, which gradually tapers to very slow increments by the final stages. By step 100, holdings stabilize near 47.5 units per household, considerably below the initial value but approximately 16 percent above the minimum threshold observed at step 37.

This trajectory suggests that while the mixed learning behaviors facilitate adaptation toward sustainable herd sizes following an initial overshoot, the system requires an extended period to rebuild livestock numbers after the initial destocking shock. The oscillatory tendencies with a periodicity of approximately 49 steps indicate potential cyclic adjustments around the carrying capacity as the different behavioral types interact and compete within the stationary household context.

For decision makers, the trajectory indicates that initial stocking rates of 90 units per household significantly exceed sustainable limits under the specified climatic variability, necessitating substantial destocking to avoid degradation. The incomplete recovery within the 100-step timeframe suggests that conservative initial stocking rates around 45–50 units would prevent costly boom-and-bust cycles and reduce the recovery burden on smallholder households, while the demonstrated recovery capacity highlights the value of promoting diverse learning mechanisms in grazing communities.
