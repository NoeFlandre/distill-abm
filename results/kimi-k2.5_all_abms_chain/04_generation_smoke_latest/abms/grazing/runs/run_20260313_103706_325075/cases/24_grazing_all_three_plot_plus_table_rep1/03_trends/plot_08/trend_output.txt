The diffusion of social learning among households initiates from zero and undergoes rapid acceleration within the first 7 steps, reaching approximately 20 households on average by step 20. Growth rates decelerate substantially after step 28, with notable inflection points at steps 30, 33, and 38 marking progressively slower adoption as the system approaches equilibrium. A structural shift occurs near step 40, transitioning from active diffusion to stabilization, with the aggregate mean peaking at approximately 32 households by step 63 and maintaining this level through step 100.

However, individual simulation trajectories exhibit substantial divergence rather than convergence. While the mean stabilizes around 32 households (53% of the population), actual outcomes range widely from 18 to 54 households (30% to 90% adoption) by the conclusion of the simulation. Distinct clusters emerge: some trajectories plateau near 18 households, others between 30 and 42, and the remainder achieve near-complete adoption at 48 to 54 households. Oscillations between steps 51 and 59 indicate temporary fluctuations in engagement as households potentially reassess the utility of social information against private experience.

Implications for decision makers:
The persistent variability in final adoption rates, despite identical starting conditions, indicates that social learning diffusion is highly sensitive to stochastic early-phase factors and path-dependent dynamics. Agricultural extension programs should not assume uniform adoption; instead, they should prepare for scenarios where 30% to 90% of farmers may ultimately engage with social learning. The critical intervention window occurs within the first 20 steps, after which adoption trajectories become largely fixed. To achieve universal or near-universal adoption, complementary targeted interventions may be necessary after step 40 to engage the 40-50% of households who do not adopt through spontaneous peer diffusion alone.
