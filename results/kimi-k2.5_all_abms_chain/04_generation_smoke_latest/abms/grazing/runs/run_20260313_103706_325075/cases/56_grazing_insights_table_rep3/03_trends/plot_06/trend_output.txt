The dynamics of livestock destocking exhibit an initial shock followed by gradual stabilization with late-stage fluctuations. At initialization (time step 0), no destocking occurs, but this rapidly escalates to a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals per household by time step 3, representing the peak destocking event. Following this initial spike, the system enters a prolonged decline phase. Between time steps 0–10, 11–21, and 22–32, statistically significant decreasing trends are observed, with values falling from the initial peak toward stabilization around 0.3–0.9 animals per household by time step 30.

A change point occurs around time step 30, after which the dynamics shift to a lower-magnitude oscillatory pattern. Between time steps 33–43 and 55–65, no significant directional trends emerge, indicating relative stability with values fluctuating between approximately 0.5 and 1.0 animals per household. However, from time steps 44–54, another significant decreasing trend appears, pushing values down to approximately 0.3 by time step 54.

In the later stages (time steps 66–76), a significant increasing trend emerges, with destocking rising from near 0.3 to approximately 0.6–0.8 animals per household, suggesting renewed pressure. By the final time step (100), the system settles at approximately 0.31 animals per household, with a dominant oscillation period of approximately 49 time steps indicating cyclical patterns in resource stress.

These dynamics reflect the adaptation processes of a heterogeneous population comprising 60 households with equal representation of three behavioral types—E-RO (baseline), E-LBD (learning-by-doing), and E-RO-SL1 (social learning)—operating in a private property grazing system with high rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100). The initial peak at time step 3 likely represents an adjustment period where households, starting with 90 livestock units each, face immediate resource constraints before adaptive mechanisms activate. The subsequent decline through time step 30 suggests successful adaptation as learning processes enable households to better match stocking rates with pasture productivity.

The stabilization phase between time steps 30–65 indicates that the mixed behavioral types reach a temporary equilibrium in their grazing strategies, with destocking events becoming less frequent but persistent. The secondary decline around time step 54 may reflect favorable climatic variations that temporarily reduce resource pressure. The late-stage increase (time steps 66–76) potentially signals long-term pasture degradation or competitive dynamics between different learning types, where divergent efficiencies lead to renewed resource stress for less adaptive households. The final value of approximately 0.31 suggests that despite adaptation mechanisms, a baseline level of destocking persists due to climatic variability and the inherent uncertainty (initial risk attitude 0.5) in household decision-making.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

For policymakers and development practitioners, these results suggest that learning-based adaptation strategies significantly reduce but cannot eliminate livestock destocking in variable climates. The initial three time steps represent a critical vulnerability window where households face high destocking risk before learning mechanisms take effect, suggesting need for targeted safety nets during early adoption phases. The long-term oscillatory pattern with a ~49-time-step cycle indicates that destocking events follow multi-year patterns linked to climatic or vegetation recovery cycles, allowing for predictive risk management. The persistence of low-level destocking (0.3 animals/household) at equilibrium suggests that complete elimination of stock losses is unrealistic in high-variability rainfall environments, and policies should focus on resilience-building rather than zero-destocking targets. The comparable aggregate performance across the mixed behavioral types suggests that supporting any form of structured learning—whether individual learning-by-doing or social learning—yields similar benefits for household stability in private grazing systems.
