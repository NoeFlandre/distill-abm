Following this low point, the system enters a recovery phase characterized by rapid increase between steps 40 and 70, with biomass climbing from the minimum values to levels between approximately 110,000 and 125,000. After step 70, the trajectories begin to stabilize, though they do not converge. By step 100, the final values spread across a wide range from approximately 111,000 to 128,000, indicating substantially different long-term ecological outcomes across the simulation runs.

The variance across simulations remains relatively tight during the initial 20 steps but begins to diverge noticeably after the minimum point around year 40. This divergence accelerates during the recovery phase and persists through the end of the simulation, suggesting that initial similarities in farmer behavior and ecological conditions give way to divergent pathways as learning processes and feedback mechanisms compound over time.

Interpreted within the context of smallholder grazing management, the initial decline likely reflects an adjustment period where starting livestock levels of 90 animals per household initially exceed sustainable carrying capacity given the high rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200). The trough around years 30-40 represents a critical degradation threshold where vegetation condition reaches its lowest point before feedback mechanisms prompt adjusted stocking decisions. The subsequent recovery and wide dispersion of outcomes by year 100 indicate that the different learning strategies—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—produce markedly different abilities to rebuild and maintain pasture health. Final biomass levels varying by nearly 15,000 units between the highest and lowest performing strategies suggest that decision-making approaches significantly influence long-term ecological resilience. For decision makers, this implies that investing in specific learning mechanisms and information-sharing systems among farmers could substantially affect the sustainable productivity of grazing lands, with the difference between poorly and well-adapted strategies potentially representing the margin between degraded and healthy pastures.
