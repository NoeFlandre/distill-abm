The mean reserve biomass across all patches begins at approximately 90,000 units. During the initial phase spanning roughly the first 15 to 20 time steps, this metric declines substantially, reaching minimum values ranging between 80,000 and 85,000. This initial depletion corresponds to the grazing pressure exerted by the initial livestock herds, which are set at 90 animals per household, as the system adjusts to the combined biomass consumption before vegetation recovery processes and adaptive management responses take full effect.

Following this decline, the system enters a recovery phase beginning around time step 20 and continuing through approximately time step 60. During this period, the reserve biomass increases sharply, with particularly rapid growth occurring between time steps 40 and 60, where values rise from approximately 90,000 to well above 110,000. This recovery trajectory suggests that the learning mechanisms implemented by the different behavioral types—whether through individual learning-by-doing, social learning, or baseline optimization—enable households to adjust their grazing strategies effectively, allowing vegetation reserves to regenerate beyond initial levels.

From approximately time step 60 onward, the reserve biomass stabilizes into a plateau phase that persists through the end of the simulation at time step 100. During this plateau phase, the metric exhibits considerable variation across simulation runs, with stable values ranging between approximately 110,000 and 128,000. This divergence of approximately 18,000 units between the lowest and highest long-term outcomes indicates that the heterogeneous behavioral types—E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1—achieve substantively different long-term pasture conditions. The persistence of this variance suggests that while all learning mechanisms facilitate ecological recovery above baseline levels, their specific efficiency in optimizing long-term reserve accumulation varies significantly, likely influenced by the interaction between learning strategies, local ecological conditions, and the private property structure of the grazing system.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that initial grazing pressure will likely cause temporary resource depletion, but adaptive learning mechanisms can facilitate recovery to stable, higher biomass states. However, the variability in final outcomes suggests that the specific mix of behavioral types in a community significantly influences long-term pasture health, implying that interventions promoting specific learning mechanisms may yield different ecological dividends. The substantial gap between the best and worst performing behavioral configurations highlights the importance of understanding which learning strategies are most effective in specific smallholder contexts.
