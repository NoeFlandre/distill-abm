The count of households engaged in social learning initiates at zero across all simulation replicates. During the first approximately 20 steps, the system exhibits rapid adoption, with statistically significant increasing trends (p<0.001) and slopes exceeding 1.4 households per step in the initial decade, followed by rates near 1.1 households per step through step 21. This early phase represents the diffusion of social learning behaviors through the farming community as households begin observing and adopting strategies from within their knowledge radius.

Growth decelerates markedly after step 20, with inflection points detected at steps 28, 30, 33, and 38 indicating progressively slowing adoption rates. Between steps 22 and 43, the accumulation rate drops to approximately 0.18 households per step, suggesting that most readily susceptible households have already adopted social learning while others require extended exposure or specific ecological conditions to transition. A significant change point at step 40 demarcates the transition from active adoption to a saturation phase.

By step 60, the aggregate mean reaches its maximum value of approximately 32 households (median 31.9), after which the count stabilizes with minimal additional accumulation (slope <0.01 per step) through the conclusion of the simulation at step 100. This plateau indicates an equilibrium where approximately 53% of the 60 households engage in social learning on average, though the underlying social and ecological constraints prevent universal adoption across all runs.

Considerable heterogeneity characterizes the final outcomes across the 30 simulation trajectories. Individual runs terminate with final counts ranging from roughly 18 to 54 households engaged in social learning, with a standard deviation of 8.92 around the mean trajectory. This wide variability suggests that stochastic elements—particularly the significant rainfall variability (rain-std = 100) and individual risk dispositions (risk-att-init = 0.5)—generate divergent social learning diffusion patterns. In some instances, social learning propagates to nearly the entire population (90%), while in others it remains confined to smaller subgroups (30%), reflecting the sensitivity of behavioral adoption to initial conditions and environmental fluctuations in the grazing system.
