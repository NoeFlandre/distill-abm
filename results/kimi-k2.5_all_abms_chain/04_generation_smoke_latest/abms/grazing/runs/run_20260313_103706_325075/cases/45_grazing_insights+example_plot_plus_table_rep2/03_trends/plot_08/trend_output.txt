This variance suggests that while the behavioral innovation generally propagates successfully across the stationary smallholder population, stochastic factors—such as local social network configurations or initial risk-attitude distributions—can produce markedly different penetration depths, with some scenarios achieving near-universal adoption among the 60 households while others result in more constrained diffusion.

The adoption dynamics feature discrete, step-like transitions rather than perfectly smooth growth, evidenced by inflection points at steps 28, 30, 33, 38, and again at 57 and 60. These discontinuities correspond to successive waves of households entering the social learning state. Additionally, oscillatory patterns detected between steps 22–32 and 51–59 indicate periods of active behavioral adjustment where temporary plateaus or minor fluctuations precede further increments.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that social learning interventions in private grazing systems can expect rapid initial uptake, with the majority of adoption occurring within the first 40 time steps. However, the significant variability in final penetration rates—spanning from less than one-third to nearly full population coverage—suggests that passive diffusion alone may be insufficient to ensure universal participation. The pronounced plateau observed around step 60, where the average count stabilizes below the total population size, implies that without enabling mechanisms for continued behavioral spread (such as strategy switching or enhanced knowledge-radius expansion), social learning may reach a natural ceiling, leaving a substantial subset of households reliant on non-social learning behaviors. Targeted support for late adopters or those in isolated network positions may be necessary to maximize the resilience benefits of adaptive co-management across the entire community.
