At initialization, the system begins with approximately 45,000 units of mean green biomass across all patches. During the first 20 to 25 steps, biomass declines consistently across all simulation runs, reaching minimum values between 40,000 and 42,000, with the lowest points occurring approximately at step 20. This initial reduction reflects the immediate grazing pressure from the initial livestock population.

Following this trough, a recovery phase begins around step 25 and continues through step 50. During this period, green biomass increases gradually from the minimum values to approximately 50,000 to 55,000 units. The rate of increase accelerates between steps 50 and 65, representing a rapid growth phase where biomass climbs steeply from roughly 55,000 to values between 58,000 and 63,000.

After approximately step 70, the trajectories transition into a stabilization phase where growth rates diminish and biomass levels plateau. By step 100, final values range between approximately 55,000 and 64,000, depending on the specific simulation run. This divergence becomes pronounced after step 60, with inter-run variability increasing substantially over time despite initial convergence during the early decline phase.

These dynamics suggest that the vegetation system initially experiences a shock from livestock grazing that depresses biomass below steady-state levels. The subsequent recovery and overshoot indicate that vegetation productivity exceeds grazing pressure as the system adapts, potentially through the learning mechanisms (learning-by-doing and social learning) employed by the heterogeneous household agents. The widening spread in final biomass values across runs reflects the influence of stochastic rainfall variability (with standard deviation of 100) and divergent adaptive outcomes among the mixed behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), resulting in multiple stable equilibria rather than a single deterministic outcome. The plateau phase indicates that the social-ecological system reaches a dynamic equilibrium where green biomass production balances with consumption demand from the stationary households managing private pastures.
