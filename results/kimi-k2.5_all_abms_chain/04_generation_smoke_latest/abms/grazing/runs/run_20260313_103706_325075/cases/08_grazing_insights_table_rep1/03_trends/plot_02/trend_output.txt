In the simulation of the private-property grazing extension model with mixed behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), the average household risk attitude (r-parameter) initiates at minimal values near 0.007 and undergoes a pronounced three-phase trajectory over the 100-timestep sequence. During the initial approximately 33 timesteps, the metric exhibits a steep, statistically significant increase, ascending rapidly through intermediate values to reach a maximum of 0.3814 around timestep 33. This early phase demonstrates consistent upward momentum, with the aggregate parameter value surpassing 0.2 by timestep 11 and approaching 0.38 by timestep 29, suggesting rapid collective adjustment in uncertainty management strategies among the 60 stationary households.

Following this peak, the system enters a sustained decline phase spanning roughly timesteps 33 through 65, during which the average risk attitude decreases from approximately 0.38 to values around 0.20–0.27. This downward trajectory is statistically significant and relatively consistent, though interrupted by minor oscillatory fluctuations, including a temporary plateau around timestep 48 (value 0.2723) before continuing the descent.

In the final phase from timestep 66 onward, the metric stabilizes and exhibits no statistically significant trend, converging toward a mean value of approximately 0.19 through the conclusion of the simulation, with the final recorded value at timestep 100 standing at 0.1911. Throughout this late phase, values oscillate mildly around the 0.20 threshold without directional bias.

The trajectory displays notable oscillatory dynamics superimposed on the overall rise-and-fall pattern, particularly concentrated during the peak period between timesteps 20 and 37, where local maxima occur at timesteps 21 (0.3573), 25 (0.3757), 27 (0.3754), 29 (0.3793), 33 (0.3814), and 37 (0.3802). These fluctuations follow a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 timesteps, indicating cyclical adjustments in collective risk attitudes as households with social learning and learning-by-doing capabilities continuously adapt their strategies in response to observed pasture conditions and neighbor performance.

For decision makers, these patterns suggest that smallholder grazing systems implementing adaptive co-management undergo substantial initial recalibration of risk management parameters, with households collectively adopting higher uncertainty management levels (or risk aversion) during the first third of the operational period before moderating toward stable, intermediate configurations. The persistent oscillatory behavior, rather than convergence to a fixed equilibrium, indicates that private-property grazing systems with learning-enabled agents inherently exhibit dynamic, cyclical adjustments in risk attitudes. This implies that governance interventions and support policies should incorporate adaptive flexibility rather than assuming static household risk preferences, particularly during the critical establishment phase (approximately years 20–40 in the simulation timeframe) when risk attitudes peak and fluctuate most dramatically.
