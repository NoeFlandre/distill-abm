Across the 100-year simulation horizon, mean green biomass exhibits a consistent U-shaped trajectory marked by initial degradation followed by recovery, though with substantial variation across repeated runs regarding both the depth of decline and the magnitude of recovery. At initialization (step 0), all simulations commence at approximately 45,000 units of biomass. Following a brief stabilization or marginal increase during the first 5 steps, the system enters a degradation phase lasting until approximately step 40, during which biomass declines to minimum values ranging between 40,000 and 44,000 units depending on the stochastic realization. The timing of this nadir varies across runs, with some reaching their lowest point near step 20 while others continue declining until approximately step 35-40.

Subsequently, a recovery phase initiates between steps 25 and 45, characterized by accelerating biomass accumulation. Between steps 45 and 60, biomass increases sharply from approximately 45,000 units to values exceeding 55,000 units in most simulations. By step 60, the recovery transitions into a stabilization phase, with biomass continuing to rise gradually until reaching asymptotic values between approximately 56,000 and 64,000 units by step 100. Notably, the simulations diverge significantly in their final equilibrium states, maintaining persistent variation of roughly 8,000 units in mean green biomass at the end of the century-long period despite identical initial configurations.

This trajectory indicates that initial stocking levels of 90 animals per household initially exceed the sustainable carrying capacity under the variable rainfall regime (mean 200 units, standard deviation 100), driving the early degradation of vegetation across private pastures. The subsequent recovery reflects successful adaptation through the learning mechanisms employed by the three behavioral types—experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning with single-loop adaptation—as households adjust stocking decisions based on observed pasture conditions within their knowledge radius and neighbors' outcomes. The divergence in final outcomes suggests that specific sequences of rainfall variability and the spatial interaction of different learning strategies produce substantially different long-term productivity levels, with some systems stabilizing near 56,000 units while others achieve 64,000 units.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

These dynamics demonstrate that while adaptive learning strategies eventually enable pasture recovery from initial overstocking, the system traverses a critical vulnerability window between steps 5 and 40 where biomass drops significantly. Interventions targeting this degradation period—such as temporary destocking support or enhanced information sharing within the knowledge radius—could prevent biomass from falling below 42,000 units, thereby reducing the risk of prolonged recovery trajectories or permanently suppressed equilibria. Additionally, the 8,000-unit range in final outcomes across equally configured systems indicates that supporting robust learning mechanisms, while essential, does not guarantee optimal resource management outcomes; environmental variability significantly constrains possible productivity levels, suggesting that decision makers should prepare for persistent heterogeneity in landscape condition even under identical management regimes.
