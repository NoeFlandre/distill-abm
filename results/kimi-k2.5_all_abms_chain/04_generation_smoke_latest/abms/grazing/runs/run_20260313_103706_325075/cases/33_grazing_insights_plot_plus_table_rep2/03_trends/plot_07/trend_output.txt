The Gini coefficient measuring inequality in healthy livestock distribution across households initiates at approximately zero, reflecting perfectly equal initial allocations of livestock. Throughout the 100-step simulation horizon, this coefficient demonstrates a robust increasing trend, with the aggregate mean across 30 simulation series rising monotonically to reach 0.0726 by the final step.  

The trajectory of inequality accumulation is markedly nonlinear, commencing with a gradual ascent during the first 50 steps, followed by pronounced acceleration thereafter. Statistical change point analysis identifies step 51 as a critical threshold where the rate of inequality growth intensifies, with a secondary acceleration occurring at step 71. Multiple inflection points at steps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, 37, and 57 indicate variable rates of divergence throughout the early and middle phases of the simulations. Rolling trend analysis confirms statistically significant increases across all sequential windows (p<0.001), with slope magnitudes transitioning from negligible values in early periods to 0.001 increments in later stages.  

By the terminal step, between-simulation variability expands considerably, with individual run outcomes spanning a range from approximately 0.03 to 0.10. While the overall mean across all time steps remains modest at 0.0302, terminal values cluster around 0.07–0.09 for the majority of runs. The presence of oscillation indices concentrated between steps 78 and 93 suggests periodic fluctuations superimposed on the upward trend during the late simulation phase.  

Within the context of mixed populations employing distinct learning mechanisms—baseline optimization (E-RO), learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1)—these dynamics indicate that initial parity in livestock holdings erodes systematically over time. The acceleration of inequality after step 50 implies that accumulated advantages from adaptive decision-making and social learning processes compound differentially across households, leading to persistent stratification in livestock health outcomes. The substantial terminal variability across simulation runs suggests that stochastic elements in the learning and ecological processes generate divergent pathways of household wealth accumulation, even when starting conditions are identical.  

**Implications for decision makers:** The inevitable rise in inequality suggests that interventions to maintain equitable livestock distributions must be implemented prior to step 50 to prevent the entrenchment of disparities. The wide variation in final outcomes indicates that identical initial conditions and behavioral heterogeneity do not guarantee predictable distributional results, necessitating robust monitoring systems and potentially adaptive redistribution mechanisms. As learning behaviors diffuse and compete within pastoral communities, policies should account for the stochastic nature of household trajectories and the potential for inequality traps to emerge after approximately 50 decision cycles.
