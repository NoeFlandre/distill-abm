The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock begins at effectively zero, indicating perfect equality in livestock health distribution across all 60 households at initialization. Over the 100-step simulation period, inequality exhibits a persistent and statistically significant increasing trend across all analysis windows (p < 0.001), with the aggregate mean reaching 0.0726 and individual simulation runs diverging to terminal values ranging between approximately 0.030 and 0.095.

During the initial phase (steps 0-20), inequality accumulates gradually from zero to roughly 0.010-0.015, marked by inflection points at steps 8, 13, 15, and 20 that indicate subtle variations in the rate of differentiation. This early divergence likely reflects the initial heterogeneity in household risk attitudes (r-parameter = 0.5) and the stochastic influence of rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100) on vegetation dynamics and livestock outcomes.

A transition occurs around step 37, followed by a change point at step 51, where the rate of inequality accumulation accelerates substantially. Between steps 51 and 71, the system enters a high-growth phase characterized by rapid divergence among simulation runs, with the Gini coefficient increasing from approximately 0.030 to 0.060 on average. This period corresponds to the intensification of feedbacks between private pasture conditions, heterogeneous learning behaviors (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), and the cumulative effects of sequential decision-making under uncertainty.

From step 71 onward, inequality continues its upward trajectory but with increasing variance across simulations (standard deviation 0.024), suggesting that stochastic factors and initial condition sensitivities play larger roles in determining final distributions. The presence of a dominant oscillation period of 49 steps and clustered oscillation indices between steps 78-93 indicates cyclical fluctuations in inequality dynamics superimposed on the long-term trend, potentially arising from the interaction between biomass recovery cycles and adaptive livestock management strategies.

The sustained increase in inequality throughout the simulation suggests that despite the presence of learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms, the combination of private property boundaries, climate variability, and heterogeneous agent behaviors drives progressive differentiation in livestock health outcomes, with some households maintaining significantly healthier herds than others by the terminal period.
