The mean green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units across all simulation runs. During the initial 10 steps, it undergoes a minor fluctuation, increasing briefly to roughly 45,500 before commencing a sustained decline. Between steps 10 and 30, biomass decreases steadily, reaching minimum values between 40,000 and 41,000 in most runs, though one simulation maintains levels approximately 3,000 units higher during this depression period.

From step 30 to approximately step 60, the biomass enters a phase of rapid recovery, increasing steeply from lows near 40,000 to values ranging between 60,000 and 65,000. The sharpest growth occurs between steps 40 and 55, with some runs gaining over 1,000 units per step during this interval.

After step 60, the biomass stabilizes, with values plateauing and showing minimal net change through step 100. Final equilibrium levels span a considerable range from approximately 56,000 to 64,000 units, indicating substantial divergence across simulation outcomes. Variability among runs remains low during the first 30 steps but increases markedly after step 40, with the spread between maximum and minimum values widening to roughly 9,000 units by the conclusion of the simulations.

The initial decline reflects the immediate impact of grazing pressure as households deploy their initial livestock endowments onto private pastures, temporarily exceeding vegetation regrowth rates. The subsequent recovery aligns with the activation of learning mechanisms, as households with E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types adapt their grazing strategies based on accumulated experience and social observation. The divergence across runs after step 40 stems from the interaction between stochastic rainfall patterns and heterogeneous learning outcomes, whereby different trajectories of behavioral adaptation lead to varying efficiencies in pasture management. The simulation maintaining higher biomass during the decline phase likely experienced more favorable early rainfall conditions or rapid initial learning that mitigated overgrazing pressure.

For decision makers, the wide variation in final biomass levels indicates that long-term pasture productivity in private property grazing systems depends significantly on the specific pathways of adaptive learning that emerge under climatic uncertainty. While the system demonstrates resilience and capacity for recovery from initial degradation, the divergence in final states suggests that supporting robust learning mechanisms—particularly during the critical transition window between steps 30 and 60—may be essential for achieving higher-productivity ecological outcomes. Interventions that facilitate effective learning-by-doing or social learning processes could help shift systems toward the more favorable equilibria observed in the upper range of simulation results.
