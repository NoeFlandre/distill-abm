Across the 100-timestep simulation period, mean green biomass exhibits a consistent pattern of initial decline, minimum trough, and subsequent recovery, though with considerable variation in magnitude across repeated simulation runs. Initially, biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units. During the first 5 to 10 timesteps, values remain relatively stable or decrease slightly before entering a sustained decline. This downward trajectory continues until reaching minimum values between timesteps 20 and 40, where biomass falls to between approximately 40,000 and 44,000 units depending on the specific run.

A rapid recovery phase commences around timestep 40 to 45, characterized by steep increases continuing through approximately timestep 60. During this period, biomass rises from its minimum values to between 55,000 and 60,000 units across the various runs. Beyond timestep 60, the rate of increase diminishes significantly, with values either plateauing or increasing gradually through the remainder of the simulation. By timestep 100, final biomass levels vary substantially across runs, ranging from approximately 55,000 to over 63,000 units, with divergence between runs becoming pronounced during the recovery phase.

**Interpretation**

The initial decline reflects vegetation degradation under initial grazing pressure from the 60 households managing herds of 90 animals each on private pastures. The minimum biomass occurring between timesteps 20 and 40 represents the degradation threshold before adaptive learning mechanisms enable effective stocking adjustments. The subsequent sharp recovery indicates that the learning strategies—whether experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning—successfully facilitate adaptive management that allows vegetation regeneration. The persistent variation in final biomass levels, with some runs achieving values exceeding 63,000 while others stabilize near 55,000, indicates that specific configurations of learning behaviors and risk dispositions produce markedly different long-term carrying capacities despite all systems recovering from initial overgrazing.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

Policy interventions should focus on accelerating learning during the critical degradation window (timesteps 20-40), as the speed and effectiveness of adaptation during this period determines long-term pasture productivity. The substantial variation in final biomass levels suggests that promoting specific learning strategies may yield significantly higher sustainable carrying capacities than others. Additionally, decision makers should recognize that the system naturally stabilizes after approximately 60 timesteps, indicating that adaptive mechanisms automatically constrain further expansion and establish equilibrium stocking rates without additional external regulation.
