The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across households begins at zero and rises rapidly during the first 20 time steps, reaching values between approximately 50 and 80 across simulation runs. This initial surge reflects immediate household responses to pasture conditions and livestock management pressures as the system initializes, with all behavioral types engaging in destocking to adapt to the private property grazing constraints and initial herd sizes of 90 animals.

Between time steps 20 and 40, the trajectories diverge significantly. Some simulation runs exhibit a marked deceleration, with cumulative destocking stabilizing at values between approximately 58 and 85 for the remainder of the 100-step period. This plateau suggests that households in these runs successfully adapted their strategies—potentially through learning-by-doing or social learning mechanisms—to achieve a sustainable equilibrium where further destocking becomes unnecessary.

In contrast, other simulation runs maintain steady or accelerating accumulation rates throughout the entire period, reaching cumulative destock totals between 100 and 173 by time step 100. These continuously rising trajectories indicate scenarios where households face persistent ecological stress, possibly due to stochastic rainfall variations (with standard deviation of 100), or where behavioral strategies fail to stabilize livestock populations, leading to ongoing herd reductions.

The variance across simulation runs remains low during the initial 10 steps but expands dramatically after step 20, with the range between minimum and maximum values growing from approximately 20 units at step 20 to over 115 units by step 100. This divergence reflects the sensitivity of long-term destocking outcomes to the interaction between heterogeneous learning behaviors (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1), stochastic climate conditions, and the stationary household configuration on private property patches. The presence of both stabilizing and continuously accumulating trajectories suggests that the mixed population of behavioral types produces divergent resilience outcomes, with some households achieving stable livelihoods while others experience progressive livestock depletion.
