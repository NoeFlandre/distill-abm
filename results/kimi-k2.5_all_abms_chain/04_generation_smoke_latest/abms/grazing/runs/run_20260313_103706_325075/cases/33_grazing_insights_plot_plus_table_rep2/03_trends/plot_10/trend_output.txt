**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The data suggests that interventions promoting adaptive learning in grazing systems should anticipate heterogeneous outcomes, with a substantial minority of households likely to persist with traditional non-learning behaviors regardless of demonstration effects from early adopters. Policy designs must account for this "hard core" of approximately 25% of households that resist behavioral change, potentially requiring targeted support structures or alternative incentive mechanisms distinct from those aimed at the learning-capable majority. Furthermore, the critical window for influencing behavioral change occurs within the first 20 steps, after which the rate of strategy switching decelerates and populations stabilize, indicating that early engagement efforts will yield disproportionate impacts compared to later interventions.
