The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock across the 60-household population exhibits a general temporal increase from initial conditions of near-perfect equality (values approaching 0.000) at initialization to substantially higher levels of inequality by the 100th timestep. In the first 20 steps, inequality remains minimal across all simulation runs, with coefficients generally below 0.015, indicating relatively even distribution of healthy livestock among households during the establishment phase. Between steps 20 and 60, values increase gradually to ranges between approximately 0.010 and 0.045, marking the emergence of differentiation in household outcomes as learning processes (social learning and learning-by-doing) and heterogeneous risk attitudes begin to influence grazing decisions. From step 60 to 100, accelerated divergence occurs, with coefficients rising steeply to final values spanning from approximately 0.030 to 0.095. This late-phase divergence reveals the emergence of distinct household classes regarding livestock holdings, where advantages consolidate through adaptive learning mechanisms while other households experience relative decline. The final distribution at step 100 shows substantial heterogeneity: some runs conclude with moderate inequality (0.030-0.060), while others reach high levels (0.075-0.095), with trajectories maintaining lower inequality throughout remaining below 0.045.

This pattern reflects the interaction between private property regimes, heterogeneous risk management strategies among the three behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, E-RO-SL1), and stochastic rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100). The stationary nature of households combined with private pasture access allows for accumulation of livestock advantages, while learning capabilities create divergent pathways depending on initial conditions and neighbor information exchange within the localized knowledge radius.

For decision makers, the inevitable rise in livestock inequality over time suggests that interventions should focus on early-stage equity maintenance mechanisms (before step 40) and safety-net provisions during the critical acceleration phase (steps 60-100). The high variability in final outcomes indicates that management strategies must account for multiple potential inequality trajectories rather than expecting deterministic convergence, particularly emphasizing support for households with less effective learning outcomes or higher risk aversion parameters to prevent excessive consolidation of livestock wealth.
