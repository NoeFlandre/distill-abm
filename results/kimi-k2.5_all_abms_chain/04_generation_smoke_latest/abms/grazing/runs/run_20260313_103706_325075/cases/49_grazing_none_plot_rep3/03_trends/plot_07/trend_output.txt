The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock distribution begins at 0 across all simulation runs, indicating perfect equality in the initial distribution of healthy animals among the 60 households. Over the 100-year simulation period, inequality increases monotonically in every run, though the rate and final magnitude vary substantially across replicates.

During the first 20 timesteps, the Gini coefficient rises gradually from 0 to values between approximately 0.005 and 0.015. Between timesteps 20 and 60, the increase continues with growing variability between runs; by timestep 40, values range from roughly 0.01 to 0.03, and by timestep 60, the spread extends from approximately 0.025 to 0.05.

After timestep 60, the trajectories diverge sharply. In the most extreme cases, the Gini coefficient accelerates rapidly, reaching values of 0.09 to 0.095 by timestep 100. In contrast, other runs exhibit much slower growth, ending at values between approximately 0.03 and 0.045. The majority of runs fall between these extremes, with final values clustering in the 0.06 to 0.085 range.

This pattern indicates that in a mixed population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types operating under private property regimes with climatic variability, initial equality in healthy livestock gives way to persistent and growing inequality over time. The divergence across simulation runs suggests that stochastic factors—likely including rainfall variability and initial spatial configuration of the three behavioral types—generate divergent pathways of wealth accumulation, with some trajectories developing high inequality (Gini > 0.09) while others maintain relatively more equitable distributions (Gini < 0.05) despite all starting from identical initial conditions.
