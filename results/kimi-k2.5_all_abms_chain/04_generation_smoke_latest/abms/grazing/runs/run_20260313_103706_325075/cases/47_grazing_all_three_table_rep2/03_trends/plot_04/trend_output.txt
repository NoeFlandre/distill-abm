The available green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units and declines over the initial three decades, reaching a minimum of roughly 42,000 units around year 27. This initial degradation likely reflects the lag between the commencement of grazing activities—beginning with 90 animals per household—and the development of effective adaptive management strategies. During this establishment phase, grazing pressure temporarily exceeds sustainable levels as farmers rely on initial rule-of-thumb approaches before experiential and observational learning can inform stocking decisions.

Following the trough at year 27, biomass enters a sustained recovery phase, accelerating particularly between years 33 and 50 as learning effects accumulate and farmers adjust their herd management in response to observed pasture conditions. The trajectory shifts decisively around year 50, transitioning from rapid accumulation to a prolonged period of gradual increase that continues through the remainder of the century. By year 100, biomass stabilizes near 60,500 units, substantially exceeding initial levels despite significant annual rainfall variability. Superimposed on this upward trend is a periodic oscillation with roughly 49-year cycles, consistent with the system's response to stochastic rainfall patterns modulating vegetation recovery rates.

For decision makers, these dynamics indicate that learning-based grazing management requires approximately three decades to transition from initial resource depletion to measurable ecological recovery. Interventions designed to support households during this vulnerable early period could prevent excessive degradation while natural adaptation mechanisms develop. The eventual overshoot of initial biomass levels suggests that patient investment in adaptive capacity yields long-term productivity gains, though the persistent oscillations underscore the necessity of maintaining flexible stocking strategies to accommodate climatic uncertainty.
