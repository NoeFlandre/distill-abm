The grazing system exhibits a pronounced three-phase trajectory over the century-long simulation. Initially, aggregate livestock holdings begin at 90 animals per household but undergo a steep, sustained decline throughout the first 37 years, reaching a minimum of approximately 41 animals. This represents a reduction of more than half the initial herd size, indicating that the initial stocking rate substantially exceeds the long-term carrying capacity given the rainfall variability (mean 200, standard deviation 100) and vegetation growth parameters.

Following this crash, the system enters a stabilization period between roughly years 33 and 43, where the decline halts and values hover near the minimum without significant directional trend. This plateau suggests that farmers across all three learning strategies—baseline rule-of-thumb (E-RO), learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1)—converge on conservative stocking decisions as they process environmental feedback and adjust their risk dispositions from the initial 0.5 baseline.

After year 44, the dynamics shift to a gradual recovery phase that continues through year 100. During this 56-year period, aggregate livestock holdings slowly increase from the trough of 41 to approximately 48 animals, with the trend remaining consistently positive but decelerating over time (slope decreasing from 0.313 to 0.002). By the final timestep, the system settles near 47.5 animals, establishing a new equilibrium roughly 47% below initial levels but significantly above the crisis minimum. This long oscillation period of approximately 49 years suggests that full adaptation to sustainable stocking rates requires multiple decades of iterative learning and vegetation recovery cycles.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The initial destocking phase demonstrates that static, high stocking rates are unsustainable under variable rainfall conditions, requiring policymakers to anticipate significant herd reductions (potentially 50% or more) during the first four decades of system development. The 37-year minimum represents a critical vulnerability window where household livelihoods face maximum stress; support mechanisms should target this period. The eventual stabilization above the minimum suggests that integrating experiential and social learning mechanisms enables farmers to find sustainable equilibria, though final herd sizes remain well below initial expectations, indicating that long-term carrying capacity assessments must account for climate variability and learning lag times.
