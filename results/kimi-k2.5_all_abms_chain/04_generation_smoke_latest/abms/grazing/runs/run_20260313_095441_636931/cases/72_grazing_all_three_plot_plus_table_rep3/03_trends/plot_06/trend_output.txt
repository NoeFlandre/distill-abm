Between steps 30 and 100, the destock rate stabilizes at low levels, oscillating around 0.3 to 0.5 with diminishing amplitude. A change point occurs at step 30, marking the transition from the aggressive adjustment phase to a steady-state regime. During this latter period, some household clusters stabilize near zero destocking while others maintain slightly elevated baseline levels around 0.8 to 1.0, suggesting that different learning strategies converge to distinct equilibria regarding risk tolerance and stocking density.

The standard deviation of 1.43 and the diminishing oscillation pattern indicate that while all simulation runs follow the same general trajectory, substantial heterogeneity persists in individual household outcomes during the first 30 steps. By the conclusion at step 100, the metric settles at 0.31, implying that even after full adaptation, a baseline level of destocking persists as households continue managing residual risks associated with rainfall variability.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The initial crisis period (steps 0-10) requires immediate intervention capacity, as households face severe destocking pressure before learning mechanisms can take effect. The 30-step adjustment horizon suggests that policy support for destocking decisions should be concentrated in the early simulation years. The persistent low-level destocking in later periods indicates that even optimal learning cannot eliminate all climate variability risks, implying a need for long-term safety nets or insurance mechanisms rather than emergency crisis responses. The divergence in final destock levels across behavioral types suggests that promoting social learning networks may yield different resilience outcomes than relying solely on individual experiential learning.
