The cumulative number of livestock destocked begins at zero and increases rapidly throughout the first 25 time steps, rising steeply as households immediately adjust their herds to initial pasture conditions and uncertainty levels. This sharp initial accumulation, occurring across all three behavioral types—E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1—likely reflects rapid destocking in response to early ecological constraints and initial risk-management strategies as stationary households calibrate their livestock placement on private pastures.

Between approximately time steps 25 and 60, the rate of cumulative destocking decelerates considerably while remaining positive, slowing to roughly a third of its initial velocity. This transition marks a shift from rapid equilibration to a more moderated adjustment phase, suggesting that learning-by-doing and social learning processes are refining household decision-making and reducing the frequency of necessary destocking interventions.

From step 60 through the conclusion at step 100, the cumulative total approaches approximately 97 destocked livestock per household with minimal further acceleration, settling into a nearly linear but gradual increase. By this final phase, the accumulation indicates that households have converged on stable long-term herd sizes relative to their private resource bases, with destocking continuing at maintenance rates under the stationary household configuration with heterogeneous uncertainty management parameters.
