The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock begins at zero, indicating perfect equality in the initial distribution of healthy animals across all households. Over the course of the simulation, inequality increases monotonically, with the Gini coefficient rising to approximately 0.073 by the final time step.

The trajectory of increasing inequality follows a nonlinear pattern characterized by distinct phases. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 20 time steps, the increase occurs gradually with a relatively shallow slope. Between time steps 20 and 50, the rate of increase accelerates moderately, with inflection points occurring around steps 37 and 57 that mark transitions in the curvature of the trend. After time step 60, the growth becomes markedly steep, with the most rapid increases in inequality occurring during the final 40 time steps of the simulation.

This progressive divergence in healthy livestock holdings suggests that initial homogeneous conditions give way to heterogeneous outcomes as the system evolves. In the context of private property grazing systems with stationary households employing different learning strategies, the widening Gini coefficient indicates that household-level differences in adaptive capacity, risk management, or resource access begin to compound over time. The three behavioral types—baseline E-RO, social learning E-RO-SL1, and learning-by-doing E-LBD—appear to experience divergent trajectories of livestock health outcomes, leading to an increasingly stratified distribution of productive assets.

For decision makers, the monotonic increase in inequality carries significant implications for the long-term sustainability and equity of grazing system interventions. The finding that inequality accelerates over time rather than stabilizing suggests that without external intervention or institutional mechanisms to redistribute resources or support struggling households, the gap between resource-rich and resource-poor households will continue to widen. Policy approaches should consider whether the observed stratification represents efficient specialization or problematic marginalization, and whether safety nets or targeted support for households with declining healthy livestock stocks are warranted to prevent excessive concentration of productive assets.
