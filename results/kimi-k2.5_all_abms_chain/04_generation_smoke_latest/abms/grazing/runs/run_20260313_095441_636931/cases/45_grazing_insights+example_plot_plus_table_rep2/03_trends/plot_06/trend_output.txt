The average number of livestock destocked per household initiates at zero and surges rapidly to a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals by the third yearly time step. This initial spike reflects the immediate resource crisis as households begin with herds of 90 animals under variable rainfall conditions, forcing substantial forced removal of livestock as pastures cannot support the initial stocking density. The severity of this peak indicates a significant overshoot of carrying capacity at the simulation outset.

Following this maximum, destocking rates decline sharply through the first 30 time steps, with secondary peaks occurring around steps 9 and 12 at values of approximately 3.4 and 2.7 animals respectively. These oscillations represent continued volatility as the heterogeneous population—comprising 20 households each of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types—differentially adjusts strategies through learning-by-doing and social learning mechanisms. The pronounced decline during this period suggests rapid adaptation as households reduce herds to sustainable levels.

After approximately step 30, the system transitions to a low-destocking regime where rates stabilize below one animal per household for the remainder of the simulation. From steps 33 to 65, destocking exhibits no significant trend, fluctuating at low levels as the population maintains relatively stable herd sizes adapted to pasture productivity. A slight increasing trend emerges between steps 66 and 76, though values remain minimal, before settling at an end value of approximately 0.3 animals by step 100.

The trajectory reveals high variability across simulation runs during the volatile first 20 steps, gradually converging toward low variance as households equilibrate. The dominant oscillation period of approximately 49 steps suggests cyclical adjustments linked to vegetation dynamics and rainfall variability, though destocking magnitudes diminish significantly after the initial adjustment phase.

Implications for decision makers: The severe initial destocking peak demonstrates that starting herds of 90 animals substantially exceed sustainable carrying capacity under the modeled rainfall variability, suggesting that interventions to limit initial stocking rates or implement gradual herd build-up could prevent significant asset losses during the critical first three years. The stabilization after step 30 indicates that the combination of learning mechanisms eventually enables sustainable management, but the extended volatile period (0-30 steps) highlights a vulnerability window where households require support. The persistent low-level destocking through year 100 indicates that even adapted systems remain vulnerable to stochastic events, suggesting the value of maintaining contingency reserves or destocking safety nets throughout the simulation period rather than only during initialization. The comparable performance across behavioral types by the end suggests that multiple learning pathways can achieve sustainable outcomes, though the E-RO-SL1 and E-LBD types may reach stability faster than baseline E-RO households.
