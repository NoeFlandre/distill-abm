The dynamics of livestock destocking exhibit a pronounced initial crisis followed by sustained adaptation and long-term stabilization. In the initial phase, destocking spikes dramatically to over 6.6 livestock units by year 3, indicating severe resource constraints as households confront the mismatch between initial herd densities and pasture carrying capacity under variable climatic conditions. This acute phase transitions into a volatile adjustment period spanning approximately years 5 through 25, characterized by declining but fluctuating destocking rates—peaking at roughly 3.4 in year 9, 2.7 in year 12, and gradually diminishing to approximately 1.2 by year 22. These oscillations reflect the heterogeneous learning processes among behavioral types as E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 agents adapt their uncertainty management and stocking strategies through individual learning-by-doing and social learning within the private property grazing context.

By years 25 to 40, the system enters a stabilization phase where destocking rates decline below 1.0 and converge toward a long-term equilibrium. From year 40 through the end of the observation period at year 100, destocking stabilizes at a persistent low level averaging approximately 0.3 livestock units, with minimal variation around this baseline. This equilibrium suggests that while learning mechanisms successfully reduce vulnerability to resource shocks over time, residual climatic variability and inherent uncertainty in private property grazing systems maintain a baseline level of destocking that cannot be fully eliminated through adaptation.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The inevitable initial destocking crisis when establishing high-density grazing systems suggests that decision makers should implement gradual stocking protocols or initial capacity assessments rather than immediate full stocking rates. The 25-to-40-year adaptation timeline indicates that investments in agricultural extension services and social learning networks yield substantial long-term resilience benefits, but require sustained commitment before vulnerability reductions materialize. Finally, the persistent low-level destocking at equilibrium highlights the need for contingency planning, risk insurance mechanisms, or safety-net programs to address residual climatic risks that persist even in well-adapted, established grazing systems.
