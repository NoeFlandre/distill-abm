The Gini coefficient measuring inequality in healthy livestock holdings across households initiates at effectively zero, consistent with the initialization of 60 households each possessing identical herds of 90 animals. Over the simulation horizon, the coefficient exhibits a monotonic increase, terminating at approximately 0.073 by year 100, though individual trajectories diverge to range between 0.030 and 0.095 across the 30 simulation repetitions. The aggregate mean of 0.030 and median of 0.023 indicate that while inequality remains modest for the majority of the time series, the distribution becomes increasingly skewed toward higher inequality values in later periods.

During the initial 20 timesteps, inequality accumulates gradually from the origin, with the rate of increase remaining relatively modest. Inflection points detected around steps 8, 13, and 15 mark subtle transitions in the accumulation dynamics as households begin applying their distinct learning strategies—baseline experiential rules, learning-by-doing, and social learning—to process environmental information within their restricted knowledge radius of one patch. This early phase represents the period where stochastic rainfall variation (mean 200, standard deviation 100) first begins to differentiate household outcomes based on initial patch conditions and risk dispositions.

Between steps 20 and 60, the system enters a phase of accelerating inequality accumulation, characterized by inflection points at steps 25, 37, and 57, and a significant change point at step 51. During this interval, the divergence between household fortunes intensifies as the cumulative effects of stocking decisions interact with vegetation dynamics (governed by growth rates of 0.5 and 0.1) and the high inter-annual rainfall variability. The consistent significant increasing trends detected across rolling windows suggest that initial small advantages compound as households with more adaptive learning strategies or favorable local conditions maintain healthier herds while others face degradation.

Following step 71, where a second structural change point occurs, inequality accumulation enters its most rapid phase, with the Gini coefficient slope increasing markedly toward its terminal value. The final 30 timesteps exhibit substantial variance across simulation runs, indicating path-dependent trajectories where early stochastic events and local interaction patterns generate divergent distributional outcomes. Multiple oscillation indices detected between steps 78 and 93 suggest localized fluctuations in the rate of inequality growth during this late phase, reflecting ongoing adaptive adjustments as the system approaches its terminal state.

The progressive stratification of livestock holdings implies that the combination of bounded rationality, heterogeneous learning capabilities, and environmental stochasticity creates structural asymmetries in resource access. Households employing learning-by-doing or social learning mechanisms appear to achieve differential advantages compared to baseline rule-of-thumb approaches, particularly as the system evolves beyond the initial transient phase, resulting in unequal distributions of healthy animals despite the absence of initial disparities.
