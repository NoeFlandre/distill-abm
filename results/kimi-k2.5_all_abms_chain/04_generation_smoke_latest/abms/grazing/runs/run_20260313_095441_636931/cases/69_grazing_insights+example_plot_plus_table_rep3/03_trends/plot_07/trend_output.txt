The distribution of healthy livestock across households begins in a state of perfect equality, with the Gini coefficient initially at approximately zero. Over the 100-year simulation period, inequality exhibits a persistent and statistically significant increasing trend, reaching a mean final value of 0.0726. This trajectory represents a transition from complete equality to moderate inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock.

The increase follows a non-linear pattern characterized by distinct phases. During the initial approximately 50 time steps, inequality accumulates at a moderate pace. Subsequently, between time steps 51 and 71, the rate of increase accelerates markedly. After time step 71, the growth rate moderates slightly but continues upward through the remainder of the simulation. Throughout all observed intervals, the trend remains strictly monotonic with no periods of decline or stabilization.

Individual simulation runs exhibit substantial variation in the magnitude of inequality by the end of the simulation, with final Gini coefficients ranging from approximately 0.03 to 0.095 across the 30 replicates. Despite this variation, all trajectories maintain the consistent upward trend, indicating that increasing inequality is a robust feature of the system dynamics.

The progressive divergence in livestock holdings reflects the interplay between heterogeneous decision-making strategies—specifically the coexistence of baseline (E-RO), learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1) behavioral types—and stochastic environmental conditions. The stationary nature of households on private property patches allows initial advantages, whether derived from favorable pasture conditions, superior learning outcomes, or stochastic rainfall patterns, to compound over time. The presence of learning mechanisms does not prevent inequality accumulation; rather, the differential effectiveness of learning across households appears to contribute to the widening gap between high and low performers. The acceleration of inequality after time step 50 suggests that learning effects and environmental feedbacks become more pronounced as households accumulate experience and as pasture conditions diverge across the landscape.

**Implications for decision makers:** The inevitable rise in inequality indicates that market or natural dynamics in private property grazing systems will lead to wealth concentration without intervention. The acceleration phase around time step 50-70 suggests a critical window for policy intervention before disparities become structurally entrenched. The variation across simulation runs implies that local contextual factors significantly influence inequality levels, requiring tailored rather than uniform interventions. Strengthening social learning mechanisms or facilitating knowledge transfer between households may help moderate inequality growth, though the current model suggests learning alone is insufficient to prevent concentration.
