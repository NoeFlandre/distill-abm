The mean green biomass across all patches begins at approximately 45,000 units. During the initial five time steps, the metric experiences a marginal increase to roughly 45,500 units, representing the initial vegetation state before intensive grazing pressure manifests.

Subsequently, from steps 5 through 35, the biomass undergoes a sustained decline, reaching minimum values between 40,000 and 44,000 units around steps 25 to 35. This reduction reflects the pressure of initial livestock populations set at 90 animals per household across the 60 households, as farmers begin with baseline decision rules prior to the full activation of learning mechanisms. The most pronounced declines approach 40,000 units, representing an 11% reduction from initial levels, while certain trajectories exhibit more moderate decreases to approximately 44,000 units, suggesting earlier adaptive responses in those particular realizations.

Beginning around step 35, the biomass enters a phase of rapid recovery, increasing steeply through approximately step 60. During this interval, values rise from their nadirs to between 55,000 and 60,000 units. The rate of increase then moderates, with continued gradual growth from step 60 through step 100, ultimately stabilizing at final values ranging from approximately 56,000 to 64,000 units.

The trajectories exhibit substantial divergence across simulation runs, particularly during the recovery and stabilization phases. By the conclusion of the 100-step period, the spread between the lowest and highest values extends approximately 8,000 units, with most runs converging between 60,000 and 64,000 units. This variation reflects the interaction of stochastic rainfall variability (coefficient of variation of 50%) with heterogeneous risk dispositions and the three distinct learning strategies—baseline rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—as farmers adjust stocking decisions based on local pasture conditions and neighbor observations within the one-patch knowledge radius.

The overall pattern indicates an initial degradation phase followed by recovery to levels 24% to 42% above initial conditions, suggesting that despite initial overstocking, the adaptive learning processes enable farmers to identify sustainable grazing intensities that support enhanced vegetation biomass relative to starting conditions.
