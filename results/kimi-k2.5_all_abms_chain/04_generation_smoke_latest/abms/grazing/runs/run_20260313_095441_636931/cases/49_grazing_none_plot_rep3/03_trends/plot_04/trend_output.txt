The simulations exhibit a consistent pattern of initial biomass decline followed by recovery and stabilization at elevated levels, though with considerable variation across runs. 

At initialization (step 0), mean green biomass starts at approximately 45,000 units. During the first 5 steps, biomass remains relatively stable or increases slightly to around 45,300, reflecting initial vegetation growth before significant grazing impact accumulates.

From step 5 to approximately step 40, biomass undergoes a sustained decline across most simulation trajectories, reaching minimum values ranging between 40,000 and 47,500. This degradation phase corresponds to the pressure exerted by initial livestock populations of 90 animals per household on pasture resources. The severity of decline varies substantially across runs, with most simulations bottoming out near 40,000-42,000 while one trajectory maintains higher levels around 47,500.

Beginning around step 40, all simulations enter a rapid recovery phase where biomass increases sharply through approximately step 65. During this period, mean green biomass rises from minimum values to between 55,000 and 60,000.

From step 65 to step 100, the system stabilizes, with biomass approaching asymptotic values ranging from approximately 56,000 to 64,000. This final equilibrium state substantially exceeds initial conditions by roughly 11,000 to 19,000 units. The divergence in final biomass levels across simulations spans approximately 8,000 units, indicating that stochastic environmental factors and heterogeneous learning dynamics generate divergent long-term ecological outcomes.

**Interpretation:** The U-shaped trajectory reflects the interplay between initial grazing pressure and gradual effectiveness of learning mechanisms in the private property system. The initial decline represents an adjustment period where households apply baseline strategies with initial risk attitudes of 0.5, potentially overstocking their private patches. The minimum values reached at step 40 represent the critical point of pasture degradation before learning effects manifest. 

The recovery phase coincides with the operation of learning-by-doing in E-LBD agents and social learning in E-RO-SL1 agents, who update their risk parameters based on observations within the knowledge radius of 1. These adaptive processes enable households to optimize grazing decisions, allowing vegetation recovery. The stabilization at elevated levels above initial conditions suggests that learning facilitates a transition to improved management regimes that enhance pasture productivity. The substantial variation across the 30 simulation runs reflects the influence of high rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 around a mean of 200) interacting with the mixed population of 20 E-RO, 20 E-LBD, and 20 E-RO-SL1 households, where different learning trajectories and stochastic weather sequences produce divergent social-ecological outcomes despite identical starting conditions.
