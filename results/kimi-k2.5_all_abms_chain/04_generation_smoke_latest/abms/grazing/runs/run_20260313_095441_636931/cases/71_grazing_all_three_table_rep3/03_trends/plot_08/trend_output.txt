The prevalence of social learning among households begins at zero and undergoes a sustained diffusion process throughout the century-long simulation. During the initial decade, engagement increases rapidly, rising to approximately 14 households by year 10 as farmers initially adopt observational strategies under conditions of environmental uncertainty. This early phase is characterized by a steep adoption curve, indicating immediate recognition of the utility of neighbor-based information when managing private pastures under variable rainfall regimes.

Between years 11 and 40, the diffusion continues but at a markedly decelerating rate, with the count approaching 31 households. Multiple inflection points occur during this middle period—specifically around years 28, 30, and 33—indicating shifts in adoption velocity as the pool of potential adopters diminishes and social learning becomes entrenched within the community structure. A significant change point at year 40 marks the transition from active diffusion to saturation dynamics.

From year 40 to 63, the number of households engaged in social learning approaches its maximum of approximately 32 households, after which it stabilizes for the remainder of the simulation period through year 100. This plateau at roughly 53 percent of the total household population is particularly noteworthy given that only one-third of households (20 of 60) were initially designated as social learning types. The saturation above the initial cohort size indicates that social learning diffuses across behavioral type boundaries, with approximately 12 additional households from the rule-of-thumb and learning-by-doing categories adopting social learning behaviors despite constraints on fundamental strategy switching.

The sustained elevation in social learning engagement throughout the latter half of the simulation indicates that observation of neighbors becomes a persistent feature of the decision-making landscape. For decision makers concerned with grazing management policy, this diffusion pattern suggests that social learning mechanisms can spread rapidly through farming communities during the initial decades of intervention, potentially accelerating the adoption of sustainable stocking practices through peer observation. However, the stabilization at slightly above half the population indicates that a substantial minority of farmers may remain reliant on individual experiential learning or static rules-of-thumb, suggesting that policy approaches should account for heterogeneous decision-making strategies even after extended periods of community exposure to social learning opportunities. The plateau achieved by year 63 implies that policy interventions targeting social learning diffusion should focus on the first six decades of implementation, after which adoption rates naturally stabilize regardless of continued exposure.
