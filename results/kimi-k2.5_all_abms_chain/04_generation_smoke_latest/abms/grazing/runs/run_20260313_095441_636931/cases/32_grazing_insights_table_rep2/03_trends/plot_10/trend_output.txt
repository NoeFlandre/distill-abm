The simulation tracking non-learning E-RO households reveals a characteristic trajectory of initial stability followed by progressive attrition over the 100-timestep period. At initiation, the population comprises 20 E-RO households. During the first approximately 10 timesteps, the count exhibits a transient increase, reaching a maximum value of roughly 21.1 households around timestep 8, suggesting a brief period where baseline behaviors appear viable before adaptive pressures intensify.

Following this early peak, the system enters a pronounced and sustained decline. A significant decreasing trend emerges between timesteps 11 and 21, characterized by a steep reduction in household numbers. This rapid attrition moderates but continues through subsequent decades, with change points in the dynamics occurring near timesteps 20 and 45. By timestep 45, the decline decelerates substantially, transitioning into a stabilization phase where the population fluctuates without significant directional trend through timestep 100. Throughout this later period, values oscillate around a mean of approximately 16.4 households, reaching minimum values near 14.7 around timestep 92, and concluding at 14.7 households by the final timestep.

Within the context of this private-property, stationary-household grazing system, the gradual displacement of E-RO agents—who lack learning-by-doing and social learning capabilities—indicates that non-adaptive baseline strategies confer lower long-term resilience under variable ecological conditions. The persistence of approximately 15 households (roughly 25% of the initial E-RO cohort and 25% of the total system) by simulation end suggests that heterogeneity in risk management and local conditions allows some non-learning households to persist, despite the broader competitive advantage held by E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The trajectory suggests that in smallholder grazing systems with stationary land tenure, reliance on traditional non-learning management strategies leads to higher vulnerability and exit rates over multi-decadal timescales. Early simulation periods may temporarily mask this vulnerability, as evidenced by the initial peak at timestep 8, but adaptive capacity becomes critical after approximately 10–20 timesteps. Policymakers should prioritize institutional support for learning mechanisms—particularly social learning networks and experiential adaptation—while recognizing that a residual population may continue utilizing baseline practices due to specific risk tolerances or resource endowments. Interventions targeting the most vulnerable non-learning households during the critical transition window (timesteps 10–45) could mitigate attrition and stabilize rural livelihoods.
