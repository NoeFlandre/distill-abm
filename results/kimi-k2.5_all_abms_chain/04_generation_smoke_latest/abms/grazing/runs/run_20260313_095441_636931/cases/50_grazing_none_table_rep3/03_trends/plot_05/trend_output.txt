The simulation begins with an initial average livestock holding of 90 animals per household at year 0. A sharp decline occurs during the first 37 years, with livestock numbers falling rapidly to reach a minimum of approximately 41 animals by year 37. This period represents a crash phase where initial overstocking leads to significant destocking and pasture degradation under private property constraints. Following this decline, the system enters a stabilization phase between years 37 and 55, where livestock numbers fluctuate narrowly around 41 to 47 animals as households adjust to sustainable stocking levels. From year 55 onward, a gradual recovery trend emerges, with livestock numbers slowly increasing through the remaining simulation period. By year 100, the average livestock holding reaches approximately 47.5 animals. This late-stage recovery reflects the influence of learning processes—particularly learning-by-doing among E-LBD agents and social learning among E-RO-SL1 agents—enabling households to adapt their risk management strategies and improve livestock retention compared to the baseline E-RO behavioral type. The final equilibrium remains well below initial stocking levels, indicating that learning-based adaptation facilitates partial recovery but cannot fully overcome the ecological constraints of the private property grazing system within the simulated timeframe.
