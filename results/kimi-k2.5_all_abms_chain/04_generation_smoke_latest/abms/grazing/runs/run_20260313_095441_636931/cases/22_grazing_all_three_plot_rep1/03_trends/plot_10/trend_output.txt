The count of households following non-learning strategies begins at approximately 20 across all simulation runs. Within the first 10 time steps, the population trajectory diverges dramatically between distinct pathways. Approximately half of the simulation runs exhibit a rapid increase, peaking at between 38 and 40 households around step 10 to 15, while the remaining runs show immediate decline from the initial value.

For runs experiencing initial growth, the elevated count persists through step 40, maintaining levels between 35 and 40 households with moderate fluctuation. After step 40, these populations enter a steady decline phase, decreasing from approximately 35 down to 24–27 households by step 100. This suggests that transient conditions in these runs temporarily favor non-learning households or allow accumulation of additional non-learning agents through mechanisms such as strategy abandonment or household entry, before longer-term pressures reduce their numbers.

In contrast, runs showing immediate decline demonstrate continuous attrition throughout the simulation. Some trajectories stabilize at intermediate levels around 15–18 households by step 60, maintaining this state through the conclusion. Others exhibit persistent decline to minimal levels, with several runs reaching near-zero counts by step 65 and remaining at 0–2 households thereafter. A subset stabilizes between 8 and 13 households from approximately step 50 onward.

By the final time step, the distribution of non-learning households spans nearly the entire possible range, from approximately 0 to 27 individuals across different runs. No convergence to a common equilibrium is observed, indicating that stochastic variation in initial conditions or early-period dynamics creates lasting path dependencies. The presence of trajectories maintaining roughly 25–27% of the initial non-learning population suggests that rule-of-thumb strategies can persist under favorable conditions, while the existence of collapse trajectories indicates high vulnerability to early adverse events.

For decision makers, this high variance implies that supporting non-learning agricultural communities carries substantial uncertainty. Interventions should account for the possibility of rapid population collapse in non-learning systems, particularly during the initial adaptation period. The absence of convergence by year 100 suggests that transient support measures may have lasting effects on community composition, and that early warning systems should prioritize detection of initial decline patterns which appear predictive of long-term outcomes.
