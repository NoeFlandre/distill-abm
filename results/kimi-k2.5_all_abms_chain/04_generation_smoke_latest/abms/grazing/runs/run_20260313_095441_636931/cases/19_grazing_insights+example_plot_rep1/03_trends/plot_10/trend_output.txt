The count of non-learning households (E-RO type) initiates at 20 across all simulation runs, consistent with the initial parameter configuration. Within the first 5 to 15 steps, the population diverges rapidly, with some trajectories surging to between 30 and 42 households, while others decline to fewer than 10. This early volatility suggests that initial stochastic variations in rainfall, biomass availability, or neighbor interactions create strong path dependencies in strategy adoption.

Between steps 15 and 35, simulations that experienced early growth in non-learning households reach peak values of approximately 40 to 42, temporarily comprising the majority of the 60-household system. Conversely, other trajectories bottom out near 2 to 5 households during this same interval, indicating near-complete adoption of learning-based strategies (E-LBD or E-RO-SL1) in those particular runs.

A transition phase occurs between steps 35 and 50, wherein the highest trajectories undergo sharp declines from their peaks (dropping from roughly 40 to the mid-20s), while low-count trajectories largely stabilize or continue gradual declines. By step 65, one trajectory reaches zero non-learning households, where it remains through the end of the simulation, representing the complete displacement of baseline behaviors by learning strategies.

From step 60 to 100, the system settles into distinct stable equilibria. Final counts at step 100 cluster at several distinct levels: near zero (complete adoption of learning), approximately 5 to 9 (strong minority of learning households), approximately 13 to 18 (mixed equilibrium), and approximately 24 to 26 (persistence of non-learning majority). Notably, several terminal states maintain counts above the initial 20 households, suggesting conditions where non-learning behaviors not only persist but proliferate relative to the starting configuration. One trajectory remains fixed at exactly 20 throughout, serving as a stable baseline against which the variability of active learning dynamics becomes apparent.

These dynamics reveal that in private-property smallholder grazing systems, the diffusion of learning behaviors is non-linear and context-dependent. The existence of high-level stable equilibria (24–26 households) demonstrates that traditional non-learning strategies can remain resilient or even expand under certain ecological feedbacks or social learning barriers, while the zero-equilibrium outcomes show that complete behavioral transformation is possible under alternative stochastic conditions.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The high variability in outcomes suggests that uniform policies promoting adaptive management may yield unpredictable results across different communities. Decision makers should anticipate heterogeneous adoption patterns, where some localities may rapidly abandon traditional practices while others remain anchored to non-learning behaviors. Interventions may need to be tailored to local trajectory clusters—targeting intensive support for communities showing early declines in learning adoption, while leveraging the natural momentum in communities already trending toward low non-learning household counts. The persistence of high non-learning equilibria indicates that transitional support programs may require long-term commitment in contexts where initial displacement of learning behaviors occurs.
