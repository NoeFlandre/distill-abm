The average number of livestock destocked begins at zero, reflecting initial full resource availability. A sharp increase occurs immediately, reaching a maximum of approximately 6.6 animals per household by the third timestep. This elevated level of destocking continues through the first decade of the simulation, with notable secondary peaks of roughly 3.4 and 2.7 animals at timesteps 9 and 12 respectively.

Following this initial period, the system exhibits a sustained decreasing trend through approximately timestep 30, with destocking levels declining from above 2.0 to below 1.0 animals. This transition point around timestep 30 marks a shift from high-variability destocking events to a more stable regime. After timestep 30, destocking rates stabilize at low levels, fluctuating without significant directional trend through the middle period, though a slight increasing tendency appears in the final quarter.

By the conclusion of the simulation at timestep 100, destocking has diminished to approximately 0.31 animals per household, remaining near the median value observed across the entire temporal sequence. The long-term trajectory indicates a transition from an initial crisis phase characterized by high resource pressure and forced animal removal, toward a stabilized system where destocking becomes rare.

In the context of the mixed behavioral population—comprising baseline (E-RO), learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1) agents—the initial destocking likely reflects the mismatch between initial herd sizes and pasture carrying capacity under variable climatic conditions. The subsequent decline suggests effective adaptation through both individual experiential learning and social learning processes, as agents adjust their risk parameters and grazing strategies in response to observed neighbor performance and direct experience. The low final destocking rates indicate that the heterogeneous learning mechanisms enable households to stabilize their herds at sustainable levels relative to available biomass, despite the stationary, private-property constraints of the smallholder farming context.
