The time series data reveal a consistent and statistically significant upward trajectory in the inequality of healthy livestock distribution across households over the 100-year simulation horizon. Beginning from a state of near-perfect equality at initialization (values approximating zero), the Gini coefficient exhibits a monotonic increase throughout the entire period, concluding with an aggregate mean value of 0.0726 by the final timestep.

The progression of inequality unfolds through several distinct phases characterized by varying rates of acceleration. During the initial two decades, the coefficient remains relatively suppressed, staying below 0.015 until approximately step 20, with inflection points detected at steps 8, 13, 15, and 20 indicating subtle shifts in the accumulation rate. A notable transition occurs around the midpoint of the simulation, with changepoint analysis identifying step 51 as a critical juncture where the pace of divergence intensifies sharply. This acceleration continues through step 71, marking a secondary changepoint before sustained steep growth carries through to the conclusion at step 100.

Statistical trend analysis confirms the robustness of this pattern, with rolling Mann-Kendall tests across all ten windows (spanning steps 1–100) indicating significantly increasing trends (p < 0.000). The slope of inequality accumulation gradually escalates from negligible values in early windows (0.000) to approximately 0.001 per step in later decades, confirming that household divergence accelerates over time rather than stabilizing. Oscillation indices detected between steps 78 and 93 suggest periodic fluctuations superimposed on the dominant upward trend, potentially reflecting cyclical environmental feedbacks or adjustment lags in the vegetation-livestock system operating under high rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100 units around a mean of 200).

Throughout the simulation, the system maintains a median Gini coefficient of 0.0229 with considerable dispersion (standard deviation 0.0239), indicating substantial heterogeneity in inequality outcomes across the 30 repeated runs despite identical initial conditions. This persistent divergence emerges even though all 60 households began with identical livestock endowments (90 animals each) and operated under structured learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning) within private pasture boundaries.

**Interpretation**: The systematic increase in the Gini coefficient demonstrates that under conditions of significant climatic variability and stationary private property arrangements, initial equality in livestock health deteriorates progressively. The three distinct learning strategies generate differentiated adaptation outcomes, with households experiencing heterogeneous local environmental realizations or utilizing different decision-making heuristics accumulating disparities in animal health over decades. The marked acceleration of inequality after year 50 suggests that initial stochastic advantages or disadvantages become entrenched through positive feedback mechanisms, as differences in pasture condition and livestock health compound over time without double-loop learning capability to switch strategies.

**Implications for Decision Makers**: The inevitable emergence of asset inequality in autonomous private grazing systems—even among equally endowed households employing diverse adaptive strategies—indicates that long-term equity requires explicit policy attention. Decision makers should anticipate that climate variability coupled with individual learning will naturally stratify household livestock health outcomes, particularly accelerating after the fifth decade of operation. Interventions such as early-warning systems, targeted veterinary or restocking assistance for households falling behind, and knowledge dissemination programs that diffuse effective learning strategies across the population may help mitigate excessive polarization. Furthermore, the stable oscillations in late-stage inequality suggest that monitoring systems should account for cyclical patterns of relative advantage when designing equity-enhancing safety nets.
