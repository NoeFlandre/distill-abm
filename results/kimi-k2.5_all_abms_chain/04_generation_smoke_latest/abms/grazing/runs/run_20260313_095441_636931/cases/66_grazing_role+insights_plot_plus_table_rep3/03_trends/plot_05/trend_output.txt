The aggregate livestock holdings across all households initiate at 90 animals per household and undergo a pronounced decline throughout the early simulation period, reaching a minimum of approximately 41 animals by year 37. This represents a reduction of roughly 54% from initial levels. Following this nadir, the system exhibits a gradual recovery phase extending from approximately year 40 through year 100, with final holdings stabilizing near 47.5 animals per household—substantially below initial stocking rates but above the crisis minimum.

The trajectory reveals distinct temporal phases characterized by different rates of change. The initial decline is steep and consistent, with statistically significant decreasing trends detected across consecutive windows spanning years 0 through 32. The rate of decline moderates considerably around year 20, transitioning from rapid reduction to a more gradual descent toward the minimum at year 37. Between approximately years 33 and 43, the system exhibits no statistically significant directional trend, indicating a stabilization or transition period. Subsequently, from year 44 onward through year 98, significant increasing trends emerge, though the rate of recovery progressively diminishes over time—from steeper initial gains to very gradual increases in later decades.

Within the context of this grazing system, the initial decline reflects an inevitable correction from unsustainable starting densities of 90 animals, as vegetation conditions cannot support such stocking levels given the rainfall variability and pasture productivity parameters. The convergence of all household trajectories toward the minimum near year 37 suggests a system-wide crisis where pasture degradation forces substantial destocking across all learning strategy types. The subsequent divergence and partial recovery indicates differential adaptation capacities among the three behavioral types—baseline rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning—with some households better able to rebuild herds while others remain at lower stable levels. The final stabilization well below initial levels suggests that sustainable equilibrium in this system requires permanent reduction in stocking densities, with long-term carrying capacity appearing to settle near 47-48 animals per household rather than the initial 90.

For decision makers, these dynamics highlight critical vulnerabilities in high initial stocking strategies and the importance of early intervention before year 20 to prevent the steep decline phase. The recovery limitations suggest that policies should not assume full recovery to initial herd sizes is biologically feasible or economically optimal; instead, supporting adaptation to lower sustainable stocking levels around 50 animals may be more realistic. The differential recovery trajectories after year 40 indicate that investment in learning mechanisms—whether experiential or social—can significantly influence long-term livelihood outcomes, suggesting that extension services and knowledge sharing networks may improve resilience during the recovery phase. The prolonged low-level equilibrium after year 55 implies that waiting for spontaneous recovery is insufficient; active management interventions may be necessary to lift households above the crisis minimum threshold.
