The aggregate livestock inventory per household, inclusive of animals that have been destocked, initiates at 90 animals and declines sharply throughout the first two decades. By year 20, the rate of decrease moderates markedly, yet the downward trajectory persists until reaching a minimum of approximately 41 animals at year 37. This extended decline reflects the adaptive responses of households as they adjust initial overstocking to the carrying capacity of private pastures under variable rainfall, with the inflection at year 3 marking the transition from immediate destocking to more measured reduction strategies.

Between years 37 and 46, the system stabilizes at its nadir, indicating a temporary equilibrium where destocking pressures abate and households consolidate their remaining herds. A pronounced shift occurs around year 46, initiating a sustained recovery phase that accelerates through year 55. From year 55 onward, the inventory continues to increase but at a progressively decelerating rate, approaching approximately 47.5 animals by year 100. This recovery demonstrates the capacity of the learning strategies to facilitate gradual restocking, though the final inventory remains substantially below initial levels.

The entire series exhibits a dominant oscillatory cycle of approximately 49 years, superimposed over the long-term trajectory. Multiple oscillation peaks between years 5 and 20, followed by additional fluctuations around years 39-42, suggest that household decisions engage in continuous feedback with vegetation dynamics, creating periodic fluctuations around the declining and subsequent recovery trends.

Implications for decision makers: The prolonged descent to year 37 indicates that initial stocking levels significantly exceed sustainable capacities under the modeled rainfall variability, exposing farmers to extended periods of capital depletion before stabilization occurs. The gradual recovery post-year 46 suggests that while adaptive learning enables herd rebuilding, reliance on internal ecological dynamics alone cannot restore initial herd sizes within a century, highlighting the potential need for external livestock inputs or alternative livelihood strategies during the adjustment period. The 49-year oscillation pattern underscores the importance of long-term planning horizons that account for multi-decadal ecological cycles rather than expecting stable year-to-year productivity.
