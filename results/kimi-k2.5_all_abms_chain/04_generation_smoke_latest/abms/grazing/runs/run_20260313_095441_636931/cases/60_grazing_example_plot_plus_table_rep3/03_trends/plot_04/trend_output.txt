The average available green biomass across all patches initiates at approximately 45,000 units. This downward trajectory persists through step 27, where the biomass reaches its minimum value of approximately 42,000 units. This initial reduction reflects the immediate impact of grazing pressure as households deploy their initial livestock endowments on private pastures, temporarily exceeding the vegetation's regenerative capacity during the early establishment phase.

Following the trough at step 27, the system undergoes a sustained and statistically significant recovery. Between steps 33 and 54, biomass increases at an accelerating rate, with the most rapid accumulation occurring during the interval from step 44 to 54. The expansion continues through the remainder of the simulation, though at progressively decelerating rates from steps 55 to 100. By the terminal step, biomass attains approximately 60,500 units, surpassing the initial value by over 15,000 units and the minimum by roughly 18,500 units.

Across the 30 simulation runs, trajectories exhibit minimal variance during the initial decline phase, with all realizations converging toward minimum values within a narrow range centered near step 27. However, substantial divergence emerges during the recovery phase, particularly after step 60, where final biomass values at step 100 spread across a range of approximately 8,000 units. This heterogeneity in outcomes suggests that while early system behavior is largely determined by initial stocking densities and uniform vegetation parameters, long-term pasture productivity becomes increasingly influenced by the specific adaptive decisions and heterogeneous learning processes—including learning-by-doing and social learning of risk attitudes—employed by the mixed population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types.

The overall pattern of initial degradation followed by robust recovery exceeding baseline conditions indicates that the combination of vegetation dynamics and adaptive household behaviors eventually enables sustainable biomass management, allowing pastures to recover and stabilize at higher productivity levels despite continuous grazing activity.
