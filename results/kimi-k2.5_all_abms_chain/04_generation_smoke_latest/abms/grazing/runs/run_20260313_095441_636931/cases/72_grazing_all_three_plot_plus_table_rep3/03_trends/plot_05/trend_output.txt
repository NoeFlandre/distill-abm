The average livestock holdings per household begin at 90 animals and undergo a severe contraction over the first 37 years. During the initial phase (years 0–20), holdings plummet rapidly with rates exceeding three animals per year initially, declining to approximately 45 animals as farmers destock aggressively in response to pasture degradation. This steep drop reflects the unsustainability of initial stocking densities given the high rainfall variability and vegetation recovery dynamics.

Between years 20 and 37, the decline continues but decelerates significantly, reaching a minimum of approximately 41 animals around year 37. This nadir represents the point of maximum adjustment, where herds have been reduced to their lowest sustainable base before recovery mechanisms take effect.

From year 37 to year 55, the system stabilizes near this minimum, exhibiting no significant directional trend as households maintain conservative stock levels while learning to manage pasture conditions under uncertainty.

A recovery phase initiates around year 55, with livestock holdings increasing steadily toward approximately 47.5 animals by year 100. The recovery follows a saturating trajectory: rapid initial gains with slopes around 0.31 animals per year during years 44–54 and 0.15 during years 55–65 gradually diminish to near-zero slopes (0.002) by the final decade, indicating convergence toward a new equilibrium significantly below initial levels.

The dominant oscillation period of 49 years suggests cyclical adjustments in the vegetation-livestock-learning feedback system, while the overall 47% reduction from initial to final values indicates permanent adaptation to lower carrying capacities. The change points at years 20 and 55 demarcate transitions between the collapse, stabilization, and recovery phases.

**Implications for decision makers:** The prolonged 37-year decline to minimum herd sizes suggests that initial overstocking creates decades of hardship before recovery becomes possible. Conservative initial stocking rates or early destocking interventions could prevent the severe capital losses observed in the first four decades. The incomplete recovery to initial levels—ending at 47.5 versus starting at 90—demonstrates that ecological thresholds, once crossed, constrain long-term productivity, making prevention of initial overgrazing critical. The slowing recovery rate in later years indicates that spontaneous recovery through learning alone may take generations to approach equilibrium, suggesting potential value in external support or accelerated learning programs during the transition period.
