The cumulative destock totals measured across repeated simulations show substantial variation in long-term outcomes, with final values at step 100 ranging from approximately 58 to 173 animals destocked per household on average.

During the initial 20 steps, all simulations exhibit rapid accumulation of destocked livestock, with mean totals increasing uniformly from 0 to values between 60 and 80. This consistent early response reflects initial adjustments to pasture conditions as households apply their respective learning strategies under rainfall variability with a standard deviation of 100 units.

After step 20, outcomes diverge significantly. Approximately one-third of the simulation runs stabilize, with cumulative destocking plateauing between steps 30 and 40 at values from 58 to 85, then maintaining these levels through step 100. The remaining runs continue accumulating destocked animals at varying rates. By step 60, the spread ranges from roughly 65 to 130. By step 100, the higher outcomes reach between 110 and 173, while stabilized runs remain near 60-85.

This divergence corresponds to the performance of the three distinct learning strategies operating under stochastic ecological conditions. Outcomes stabilizing below 90 indicate household groups that successfully adapted stocking decisions to environmental feedback, minimizing forced destocking after the initial adjustment period. Intermediate outcomes around 100-125 suggest moderate adaptation difficulties requiring periodic destocking events. The highest outcomes exceeding 130 indicate learning mechanisms that inadequately responded to pasture degradation signals, resulting in continuous destocking pressure throughout the simulation period. The sustained linear growth observed in the upper ranges implies ongoing failure to establish sustainable stocking levels, leading to repeated forced divestment of livestock across the 100-year period.
