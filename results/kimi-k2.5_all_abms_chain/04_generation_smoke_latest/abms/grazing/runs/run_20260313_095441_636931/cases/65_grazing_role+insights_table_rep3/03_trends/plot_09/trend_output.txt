The cumulative destocking of livestock exhibits a monotonically increasing pattern throughout the century-long simulation, beginning at zero and accumulating to approximately 97 animals per household by the terminal year. 

The trajectory reveals distinct phases in the intensity of destocking. During the initial quarter-century, households undergo rapid destocking at an accelerated pace, with the majority of accumulated removals occurring within these early decades. This steep initial ascent suggests immediate pressure to reduce livestock numbers substantially below the starting endowment of 90 animals per household, as farmers confront the biological constraints of their private pastures and high rainfall variability.

Following this initial period, the rate of destocking moderates considerably through the middle interval extending to approximately year 60. While destocking continues to accumulate during this phase, the pace slows to roughly one-third of the initial rate, indicating households are progressively approaching equilibrium stocking levels relative to land carrying capacity. Multiple subtle adjustments in the rate of increase occur throughout this middle period, suggesting ongoing fine-tuning of stocking decisions as learning strategies process environmental feedback.

After approximately year 60, the accumulation enters a stabilization phase characterized by minimal additional destocking through the remainder of the simulation. The curve flattens to its lowest sustained level, with annual increments becoming marginal, suggesting that household herds have largely stabilized at long-term sustainable levels.

Superimposed on this general decelerating trend are periodic oscillations suggesting cyclical adjustment patterns, with notable fluctuations clustering around years 22–26, 49–61, and 79–80. These oscillations likely reflect responsive destocking behaviors triggered by interannual rainfall variability, where households periodically remove animals during dry periods while the cumulative metric captures the net upward progression.

By the conclusion of the simulation, the average household has destocked a cumulative total exceeding their initial herd size, indicating that maintaining the initial stocking rate proves unsustainable over the long term given ecological constraints.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The first 25 years represent a critical adjustment window where significant herd reductions are necessary to align livestock numbers with pasture carrying capacity. Decision makers should anticipate that initial stocking rates substantially exceed sustainable levels, requiring cumulative destocking greater than 100% of initial herds over the long term. The stabilization observed after year 60 suggests that learning and adaptation eventually lead to sustainable management regimes, but the transition period involves substantial livestock removal. The cyclical patterns indicate that even after stabilization, maintaining flexibility for periodic destocking in response to environmental variability remains essential for sustainable private pasture management. Policies supporting early-stage adaptation and risk management during this transition phase could improve household resilience during the necessary adjustment period.
