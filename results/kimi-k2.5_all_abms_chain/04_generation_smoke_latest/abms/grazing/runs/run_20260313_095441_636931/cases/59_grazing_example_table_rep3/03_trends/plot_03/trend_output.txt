This plateau suggests a temporary equilibrium where grazing pressure and biomass regeneration rates balance, potentially corresponding to the period where E-RO-SL1 type households observe neighbor performance and update their uncertainty parameters while E-LBD types accumulate experience.

From time step 33 onward, the reserve biomass exhibits a sustained and statistically significant increasing trend that continues through the end of the simulation at time step 100, where it reaches approximately 121,073 units. This upward trajectory, initially steep and gradually moderating in rate, indicates successful adaptation through the learning capabilities implemented in the model. The recovery and growth of biomass suggest that social learning and learning-by-doing behaviors enable households to improve grazing management over time, reducing pressure on pastures and allowing reserve biomass to accumulate beyond initial levels. The inflection point around time step 33 and the subsequent steady increase demonstrate the system's capacity for recovery when agents adapt strategies based on accumulated experience and social observation in private property grazing contexts.
