Around year 25, the rate of accumulation slows markedly, indicating a transition from shock adjustments to more sustained management patterns. These cyclical patterns appear between years 20–25 and again around years 50–60, suggesting multi-decadal rhythms likely linked to vegetation recovery cycles or extended rainfall variability affecting pasture productivity.

Inflection points clustered around years 45–47 and 53–57 indicate shifts in the acceleration of destocking rates, while a change point at year 60 marks further stabilization in the accumulation pattern. By the final decades (60–100), the metric proceeds at a reduced but persistent pace with minimal additional deceleration, suggesting the system approaches a steady-state where destocking becomes part of regular adaptive management rather than crisis response.

The magnitude of cumulative destocking—exceeding initial herd sizes over the century—implies multiple restocking and destocking cycles as households adapt to ecological variability. The declining rate of accumulation over time suggests that learning strategies enable farmers to reduce the severity or frequency of destocking events as they accumulate experience, though they cannot eliminate them entirely.

For decision makers, the concentration of destocking activity in the first 25 years highlights the critical importance of early support mechanisms for new entrants to grazing systems. The persistence of destocking needs throughout the century, despite learning, indicates that even experienced managers require frameworks capable of handling multi-decadal climatic cycles, and that stocking decisions under high rainfall variability necessitate ongoing adaptive capacity regardless of the specific learning approach employed.
