The mean cumulative destock total initiates at zero and undergoes a rapid, near-linear accumulation during the first 20 time steps, converging uniformly to levels between approximately 60 and 80 units across all simulation trajectories. This initial surge reflects immediate livestock liquidation as households adjust their initial stocking levels of 90 animals per household to align with observed pasture biomass and risk management requirements under stochastic rainfall conditions.

Following the initial adjustment phase, the trajectories bifurcate markedly into distinct long-term behavioral patterns. A distinct subset of simulation runs plateau abruptly by step 30, stabilizing at cumulative destock totals between 58 and 60 for the remainder of the 100-step duration. This stabilization suggests that certain behavioral configurations, likely those incorporating effective learning-by-doing or social learning mechanisms, successfully established sustainable equilibrium herd sizes with minimal subsequent need for livestock liquidation.

Conversely, another subset exhibits continuous linear growth throughout the simulation period, with cumulative destocking reaching values between 130 and 173 by step 100. These sustained accumulation patterns indicate persistent destocking pressures, potentially driven by recurrent ecological stress under high rainfall variability, conservative risk attitudes characterized by high r-parameter values, or ineffective adaptive responses to deteriorating pasture conditions.

Intermediate trajectories display moderate, steady increases from the initial plateau, settling between 90 and 120 cumulative destocks by the terminal timestep. Variance across simulation runs remains negligible during the first 20 steps but expands substantially thereafter, reflecting the compounding effects of heterogeneous uncertainty management, distinct learning capabilities among E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types, and stochastic rainfall realizations with a standard deviation of 100.

For decision makers, the divergence between stabilization and continuous destocking pathways after step 20 identifies a critical intervention window during which support for adaptive learning and information sharing networks could facilitate transitions to sustainable herd management. The high variance in long-term outcomes underscores that early destocking decisions (within the first 20-30 steps) significantly influence whether households achieve stable livestock levels or enter trajectories of continuous asset liquidation, highlighting the need for early warning systems and risk mitigation strategies during the initial establishment phase of private grazing operations.
