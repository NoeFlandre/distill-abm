The mean green biomass begins at approximately 45,000 units. During the initial five steps, it undergoes a minor fluctuation, rising slightly before beginning a decline. Over the subsequent twenty to thirty steps, the biomass decreases substantially across most simulation runs, reaching minimum values between 40,000 and 42,000 units around steps 20 to 30. However, one trajectory exhibits markedly different behavior during this period, maintaining biomass levels between 45,000 and 47,000 units throughout the decline phase before later converging with the general recovery pattern.  

Beginning around steps 35 to 40, the biomass enters a phase of rapid increase that continues through approximately step 60. During this period, values rise sharply from their minimum levels to reach between 55,000 and 65,000 units. After step 60, the growth rate diminishes considerably, and the system approaches a plateau that persists through step 100.  

At the conclusion of the simulation period, biomass levels range between approximately 56,000 and 64,000 units, displaying substantial heterogeneity across simulation runs with a spread of roughly 8,000 units between the highest and lowest outcomes. Most trajectories cluster in the 60,000 to 63,000 range.  

This pattern suggests an initial phase of vegetation depletion corresponding to grazing pressure from the initial livestock populations. The subsequent recovery reflects the vegetation dynamics sub-model's regeneration capabilities, potentially amplified by adaptive decision-making processes of the households adjusting their grazing strategies. The plateau phase represents a social-ecological equilibrium where grazing pressure balances with vegetation growth under the specified rainfall regime. The considerable variance across simulation runs indicates sensitivity to stochastic factors such as rainfall variability or heterogeneous initial conditions, demonstrating that identical behavioral parameters can result in divergent ecological outcomes under private property arrangements. The divergent trajectory that maintained higher biomass during the stress period suggests that certain stochastic configurations of the system or particular combinations of household risk attitudes and learning behaviors can substantially buffer against initial biomass depletion.
