The cumulative number of livestock destocked begins at zero and rises monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation, ultimately reaching approximately 97 animals per household by the final time step. This accumulation follows a markedly decelerating trajectory, with the rate of destocking declining systematically across three distinct phases as households progressively adapt their grazing management.

During the initial 25 years, destocking accumulates most rapidly, with rates exceeding five animals per year in the first decade before moderating to roughly 1.5 animals annually by year 21. This early period of intensive destocking reflects the initial adjustment phase where farmers, particularly those relying on baseline rule-of-thumb strategies without active learning, overstock their pastures relative to the actual carrying capacity and subsequently reduce herds in response to deteriorating vegetation conditions. The high initial destocking rates represent the costs of trial-and-error adaptation as households encounter the consequences of stocking decisions made under incomplete information and uncertainty.

Between years 25 and 60, the accumulation rate drops substantially to approximately 0.35 animals per year, indicating a stabilization of stocking decisions as experiential learning and social observation enable farmers to better calibrate their herds to pasture conditions. This deceleration suggests that by the quarter-century mark, households have largely transitioned from reactive destocking in response to pasture degradation toward more anticipatory management informed by accumulated experience and neighbor observation within the knowledge radius.

After year 60, the rate stabilizes further at approximately 0.31 animals annually through the simulation's conclusion, maintaining a low but persistent positive trend. This continued accumulation, even after learning convergence, reflects ongoing adaptive adjustments necessary to navigate the significant interannual rainfall variability (coefficient of variation of 50 percent) that characterizes the system.

Superimposed on this decelerating trend are oscillatory dynamics with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 years, evidenced by fluctuating accumulation rates around time steps 22–26, 49–61, and 79–80. These medium-term cycles likely correspond to destocking responses triggered by sequences of drought years or favorable rainfall periods, illustrating how farmers adjust livestock numbers in reaction to climate variability despite accumulated learning. The system does not eliminate destocking but rather incorporates it as a regular adaptive mechanism.

For decision makers, the high initial destocking rates during the first quarter-century indicate substantial early-period adjustment costs and potential livelihood disruptions as farmers develop effective management practices. Targeted support during this vulnerable learning phase, such as safety nets or technical advisory services, could improve welfare outcomes and reduce the social costs of adaptation. Furthermore, the persistent low-level destocking throughout the simulation, even after behavioral convergence, underscores the necessity of maintaining flexible stocking strategies and market access that accommodate inherent climate variability, rather than policies that attempt to eliminate destocking entirely or promote rigid stocking rates that prove unsustainable during drought sequences.
