The engagement of households in social learning exhibits a distinct diffusion pattern across the 100-timestep simulation horizon, evolving from complete absence to partial saturation. Initially, at timestep 0, no households record active social learning engagement. The metric then undergoes a prolonged phase of rapid growth through approximately timestep 40, characterized by statistically significant monotonic increases with initially steep slopes that gradually attenuate. During this expansion phase, inflection points at timesteps 7, 28, 30, 33, and 38 indicate successive decelerations in the adoption rate, suggesting evolving barriers to diffusion as early adopters saturate and remaining households prove more resistant.

A pronounced structural change occurs around timestep 40, after which adoption continues but at substantially reduced rates, with slopes diminishing toward near-zero values by timestep 63. The series reaches its maximum value of approximately 32 households (representing roughly 53 percent of the 60-household population) at timestep 63, maintaining this saturation level through timestep 100 with an end value of 32.07. This plateau indicates that social learning engagement stabilizes well below universal adoption, implying persistent heterogeneity in behavioral types or ecological conditions that preclude full population conversion even after extended simulation periods.

Superimposed on this logistic-like trajectory are significant oscillatory dynamics with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 timesteps, evident in clustered fluctuations around timesteps 22–32 and 51–59. These cycles suggest that social learning adoption responds to recurring ecological or climatic forcing—likely linked to the high rainfall variability (standard deviation of 100) and biomass dynamics inherent to the grazing system—where adverse or favorable conditions periodically trigger surges or retreats in learning behavior.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The saturating adoption curve at approximately half the population suggests that uniform social learning policies may face inherent ceilings in heterogeneous smallholder contexts, necessitating targeted interventions for the remaining resistant households. The 49-timestep oscillation pattern indicates that adoption is sensitive to ecological cycles; therefore, timing extension services or social learning facilitation to coincide with specific phases of the climatic cycle could enhance uptake efficiency. Finally, the rapid initial growth phase (first 40 timesteps) demonstrates that early investment in social learning infrastructure yields diminishing returns after saturation, suggesting that resources should shift to maintenance and quality of learning networks once approximately 50 percent adoption is achieved.
