The number of households employing the baseline experiential rule-of-thumb strategy without active learning begins at 20, representing the initial cohort assigned to this behavioral type. During the first approximately 8 years, this count exhibits a slight increase, reaching a peak of roughly 21 households. This brief initial growth may reflect stochastic variation in early survival outcomes or transient benefits from initial livestock endowments before environmental pressures and rainfall variability manifest their full effects.

Following this early peak, the number of E-RO households enters a sustained decline lasting from approximately year 11 through year 65. Throughout this phase, the count decreases from around 21 to approximately 15 households, indicating steady attrition of non-learning agents from the system. This reduction suggests that rule-of-thumb decision-making, which does not adapt to changing pasture conditions through accumulated experience or social observation, becomes increasingly vulnerable to failure under the significant rainfall variability and grazing pressure encountered over time.

After approximately year 70, the decline attenuates and the count stabilizes near 15 households, remaining relatively constant through the end of the simulation at year 100 with a final value of approximately 14.7. This plateau, which represents the minimum level observed near year 92, indicates that a subset of E-RO households achieves long-term persistence despite the lack of adaptive learning. These survivors likely represent cases where initial conditions, conservative stocking levels, or favorable environmental stochasticity allowed them to avoid the failures that removed roughly 26 percent of their cohort during the first two-thirds of the simulation.

For decision makers, these trends highlight that static, non-adaptive management strategies carry substantial risk of system exit under variable climatic conditions, with household attrition concentrated in the first 60-70 years of operation. This implies that interventions to support non-adopting households may be most critical during this middle period, or alternatively, that promoting transitions to learning-based strategies could prevent the approximately 25-30 percent attrition rate observed among rule-of-thumb practitioners over the century-long timeframe.
