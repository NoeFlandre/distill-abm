The mean household risk attitude exhibits complex temporal dynamics characterized by an initial rapid escalation followed by a prolonged decline and eventual stabilization, with substantial heterogeneity across simulation runs. Initially, the aggregate mean begins at a minimum value of approximately 0.0074, remaining near this level through the third time step. During the first 33 steps, risk attitudes increase significantly and monotonically across the population, reaching a maximum aggregate value of 0.3814. This early period corresponds to intensive learning activity, where agents—particularly those with social learning (E-RO-SL1) and learning-by-doing (E-LBD) capabilities—actively update their uncertainty management parameters based on initial observations of vegetation dynamics, reserve biomass states, and neighbor performance within the private property grazing context.

Following the peak at step 33, the aggregate trajectory reverses, entering a declining phase that persists through approximately step 65. During this interval, the mean decreases from 0.38 to roughly 0.19, reflecting a calibration process where households adjust initially elevated risk parameters downward as they accumulate experiential knowledge and refine their grazing strategies. Statistical trend analysis confirms significant decreasing trends across consecutive windows spanning steps 33 to 65, indicating consistent downward adjustment during this middle phase as agents transition from exploratory uncertainty management to more calibrated risk attitudes.

After step 65, the system enters a stable equilibrium where the aggregate mean risk attitude plateaus around 0.19 through the conclusion of the simulation at step 100. The final value of approximately 0.1911 represents a moderate risk disposition, significantly below the initial peak but elevated above the starting minimum. This suggests that while adaptive learning processes reduce initial uncertainty-driven caution through feedback between pasture conditions and household decisions, persistent ecological variability and the stationary nature of household patches maintain risk awareness above baseline levels.

Beneath these aggregate patterns, individual household and simulation trajectories display marked divergence, with final risk attitudes dispersing across a broad spectrum from -0.4 to 0.8. Distinct outcome clusters emerge: a substantial portion of trajectories stabilize at high levels between 0.7 and 0.8, indicating successful adoption of conservative uncertainty management or effective social learning diffusion among E-RO-SL1 agents; a central group fluctuates between 0.2 and 0.6, representing moderate adaptation to heterogeneous local ecological conditions; while a minority declines to negative values approaching -0.4, suggesting either the adoption of risk-seeking behaviors, failed learning calibration under adverse biomass conditions, or the differential impact of initial herd sizes and demographic settings. This heterogeneity intensifies after step 20, with trajectories diverging sharply between steps 30 and 50 as behavioral types differentiate in their learning outcomes, before stabilizing into distinct risk-attitude regimes after step 60.

The temporal evolution is punctuated by multiple inflection points concentrated at steps 7, 12-13, 39, and 46, marking transitions between learning-by-doing phases, social learning updates, and strategic adjustment periods tied to the yearly vegetation and decision-making cycle. The oscillation structure, with a dominant period of 49 steps, aligns with the transition from the initial learning-intensive phase to the stabilized adaptive co-management regime, reflecting the model's feedback mechanisms between biomass observation, social comparison, and the updating of r-parameters that govern uncertainty management in grazing decisions.
