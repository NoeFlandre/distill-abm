The persistent variance in late-stage values, ranging from near-zero for efficient adapters to approximately 1.0 for others, reflects the heterogeneity in risk-attitude parameters (r-parameter) and learning speeds across the household population.

The rapid convergence to low destocking levels after step 30 suggests that supporting adaptive learning capacities—rather than imposing static stocking limits—can effectively achieve long-term system resilience. The biennial cyclicality implies that policy interventions should account for multi-year ecological feedbacks, and the 30-step adjustment period defines the minimum timeframe required for social learning to stabilize livelihood outcomes in variable climatic conditions.
