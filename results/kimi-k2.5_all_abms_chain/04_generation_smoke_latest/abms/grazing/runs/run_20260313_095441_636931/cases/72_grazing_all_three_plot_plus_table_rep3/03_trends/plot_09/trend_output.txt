The cumulative number of livestock destocked across households begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 100-year simulation period, reaching an average of approximately 97 animals per household by the final time step. The trajectory of accumulation is characterized by a markedly rapid increase during the initial 20–25 years, followed by a gradual deceleration in the rate of destocking through the remainder of the simulation. Statistical change points at year 25 and year 60 delineate these transitions, with the slope of accumulation decreasing from roughly 5 animals per year in the earliest decade to approximately 0.3 animals per year after year 60.

Substantial heterogeneity exists across simulation runs, with final cumulative destocking values ranging from approximately 60 to 170 animals per household. This divergence suggests that the specific sequences of rainfall variability and the particular learning strategies employed—whether baseline rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, or social learning from neighbors—generate significantly different long-term destocking requirements. The oscillation patterns detected around years 45–75 indicate ongoing adjustments in destocking rates as households adapt to feedback from pasture conditions.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that stationary private grazing systems under variable rainfall necessitate continuous livestock destocking over extended periods, with the most critical reductions required in the first quarter of the management timeframe. The persistent upward trajectory throughout the century suggests that adaptive learning strategies, while varying in effectiveness, do not eliminate the need for ongoing destocking but rather modulate its total magnitude. The wide range of outcomes across scenarios highlights the importance of accounting for both climatic variability and social learning processes when projecting long-term livestock carrying capacities and planning for herd reductions in smallholder grazing systems.
