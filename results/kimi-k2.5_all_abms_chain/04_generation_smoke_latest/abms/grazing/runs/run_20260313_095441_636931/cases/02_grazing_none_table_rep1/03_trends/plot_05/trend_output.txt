The average livestock holdings per household, encompassing both active stock and destocked animals, follow a distinct trajectory of sharp initial decline, trough stabilization, and modest recovery over the 100-timestep simulation. Beginning at 90 animals per household at timestep 0, the metric undergoes an immediate and steep reduction through timestep 10, decreasing at a rate of approximately 3.47 animals per timestep. This rapid destocking phase begins moderating around timestep 3, as indicated by an inflection point where the rate of decline slows considerably.

Between timesteps 10 and 20, the downward trend continues but at decelerating slopes (from -0.963 to -0.368), leading to a system minimum of approximately 41 animals per household at timestep 37. From roughly timestep 33 to 43, the series exhibits no significant directional trend, plateauing with minor fluctuations around a median value of 47.3 animals, suggesting a temporary equilibrium where destocking pressures balance against retention decisions.

A fundamental shift occurs around timestep 46, corresponding to change point 55, where the trend reverses to a gradual increase. During the interval from timestep 44 to 54, holdings rise at a rate of 0.313 animals per timestep. This growth continues through the remainder of the simulation but at progressively diminishing rates (slowing to 0.146, then 0.024, and finally 0.002 animals per timestep by the final windows), indicating convergence toward a stable level. By timestep 100, the metric reaches approximately 47.5 animals per household, stabilizing at roughly 53% of the initial value.

This pattern suggests that the heterogeneous population of E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 households initially overstocked relative to private pasture carrying capacity, necessitating heavy destocking, but subsequently entered a recovery phase as learning behaviors and adaptive co-management processes enabled improved matching of livestock numbers to biomass availability under variable climatic conditions.
