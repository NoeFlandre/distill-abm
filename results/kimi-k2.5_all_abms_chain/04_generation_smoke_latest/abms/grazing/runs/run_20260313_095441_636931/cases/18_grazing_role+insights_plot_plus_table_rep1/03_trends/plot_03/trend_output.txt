The mean reserve biomass exhibits a pronounced U-shaped pattern characterized by an initial decline phase followed by a sustained recovery and growth trajectory over the 100-year simulation period. Starting at 90,000 units, biomass undergoes a statistically significant decreasing trend during the first 26 years, reaching a minimum value of approximately 84,114 units. This represents a degradation of roughly 6.5% from initial conditions and marks the critical trough of the system's vegetation depletion.

Following the minimum at year 26, the system enters a brief stabilization period lasting until approximately year 33, during which no significant directional trend is detected. Subsequently, a robust and sustained increasing trend emerges from year 33 onward, persisting through the remainder of the simulation. The recovery phase exhibits varying rates of growth, with the most rapid increase occurring between years 44 and 54, where biomass accumulates at approximately 1,909 units per year. An inflection point around year 48 indicates a transition from rapid acceleration to a more moderated growth rate, though the increasing trend remains statistically significant through year 98.

By the conclusion of the simulation at year 100, mean reserve biomass reaches 121,073 units, representing a 34.5% increase above initial levels and a 44% recovery from the minimum observed at year 26. The overall mean biomass across the entire simulation period is 103,366 units, indicating that despite the initial degradation, the system's long-term average condition exceeds the starting value. The dynamics exhibit an oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of approximately 49 years, aligning with the transition from the degradation phase to the recovery phase.

Interpreted within the context of smallholder grazing management with three distinct learning strategies, the initial decline reflects the pressure of initial stocking decisions where farmers begin with 90 animals each but have not yet adapted their management to local pasture conditions. The minimum at year 26 represents the point of maximum vegetation depletion before experiential and social learning mechanisms enable adaptive responses. The subsequent recovery indicates that learning-by-doing and social learning strategies successfully guide farmers toward adjusted stocking rates or improved management practices that facilitate pasture recovery. By year 100, biomass levels exceeding initial conditions suggest that learning mechanisms facilitate the emergence of sustainable management practices that enhance long-term pasture productivity beyond the starting state, with the system stabilizing at higher productivity levels.

For decision makers, these results indicate that initial pasture degradation may be an unavoidable consequence of adaptation in novel systems, but that investment in learning mechanisms—both experiential and social—can facilitate recovery and ultimately achieve superior long-term outcomes compared to static management approaches. The 26-year timeframe to reach the degradation minimum and the subsequent 74-year recovery trajectory indicate that pasture rehabilitation requires multi-decadal planning horizons, and that the most rapid improvements occur during the middle phase of recovery (years 44-54), suggesting that patience is required before the full benefits of adaptive learning manifest.
