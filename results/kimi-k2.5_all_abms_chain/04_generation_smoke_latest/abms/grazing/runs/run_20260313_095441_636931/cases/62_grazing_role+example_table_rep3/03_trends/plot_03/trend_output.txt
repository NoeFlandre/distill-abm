The average reserve biomass across the landscape begins at 90,000 units and exhibits a pronounced U-shaped trajectory over the 100-year simulation period. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 26 years, biomass undergoes a significant and steady decline, reaching a minimum value of roughly 84,114 units. This early depletion reflects the immediate grazing pressure exerted by the initial livestock endowments—90 animals per household across 60 farms—which temporarily exceeds the vegetation's regenerative capacity under the regime of high rainfall variability (mean 200 units annually with standard deviation of 100).

Following this nadir at year 26, the system enters a transitional recovery phase lasting until approximately year 48. Notable inflection points in the trend dynamics occur around years 8 and 48, signaling shifts in the rate of change as the ecological and behavioral components of the system interact. By year 50, a definitive structural change occurs, marking the onset of a sustained accumulation phase. From this midpoint onward, reserve biomass increases monotonically through the remainder of the simulation, ultimately reaching approximately 121,073 units by year 100.

The trajectory exhibits oscillatory characteristics, particularly clustered around years 10–20 and 41–49, with a dominant periodicity of approximately 49 years. These cycles likely reflect the interaction between stochastic rainfall events and the adaptive responses of farmers employing distinct learning strategies (experiential rule-of-thumb, learning-by-doing, and social learning). The recovery pattern suggests that as farmers utilize their knowledge radius to observe pasture conditions and neighbor outcomes, they adjust stocking decisions to reduce grazing pressure below carrying capacity. 

Statistical evidence indicates highly significant decreasing trends in the earliest windows (p < 0.01), transitioning through a brief period of no significant trend around years 22–32, followed by consistently significant increasing trends from year 33 onward (p < 0.001). Notably, the rate of biomass accumulation decelerates over time—from slopes exceeding 1,900 units per year in the mid-recovery phase to merely 5 units per year in the final decade—suggesting the system asymptotically approaches a higher biomass equilibrium as learning-mediated stocking rates stabilize under conditions of ecological uncertainty and initial risk disposition of 0.5.
