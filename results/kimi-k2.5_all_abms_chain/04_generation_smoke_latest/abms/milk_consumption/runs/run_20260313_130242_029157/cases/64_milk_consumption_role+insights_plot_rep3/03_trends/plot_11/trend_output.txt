Based on the simulation results tracking mean health value deviation over 32 time steps representing the 1974-2005 period, the metric exhibits a consistent upward trajectory from an initial value of 0.0 to approximately 0.72-0.75, with three distinct phases of growth dynamics.

**Phase 1: Rapid Escalation (Steps 0-10)**
The deviation increases sharply from 0.0 at step 0 to approximately 0.50-0.52 by step 10. This represents the most aggressive growth phase, with the metric gaining roughly 0.05 per time step. During this period, agents rapidly update their health perceptions regarding skimmed and semi-skimmed milk through learning mechanisms, while the strong incumbent habit strength (10.0) and high habit threshold (2.0) prevent corresponding behavioral changes. The growing gap reflects emerging cognitive dissonance as consumers increasingly recognize alternatives as healthier yet maintain their whole milk consumption patterns due to ingrained habits.

**Phase 2: Decelerated Growth/Plateau (Steps 10-25)**
From step 10 to step 25, the deviation increases much more gradually, rising from approximately 0.52 to only 0.58-0.62. This plateau phase spans roughly 15 time steps (representing approximately 1984-1999) and shows a marked reduction in the rate of divergence between held values and implied values. During this period, the historical crossover in market share likely occurs, where social influence mechanisms (operating at p-interact = 0.3 and social-susceptibility = 0.2) facilitate actual switching behaviors among some agents, temporarily balancing the continued learning of remaining whole milk consumers.

**Phase 3: Late-Stage Acceleration (Steps 25-32)**
After step 25, the deviation resumes a steeper ascent, climbing from approximately 0.60 to 0.70-0.75 by the final time step. This renewed acceleration suggests that by the late 1990s and early 2000s, the remaining whole milk consumers—likely those with the strongest habitual attachments or lowest social connectivity within the Watts-Strogatz network structure—maintain choices that increasingly contradict their learned health valuations. The deviation reaching approximately 0.75 by 2005 indicates substantial persistent misalignment between internal health beliefs and actual consumption behaviors.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The sustained growth in health value deviation throughout the simulation reveals that health education and awareness campaigns alone generate significant psychological tension (cognitive dissonance) but do not automatically overcome habitual behavior. The plateau phase during the market transition period suggests that interventions targeting habit disruption—rather than merely information provision—may be most critical when market shares are near equilibrium. Furthermore, the persistence of high deviation (~0.75) even after the historical crossover point indicates that a substantial consumer segment maintains choices contradictory to their health values, suggesting opportunities for behavioral interventions targeting the 51% social blindness parameter or leveraging the 0.28 social conformity weight to reduce this misalignment.
