The mean environmental deviation metric exhibits a pronounced sigmoidal trajectory across the 32-step simulation period, characterizing the temporal dynamics of the attitude-behavior gap in milk consumption choices. At initialization (step 0), the deviation registers at approximately 0, indicating initial alignment between consumer environmental valuations and their milk selections. This near-zero starting condition reflects the baseline state where agents' perceptions of milk environmental attributes are consistent with their habitual whole milk consumption patterns.

Between steps 0 and 5, the deviation undergoes its most dramatic transformation, escalating rapidly from near 0 to approximately 12-13 units. This represents a twelve-fold or greater increase occurring within the first 15% of the simulation timeframe. The steepest ascent appears concentrated specifically between steps 1 and 3, where the metric climbs from roughly 2 to 12 units. This rapid escalation captures the critical phase where information dissemination, social learning mechanisms (operating at interaction probability 0.3 with susceptibility 0.2), and memory updating (over a 5-step window) quickly elevate agents' environmental perceptions of skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives from their initial mean of 1.12 toward substantially higher values. However, the strong incumbent habit strength of 10.0 and active habit maintenance mechanisms (threshold 2.0) create substantial behavioral inertia that prevents immediate choice modification, generating increasing cognitive dissonance.

During the intermediate phase (steps 5 through 10), the growth rate decelerates markedly as the metric advances from approximately 12-13 to 13.5 units. This slowing trajectory reflects the gradual erosion of habitual barriers for a subset of the population and the beginning of behavioral transition, though the mean deviation continues rising as perception updating outpaces the rate of actual switching.

From approximately step 10 through the conclusion at step 32, the system enters a prolonged plateau phase where the deviation stabilizes tightly around 13.5 units (with observed variance across multiple simulation runs remaining within approximately 0.2 units). This remarkable stability persists throughout the latter two-thirds of the simulation, suggesting that the environmental value-action gap becomes a persistent equilibrium feature rather than a transient phenomenon. Even as the model replicates the historical market transition where skimmed and semi-skimmed consumption surpasses whole milk, the mean deviation maintains its elevated position, indicating that substantial misalignment between environmental attitudes and consumption behaviors characterizes the steady-state condition. The minor compressional trends visible after step 25 suggest only marginal reductions in this gap even over extended time horizons.

The consistency across multiple simulation runs (demonstrated by the tightly clustered trajectories) indicates these dynamics are structurally robust to stochastic variation in the social learning and choice processes.

**Interpretation and Implications:**

This trajectory reveals a critical insight for decision makers: environmental attitude formation occurs substantially faster than behavioral adaptation in habitual consumption domains. The rapid initial rise (steps 0-5) demonstrates that consumers quickly internalize environmental information about product alternatives through social networks and experience, but the plateau at elevated deviation levels (steps 10-32) indicates that these updated attitudes translate imperfectly into actual choices. The persistent deviation of approximately 13.5 units throughout the equilibrium phase suggests that even successful market transitions driven by health or environmental messaging will be accompanied by significant consumer segments exhibiting environmentally incongruent behavior due to habit maintenance.

For policy makers and marketers, the stabilization rather than decline of this metric implies that interventions targeting habit disruption may be more cost-effective than continued environmental education campaigns. The model suggests that once environmental perceptions are updated (occurring by step 5-10), additional messaging yields diminishing returns for behavioral change. Instead, mechanisms that reduce the effective habit threshold or increase the cognitive dissonance sensitivity (currently set at 0.15) may be necessary to close the value-action gap. The historical crossover to lower-fat milk appears to have occurred within a population maintaining substantial environmental attitude-behavior inconsistencies, suggesting that market share shifts can be driven by factors other than pure value alignment, including social conformance pressures (norms activation) and justification mechanisms.
