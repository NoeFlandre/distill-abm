The simulation depicts a perfectly linear accumulation of consumer choices over the 33 time steps representing the 1974–2005 period. The aggregate count begins at zero choices and increases at a constant rate of exactly 1,000 decisions per time step, reaching a total of 32,000 choices by the final period. This trajectory produces a mean value of 16,000 choices across the time series, with the median identically positioned at the midpoint of the simulation (time step 16). The absence of peaks, valleys, or inflection points indicates no acceleration or deceleration in the rate of decision-making throughout the entire modeled timeframe. Statistical validation confirms this upward trend is monotonic and highly significant across all rolling windows (Mann-Kendall tau = 1.000, p = 0.027), indicating unwavering consistency in the accumulation pattern. Interpreted within the context of the agent-based model, this linear growth reflects the steady-state decision-making behavior of the 1,000 virtual consumers, each making approximately one milk selection per time step throughout the simulation. The constant flow of choices suggests that the model holds consumption frequency stable, thereby isolating the market shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties as a phenomenon driven by changing preferences and social influences rather than by variations in overall milk purchase volume.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The constancy of total choice volume implies that historical dietary transitions in milk consumption involved substitution between product types rather than category expansion or contraction. For public health officials and agricultural policymakers, this indicates that successful interventions to promote low-fat milk varieties need not account for changes in total consumption frequency, but should focus on influencing the decision calculus at existing purchase occasions. Marketers and retailers can rely on stable demand volumes when forecasting inventory, allowing resources to concentrate on shifting share between milk categories through targeted messaging about health and environmental attributes. The predictable baseline also provides a robust foundation for sensitivity testing, enabling stakeholders to identify which psychological parameters (habit thresholds, social susceptibility) most effectively disrupt incumbent preferences when the underlying rhythm of consumer choice remains constant.
