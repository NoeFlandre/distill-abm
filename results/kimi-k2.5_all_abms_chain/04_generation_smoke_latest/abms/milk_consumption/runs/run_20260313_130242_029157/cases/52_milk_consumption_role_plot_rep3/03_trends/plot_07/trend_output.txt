The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed follows a three-phase trajectory characterized by rapid early adoption, an extended stagnation period, and late-stage resurgence. At initialization (step 0), consumption begins near zero units, reflecting the initially low health perceptions (1.69) and environmental perceptions (1.12) of alternative milk varieties combined with the strong incumbent habit strength (10.0) favoring whole milk.

Between steps 0 and 11, the metric undergoes steep acceleration, rising from approximately 10 to 1200–1280 units. This initial growth phase represents the penetration of alternative milk consumption among early adopters, driven by social influence mechanisms operating through the Watts-Strogatz network structure (parameter 8.0) with moderate interaction probability (0.3) and social susceptibility (0.2).

From step 11 through step 25, the system enters a prolonged plateau where consumption stabilizes in the 1180–1250 range. During this middle phase, the trajectory exhibits minor oscillations, including a temporary decline to approximately 1180 around step 18 before recovering to 1220–1240. This stagnation reflects a temporary equilibrium where habit maintenance mechanisms (habit-threshold: 2.0) and social blindness (0.51) create resistance to further switching among the remaining population, despite ongoing social conformity pressures (0.28) and active normative influence.

Beginning at step 25, the metric resumes upward momentum, climbing from approximately 1220 to reach 1450–1480 units by step 32. This secondary growth phase suggests that accumulated social pressure, combined with memory-based learning over the five-step memory lifetime, eventually overwhelms habitual barriers among late-adopting agents. The justification mechanism (0.61) and cognitive dissonance threshold (0.15) likely facilitate this final transition by allowing agents to rationalize breaking long-standing consumption habits.

Across all simulation repetitions, the trajectories demonstrate high consistency with minimal variance, particularly in the plateau and final phases, indicating robust convergence of the stochastic processes governing agent decision-making. The crossover from whole milk dominance to alternative milk majority appears to occur within the first 11 steps, with the remaining dynamics representing the gradual conversion of resistant segments of the population.
