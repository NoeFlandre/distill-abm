The trajectory of decline is not uniform but rather exhibits distinct phases characterized by varying rates of change. During the initial interval from steps 0 through 5, the series experiences a rapid contraction, with statistically significant decreasing trends detected through non-parametric testing. This early phase likely corresponds to the initial disruption of incumbent consumption habits, where the high initial habit strength parameter begins to erode as agents encounter novel health and environmental information regarding alternative milk varieties.

Between steps 5 and 14, the rate of decline moderates yet remains statistically significant and downward. Inflection points around steps 13 and 16 suggest oscillations in the rate of habit decay and social diffusion. During this middle period, social influence mechanisms—operating through the Watts-Strogatz network topology with moderate interaction probability and social susceptibility—likely facilitate the gradual normalization of alternative milk consumption, though the process encounters periodic resistance as agents with stronger habitual attachments maintain their incumbent choices.

A notable stabilization occurs between steps 15 and 19, where statistical tests indicate no significant directional trend, suggesting a temporary equilibrium where the forces of habit maintenance and social or informational pressure reach approximate balance. This plateau, peaking locally around step 19 at approximately 774 units, may represent a population subset resistant to initial persuasion campaigns, requiring accumulated social proof or generational turnover to shift.

Following this plateau, the series resumes its decline between steps 20 and 24 with renewed statistical significance, potentially reflecting threshold effects in social influence or critical mass dynamics where late adopters finally succumb to dominant social norms. The final phase from steps 25 through 30 exhibits another period of reduced volatility with no significant trend, as the market approaches saturation with minimal remaining whole milk consumption, ultimately stabilizing near the terminal value of 253 units.

For decision makers in public health and agricultural policy, these dynamics suggest that behavior change campaigns targeting dietary shifts must account for extended time horizons and phased intervention strategies. The initial rapid decline phase indicates that early adopters respond readily to health information campaigns, suggesting that initial marketing efforts should focus on information dissemination. However, the prolonged middle phase and temporary plateau reveal significant habit persistence and social network inertia, implying that mid-campaign strategies must emphasize social norming and community-based interventions rather than purely informational approaches. The final convergence suggests that once alternative products achieve sufficient market penetration and social legitimacy, the transition becomes self-sustaining, allowing policy resources to shift toward maintenance rather than conversion efforts.
