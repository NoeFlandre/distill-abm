The deviation between consumers’ held health values and the values implied by their actual milk choices initiates at zero and accumulates steadily throughout the 31-year transition period, ultimately reaching approximately 0.73 by the final time step. This trajectory indicates an expanding psychological gap wherein consumers increasingly recognize the superior health attributes of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties yet persist in habitual whole milk consumption, creating mounting cognitive dissonance within the population.

The growth manifests distinctly across three temporal phases. During the initial period (time steps 0 through 4, corresponding roughly to 1974–1978), the deviation accelerates rapidly with an initial slope of 0.063, followed by a sustained rate of 0.041 through step 9. This steep early ascent reflects the immediate impact of emerging health information penetrating the consumer network, wherein agents begin updating their health perceptions of alternatives from the initial baseline of 1.69 while maintaining strong incumbent habits (strength 10.0) for whole milk. A pronounced inflection occurs around time steps 4 and 6, after which the rate of dissonance growth decelerates significantly. The oscillatory patterns detected with a dominant period of 15 steps (centered near step 16–19) indicate cyclical waves of justification behavior and partial reconsideration during this interval.

Notably, the median deviation (0.558) exceeds the mean (0.497), indicating that the population spends disproportionate time at higher dissonance levels during the latter half of the simulation. The consistent monotonic increase across all rolling windows—with significant Mann-Kendall trends throughout—confirms that cognitive dissonance never resolves within the modeled timeframe; rather, it compounds as the market transition approaches completion.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

These dynamics reveal that during major dietary transitions, consumer populations can sustain significant and growing psychological tension for decades. Public health campaigns should recognize that information dissemination creates immediate dissonance but not immediate behavior change; interventions targeting the "middle plateau" phase (when dissonance growth slows but persists) may prove most cost-effective for converting resistant segments. Furthermore, the late-stage acceleration suggests that policy makers and marketers should prepare for intensified rationalization behaviors among final holdouts, as these consumers face maximum psychological pressure while maintaining habitual choices. The persistent gap between attitudes and behaviors underscores the necessity of addressing habitual mechanisms directly, rather than relying solely on health messaging, to reduce the psychological burden of dietary transitions.
