**Trend Analysis of Skimmed and Semi-Skimmed Milk Consumption (1974-2005 Simulation)**

The simulations reveal a four-phase adoption trajectory for alternative milk consumption across 32 time steps, representing the 31-year historical period. 

**Phase 1: Initial Stagnation (Steps 0-1)**
Consumption remains negligible, starting at approximately 5-10 units and showing minimal change through step 1. This initial inertia reflects the model parameters where whole milk maintains dominant incumbent habit strength (10.0) and agents hold relatively low initial perceptions of alternative milk health (1.69) and environmental benefits (1.12).

**Phase 2: Rapid Early Adoption (Steps 1-11)**
A steep acceleration occurs between steps 1 and 11, with consumption rising from approximately 50 units to between 1,200 and 1,280 units. The growth rate peaks between steps 5 and 10, where values climb from roughly 500 to over 1,150 units—representing a 130% increase in just five time steps. By step 11, most simulation runs reach approximately 1,250-1,280 units. This phase captures the initial breakthrough where accumulated consumption experiences (within the 5-step memory window) and social network influences (operating at 30% interaction probability and 20% individual susceptibility) begin overwhelming the habitual preference for whole milk.

**Phase 3: Extended Plateau (Steps 12-25)**
The trajectory enters a prolonged stabilization period lasting approximately 14 time steps, with consumption fluctuating between roughly 1,180 and 1,250 units. Following the initial peak around step 11-12, the data show a gradual decline to approximately 1,180 units by step 18, followed by stabilization around 1,220-1,240 units through step 25. Minor oscillations characterize this phase, with some runs dipping to 1,180 while others maintain 1,240-1,260 levels. This plateau suggests a temporary equilibrium where habit maintenance mechanisms (habit threshold: 2.0) and social blindness effects (0.51) create resistance to further adoption, despite ongoing social conformity pressures (0.28 weight).

**Phase 4: Late-Stage Acceleration (Steps 26-32)**
A secondary growth phase emerges in the final period, with consumption accelerating from approximately 1,250 units to reach 1,450-1,480 units by step 32. This represents an increase of roughly 200 units (16%) over the final six steps, with particularly sharp growth between steps 27 and 30 (climbing from ~1,250 to ~1,400). The convergence across simulation runs tightens considerably in this phase, with most trajectories clustering between 1,420 and 1,480 units by the conclusion.

**Variability and Crossover Dynamics**
Across the 1,000-agent population simulations, trajectories demonstrate high consistency in the overall S-curve pattern, though individual runs exhibit minor variations in plateau depth and late-stage timing. The historical crossover point—where skimmed and semi-skimmed consumption surpasses whole milk—appears to occur between steps 10 and 12, when consumption first sustains levels above 1,200 units.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The pronounced plateau phase spanning approximately 14 time steps (representing nearly half the simulation period) suggests that market transitions in habitual food categories face significant mid-cycle resistance. Interventions specifically targeting habit disruption—such as environmental restructuring or point-of-purchase prompts—may be necessary during steps 12-25 to prevent adoption stagnation.

The secondary acceleration in steps 26-32 indicates that normative tipping points can reignite growth even after extended periods of market stability. This suggests that long-term strategic planning should account for multi-decade adoption curves rather than expecting continuous linear growth. Policymakers and marketers should prepare for potential "middle slumps" in adoption and reserve resources for later-stage normative reinforcement when social conformity mechanisms (evidenced by the tightening variance across runs) become most potent.

The consistency across simulation runs implies robust systemic behavior in consumer transitions, suggesting that once initial habit barriers are overcome and social networks activate, market shifts toward healthier alternatives follow predictable trajectories, albeit with potentially lengthy intermediate equilibrium periods.
