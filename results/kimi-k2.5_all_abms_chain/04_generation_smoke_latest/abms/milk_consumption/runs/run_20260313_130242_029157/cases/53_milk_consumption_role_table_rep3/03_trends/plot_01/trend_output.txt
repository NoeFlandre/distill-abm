The consumption trajectory of whole milk exhibits a pronounced secular decline from 1974 to 2005, with average weekly per-agent consumption falling from approximately 2654.8 units to 253.1 units—a reduction of nearly 90%. This downward trend, while consistently negative, displays non-linear characteristics with eight distinct inflection points at temporal indices 5, 13, 16, 19, 24, 25, 27, and 30, indicating periodic shifts in the velocity of behavioral change.

The initial phase (1974–approximately 1979) demonstrates the steepest rate of decline, with consumption dropping sharply from the initial habitual dominance of whole milk. Following this precipitous fall, the middle period (approximately 1980–1995) exhibits a decelerating but continued decrease punctuated by minor local oscillations. Specifically, temporary fluctuations appear around index 18–19 (valley at approximately 760.0 followed by a peak at 774.0), index 26–27 (valley at 594.3 followed by peak at 621.0), and index 29–30 (valley at 323.5 followed by peak at 335.2). These micro-fluctuations suggest intermittent resistance to the dominant downward trend or stochastic variations in habit-breaking events.

The final phase (approximately 1995–2005) shows consumption approaching asymptotic low levels, declining from roughly 600 units to the terminal value of 253.1. Throughout this trajectory, the median consumption of 816.9 units lies substantially below the mean of 1079.8 units, indicating a right-skewed distribution driven by the high initial values of the 1974 baseline.

This consumption pattern reflects the interplay of habitual maintenance, evolving health and environmental perceptions, and social network influences. The steep early decline corresponds to the erosion of initially strong habitual preferences (habit strength 10.0) for whole milk as agents accumulate positive perceptions of alternative milk varieties (initial health perception 1.69, environmental perception 1.12) and respond to social pressures within the Watts-Strogatz network structure. The varying rates of decline across inflection points capture the threshold-based or probabilistic mechanisms of habit-breaking interacting with social conformity pressures (conformity weight 0.28, social susceptibility 0.2, interaction probability 0.3). The minor local peaks and valleys represent stochastic fluctuations inherent in individual-level decision-making and the partial social blindness (0.51) and justification mechanisms (0.61) that occasionally slow or temporarily reverse the aggregate decline before the dominant downward trajectory reasserts itself.
