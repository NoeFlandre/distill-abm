The mean weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent initiates at approximately zero, consistent with the 1974 baseline where whole milk dominated the market. During the first ten steps, consumption undergoes rapid, approximately exponential growth, surging from negligible levels to between 1,200 and 1,280 units. This swift initial adoption reflects the success of disposition mechanisms in triggering consideration of alternatives, as agents encounter health and environmental information that challenges incumbent whole milk preferences.

From step 10 through step 27, the system enters an extended plateau phase characterized by relative stability with minor oscillations. Consumption fluctuates between approximately 1,180 and 1,250 units, with a temporary decline to the lower end of this range around step 18 followed by gradual recovery toward step 25. This stagnation suggests that habit persistence and cognitive dissonance thresholds temporarily balance the influence of social norms and informational campaigns, creating a quasi-equilibrium where adoption struggles to advance despite ongoing social diffusion through the Watts-Strogatz network topology.

Beginning around step 28 and continuing through step 32, the metric exhibits a pronounced secondary acceleration, climbing sharply from approximately 1,250 to between 1,440 and 1,480 units. This late-stage surge indicates that accumulated social influence effects and progressive habituation to skimmed varieties eventually overwhelm resistance mechanisms, enabling consumption to reach mature adoption levels. Across simulation runs, trajectories demonstrate tight convergence during the initial growth phase and final acceleration, but display increased dispersion during the critical transition window between steps 8 and 12, reflecting sensitivity to stochastic variation in agent interaction probabilities during the shift from early to mainstream adoption.

These dynamics suggest that innovation diffusion in food choice contexts may proceed through distinct stages with different driving forces: initial information-driven adoption, habit-locked stagnation, and socially-validated maturation. For public health decision makers, the simulation indicates that early rapid gains in healthy behavior should not be expected to sustain linearly; the 15-step plateau demonstrates that incumbent habits can stall progress for extended periods. The eventual breakthrough to higher consumption levels (steps 28-32) implies that maintaining consistent long-term intervention strategies may be necessary to allow social norm effects to reach critical mass, rather than expecting immediate universal adoption after initial awareness campaigns.
