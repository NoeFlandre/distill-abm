The deviation between agents' held health values and their implied consumption choices begins at zero in 1974, then follows a prolonged upward trajectory to reach approximately 0.73 by 2005. This overall trend indicates a widening divergence between health perceptions and actual milk consumption behaviors throughout the three-decade dietary transition.

The initial phase from 1974 through approximately 1978 exhibits rapid growth in the deviation metric, increasing steeply from 0.00 to roughly 0.25. This early acceleration reflects the immediate friction between deeply entrenched whole milk consumption habits and emerging health information favoring lower-fat alternatives, suggesting that agents initially maintained incumbent choices despite accumulating countervailing health knowledge.

Between approximately 1979 and 1988, the rate of increase moderates substantially, with the deviation expanding more gradually from 0.25 to approximately 0.45. During this deceleration phase, health consciousness continued to diffuse through the Watts-Strogatz social network, yet behavioral change increasingly lagged behind perception shifts as habit strength and social blindness parameters buffered agents against immediate modification of their choice functions.

A pronounced plateau emerges from roughly 1989 to 1998, spanning approximately ten years, where the deviation increases minimally from 0.45 to around 0.50. This stagnation indicates that the population reached a temporary equilibrium wherein cognitive dissonance thresholds permitted agents to sustain consumption patterns that contradicted their stated health values without triggering sufficient disposition to reconsider alternatives. The persistence of high initial habit strength appears to have stabilized the system during this interval, creating a significant gap between what agents knew about health benefits and what they actually consumed.

However, beginning around 1999 and continuing through 2005, the deviation resumes a moderate but steady upward trajectory, climbing from approximately 0.55 to 0.73. This late-stage increase suggests that sustained global norm pressures and accumulated social influence eventually overcame habit persistence thresholds, particularly among late-adopting agents who maintained stronger disconnects between their health valuations and milk choices until the final years of the transition.

Superimposed upon these secular trends, the metric exhibits oscillatory behavior with a dominant periodicity of approximately fifteen years, manifesting in fluctuating patterns around the late 1980s and again in the early 2000s. These cyclical variations likely reflect periodic waves of behavioral reconsideration and post-choice evaluation within the agent population, where consumption choices temporarily aligned with or diverged from health values as agents updated their scored choice functions in response to social network interactions.

For decision makers, the substantial and persistent deviation between health values and actual choices demonstrates that dietary behavioral change significantly lags behind health education campaigns, with a pronounced decade-long plateau where increased awareness fails to translate into proportional behavioral shifts. Public health interventions should anticipate this delayed response structure and consider phased strategies to overcome habit thresholds during middle-period stagnation, while leveraging the cyclical reconsideration patterns evident in the oscillatory components to time interventions during windows of heightened behavioral plasticity. The eventual late-stage convergence suggests that patience in policy implementation may be required, as social norm effects eventually bridge the value-action gap even when early educational efforts produce limited immediate behavioral returns.
