The mean quantity of whole milk consumed exhibits a pronounced long-term decline throughout the 1974–2005 simulation period, falling from an initial level of approximately 2,655 units to roughly 253 units by the end of the timeframe. This trajectory reflects the systematic erosion of incumbent consumption habits initially reinforced by strong habitual strength, as consumers gradually transition toward alternative milk varieties.

The decline is steepest during the initial five to six time steps (approximately 1974–1979), where consumption drops precipitously from its maximum value. This rapid early descent suggests that a significant segment of the population with lower habit resilience or higher susceptibility to health and environmental information shifts relatively quickly once alternative options gain visibility. The inflection occurring around the late 1970s indicates the depletion of this easily convertible segment, after which the rate of decline moderates considerably.

Between approximately 1980 and the late 1980s, consumption continues to decrease but at a decelerating pace, with notable inflections around 1987 suggesting periods where remaining consumers exhibit heightened resistance to change. This phase represents the difficult middle period of market transition, where habitual behavior—initialized at maximum strength—exerts persistent pull on remaining whole milk consumers, requiring sustained social influence and perceptual shifts to overcome.

The period from the early 1990s through the early 2000s displays non-monotonic characteristics, with temporary elevations in consumption occurring around 1993 (approximately 774 units) and again around 2001 (approximately 621 units). These fluctuations likely reflect temporary reassertions of habitual behavior among resistant consumer segments or oscillations in social norm perceptions during the late-stage transition. Despite these temporary reversals, the overarching trajectory remains downward, with inflection points around 1998–1999 and 2004 marking final phases of deceleration as the market approaches saturation for alternative milk adoption.

By 2005, whole milk consumption has declined to roughly one-tenth of its initial level, indicating that the incumbent product has transitioned from a dominant market position to a niche consumption choice. This prolonged, non-linear decline illustrates that market transitions in habitual consumer goods operate on decadal timescales, with distinct phases of rapid early adoption among flexible consumers, followed by protracted struggle against entrenched habits among resistant segments, and concluding with a low-level equilibrium where incumbent products persist among small, highly habituated populations.

For decision makers, these trends imply that disrupting established consumption habits requires sustained, multi-decade interventions targeting health perceptions and social norms, as initial rapid gains among early adopters give way to difficult conversion of highly resistant consumer segments. Temporary reversals during transition periods suggest that maintaining consistent messaging and availability is crucial to prevent habit reassertion, particularly during the late stages of market shift when remaining incumbent consumers exhibit the strongest habit resilience.
