The simulation of British milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 demonstrates a pronounced and continuous decline in mean whole milk quantity across the 33 time steps, beginning at 2654.81 units and falling to 253.10 units by the final period. This trajectory reflects the historical market transition from whole milk dominance to the widespread adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives, with an overall series mean of 1078.20 units and substantial variation (standard deviation of 693.08) indicative of the volatile nature of the transition.

The decline occurs in three distinct phases characterized by differing rates of change. During the initial phase (steps 0 through approximately 10), the mean quantity decreases sharply from 2654.81 to roughly 960–1000 units, representing a rapid initial abandonment of whole milk consumption. This steep reduction aligns with the period of early adoption where agents, despite beginning with high incumbent habit strength of 10.0, overcome habitual inertia through the accumulation of positive perceptions regarding alternative milk varieties (initialized with health perceptions of 1.69 and environmental perceptions of 1.12) combined with social influence mechanisms operating within the Watts-Strogatz network structure (network-parameter of 8.0). The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms statistically significant decreasing trends during this initial period, with windows covering steps 0–4 and 5–9 showing steep negative slopes of -115.85 and -221.11 respectively (p = 0.027).

Following this rapid descent, the second phase (steps 10 through 20) exhibits a moderated but steady decline, with values decreasing from approximately 960 to 760–774 units. This period includes minor fluctuations, specifically a local minimum of 760.00 at step 18 followed by a slight rebound to 773.96 at step 19, indicating temporary stabilization or slight resistance among remaining whole milk consumers. While the window covering steps 10–14 maintains a significant decreasing trend (slope of -38.13, p = 0.027), the period spanning steps 15–19 shows no statistically significant trend (p = 0.086), suggesting that the habit threshold of 2.0 temporarily stabilizes consumption among resistant agents with high habitual attachment.

The final phase (steps 20 through 32) demonstrates a more gradual decline characterized by increased volatility, with the mean quantity falling from approximately 773.96 to the terminal value of 253.10. This period exhibits notable oscillations including local peaks at step 27 (620.98) and step 30 (335.18), interspersed with valleys at step 26 (594.35) and step 29 (323.46). While the rolling window covering steps 20–24 maintains a significant decreasing trend (slope of -17.45, p = 0.027), the final window spanning steps 25–29 shows no significant trend (p = 0.086), consistent with the observed fluctuations. These oscillations likely reflect the stochastic nature of the psychological mechanisms governing habit breaking, combined with varying degrees of social susceptibility (0.2) and social blindness (0.51) among the remaining consumer base, as well as the influence of social conformity (0.28) and justification mechanisms (0.61) that allow agents to rationalize maintaining or changing consumption patterns.

The trajectory features eight distinct inflection points at steps 5, 13, 16, 19, 24, 25, 27, and 30, marking transitions between these phases of rapid, moderate, and fluctuating decline. These inflection points correspond to shifts in the rate at which the population overcomes initial habit strength through the interplay of memory-limited learning (memory-lifetime of 5.0), evolving perceptions of health and environmental attributes, and network-mediated social influence (p-interact of 0.3). The continuous downward movement, despite temporary plateaus and minor reversals, ultimately achieves a 90.5% reduction from initial whole milk consumption levels, successfully capturing the historical crossover point where alternative milk varieties surpassed whole milk in consumer preference.
