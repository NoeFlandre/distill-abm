The mean weekly whole milk consumption per agent initiates at approximately 2,650 units and follows a convex declining trajectory over 32 time steps, ultimately reaching roughly 250 units. The most pronounced decrease occurs during the initial 10 steps, where consumption plummets by approximately 1,700 units to reach 950. This rapid early decline reflects the substantial pool of consumers whose initial habit strength, despite being set at maximum values, proves insufficient to withstand the combined influence of positive alternative perceptions and social network effects.

During the subsequent 10 steps (10-20), the decline moderates substantially, with consumption falling by only about 200 units to reach 750. This deceleration aligns with the theoretical expectation that consumers remaining after the initial wave possess habit strengths exceeding the threshold of 2.0 or exhibit higher social blindness (0.51), rendering them resistant to the influence mechanisms that drove early switching. Notably, outcomes across simulation runs converge tightly during this middle phase, suggesting that once the system passes the initial transition point, the aggregate behavior becomes highly predictable regardless of stochastic variation in individual interactions.

In the final phase from step 20 to 32, consumption continues its downward trend but exhibits greater variability across runs, ultimately settling between 250 and 300 units. The increased dispersion in outcomes during this period indicates that stochastic elements in the psychological processing mechanisms—particularly the probability-based reconsideration of habits and the probabilistic nature of social interactions (p-interact = 0.3)—exert stronger influence when only the most habitually entrenched consumers remain. The persistent residual consumption at the terminal step suggests that a subset of agents maintain habit strengths sufficient to withstand all but the most sustained social and informational pressures.

**Implications for decision makers:** The steep initial decline demonstrates that market transitions can achieve rapid momentum when new products enter with favorable health and environmental perceptions within well-connected social networks. However, the convex curvature and substantial terminal values indicate that the final 10-20% of market share may require decades to convert or may remain permanently entrenched due to habit formation. The predictability of the middle phase suggests reliable forecasting windows during market transitions, while the late-stage variance implies increasing uncertainty in predicting when or whether complete conversion will occur. Strategies targeting the "hard core" of resistant consumers may need to differ fundamentally from those effective in early adoption phases.
