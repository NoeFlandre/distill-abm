The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the implied values of their milk choices exhibits a sustained monotonic increase from 0.0000 at initiation to 0.7251 at the conclusion of the 31-year simulation period. This trajectory indicates a progressive decoupling between consumer health valuations and their actual consumption behaviors as the market transition unfolds.  

The initial phase, spanning time steps 0 through 4, demonstrates the most rapid acceleration with a slope of 0.063. During this formative period, the deviation increases sharply from zero as agents begin encountering health information about alternative milk varieties that conflicts with their entrenched whole milk habits (initial habit strength of 10.0). This early divergence reflects the onset of cognitive dissonance as the pre-1974 habit-driven equilibrium encounters initial pressures from evolving health perceptions (initial alternative health mean of 1.69) and nascent social influence mechanisms.  

Following inflection points at steps 4 and 6, the expansion moderates considerably between steps 5 and 24, with slopes declining to 0.041, then 0.012, and reaching a minimum of 0.003 during the interval spanning steps 15 through 19. This prolonged plateau phase, corresponding roughly to the 1980s and early 1990s, suggests a temporary stabilization of value-choice misalignment during the historical crossover period when skimmed and semi-skimmed consumption challenged whole milk dominance. The reduced velocity of deviation growth during this middle period likely reflects the compensatory effects of social conformity mechanisms (social-conformity 0.28) and justification processes (justification 0.61) as agents rationalize transitional choices within their social networks (network-parameter 8.0, watts-strogatz topology).  

A notable inflection occurs at step 22, after which the deviation resumes accelerated growth with a slope of 0.019 through step 29, ultimately reaching the maximum value of 0.7251 at step 32. This late-phase intensification suggests that as the market approaches the 2005 endpoint, remaining pockets of habitual whole milk consumption generate heightened psychological tension against pervasive health-oriented social norms, or alternatively, that late adopters of alternative milks experience elevated dissonance as they attempt to align deeply ingrained whole milk preferences with new consumption patterns.  

Oscillatory patterns detected around indices 18-20 and 27-31 indicate secondary fluctuations superimposed on the primary trend, potentially reflecting cyclical network interaction effects (p-interact 0.3) or memory window effects (memory-lifetime 5.0) where agents periodically reassess their consumption history against current health valuations. The series maintains consistent directional behavior across all rolling Mann-Kendall windows, with the median value (0.5584) exceeding the mean (0.4965), indicating that the distribution of deviation values skews toward higher magnitudes in the latter half of the transition period. The relatively constrained standard deviation (0.1702) across the 30 simulation runs suggests robust consistency in the psychological trajectory of value-choice divergence despite stochastic elements in individual agent decision-making.
