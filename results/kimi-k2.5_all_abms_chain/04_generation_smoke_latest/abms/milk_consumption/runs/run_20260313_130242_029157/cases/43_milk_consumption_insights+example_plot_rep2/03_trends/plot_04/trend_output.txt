The total number of skimmed/semi-skimmed milk choices initiates at approximately zero and follows a trajectory characterized by initial quiescence, subsequent acceleration, and sustained linear growth, ultimately reaching between 25,500 and 26,000 cumulative choices by step 32. Across all simulation iterations, outcomes demonstrate high consistency with minimal deviation, suggesting deterministic macro-level patterns emerge despite individual-level stochasticity.

During the initial five steps, accumulated choices remain below 1,500, indicating limited early adoption. This lag phase reflects the model's initialization with high incumbent habit strength (10.0) for whole milk and initial health and environmental perception means of 1.69 and 1.12 respectively, which are insufficient to trigger immediate displacement of established consumption patterns given the habit threshold of 2.0 and memory lifetime of 5.0.

Between steps 5 and 15, the adoption rate accelerates substantially, with cumulative choices increasing from approximately 1,500 to 10,000. This rapid growth corresponds to the threshold where social influence mechanisms—operating through the Watts-Strogatz network topology with interaction probability 0.3 and social susceptibility 0.2—combine with evolving perceptions to overcome habitual inertia. The social conformity parameter of 0.28 facilitates this transition as agents increasingly weight peer choices in their decision functions, while the cognitive dissonance threshold of 0.15 enables agents to justify deviations from previous behaviors.

From step 15 through the conclusion at step 32, the metric exhibits approximately linear growth, adding roughly 1,000 to 1,100 choices per step. By step 20, the cumulative total reaches approximately 15,000, and by step 30, it approaches 25,000. This sustained linear increase without signs of saturation suggests the transition from whole to skimmed/semi-skimed milk remained an ongoing process through 2005, with the trajectory implying continued growth beyond the simulation timeframe had the model extended further.

The convergence of outcomes across repeated runs indicates that once initial habit barriers diminish, the interplay of network effects, social norms, and justification mechanisms produces highly predictable adoption patterns. The final value represents approximately 80 percent of the theoretical maximum possible choices (32,000), suggesting substantial market transformation while indicating residual preference for whole milk among a persistent minority of the 1,000-agent population.

Implications for decision makers: The predictable linear growth phase suggests that public health initiatives targeting milk consumption can anticipate consistent adoption rates once initial habit barriers are overcome, typically within the first 10 steps of the transition period. However, the persistence of roughly 20 percent non-adoption by the simulation's end highlights the challenge of converting remaining holdout consumers, suggesting that universal adoption may require targeted interventions beyond standard social influence mechanisms or extended timeframes beyond the 32-year window.
