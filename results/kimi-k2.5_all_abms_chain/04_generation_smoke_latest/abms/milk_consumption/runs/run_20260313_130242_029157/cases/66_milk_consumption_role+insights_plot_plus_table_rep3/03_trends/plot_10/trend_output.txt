In the context of the British milk transition, this metric captures the evolving heterogeneity in how consumers weighted health attributes, environmental considerations, habitual tendencies, and social pressures when selecting milk varieties. The initial uniformity (value = 0.0000) reflects the homogenized preference structure of 1974, when incumbent whole milk habits were universally strong. The progressive divergence indicates that as the market transitioned, agents developed increasingly individualized evaluation frameworks—some maintaining strong habitual attachments to whole milk while others rapidly adopted alternative varieties based on health perceptions or social influence.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The emergence of substantial choice function heterogeneity (reaching 75% deviation between extreme evaluations by step 32) suggests that successful market intervention requires segmented rather than uniform strategies. The rapid early divergence (steps 0–7) indicates a critical window where consumer preferences are most malleable; communications emphasizing health and environmental benefits would achieve maximum penetration during this phase. The subsequent deceleration implies that late-stage conversion becomes increasingly difficult and costly as consumer evaluation frameworks crystallize. The sustained, albeit slowing, growth through step 32 suggests that complete market stabilization requires extended time horizons, and premature abandonment of transition-supporting policies may leave significant segments locked into suboptimal habitual consumption patterns.
