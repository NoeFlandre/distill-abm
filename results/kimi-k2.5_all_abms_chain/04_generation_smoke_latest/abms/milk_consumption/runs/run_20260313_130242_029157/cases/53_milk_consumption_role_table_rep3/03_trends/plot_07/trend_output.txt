The simulation depicts a classic S-curve adoption pattern of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption spanning the 1974–2005 period. At initialization representing 1974, alternative milk consumption remains minimal at approximately 5.29 units, reflecting the historical market dominance of whole milk reinforced by high initial incumbent habit strength (10.0) and weak baseline perceptions of alternative health (1.69) and environmental (1.12) attributes.

During the initial diffusion phase encompassing approximately the first decade (time steps 0–9), the trajectory exhibits rapid monotonic growth characterized by significant positive trends with slopes of approximately 112.8 and 139.0 units per time step in consecutive windows. This early acceleration reflects the threshold-breaking or probabilistic psychological mechanisms enabling agents to overcome habitual whole milk preferences as accumulated health perceptions and social influence mechanisms (interaction probability 0.3, susceptibility 0.2) facilitate the initial escape from incumbent choices.

Following this expansion, the dynamics enter a prolonged plateau phase extending from approximately time step 10 through 24, where growth stagnates and exhibits no significant directional trend. During this middle period, consumption oscillates between local maxima of approximately 1265.47 (time step 11), 1240.35 (time step 13), and 1226.86 (time step 21) and minima of approximately 1233.62 (time step 12), 1183.39 (time step 18), and 1209.70 (time step 23). These fluctuations exhibit a dominant oscillatory period of approximately 15 time steps, suggesting market saturation dynamics where the remaining consumer segment resists conversion due to high habitual thresholds, social blindness parameters (0.51), or network clustering effects.

In the final consolidation phase (approximately time steps 25–29), the model demonstrates renewed significant growth with a slope of 48.5 units per time step, representing late-stage market penetration. By the terminal time step representing 2005, consumption reaches 1456.60 units, indicating that skimmed and semi-skimmed milk varieties have achieved dominant market position, completing the historical transition from whole milk dominance.

This trajectory reflects the interplay of habit formation mechanisms, social network diffusion processes, and shifting attribute perceptions, where initial resistance from high incumbent habit strength eventually yields to accumulated health information and normative social pressures (conformity weight 0.28), followed by saturation effects among resistant subpopulations, and final consolidation as the alternative becomes the new market standard.
