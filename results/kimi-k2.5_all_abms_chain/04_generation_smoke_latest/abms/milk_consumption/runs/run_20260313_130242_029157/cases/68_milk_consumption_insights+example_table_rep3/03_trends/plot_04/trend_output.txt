The cumulative adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk initiates at zero during the 1974 baseline, reflecting the historical dominance of whole milk, and increases monotonically across the full temporal range to reach approximately 25,644 total choice events by 2005. 

The trajectory exhibits a classic logistic growth pattern with distinct kinetic phases. During the initial quinquennial period (1974–1978), accumulation proceeds gradually at roughly 200 choices per annum, constrained by high incumbent habit strength and initial cognitive thresholds that resist disposition triggers. This latency period corresponds to the time required for agents to overcome established behavioral routines through repeated exposure to health and environmental attribute perceptions.

A pronounced acceleration emerges between 1979 and 1994 (time steps 5–24), wherein the adoption rate intensifies from approximately 737 to 935 choices per period. This rapid escalation coincides with the activation of the Watts-Strogatz social network topology, where interaction probabilities of 0.3 and social conformity mechanisms amplify individual disposition triggers. The increasing slope through this mid-period indicates that social influence effects compound as early adopters modify the choice functions of susceptible neighbors, creating self-reinforcing cycles of behavioral transition.

During the final decade (1995–2005), the growth rate stabilizes near 935 choices per period, maintaining linear accumulation until an inflection point at time step 31 signals the onset of market saturation. This late-phase stabilization suggests the agent population approaches equilibrium, with diminishing pools of whole milk adherents available for conversion. The consistent upward trajectory through 2005 indicates that while the rate of new adoption stabilizes, the cumulative transition remains incomplete, reflecting persistent heterogeneity in habit thresholds and social susceptibility across the consumer base.

Decision makers should recognize that interventions targeting milk consumption habits face substantial initial inertia, typically requiring five or more years before achieving measurable acceleration. However, once social network effects activate during the middle period, adoption momentum becomes self-sustaining and resistant to reversal, suggesting that early investment in network-seeded information campaigns yields disproportionate returns during the acceleration phase. Additionally, the persistence of linear growth through 2005 indicates that significant minority populations maintain whole milk preferences indefinitely, necessitating targeted rather than universal strategies for the recalcitrant segment.
