The mean weekly consumption of whole milk per agent begins at approximately 2,655 units at the initial time step, representing the market dominance of whole milk in 1974. Over the first ten time steps, consumption declines rapidly and steeply, falling to roughly 1,000 units. This precipitous drop suggests that the combined mechanisms of shifting health and environmental perceptions, coupled with social influence effects, rapidly eroded the incumbent habit strength of whole milk once the transition process initiated. The rate of decline moderates considerably between time steps 10 and 20, with consumption fluctuating between approximately 760 and 850 units. This plateau phase indicates that while the majority of agents had transitioned to alternative milk varieties, a substantial residual segment maintained whole milk consumption through strong habitual persistence and resistance to social pressure. From time step 20 onward, the decline resumes but at a more gradual pace, with consumption falling from approximately 750 units to 253 units by the final time step. This late-stage reduction suggests that even habitually entrenched consumers eventually succumbed to accumulated social influence and evolving perceptions, though a core loyal segment remained. Statistical analysis confirms significant decreasing trends in the early phases (steps 0-14) and late phase (steps 20-24), with non-significant trends during the middle plateau (steps 15-19) and final period (steps 25-29), indicating periods of transition equilibrium.

For decision makers, these dynamics suggest that market transitions away from established products can occur rapidly once critical mass is achieved, but planners should anticipate a persistent loyal core that resists complete conversion. Interventions are most effective when targeted early in the transition cycle to accelerate the initial decline, while later efforts may yield diminishing returns due to habit persistence among residual consumers.
