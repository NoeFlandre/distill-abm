The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent commences at approximately zero units during the initial time step, remaining stagnant through step 1. This initial latency reflects the model's starting conditions where incumbent whole milk habits are maximally strong (initial habit strength 10.0) and agent perceptions of alternative milk health and environmental attributes are at baseline lows (1.69 and 1.12 respectively), preventing immediate adoption despite the presence of social influence mechanisms.

Between steps 1 and 11, consumption undergoes a rapid, approximately linear escalation, surging from near zero to roughly 1,250 units. This steep ascent represents the critical transition period where accumulated perceptions of health and environmental benefits, combined with social network effects propagating through the Watts-Strogatz topology (network-parameter 8.0), begin to overcome the high habitual barriers to change. The spread across simulation runs widens considerably during this phase, indicating that stochastic factors in social interaction timing (p-interact 0.3) and individual susceptibility (social-susceptibility 0.2) generate divergent adoption trajectories during the market tipping point.

From step 11 through step 26, the trajectory enters a prolonged plateau phase characterized by oscillation between approximately 1,180 and 1,280 units. This stabilization suggests a temporary equilibrium where early adopters have established new consumption habits while the remaining population of agents with high habit thresholds or lower social conformity (0.28) resist further switching. A pronounced trough occurs around step 18, where consumption dips to its lowest point in this middle period (~1,180), potentially reflecting a temporary backlash as habit maintenance mechanisms stabilize among existing alternative milk consumers before social norm pressures reaccelerate diffusion.

After step 26, the metric enters a second growth phase, climbing sharply from approximately 1,250 to reach roughly 1,450 units by step 32. This late-stage surge indicates that sustained normative influence and the cumulative effect of social learning eventually penetrate the remaining resistant segments of the population, driving market saturation toward the end of the simulation period. Variance across runs persists through this final phase but remains stable, suggesting that while random variations in network interactions affect the timing of adoption waves, the overall macro-level shift toward alternative milk consumption is structurally robust under the specified parameter configuration.

For decision-makers, the dual-phase growth pattern—with an initial rapid rise followed by a significant plateau—suggests that market transitions in habitual consumer goods may experience extended periods of stagnation after early success. Interventions targeting the plateau phase (approximately steps 12-25) could accelerate overall transition speed, as the model indicates that social conformity mechanisms and norm activation become critical drivers only after a critical mass of adoption has occurred and stabilized.
