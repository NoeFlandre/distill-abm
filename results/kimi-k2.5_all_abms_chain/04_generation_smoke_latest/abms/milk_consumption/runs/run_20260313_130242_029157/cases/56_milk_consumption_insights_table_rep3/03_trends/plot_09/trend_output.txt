The simulation spanning 33 time steps reveals a general upward trajectory in the habit-driven reinforcement of alternative milk choices, with the mean habit factor initiating at a neutral baseline of 1.0000 and culminating at 1.5879. This progression indicates a substantial strengthening of habitual preferences toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties over the modeled period from 1974 to 2005.

The temporal dynamics exhibit distinct phases. During the initial interval (time steps 0-4), the metric remains essentially static at approximately 1.0, showing no statistically significant trend. This stagnation reflects the initial market conditions where whole milk dominance and strong incumbent habits suppress alternative choice reinforcement, creating a lag period before measurable habit shifts occur.

A transition emerges at time step 5, initiating a sustained upward progression that persists through the remainder of the simulation. Between time steps 5-9, the factor begins increasing with a slope of 0.010, followed by an acceleration phase from time steps 10-14 where the steepness increases to 0.030. This interval represents the most rapid habit formation, likely corresponding to the critical tipping point in the historical transition where social influence and repeated choices synergistically reinforced alternative milk selection.

At time step 15, a significant change point occurs, after which the growth rate moderates while remaining positive. The subsequent intervals (15-19, 20-24, and 25-29) demonstrate progressively decelerating slopes of 0.027, 0.022, and 0.018 respectively, indicating that while habit continues to strengthen alternative milk preferences, the rate of new habit formation gradually slows as the population approaches saturation.

The final segment (time steps 26-32) exhibits oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of 15 time steps, suggesting the system approaches equilibrium with minor fluctuations around the maximum value. This pattern demonstrates that habit formation operates as a self-reinforcing mechanism that initially requires significant time to overcome incumbent preferences, accelerates dramatically during the mid-transition period, and eventually saturates as alternative milk consumption becomes normalized across the agent population.

**Implications for decision makers:** The initial five-step stagnation suggests that policy interventions targeting dietary transitions should anticipate significant lag periods before measurable habit changes emerge, requiring sustained rather than short-term efforts. The subsequent acceleration phase indicates that once habits begin shifting, they create self-reinforcing cycles that can drive rapid market transformation without continuous external intervention. Finally, the saturation dynamics imply that there are natural limits to how much habit formation can drive further market penetration beyond a certain threshold, suggesting that complementary strategies may be needed once the majority of the population has adopted new consumption patterns.
