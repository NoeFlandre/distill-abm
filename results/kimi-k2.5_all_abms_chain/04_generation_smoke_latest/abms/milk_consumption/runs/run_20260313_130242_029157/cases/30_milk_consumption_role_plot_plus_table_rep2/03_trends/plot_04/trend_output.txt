The aggregate time series of cumulative consumer selections of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk varieties across the 1974–2005 period exhibits a monotonic upward trajectory characterized by three distinct phases of adoption dynamics.

Initially, at step 0 representing 1974 market conditions, cumulative alternative milk selections commence at zero, reflecting the incumbent dominance of whole milk reinforced by maximum habitual strength and nascent health and environmental perceptions. During the introductory phase spanning approximately steps 0 through 7, the adoption curve displays a concave upward curvature with a relatively modest initial growth rate of 201 choices per step. This sluggish early adoption aligns with theoretical expectations that strong habitual barriers and limited social diffusion would inhibit immediate behavioral transition, resulting in fewer than 1,500 cumulative selections by step 5.

The series transitions into an acceleration phase roughly between steps 7 and 15, wherein the slope of cumulative choices steepens considerably. By step 10, cumulative selections approximate 5,000 total choices, while by step 20, the metric reaches approximately 14,200 selections. Mann-Kendall trend analysis confirms statistically significant monotonic increases throughout all observation windows (p=0.027), with slope coefficients escalating from 737 during steps 5–9 to 927 during steps 10–14, indicating gathering momentum in consumer transition behaviors. This acceleration corresponds to the mechanism wherein social influence and evolving health perceptions progressively overcome initial habit thresholds.

In the mature phase spanning steps 15 through 32, the growth trajectory stabilizes into an approximately linear pattern with slope coefficients plateauing near 935 choices per step across consecutive five-step windows. The series terminates at step 32 with a cumulative total of 25,644 alternative milk selections. Notably, the variance across the 30 replicate series remains tightly bounded throughout, suggesting robust deterministic dynamics underlying the stochastic social influence mechanisms, with minor oscillations becoming perceptible only in the final indices (27–31) as the system approaches the terminal time point.

This temporal pattern corresponds to the historical cross-over dynamics of British dairy consumption, wherein initial institutional inertia gave way to accelerating health-conscious adoption during the 1980s and 1990s, ultimately stabilizing into consistent year-over-year growth in reduced-fat milk preferences by the early 2000s.
