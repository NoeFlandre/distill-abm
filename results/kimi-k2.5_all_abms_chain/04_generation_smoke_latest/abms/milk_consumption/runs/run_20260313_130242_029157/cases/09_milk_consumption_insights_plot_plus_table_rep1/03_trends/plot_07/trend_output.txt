The simulation of milk consumption adoption from 1974 to 2005 reveals a three-phase trajectory in the mean weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per person. 

**Phase 1: Rapid Adoption (Steps 0-9)**
Beginning at approximately 5.3 units at step 0, representing the minimal market share of low-fat milk in 1974, consumption increases sharply and monotonically through step 9, reaching approximately 1,200 units. This period exhibits statistically significant increasing trends with steep slopes exceeding 112 units per step, indicating rapid diffusion as agents overcome initial incumbent habits and disposition triggers activate consideration of alternatives based on perceived health benefits.

**Phase 2: Stabilization and Oscillation (Steps 10-24)**
From step 10 through step 24, consumption stabilizes into a plateau ranging between approximately 1,180 and 1,265 units, with no statistically significant trend detected. This 15-step period exhibits oscillatory behavior characterized by minor fluctuations: local peaks occur at steps 11 (approximately 1,265), 13 (approximately 1,240), 19 (approximately 1,222), and 21 (approximately 1,227), interspersed with valleys at steps 12 (approximately 1,234), 18 (approximately 1,183), 20 (approximately 1,214), and 23 (approximately 1,210). These dynamics suggest that habit formation and cognitive dissonance thresholds create resistance to further change, while social influence within the Watts-Strogatz network generates temporary variations around an equilibrium.

**Phase 3: Late-Stage Growth (Steps 25-32)**
Beginning at approximately 1,220 units at step 25, consumption resumes a significant upward trajectory through the final step 32, reaching approximately 1,457 units. The period from steps 25-29 shows a statistically significant increasing trend with a slope of approximately 48.5 units per step. This renewed growth suggests that accumulated social influence, memory effects, and shifts in social norms eventually overcome habit barriers, driving additional agents to adopt lower-fat varieties even after prolonged stagnation.

**Interpretation and Implications**
The three-phase pattern reflects the interplay of disposition mechanisms, habit thresholds, and social network effects. The initial rapid growth demonstrates the effectiveness of triggering mechanisms in overcoming incumbent preferences when health perceptions are favorable. The extended plateau highlights the resilience of consumption habits and the limitations of social influence during middle phases of transition, suggesting that temporary setbacks and oscillations are inherent to behavioral change processes. The late-stage growth indicates that persistent health messaging and network effects can yield continued adoption even decades into the transition.

For public health officials and industry stakeholders, these findings suggest that dietary transition policies should anticipate distinct phases: intensive initial campaigns to trigger rapid adoption, sustained long-term interventions to overcome habit persistence during plateau periods without abandoning efforts due to temporary stagnation, and continued investment in social influence mechanisms to capture late-stage adopters. The significant oscillations during the stable phase indicate that monitoring should distinguish between temporary fluctuations and true trend reversals to avoid premature policy adjustments.
