The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent begins at approximately 2,650 milliliters at the initial time step, reflecting the historical market dominance of whole milk characteristic of 1974 starting conditions. Over the first ten time steps, consumption declines rapidly to approximately 950 milliliters, indicating a swift initial transition as agents respond to health and environmental perception factors that trigger consideration of alternative milk varieties.

From time step ten to time step twenty, the rate of decline moderates considerably, with consumption decreasing gradually from 950 to approximately 750 milliliters. This slowing transition likely reflects the persistence of strong initial habits and the influence of social network structures, where agents with high habit strength or lower susceptibility to social influence resist switching despite ongoing exposure to alternative options.

Between time steps twenty and twenty-five, consumption continues a steady decline to roughly 650 milliliters, followed by a more pronounced drop between time steps twenty-five and thirty, falling from 650 to approximately 350 milliliters. This late-stage acceleration suggests threshold effects within the social network, where declining social proof for whole milk consumption among remaining holdouts reaches critical levels, or where normative pressures intensify as alternative milk varieties achieve dominance.

By the final time step at approximately thirty-two steps, consumption stabilizes near 250 milliliters, representing a residual population of agents who maintain whole milk consumption despite the broader transition. The trajectories remain tightly clustered across simulation runs throughout the entire period, indicating robust and consistent model behavior with minimal sensitivity to stochastic variations in social network interactions.

For decision makers, these dynamics suggest that public health interventions targeting dietary transitions may achieve rapid initial gains as receptive populations respond to informational campaigns, but should anticipate a protracted middle phase where habit persistence creates resistance among certain demographic segments. The late-stage acceleration indicates that maintaining consistent messaging over extended periods can eventually trigger tipping points that rapidly convert remaining holdouts, suggesting that long-term commitment to intervention strategies may yield disproportionate returns in later phases compared to short-term intensive campaigns.
