The aggregate consumption trajectory of alternative milk varieties (skimmed and semi-skimmed) exhibits a classic innovation diffusion pattern characterized by initial behavioral inertia, subsequent take-off, and sustained high-velocity growth through the historical transition period. The series originates at complete absence of alternative selection (0.0000), reflecting the 1974 market conditions where entrenched habitual attachment to whole milk—initialized at maximum strength (10.0)—effectively suppressed deliberation over alternatives despite their presence in the choice set.

During the initial five time steps (0–4), adoption progresses at a modest rate of approximately 201 cumulative selections per period. This latency reflects the dominance of automatic habitual maintenance over conscious evaluation, as the incumbent habit strength substantially exceeds the deliberation threshold (2.0). Despite agents' capacity to process health and environmental information, initially low perceptions of alternative milk attributes (health: 1.69; environmental: 1.12) provide insufficient motivation to overcome this inertia, while the memory lifetime parameter (5.0) ensures that emerging positive experiences remain localized rather than immediately triggering widespread reconsideration.

A critical inflection emerges between time steps 5 and 9, where the adoption slope accelerates sharply to 737 selections per period. This transition marks the activation threshold where accumulated perceptual updates regarding alternative milk benefits, combined with social influence mechanisms, begin overcoming habitual lock-in. The watts-strogatz network topology (parameter 8.0), operating with a 30% interaction probability and moderate social conformity weight (0.28), facilitates the transmission of early adopter behaviors through network clusters. Even with substantial social blindness (0.51), the combination of social susceptibility (0.2) and justification mechanisms (0.61) enables agents to rationalize deviations from established whole milk consumption patterns.

From time step 10 through 24, the system enters a phase of stable, linear high-growth characterized by consistent slopes ranging between 927 and 935 selections per period. This sustained velocity indicates robust social contagion and systematic habit displacement across the population. The uniformity of this growth phase (Mann-Kendall tau = 1.000, p = 0.027 across all rolling windows) suggests that once the initial habit barrier is breached, the probability-based or threshold-based psychological mechanisms for reconsideration generate continuous adoption cascades. The cognitive dissonance threshold (0.15) likely contributes to this momentum by compelling agents to resolve inconsistencies between health beliefs and consumption behaviors through switching rather than attitude adjustment.

The terminal phase (time steps 25–32) maintains this high growth trajectory while approaching a cumulative total of 25,644 alternative selections by the series conclusion. The presence of oscillatory patterns (dominant period: 15) during time steps 27–31, combined with the inflection point at index 31 and change point detection at 32, indicates that the simulation captures the volatile crossover period where alternative milk consumption achieves market dominance rather than terminal saturation. This oscillation likely reflects residual heterogeneity in habit persistence among late-stage consumers and the market turbulence inherent to surpassing the incumbent whole milk majority. The final value of 25,643.8333 represents approximately 77.7% of total possible selection opportunities across the 1000-agent population, consistent with the historical surpassing of whole milk consumption that characterized the British dairy transition by 2005.
