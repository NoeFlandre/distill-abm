The habit strength factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk commences at a neutral baseline of 1.0000, reflecting the absence of pre-existing habitual tendencies for these alternatives at the simulation outset. During the initial five time steps, the factor remains effectively dormant, registering minimal fluctuation around 1.0167 and exhibiting no statistically significant trend. This stagnation corresponds to the psychological dominance of whole milk consumption, which begins with maximum habit strength of 10.0 and creates substantial inertia against behavioral switching.

A marked transition occurs at approximately the fifth time step, initiating a sustained phase of habit formation that persists through the remainder of the 33-step simulation. Between steps 5 and 15, the habit factor undergoes rapid acceleration, increasing from roughly 1.02 to surpass the series mean of 1.26, with statistically significant upward trends detected across consecutive windows (p=0.027). The steepest rate of increase occurs between steps 10 and 14, where the slope reaches 0.030, indicating an inflection point around step 12 where the curvature shifts from convex to concave.

Following a structural change point at step 15, the growth continues but at a gradually decelerating rate, with slopes declining from 0.027 in steps 15-19 to 0.018 in steps 25-29. By the simulation's conclusion at step 32, the habit factor reaches 1.5879, representing a 58.8% increase from baseline and a median value of 1.2578 across the series. The trajectory exhibits low variance throughout (standard deviation of 0.2035), suggesting robust and consistent habit consolidation dynamics despite the stochastic elements of consumer decision-making.

This pattern indicates that habit formation for alternative milk varieties follows a classic diffusion curve with three distinct phases: an initial lag period where incumbent habits suppress new pattern formation, a middle phase of explosive growth as consumers repeatedly choose alternatives and develop automatic behavioral scripts, and a late phase of maturing consolidation where growth continues but approaches saturation. The rapid acceleration after step 5 suggests that once consumers overcome initial habit thresholds, the self-reinforcing nature of repeated choice quickly transforms deliberate decisions into automatic behaviors.

**Implications for decision makers:** The pronounced delay in habit formation (approximately five time steps of stagnation) followed by rapid consolidation implies that marketing interventions and policy incentives for alternative milk adoption should be front-loaded and patient, maintaining support through the initial lag phase when resistance is highest. The inflection around step 12 and change point at step 15 mark critical thresholds where behavioral change becomes self-sustaining; resources can be strategically redirected once the habit factor exceeds 1.2, as social contagion and individual habituation take over as primary drivers. The high consistency across simulation runs (variance of 0.0414) indicates these dynamics are structurally robust, allowing confident prediction that initial adoption investments will yield predictable habit formation outcomes once critical mass is achieved.
