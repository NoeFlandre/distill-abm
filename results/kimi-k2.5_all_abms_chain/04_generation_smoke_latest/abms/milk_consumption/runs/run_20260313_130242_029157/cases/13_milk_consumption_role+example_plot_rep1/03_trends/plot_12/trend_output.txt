The mean value environmental deviation begins at approximately zero, indicating that initially, consumers' milk choices are closely aligned with their held environmental values. Over the first seven steps, this deviation increases rapidly and sharply, rising to approximately 13. This steep ascent suggests that as agents develop stronger environmental perceptions regarding alternative milk options, their actual consumption choices remain constrained by high initial habit strength associated with whole milk, creating a growing disconnect between environmental values and behavior.

Between steps 7 and 12, the rate of increase slows considerably as the deviation approaches a value of approximately 13.4. This deceleration indicates that while the gap between values and choices continues to widen, the system is approaching a saturation point where the forces driving increased deviation—such as rising environmental awareness—are beginning to be counterbalanced by agents who are starting to overcome habitual barriers and adjust their consumption patterns.

From approximately step 12 through step 32, the mean value environmental deviation stabilizes and remains nearly constant at around 13.4, with minimal fluctuation across simulation runs. This persistent plateau indicates that the system has reached a steady-state equilibrium where the population-level misalignment between environmental values and actual consumption behavior remains constant. This stability suggests that while individual consumers may continue to transition from whole milk to alternatives over time, the aggregate deviation remains fixed due to a dynamic balance between newly aware consumers entering states of high value-action dissonance and other consumers successfully resolving this dissonance through behavioral change.
