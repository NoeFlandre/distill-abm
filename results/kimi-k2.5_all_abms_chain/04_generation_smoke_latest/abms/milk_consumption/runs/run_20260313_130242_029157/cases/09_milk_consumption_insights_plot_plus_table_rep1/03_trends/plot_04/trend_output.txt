The simulation of milk consumption adoption from 1974 to 2005 demonstrates a complete transition trajectory in which the cumulative count of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk selections accumulates from zero to approximately 25,644 choices across the 1,000-agent population over 33 time steps. This metric represents the aggregate number of times agents selected alternative milk varieties, starting from the 1974 baseline where whole milk dominated the market.

The adoption process exhibits three distinct phases characterized by progressively stabilizing growth rates. During the initial period from time step 0 through approximately time step 4, the accumulation proceeds at a measured pace of roughly 201 choices per time step, reflecting the difficulty of overcoming entrenched habits and incumbent preferences. During this foundation phase, the cumulative total remains relatively modest as early adopters begin experimenting with alternative varieties.

Between time steps 5 and 9, the adoption rate accelerates substantially to approximately 737 choices per time step, representing a nearly fourfold increase in the velocity of behavioral change. By time step 10, the cumulative total reaches approximately 5,000 choices, indicating that social influence mechanisms and perception shifts begin driving broader acceptance.

From time step 10 onward, the adoption rate stabilizes into sustained linear growth averaging approximately 933 to 936 choices per time step. This consistent rate persists through time step 29, carrying the cumulative total from roughly 9,300 at time step 10 to the final value of 25,644 at time step 32. The median cumulative value of approximately 10,679 occurs around the middle time steps, marking the transition point where half of the total adoption events have occurred. The trajectory maintains strictly monotonic growth throughout with no detected declines, indicating continuous progressive adoption without regression to whole milk consumption.

For decision makers, these trends suggest that successful dietary transitions require sustained investment during initial slow-adoption phases, as overcoming habitual behaviors and incumbent preferences takes time. The acceleration around time step 5 and stabilization by time step 10 indicate that once social norms shift and awareness reaches critical mass, adoption becomes self-sustaining at predictable rates. The linear growth phase in the latter half of the simulation demonstrates that well-established behavioral change programs can rely on social diffusion mechanisms to maintain consistent progress, reducing the need for intensive intervention once momentum is established. The final cumulative total approaching 26,000 choices out of a theoretical maximum of 33,000 possible selections suggests that the modeled interventions achieve substantial but not universal adoption, highlighting the persistence of diversity in consumer preferences even during major market transitions.
