The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent initiates at a minimal level of 5.29 units, reflecting the 1974 baseline where whole milk maintained dominant habitual preference. Over the first nine time steps, the metric undergoes a steep and statistically significant increase, with inflection points at time steps 4 and 9 marking transitions between accelerating growth phases. By time step 9, consumption values approach 1200 units, representing a rapid departure from initial conditions.

Between time steps 10 and 24, the series enters a prolonged plateau characterized by substantial oscillation and absence of significant directional trend. During this middle period, consumption fluctuates between a local minimum of 1183.39 units at time step 18 and a local maximum of 1265.47 units at time step 11, with additional peaks at time steps 13 (1240.35 units) and 21 (1226.86 units) interspersed with troughs at time steps 12 (1233.62 units), 20 (1214.46 units), and 23 (1209.70 units). Rolling trend analysis confirms no statistically significant directional movement across consecutive five-step windows during this interval, indicating a temporary stabilization where adoption dynamics reached equilibrium.

Following time step 24, the metric resumes a significant upward trajectory, with statistically significant increasing trends detected between time steps 25 and 29. This final growth phase progresses through inflection points at time steps 27 and 30, culminating at time step 32 with a terminal value of 1456.60 units—the maximum observed consumption level—which substantially exceeds the plateau values maintained during the middle period.

This trajectory reveals three distinct behavioral phases consistent with the model's psychological mechanisms. The initial explosive growth reflects the erosion of incumbent habitual preferences as early-adopting agents accumulated sufficient health and environmental perceptions to exceed the habit threshold required for automatic maintenance of whole milk choices. The middle oscillatory plateau suggests that remaining agents possessed stronger habitual defenses or social blindness, creating dynamic equilibrium where social influence and habit strength produced recurrent cycles of adoption and reversion without net directional change. The final ascent indicates that sustained social network interactions and normative conformity pressures eventually overcame residual habitual resistance, consolidating the market shift and establishing alternative milk varieties as the dominant consumption choice by the simulation's conclusion.
