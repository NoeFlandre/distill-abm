The trend maintains a steady positive slope, with rolling window analyses confirming significant increasing patterns (Kendall's tau = 1.000) across all observed intervals.

Temporal analysis reveals subtle non-linearities around time step 7 and time step 12, where inflection points indicate minor shifts in the rate of habit factor accumulation. Additionally, the series exhibits oscillatory behavior with a dominant periodicity of approximately 15 years, manifesting in cyclical fluctuations concentrated around time steps 3 through 7, 15 through 18, and 26 through 30. A change point is identified at time step 32, corresponding to the maximum observed value of 1.0502.

Throughout the entire simulation, the metric never falls below its initial value, indicating that the habitual influence on whole milk consumption decisions either maintained its baseline strength or intensified progressively over time. By the final time step, the habit factor represents a 5% increase over the starting value.

Within the context of the British milk transition (1974–2005), this pattern suggests that as the market shifted toward skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives, the agent population experienced selective attrition based on habit strength. Agents with weaker habitual attachments to whole milk likely transitioned to alternative varieties when exposed to health perceptions, social influence, and environmental information, while those remaining with whole milk possessed increasingly entrenched habitual patterns. Consequently, the rising mean habit factor reflects a "hardening" of habitual commitment among the residual whole milk consumer base, where the psychological weight of existing consumption patterns intensified as alternatives became more normative in the social environment. The low variance across simulations indicates that this entrenchment process operated consistently regardless of stochastic variations in agent interactions and decision-making events.
