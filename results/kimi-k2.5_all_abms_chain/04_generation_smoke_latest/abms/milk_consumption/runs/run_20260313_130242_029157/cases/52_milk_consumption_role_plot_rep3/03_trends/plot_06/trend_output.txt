The mean quantity of whole milk consumed demonstrates a consistent downward trajectory across the 31-year simulation period, beginning with initial consumption levels of approximately 2,650 units at Step 0 and declining to terminal values between 200-300 units by Step 32.

The early phase (Steps 0-10) exhibits the most dramatic reduction, with mean consumption plummeting by roughly 62-64% from the initial value to approximately 950-1,000 units. This period shows a steep, nearly linear decline with the fastest rate of change occurring between Steps 0-5, where consumption drops by over 600 units. The rapid early decline reflects the initial disruption of the strong incumbent habits (parameterized at maximum strength of 10.0) as agents begin processing information about health and environmental attributes of milk alternatives.

The middle phase (Steps 10-25) demonstrates a marked moderation in the rate of decline, with consumption decreasing gradually from approximately 950-1,000 units down to 600-700 units. This 15-step period shows a reduction of only about 30-35% relative to the starting point of this phase, representing a substantially slower rate of change. Notably, between Steps 18-20, the trajectory temporarily plateaus with values stabilizing around 750-800 units, suggesting a transient equilibrium where habitual maintenance mechanisms and social influence effects temporarily balance the growing perceptions favoring alternative milk types.

The late phase (Steps 25-32) exhibits renewed acceleration in the decline, with consumption dropping sharply from approximately 600-650 units to roughly 350-400 units by Step 28, and continuing to fall to approximately 200-300 units by the final step. This represents a decline of approximately 50-60% from the Step 25 baseline over just seven time steps. The steep terminal decline likely corresponds to the cumulative social influence effects reaching critical mass, where global social norms (activated in the model) increasingly favor alternative milk varieties, combined with memory decay processes (five-step lifetime) progressively eroding residual habitual attachments to whole milk.

Across all simulation replications, the aggregate trajectories remain highly consistent with minimal variation, indicating robust macro-level behavior despite stochastic individual-level decision mechanisms. The overall pattern captures the historical transition from whole milk dominance toward the alternative milk varieties, with the non-linear decline rate reflecting the complex interplay between habit strength decay, perception formation regarding health and environmental attributes, and social network influence effects operating through the Watts-Strogatz network structure.
