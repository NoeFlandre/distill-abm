The simulation data reveals a pronounced non-linear decline in mean whole milk consumption over the 31-year period, characterized by four distinct phases with varying rates of change and increasing heterogeneity across simulation runs.

**Phase 1: Rapid Initial Decline (Steps 0–10)**
At initialization (Step 0), the mean whole milk quantity stands at approximately 2,650–2,700 units, reflecting the strong incumbent habit strength (parameter value: 10.0) representative of 1974 market dominance. Over the first 10 steps, consumption plummets by roughly 1,650 units to approximately 1,000 units, representing an average decline rate of 165 units per step. This steep trajectory indicates that despite initially strong habitual attachment to whole milk, the combination of evolving health perceptions (initial alternative health perception: 1.69), environmental attribute recognition, and social influence mechanisms (interaction probability: 0.3, social susceptibility: 0.2) rapidly overcomes habitual inertia for a substantial segment of the consumer population.

**Phase 2: Decelerated Decline (Steps 10–20)**
Between steps 10 and 20, the rate of decline moderates substantially to approximately 25 units per step, with consumption falling from roughly 1,000 units to 750 units. This deceleration suggests the emergence of a resistant core of consumers whose habit strength exceeds the threshold for change (habit-threshold: 2.0) or whose social network configurations reinforce continued whole milk consumption. The temporary equilibrium indicates that for this segment, the cumulative effects of health perception updates and social influence are insufficient to trigger behavioral switching, creating a plateau in the transition dynamics.

**Phase 3: Plateau Behavior (Steps 20–25)**
From step 20 to step 25, the decline further slows to approximately 20 units per step, with consumption decreasing from 750 to 650 units. This near-stagnation represents the most resistant consumer segment, likely characterized by high social blindness (parameter: 0.51) to alternative consumption patterns or strong network clustering that insulates agents from normative pressures. The minimal change during this interval suggests that without external perturbations or significant shifts in social norms, this population subset maintains habitual consumption patterns despite widespread market transition.

**Phase 4: Secondary Acceleration with Increased Variability (Steps 25–32)**
After step 25, the decline accelerates dramatically to approximately 55 units per step, with consumption falling from 650 units to roughly 250 units by step 32. This secondary acceleration coincides with substantially increased heterogeneity across simulation runs, as evidenced by diverging trajectories in the later steps. The pattern indicates that as alternative milk consumption becomes normative (social-conformity weight: 0.28, norms mechanism: active), social pressure on the remaining whole milk consumers intensifies, potentially triggering cascade effects through the network structure (Watts-Strogatz topology, parameter: 8.0). The increased variability suggests that stochastic factors, specific network configurations, and the probabilistic nature of social interactions (p-interact: 0.3) create divergent pathways for the final transition of resistant consumers, with some runs achieving near-complete transition while others retain small residual populations of whole milk consumers.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The four-phase pattern reveals that market transitions in habitual consumer goods follow non-linear trajectories with distinct segments: easily convertible early adopters, a resistant middle majority requiring targeted intervention, and a highly variable late-adopter segment sensitive to normative pressures. The pronounced plateau between steps 20–25 suggests that mid-transition strategies—such as intensified social marketing or network-targeted interventions—are critical to prevent stagnation. The increasing heterogeneity in final stages indicates that universal messaging becomes less effective as the market matures, necessitating segmented approaches for niche residual populations. The substantial early decline (steps 0–10) demonstrates that habit disruption is most effective when combined with simultaneous social and informational campaigns, while the later acceleration suggests that leveraging emerging social norms can overcome even entrenched resistant segments.
