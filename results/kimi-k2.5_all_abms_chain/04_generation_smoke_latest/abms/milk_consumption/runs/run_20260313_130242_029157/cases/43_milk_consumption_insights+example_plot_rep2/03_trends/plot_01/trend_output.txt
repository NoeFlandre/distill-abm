Between steps 10 and 20, the rate of decline moderates considerably, with consumption decreasing gradually to approximately 750 units. From step 20 to step 25, the downward trajectory continues but at a diminished pace, settling near 600 units with observable minor fluctuations. During this period, the remaining consumer base represents agents with high habit thresholds or limited social connectivity, maintaining their preferences despite the widespread shift to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties that has permeated the broader network.

A marked discontinuity emerges around steps 26 and 27, where consumption plummets sharply from approximately 600 units to below 400. This sudden drop suggests a critical transition point in the simulation wherein cumulative social influence and conformist pressures reach a tipping point sufficient to overcome the cognitive thresholds of previously resistant agents, resulting in synchronized reconsideration events.

Throughout the final sequence from step 28 to step 32, consumption stabilizes at low levels between 200 and 300 units, indicating a persistent but small segment of the population that maintains whole milk consumption regardless of prevailing social norms. The consistency across simulation runs remains high throughout, though slightly elevated variance during the middle phase (steps 5 through 15) reflects stochastic differences in the timing of individual agent interactions and disposition triggers.

Implications for decision makers: The pronounced initial decline suggests that leveraging early adopters and social network influencers can effectively catalyze rapid dietary shifts during transition periods. The extended plateau preceding the late-stage sharp drop indicates that traditional persuasion tactics may face resistance from habit-entrenched consumers until social norms achieve critical mass, implying that resource allocation should prioritize network-based interventions over individual-focused campaigns during later adoption phases. The ultimate stabilization at low but non-zero levels suggests that complete market elimination of whole milk may require specialized targeting of residual consumer segments rather than broad-spectrum approaches.
