This aggregate pattern, derived from 30 replicated series with a standard deviation of 654.7, indicates substantial between-run heterogeneity in consumption pathways, with terminal values spanning roughly 2,000 to 2,400 across the simulation ensemble.

The rate of accumulation displays distinct non-linear dynamics characterized by an initial acceleratory phase followed by progressive deceleration. Rolling trend analysis reveals that the slope increases from approximately 70.8 choices per step during the first interval (steps 0–4) to 76.2 choices per step in the subsequent window (steps 5–9), before entering a consistent deceleration phase. From steps 10–14 through steps 25–29, the accumulation rate declines sequentially from 71.4 to 71.4, 66.7, 64.8, and finally 64.1 choices per step, with all intervals maintaining statistically significant monotonic increases (Mann-Kendall tau = 1.000, p = 0.027). Inflection points at indices 1, 7, and 31 mark key transitions in these growth dynamics, while the dominant oscillation period of 15.0 steps, manifesting at indices 27–30, suggests cyclical variation in choice behavior during the latter stages of the time series.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The decelerating accumulation rate, despite strong initial habit parameters, suggests that habitual dominance in food choices erodes gradually rather than collapsing abruptly when exposed to competing health perceptions and social network influences. The persistent between-run variation (coefficient of variation approximately 58%) indicates that micro-level stochastic factors—such as probabilistic interaction events and individual thresholds for habit disruption—generate substantial macro-level uncertainty in consumption forecasts, implying that market projections should account for significant variance intervals even when aggregate behavioral parameters are well-estimated. The temporary acceleration in whole milk selection during steps 5–9 further suggests that incumbent products may experience short-term reinforcement before tipping points toward alternatives take effect, warning against premature intervention withdrawal during transitional periods.
