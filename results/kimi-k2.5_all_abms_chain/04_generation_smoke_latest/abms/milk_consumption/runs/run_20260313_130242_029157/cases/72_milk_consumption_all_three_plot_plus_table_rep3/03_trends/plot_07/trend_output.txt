The mean consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk initiates at approximately 5 units at time step 0, reflecting the market dominance of whole milk at the simulation's outset. During the first four time steps, consumption accelerates rapidly with a significant monotonic increase, marking the initial phase of habit disruption where early adopters begin processing health and environmental information about alternative milk varieties. By step 10, consumption reaches approximately 1,200 units, and peaks at 1,265 units by step 11, representing the conclusion of the primary adoption phase.

Between steps 11 and 25, the system exhibits a prolonged plateau characterized by oscillatory behavior around a mean of approximately 1,220 units, with local peaks at steps 13, 19, and 21, and corresponding valleys at steps 12, 18, and 20. This stabilization period reflects a market equilibrium where the remaining strength of habitual whole milk consumption among resistant agents balances against ongoing social influence and perception updating mechanisms. The oscillation pattern, with a dominant period of approximately 15 time steps, indicates continuous micro-adjustments as agents encounter social information and reassess their choices without achieving net market growth. Statistical analysis confirms non-significant trends during this plateau period.

A second growth phase emerges around step 25, with consumption increasing from approximately 1,220 units to 1,456 units by step 32. This late-stage acceleration, marked by a resumption of significant monotonic increase in the final window, suggests that sustained social norm activation and cumulative perception updating eventually overcome residual habit barriers, potentially through network effects reaching critical thresholds among the remaining resistant population.

Inflection points at steps 4, 12, 13, 16, 19, 22, 27, and 30 mark transitions between acceleration and deceleration regimes, reflecting the complex interplay between habit formation, social influence, and perception dynamics. The trajectory demonstrates high consistency across simulation runs during the initial growth phase, with increasing variance during the plateau period, indicating that while early adoption dynamics are robustly determined by initial conditions, the timing and duration of market stabilization exhibit greater sensitivity to stochastic elements in social interactions.

For decision makers, these dynamics suggest that market transitions in food categories dominated by habitual consumption follow a punctuated pattern rather than smooth logistic growth. Initial rapid adoption driven by health and environmental messaging should be expected to encounter significant multi-period plateaus where habit strength creates market resistance. Effective strategies require sustained investment in social norm activation and long-term perception management to overcome these barriers. The observed late-stage growth indicates that patience and consistent messaging can yield substantial returns even after extended periods of apparent market stagnation, suggesting that premature withdrawal of marketing efforts during plateau phases would forfeit significant eventual market share.
