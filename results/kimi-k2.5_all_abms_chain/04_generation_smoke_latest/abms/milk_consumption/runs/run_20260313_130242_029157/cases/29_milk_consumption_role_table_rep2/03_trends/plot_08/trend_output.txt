The metric tracking habitual influence on whole milk selection exhibits a modest but statistically significant upward trajectory throughout the simulation period. Beginning at a baseline value of 1.0000, the factor applied to whole milk choice due to habit demonstrates a monotonic increase, culminating at approximately 1.0502 by the final time step (index 32). This represents a 5% strengthening of the habitual component in the choice function over the 31-year simulation timeframe, with a series mean of 1.0359 and remarkably low volatility (standard deviation of 0.0110).

The temporal dynamics reveal distinct phases of evolution. An initial inflection occurs at time step 7, followed by a second inflection point at time step 12, suggesting three distinct regimes of habit formation intensity. Prior to the first inflection, the metric exhibits its sharpest relative increase; between indices 7 and 12, the rate moderates; and following index 12, the progression continues steadily toward the terminal value. Rolling trend analysis confirms consistent monotonicity, with Mann-Kendall tests across six distinct temporal windows (each spanning 5 time steps) uniformly indicating significant increasing trends (τ = 1.000, p = 0.027).

Superimposed upon this steady ascent is a cyclical pattern characterized by a dominant oscillation period of approximately 15 time steps. This periodicity introduces regular fluctuations around the central trend, with notable oscillatory activity clustering around time steps 3–7, 15–18, and 26–30, suggesting periodic reinforcement or relaxation cycles in habitual strength that may correspond to generational turnover or shifting network dynamics within the Watts-Strogatz social topology.

Within the context of the British milk transition, this modest increase in the habit factor—despite the historical trajectory away from whole milk consumption—suggests that habitual anchoring to the incumbent product intensifies as the market diversifies. Given the model's parameters, wherein agents begin with substantial initial habit strength (10.0) and face a moderate threshold for automatic habit maintenance (2.0), the slight elevation from 1.0 to 1.05 likely reflects the behavioral residue of persistent whole milk consumers who, facing increasing social pressure to switch (social-susceptibility = 0.2, social-conformity = 0.28) and growing health consciousness regarding alternatives, instead experience consolidation of their existing consumption patterns. The inflection points at approximately 21% and 36% through the simulation period may correspond to critical junctures where early adopters have exited the whole milk category, leaving a residual population whose habitual attachment requires greater psychological justification (justification parameter = 0.61) to maintain in the face of mounting cognitive dissonance (threshold = 0.15).

The absence of detected peaks or valleys combined with the identification of a change point at the final time step (index 32) indicates that the habit factor had not stabilized by the simulation's conclusion, potentially suggesting that while whole milk consumption declined historically, the psychological mechanisms of habit persistence among remaining consumers continued intensifying up to 2005.
