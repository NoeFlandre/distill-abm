The sustained increase from 1.0 to nearly 1.6 demonstrates that habit acts as an amplifying feedback loop in the adoption of alternative milk varieties, progressively weighting choice functions toward skimmed and semi-skimmed options as the process advances from initial 1974 market conditions toward 2005.

For decision makers, this trajectory indicates that initial interventions to promote alternative milk adoption face high inertia due to entrenched whole milk habits, but once adoption crosses a critical threshold around the fifth time step, habitual momentum becomes a self-sustaining force that drives continued transition without additional external pressure. Public health campaigns should therefore anticipate a delayed but persistent return on investment, with the strongest shifts in consumption patterns occurring in the middle phase of the intervention period as new habits crystallize and reinforce through social networks.
