The simulation reveals a monotonic increase in aggregate alternative milk consumption across the entire 1974-2005 period, commencing from zero instances at the initial time step and accumulating to approximately 25,644 total annual choices by the final year. This trajectory reflects a classic innovation diffusion process within the agent population, driven by the interaction of social influence, habit formation, and perception mechanisms embedded in the Watts-Strogatz network topology.

During the initial phase spanning time steps zero through four (1974-1978), adoption proceeds gradually with a growth rate of roughly 201 additional choices per time step, representing an incubation period where disposition mechanisms begin activating but habitual whole milk consumption remains dominant among agents with high incumbent initial habit strength.

A pronounced acceleration emerges between time steps five and nine (1979-1983), where the adoption rate surges to approximately 737 choices per step and continues climbing to roughly 927 choices per step during time steps 10-14 (1984-1988). This inflection indicates that threshold-based or probability-based disposition triggers have activated sufficiently within the network to generate rapid reconsideration of milk choices, as social susceptibility and conformity effects compound through local interactions.

From time step 15 onward (1989-2005), the growth stabilizes into a consistent linear pattern with rates fixing at approximately 933-936 choices per year through time step 29. This sustained linear momentum demonstrates that once social norms become entrenched and habit formation processes mature, alternative milk adoption achieves steady, self-reinforcing expansion. Minor oscillatory patterns with a dominant period of approximately 15 years emerge at oscillation indices 27 through 31 (roughly 2001-2005), coinciding with an inflection point at time step 31, suggesting emergent saturation dynamics as the market approaches maturity, though the overall trajectory maintains its increasing character without reversal.

**Implications for decision makers:** The distinct phase transitions—from slow initial growth through rapid acceleration to stabilized linear increase—suggest that public health initiatives targeting dietary transitions should anticipate significant incubation periods before network effects generate self-sustaining adoption momentum. However, once the linear growth phase establishes, behavioral changes become deeply entrenched through habit formation and social conformity mechanisms, indicating that early investment in shifting social norms yields persistent, low-volatility returns over extended time horizons without requiring continuous intervention. The absence of decline phases throughout the simulation suggests that reversal to previous consumption patterns becomes increasingly unlikely as the population's cognitive dissonance thresholds adapt to new consumption behaviors.
