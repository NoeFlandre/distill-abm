The mean weekly whole milk consumption per agent initiates at approximately 2,650 units, representing the initial dominance of whole milk preferences among the agent population in 1974. During the first ten steps, consumption plummets rapidly to roughly 1,000 units, suggesting an immediate and substantial shift as agents respond to initial perceptions of health and environmental characteristics associated with skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives, overcoming incumbent habits through disposition triggers and social influence mechanisms.

From step 10 through step 20, the rate of decline moderates significantly, with consumption decreasing gradually to approximately 750 units. This plateau effect indicates that while the majority of agents transition quickly, a residual population maintains whole milk consumption due to stronger habit persistence, higher cognitive dissonance thresholds, or lower social susceptibility, requiring extended exposure to social norms and information dissemination before reconsidering their choices.

Between steps 20 and 32, consumption exhibits a stepped decline pattern, ultimately reaching 200 to 250 units. These discrete downward shifts likely correspond to the activation of threshold-based or probability-based disposition mechanisms that trigger intermittent waves of reconsideration among resistant agents, followed by the adoption of alternative milk types and subsequent habit formation around new preferences.

Across the repeated simulation runs, consumption trajectories demonstrate high consistency during the initial rapid decline, reflecting deterministic effects of initial conditions and parameter settings. Variability emerges primarily during the middle phase, approximately between steps 5 and 15, where stochastic elements of social network interactions and individual decision thresholds generate divergence in adoption timing. By the final steps, runs reconverge toward similar low consumption levels, indicating that regardless of the specific path taken, the combined effects of social conformity, habit adjustment, and sustained informational pressure drive the population toward near-complete abandonment of whole milk consumption over the simulated 31-year period.
