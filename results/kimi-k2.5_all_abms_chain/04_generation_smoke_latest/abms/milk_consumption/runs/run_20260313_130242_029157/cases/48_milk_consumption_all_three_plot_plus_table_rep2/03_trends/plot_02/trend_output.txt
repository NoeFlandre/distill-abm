The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at 5.29 units and undergoes a distinct three-phase evolution over 32 time steps, terminating at 1456.60 units. 

**Phase 1: Rapid Adoption (Steps 0–9)**
The series exhibits strong monotonic growth during the initial decade of steps. From steps 0 to 4, the metric increases with significant acceleration (Mann-Kendall tau = 0.900, p = 0.043), marking an inflection point at step 4 where the rate of change begins to moderate. The growth continues unabated through step 9 (Mann-Kendall tau = 1.000, p = 0.027), with the series approaching approximately 1200 units by the end of this phase. This period represents the critical diffusion window where early and middle adopters transition from whole milk to alternative varieties.

**Phase 2: Market Saturation and Oscillation (Steps 10–24)**
Following the initial surge, the series enters a prolonged plateau characterized by high variance and cyclical fluctuations around a mean of approximately 1220 units. Statistical testing confirms the absence of significant trend during sub-periods 10–14, 15–19, and 20–24 (all p-values > 0.05). The dynamics exhibit an oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of 15 steps, manifesting as successive peaks at step 11 (1265.47), step 13 (1240.35), step 19 (1221.57), and step 21 (1226.86), interspersed with valleys at step 12 (1233.62), step 18 (1183.39), step 20 (1214.46), and step 23 (1209.70). Eight distinct inflection points occur during this phase, indicating complex micro-dynamics where social influence, habit persistence, and perceptual updating create temporary resistance to further adoption gains.

**Phase 3: Late-Stage Growth (Steps 25–32)**
The final segment demonstrates renewed significant growth (Mann-Kendall tau = 1.000, p = 0.027 for steps 25–29), with the metric ascending from approximately 1220 units at step 25 to the terminal value of 1456.60 units at step 32. This late acceleration suggests the eventual overcoming of habitual barriers among resistant consumer segments or the cumulative effect of sustained social norm shifts that finally penetrate late-adopter populations.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The three-phase pattern indicates that market transitions in habitual consumer goods follow a non-linear trajectory requiring different intervention strategies across phases. The initial rapid growth phase (steps 0–9) suggests that achieving critical mass among early adopters can generate self-sustaining momentum, making initial investment in awareness and accessibility crucial. However, the extended plateau phase (steps 10–24) reveals that habitual behavior creates substantial market friction, where consumption levels may stagnate for prolonged periods despite earlier successes. Decision makers should anticipate this "middle adoption trap" and prepare targeted interventions to disrupt habitual inertia during this window. Finally, the late-stage growth (steps 25–32) demonstrates that persistent efforts can eventually capture resistant segments, suggesting that abandoning transition efforts during the plateau phase would forego significant long-term market potential. The oscillatory dynamics during the plateau also imply that temporary setbacks are normal and should not trigger premature strategy abandonment.
