The mean habit factor applied to whole milk choice begins at a neutral baseline of 1.0 and exhibits a steady, monotonic increase throughout the 33 time steps, reaching approximately 1.05 by the conclusion of the simulation period. This progression indicates a gradual intensification of habitual commitment to whole milk consumption, with the reinforcing effect of habit growing by roughly 5% over the course of the market transition. The trajectory displays minimal variance (standard deviation of 0.011), suggesting highly consistent habit formation dynamics across the population with little deviation in the rate of habit strengthening.

This pattern reveals that despite the historical transition toward lower-fat milk alternatives, the psychological mechanism of habit actually reinforces incumbent whole milk preferences over time for those who maintain this choice. The increasing habit factor suggests that continued consumption of whole milk strengthens behavioral inertia, creating escalating resistance to change as the simulation progresses. Inflection points observed around time steps 7 and 12 indicate subtle shifts in the rate of habit accumulation, potentially corresponding to early phases of market transition where social and informational pressures begin interacting with habitual tendencies, temporarily slowing or accelerating the reinforcement rate.

For decision makers seeking to facilitate dietary transitions, these findings suggest that interventions targeting habitual behavior must account for the self-reinforcing nature of consumption patterns. As consumers continue with incumbent choices, their commitment deepens, making later-stage behavior change increasingly difficult and costly to achieve. Early intervention strategies, implemented before habit strength accumulates significantly, may prove substantially more effective than attempts to disrupt well-established consumption patterns. Additionally, the consistent upward trend implies that reliance on habit disruption alone, without addressing the accumulating strength of existing behaviors, may underestimate the psychological barriers to market transition.
