The aggregate health-value deviation commences at zero, indicating perfect alignment between consumer health perceptions and actual purchasing behavior during the initial period corresponding to 1974. Over the first four time steps, representing approximately the late 1970s, the deviation increases sharply to roughly 0.25. This rapid accumulation of cognitive dissonance likely reflects the initial phase where emerging health information favoring low-fat milk alternatives began challenging entrenched habitual preferences for whole milk, creating a widening gap between what consumers value and what they actually consume.

Between time steps five and twenty-four, spanning roughly 1979 to 1998, the deviation continues to climb but at a markedly decelerated rate, with particularly slow growth during the middle period around steps fifteen through twenty where the slope approaches near-zero. This prolonged plateau likely corresponds to the historical crossover period when skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption approached parity with whole milk. During this transition phase, the stabilizing deviation suggests that while some consumers successfully aligned their behavior with health values by switching to alternatives, others maintained whole milk consumption despite growing psychological tension, creating a temporary equilibrium in the aggregate misalignment metric.

In the final phase from approximately step twenty-five onward, corresponding to the late 1990s through 2005, the deviation resumes a steeper trajectory, ultimately reaching 0.7251 by the final time step. This late-stage acceleration indicates that the remaining whole milk consumers experienced intensifying cognitive dissonance as alternative milk became socially normative and health perceptions increasingly favored low-fat options, yet habit persistence and network effects continued to delay behavioral adaptation for this holdout segment.

Periodic oscillations detectable in the latter half of the series, with fluctuations approximately every fifteen time steps visible around indices eighteen through twenty and again near the conclusion, suggest cyclical reinforcement of social norms or generational turnover effects that modulate the rate of psychological tension accumulation. The complete absence of decline in the deviation metric throughout the thirty-two step period indicates that once established, value-behavior misalignment in food choices tends to compound rather than resolve spontaneously, even as market shares undergo substantial transformation.

**Implications for decision makers:** The persistent and ultimately increasing gap between health values and consumption behavior demonstrates that public health campaigns creating awareness without addressing habit disruption mechanisms may inadvertently generate sustained psychological discomfort without producing proportional behavioral change. The mid-series plateau suggests that interventions targeting the transition period when markets approach parity may be most effective at preventing the late-stage divergence observed in the final years, while the identification of distinct growth phases indicates that policy strategies should adapt as market maturation progresses from initial education through normative restructuring.
