The deviation between consumers' environmental values and their milk choices initiates at zero, indicating complete alignment between environmental valuations and consumption behaviors at the simulation's outset, corresponding to the 1974 baseline period. Over the first approximately thirteen time steps, this metric exhibits a sharp, sustained increase, ascending rapidly to reach a maximum value of 13.48 around time step 13. This steep upward trajectory reflects a significant and rapid divergence emerging as agents begin departing from habitual whole milk consumption—driven by evolving health perceptions and social influence mechanisms—and adopting alternative varieties that conflict with their underlying environmental values. Statistical evidence confirms significant positive trends during the initial ten time steps, with the rate of increase moderating but continuing through this establishment phase as the population breaks from initial habit strength of 10.0.

Subsequent to time step 16, the series transitions into a stable plateau characterized by high persistent deviation, fluctuating narrowly between approximately 13.42 and 13.48 through the conclusion of the simulation at time step 32. While the metric exhibits minor oscillations throughout this latter period—including local peaks near time steps 19, 21, and 27, and subtle troughs at intervening points—the overall trajectory demonstrates no significant directional trend after the initial rise, indicating that the system has settled into a steady state of enduring value-behavior misalignment. The presence of a dominant oscillation period of approximately fifteen time steps, coupled with inflection points at time steps 19, 24, 26, and 28, suggests ongoing cyclical adjustments in consumer decision-making processes that maintain the baseline level of environmental deviation without resolving the underlying tension.

Interpretation within the model context reveals that this pattern captures the secondary role of environmental considerations during Britain's historical milk transition. The initial absence of deviation reflects the alignment between strong incumbent habits for whole milk and the limited environmental differentiation between options at the simulation start (alt-env-mean-initial 1.12). As agents overcome these habitual barriers and increasingly select skimmed or semi-skimmed varieties motivated by health attribute perceptions (alt-health-mean-initial 1.69) and social conformity pressures (social-conformity 0.28), their choices increasingly mismatch their environmental values. The stabilization at elevated deviation levels suggests that once the market shift toward alternatives becomes entrenched around time step 13-16, the environmental dimension of milk consumption remains inadequately addressed by the available product mix, leaving consumers in a persistent state of cognitive dissonance despite the presence of justification mechanisms (justification 0.61).

For decision makers, the sustained high deviation throughout the latter half of the simulation period indicates that market transitions driven primarily by health benefits can inadvertently lock in long-term misalignments with other consumer values, particularly environmental sustainability. This suggests opportunities for differentiated positioning of milk alternatives that explicitly bridge this gap, such as emphasizing environmental credentials of low-fat options or developing new products that satisfy both health and environmental criteria simultaneously. Furthermore, the rapid emergence of this deviation during early transition phases highlights the importance of proactive environmental messaging during periods of category disruption, before alternative consumption patterns become habitual and resistant to further value-based realignment.
