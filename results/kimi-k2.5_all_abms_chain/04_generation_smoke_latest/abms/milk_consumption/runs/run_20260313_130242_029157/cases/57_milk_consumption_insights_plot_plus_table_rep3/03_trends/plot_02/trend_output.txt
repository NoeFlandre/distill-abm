During the 33-step simulation period representing the transition from 1974 to 2005, average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent exhibited a clear upward trajectory, beginning at approximately 5.29 units and culminating at 1456.60 units by the final step.

The temporal dynamics unfold through three distinct phases. An initial rapid growth period spans steps 0 through 9, characterized by statistically significant increasing trends (tau=0.900 to 1.000). During this phase, consumption accelerates sharply from the minimal baseline of 5.29 to reach approximately 1265 units by step 11, representing the most dramatic expansion in alternative milk adoption as health perceptions and initial social influence overcome incumbent whole milk habits.

Following this initial surge, the system enters an extended plateau phase spanning roughly steps 10 through 24, where consumption stabilizes and exhibits significant oscillatory behavior around a median value of approximately 1220 units. This period displays a dominant oscillation period of 15 steps, with localized peaks occurring at steps 11 (1265.47), 13 (1240.35), 19 (1221.57), and 21 (1226.86), interspersed with valleys at steps 12 (1233.62), 18 (1183.39), 20 (1214.46), and 23 (1209.70). Statistical trend analysis confirms no significant directional movement during windows 10-14, 15-19, and 20-24, indicating market saturation or equilibrium conditions where adoption rates balance against habit formation and cognitive dissonance thresholds among agents.

A third phase emerges from step 25 onward, marked by a resumption of statistically significant increasing trends through step 29 (tau=1.000, slope=48.54), driving consumption from approximately 1200 units to the terminal value of 1456.60 at step 32. This late-stage growth suggests renewed conversion momentum among remaining whole milk consumers as social norms solidify and late adopters ultimately adjust their choice functions.

**Implications for decision makers:** Public health officials and dairy industry stakeholders should anticipate logistic-like adoption curves when promoting dietary transitions, preparing infrastructure and messaging for initial rapid uptake followed by potentially lengthy plateau periods before achieving final market penetration. The pronounced oscillations during the saturation phase suggest that social influence and habit formation create strong resistance to further change once initial adopters have converted, requiring sustained intervention strategies to overcome cognitive dissonance thresholds among entrenched whole milk consumers. The final growth phase indicates that persistent policy efforts can eventually shift even resistant consumption patterns, though at substantially slower rates than initial adoption waves, emphasizing the need for long-term commitment to health education campaigns.
