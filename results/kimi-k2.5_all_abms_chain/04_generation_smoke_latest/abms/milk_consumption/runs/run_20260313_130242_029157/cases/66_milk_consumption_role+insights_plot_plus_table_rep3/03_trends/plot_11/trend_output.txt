The mean value-health deviation exhibits a persistent monotonic increase throughout the 33-step simulation period, commencing at a baseline of 0.0 and reaching approximately 0.725 by the final time step. This trajectory reflects a systematic decoupling of consumer health perceptions from actual consumption behaviors, evolving from perfect alignment toward substantial cognitive dissonance across the virtual population.

The dynamics unfold through three distinct phases characterized by varying rates of divergence. During the initial phase (steps 0-10), the deviation accelerates rapidly with pronounced steepness—achieving slopes of 0.063 between steps 0-4 and 0.041 between steps 5-9—reaching approximately 0.5 by step 10. This sharp early rise coincides with inflection points at steps 4 and 6, indicating swift initial updating of health perceptions regarding alternative milk varieties while strong incumbent habits (initial habit strength of 10.0) lock consumers into whole milk consumption.

The middle phase (steps 10-25) demonstrates marked deceleration, with growth rates diminishing to 0.012 (steps 10-14) and further plateauing to 0.003-0.004 (steps 15-24). During this extended plateau, the deviation stabilizes in the 0.55-0.60 range with inflection points at steps 16, 19, and 22, suggesting a temporary equilibrium where social learning continues to enhance health perceptions of alternatives, yet habit persistence and the threshold-based psychological mechanism (threshold of 2.0) temporarily inhibit behavioral switching for a significant market segment.

The final phase (steps 25-32) exhibits renewed acceleration with a slope of 0.019 (steps 25-29), driving the deviation to its maximum value of 0.7251 at step 32. This late-stage intensification, marked by inflection points at steps 28 and 31, indicates that among remaining whole milk consumers, the gap between held health values and choice-implied values widens considerably as health perceptions continue evolving through network effects (network parameter 8.0, interaction probability 0.3) while behavioral change encounters increasing resistance from deeply entrenched habits.

Across all 30 simulation replications, this increasing trend achieves statistical consistency with Kendall's tau of 1.0 and p-values of 0.027 in every rolling observation window, despite moderate variability in absolute values (standard deviation 0.1702, variance 0.0290). The aggregate series maintains a mean of 0.4965 with a median of 0.5584, indicating slight right-skewness where certain simulation runs exhibited particularly pronounced value-action gaps. The presence of oscillation patterns with a dominant period of 15 steps superimposed on the trend suggests cyclical reconsideration behaviors, with oscillation indices clustering at steps 18-20 and 27-31 coinciding with periods of accelerated divergence.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The persistent growth in value-health deviation to 0.725—substantially exceeding the cognitive dissonance threshold of 0.15—reveals that informational campaigns and health messaging alone are insufficient to drive behavioral change when confronting strong habitual consumption patterns. The pronounced middle-phase plateau (steps 10-25) identifies a critical intervention window where habit-disruption strategies, rather than continued education, may be most cost-effective in preventing the divergence from becoming entrenched. The late-stage acceleration suggests that remaining market segments represent increasingly resistant consumers for whom social influence mechanisms (operating at 30% interaction probability with 51% social blindness) and accumulated cognitive dissonance have failed to overcome deep-seated behavioral inertia, indicating a need for targeted approaches addressing specific habit structures or environmental restructuring rather than broad social marketing campaigns. The consistent oscillation patterns suggest optimal timing for interventions synchronized with natural reconsideration cycles in consumer decision-making.
