The simulation demonstrates a pronounced and sustained decline in average weekly whole milk consumption per person across the 33 time steps representing the period from 1974 to 2005. The trajectory begins at approximately 2,655 units at the initial step and concludes at roughly 253 units at the final step, representing a reduction of about 90% over the simulated timeframe.

The decline occurs at markedly different rates across three distinct phases. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first ten steps, consumption drops precipitously from the starting value of 2,655 to around 1,000 units. This phase exhibits the steepest rate of decrease, with particularly sharp reductions occurring between steps 5 and 9 where the slope of decline reaches approximately 221 units per step.

The second phase, roughly covering steps 10 through 20, shows a substantially moderated rate of decline. Consumption decreases gradually from approximately 1,000 units to around 750 units during this interval. The trajectory flattens considerably compared to the initial phase, with brief periods of stabilization and minor fluctuations around steps 15-19 where the trend shows no statistically significant directional movement.

The final phase from step 20 to step 32 continues the downward trajectory but at a reduced and more variable pace. Consumption declines from approximately 750 units to the terminal value of 253 units. This phase exhibits intermittent fluctuations, including temporary plateaus around steps 25-29 followed by sharper drops in the final steps. The oscillation pattern suggests periodic resistance to further decline interspersed with episodes of accelerated reduction.

Throughout the simulation, the mean consumption level across all time steps registers approximately 1,078 units, with a median value of 817 units, indicating that the distribution of consumption values skews toward the lower end as the decline progresses. The statistical analysis confirms significant monotonic decreasing trends in five of six examined intervals, with only the periods covering steps 15-19 and 25-29 showing no statistically significant trend, consistent with the observed plateau phases.

This trajectory reflects the modeled transition in British milk consumption patterns, capturing the historical shift away from whole milk toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The steep early decline corresponds to rapid initial adoption of alternatives among susceptible consumers, while the flattening curve in later stages represents the increasing difficulty of converting remaining holdouts characterized by stronger habitual attachments, lower social susceptibility, or higher thresholds for cognitive dissonance. The residual consumption level of approximately 253 units at the conclusion suggests that even after three decades of transition, a persistent minority of consumers maintains whole milk consumption, consistent with the calibrated parameters governing habit strength and social influence resistance.
