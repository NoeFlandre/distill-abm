The habit-based reinforcement factor for alternative milk varieties initiates at a neutral value of 1.0000, reflecting the absence of established habitual tendencies toward skimmed and semi-skimmed options at the 1974 starting point. During the initial five time steps, the metric exhibits minimal variation, maintaining values between 1.0000 and approximately 1.0205, with a slight depression to 1.0167 at time step 5. This early plateau indicates that despite agents possessing positive initial health perceptions (1.69) and environmental awareness (1.12) regarding alternatives, the choice functions remain dominated by the incumbent whole milk habit strength (10.0), requiring a gestation period before new habitual patterns can overcome this inertia.

Between time steps 5 and 14, the system undergoes a phase transition characterized by accelerating habit formation. From time steps 5 to 9, the factor increases significantly at a rate of 0.010 per step (p=0.027), followed by a more aggressive ascent of 0.030 per step between time steps 10 and 14—the steepest sustained growth observed in the series. This rapid escalation suggests that agents have accumulated sufficient consumption experiences within their memory windows (5.0 time steps) to cross the habit threshold (2.0), transforming alternative milk selection from deliberate, information-processing decisions into automatic behavioral patterns reinforced by social conformity mechanisms (0.28) and network interactions (p=0.3).

At time step 15, a structural change point occurs, shifting the dynamics toward moderated growth. The rate decelerates to 0.027 per step during time steps 15-19, then progressively slows through subsequent intervals: 0.020 (time steps 20-24) and 0.018 (time steps 25-29). Despite this deceleration, the metric maintains monotonic growth, ultimately reaching 1.5879 by time step 32—representing nearly a 59% increase in habit-driven preference for alternative milk compared to the neutral baseline. This saturation pattern indicates that while alternative milk habits continue strengthening throughout the simulation period, the rate of habit consolidation slows as the population approaches a new equilibrium and the influence of social learning diminishes against established routines.

Superimposed on this secular trend, oscillatory fluctuations emerge around time steps 17-18 and 26-30, introducing short-term volatility with a dominant periodicity of approximately 15 time steps. These oscillations likely reflect the interaction between memory decay cycles and the propagation of social influence through the Watts-Strogatz network topology, creating temporary windows where habit strength wavers before reconsolidating.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The identification of a 5-time-step latent period before habit formation accelerates suggests that early-stage interventions targeting alternative product adoption should prioritize reducing cognitive and social barriers to initial trial, as behavioral automaticity only develops after sustained exposure beyond the threshold period. The steep acceleration phase (time steps 10-14) represents a critical leverage point where marketing and policy efforts can achieve maximum impact on long-term behavioral lock-in, as habits formed during this window exhibit the strongest persistence. Once the change point at time step 15 is passed and growth rates moderate, shifting consumption patterns becomes increasingly resource-intensive due to entrenched habits, indicating that late-stage market entry faces significantly higher switching costs. The persistent oscillations in mature stages (time steps 26-30) reveal cyclical vulnerability windows where competing products might successfully disrupt established routines during temporary habit-weakening intervals.
