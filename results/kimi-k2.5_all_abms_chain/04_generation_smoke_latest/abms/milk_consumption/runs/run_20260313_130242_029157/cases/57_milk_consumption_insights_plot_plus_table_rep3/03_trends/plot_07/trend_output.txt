The simulation of milk consumption adoption from 1974 to 2005 reveals a three-phase trajectory in the aggregate consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. Initially, at step 0, consumption begins at minimal levels of approximately 5.3 units per person. Through step 10, the system undergoes rapid exponential growth, reaching approximately 1,240 units, representing the initial diffusion of low-fat alternatives into the consumer population as agents begin overcoming incumbent habits.

Following this rapid uptake, the system enters a prolonged plateau phase spanning approximately steps 10 through 25, where consumption fluctuates moderately between roughly 1,180 and 1,265 units. During this middle period, the adoption rate stagnates as the market approaches saturation among early adopters and encounters resistance from entrenched whole milk consumption habits, with minor oscillations reflecting ongoing social influence dynamics and cognitive dissonance thresholds within the network.

In the final phase from step 25 to step 32, consumption resumes a steady upward trajectory, climbing from approximately 1,220 units to reach 1,456 units by the simulation's conclusion. This late-stage growth suggests the eventual overcoming of habit persistence and social blindness barriers, allowing the remaining resistant agents to transition toward low-fat alternatives as social norms and accumulated peer influence penetrate previously resistant network segments.

The overall pattern indicates that social influence and habit formation create significant inertia in dietary transitions, with adoption proceeding through distinct phases of rapid innovation, mid-cycle stagnation, and eventual renewed diffusion as normative pressures and accumulated social learning overcome initial resistance.

For decision makers, these findings suggest that public health interventions targeting dietary change should anticipate prolonged plateau periods where progress appears stalled, requiring sustained effort beyond initial adoption waves. The late-stage growth indicates that persistent messaging and social norm cultivation can eventually penetrate resistant population segments, suggesting that abandoning campaigns during apparent stagnation phases would be premature. Additionally, the significant role of social influence implies that interventions leveraging network effects and peer modeling may accelerate transitions more effectively than individual-focused approaches.
