The cumulative frequency of whole milk selections initiates at zero and exhibits a continuous monotonic increase throughout the entire simulation timeframe, terminating at approximately 2,195 total selection events. This persistent upward trajectory indicates that whole milk consumption choices occur consistently across all periods from 1974 to 2005, though the pace of accumulation varies significantly across distinct temporal phases.

The accumulation rate demonstrates an initial intensification followed by progressive deceleration. During the earliest interval spanning time steps 0 through 4, whole milk selections accumulate at a rate of approximately 71 events per step. This velocity briefly accelerates to roughly 76 events per step during the subsequent interval (time steps 5 through 9) before entering a sustained decline. By the middle phase (time steps 15 through 19), the accumulation rate moderates to approximately 67 events per step, and by the later observation windows (time steps 25 through 29), the rate further diminishes to roughly 64 events per step. This pattern of decelerating accumulation persists through the penultimate periods, with a notable change point occurring at time step 32.

Distinct inflections in the accumulation dynamics emerge at time steps 1, 7, and 31, indicating subtle shifts in the curvature of the cumulative trend. Specifically, the transition at time step 7 marks the boundary between the initial high-growth phase and the subsequent period of declining accumulation rates. During the final stages of the simulation, particularly between time steps 27 and 30, oscillatory indicators suggest the emergence of cyclical fluctuations in the underlying selection patterns, implying increased volatility in whole milk choice behavior during the early 2000s despite the absence of local maxima or minima in the aggregate cumulative series.

For decision makers, these dynamics reveal that while the British public's transition toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties substantially reduces the velocity of whole milk adoption over the three-decade period, whole milk maintains a persistent and non-trivial residual demand that never fully dissipates. The gradual deceleration—rather than abrupt cessation—of accumulation suggests that deep-seated habit persistence and social network reinforcement sustain whole milk consumption longer than might be predicted by threshold-based adoption models. The oscillatory patterns observed in the final years indicate that consumer commitment to alternatives remains vulnerable to periodic reconsideration, warranting sustained distribution infrastructure for whole milk alongside alternative varieties. Marketing and supply chain strategies should account for a gradually shrinking but stable baseline market, with particular attention to volatility management during periods of shifting social norms.
