The simulation depicts a marked transformation in aggregate alternative milk consumption over the 31-year period. Initially, at the starting time step representing 1974, consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties remains minimal at approximately 5.3 units per agent, reflecting the historical dominance of whole milk and weak initial health and environmental perceptions regarding alternatives.

A rapid acceleration phase characterizes the first decade, with consumption rising steeply to exceed 1200 units by the eleventh time step. This period corresponds to the initial diffusion of health consciousness regarding fat content and the early erosion of habitual whole milk consumption, as agents begin overcoming the strong incumbent habit strength of 10.0. Notably, this growth phase exhibits minor oscillations, including a temporary dip around the twelfth time step to approximately 1234 units before resuming upward momentum.

Following this initial surge, the system enters an extended plateau phase spanning roughly from the tenth to the twenty-fourth time step, where consumption stabilizes in the 1180 to 1260 unit range with notable fluctuations. This plateau suggests market segmentation where a substantial portion of consumers maintain whole milk habits despite social influence mechanisms, while early adopters have already transitioned. The oscillatory behavior during this period, with local peaks around steps 13, 19, and 21, and troughs near steps 12, 18, 20, and 23, indicates ongoing dynamic tension between habit maintenance and social diffusion pressures.

In the final phase, beginning around the twenty-fifth time step and continuing through the simulation endpoint, consumption resumes a steady upward trajectory, climbing from approximately 1210 units to reach 1456.6 units by the final time step representing 2005. This late-stage growth reflects the cumulative success of sustained social influence, the gradual weakening of incumbent habits among remaining holdouts, and the reinforcement of alternative milk consumption as the new social norm.

For decision makers, the simulation suggests three critical insights: First, initial market penetration faces the strongest resistance from incumbent habits, requiring concentrated efforts to overcome initial preference inertia. Second, markets may experience prolonged saturation periods where growth stalls despite ongoing campaigns, indicating the need for novel intervention strategies to activate resistant consumer segments. Third, sustained long-term growth remains achievable through cumulative social influence, suggesting that consistent messaging over decades yields compounding returns as social norms progressively shift.
