The mean deviation between consumers' held health values and the values implied by their actual milk choices begins at zero and increases monotonically throughout the 31-year simulation period, reaching approximately 0.725 by the final time step. This consistent upward trajectory indicates a growing misalignment between what consumers believe about health attributes and the choices they actually make, with the average deviation across all simulation runs settling at 0.497 over the full period.

The progression follows a distinct three-phase pattern. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first ten time steps, the deviation accelerates rapidly from 0 to roughly 0.52, exhibiting the steepest slope of 0.063 observed in any segment of the series. This early surge likely reflects the period when agents initially process information about the health benefits of alternative milk varieties, creating an immediate gap between their updating health perceptions and their habitual consumption of whole milk, which remains locked in by the high initial habit strength of 10.0.

The middle phase, extending from step 10 through step 25, demonstrates a marked deceleration in the rate of divergence, with the slope dropping to approximately 0.003 between steps 15-19. During this plateau period, the deviation increases only modestly from 0.52 to approximately 0.58, suggesting a temporary stabilization of the tension between health values and choice behavior. This inflection aligns temporally with the historical crossover point where skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption surpasses whole milk, indicating that as the market transitions, the relationship between health perceptions and actual choices reaches a temporary equilibrium.

The final phase, from step 25 to step 32, exhibits a secondary acceleration with the slope increasing to 0.019, driving the deviation upward to its terminal value of 0.725. Notable oscillations emerge during this late period, particularly around steps 18-20 and again at steps 27-31, with a dominant periodicity of 15 time steps suggesting cyclical adjustments in consumer decision-making as social influence mechanisms and network effects create feedback loops in the mature market.

Across all 30 simulation runs, the series maintains low variance through the initial ten steps, after which individual runs diverge slightly while preserving the core S-curve trajectory. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms statistically significant monotonic increases across all windows (p=0.027), validating the robustness of the upward trend.

For decision makers, the persistent growth in value-health deviation throughout the transition period indicates that health messaging alone cannot fully explain or drive consumption changes; habitual behavior and social conformity mechanisms create substantial friction that causes consumers' choices to increasingly diverge from their stated health values over time. The three-phase structure suggests distinct intervention opportunities: early-stage education to address the rapid initial divergence, mid-stage transitional support during the market crossover when habit breaking is most critical, and late-stage strategies to address the renewed divergence driven by social network effects in the consolidated market.
