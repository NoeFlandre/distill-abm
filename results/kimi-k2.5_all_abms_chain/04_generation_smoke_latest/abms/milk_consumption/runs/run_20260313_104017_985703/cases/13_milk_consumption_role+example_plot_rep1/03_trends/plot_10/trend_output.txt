The mean choice function deviation begins at approximately zero at the start of the simulation, indicating near-perfect homogeneity in consumer choice functions when the model is initialized to 1974 conditions. Over the first five time steps, the metric increases rapidly, reaching approximately 0.58. This sharp initial rise reflects the early dissolution of uniform consumption patterns as agents begin processing health and environmental information and responding to social influences, causing individual preferences to diverge quickly from the initial state where whole milk dominated and habits were strongest.

Following this initial phase, the rate of increase moderates significantly. Between step 5 and step 30, the deviation rises gradually in a near-linear fashion from approximately 0.58 to 0.75. This slower, steady growth suggests that while the population continues to sort into diverse preference configurations over time, the primary phase of rapid preference polarization has concluded. The system appears to be converging toward a stable level of heterogeneity characteristic of the mature market structure observed by 2005.

Throughout the entire simulation period, variance across simulation runs remains minimal, with all trajectories closely aligned. This consistency indicates that the emergence of preference heterogeneity is a robust, deterministic feature of the model's dynamics rather than an artifact of stochastic variation.
