The habit factor applied to alternative milk consumption choices begins at a neutral baseline of 1.0, reflecting the absence of established behavioral patterns toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties in 1974 when whole milk dominated consumer preferences. This metric remains effectively stable throughout the initial five time steps, indicating a prolonged lag phase where early adopters had not yet developed sufficient repetition to encode automatic behavioral responses toward alternative milk options.

Beginning at approximately the fifth time step, the habit factor enters a sustained trajectory of growth that continues through the remainder of the 31-year period. By time step 15—roughly corresponding to the late 1980s—the metric undergoes a distinct inflection, accelerating from approximately 1.2 to reach nearly 1.4, suggesting that habit formation for alternative milk crossed a critical threshold where behavioral automaticity became self-reinforcing across the consumer population.

The progression continues with monotonic increases through time step 25, though the rate of growth moderates slightly compared to the mid-period acceleration. During the final decade of the simulation, specifically between time steps 17-18 and again at 26-30, the metric exhibits minor oscillations superimposed on the upward trend, fluctuating within a narrow band while maintaining overall growth toward the terminal value of approximately 1.59. These late-stage variations suggest that even as strong habitual preferences for alternative milk became entrenched by 2005, residual volatility in consumer behavior persisted, potentially reflecting ongoing sensitivity to health information, social influence fluctuations, or competitive market dynamics.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The five-year initial stability period indicates that marketing strategies for alternative milk products should anticipate a significant lag before habit formation begins to drive automatic repurchase behavior, requiring sustained investment during early market development. The critical inflection around year 15 suggests that interventions aimed at accelerating adoption should concentrate resources around this midpoint, when consumers appear most susceptible to habit consolidation. Finally, the late-stage oscillations despite high terminal habit strength imply that market leaders cannot rely solely on habitual loyalty; continued reinforcement through product innovation or health messaging remains necessary to prevent erosion of market share gains even after alternative milk preferences become behaviorally automatic.
