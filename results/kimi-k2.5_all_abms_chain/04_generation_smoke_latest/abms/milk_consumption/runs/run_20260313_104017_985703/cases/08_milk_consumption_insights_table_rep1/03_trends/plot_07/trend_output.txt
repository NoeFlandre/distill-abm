Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk began the simulation period at approximately 5.3 units per person per week, reflecting the initial market dominance of whole milk among the agent population in 1974. During the first nine time steps, the metric underwent sharp, statistically significant increases, with weekly consumption rising rapidly to approach 1265 units by time step 11.

Following this initial surge, the trajectory entered an extended phase of fluctuation lasting from approximately time step 10 through time step 24. Throughout this middle period, consumption oscillated between minimum values near 1183 units and maximum values approaching 1265 units, exhibiting no significant long-term directional trend. Specific oscillations occurred around time steps 11-13, 18-21, and 23, with values repeatedly rising and falling within a constrained band of roughly 1200 to 1265 units.

After time step 24, the pattern shifted toward renewed growth. From time step 25 through the conclusion of the period at time step 32, consumption resumed a significant upward trajectory, ultimately reaching approximately 1456.6 units. This final phase added roughly 240 units to the consumption level, representing the peak values observed across the entire timeline.

The overall progression reveals a multi-stage adoption process: an initial rapid transition away from incumbent whole milk consumption, followed by a prolonged intermediate equilibrium where alternative milk adoption stabilized among a substantial subset of agents, and concluding with a secondary wave of adoption driving consumption to maximum levels. This pattern suggests that habit formation and social network effects initially facilitated rapid change but subsequently created a temporary plateau as the model population stratified into distinct adopter and non-adopter groups, before final convergence toward near-universal adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties.

For decision makers, these dynamics indicate that dietary transitions within populations occur through distinct phases rather than smooth continuous growth. The prolonged plateau observed during the middle period implies that social influence mechanisms can stabilize consumption patterns at intermediate levels for extended durations, potentially requiring targeted interventions to overcome incumbent habits among remaining non-adopters. The resumption of growth in the final phase suggests that persistent environmental or health perception shifts can eventually overcome these stabilization forces, but such transitions may operate on longer time horizons than initial adoption waves.
