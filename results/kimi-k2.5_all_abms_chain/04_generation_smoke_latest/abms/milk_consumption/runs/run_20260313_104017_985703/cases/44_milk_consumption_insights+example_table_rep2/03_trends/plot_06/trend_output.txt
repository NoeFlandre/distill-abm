The average weekly consumption of whole milk begins at approximately 2,655 milliliters per person at the outset of the 1974–2005 period, reflecting the historical market dominance of whole milk. Over the subsequent 32 time steps, consumption declines monotonically though non-linearly, reaching approximately 253 milliliters by the end of the period.

The decline exhibits distinct phases. An initial steep decrease occurs during the first five time steps, likely representing the rapid adoption by health-conscious early movers responding to emerging nutritional information about saturated fat content. The rate of decline moderates between time steps 13 and 16, suggesting a temporary equilibrium as easily persuaded consumers have already switched while the remaining population exhibits stronger habit persistence or network isolation.

Superimposed on the downward trajectory are oscillatory fluctuations with a dominant period of approximately 15 time steps, most visible during the middle phase between time steps 15 and 25. These cycles likely reflect the periodic breaking of habit thresholds and the wave-like propagation of social influence through the Watts-Strogatz network topology, where clusters of agents simultaneously reconsider their choices before settling into new habitual patterns.

By the final time step, consumption stabilizes at low levels near 253 milliliters, with the terminal period marking a change point that indicates the completion of the dietary transition. The trajectory demonstrates how initial high-habit strength among incumbent whole milk consumers gradually erodes through cumulative social influence and shifting health perceptions until alternative milk varieties dominate consumption patterns.

For public health decision makers, the steep initial decline suggests that early intervention periods offer the highest leverage for shifting population-level dietary behaviors. The prolonged tail and oscillatory dynamics indicate that sustained messaging campaigns are necessary to overcome entrenched habits, as social influence effects require multiple cycles to fully penetrate the consumer network.
