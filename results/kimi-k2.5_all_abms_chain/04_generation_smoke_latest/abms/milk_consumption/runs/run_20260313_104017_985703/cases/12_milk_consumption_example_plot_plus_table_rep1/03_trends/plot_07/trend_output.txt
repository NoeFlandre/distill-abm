The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed per agent initiates at approximately 5.3 units, reflecting the historical dominance of whole milk at the simulation's outset in 1974. During the initial 11 steps, consumption undergoes a rapid, statistically significant increase, with pronounced upward trends detected in the intervals from steps 0–4 and 5–9. This acceleration reflects the early adoption phase where health perceptions and social influence begin overcoming initial habit strength. The trajectory reaches a first major peak of 1,265.5 units at step 11, marking the conclusion of the initial growth period.

Following this expansion, the system transitions into an extended plateau phase characterized by oscillation and temporary equilibrium, persisting from approximately step 12 through step 27. At step 12, the metric registers a decline to 1,233.6 units, followed by a recovery to a secondary peak of 1,240.3 units at step 13. The dynamics continue to fluctuate within a bounded range, with notable valleys occurring at step 18 (1,183.4 units), step 20 (1,214.5 units), and step 23 (1,209.7 units), interspersed with local peaks at step 19 (1,221.6 units) and step 21 (1,226.9 units). Statistical analysis confirms the absence of significant monotonic trends between steps 10–14, 15–19, and 20–24, indicating a stable regime where habit persistence and cognitive dissonance balance against continued social influence. This period exhibits a dominant oscillatory cycle of approximately 15 steps, suggesting cyclic adjustments in agent decision-making as the market approaches saturation.

In the final segment from step 28 to step 32, the consumption metric resumes a significant upward trajectory, with step 27 serving as an inflection point marking the end of the plateau. This late-stage growth, supported by a significant increasing trend in the interval from steps 25–29, drives the quantity from approximately 1,245 units to a terminal maximum of 1,456.6 units at step 32. This final surge represents the completion of the transition, as the remaining holdout agents adopt skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties, resulting in the near-total displacement of whole milk consumption by the simulation's conclusion in 2005. The pattern remains consistent across all 30 simulation runs, indicating robust replication of the historical adoption curve despite stochastic variation in individual agent interactions.
