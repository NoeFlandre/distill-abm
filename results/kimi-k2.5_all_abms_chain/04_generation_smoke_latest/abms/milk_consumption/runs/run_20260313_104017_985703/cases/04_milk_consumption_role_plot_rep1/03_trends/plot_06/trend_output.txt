The data reveal a consistent downward trajectory in mean whole milk consumption across all simulation runs, declining from approximately 2,650 units at step 0 to roughly 200–300 units by step 32. This 31-step period captures the full transition from 1974 market dominance of whole milk to 2005 market conditions.

The decline occurs through four distinct phases. During the initial phase (steps 0–10), consumption drops sharply by approximately 60%, falling from ~2,650 to ~950–1,100 units. This rapid early reduction reflects the initial disruption of strong incumbent habits (initialized at maximum strength of 10.0) as agents begin processing information about health and environmental attributes of alternative milk varieties.

The second phase (steps 10–25) exhibits a moderated, steady decline from approximately 1,100 to 600–700 units, representing a 35–45% reduction over 15 steps. During this middle period, social influence mechanisms operate through the Watts-Strogatz network structure (with parameter 8.0 and interaction probability 0.3), gradually spreading alternative milk adoption as agents observe and respond to peer behavior with moderate susceptibility (0.2).

The third phase (steps 25–30) shows accelerated decline, dropping from ~650 to ~300–350 units—a reduction of nearly 50% in just 5 steps. This late-stage acceleration suggests a tipping point dynamic where alternative milk varieties achieve critical mass, transforming social norms such that whole milk consumption becomes increasingly stigmatized or peripheral within the consumer network.

The final phase (steps 30–32) stabilizes at residual levels of 200–300 units, indicating a persistent minority of consumers who maintain whole milk consumption despite the dominant market shift. This floor effect likely represents agents with high habit thresholds, low social conformity weights (0.28), or high social blindness (0.51) parameters that insulate them from broader normative pressures.

Across simulation runs, variability remains minimal at initialization (converging at ~2,650) and at termination (converging at ~250), but diverges moderately during the transition phases, particularly between steps 8–12 where different stochastic realizations of social interactions and probabilistic habit-breaking generate temporary dispersion in consumption trajectories before reconvergence toward the historical trend.
