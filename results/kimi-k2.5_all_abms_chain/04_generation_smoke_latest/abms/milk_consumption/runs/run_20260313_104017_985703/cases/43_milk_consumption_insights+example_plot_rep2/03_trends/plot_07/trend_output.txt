The mean weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately zero units, consistent with the 1974 baseline where whole milk dominated market share. Over the first ten steps, consumption undergoes rapid acceleration, steeply rising to values between 1,200 and 1,280 units. This sharp initial trajectory indicates a period of swift adoption driven by the diffusion of health perceptions and early social influence propagation through the Watts-Strogatz network topology, as agents with low initial habits encounter alternative choice characteristics.

Between steps 10 and 25, the consumption metric enters a prolonged plateau phase, oscillating between approximately 1,180 and 1,250 units with minor fluctuations. This stabilization likely reflects a temporary equilibrium where the adoption rate decelerates due to habit persistence and cognitive dissonance thresholds among remaining whole milk consumers, while social influence continues to operate primarily through reinforcement rather than conversion. The slight dip observed around step 18 suggests transient periods where habit strength temporarily outweighs social and health considerations before rebalancing.

From step 25 onwards, the metric exhibits a second distinct growth phase, climbing steadily from approximately 1,220 units to between 1,420 and 1,480 by step 32. This late-stage increase suggests that sustained exposure to social norms and accumulated network effects eventually overcome initial resistance mechanisms, capturing late adopters or intensifying commitment among existing converts through post-choice evaluation and justification processes.

Across repeated simulation runs, trajectories demonstrate consistently low variance throughout the initial and middle phases, with values tightly clustered within a narrow band. This reproducibility indicates that the specified parameter configuration—including the interaction probability of 0.3, social susceptibility weightings, and memory lifetime of 5—generates robust adoption dynamics insensitive to stochastic variation in agent interactions. Marginal divergence emerges only in the final steps, suggesting increased sensitivity to random network effects as the agent population approaches saturation.

For public health decision makers, the distinct three-phase pattern—rapid early adoption, mid-simulation plateau, and late-stage growth—indicates that intervention timing critically determines efficacy. Health messaging campaigns achieve maximum penetration when deployed during the initial growth phase, while the plateau period requires sustained rather than intensive efforts to overcome habit-based resistance. The predictability of the adoption trajectory suggests that social influence mechanisms in dietary choice follow reproducible patterns, supporting the strategic use of early-stage social norm marketing to accelerate the transition from whole to lower-fat milk varieties.
