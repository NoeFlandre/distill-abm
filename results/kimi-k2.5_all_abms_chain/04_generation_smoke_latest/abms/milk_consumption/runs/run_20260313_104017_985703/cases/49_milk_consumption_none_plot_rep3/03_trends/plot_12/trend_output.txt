The metric measuring mean deviation between agents' held environmental values and the environmental values implied by their milk choices exhibits a rapid sigmoidal increase followed by long-term stabilization. At step 0, the deviation initiates at approximately 0, indicating initial alignment between consumer environmental values and milk selections. The deviation increases sharply during the first five steps, ascending from 0 to approximately 13, with particularly rapid growth between steps 0 and 3 where values rise from 0 to roughly 12. Between steps 5 and 10, the growth rate decelerates as the metric approaches 13.5. From step 10 through step 32, the deviation stabilizes, maintaining values consistently between 13.3 and 13.5 with minimal variation across simulation replicates.

This trajectory reflects the modelled transition from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The initial near-zero deviation indicates that whole milk consumption was environmentally consistent with agents' held values. The rapid increase between steps 0 and 10 corresponds to the primary adoption period of alternative milk varieties, suggesting that these alternatives carry environmental implications that increasingly conflict with agents' environmental values—likely due to higher processing impacts associated with reduced-fat milk production. The stabilization after step 10 indicates completion of the market transition, with the persistent deviation of approximately 13.5 representing a steady-state misalignment between environmental values and actual consumption behavior. The tight clustering across all 1,000-agent simulation runs demonstrates robust convergence to this outcome.
