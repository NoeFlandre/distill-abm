The mean whole milk quantity exhibits a consistent downward trajectory across the 31-step simulation period representing the years 1974 to 2005. The metric initiates at approximately 2,650 units at step 0, followed by a steep decline during the initial phase. By step 10, the quantity decreases to roughly 950-1,000 units, representing a reduction of approximately 62-64% from the initial value.

During the intermediate phase spanning steps 10 through 20, the rate of decline moderates considerably. The mean whole milk quantity decreases gradually from approximately 1,000 units to around 700-750 units by step 20. This phase exhibits reduced volatility compared to the initial steep decline, suggesting a stabilization of the transition dynamics.

In the final phase from step 20 to step 31, the downward trend continues albeit at a slower pace with some minor fluctuations. The quantity declines from approximately 750 units to a terminal value of roughly 250-300 units by step 31. This represents a total reduction of approximately 88-90% from the initial 1974 baseline.

The trajectory reflects the historical market transition from whole milk to lower-fat alternatives, driven by evolving health perceptions, social influence mechanisms, and habit formation processes embedded in the agent-based structure. The consistent decline across all simulation runs indicates robust model behavior in capturing the macro-level shift in consumer preferences.

Insights for decision makers: The steep initial decline suggests that intervention strategies and market transitions occur most rapidly during early adoption phases, indicating that initial consumer education and product availability are critical during market disruption periods. The persistence of whole milk consumption even after three decades, albeit at reduced levels, suggests the existence of a resilient consumer segment potentially driven by strong habitual attachments or resistance to health messaging. Future policy and marketing strategies should account for this long tail of traditional consumption, potentially requiring targeted approaches for habituated consumers rather than broad-based campaigns as the market matures. The mid-period moderation in decline rates suggests that maintaining momentum in dietary transitions requires sustained effort, as initial rapid shifts may naturally slow without continued reinforcement of behavioral change drivers.
