The simulation of milk adoption behaviors among British consumers from 1974 to 2005 reveals a three-phase trajectory in the consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk varieties. The process begins at step 0 with minimal alternative milk consumption at approximately 5.3 units per person weekly, reflecting the historical market dominance of whole milk in 1974. 

During the initial adoption phase spanning steps 0 through 11, consumption increases rapidly and significantly, with statistically significant upward trends detected in windows 0-4 (slope: 112.8) and 5-9 (slope: 139.0). By step 11, consumption reaches approximately 1,265 units, marking the transition from negligible adoption to widespread use.

A prolonged plateau phase emerges between steps 11 and 26, characterized by oscillation around a stable mean of approximately 1,220 units. During this period, consumption fluctuates between local minima of roughly 1,183-1,214 units and local maxima of 1,221-1,265 units, with no statistically significant long-term trend. Notable oscillations occur with a dominant period of 15 steps, and inflection points at steps 12, 13, 16, 19, and 22 indicate shifts in the rate of change without sustained directional movement.

The final phase from steps 26 to 32 exhibits renewed significant growth (slope: 48.5), with consumption rising from approximately 1,220 units to reach 1,456.6 units by step 32. This represents the maximum value observed across the 33-step simulation, indicating continued market penetration of lower-fat milk varieties toward the end of the study period.
