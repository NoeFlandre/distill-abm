Temporal evolution of the mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk across 32 time steps reveals three distinct phases of behavioral consolidation. During the initial seven steps, values remain relatively stable near the baseline of 1.0, fluctuating minimally between approximately 1.0 and 1.02, indicating sustained resistance to habit formation for alternative milk varieties during this latency period. Between steps 7 and 15, the metric undergoes rapid acceleration, increasing from roughly 1.03 to approximately 1.23, suggesting a critical transition window where accumulated experiences and social influences exceed thresholds necessary for habitual adoption. From step 15 through the conclusion at step 32, the values continue ascending at a sustained high rate, reaching approximately 1.58 to 1.59, representing a nearly 59% increase from initial levels and indicating the establishment of robust habitual preferences for lower-fat milk options.

Interpreting these dynamics within the context of the 1974-2005 British milk transition, the initial stagnation reflects the dominance of incumbent whole milk habits (parameterized at initial strength 10.0) and the memory-lifetime constraint of 5.0 steps that delays significant habit accumulation for alternatives. The inflection around step 7-10 corresponds to the point where agent populations begin overcoming cognitive dissonance thresholds (0.15) and social susceptibility mechanisms (0.3 probability of interaction, 0.28 conformity weight) facilitate collective shifts away from automatic whole milk selection. The sustained linear growth in later steps demonstrates that once habit thresholds (2.0) are breached for alternative products, reinforcement mechanisms create self-sustaining behavioral momentum that continues strengthening through the end of the simulation period.

For decision makers, these trends suggest that interventions targeting milk consumption habits face a critical temporal constraint: the first third of the transition period exhibits minimal responsiveness due to entrenched habitual inertia, while the middle phase (approximately steps 8-15) represents a high-leverage window where habit formation is most malleable to external influence. Once the trajectory enters the final phase, habits become sufficiently automatized that changing consumption patterns requires substantially greater counter-inertial force, implying that public health or marketing initiatives would achieve maximum efficacy when deployed during the acceleration phase rather than during periods of established habit dominance.
