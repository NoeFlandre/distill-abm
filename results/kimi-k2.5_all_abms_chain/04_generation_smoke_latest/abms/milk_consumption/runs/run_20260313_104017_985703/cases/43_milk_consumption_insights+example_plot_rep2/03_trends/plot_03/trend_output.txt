The cumulative count of whole milk choices initiates at zero and increases approximately linearly throughout the 32-step simulation period, reaching values ranging from roughly 2,000 to 2,400 total selections by the final step. The trajectories remain tightly clustered with minimal variance during the initial five steps, but subsequently exhibit progressively widening divergence across simulation runs, with the inter-run spread reaching approximately 400 choices by the conclusion. This sustained linear accumulation indicates that whole milk maintains a persistent baseline selection rate among agents, suggesting that strong incumbent habit formation and disposition triggering mechanisms create resilient consumption patterns that resist displacement by alternative milk varieties despite social influence and health perception factors. The expanding variance demonstrates that stochastic elements in social network interactions and probabilistic decision-making generate path-dependent outcomes, where initial random fluctuations in agent behaviors compound over time to produce substantially different aggregate consumption levels across simulation replicates.

For decision makers, these findings suggest that transitions in dietary behaviors may leave persistent residual demand for incumbent products, requiring targeted strategies for the subset of consumers with deeply entrenched habits rather than expecting universal adoption of alternatives. The significant variability across trajectories implies that early stochastic events and social network structures critically influence long-term consumption patterns, indicating that interventions should prioritize identifying and influencing key network nodes to maximize impact. The constant rate of whole milk selection further suggests that without active disruption of habit thresholds, incumbent consumption may stabilize at a steady-state level rather than declining to zero, warranting sustained intervention efforts rather than time-limited campaigns.
