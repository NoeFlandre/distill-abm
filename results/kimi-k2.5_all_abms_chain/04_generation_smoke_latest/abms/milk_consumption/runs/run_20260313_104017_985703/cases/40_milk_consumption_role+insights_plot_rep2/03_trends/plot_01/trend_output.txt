The simulation reveals a pronounced, non-linear decline in mean whole milk consumption across the 31-year period, characterized by four distinct phases. At initialization (Step 0), consumption begins at approximately 2,650-2,680 units, reflecting the market dominance of whole milk in 1974. During the first phase (Steps 0-10), a steep, accelerated decline occurs, with consumption plummeting by roughly 60% to reach approximately 1,000 units by Step 10. This rapid initial drop corresponds to the early adoption period where consumers first began shifting to skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives, driven by emerging health perceptions and environmental awareness.

The second phase (Steps 10-20) exhibits a marked deceleration in the rate of decline, with consumption decreasing gradually from 1,000 to approximately 750 units. This plateau-like behavior suggests the operation of strong habit persistence and social network effects, where incumbent whole milk consumers resisted change despite growing social pressure, creating a temporary stabilization in the market transition.

During the third phase (Steps 20-27), the decline resumes at a moderate pace, dropping from 750 to roughly 600 units, indicating the progressive erosion of habitual barriers as social norms increasingly favored alternative milk varieties. Finally, the fourth phase (Steps 27-32) demonstrates another sharp acceleration in decline, with consumption falling rapidly from 600 to approximately 220-250 units by the terminal period. This late-stage acceleration reflects the maturation of the market shift, where remaining whole milk consumers became increasingly isolated within their social networks, facing intensified social pressure and diminishing infrastructure support for their preferred choice.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The multi-phase nature of the decline suggests that intervention strategies must be temporally adaptive. During early phases (Steps 0-10), public health campaigns focusing on health attribute education prove most effective, as consumers are actively seeking information. During the middle plateau (Steps 10-20), targeted social network interventions and influencer marketing become critical to overcome habit persistence. In the final phases (Steps 27+), policy makers should focus on infrastructure support and availability of alternatives while managing the social isolation effects on remaining traditional consumers. The non-linear trajectory also indicates that market projections assuming constant rates of change will significantly underestimate consumption in early periods and overestimate it in middle periods, requiring dynamic forecasting models for accurate supply chain and agricultural planning.
