The simulation begins with perfect consistency between agents' internal health valuations and their milk consumption choices, starting at a deviation of 0.0000 in 1974. Over the 32-year simulated period, this metric exhibits a monotonic increase, ultimately reaching 0.7251 by 2005, with a mean deviation of 0.4965 across all time steps. This trajectory indicates a substantial accumulation of cognitive dissonance, wherein agents' held health beliefs become increasingly misaligned with the health implications of their actual milk consumption behaviors.

The dynamics unfold through several distinct phases characterized by changing velocities of divergence. An initial period of rapid escalation occurs between steps 0 and 4, where the deviation increases at a rate of approximately 0.063 per step. This steep early divergence reflects the immediate tension as agents encounter alternative health and environmental information regarding skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties while maintaining strong incumbent habits favoring whole milk. 

Subsequently, the rate of divergence decelerates considerably through the early-to-mid period spanning steps 5 to 14, with slopes decreasing sequentially from 0.041 to 0.012 per step. This moderation suggests that social influence mechanisms and habit formation processes begin to buffer the pure translation of health information into behavioral change, creating friction in the decision-making process. A temporary stabilization emerges around steps 15 through 19, where the deviation increases minimally at a rate of merely 0.003 per step, approaching a local plateau near 0.58. This plateau indicates a temporary equilibrium period where the population's cognitive dissonance reaches a steady state as partial adoption of alternative milk varieties occurs.

However, the late period from steps 20 to 29 exhibits renewed acceleration, with growth rates increasing to 0.004 and subsequently to 0.019 per step, driving the final value to 0.7251. Superimposed upon this underlying trend, periodic oscillations with a dominant cycle of approximately 15 steps emerge particularly in the latter stages of the simulation, manifesting around steps 18–20 and again at steps 27–31. These oscillations suggest cyclical reconciliation processes wherein agents repeatedly approach cognitive dissonance thresholds and attempt temporary resolutions before further divergence occurs.

For decision makers, these dynamics indicate that health communication strategies face substantial structural barriers when competing against entrenched social norms and habitual consumption patterns, as consumers may sustain increasingly significant gaps between their stated health values and actual purchasing behaviors over multi-decade timescales. The identification of specific inflection points—particularly the mid-period plateau and subsequent late-stage acceleration—suggests critical intervention windows where policy measures might most effectively interrupt the accumulation of cognitive dissonance or leverage natural oscillatory cycles of consumer reconsideration to promote alignment between health values and consumption behaviors.
