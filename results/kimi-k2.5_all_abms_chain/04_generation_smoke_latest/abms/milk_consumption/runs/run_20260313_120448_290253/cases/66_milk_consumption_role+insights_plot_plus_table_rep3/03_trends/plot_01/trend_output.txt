**Phase 1: Initial Inertia and Rapid Disruption (Steps 0–10)**
At initiation, consumption remains stable at roughly 2655 units, reflecting the high initial habit strength assigned to incumbent whole milk choices. Between steps 1 and 10, a statistically significant and steep decline emerges (Mann-Kendall slope estimates ranging from -139 to -221 units per step, p < 0.05), with consumption falling to approximately 1000 units by step 10. This represents the most dramatic contraction in the series, suggesting a critical mass of agents rapidly overcoming habitual barriers.

**Phase 2: Decelerated Decline with Structural Inflections (Steps 10–25)**
Following the initial drop, the rate of decline moderates substantially while exhibiting multiple inflection points at steps 13, 16, 19, and 24. Consumption decreases from roughly 1000 units to approximately 600–800 units, with temporary oscillations including a local peak of about 774 units at step 19 and a valley of 760 units at step 18. Statistical testing reveals no significant monotonic trend during windows 15–19 and 25–29 (p > 0.05), indicating periods of temporary market stabilization where social influence mechanisms and habit maintenance reach temporary equilibrium.

**Phase 3: Terminal Contraction and Low-Level Stabilization (Steps 25–32)**
From step 25 onward, consumption resumes a downward trajectory, reaching approximately 253 units by step 32. This phase features continued oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of approximately 15 steps, evidenced by peaks around 621 units (step 27) and 335 units (step 30), suggesting persistent cyclical patterns even as overall consumption approaches minimal levels.

**Oscillatory and Statistical Properties**
The series demonstrates significant oscillatory characteristics superimposed on the declining trend, with inflection points suggesting repeated adjustments in the rate of behavioral change. The rolling analysis confirms predominantly decreasing trends in early windows (steps 0–14) and resumption of significant decline in the 20–24 window, while middle and late periods exhibit the aforementioned non-significant fluctuations indicative of complex social dynamics.

**Behavioral Interpretation**

The sharp decline between steps 1–10 likely reflects the intersection of social influence mechanisms (30% interaction probability, 0.2 susceptibility) and the habit threshold (2.0) being systematically breached as health and environmental perceptions diffuse through the Watts-Strogatz network structure. The subsequent oscillations and inflection points align with the 5.0-step memory lifetime and justification mechanisms (0.61), creating cycles where agents periodically reconsider choices based on accumulated dissonance and social feedback. The 15-step oscillation period suggests that memory decay and social realignment operate on roughly triennial cycles relative to the simulation timeframe.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The steep early decline indicates that markets dominated by habitual consumption exhibit high initial inertia followed by rapid collapse once critical psychological thresholds are breached, suggesting that early intervention in habit formation phases yields disproportionate returns compared to later efforts. The oscillatory patterns in later stages demonstrate that consumer markets may exhibit temporary "rebound" effects even during secular declines, requiring sustained engagement strategies rather than one-time educational campaigns. Finally, the persistent low-level consumption at simulation end suggests that niche market segments remain viable for traditional products through hyper-targeted retention of high-habit consumers, even as the broader market has structurally shifted.
