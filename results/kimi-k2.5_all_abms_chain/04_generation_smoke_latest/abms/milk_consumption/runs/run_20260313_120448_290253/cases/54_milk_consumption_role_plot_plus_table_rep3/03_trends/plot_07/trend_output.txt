The simulation of British milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 reveals an S-curve adoption trajectory for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, beginning at approximately 5.29 units and culminating at 1456.60 units by the final time step. This represents a dramatic market transformation from near-complete dominance of whole milk to substantial adoption of alternative varieties.

The adoption process unfolds through three distinct phases characterized by different consumer behavioral dynamics. During the initial phase (time steps 0-9), consumption exhibits rapid exponential growth with statistically significant increasing trends (Mann-Kendall tau=1.000, p=0.027) and steep slopes exceeding 112 units per step in consecutive windows. By time step 9, consumption reaches approximately 1200 units, indicating that early adopters rapidly overcome initial habitual preferences (which began at strength 10.0) and respond to health and environmental perceptions (initially set at 1.69 and 1.12 respectively) through information processing and social network interactions operating at a 30% interaction probability with 20% social susceptibility.

The middle phase (time steps 10-24) demonstrates market stabilization with oscillatory behavior around a plateau of approximately 1183-1265 units. During this period, the aggregate trend shows no significant monotonic direction, with consumption fluctuating between localized peaks at time steps 11 (1265.47 units), 13 (1240.35 units), 19 (1221.57 units), and 21 (1226.86 units), and valleys at time steps 12 (1233.62 units), 18 (1183.39 units), 20 (1214.46 units), and 23 (1209.70 units). These oscillations reflect the psychological tension between strong habitual maintenance mechanisms (habit threshold 2.0) and evolving social norms, with the oscillation dominant period of 15.00 time steps suggesting cyclical patterns of experimentation and reversion among agents with high social blindness (0.51) and moderate conformity weights (0.28). The inflection points at time steps 12, 13, 16, 19, and 22 indicate repeated shifts in the acceleration of adoption as different consumer segments encounter barriers to change.

The final phase (time steps 25-32) exhibits renewed significant growth (Mann-Kendall tau=1.000, slope=48.54, p=0.027), driving consumption from approximately 1220 units to the terminal value of 1456.60 units. This late-stage surge suggests that accumulated social influence and shifting normative perceptions eventually overcome residual habitual resistance among the remaining whole milk consumers, completing the historical crossover where alternative milk varieties achieve market dominance.

Across the entire 33-step series, the median consumption level of 1220.21 units indicates that the system spends the majority of the simulation period in the plateau phase, with substantial variance (160,853.83) reflecting the wide dynamic range between initial adoption and saturation. The eight documented inflection points throughout the series, particularly the cluster between steps 12-22, capture the complex curvature of consumer transition as psychological justification mechanisms (0.61) and cognitive dissonance thresholds (0.15) interact with memory decay over five time steps to gradually erode incumbent habits.
