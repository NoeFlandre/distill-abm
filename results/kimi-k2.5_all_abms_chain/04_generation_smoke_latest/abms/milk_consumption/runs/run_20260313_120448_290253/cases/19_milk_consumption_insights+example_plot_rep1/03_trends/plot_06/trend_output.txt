Mean whole milk consumption begins at approximately 2,650 units per agent at the outset of the simulation. A steep decline occurs throughout the first ten steps, with consumption dropping to roughly 950–1,000 units by step 10. This rapid initial decrease suggests a swift early transition period where agents begin abandoning whole milk in favor of alternatives, likely driven by the initial perception of health and environmental characteristics combined with social influence effects propagating through the Watts-Strogatz network topology.

Between steps 10 and 25, the decline moderates considerably, decreasing gradually from approximately 900 units to 600–650 units. During this intermediate period, consumption levels exhibit slight plateaus around steps 18–20, where the rate of decrease temporarily stabilizes near 750–800 units. This slowing trajectory likely reflects the influence of habit strength and incumbent initial preferences creating resistance to change, while social norms and network interactions continue to exert pressure toward alternative milk varieties.

From step 25 onward, the decline accelerates again, punctuated by sharp drops around step 27 where consumption falls from roughly 600 units to approximately 400 units, followed by continued decrease to 250–300 units by the conclusion of the simulation at step 32. These late-stage drops may indicate cascade effects within the social network as threshold-based or probability-based disposition mechanisms trigger mass reconsideration of milk choices among remaining whole milk consumers.

Across all simulation runs, mean whole milk quantities remain tightly clustered during the initial phase, indicating high consistency in the early transition dynamics. Slight divergences emerge during the middle steps, particularly around steps 8–12, potentially reflecting variations in how different parameter combinations or stochastic interactions affect the timing of habit disruption. The trajectories reconverge toward the end, suggesting that despite different intermediate pathways, the model consistently drives whole milk consumption toward minimal levels.

Implications for decision makers: The trajectory suggests that interventions targeting early adoption stages may yield the most rapid reductions in whole milk consumption, as this period shows the highest susceptibility to change. However, the middle plateau indicates that sustained efforts are necessary to overcome habit persistence and cognitive dissonance thresholds. The late-stage acceleration implies that maintaining social influence mechanisms and normative pressures can help capture resistant consumer segments. Public health campaigns should anticipate an initial quick response followed by a slower conversion period requiring persistent messaging, with potential for renewed acceleration as social tipping points are reached.
