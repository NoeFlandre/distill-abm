The deviation between consumers' held health values and their actual milk choices begins at zero, indicating perfect alignment between health perceptions and consumption behavior at the start of the simulation period (1974). This initial state reflects the dominance of whole milk, where consumer choices naturally aligned with prevailing health perceptions of that era.

Over the subsequent 31-year period, this deviation increases monotonically to approximately 0.725, indicating a growing misalignment between what consumers believe about health and what they actually choose. The trajectory is not linear; early rapid escalation occurs within the first four to six time steps, suggesting an initial period where changing health information began to outpace behavioral adaptation. 

A notable inflection occurs around the sixteenth time step, where the rate of deviation growth accelerates before moderating again around the nineteenth step. This pattern suggests periodic waves of cognitive dissonance as consumers encountered new health information or social pressures. The oscillation pattern with a dominant period of approximately 15 time steps indicates cyclical reassessment periods, possibly reflecting annual or seasonal reconsideration of consumption habits.

Throughout all observed windows, the trend remains significantly increasing, confirming that the gap between health beliefs and actual choices widened consistently across the entire simulation period. By the final time step, the deviation reaches its maximum value, indicating that by 2005, consumers were choosing milk types that substantially diverged from their personal health valuations, likely maintaining whole milk consumption through habit persistence despite shifted health perceptions favoring lower-fat alternatives.

For decision makers, these trends highlight the substantial time lag between health perception shifts and actual behavioral change in food markets. The persistent and growing deviation suggests that informational campaigns alone may be insufficient to drive rapid dietary transitions, as habitual consumption patterns create significant inertia even when consumers intellectually accept new health information. Interventions targeting habit disruption or leveraging social influence mechanisms may be necessary to reduce the gap between health beliefs and consumption behaviors, particularly during the critical transition periods where deviation accelerates.
