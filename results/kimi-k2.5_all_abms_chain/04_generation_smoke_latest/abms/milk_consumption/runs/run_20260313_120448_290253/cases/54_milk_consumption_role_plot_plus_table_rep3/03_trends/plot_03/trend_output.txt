The cumulative count of whole milk selections across the population initiated at zero at the start of the simulation (time step 0) and exhibited a strictly monotonic increasing trajectory throughout the entire 32-step period representing the years 1974 to 2005. By the final time step, the aggregate mean across 30 simulation runs reached approximately 2195 total whole milk choices, with individual runs producing final values ranging from roughly 2000 to 2350, indicating substantial inter-run variability in the accumulation rate despite consistent directional trends.

Statistical analysis confirms a significantly increasing trend across all examined time windows, with Mann-Kendall test statistics of tau=1.00 and p=0.027 throughout. The rate of accumulation demonstrated a gradual moderation over the simulation period, with the slope peaking at approximately 76 choices per step during the early phase (steps 5-9) and subsequently decelerating to roughly 64 choices per step by the late phase (steps 25-29). This deceleration aligns with the model's objective of capturing the historical transition away from whole milk dominance, as the diminishing rate reflects the gradual reduction in whole milk's market share even as the absolute cumulative count continued to rise. Inflection points in the accumulation dynamics were detected at steps 1, 7, and 31, suggesting subtle shifts in the underlying decision-making processes at these junctures.

Within the context of the British milk market transition, these dynamics illustrate that while whole milk consumption declined as a proportion of total milk consumption over the period, the cumulative historical total of whole milk selections continued to accrue from the initial baseline where whole milk held maximum habitual strength (10.0). The divergence across simulation runs—evident in the expanding range of values over time—stems from the stochastic elements of the consumer decision-making process, including probabilistic social interactions, individual threshold-based habit reconsideration, and the social network structure parameterized within the model.
