The multiplicative habit factor applied to whole milk choice functions initiates at a baseline value of 1.0000 and undergoes an immediate sharp increase during the first two time steps, rising rapidly to approximately 1.0200. Following this initial surge, the factor exhibits a steady, monotonic upward trajectory throughout the remaining simulation duration, ultimately reaching 1.0502 by step 32. This progression represents a cumulative 5% enhancement in the relative influence of habitual behavior on whole milk consumption decisions over the modeled 31-year period.

Statistical analysis confirms consistently significant positive trends across all examined intervals, with Mann-Kendall tests revealing uninterrupted increases (tau=1.000, p=0.027) in every rolling window from the initial phase through step 29. The expansion maintains remarkable consistency, exhibiting subtle inflections around steps 7 and 12 where the rate of habit reinforcement undergoes minor modulations without reversing direction.

Oscillatory patterns with a dominant periodicity of 15 steps overlay the primary growth trend, manifesting at intervals including steps 3-7, 15-18, and 26-30, which introduce minor fluctuations around the upward trajectory. Variance across the 30 simulation runs remains relatively contained (standard deviation: 0.0110) though showing slight divergence toward the conclusion, with terminal values ranging between approximately 1.044 and 1.055.

Within the context of the British milk market transition, this increasing habit factor suggests that consumers maintaining whole milk consumption experience progressively stronger habitual anchoring to this incumbent choice, even as aggregate population behavior shifts toward skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives. The upward trend indicates that habit maintenance mechanisms generate a self-reinforcing cycle wherein continued consumption strengthens the automaticity of choice, creating psychological inertia that persists despite evolving health perceptions, environmental awareness, and shifting social norms that favor alternative milk varieties. The stability of this increase across simulation replications suggests robust system dynamics where habitual behavior acts as a steadily strengthening anchor for the remaining whole milk consumer base.
