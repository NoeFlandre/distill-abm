The total number of milk choices made accumulates linearly from zero at time step 0, increasing at a constant rate of approximately 1,000 choices per time step to reach roughly 32,000 by time step 32. This uniform accumulation reflects the fixed population of 1,000 agents, each executing exactly one consumption decision per time step throughout the entire simulation period. The trajectory exhibits negligible variance across repeated simulation runs, indicating that population-level aggregation operates deterministically despite stochastic individual-level decision mechanisms involving habit thresholds, social interactions, and probabilistic reconsiderations of choices.

Implications for decision makers: The linear growth confirms that market share transitions observed in the model derive purely from behavioral substitution within a stable consumer population, uncomplicated by demographic turnover or varying participation rates. This validates that shifts from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties can be attributed entirely to the modeled psychological and social mechanisms—specifically the erosion of incumbent habits through health and environmental information processing and social network effects—providing a clean experimental framework for isolating the drivers of dietary transitions in stable markets.
