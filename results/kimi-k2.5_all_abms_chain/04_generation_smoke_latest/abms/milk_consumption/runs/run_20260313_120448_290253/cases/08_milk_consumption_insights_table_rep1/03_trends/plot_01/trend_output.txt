The simulation of British milk consumption behaviors from 1974 to 2005 demonstrates a substantial long-term contraction in whole milk adoption, with average weekly per-person consumption initiating at approximately 2655 units and declining to roughly 253 units across the 33 time steps modeled. This reduction reflects the calibrated transition from whole milk dominance toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties within the agent population.  

The trajectory exhibits pronounced non-linear characteristics with distinct phases of variable decline rates. An initial steep reduction characterizes the first five time steps, followed by an intensified rate of decrease between steps five and nine. The progression then moderates substantially between steps ten and fourteen, before encountering a complex stabilization and fluctuation pattern throughout the middle and latter portions of the simulation. Inflection points at steps thirteen, sixteen, and nineteen mark transitions between declining and temporarily stable regimes, with minor elevations appearing at steps nineteen, twenty-seven, and thirty, suggesting intermittent resistance to the downward trend. By step thirty-two, consumption reaches its minimum value, representing roughly a tenfold reduction from initial levels.  

These patterns indicate that the interaction between strong incumbent habits, memory-limited information processing, and social influence structures within the Watts-Strogatz network topology produces an abandonment curve characterized by initial persistence, accelerating rejection as health and environmental perceptions shift, and eventual market saturation at low whole milk adoption rates. The oscillatory elements, particularly the stabilization around the median value of approximately 817 units, reflect periods where cognitive dissonance thresholds and social conformity pressures temporarily arrest the decline before the dominant trend resumes.  

For decision makers, these findings suggest that food preference transitions follow predictable S-curve dynamics with built-in inertia periods, indicating that policy interventions should anticipate initial lag phases, prepare for rapid shifts once perceptual thresholds are breached, and utilize identified stability windows for reinforcement of alternative behaviors before the final convergence to new consumption equilibria.
