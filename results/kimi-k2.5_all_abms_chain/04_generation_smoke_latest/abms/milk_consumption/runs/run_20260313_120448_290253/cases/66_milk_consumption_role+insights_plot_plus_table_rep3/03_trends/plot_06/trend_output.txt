The mean whole milk quantity exhibits a pronounced secular decline across the 32-step simulation horizon, falling from an initial value of 2,654.81 units to a terminal value of 253.10 units—a reduction of approximately 90.5%. The trajectory is characterized by three distinct phases superimposed with periodic volatility in the latter stages.

**Phase 1: Rapid Disruption (Steps 0–10)**  
The initial phase demonstrates the steepest rate of decline, with aggregate mean values plummeting by roughly 62% within the first ten steps, reaching approximately 1,000 units by step 10. Statistical analysis confirms significantly decreasing trends during windows 0–4 (slope = -115.85, p = 0.027) and 5–9 (slope = -221.11, p = 0.027), indicating robust momentum away from incumbent consumption patterns. The first inflection point at step 5 marks a transition between extremely rapid and merely rapid decline, likely reflecting the initial erosion of the strong habitual anchor (initial habit strength of 10.0) as agents encounter health and environmental information about alternatives.

**Phase 2: Decelerating Transition (Steps 10–18)**  
Between steps 10 and 18, the descent moderates considerably, with the series declining from roughly 1,000 units to approximately 760 units. Inflection points at steps 13 and 16 signal successive slowdowns in the rate of decrease. The rolling trend analysis for window 10–14 remains significantly decreasing (slope = -38.13, p = 0.027), though at roughly one-sixth the velocity of the initial phase. This deceleration aligns with the model's habit threshold mechanism (set at 2.0); as less-habituated consumers switch early, the remaining population possesses stronger habitual defenses or lower susceptibility to social influence (social-susceptibility = 0.2, social-blindness = 0.51).

**Phase 3: Volatile Contraction (Steps 18–32)**  
The final segment exhibits marked oscillation around a gradually declining baseline. Local minima occur at step 18 (760.00), step 26 (594.35), and step 29 (323.46), while local maxima appear at step 19 (773.96), step 27 (620.98), and step 30 (335.18). Notably, statistical tests for windows 15–19 and 25–29 reveal no significant monotonic trend (p = 0.086), indicating that the market enters a fluctuating equilibrium state. The oscillation dominant period of 15.00 steps suggests cyclical reconsideration behaviors among the residual consumer base. By step 32, consumption stabilizes at 253.10 units, representing a persistent "hard core" of approximately 9.5% of initial demand.

**Interpretation and Implications**  
The curvature of the trajectory—with steep early adoption followed by prolonged, volatile decline—reflects the interaction of high initial habit strength (10.0) with threshold-based psychological processing. The model suggests that market transformation in habitual consumption categories follows a diffusion pattern where the first third of the transition period captures the majority of switching behavior, while the final third requires decades to dislodge resistant consumers.

For decision makers, these dynamics imply that public health or environmental campaigns targeting dietary shifts should allocate resources for sustained, multi-decade interventions rather than expecting rapid universal adoption. The oscillations in steps 18–32 indicate that even after substantial market penetration of alternatives, consumption of incumbent products never reaches zero and exhibits instability, suggesting that policy makers must prepare for long-tail persistence of traditional consumption patterns and potential resurgence episodes among remaining holdouts. The parameters suggest that reducing the habit threshold or increasing social connectivity (network-parameter = 8.0, p-interact = 0.3) could potentially accelerate the transition, though the strong initial habit strength constructed by historical dominance creates structural resistance that procedural mechanisms cannot easily overcome.
