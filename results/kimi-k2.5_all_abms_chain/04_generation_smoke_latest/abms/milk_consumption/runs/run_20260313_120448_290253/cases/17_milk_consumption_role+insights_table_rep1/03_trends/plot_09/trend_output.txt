The simulation of British milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 reveals a three-phase trajectory in the development of habitual preference for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk varieties. The mean habit factor initiates at a neutral baseline of 1.0000, indicating that alternative milk options initially exerted no habitual pull on consumer decision-making, consistent with the market dominance of whole milk during the mid-1970s.

During the first five time steps (1974–1978), the habit factor exhibits stagnation, maintaining values near 1.0 with minimal fluctuation, including a slight depression to approximately 1.0167 at time step 5. This initial plateau reflects the strong incumbent advantage of whole milk consumption patterns, where established behavioral routines effectively suppressed the formation of competing habits despite emerging health and environmental information.

A significant transition occurs around time step 5 (1979), marking the onset of sustained growth that persists throughout the remainder of the simulation. Between time steps 5 and 14 (1979–1988), the habit factor enters a steep acceleration phase, with the rate of increase reaching its maximum between indices 10 and 14. This period corresponds to the critical market transition where social influence mechanisms and shifting attribute perceptions overcame habitual resistance, driving rapid adoption and subsequent habituation to alternative milk varieties.

Time step 15 (approximately 1989) constitutes a notable change point where the growth dynamics shift character. While the habit factor continues ascending through time steps 15–29 (1989–2003), the rate of increase progressively decelerates, with the slope diminishing from 0.027 to 0.018. This deceleration suggests approaching market saturation as the population of potential switchers dwindles and new habit formation becomes increasingly concentrated among resistant consumer segments. Minor oscillations emerge during the final periods (indices 17–30), exhibiting cyclical patterns consistent with generational turnover or economic fluctuations, before the metric stabilizes near 1.5879 by the conclusion of the simulation period.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The pronounced latency period (indices 0–4) demonstrates that behavior change campaigns targeting habitual consumption face initial resistance lasting approximately five years before gaining traction, suggesting the need for sustained rather than burst-focused interventions. The acceleration phase (indices 5–15) represents a critical window where marketing investments and public health messaging yield compounding returns through the interaction of social influence and emerging habit formation. Finally, the deceleration observed after time step 15 indicates that once alternative consumption becomes habitual for the majority, the market achieves stability resistant to rapid subsequent disruption, implying that late-entry competitors face substantially higher barriers to adoption.
