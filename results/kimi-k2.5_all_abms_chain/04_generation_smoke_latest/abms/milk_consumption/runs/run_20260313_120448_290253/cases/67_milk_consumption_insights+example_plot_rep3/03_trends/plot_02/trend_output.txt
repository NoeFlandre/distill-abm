The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at approximately 5 milliliters during the initial time step, consistent with the 1974 baseline market conditions where whole milk dominated consumer preferences. Between steps 1 and 11, consumption increases rapidly in an approximately linear fashion, reaching roughly 1,250 milliliters per agent per week. This sharp acceleration indicates a period of rapid adoption driven by initial disposition triggers, social influence propagation through the network topology, and agents' perception of health and environmental characteristics associated with alternative milk varieties.

From step 11 through step 26, the consumption trajectory enters a prolonged plateau phase, fluctuating between approximately 1,180 and 1,280 milliliters without sustained growth. This stabilization likely reflects the influence of strong incumbent habits (parameterized at 10.0) creating resistance among agents with high habit strength, while the probability-based disposition mechanism and cognitive dissonance thresholds (0.15) slow the rate of new adopters. The temporary equilibrium suggests that social conformity effects and network interactions maintain adoption among transitioned agents while habit persistence impedes further market penetration.

A second distinct growth phase emerges between steps 26 and 32, with consumption rising sharply from approximately 1,250 to 1,450 milliliters per week. This late-stage surge indicates that accumulated social norm effects, combined with memory-lifetime dynamics and repeated exposure to alternative choices, eventually overcome residual habit barriers among remaining agents, or that generational turnover effects within the simulation period enable final market saturation.

Variance across simulation runs remains minimal during the initial rapid growth phase through step 8, indicating highly predictable early adoption dynamics, but increases noticeably during the plateau and final acceleration phases. This divergence suggests that stochastic elements in social interaction probabilities and network-specific influence patterns create multiple possible adoption pathways as the system matures, though all trajectories converge toward high consumption levels by step 32.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The S-curve adoption pattern with an extended plateau indicates that dietary behavior change involves distinct phases of rapid early adoption, middle-period resistance, and eventual saturation. This suggests that public health interventions targeting milk consumption should maintain consistent messaging through plateau periods rather than abandoning campaigns during temporary stagnation, and that strategies specifically addressing habit disruption and late-adopter segments become critical after initial rapid growth subsides.
