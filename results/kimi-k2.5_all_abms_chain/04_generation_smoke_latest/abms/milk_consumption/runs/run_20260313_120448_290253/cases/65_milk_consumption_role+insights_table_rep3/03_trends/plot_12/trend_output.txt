The simulation depicts a pronounced and sustained divergence between consumers' environmental values and their actual milk consumption choices over the 31-year period. Initially, at index 0, the deviation registers at 0.0, indicating alignment between held environmental values and consumption patterns. This baseline reflects the starting conditions where agents possess strong habitual attachments to whole milk (initial habit strength of 10.0) coupled with relatively low initial environmental perceptions of alternative options (mean initial environmental perception of 1.12).

During the early phase spanning indices 0 through 4, the deviation escalates rapidly with a significant upward trend (Mann-Kendall tau = 1.0, p = 0.027), reaching approximately 11.4 units. This steep ascent suggests that environmental awareness and valuation of alternative milk options spread quickly through the social network (operating with interaction probability of 0.3 and social susceptibility of 0.2), while habitual consumption patterns remain largely unchanged.

The growth rate moderates considerably during indices 5 through 9, where the deviation continues to increase but at a significantly reduced slope of 0.109 (still statistically significant, tau = 1.0, p = 0.027). By index 9, values approach 11.9 units, indicating that while environmental values continue to strengthen, the habitual threshold (set at 2.0) begins to exert moderating influence on the expansion of the value-action gap.

From index 10 onward, the system enters a prolonged plateau phase characterized by statistical stability. The deviation peaks at index 13 with a value of 13.4756, representing the maximum misalignment between environmental values and consumption behavior. Subsequent periods show minor oscillations around a mean of approximately 13.43, with values at indices 19, 21, 27, and 30 registering 13.4515, 13.4543, 13.4358, and 13.4187 respectively. Rolling trend analyses for windows beyond index 10 reveal no statistically significant directional trends (p-values ranging from 0.086 to 0.221), indicating that the value-action gap stabilizes despite ongoing social influence mechanisms and normative pressures (social conformity weight of 0.28).

The series exhibits a dominant oscillation period of 15 indices, with minor peaks and valleys recurring throughout the plateau phase (notable valleys at indices 20, 26, 29, and 31 with values between 13.4153 and 13.4510). These micro-fluctuations suggest cyclical adjustments in consumer value-behavior alignment, potentially reflecting intermittent episodes of cognitive dissonance approaching the tolerance threshold (0.15) followed by successful justification mechanisms (justification parameter of 0.61) that allow maintenance of habitual choices without behavioral change.

By the final index (32), the deviation registers 13.4186, representing a persistent gap where consumers maintain strong environmental values regarding milk alternatives yet continue consumption patterns misaligned with these values. The inflection points detected at indices 16, 19, 24, 26, 28, and 31 mark subtle transitions in the curvature of this stable high-deviation regime, suggesting ongoing but balanced tensions between social influence, habit persistence, and psychological rationalization processes.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The rapid initial growth of the value-action gap (indices 0-9) followed by persistent high deviation levels indicates that environmental awareness campaigns and information dissemination alone are insufficient to drive behavioral change in habitual consumption domains. The stabilization of deviation at approximately 13.4 units suggests that without targeted interventions to disrupt established habits (given the high initial habit strength of 10.0 and active justification mechanisms at 0.61), consumers will maintain significant misalignment between their environmental values and purchasing decisions indefinitely.

Decision makers should prioritize habit-disruption strategies over pure informational approaches, as the data indicates that values can shift substantially faster than behaviors. The presence of a stable plateau rather than continued growth or decline suggests that current social influence mechanisms (with social blindness at 0.51 and interaction probability at 0.3) are inadequate to overcome habitual inertia. Interventions that reduce the psychological justification capacity or lower the threshold for cognitive dissonance activation may be necessary to convert environmental values into actual consumption changes, rather than allowing the persistence of the observed 13-unit value-action gap that characterizes the mature market phase.
