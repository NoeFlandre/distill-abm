The mean habit factor for whole milk begins at a baseline value of 1.00 at the start of the simulation. Within the first two time steps, it undergoes a rapid increase, rising sharply to approximately 1.02. This initial jump reflects the immediate activation of the high initial habit strength assigned to whole milk consumption at the model's outset, corresponding to 1974 market conditions when whole milk dominated consumer preferences.

Following this initial surge, the metric exhibits a steady, approximately linear growth trajectory from step 2 through step 32, reaching final values ranging between 1.04 and 1.055 across different simulation runs. The consistent upward slope indicates that the habitual advantage conferred to whole milk strengthens progressively throughout the 31-year period, with the factor increasing by roughly 0.001 per time step during this sustained growth phase.

While all simulation runs follow this identical qualitative pattern, minor divergence emerges after approximately step 5, with variance gradually increasing toward the end of the simulation. By the final time step, the spread between the lowest and highest values reaches approximately 0.01 to 0.015, suggesting some sensitivity to stochastic elements in the social influence and decision-making processes, though the overall directional trend remains robust across runs.

This pattern reveals that as the marketplace evolves and some consumers transition to skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives, those remaining with whole milk develop progressively stronger habitual attachments. The increasing mean factor suggests that continuing consumers reinforce their behavioral patterns through repeated consumption, creating an entrenched subpopulation with heightened resistance to change. This dynamic implies that habitual barriers to switching are not static but compound over time for persistent whole milk consumers.

For decision makers, this trend indicates that interventions aimed at reducing whole milk consumption face increasing difficulty over time, as the population of remaining whole milk consumers becomes self-selected for strong habitual behavior and progressively more resistant to change. Early engagement strategies may prove more effective than delayed efforts, as the habitual lock-in effect strengthens with each passing period, narrowing the window for successful behavior modification.
