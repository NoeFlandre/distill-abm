The simulation of British milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 reveals a substantial and sustained decline in average weekly whole milk consumption per agent. The process initiates at approximately 2,655 ml per week and terminates at roughly 253 ml per week, representing a reduction of about 90% across the 33 time steps.

The decline occurs in distinct phases characterized by varying rates of decrease. During the initial period spanning steps 0 through 10, consumption drops precipitously from the starting value to approximately 950 ml per week, with the steepest descent occurring between steps 5 and 9. This rapid transition reflects the initial displacement of whole milk as agents begin considering and adopting skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives.

Following the steep initial decline, the rate of decrease moderates substantially between steps 10 and 15, with consumption falling from approximately 950 ml to around 900 ml per week, then continuing to approximately 800 ml by step 15. Between steps 15 and 24, the trend stabilizes considerably, exhibiting minor fluctuations around a plateau of roughly 760–775 ml per week. This period includes specific oscillations, with a temporary valley at step 18 (approximately 760 ml) and a small peak at step 19 (approximately 774 ml), suggesting a temporary equilibrium where habit strength and social influence partially resist further decline.

After step 24, the decline resumes with renewed momentum. Consumption falls from around 775 ml to approximately 595 ml by step 26, followed by a brief rebound to roughly 621 ml at step 27. A sharp contraction then occurs between steps 27 and 29, dropping consumption to approximately 323 ml. Minor fluctuations persist in the final phase, with a small peak at step 30 (approximately 335 ml) before the final descent to the terminal value of 253 ml at step 32.

Statistical analysis confirms significant decreasing trends during the initial steep decline, with slopes ranging from –139 to –221 ml per step in windows spanning steps 0–14, while later periods show reduced but generally persistent downward movement with intermittent stabilization.

**Implications for decision makers:** The model indicates that dietary behavior change follows a non-linear trajectory with distinct phases of rapid adoption, temporary stabilization, and continued gradual decline. The steep initial drop suggests that early adopters drive rapid market shifts when disposition mechanisms trigger consideration of alternatives, while the mid-simulation plateau around steps 18–19 indicates that habitual consumption patterns and social network effects create substantial resistance requiring sustained intervention to overcome. The persistence of low-level whole milk consumption through the final time steps implies that complete market transition may require extended periods even after majority adoption of alternatives has occurred, suggesting that policies should account for long-term persistence among resistant population segments.
