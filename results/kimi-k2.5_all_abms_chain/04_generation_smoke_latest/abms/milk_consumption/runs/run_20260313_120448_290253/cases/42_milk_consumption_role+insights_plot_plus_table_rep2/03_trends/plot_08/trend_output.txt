The mean habit factor for whole milk exhibits a consistent monotonic upward trajectory across all 33 time steps of the simulation, beginning at a baseline value of 1.0000 and culminating at approximately 1.0502. This represents a cumulative increase of roughly 5% over the simulated time period, indicating a gradual but persistent strengthening of habitual attachment to whole milk consumption among the agent population.

The progression demonstrates remarkable consistency, with statistically significant increasing trends detected across all examined time windows (p < 0.05), each exhibiting identical positive slopes of approximately 0.001 per step. Notable inflection points occur at time steps 7 and 12, where the rate of habit formation undergoes subtle modulation, though the overall directional trend remains unambiguously positive throughout. The data also reveals an underlying oscillatory component with a periodicity of 15 time steps, suggesting the presence of cyclical fluctuations superimposed upon the dominant growth trend.

Within the context of the modeled transition from whole milk to alternative varieties, this sustained increase in the mean habit factor indicates that consumers who continue selecting whole milk develop progressively stronger habitual attachments to this option over time. Even as the broader market shifts toward skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives, the remaining whole milk consumers exhibit deepening behavioral rigidity, with habit strength accumulating approximately 0.0016 units per time step on average. This suggests that habitual mechanisms create a self-reinforcing cycle wherein continued consumption of the incumbent product progressively raises the psychological threshold required for agents to consider switching to alternatives.

For decision makers seeking to influence milk consumption patterns, these findings suggest that interventions targeting whole milk reduction face increasing difficulty as time progresses. The approximately 5% growth in habit strength over the simulation period implies that delay in implementing switching incentives substantially hardens consumer resistance. Early-stage interventions appear critical, as habit formation exhibits a compounding effect that becomes progressively more difficult to reverse. Additionally, the observed 15-step oscillatory pattern suggests that timing interventions to align with periodic windows of heightened consumer openness—potentially corresponding to seasonal or cyclical behavioral patterns—may yield greater efficacy than uniformly distributed efforts. The consistent linear growth trend further indicates that without active intervention, natural market evolution will not spontaneously reduce habitual whole milk attachment, necessitating sustained policy pressure to overcome the 0.001 per step automatic habit reinforcement.
