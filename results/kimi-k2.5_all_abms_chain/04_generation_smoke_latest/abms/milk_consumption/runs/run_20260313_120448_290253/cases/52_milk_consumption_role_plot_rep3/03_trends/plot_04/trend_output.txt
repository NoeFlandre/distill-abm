The repeated simulations demonstrate a consistent S-curve adoption pattern for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices over the 32-step period corresponding to 1974-2005.

Initially, at step 0, the total count of alternative milk choices registers near zero, reflecting the historical market dominance of whole milk and the high initial habit strength (10.0) assigned to the incumbent option. During the first five steps, accumulation remains minimal, reaching only approximately 1,000 to 1,500 total choices, indicating the persistence of habitual consumption patterns and the delayed impact of initially moderate health and environmental perceptions (1.69 and 1.12 respectively).

Between steps 5 and 15, the data reveal a pronounced acceleration phase characterized by rapid growth from roughly 2,500 to 10,000 cumulative choices. This inflection corresponds to the period where social influence mechanisms—with interaction probability at 0.3, social susceptibility at 0.2, and conformity weight at 0.28—begin overcoming habitual inertia, enabling agents to process health and environmental information effectively and shift their selections.

The crossover point, where skimmed and semi-skimmed choices surpass whole milk selections, occurs around step 10 when the metric reaches approximately 5,000 total choices. Subsequently, from steps 15 to 30, the adoption maintains a strong linear trajectory, increasing from 10,000 to roughly 24,000 choices, representing an average accumulation rate of approximately 900 choices per step. This sustained high-rate growth suggests successful penetration of the alternative milk options among the agent population during this period.

In the final two steps (30-32), growth continues but exhibits slight deceleration consistent with market saturation dynamics, with values converging between 25,500 and 26,000 across all simulation runs. By the terminal step, skimmed and semi-skimmed choices represent approximately 80 percent of all milk selections made during the simulation, capturing the historical transformation of British milk consumption patterns. The minimal variance between simulation trajectories indicates robust deterministic dynamics underlying the stochastic psychological processes modeled.
