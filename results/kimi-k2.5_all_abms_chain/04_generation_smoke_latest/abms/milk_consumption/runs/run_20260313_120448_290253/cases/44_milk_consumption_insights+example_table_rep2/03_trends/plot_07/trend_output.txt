Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at minimal levels in 1974, with average weekly per capita intake starting at 5.29 units. Over the following nine years, consumption increases rapidly and consistently, following a steep upward trajectory through the early 1980s as initial health information penetrates the population and triggers early adoption. This period represents the initial response phase where perception of health benefits drives a swift transition away from whole milk dominance.

Beginning in the mid-1980s and continuing for approximately fifteen years, the growth pattern shifts to an extended phase of fluctuation without clear directional trend. During this middle period, consumption oscillates between local maxima near 1,265 units and periodic troughs around 1,183 units. Notable peaks occur around 1985 and 1987, separated by intervening valleys that suggest temporary stabilization or minor reversals in adoption rates. This volatility likely reflects the tension between emerging social norms favoring lower-fat alternatives and the persistence of established consumption habits, creating a dynamic equilibrium where adoption proceeds unevenly across the population.

In the final years of the observation window, from approximately 1999 to 2005, consumption resumes a steady upward climb, breaking through the previous plateau to reach 1,456.60 units by the end of the series. This late-stage acceleration indicates that social influence mechanisms and reinforced habit formation have finally overcome initial resistance, driving the market toward consolidated adoption of alternative milk varieties.

For decision makers, these dynamics demonstrate that dietary transitions follow distinct phases: an initial rapid response to health messaging, a protracted turbulent period requiring sustained intervention efforts, and a final consolidation where behavioral changes become self-reinforcing. Public health initiatives targeting similar lifestyle modifications should anticipate middle-period volatility and maintain consistent engagement throughout the transition to prevent stagnation and ensure the shift completes successfully rather than remaining trapped in oscillation.
