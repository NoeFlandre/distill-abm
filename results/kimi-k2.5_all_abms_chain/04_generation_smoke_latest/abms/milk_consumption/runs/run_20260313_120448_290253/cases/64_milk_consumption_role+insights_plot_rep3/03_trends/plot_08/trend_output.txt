The mean habit factor applied to whole milk consumption decisions exhibits a monotonic upward trajectory throughout the 32-step simulation period, rising from an initial baseline of 1.00 at Step 0 to terminal values ranging between approximately 1.043 and 1.055 by Step 32. 

An immediate discontinuity occurs at the first time step, with values jumping sharply to approximately 1.02 across all simulation runs. Following this initial escalation, the metric increases gradually and consistently throughout the remaining duration, with most trajectories converging around 1.05 by the final time step. The variance across simulation runs remains relatively constrained initially but widens modestly in later steps, indicating divergent path dependencies in how habit strength evolves across different stochastic realizations of the social network and decision-making processes.

This pattern reflects a selection mechanism inherent in models of habit formation and market transition. At initialization, all agents possess strong habitual attachments to whole milk (incumbent-initial-habit = 10.0), but as the simulation progresses, agents encounter information about health and environmental attributes of alternative milk varieties and respond to social influences from network connections. Consumers with weaker habitual resistance or higher susceptibility to social influence transition to skimmed or semi-skimmed milk, exiting the pool of whole milk consumers. Consequently, the remaining population of whole milk drinkers becomes increasingly composed of agents with strong habitual attachments, driving the mean habit factor upward despite the aggregate decline in whole milk market share. The continued growth throughout the simulation suggests that habit strength not only filters who remains but potentially reinforces itself among residual consumers, or that late-stage switchers are those with progressively stronger initial habits.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The increasing concentration of habit strength among residual whole milk consumers suggests that market interventions targeting this segment will face escalating resistance over time. As the remaining whole milk drinkers represent a self-selected group of habitually entrenched consumers, conventional health messaging and gradual persuasion strategies are likely to prove insufficient. Decision makers should recognize that late adopters who remain in the whole milk category may require more intensive, personalized interventions, disruptive policy mechanisms, or alternative value propositions that specifically address deep-seated habitual behaviors rather than superficial preferences.

The rapid initial consolidation of habit effects within the first time step indicates that intervention timing is critical. Delays in addressing consumption patterns allow habits to strengthen quickly among those who remain, making subsequent behavior change more costly and difficult to achieve. Early-stage interventions that prevent habit consolidation or target consumers before they self-select into the residual whole milk category are likely to yield higher returns on investment than late-stage efforts focused on the most habitually resistant segment.
