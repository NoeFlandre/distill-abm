The data from 30 simulation series of cumulative whole milk choices demonstrate a consistent monotonic increase from an initial value of zero to a mean terminal value of approximately 2,195 choices across 33 time steps. Individual series exhibit final values ranging between roughly 1,980 and 2,310, indicating relatively low inter-run variance in cumulative adoption trajectories. The growth pattern follows an approximately linear trend throughout the simulation period, with rolling window analyses revealing statistically significant positive slopes ranging from approximately 64 to 76 units per time step across consecutive intervals. While the rate of increase remains substantially constant, minor deceleration is observable in later periods as slopes decrease marginally from early-phase values near 76 to late-phase values near 64.

Within the context of the milk consumption transition model spanning the 1974-2005 period, these cumulative trajectories represent the persistent historical record of whole milk selections by agents despite ongoing behavioral transitions toward skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives. The linear accumulation indicates that while individual agents may reduce their contemporary whole milk consumption, the total historical count of whole milk choices continues to grow deterministically as the population repeatedly selects among available milk types at each decision point. The consistency across simulation runs suggests robust behavioral dynamics wherein social influence, habit formation, and network effects produce predictable cumulative adoption patterns even as the system undergoes structural transitions in preference distributions.

For decision makers examining product lifecycle transitions in food systems, these results indicate that incumbent products maintain substantial historical inertia even during active market transitions. The linear growth of cumulative choices suggests that legacy consumption patterns continue accumulating at steady rates regardless of adoption rate changes for alternative products. Policymakers and industry stakeholders should anticipate that historical consumption data will consistently increase for traditional products throughout transition periods, potentially masking contemporary decline in market share when examining cumulative rather than period-prevalence metrics. The reliability of these trajectories across simulation scenarios further suggests that transition dynamics in food choice systems follow predictable accumulation patterns, enabling accurate forecasting of long-term historical consumption burdens even amid complex behavioral changes.
