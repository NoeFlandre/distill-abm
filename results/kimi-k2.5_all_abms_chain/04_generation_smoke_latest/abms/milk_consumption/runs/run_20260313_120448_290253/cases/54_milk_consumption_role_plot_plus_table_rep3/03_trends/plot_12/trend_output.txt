The simulation reveals a two-phase dynamic in the alignment between consumer environmental values and milk consumption choices over the 31-year period. The metric begins at zero, indicating initial concordance between held environmental values and the implications of milk selections under 1974 market conditions where whole milk dominated. 

During the first phase spanning approximately the first nine time steps, the deviation undergoes rapid acceleration. The rate of increase is steepest in the earliest steps, with significant monotonic growth (τ = 1.000, p = 0.027) occurring at an average rate of 2.84 units per step through step 4. The growth rate subsequently decelerates substantially to 0.109 units per step through step 9, though remaining statistically significant. By step 5, the deviation reaches approximately 12.0 units, and by step 10 stabilizes around 13.4 units.

The second phase, from approximately step 10 through step 32, exhibits stabilization with minimal fluctuation around a plateau of 13.4 to 13.5 units. The series reaches its maximum value of 13.4756 at step 13, after which it maintains values consistently above 13.4 with minor oscillations. The mean deviation across the full time series is 12.6015, with a median of 13.4302, reflecting the extended duration spent in the elevated plateau state. Standard deviation of 2.5933 indicates that most variation occurs during the initial transition rather than the steady-state period.

Statistical trend analysis confirms the plateau nature of the later period, with rolling windows from steps 10-14, 15-19, 20-24, and 25-29 showing no statistically significant trends (all p > 0.05). Minor inflection points at steps 16, 19, 24, 26, 28, and 31 indicate small curvature changes during the stabilization phase, with the metric concluding at 13.4186—only 0.057 units below its peak value.

Interpreted within the context of the milk transition model, this trajectory suggests that the period of rapid market shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties generates swift emergence of misalignment between consumer environmental value structures and the environmental implications of their actual choices. Once the transition stabilizes around step 10-13, this value-choice gap persists at a high level (13.4+ units) throughout the remainder of the simulation, indicating that the new consumption equilibrium maintains a consistent degree of decoupling between environmental values and consumption behavior. The oscillation pattern with a dominant period of 15 steps suggests minor cyclical adjustments in this misalignment during the stable phase, though with negligible amplitude relative to the established plateau level.
