The choice function deviation metric begins at zero, indicating that at the outset of the simulation in 1974, all agents evaluated milk options with near-perfect uniformity. This initial homogeneity reflects the strong incumbent habit strength associated with whole milk consumption and the shared, unfavorable initial perceptions regarding the health and environmental attributes of alternative varieties.

Over the first approximately seven time steps, the deviation increases rapidly with an initial slope of 0.128, reaching roughly 0.30 by index 7. This abrupt rise signifies the initial fragmentation of consumer evaluations as the first agents begin breaking established habits—enabled by the relatively low habit threshold of 2.0 compared to the initial habitual strength of 10.0—and start incorporating updated health and environmental perceptions into their choice functions. The divergence accelerates as early adopters diverge from the majority still holding traditional preferences.

Between indices 7 and 12, the trend continues upward but undergoes a notable inflection, with the rate of increase moderating to approximately 0.007–0.009 per period. This shift likely corresponds to the critical mass period where social influence mechanisms—operating with moderate susceptibility (0.2) and partial social blindness (0.51)—begin creating feedback loops that simultaneously pull some agents toward alternatives while reinforcing traditional choices in others, generating heterogeneity without uniform convergence.

From the plateau around index 12 through index 28, the deviation continues its ascent but at progressively diminishing rates (slopes declining from 0.007 to 0.004), approaching an asymptotic value near 0.75 by the simulation's end in 2005. This late-phase deceleration suggests the population is approaching a stable configuration of diverse preference structures, where remaining whole milk loyalists—sustained by strong residual habits and justification mechanisms—maintain evaluations vastly different from those who fully transitioned to skimmed or semi-skimmed varieties.

Superimposed on this long-term increasing trend, the metric exhibits oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of approximately 15 time steps, creating local fluctuations around indices 6–8, 16–19, and 26–30. These periodic variations likely reflect the cyclical nature of social norm formation and cognitive dissonance resolution, as agents periodically realign or differentiate their evaluations in response to accumulating peer behaviors and evolving justifications for habit maintenance or abandonment. By the final time step, the deviation stabilizes at 0.7508, indicating that by 2005, the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents differed by approximately 75%, capturing the substantial polarization in milk preferences that characterized the completed market transition.
