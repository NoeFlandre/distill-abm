The simulation tracks the mean deviation between agents' held environmental values and the implied environmental values of their milk selections over 33 time steps spanning the 1974–2005 period. 

Initially, at the first time step, the deviation registers at 0.0, indicating perfect alignment between environmental values and consumption choices. This baseline reflects the starting conditions where whole milk dominates and environmental considerations may not yet be activated or differentiated in choice functions.

A pronounced upward trajectory emerges immediately thereafter. Between indices 0 and 9, the metric exhibits statistically significant monotonic increases. The steepest ascent occurs during the earliest phase (indices 0–4), where the deviation increases sharply with a slope of approximately 2.84 units per time step, followed by a continued but moderated rise (indices 5–9) with a gentler slope of 0.109. By index 9, the deviation approaches 13.0, indicating a rapidly widening gap between environmental values and actual consumption behaviors as agents begin encountering alternative milk varieties and social influences.

The system reaches a saturation point around index 13, where the deviation peaks at 13.4756. Following this maximum, the metric enters a prolonged plateau phase lasting through the remainder of the simulation (indices 13–32). During this period, values fluctuate minimally within a narrow band between approximately 13.415 and 13.476, with secondary peaks observed at indices 19, 21, 27, and 30, and shallow troughs at intermediate points. The absence of significant trends in rolling windows beyond index 9 confirms the stabilization of this gap. The final recorded value at index 32 stands at 13.4186, marginally below the mid-plateau peak but consistent with the established equilibrium range.

Across the full temporal range, the deviation maintains a mean of 12.6015 and a median of 13.4302, with the distribution skewed by the initial near-zero values. The standard deviation of 2.5933 reflects primarily the variance introduced during the initial ramp-up phase rather than instability in the plateau period.

Interpretively, the trajectory reveals a swift decoupling of environmental attitudes from consumption practices during the early years of the milk transition, followed by a persistent, stable value-action gap throughout the subsequent decades. The initial zero-alignment suggests that early consumption patterns matched the limited environmental salience of milk choices, but as alternative varieties entered the market and environmental perceptions evolved, friction from incumbent habits (initialized at high strength) and social network effects prevented immediate behavioral alignment with emerging environmental values. The plateau indicates that even after the market transition stabilizes, a substantial discrepancy remains between what agents value environmentally and what they actually consume, suggesting that habit persistence and social conformity sustain a steady-state level of cognitive dissonance or behavioral inertia.

For decision makers, these dynamics suggest that awareness campaigns targeting environmental values alone may be insufficient to close the attitude-behavior gap in food choices. The rapid emergence and subsequent stabilization of the deviation imply that interventions must specifically disrupt habitual consumption patterns or amplify social norms favoring environmentally aligned choices during the critical early transition windows. Once stabilized, the system exhibits high resistance to further alignment, indicating that policy efforts should prioritize early intervention or sustained structural changes rather than assuming gradual organic convergence between values and behaviors.
