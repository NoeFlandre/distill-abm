The simulation results reveal a three-phase adoption trajectory for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumption over the 31-year period (steps 0–32).

**Phase 1: Rapid Initial Adoption (Steps 0–11)**
The metric exhibits exponential growth, rising from near-zero baseline values at step 0 to approximately 1,200–1,280 units by step 11. This represents the initial diffusion period where consumers, beginning with strong whole milk habits (initial habit strength of 10.0) but low perceptions of alternative milk health and environmental benefits (1.69 and 1.12 respectively), rapidly transition toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties as information spreads through the Watts-Strogatz social network structure.

**Phase 2: Saturation Plateau (Steps 11–25)**
The trajectory enters an extended period of stagnation, with values fluctuating moderately between approximately 1,180 and 1,250 units. During this 14-step interval, the adoption rate slows dramatically as the market reaches a temporary equilibrium. The persistence of habit strength (threshold of 2.0) combined with moderate social influence parameters (social susceptibility of 0.2, probability of interaction 0.3, and social blindness of 0.51) creates resistance to further rapid transition, resulting in a consolidation phase where approximately 60–65% of the maximum final consumption level is maintained without significant growth.

**Phase 3: Secondary Growth (Steps 25–32)**
A renewed acceleration emerges in the final period, with values climbing from approximately 1,220 units at step 25 to reach 1,440–1,480 units by step 32. This late-stage growth suggests that after the extended plateau, cumulative social influence effects and updated health perceptions eventually overcome residual habit barriers, driving the market toward a new equilibrium with higher alternative milk consumption. The final values represent approximately 95–98% of the maximum observed values across all simulation runs, indicating convergence toward a stable high-adoption state.

**Variability and Robustness**
The between-simulation variation remains relatively constrained throughout all phases, with the coefficient of variation typically below 5%, indicating robustness in the emergent patterns despite the stochastic elements in the probability-based psychological mechanisms and social interaction processes.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The three-phase structure demonstrates that food category transitions may experience extended plateau periods lasting 10–15 years where adoption temporarily stalls despite initial rapid uptake. This suggests that market interventions targeting the consolidation phase—such as targeted health messaging or network-based social marketing—could accelerate the secondary growth phase and prevent prolonged market stagnation. The robustness across simulations indicates that the social network topology and habit parameters create predictable transition dynamics, enabling policymakers to anticipate similar S-curve adoption patterns with intermediate plateaus in comparable food category transitions involving health-conscious substitutes.
