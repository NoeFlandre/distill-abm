The simulation reveals a trajectory of increasing consumer heterogeneity in decision-making calculus throughout the 31-year transition period. At initialization, complete uniformity exists with all agents exhibiting identical choice function evaluations (0.0), reflecting the initial market condition where whole milk dominance—characterized by maximum habitual strength—creates homogeneous consumer behavior.

This uniformity rapidly erodes during the initial phase, with the divergence between consumer evaluations exhibiting steep acceleration through approximately the seventh time step, then moderating but continuing to rise through the twelfth time step. This early fragmentation corresponds to the first wave of agents overcoming the incumbent habit threshold and beginning to incorporate alternative milk perceptions into their choice functions while the majority maintains strong whole milk habits. The subsequent deceleration in divergence rates suggests that initial habit-breaking creates the most dramatic segmentation, while later adoption involves more gradual differentiation.

Through the middle and late phases, the spread between the most and least favorable evaluations of milk options continues to expand, though at progressively slower rates, with additional transitions in the pace of change occurring around the twenty-eighth time step. By the conclusion of the simulation period, this spread reaches approximately 0.75, indicating substantial segmentation in consumer decision frameworks where agents simultaneously maintain vastly different evaluation criteria for milk options.

Superimposed on this secular trend is a cyclical pattern with a dominant periodicity of approximately 15 time steps, manifesting as recurring waves of heightened differentiation around time steps 6–8, 16–19, and 26–30. These oscillations likely reflect rhythmic cycles of social influence propagation through the network structure and periodic reassessment intervals tied to information processing mechanisms, creating successive waves of agents reconsidering their habitual choices.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The documented evolution from homogeneous to highly heterogeneous choice functions suggests that market transitions create distinct consumer psychographic segments rather than a smooth aggregate shift. The rapid early divergence indicates a critical window (approximately the first quarter of the transition period) during which initial market fragmentation occurs—potentially the most decisive phase for intervention strategies. The persistent cyclical patterns suggest that consumer openness to switching alternates between periods of receptivity and resistance, offering predictable windows for targeted marketing or policy interventions. Finally, the substantial end-state diversity implies that even after alternatives achieve market dominance, a significant minority of consumers maintain distinctly traditional evaluation frameworks, necessitating continued dual-market strategies rather than complete portfolio abandonment of conventional offerings.
