This trajectory suggests that the initial phase of the dietary transition generates substantial and rapid misalignment between environmental values and consumption habits. The sharp early increase indicates that disposition mechanisms and social influences quickly alter agents' perceptions of environmental characteristics associated with milk types, while high initial habit strength for whole milk consumption creates inertia that prevents immediate behavioral adaptation. The subsequent plateau implies that although cognitive dissonance thresholds and justification mechanisms allow agents to partially reconcile this tension through post-choice evaluation, these processes prove insufficient to fully resolve the misalignment; instead, the system settles into a steady state where significant value-choice inconsistency persists across the population.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that public health and environmental campaigns promoting milk alternatives should anticipate significant and lasting gaps between the public's environmental values and actual consumption behaviors during transition periods. The rapid emergence of deviation within the first five time steps highlights the critical importance of early intervention strategies, as dissonant patterns crystallize quickly and become resistant to change. Additionally, the persistent high plateau suggests that interventions targeting habit disruption may prove more effective than value-based messaging alone, as the model demonstrates that even with active cognitive processing, consumption patterns tend to stabilize in misalignment with environmental attitudes due to the dominant influence of incumbent habits and social conformity pressures.
