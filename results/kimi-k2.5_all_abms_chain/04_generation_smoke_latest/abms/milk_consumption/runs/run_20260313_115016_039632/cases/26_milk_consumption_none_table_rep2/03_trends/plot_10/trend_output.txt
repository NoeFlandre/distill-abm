The simulation reveals a systematic transition from initial homogeneity to substantial heterogeneity in agent decision-making criteria over the 1974–2005 period. At the outset in 1974, all agents exhibited identical choice function scores, reflecting the initial market condition where whole milk dominated consumption patterns uniformly across the population. By the conclusion of the simulation period in 2005, the disparity between agents holding the maximum and minimum choice function scores had expanded to approximately 0.75, indicating that the most progressive agents' decision weights for milk alternatives diverged substantially from those maintaining traditional preferences.

The trajectory of divergence followed a markedly non-linear pattern characterized by rapid initial differentiation followed by decelerating growth. During the first several years of the simulation (approximately 1974–1979), the population experienced swift polarization, with the percentage difference between extreme choice function scores increasing sharply from zero to roughly 0.50. This steep ascent suggests that once disposition mechanisms activated—whether threshold-based or probability-based—early adopters rapidly recalibrated their choice functions away from the incumbent whole milk standard, while conservative agents maintained their initial habit strength of 10.0.

Following this initial phase, the rate of divergence moderated considerably through the 1980s and 1990s, though the trend remained monotonically increasing. Inflection points in the trajectory occurred around 1981, 1986, and 2002, corresponding to structural shifts in how rapidly the population was differentiating. Between these inflection points, the expansion of choice function deviation proceeded at a gentler slope, increasing by approximately 0.005 to 0.009 per year compared to the initial rate of 0.128. This deceleration likely reflects the stabilization of habit formations and the saturating effect of social influence within the Watts-Strogatz network topology, as agents with high social susceptibility (0.2) and moderate interaction probabilities (0.3) reached equilibrium states in their preference structures.

Throughout the entire simulation span, statistical tests confirm a consistently significant upward trend, despite the presence of underlying oscillatory dynamics with a dominant periodicity of approximately 15 years. These oscillations manifested as temporary accelerations and decelerations in the divergence rate around 1980, 1990, and 2000, potentially corresponding to generational shifts in social norms or cyclical reconsideration events triggered by cognitive dissonance thresholds (0.15). By the final years of the simulation, the population approached a near-plateau state where the choice function deviation stabilized near its maximum observed value of 0.7508, suggesting that the market transition from whole milk to alternative varieties had reached a mature phase with distinct consumer segments firmly established.
