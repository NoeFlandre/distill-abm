The deviation between agents' held health values and the values implied by their actual milk choices begins at zero in 1974, indicating perfect alignment between consumption behaviors and health perceptions at the simulation's outset when whole milk dominated the market. This metric increases monotonically throughout the entire simulation period, ultimately reaching approximately 0.725 by 2005, signifying a substantial and persistent divergence between what agents believe to be healthy and their actual consumption decisions.

The trajectory follows a distinctly non-linear pattern characterized by three primary phases. During the initial period from 1974 through approximately 1980, the deviation increases rapidly with a steep slope, suggesting an swift emergence of cognitive dissonance as health information regarding low-fat alternatives begins permeating the social network while consumption habits remain strongly anchored to incumbent whole milk varieties. This early acceleration reflects the initial lag between changing health perceptions and the inertia of established consumption patterns.

Between roughly 1984 and 1996, corresponding to the middle portion of the simulation, the rate of divergence slows considerably to near-stagnation. During this intermediate plateau, the misalignment between health beliefs and choices increases only marginally, indicating a temporary equilibrium where habit strength and social blindness suppress the disposition to reconsider alternatives, maintaining a steady state of inconsistency as the system resists behavioral change despite evolving norms.

The trend accelerates again after 1996, with the divergence increasing more rapidly through the late 1990s and early 2000s until reaching its maximum value at the simulation's conclusion. This late-stage intensification suggests that accumulated social pressure and repeated exposure to alternative health information eventually overcome habitual barriers, driving choices that may contradict initial health valuations or reflect complex justifications as the market completes its transition toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties.

Superimposed upon this general upward trajectory, the system exhibits oscillatory dynamics with a periodicity of approximately fifteen years, manifesting particularly in the later stages around the turn of the millennium. These cyclic variations likely correspond to waves of agent reconsideration triggered by disposition mechanisms and network-driven norm adoption, creating rhythmic fluctuations in the alignment between personal health valuations and consumption behaviors as the population gradually adapts to changing dietary recommendations.
