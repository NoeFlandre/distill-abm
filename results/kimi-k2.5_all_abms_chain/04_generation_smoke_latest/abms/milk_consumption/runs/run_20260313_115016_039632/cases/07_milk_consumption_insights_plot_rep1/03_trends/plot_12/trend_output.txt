The mean environmental deviation metric, measuring the gap between agents' held environmental values and the implied values of their milk choices, commences at approximately zero at the initial time step, indicating strong alignment between values and consumption behavior characteristic of the 1974 baseline. During the first five time steps, the metric undergoes a steep and rapid ascent, surging from near zero to approximately 12.5 to 13.0 units. This acceleration continues at a diminishing rate through approximately time step 10, where values reach 13.3 to 13.5 units. From time step 10 through time step 32, the metric stabilizes into a persistent plateau, maintaining values between 13.3 and 13.5 units with minimal fluctuation across all simulation runs.

Interpretation of these trends within the model context reveals a swift emergence of cognitive dissonance during the early simulation period. The initial near-zero deviation reflects the starting conditions where whole milk dominated consumption patterns and environmental considerations were minimally discordant with choices. The rapid increase to approximately 13 units within the first ten time steps indicates that environmental values or awareness shift significantly faster than actual consumption behaviors, creating a substantial and persistent value-action gap. The pronounced plateau suggests that despite the operationalization of disposition mechanisms and social influence pathways, the high initial habit strength (parameterized at 10.0) and social network effects generate sufficient inertia to prevent behavioral alignment with evolving environmental values. Agents appear to maintain consumption patterns inconsistent with their environmental priorities, stabilizing at a high deviation state where cognitive dissonance remains unresolved throughout the simulation timeframe extending to 2005.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The entrenched high deviation plateau indicates that informational campaigns or awareness-raising strategies targeting environmental values will likely prove insufficient to drive behavioral change in milk consumption without complementary interventions that disrupt habit formation. The critical transition occurs within the first five to ten time steps, suggesting a narrow intervention window exists before the value-action gap becomes structurally embedded. Policies should prioritize mechanisms that counter high initial habit inertia and leverage social conformity effects more intensely than the current network topology permits, or alternatively, accept that persistent deviation between environmental values and consumption behaviors represents an equilibrium state requiring direct habit-disruption tools rather than value-shift strategies alone.
