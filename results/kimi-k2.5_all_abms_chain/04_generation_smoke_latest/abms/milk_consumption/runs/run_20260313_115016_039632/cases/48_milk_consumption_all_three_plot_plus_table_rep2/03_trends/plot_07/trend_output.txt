Over the first ten time steps, consumption increases rapidly and non-linearly, reaching approximately 1,265 units by step 11. This initial explosive growth reflects the early adoption phase where agents with lower effective habit thresholds or higher sensitivity to health perceptions (initially 1.69) and environmental perceptions (initially 1.12) begin breaking established behavioral patterns, triggering social influence effects across the networked population with 30% interaction probability and 20% social susceptibility.\n\nFollowing this rapid ascent, the trend enters a prolonged oscillatory plateau lasting from approximately step 11 through step 24, with values fluctuating between local minima near 1,183 units (observed at step 18) and local maxima around 1,265 units (step 11). This middle period exhibits no statistically significant monotonic trend according to rolling Mann-Kendall analysis, instead displaying a dominant oscillation period of approximately 15 steps with inflection points occurring at steps 12, 13, 16, 19, 22, and 23. The oscillating dynamics suggest a temporary equilibrium between entrenched habitual behavior—reinforced by the high initial incumbent habit strength of 10.0—and accumulating social and informational pressures. This plateau represents systemic friction in the transition process, where the threshold-based psychological mechanism requires agents to accumulate sufficient motivation to overcome the habit threshold of 2.0, while social blindness (0.51) and cognitive dissonance tolerance (0.15) moderate immediate norm contagion.\n\nAfter step 24, the metric resumes significant upward movement, accelerating through steps 25 to 32 to reach a terminal value of approximately 1,457 units. This final growth phase coincides with the critical mass effect where social conformity pressures (weighted at 0.28) and justification mechanisms (0.61) enable late-adopting agents to rationalize behavioral change and finally abandon incumbent habits. The Mann-Kendall trend analysis confirms statistically significant increasing trajectories in the initial phase (steps 0-4 and 5-9) and final phase (steps 25-29), with slopes of 112.8 and 48.5 units per step respectively, while the intermediate period shows no significant directional trend.\n\nThe relatively high consistency across the 30 simulation runs during the initial growth phase, followed by increasing variability during the oscillatory middle period, suggests that while early adoption dynamics are highly predictable based on initial parameter configurations, the timing and severity of resistance from habit maintenance introduces stochasticity in the transition trajectory. The simulation concludes with alternative milk consumption achieving dominance, successfully replicating the historical crossover event where skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties surpass whole milk adoption.\n\n*Decision Making Implications:* The pronounced S-curve adoption pattern, featuring an initial rapid surge, mid-transition resistance, and final acceleration, indicates that behavioral change campaigns targeting habitual consumption must prepare for a potentially extended plateau phase where early adopters have switched but mainstream consumers remain entrenched.
