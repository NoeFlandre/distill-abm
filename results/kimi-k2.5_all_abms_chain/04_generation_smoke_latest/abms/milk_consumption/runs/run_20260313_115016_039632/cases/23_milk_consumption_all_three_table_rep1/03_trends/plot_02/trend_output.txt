Consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at minimal levels, approximately 5.3 units per agent per week, and follows a pronounced S-curve trajectory over the 31-year period, ultimately reaching approximately 1,456.6 units. This expansion represents a nearly 275-fold increase, capturing the historical transition from whole milk dominance to alternative variety prevalence in the British market.

The adoption dynamics unfold through three distinct phases. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first decade (time steps 0 through 9), consumption increases rapidly with slopes exceeding 139 units per period. This steep ascent reflects the initial breakdown of incumbent habits—despite agents beginning with strong habitual attachments to whole milk—and the influence of positive health and environmental perceptions regarding alternative options. The consistency and statistical significance of growth during this period suggests effective early penetration among segments willing to overcome habitual barriers through accumulated positive perceptions and social influence.

A transitional plateau emerges during the middle period (approximately time steps 10 through 24), where the trajectory stabilizes at elevated absolute levels between approximately 1,180 and 1,265 units. This phase exhibits pronounced oscillatory behavior characterized by alternating peaks and valleys, including local maxima near time steps 11, 13, and 21, interspersed with declines at steps 12, 18, and 20. The absence of statistically significant trend during this middle period, combined with multiple inflection points at steps 12, 13, 16, 19, and 22, suggests that habit persistence and social influence mechanisms reached temporary equilibrium. The volatility likely reflects the stochastic nature of network interactions—where limited interaction probabilities and social blindness create irregular propagation of behavioral change—counterbalanced by habit threshold effects that generate resistance among remaining whole milk consumers.

The final phase (time steps 25 through 32) exhibits renewed significant growth, though at a moderated slope of approximately 48.5 units per period. This late-stage increase indicates the eventual conversion of holdout consumers, potentially driven by cumulative social pressure reaching critical thresholds after prolonged exposure to alternative milk consumption within their networks, combined with memory decay effects that reduce the strength of incumbent habits over time. The trajectory terminates at peak values near 1,456 units, approaching market saturation where alternative varieties become the dominant consumption pattern.

The presence of oscillatory patterns with a dominant periodicity of approximately 15 time steps throughout the series indicates that the transition was not monotonic but subject to cyclical reversals and accelerations consistent with complex adaptive systems where individual habit formation, cognitive dissonance thresholds, and social network influences interact dynamically.

For decision makers, these patterns suggest that market transitions for habitual consumer goods follow predictable S-curve dynamics with distinct risk of mid-adoption stagnation. The prolonged plateau phase indicates that achieving majority market share requires sustained intervention beyond initial early-adopter success, as habitual consumption creates persistent resistance that social influence mechanisms may take decades to overcome. The oscillatory nature during the middle phase implies that short-term volatility should not be mistaken for failed adoption, but rather represents the natural friction of behavioral change in socially-connected populations with strong incumbent habits. Additionally, the renewed growth in final periods suggests that patience in policy or marketing approaches may be rewarded as late-stage adopters eventually respond to accumulated social proof and shifting norms.
