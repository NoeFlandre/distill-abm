The simulation of milk consumption adoption from 1974 to 2005 demonstrates a substantial long-term increase in the average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per person, rising from 5.29 units at the initial time step to 1456.60 units by the final time step. The trajectory unfolds across three distinct phases. During the initial phase spanning time steps 0 through 9, consumption exhibits rapid exponential-like growth, with statistically significant increasing trends (slopes of approximately 112.8 and 139.0 units per step in successive windows) that elevate consumption levels to approximately 1200 units by step 9. The middle phase, encompassing time steps 10 through 24, demonstrates a pronounced plateau characterized by oscillation around a stable mean of roughly 1200-1250 units. This period features multiple local peaks at step 11 (1265.47 units), step 13 (1240.35 units), step 19 (1221.57 units), and step 21 (1226.86 units), interspersed with valleys at step 12 (1233.62 units), step 18 (1183.39 units), step 20 (1214.46 units), and step 23 (1209.70 units), indicating a lack of significant directional trend. The final phase, covering time steps 25 through 32, exhibits renewed significant growth with a slope of approximately 48.5 units per step, driving consumption from roughly 1200 units to the terminal maximum of 1456.60 units. This pattern reflects a diffusion process with initial rapid adoption, a mid-period saturation or equilibrium phase possibly indicating market stabilization or demographic inertia, followed by renewed growth suggesting continued adoption momentum through the late 1990s and early 2000s.
