The cumulative count of whole milk selections initiates at zero and exhibits a sustained monotonic increase throughout the entire 1974–2005 simulation period, terminating at approximately 2,195 total choices. The accumulation follows a near-linear trajectory characterized by an initial acceleration phase reaching peak growth rates around the fifth to ninth time steps, followed by a gradual deceleration in accumulation velocity with consistent but slowly diminishing incremental gains through the final time step.

Statistical analysis reveals this growth trend persists with high significance across all observation windows, though the rate of increase moderates from approximately 76 units per step in early intervals to roughly 64 units per step in later periods. Inflection points at steps 1, 7, and 31 mark distinct phases in the accumulation dynamics, with the step 7 inflection coinciding with the transition from accelerating to decelerating growth rates.

Early simulation stages demonstrate high consistency across runs with tightly clustered accumulation paths, whereas later stages exhibit increasing variability as trajectories diverge, suggesting that while initial whole milk choices remain highly predictable based on 1974 incumbent preferences, long-term consumption patterns become increasingly sensitive to stochastic social network interactions and individual disposition mechanisms. The presence of oscillatory patterns with a 15-step periodicity in the later stages indicates cyclical fluctuations in choice behavior superimposed on the overall growth trend.

These dynamics indicate that while whole milk maintained persistent consumption volumes throughout the historical transition period, the steadily declining rate of new selections reflects the gradual erosion of incumbent dominance as British consumers progressively shifted toward skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives. The substantial inertia evident in the continued linear accumulation—despite slowing rates—underscores the powerful role of habit formation and initial preference structures in sustaining incumbent market positions even during extended periods of social change.

For decision makers, these trends suggest that disrupting established consumption patterns in habit-driven food categories requires sustained multi-decade intervention strategies, as even successful alternative products face resistance from deeply entrenched incumbent preferences that decay gradually rather than collapsing abruptly. The increasing variability in long-term outcomes implies that while the directional trend away from whole milk is robust, precise market share predictions remain sensitive to specific network topologies and social influence parameters, necessitating flexible policy frameworks capable of accommodating heterogeneous adoption pathways across different population segments.
