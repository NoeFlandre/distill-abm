The average weekly whole milk consumption per agent initiates at approximately 2,650 units at time step 0, consistent with the 1974 market baseline where whole milk represented the dominant choice. A precipitous decline occurs over the first 10 time steps, with consumption falling to approximately 950 units. This rapid early reduction reflects the initial phase of dietary transition, where agents with lower habit strength or higher sensitivity to health and environmental perceptions abandon whole milk in favor of skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives.

From time step 10 through time step 25, the rate of decline moderates substantially, with consumption decreasing gradually to roughly 600 units. This decelerated middle phase indicates that remaining agents possess strong incumbent habits or reduced social susceptibility, creating resistance to switching despite ongoing exposure to alternative choice characteristics and social network influences.

Between time step 25 and time step 32, the metric exhibits a renewed sharp decline, dropping to approximately 250 units. This late-stage acceleration suggests that cumulative social norm effects and the gradual erosion of habitual patterns through memory decay and cognitive dissonance eventually overcome resistance, as the social network reaches a tipping point where whole milk consumption becomes statistically deviant.

Across the simulation runs, variance remains minimal throughout the time series, particularly after the initial 5 time steps, indicating that the parameterized configuration generates consistent aggregate outcomes. The tight clustering of trajectories suggests that the interaction between disposition mechanisms, habit formation, and Watts-Strogatz network topology produces robust and reproducible population-level dynamics of milk choice transition over the 31-year period.
