The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk initializes at a baseline value of 1.0, indicating no habitual reinforcement for alternative milk varieties at the simulation outset. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first five time steps, the metric remains relatively constant, fluctuating minimally between 1.0 and 1.02 with a slight local minimum of 1.0167 reached at step 5. This early plateau corresponds to the period where agents remain anchored to whole milk consumption due to high incumbent habit strength, limiting the development of habitual alternative consumption patterns.

Beginning around step 5, the habit factor enters a sustained monotonic increase that continues through the simulation's conclusion at step 32. The rate of growth accelerates substantially between steps 10 and 14, achieving the steepest ascent with a slope of approximately 0.030 per step, before moderating gradually to roughly 0.018 per step in the final phase. By step 32, the habit factor reaches 1.5879, representing a 58.8% increase from baseline and indicating strong habitual reinforcement for lower-fat milk consumption among the agent population.

This trajectory demonstrates remarkably low variance across the 30 simulation runs, with all series following nearly identical sigmoid-like paths. The inflection around step 12 marks the transition from acceleration to deceleration in habit formation rate, consistent with the population-level shift from whole milk to lower-fat alternatives. The progression illustrates how repeated adoption decisions, influenced by social network interactions within the Watts-Strogatz topology and disposition mechanisms, gradually transform occasional alternative milk consumption into entrenched behavioral patterns that stabilize the new consumption regime observed historically between 1974 and 2005.
