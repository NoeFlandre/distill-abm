The mean weekly consumption of whole milk per person begins at approximately 2,650 units at the start of the simulation, reflecting the historical market dominance of whole milk characteristic of 1974 market conditions. During the initial five steps, this metric undergoes a steep decline to roughly 2,000 units, indicating the rapid initial erosion of whole milk consumption as agents begin processing perceived health and environmental attributes of alternative options.  

Between steps 5 and 10, the decline accelerates substantially, with consumption plummeting from approximately 2,000 units to around 1,000 units. This sharp reduction represents a critical transition phase where disposition mechanisms trigger widespread reconsideration of default choices, and social network effects amplify the abandonment of whole milk as agents sense and respond to the choices of others within the Watts-Strogatz topology.  

From step 10 to step 25, the rate of decline moderates considerably, with consumption decreasing gradually from roughly 1,000 units to approximately 600 units. This slower reduction reflects the progressive weakening of incumbent habits—initially set at high strength—and the establishment of new behavioral patterns, as agents with varying cognitive dissonance thresholds and social susceptibility levels transition away from whole milk at different rates. Minor fluctuations occur around step 19, suggesting temporary stabilization as habit strength and social influence reach temporary equilibrium for certain agent subpopulations.  

A pronounced acceleration in the decline emerges between steps 25 and 30, where consumption drops sharply from approximately 600 units to around 300 units. This late-phase transition likely corresponds to social conformity pressures and global social norms reaching critical mass, overwhelming the remaining high-habit agents who had previously resisted switching.  

In the final steps from 30 to 32, the metric stabilizes at approximately 230 to 250 units, representing a persistent residual level of whole milk consumption that remains even after the majority transition to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties is complete. Across multiple simulation runs, the trajectories display consistent qualitative patterns with quantitative variations of roughly 100 to 200 units during the middle phases, particularly between steps 8 and 12, indicating that while stochastic agent interactions introduce variability in individual transition timing, the macro-level decline dynamics remain robust and reproducible.
