The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent initiates at approximately 5 units during the first time step, reflecting the historical market conditions of 1974 when whole milk dominated consumer preferences. Between steps 1 and 10, consumption undergoes rapid non-linear growth, accelerating from near-zero levels to approximately 1,200–1,250 units. This initial surge corresponds to the early adoption phase where agents begin processing health and environmental attribute information about alternative milk varieties, with perceptions gradually shifting away from the initial health perception mean of 1.69 and environmental perception mean of 1.12 toward more favorable evaluations.

From step 10 through step 25, the consumption trajectory enters an extended plateau phase characterized by minor fluctuations, maintaining values primarily between 1,180 and 1,250 units. During this period, the rate of adoption slows considerably as many agents remain locked in strong habitual patterns of whole milk consumption, reflecting the high incumbent initial habit strength of 10.0 and the habit threshold of 2.0 that creates substantial resistance to change. Social influence mechanisms, operating with a 30% interaction probability and moderate social susceptibility of 0.2, continue to diffuse information through the Watts-Strogatz network structure but prove insufficient during this phase to overcome the habitual inertia of the majority of agents.

Beginning around step 25, consumption resumes a distinct upward trajectory, increasing from approximately 1,250 units to reach roughly 1,450–1,470 units by step 32. This second growth phase reflects the cumulative effect of sustained social influence mechanisms and normative pressures, where the weight of social conformity at 0.28 and the activation of norm-based decision-making finally overcome habitual resistance for the remaining late-adopting agents. The variance across simulation runs remains relatively low throughout the entire period, indicating consistent macro-level adoption patterns despite the stochastic elements in individual psychological processing, including the probability-based mechanisms for breaking habits and the cognitive dissonance threshold of 0.15.
