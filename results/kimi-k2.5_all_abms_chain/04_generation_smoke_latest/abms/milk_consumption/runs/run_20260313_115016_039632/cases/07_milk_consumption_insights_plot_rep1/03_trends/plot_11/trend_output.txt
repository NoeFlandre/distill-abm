The mean deviation between held health values and choice-implied health values exhibits a consistent trajectory across simulation runs, characterized by three distinct phases. Initially, the metric begins at zero and undergoes rapid increase during the first ten time steps, rising to approximately 0.5. This early acceleration corresponds to the initial market transition period where agents begin shifting from whole milk toward alternative varieties, establishing initial misalignments between personal health valuations and actual consumption behaviors.

Following this initial growth, the deviation enters a plateau phase lasting from approximately step 12 through step 25, where values stabilize in the range of 0.55 to 0.6 with only gradual upward progression. This middle period represents a temporary equilibrium in the system where the rate of new misalignment formation balances against habituation and cognitive adjustment processes.

In the final phase, from step 25 through step 32, the deviation accelerates sharply again, reaching values between 0.7 and 0.75 by the conclusion of the simulation period. This late-stage increase suggests that as the social norm solidifies around alternative milk varieties and the population nears complete transition, the aggregate gap between individual health perceptions and consumption choices widens considerably.

The tight clustering of trajectories across repeated simulations indicates robust emergence of this pattern, suggesting that the interaction between strong incumbent habits, social influence mechanisms, and cognitive dissonance thresholds systematically produces increasing health-behavior misalignment throughout the transition period, despite varying stochastic realizations of network interactions.

Implications for decision makers: The persistent growth in health deviation throughout the transition indicates that public health interventions focusing solely on consumption volumes may overlook growing cognitive dissonance within the population. The plateau phase observed in steps 15-25 suggests a critical window where belief-alignment interventions could prevent the secondary surge in misalignment observed during the final adoption phase. Furthermore, the high terminal values imply that a substantial portion of the population may ultimately consume alternative milk varieties through social pressure or habit formation rather than internalized health preferences, potentially creating vulnerability to future behavioral reversals if social norms shift.
