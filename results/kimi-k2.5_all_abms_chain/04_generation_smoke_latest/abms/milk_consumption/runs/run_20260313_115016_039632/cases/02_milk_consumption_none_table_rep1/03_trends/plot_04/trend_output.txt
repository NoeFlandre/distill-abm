The simulation of milk consumption behaviors from 1974 to 2005 reveals a continuous monotonic accumulation of alternative milk selections, beginning from a baseline of zero adoptions at initiation and rising to approximately 25,644 cumulative choices by the terminal year. This trajectory reflects the historical dietary transition from whole milk dominance toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties across the British public.

The adoption dynamics unfold through distinct temporal phases characterized by varying velocities of behavioral change. During the initial five-year period (1974–1979), the accumulation occurs gradually, with approximately 200 new alternative selections registered per year. This early phase transitions sharply during the subsequent five-year interval (1979–1984), where the rate of adoption accelerates to roughly 730 selections annually, indicating the onset of rapid diffusion through the agent population.

Following this acceleration, the system enters a prolonged phase of sustained high-velocity growth spanning approximately two decades (1984–2003). Throughout this period, the accumulation stabilizes at a relatively constant rate exceeding 900 selections per year (ranging from approximately 927 to 936 annually), suggesting steady penetration of alternative milk choices through the social network as influence mechanisms and habit formation drive consistent behavioral uptake.

In the terminal phase (2003–2005), cumulative adoption continues to increase, reaching its maximum value at the conclusion of the simulation. However, an inflection point emerges during the penultimate time step, indicating a potential deceleration in the rate of accumulation as the system approaches saturation. The absence of any declining periods or valleys throughout the entire 32-year span indicates an irreversible transition in consumption patterns, with no regression toward whole milk selections once adoption has occurred. This pattern demonstrates how perception shifts, combined with social influence operating through the Watts-Strogatz network topology and habit disposition mechanisms, generate a classic diffusion curve characterized by slow initial uptake, explosive middle-period growth, and eventual maturation of the alternative milk market.
