The cumulative count of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices begins at essentially zero and follows a trajectory of initially slow growth that accelerates through the middle period before transitioning to sustained linear accumulation, ultimately reaching approximately 25,500 to 26,000 total selections by step 32. 

During the initial five steps, accumulation proceeds slowly, with total choices reaching only around 1,000 to 1,500. This initial inertia reflects the strong incumbent habit strength associated with whole milk and the relatively low initial health and environmental perceptions that agents hold regarding alternative options, creating a barrier to early adoption despite the presence of social influence mechanisms.

Between steps 5 and 20, the rate of accumulation accelerates markedly, with total choices increasing from approximately 1,500 to 14,000–15,000. This acceleration phase corresponds to the period where social influence diffusing through the Watts-Strogatz network structure, combined with memory-weighted learning about product attributes, begins to overcome habitual barriers, allowing changing perceptions to drive choice transitions among the agent population.

From step 20 through the conclusion at step 32, the accumulation maintains an approximately linear trajectory with a steep positive slope, adding roughly 11,000 choices over the final 12 steps. This linear phase indicates that alternative milk selection has become the dominant behavioral pattern, with agents making these choices at a high and stable rate of approximately 900 selections per step, representing roughly 90 percent of the 1,000-agent population consistently choosing skimmed or semi-skimmed varieties by the end of the simulation period.

Across all simulation runs, the trajectories demonstrate remarkably low variance, remaining tightly clustered throughout the entire 32-step period. This consistency suggests that despite stochastic elements in individual decision-making—including probability-based habit-breaking mechanisms and random social interactions with 30 percent probability—the macro-level adoption pattern is highly robust and deterministic, successfully capturing the historical transition in which alternative milk varieties achieved market dominance over the incumbent whole milk option.
