The mean deviation between agents' held environmental values and the values implied by their milk consumption choices follows a distinct three-phase trajectory over the 33 time steps representing the 1974-2005 period. Initially, the metric exhibits rapid escalation from a starting value of zero, increasing steeply through the first five time steps to reach approximately 11.4. This acceleration reflects the immediate divergence between emerging environmental values and incumbent consumption patterns as agents begin transitioning from whole milk toward lower-fat alternatives. Between time steps 5 and 9, the rate of increase decelerates considerably with a gentler upward slope, approaching values near 13.4 as the system nears equilibrium. From time step 10 onward, the metric stabilizes into a prolonged plateau phase, oscillating narrowly around 13.4-13.5 with minor fluctuations exhibiting a dominant periodicity of 15 time steps. The maximum deviation of 13.4756 occurs at time step 13, after which values persist within a constrained range, ending at 13.4186 at the conclusion of the simulation. 

This pattern indicates that the misalignment between environmental values and actual milk choices widens substantially during the initial decade of dietary transition, then crystallizes into a persistent high-level discrepancy for the remaining two decades. The initial zero deviation suggests alignment when whole milk dominated the market, but as health considerations drive adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties, agents develop environmental value systems that increasingly exceed what their milk choices imply, creating substantial cognitive dissonance that remains unresolved despite ongoing social network interactions and habit formation mechanisms. The stability of the plateau after time step 13, combined with the high incumbent initial habit parameter (10.0) and active habit formation, suggests that consumption patterns become locked in early, preventing later realignment with environmental values even as social norms and susceptibility continue operating within the network.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

Interventions promoting low-fat milk on health grounds should anticipate that such campaigns may inadvertently establish persistent value-action gaps regarding environmental objectives, as consumers appear to maintain this discrepancy over extended periods once habits form. Strategies seeking to align milk consumption with environmental goals must explicitly connect environmental benefits to health-oriented choices or target the initial transition window (approximately the first 10 time steps) before habit crystallization occurs, given that post-transition interventions face significantly higher barriers due to entrenched behavioral patterns. The stability of the deviation throughout the plateau phase suggests that passive social influence alone is insufficient to resolve environmental cognitive dissonance in food choice contexts, requiring instead active reframing of choice characteristics or disruption of the justification mechanisms that allow consumers to maintain divergent values and behaviors.
