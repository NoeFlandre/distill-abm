The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the health values implied by their milk choices begins at 0.0 and increases monotonically throughout the 32 time steps of the simulation. During the initial phase from steps 0 to 10, the deviation rises rapidly from 0.0 to approximately 0.55, indicating a swift emergence of cognitive dissonance as agents begin receiving information about the health attributes of alternative milk options while maintaining strong incumbent habits for whole milk (initial habit strength of 10.0).

From steps 10 to 25, the rate of increase slows considerably, with the deviation rising gradually from roughly 0.55 to 0.60. This plateau-like phase suggests a temporary equilibrium where the population experiences stabilized tension between habit persistence and evolving health perceptions, potentially reflecting a period where early adopters have switched to alternatives (reducing their deviation) while the majority maintains habitual whole milk consumption despite growing health awareness.

In the final phase from steps 25 to 32, the deviation accelerates again, climbing steeply from approximately 0.60 to values between 0.70 and 0.75. This late-stage increase likely reflects the culmination of the market transition, where remaining holdouts continue consuming whole milk despite increasingly salient health information about alternatives, or where agents who have switched maintain health perceptions that diverge from their new consumption patterns. The low variance across simulation runs throughout all phases indicates that the habit formation mechanisms and social influence parameters produce consistent patterns of value-choice divergence regardless of stochastic variation in individual agent interactions.
