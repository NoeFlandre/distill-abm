The mean habit factor applied to whole milk choices exhibits a consistent monotonic increase throughout the 33-step simulation period, rising from an initial value of 1.0000 to 1.0502 at step 32, representing a 5.02% net increase. The trajectory demonstrates an initial rapid adjustment phase during the first 7 steps, reaching approximately 1.024 by step 7, with subsequent inflection points at steps 7 and 12 marking transitions to more moderate but sustained linear growth rates.

Statistical analysis confirms this upward trend is highly consistent across simulation runs, with a series mean of 1.0359, median of 1.0376, and minimal variability (standard deviation of 0.0110). Rolling window Mann-Kendall tests across six sequential intervals (steps 0–4, 5–9, 10–14, 15–19, 20–24, and 25–29) all indicate significant monotonic increases, each yielding Kendall's tau values of 1.000, positive slopes of 0.001, and p-values of 0.027.

Within the context of the British milk market transition, this gradual strengthening of the habit factor indicates that habitual behavior served as an increasingly powerful reinforcement mechanism for whole milk consumption among agents who maintained their incumbent choice. Despite the aggregate historical shift toward skimmed and semi-skimmed alternatives during the 1974–2005 period, the habit formation dynamics—with initial incumbent habit strength set at 10.0 and information retention across a 5-step memory window—produced progressively entrenched behavioral patterns for continuing whole milk consumers. The 5% growth in the habit multiplier suggests that psychological inertia through habit accumulation created an escalating barrier to change, requiring stronger countervailing influences from health perception advantages or social network pressures to overcome existing consumption patterns.

The change point at step 32 marks the culmination of this reinforcement trajectory, while underlying oscillation patterns with a dominant period of 15 steps indicate cyclical variations in habit strength that nonetheless conform to the dominant increasing trend.
