The mean choice function deviation exhibits a monotonic increase throughout the 33-step simulation period, beginning at a value of 0.0000 and terminating at 0.7508, representing the evolution from complete homogeneity to substantial heterogeneity in agent choice functions.

The progression occurs in three distinct phases characterized by markedly different growth rates. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first seven steps, the metric undergoes rapid acceleration with the steepest observed slope of 0.128, ascending sharply from 0 to approximately 0.60. This period corresponds to the initial disruption of uniform consumption patterns established in 1974, as agents begin processing health and environmental information and overcome incumbent habits favoring whole milk.

Following the first inflection point at step 7, the trajectory enters a prolonged transitional phase lasting through step 28, where growth continues but at substantially decelerated rates between 0.004 and 0.009. During this interval, the value advances from roughly 0.60 to approximately 0.73. The reduced velocity reflects the increasing influence of habit formation and social network structures as agents consolidate their preferences. Secondary inflection points at steps 12 and 28 demarcate subtle shifts in the curvature of this deceleration, indicating progressive stabilization of choice functions within the Watts-Strogatz network topology.

The final phase from step 28 to 32 shows the metric approaching asymptotic behavior as it reaches 0.7508, with the shallowest growth rates observed in the simulation. The aggregate mean across all time steps is 0.6323, with a median of 0.6729 and standard deviation of 0.1460, confirming the distribution is skewed toward higher deviation values characteristic of mature market diversification. Statistical testing confirms significantly increasing trends (p=0.027) across all observation windows, though the Mann-Kendall tau values remain constant at 1.0 while slopes diminish, indicating consistent directional change despite slowing momentum.

These dynamics suggest that consumer preference divergence follows a predictable trajectory: an initial rapid fragmentation of uniform behaviors driven by disposition mechanisms and perception of alternatives, followed by a slower consolidation period where individual habits and social influence solidify distinct consumption niches. The substantial terminal deviation of approximately 75% between maximum and minimum choice function scores indicates that the simulated population does not converge on a single dominant preference but rather maintains persistent diversity in milk consumption patterns.

For decision makers, these findings imply that interventions targeting dietary transitions face distinct strategic windows. The early simulation phase represents a critical period where policy measures can most effectively influence rapid behavioral change, while later phases require differentiated approaches acknowledging stable consumer segments. The enduring high deviation suggests that universal recommendations may prove less effective than targeted messaging addressing specific choice function profiles, and that market structures should accommodate sustained variety rather than expecting full standardization around skimmed or semi-skimmed alternatives.
