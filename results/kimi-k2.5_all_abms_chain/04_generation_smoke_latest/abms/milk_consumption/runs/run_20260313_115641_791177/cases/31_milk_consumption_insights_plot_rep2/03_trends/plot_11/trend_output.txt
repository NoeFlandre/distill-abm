The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the implied health values of their milk choices commences at approximately zero at the initial time step and increases monotonically throughout the 32-step simulation period, reaching terminal values ranging from 0.70 to 0.75. The trajectories remain tightly clustered across all simulation runs, indicating high consistency in the behavioral dynamics.

Three distinct temporal phases characterize this progression. During the initial phase spanning steps 0 through 10, the deviation increases rapidly with an approximately linear trajectory, rising sharply from 0 to roughly 0.50. This represents the most dramatic expansion of the gap between health perceptions and actual consumption behaviors.

In the second phase from approximately step 10 to step 25, the rate of increase attenuates substantially. The deviation continues to climb but at a markedly reduced pace, progressing from roughly 0.50 to approximately 0.58-0.60. This middle period exhibits a plateau-like character with minimal variance between runs, suggesting a temporary equilibrium in the divergence between values and choices.

The third phase from step 25 to step 32 shows renewed acceleration, with the deviation increasing from approximately 0.60 to the final values of 0.70-0.75. Around steps 26-27, a brief perturbation occurs where the deviation momentarily stabilizes or decreases slightly before resuming its steep upward trajectory through the conclusion of the simulation.

Within the context of the milk consumption transition model, this steadily increasing deviation indicates that as the simulation progresses from 1974 to 2005, agents' actual milk choices become progressively misaligned with their personal health valuations. The initial rapid divergence corresponds to the early transition period when agents begin shifting from whole milk to reduced-fat varieties, creating immediate tension between existing habits and health information. The attenuated middle phase reflects a period where habit strength and social influence temporarily stabilize choices despite ongoing cognitive dissonance. The final acceleration suggests that once social norms solidify around reduced-fat milk, social influence and habit formation increasingly override personal health assessments, driving choices that diverge further from individual health values.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that health messaging becomes increasingly decoupled from consumption behavior over time as habit and social influence dominate. Interventions targeting dietary change may be most effective during the initial transition window before habits crystallize, and policy strategies should leverage social influence mechanisms rather than relying solely on health information campaigns, particularly as social norms become entrenched and drive behaviors that increasingly override personal health valuations.
