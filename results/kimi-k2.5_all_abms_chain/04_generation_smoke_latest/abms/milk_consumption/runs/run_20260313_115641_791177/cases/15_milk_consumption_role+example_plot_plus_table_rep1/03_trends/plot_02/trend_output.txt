The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at 5.29 units in the initial time step, reflecting the market conditions of 1974 when whole milk dominated consumer preferences. Over the first ten time steps, consumption increases rapidly and substantially, reaching approximately 1,265 units by step 11. This early acceleration suggests a period of active habit disruption where the incumbent preference for whole milk, initially reinforced by high habit strength, begins to weaken as agents process health and environmental attribute information and respond to early social influence signals.

Between time steps 11 and 25, the trajectory enters a plateau phase characterized by oscillation around a stable level of approximately 1,200 to 1,240 units. Statistical validation confirms this stabilization, with Mann-Kendall tests indicating no significant trend during windows spanning steps 10-14, 15-19, and 20-24. This middle period likely represents a temporary market equilibrium where the rate of new adoption slows as the population of consumers willing to break habits through threshold-based or probabilistic mechanisms becomes saturated. The oscillations observed at indices 11-13, 18-21, and 23 suggest minor fluctuations driven by social network dynamics and periodic reassessment of consumption choices within the Watts-Strogatz network structure.

Following time step 25, consumption resumes a significant upward trajectory, increasing from approximately 1,220 units to reach 1,456.6 units by the final time step 32. The Mann-Kendall test for the window spanning steps 25-29 confirms this late-stage increase as statistically significant. This secondary growth phase may reflect the consolidation of new social norms around reduced-fat milk consumption, where the accumulated effect of social influence, justification mechanisms, and cognitive dissonance resolution among late adopters finally overcomes residual habit strength. The progression from initial rapid adoption through a prolonged plateau to final consolidation successfully captures the historical curvature of the British milk transition, including the crossover point where alternative milk varieties achieved market dominance.
