The simulation reveals a pronounced non-linear decline in mean whole milk consumption over the 32 time steps, with consumption falling from approximately 2,650–2,700 units at initialization to roughly 200–250 units by the conclusion.

The trajectory exhibits five distinct phases. During the initial phase (Steps 0–10), consumption undergoes a steep decline of approximately 60%, dropping from the initial high values to around 950–1,050 units. This rapid erosion reflects the initial disruption of strong incumbent habits (strength 10.0) as agents begin processing information about alternative milk attributes and responding to early social signals, despite the moderate initial perceptions of alternatives (health perception 1.69, environmental perception 1.12).

The second phase (Steps 10–18) shows marked deceleration, with consumption declining gradually from approximately 1,000 units to 750 units. This plateau suggests that early adopters have already switched, while the majority of agents remain locked in habitual behavior above the habit threshold of 2.0, resisting change even as perceptions evolve through memory accumulation (memory lifetime 5.0) and limited social interactions (probability 0.3).

During the third phase (Steps 18–25), the decline resumes at a moderate pace, falling from 750 to approximately 600 units. This period represents the gradual erosion of habitual dominance as social influence mechanisms (susceptibility 0.2) and normative pressures (conformity weight 0.28) begin to overcome individual resistance, particularly among agents whose habit strength has decayed below the critical threshold.

The fourth phase (Steps 25–28) exhibits a second sharp decline, with consumption dropping precipitously from 600 to roughly 300–350 units. This acceleration indicates a critical tipping point where social norms have shifted decisively against whole milk, creating a cascade effect as the remaining holdouts face intensified social pressure and the normalization of alternative milk consumption, overcoming the social blindness parameter (0.51).

In the final phase (Steps 28–32), the decline continues but at a decelerating rate, stabilizing between 200–250 units. This residual consumption represents a hard core of agents whose habit strength remains resilient above the threshold or who possess idiosyncratic preferences that resist social influence, indicating that whole milk persists as a niche choice even after the market transformation.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The pronounced multi-phase pattern suggests that market transitions in habitual consumer goods follow predictable dynamics, with initial rapid change driven by information dissemination, followed by a protracted middle period (approximately Steps 10–25) requiring sustained intervention to overcome habit inertia, and culminating in a tipping point where social norm shifts drive rapid completion of the transition. Policymakers and marketers should anticipate that the most resource-intensive phase occurs in the middle period, where habit strength peaks and social influence has not yet reached critical mass, suggesting that interventions should be intensified during this window rather than at the beginning or end of the transition period. Additionally, the residual 200–250 units indicates that some market segments will remain resistant to change regardless of social pressure, suggesting that complete market transformation may be unrealistic and resources should be allocated efficiently toward the movable middle rather than holdout segments.
