The trajectory from steps 10 to 20 shows continued but decelerated decline, with consumption decreasing from approximately 1,000 to 750 units. Statistical analysis reveals significant monotonic decreasing trends in rolling windows covering steps 0-4, 5-9, 10-14, and 20-24, with the steepest rate of decline occurring between steps 5-9 at approximately 221 units per step. Conversely, the intervals spanning steps 15-19 and 25-29 exhibit no statistically significant trends, indicating temporary stabilization phases within the broader decline.

From step 20 to the conclusion at step 32, consumption continues to decrease, reaching approximately 253 units. This terminal value represents a total decline of roughly 90% from the initial state. The series exhibits multiple inflection points at steps 5, 13, 16, 19, 24, 25, 27, and 30, marking transitions in the velocity of decline. A notable change point occurs at step 32, coinciding with the minimum observed value.

The pattern reflects the interplay of high initial habit strength for whole milk consumption, disposition mechanisms triggering consideration of alternatives, and social influence propagation through the network topology. The steep early decline suggests that once agents overcome initial habit thresholds, the combination of health and environmental perceptions drives rapid abandonment of whole milk. The intermittent stabilization phases indicate periods where remaining consumers exhibit resistance to change or where social influence effects temporarily plateau.

For decision makers, these results indicate that transitions away from established dietary habits can proceed rapidly once critical behavioral thresholds are crossed, but the process may exhibit non-linear dynamics with intermittent plateaus. The substantial decline within the first third of the time period suggests that early intervention efforts can leverage network effects for accelerated impact, while later phases require sustained efforts to influence resistant subpopulations. The model demonstrates that habit formation parameters and social network structure significantly influence the velocity and pattern of dietary transitions.
