The metric measuring the mean deviation between agents' environmental values and their actual milk choices exhibits a characteristic S-curve trajectory over the 33-step simulation period. Beginning at a baseline of 0.0000, the deviation undergoes rapid escalation during the initial phase, reaching approximately 12.7 by step 5. This acceleration continues at a decelerated rate through step 9, where the value approaches 13.4, before attaining a maximum value of 13.4756 at step 13. Subsequently, the metric stabilizes, maintaining values between 13.4 and 13.5 for the remaining duration, concluding at 13.4186 by step 32. The overall series maintains a mean of 12.6015 and median of 13.4302 across all steps.

Statistical analysis reveals distinct behavioral regimes. The initial five steps demonstrate a significantly increasing trend with a steep slope of 2.840, indicating rapid emergence of misalignment between environmental attitudes and consumption behaviors. From steps 5 through 9, while still significantly increasing, the rate slows considerably to a slope of 0.109. After step 10, the series exhibits no statistically significant trend, suggesting the system has reached equilibrium. Minor oscillations characterize the plateau phase, with peak values occurring at steps 13, 21, 19, 27, and 30, and slight valleys interspersed, reflecting ongoing micro-adjustments in agent decision-making without altering the aggregate deviation level.

Within the context of milk consumption transition dynamics, this pattern indicates that as agents begin with incumbent whole milk preferences aligned with initial low environmental value deviations, the introduction of alternative milk varieties triggers a swift divergence between held environmental values and actual choices. This gap widens substantially within the first decade (steps 0-10), after which habitual consumption patterns and social network effects lock in a persistent high-deviation state throughout the remainder of the simulation period.

For decision makers seeking to promote environmentally consistent consumption behaviors, the rapid establishment of deviation during early steps suggests that intervention windows must occur at the onset of behavioral transition, before the plateau phase entrenches the attitude-behavior gap. The persistence of high deviation values throughout the stabilization phase indicates that once established, misalignment between environmental values and milk choices becomes resistant to spontaneous correction through existing social influence and habit formation mechanisms parameterized in the model.
