The simulation reveals a consistent monotonic increase in the mean deviation between agents' held health values and the values implied by their milk choices throughout the 1974–2005 period. Beginning at a state of perfect alignment with a deviation of 0.0000 in 1974, the metric rises steadily to reach approximately 0.7251 by 2005, indicating a substantial and growing misalignment between consumer health beliefs and actual consumption behaviors over the simulation horizon.

The trajectory exhibits distinct phases of acceleration and moderation. Early in the simulation, notable inflections occur around 1978 and 1980, where the rate of divergence initially accelerates as agents begin receiving health information favoring low-fat alternatives while maintaining established consumption habits. This early period reflects the initial tension between emerging health consciousness and incumbent behavioral patterns.

During the mid-period, roughly spanning 1990 to 1993, the rate of increase moderates somewhat, suggesting a temporary stabilization as social influence mechanisms and habit thresholds partially buffer agents against rapid behavioral change despite continued exposure to health messaging. However, the divergence resumes its upward trajectory through the late 1990s, with significant inflections around 1996 and again approaching 2002, indicating that the cumulative effect of social network dynamics and sustained health information eventually overwhelms initial habit resistance, though not sufficiently to reverse the growing gap between beliefs and behaviors.

For decision makers, these trends suggest that information dissemination alone proves insufficient to align consumer behaviors with health preferences, as habit formation and social network structures create substantial inertia. Interventions targeting habit disruption during early adoption phases—particularly around the late 1970s and early 1980s in this historical context—could prove more effective than late-stage information campaigns. Furthermore, the persistent divergence throughout the 1990s indicates that policy mechanisms must account for social conformity effects, as agents appear to delay behavioral transitions despite personal health convictions, suggesting that leveraging social influence rather than merely combating it may accelerate adoption of preferred health behaviors.
