The mean value health deviation initiates at a baseline of 0.0000 and exhibits a strictly monotonic increase throughout the 33-step simulation period, ultimately reaching 0.7251. This trajectory is characterized by significant non-linearity, with the overall series mean of 0.4965 falling below the median of 0.5584, indicating that lower values concentrated in early steps exert downward pressure on the central tendency while the distribution skews toward higher deviations in later periods. The temporal progression reveals distinct kinematic regimes demarcated by inflection points at steps 4, 6, 16, 19, 22, 28, and 31, which signal alterations in the rate of divergence between agents' held health valuations and the health characteristics implied by their milk selections.

The initial regime (steps 0–6) demonstrates the most rapid acceleration, with a slope of approximately 0.063 per step, reflecting an immediate and sharp decoupling of health perceptions from consumption behaviors as agents encounter health information regarding alternative milk varieties while maintaining strong incumbent habits for whole milk. Following this explosive opening, the growth rate attenuates considerably during the middle phase (steps 10–19), where the slope diminishes to roughly 0.003–0.012 per step, suggesting a temporary quasi-equilibrium where habitual inertia partially stabilizes the divergence even as social influence mechanisms propagate information through the network. This plateau phase corresponds to a period where memory constraints (lifetime of 5 steps) and habit thresholds create resistance to immediate behavioral adjustment despite accumulating health information.

A secondary acceleration emerges after step 25, with the slope increasing to 0.019 per step through the terminal window (steps 25–29), driving the final values toward their maximum. This late-stage intensification likely captures the critical historical transition period where sustained social influence probabilities (0.3) and conformity pressures (0.28) eventually overcome the incumbent habit strength (10.0) for resistant agents, maximizing the observed deviation between internal health valuations and actual choice patterns. The oscillatory component with a dominant period of 15 steps superimposed upon the trend suggests cyclical reassessment patterns potentially linked to memory renewal cycles or network interaction rhythms.

Across all simulation runs, variance remains minimal at initialization but expands to a standard deviation of 0.1702 by conclusion, reflecting stochastic divergence in how individual agents' psychological thresholds and network positions process the transition. The presence of a change point at step 32, combined with the terminal value representing the series maximum, indicates that the system has not achieved equilibrium by the simulation's end, but rather approaches a critical transition point where health-behavior misalignment peaks. The consistent significant increasing trend across all rolling Mann-Kendall windows (τ = 1.000, p = 0.027) confirms that the pressure toward deviation is unidirectional and statistically robust throughout the historical transition period from 1974 to 2005, never experiencing a reversal despite varying rates of intensification.
