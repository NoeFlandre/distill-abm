This pattern indicates that the British milk market transitioned from a state of high consensus, where whole milk dominated due to uniform habitual preferences, to a fragmented market characterized by distinct consumer segments with highly heterogeneous valuation structures. The early rapid divergence (1974–1981) corresponds to the initial disruption of uniform habits as health and environmental information began penetrating the social network, causing some agents to rapidly reweight their choice functions while others remained anchored to traditional preferences. The mid-1980s plateau suggests a period where social influence effects temporarily stabilized the population into early and late adopter categories before further differentiation occurred. By the turn of the millennium, the high divergence levels indicate that agents had developed widely varying internal decision weights, with some maintaining strong whole milk preferences and others having fully transitioned to valuing skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties.
