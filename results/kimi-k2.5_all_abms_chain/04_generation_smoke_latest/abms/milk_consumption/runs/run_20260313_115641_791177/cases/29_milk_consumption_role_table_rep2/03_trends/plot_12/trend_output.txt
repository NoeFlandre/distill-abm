The simulation of British milk consumption dynamics reveals a pronounced divergence between consumers' environmental values and their actual milk selections over the 31-year period. At initialization (time step 0), the deviation metric registers 0.0000, indicating perfect alignment between agents' held environmental values and their milk choices. This corresponds to the 1974 baseline where whole milk dominated consumption and environmental considerations were presumably consistent with prevailing consumption patterns.

During the initial four time steps, the deviation increases rapidly with a steep trajectory, reaching approximately 11.36 by time step 4. This sharp ascent signifies that agents' environmental values shift substantially faster than their actual consumption behaviors, creating a growing value-action gap as consumers begin recognizing environmental attributes of milk alternatives while remaining locked into habitual whole milk consumption.

From time step 5 through time step 13, the deviation continues to increase but at a markedly reduced rate, eventually reaching its maximum value of 13.4756 at time step 13. This represents the peak discrepancy between environmental values and actual choices within the simulation.

Following this peak, the metric enters a prolonged plateau phase characterized by high-frequency, low-amplitude oscillations around approximately 13.4 to 13.5 for the remainder of the simulation (time steps 13 through 32). Minor local peaks occur at time steps 19, 21, 27, and 30, with corresponding valleys at time steps 18, 20, 26, 29, and 31, though these fluctuations remain within a narrow band. The simulation concludes at time step 32 with a value of 13.4186, indicating that the substantial value-action gap persists through the end of the study period in 2005.

This trajectory suggests that while British consumers rapidly developed environmental concerns regarding milk production over the simulation period, habitual mechanisms—with an initial incumbent habit strength of 10.0 and a maintenance threshold of 2.0—created substantial inertia that prevented consumption patterns from fully aligning with these evolving values. The persistent plateau throughout the latter two-thirds of the simulation indicates that social influence mechanisms and habit formation maintained a stable, high level of cognitive dissonance among agents, with the psychological mechanisms for habit breaking proving insufficient to close the gap between environmental values and actual milk selections before 2005.
