The mean habit factor for whole milk begins at a neutral value of 1.000 and exhibits a consistent, monotonic increase throughout the 32-step simulation period, terminating at approximately 1.050. This steady upward trajectory indicates that habitual reinforcement of whole milk consumption strengthens progressively over time across the agent population.

The growth pattern displays two notable inflection phases. During the initial seven steps, the habit factor accelerates rapidly from the baseline, suggesting an initial phase where early repeated consumption decisions quickly solidify into behavioral patterns. Between steps 7 and 12, the rate of increase moderates slightly, potentially representing a consolidation phase where habits become more entrenched but require continued reinforcement to grow. After step 12, the increase resumes a steady linear progression through the remainder of the simulation, indicating sustained habitual reinforcement without saturation.

This persistent increase in the habit factor implies that, within the modeled social and cognitive framework, whole milk consumption becomes increasingly automatic and resistant to change as agents accumulate consumption history. The absence of decline or stabilization suggests that counter-pressures, such as health information or social norms favoring alternatives, are insufficient to disrupt habit formation within the simulated timeframe.

For decision makers seeking to influence dietary patterns, these results indicate that interventions targeting whole milk consumption must be implemented early in the behavioral timeline, as habits strengthen continuously and become progressively more difficult to modify. Once established beyond the initial phases, whole milk consumption patterns appear to achieve considerable inertia, suggesting that prevention of habitual formation may be more effective than attempting to disrupt established routines. Public health strategies should prioritize early disruption of initial consumption patterns before the habit factor exceeds 1.02, after which the rate of habit consolidation, while steady, becomes deeply embedded in the agent decision-making architecture.
