The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits a trajectory marked by initial stability followed by sustained, gradually decelerating growth across the 33-step simulation period representing 1974 to 2005. The metric initiates at a baseline value of 1.0000, indicating neutral habit influence at the start. During the first five steps, values remain effectively flat, registering a minor peak of 1.0205 at step 1 before declining slightly to a valley of 1.0167 at step 5. This early plateau suggests that habitual tendencies toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties remain weak immediately following the introduction of choice alternatives, with initial adoption driven by non-habitual factors such as health perception or environmental concerns.

Between steps 5 and 12, the series undergoes a marked transition characterized by accelerating increase. Statistical analysis identifies inflection points at steps 3 and 12, with the latter marking the transition from concave to convex growth patterns. The most rapid expansion occurs between steps 10 and 14, where the trend slope reaches 0.030 units per step. During this phase, values climb from approximately 1.07 to 1.24, representing a 15.9% increase in habit strength over just five steps. This acceleration indicates that as initial adoption thresholds are crossed, positive feedback mechanisms in habit formation begin to dominate individual decision-making processes.

From step 15 onward, the growth rate decelerates progressively while remaining statistically significant and positive throughout. Slope coefficients decline sequentially from 0.027 (steps 15-19) to 0.022 (steps 20-24) and finally to 0.018 (steps 25-29), though all trends remain significant at the p=0.027 level. The metric achieves a final value of 1.5879 at step 32, representing a 58.8% cumulative increase from baseline. Notable oscillations emerge in later steps (particularly indices 26 through 30), suggesting increased variability in habit strength as values approach saturation levels across the agent population.

Throughout the entire series, cross-simulation consistency remains high, with a mean value of 1.2615 and median of 1.2578 indicating symmetric distribution of outcomes across the 30 replication runs. The trajectory demonstrates that habit formation for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk operates as a cumulative, self-reinforcing process that builds momentum approximately one-third through the simulation period before gradually stabilizing.

For decision makers, these dynamics imply that interventions targeting milk consumption transitions should account for a significant time lag before habit becomes a self-sustaining driver of behavior. The 5-10 step immature phase represents a critical window where alternative disposition mechanisms (health messaging or social norms) must compensate for weak habitual drivers. Once step 12 is surpassed, the self-reinforcing nature of habit suggests that policy efforts can shift toward consolidation rather than active promotion, as behavior becomes increasingly 'locked in' by routine. The persistent growth throughout the simulation indicates that habit strength never fully saturates, suggesting long-term potential for further consolidation of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk preferences even after three decades of market transition.
