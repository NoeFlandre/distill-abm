The mean weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5 units and exhibits a three-phase trajectory over 33 time steps, ultimately reaching roughly 1,457 units.

The first phase comprises rapid growth from step 0 through step 11, where the metric increases from 5.29 to a local maximum of 1,265.47. This represents an average increase of over 100 units per step during the steepest ascent. Statistical validation confirms this upward trajectory, with Mann-Kendall tests indicating significant monotonic increases during intervals 0-4 and 5-9.

Following this initial expansion, the series enters a prolonged plateau phase spanning steps 12 through 27. During this 16-step interval, values oscillate between approximately 1,183 and 1,265, exhibiting multiple local peaks at steps 13 (1,240.35), 19 (1,221.57), and 21 (1,226.86), interspersed with valleys at steps 12 (1,233.62), 18 (1,183.39), and 20 (1,214.46). The Mann-Kendall tests for windows 10-14, 15-19, and 20-24 show no significant trend, confirming the stagnation characteristic of this phase.

The final phase initiates at step 27, where the metric breaks from the plateau at approximately 1,240 and accelerates upward to the terminal value of 1,456.60 at step 32. This represents a gain of roughly 216 units over five steps. The Mann-Kendall test for interval 25-29 confirms this late-stage growth as statistically significant.

Within the context of milk adoption behaviors, the initial rapid growth corresponds to the early diffusion phase where consumer awareness and health perceptions drive swift transitions from whole milk to lower-fat alternatives. The subsequent plateau reflects market saturation among early adopters and the establishment of stable consumption habits, where social influence and habit strength create resistance to further change. The late-stage resurgence suggests the activation of previously resistant consumer segments or the amplification of social norms that overcome initial disposition barriers.

For decision makers, the distinct phases indicate that intervention strategies should be temporally differentiated. The early phase (steps 0-11) offers maximal leverage for rapid adoption campaigns, while the plateau phase (steps 12-27) requires targeted mechanisms to disrupt habit persistence and social conformity. The late growth phase (steps 27-32) presents opportunities for capturing residual market segments through intensified social influence campaigns.
