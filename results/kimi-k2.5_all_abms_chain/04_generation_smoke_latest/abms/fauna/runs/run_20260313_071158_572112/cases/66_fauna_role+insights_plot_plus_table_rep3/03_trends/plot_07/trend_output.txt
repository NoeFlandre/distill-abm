Cumulative mortality of megaherbivore populations exhibits a characteristic saturation pattern across experimental replicates. Beginning from zero mortality at initialization, death counts increase rapidly during the initial approximately 400 to 500 time steps, with the aggregate mean reaching roughly 150 to 200 deaths by step 500. This initial phase corresponds to the period of highest hunting vulnerability as the naive population encounters human foragers.

Between approximately step 500 and step 1200, the rate of mortality accumulation decelerates markedly, with cumulative deaths rising from approximately 200 to between 230 and 235. Statistical inflection points detected around step 362 and step 1241 delineate these transitions from exponential-like growth to linear slowing, and finally to asymptotic approach.

After approximately step 1200 to 1500, cumulative mortality stabilizes in a plateau phase, with mean values hovering between 235 and 240 deaths for the remainder of the simulation period, which extends to approximately step 2312 in the aggregate series. Individual replicates demonstrate variability in final cumulative mortality, ranging from approximately 225 to 250 deaths, and in the timing of plateau onset, with some series stabilizing as early as step 1000 while others continue gradual accumulation until step 1800 or beyond.

This pattern indicates that under the specified hunting pressure and environmental conditions, the initial cohort of 250 animals experiences near-total mortality turnover, with approximately 94 to 96 percent of individuals eventually dying from the combined effects of hunting, starvation, and senescence within the simulated temporal horizon. The early saturation of cumulative mortality suggests that hunting pressure rapidly depletes the vulnerable population, after which the system enters a low-mortality steady state characterized by small surviving subpopulations or refugia effects.

For decision makers, these results highlight the critical importance of early intervention in managing human-wildlife interactions. The rapid accumulation of mortality during the initial phase demonstrates that delayed conservation action may result in irreversible population declines before management measures can take effect. Once cumulative mortality exceeds approximately 80 percent of the initial population, the system enters a depleted state from which recovery may be difficult even with reduced hunting pressure. Monitoring programs should prioritize the early detection of hunting impacts, as the window for effective intervention appears to close within the first 500 to 1000 time steps of sustained hunting pressure.
