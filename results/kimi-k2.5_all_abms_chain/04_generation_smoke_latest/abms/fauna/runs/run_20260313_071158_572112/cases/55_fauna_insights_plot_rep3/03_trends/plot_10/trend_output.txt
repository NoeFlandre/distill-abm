Across all simulation runs, the total prey population exhibits a consistent downward trajectory from an initial value of approximately 250 animals, with every run converging toward extinction or near-extinction conditions. The population decline is monotonic but characterized by substantial variation in the rate of decrease across different runs. The most rapid declines reduce populations from 250 to fewer than 10 individuals within 400 to 500 steps, while the most gradual declines extend the persistence of small populations (1–50 individuals) for 2000 or more steps before reaching similarly depleted levels.

During the initial phase (approximately steps 0–400), populations experience steep declines, with most runs falling below 100 animals and many dropping below 50 animals within this window. As populations diminish below approximately 50 individuals, the rate of decline typically moderates, and many runs enter a prolonged persistence phase where small numbers of animals (ranging from 1 to 25 individuals) survive for extended periods. By step 750–1000, the majority of runs have declined to populations below 25 animals. Beyond step 1500, nearly all trajectories reach zero or near-zero levels (0–5 animals), with only isolated runs maintaining trace populations through step 2200.

This universal trend toward extinction, combined with high variability in the timing of population collapse, indicates that the model configuration—which includes 20 immortal foragers with food storage capabilities operating in a seasonal environment with 33% grass cover—generates sustained hunting pressure that eventually overwhelms prey population resilience. The stochastic variation in extinction timing (spanning a factor of five or more in duration) suggests that demographic and spatial factors, such as the distribution of remaining prey patches, hunter encounter probabilities, and grass regrowth dynamics in the seasonal cycle, significantly influence the pace of decline even though the eventual outcome remains consistent.

**Implications for Decision Makers**: The results demonstrate that sustained hunting pressure from a fixed number of efficient foragers (who do not die and can store food) reliably drives megaherbivore populations to extinction, though the time horizon may vary considerably due to stochastic environmental and demographic factors. For conservation or management applications, this suggests that even moderate numbers of persistent hunters can be sufficient to extirpate prey populations over timescales ranging from short-term collapse (hundreds of steps) to prolonged decline (thousands of steps). Interventions that reduce hunting pressure early in the decline phase (before populations drop below 50 individuals) would be critical, as the data indicates that once populations enter the small-persistence phase, recovery becomes increasingly unlikely and the population is vulnerable to stochastic extinction. The model also implies that natural grassland productivity (at 33% cover with seasonal constraints) is insufficient to buffer prey populations against this level of predation pressure.
