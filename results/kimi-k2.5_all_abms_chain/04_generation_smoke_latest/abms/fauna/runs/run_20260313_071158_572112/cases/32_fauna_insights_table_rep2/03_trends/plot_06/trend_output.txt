**Implications for Decision Makers**  
The pronounced lag between simulation initiation and reproductive collapse (approximately 1,600+ ticks) demonstrates significant extinction debt in harvested megafauna populations, wherein demographic stability masks unsustainable extraction for multiple years before sudden collapse. The critical change point at tick 1325—preceding the absolute peak by approximately 350 ticks—provides an early warning metric for intervention: reproductive trends diverged from sustainability thresholds well before absolute birth minima were observed.

The persistence of 50-tick seasonal oscillations throughout the decline suggests that temporary hunting moratoria during peak breeding seasons could serve as effective conservation interventions, even in populations under severe pressure. Finally, the divergence between peak (5.6) and terminal (1.0) reproductive values indicates that monitoring schemes tracking only population abundance may miss critical reproductive failures; managers should prioritize tracking rolling cumulative birth rates or recruitment indices to detect unsustainable regimes before irreversible demographic bottlenecks occur.
