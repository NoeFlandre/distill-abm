The total energy stored in male animal populations begins at approximately 12,500 units. It declines precipitously over the first 500 steps, dropping to values ranging between roughly 500 and 5,000 units. This initial rapid depletion reflects intensive hunting mortality as human foragers exploit the initially abundant male prey, which carry high food value relative to their processing costs. During this phase, the steepest trajectories lose over 75 percent of initial energy within the first 300 steps, while slower declining runs maintain above 8,000 units until nearly step 400 before accelerating downward.

Between steps 500 and 1,500, the rate of decline decelerates considerably, with energy levels gradually diminishing toward zero. This intermediate period exhibits substantial variation across simulation runs: some trajectories reach near-zero energy by step 600, while others maintain levels between 1,000 and 2,000 units until approximately step 1,000. A subset of simulations shows temporary stabilization or slight oscillations between steps 300 and 600, with energy fluctuating between 3,000 and 5,000 units before resuming decline, suggesting brief periods where hunting efficiency drops due to prey dispersion or where demographic recruitment momentarily offsets losses.

By step 2,000, virtually all trajectories converge at or very near zero total energy, though individual runs persist with trace amounts extending to 2,200 steps. The complete depletion of male energy stores indicates functional extinction of males from the population, resulting from the combination of sustained hunting pressure by immortal foragers, natural mortality including senescence effects after age 7,300 ticks, and insufficient recruitment through reproduction—constrained by 730-tick birth spacing intervals—to offset these combined losses. The high variance observed during the middle phase likely stems from stochastic variation in the spatial distribution of remaining herds and the non-linear feedback between declining prey density and hunter optimal foraging decisions.
