**Trend Analysis**

The cumulative mortality trajectories exhibit three distinct phases. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 400 to 600 time steps, mortality accumulates rapidly, with death counts rising steeply from 0 to between 200 and 240 individuals. This represents an average mortality rate of roughly 0.3 to 0.6 deaths per step during the peak accumulation period. By step 600, most replicates have recorded 80-96% of their total final mortality.

The second phase, occurring between steps 600 and 1000, shows a marked deceleration in mortality rates. During this interval, the accumulation of deaths slows considerably, with many trajectories adding only 10-30 additional deaths. The curves gradually flatten as they approach asymptotic values.

The third phase, beyond approximately step 1000-1500, demonstrates complete or near-complete stabilization, with cumulative death counts remaining static at final values ranging from approximately 225 to 250. Since the initial prey population was set at 250 individuals, this indicates total mortality ranging from 90% to 100% across all replicates.

Substantial temporal variation exists in the rate of mortality accumulation. Some simulations exhibit extremely rapid collapse, exceeding 200 cumulative deaths by step 400-500, while others show more gradual decline, not reaching 200 deaths until step 800 or later. One trajectory demonstrates notably delayed mortality, remaining below 50 deaths until approximately step 300, then rising steadily to converge with the 220-250 range by step 1000. 

**Interpretation in Context**

Given the model configuration with 20 immortal hunters possessing food storage capabilities and operating under dynamic optimal foraging theory, the consistent achievement of 90-100% prey mortality indicates systematic population collapse. The rapid early mortality phase corresponds to the period when hunters actively exploit the initially abundant prey population, with the processing cost differential between males (6) and females (3) and the higher food value of males (30 vs 27) likely influencing hunting selectivity during this phase.

The plateau after step 1000 suggests prey population exhaustion or extinction. Because hunters are immortal (imortal-foragers = True) and possess food storage, hunting pressure does not diminish as prey become scarce—a critical factor preventing prey population recovery. The high seasonality parameter (0.8) likely creates periodic resource bottlenecks that compound hunting pressure, accelerating mortality during low-resource periods.

The variation in trajectory timing reflects stochastic elements in the model, including initial hunter-prey spatial distribution, random movement patterns, and seasonal vegetation fluctuations. However, the convergence of all trajectories to 225-250 deaths indicates that these stochastic factors influence the speed of collapse but not its inevitability under the current parameter set.

The final mortality counts slightly below 250 in some replicates (225-240) may reflect prey individuals that survived until the simulation termination or died from baseline mortality mechanisms (internal-mortality = 0.025, external-mortality = 0.0025) rather than predation.

**Implications for Decision Makers**

The results suggest that the combination of immortal hunters with food storage, moderate hunting parties (20 individuals), and high environmental seasonality (0.8) creates an unsustainable predation scenario that consistently drives megaherbivore populations to collapse within approximately 5.5 years (2000 daily time steps). For conservation or management applications, this configuration demonstrates no sustainable equilibrium—stochastic variation only delays the inevitable extinction rather than preventing it. 

Interventions that would require evaluation include: (1) removing hunter immortality to create natural feedback loops where hunting success declines as prey become scarce, (2) reducing food storage capabilities to limit sustained hunting effort during prey bottlenecks, or (3) modifying seasonality parameters or vegetation regrowth rates (currently 3 ticks) to provide sufficient resource buffers that allow prey populations to withstand hunting pressure. The model suggests that without such modifications, the current system exhibits zero resilience, making population collapse a deterministic outcome regardless of stochastic variation in hunting encounters or environmental conditions.
