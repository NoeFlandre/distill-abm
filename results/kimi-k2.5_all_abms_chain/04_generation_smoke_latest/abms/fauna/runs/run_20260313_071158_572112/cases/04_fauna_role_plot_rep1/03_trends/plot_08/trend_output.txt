Across all simulation replicates, the male megaherbivore population exhibits a monotonic decline from initial conditions, ultimately reaching extinction in all observed trajectories. The population initiates at approximately 125 males and follows heterogeneous decline pathways characterized by substantial inter-run variation in extinction timing.

During the first 500 time steps, populations decline rapidly, with trajectories diverging significantly. By step 500, male counts range from near zero to approximately 60 individuals across different replicates, indicating high sensitivity to stochastic processes during the early phase of population reduction. The most rapid declines reach fewer than 10 males by step 400, while more gradual trajectories maintain 50-60 males at this temporal marker.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the decline continues for remaining populations, with the majority of replicates falling below 10 males by step 800. A subset of trajectories demonstrates more gradual attrition, maintaining small populations of 5-15 males through step 900-1000.

After step 1000, nearly all replicates reach zero males, though extreme outliers persist with 1-5 individuals until approximately steps 1500-2200. The terminal extinction of all male populations by the conclusion of the simulation runs indicates that the parameter configuration produces a deterministic outcome of population collapse, mediated by stochastic variation in the temporal dynamics of decline.

The consistent downward trajectory without recovery episodes suggests that hunting pressure from 20 optimal foragers with food storage capabilities, combined with strong environmental seasonality (0.8) and baseline mortality rates, generates per-capita mortality rates that exceed the maximum potential recruitment rate of the megaherbivore population throughout the simulation period. The pronounced variability in extinction timing (spanning roughly 300 to 2200 steps) reflects the importance of stochastic demographic processes and spatial dynamics in determining the specific trajectory of collapse, despite the inevitable outcome of male population extinction under these parameter settings.
