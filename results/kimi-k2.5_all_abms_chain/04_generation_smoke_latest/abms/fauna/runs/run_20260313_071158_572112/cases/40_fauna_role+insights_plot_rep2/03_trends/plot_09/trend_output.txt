The number of female animals initiates at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation replicates and follows a universal trajectory toward extinction under the specified parameter configuration. The temporal dynamics of collapse vary stochastically, with complete extinction occurring between step 400 and step 2100.

During the initial phase (steps 0-300), the population experiences rapid attrition, declining sharply to 40-60 individuals. This represents a loss rate of approximately 0.20-0.25 females per step, reflecting the immediate impact of 20 human foragers operating with optimal foraging strategies, baseline mortality rates (0.025 internal, 0.0025 external), and the constraints of the highly seasonal environment (seasonality = 0.8). By step 500, most populations have fallen below 20 individuals.

The decline continues through the middle phase (steps 300-800), though with considerable variation in the rate of decrease. Some populations reach zero by step 400-500, while others maintain 10-30 individuals until step 600-700. This divergence likely stems from stochastic variation in hunter-prey encounter rates, spatial heterogeneity in grass regrowth (regrowth-time = 3 ticks), and demographic stochasticity in the agent-based structure.

The terminal phase (steps 800-2200) completes the extinction vortex. While approximately half of the replicates reach zero by step 800, persistent populations maintain small numbers of 5-15 individuals until steps 1000-1500. The final extinction events occur as late as steps 1800-2100 in the most resilient replicates, resulting in a variability span of roughly 1700 steps between the fastest and slowest crashes. No populations stabilize or recover above 20 individuals once falling below this threshold.

Given the 365 ticks-per-year calibration, extinctions occur within temporal windows ranging from approximately 1.1 years to 5.7 years. With a birth-spacing of 730 ticks (2 years), the interbirth interval exceeds the extinction timeframe for most replicates, preventing reproductive compensation. The 0.8 seasonality setting creates strong resource pulses that likely contribute to the variability, as stochastic alignment of births with high-resource periods may temporarily slow decline in some runs, though insufficiently to prevent ultimate extinction.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The results demonstrate that 20 human foragers create unsustainable hunting pressure that invariably drives megaherbivore populations to extinction within 1-6 years under high environmental seasonality. The complete absence of stable coexistence suggests that current parameter combinations do not permit sustainable harvest. Management interventions would need to substantially reduce hunting pressure, reduce environmental seasonality, or enhance landscape productivity to prevent collapse. The wide stochastic variation in extinction timing (spanning 1700 steps) indicates that temporary population stability does not predict long-term persistence, requiring caution in interpreting short-term demographic data as evidence of population security.
