During the initial phase, spanning roughly the first 600 ticks, the rate of female harvest increases steeply, with significant behavioral transitions occurring at change points near ticks 125, 375, 475, and 625. Between approximately tick 600 and 1,600, the accumulation continues but at a markedly reduced rate, as evidenced by the declining trend slopes in successive analytical windows. The plateau indicates that either the female subpopulation has been depressed below the critical threshold where pursuit remains energetically viable under optimal foraging constraints, or the remaining females adopt refugia behaviors in less accessible portions of the grid.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The asymptotic approach to a cumulative harvest ceiling near 124 females suggests that sustainable extraction rates asymptote rapidly under these parameter configurations, with effective saturation occurring within approximately five annual cycles (given 365 ticks per year). The 63-tick oscillation period indicates that management interventions or monitoring efforts should account for predictable seasonal vulnerability windows when female herds concentrate around resource patches. The late-phase plateau implies that once cumulative female mortality reaches approximately 49% of the initial population (124/250), the system enters a state where additional hunting pressure fails to increase harvest yield, indicating either functional extinction of accessible females or adaptive behavioral thresholds in hunter decision-making. Conservation strategies should therefore prioritize limiting cumulative mortality before the deceleration phase begins around tick 600, as beyond this point the ecological damage accumulates while the per-tick harvest efficiency declines precipitously.
