The cumulative number of male megaherbivores killed by human foragers begins at zero and increases throughout the simulation, reaching a mean value of 117.5 by the end of the 2,312-tick period. The accumulation follows a markedly decelerating trajectory: an initial phase of rapid increase from 0 to approximately 100 kills occurs within the first 1,000 ticks, followed by a substantial slowdown in the rate of accumulation between ticks 1,000 and 1,200.

After tick 1,200, the cumulative count enters an extended plateau phase characterized by minimal net growth and high variability across simulation runs. The maximum mean value of 120.2 is reached at tick 1,876, after which the cumulative count exhibits a slight decline to the final value of 117.5. This late-stage decrease, confirmed by a significant negative trend (tau = -0.446) in the final temporal window (ticks 1,856–2,087), suggests that the mean across runs stabilizes and then marginally contracts as high-exploitation simulations conclude while lower-exploitation runs continue.

Substantial heterogeneity exists across the 80 simulation runs, with a standard deviation of 32.5 and final values ranging from 0 to 120.2. The median final count of 116.5 exceeds the mean of 100.4, indicating a left-skewed distribution where the majority of simulations resulted in high cumulative kill counts (110–120), while a subset of runs exhibited considerably lower exploitation rates, some terminating with fewer than 50 males killed.

Temporal trend analysis reveals statistically significant increasing trends throughout the first 1,159 ticks, though the slope of accumulation decreases monotonically from 0.173 in the initial window to 0.008 by tick 1,159, reflecting the approaching saturation of hunting returns. Between ticks 1,160 and 1,391, no significant trend is detected, followed by a brief resumption of significant increase (slope 0.002) between ticks 1,392 and 1,623. The system then enters a period of stability (ticks 1,624–1,855) before the final decreasing phase.

Oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks emerges in the later phase of the simulation (after tick 2,170), superimposed on the plateau pattern. Given the high seasonality parameter (0.8) configured in the model, these oscillations likely reflect cyclical variation in hunting success rates driven by seasonal resource availability and corresponding changes in prey vulnerability or hunter foraging efficiency. The plateau pattern overall suggests that the system reaches a state where the male megaherbivore population is either depleted to the point of scarcity, hunters switch to alternative prey categories such as females, or the remaining males adopt behavioral strategies that reduce their encounter rates with hunters.
