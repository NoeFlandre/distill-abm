Across the 40 simulation runs, the cumulative count of prey deaths follows a characteristic trajectory of rapid initial accumulation followed by saturation and eventual stabilization. During the first approximately 850 steps (roughly 2.3 years), mortality increases monotonically and significantly from zero to approximately 230 individuals. Statistical analysis reveals steep, consistently positive slopes ranging from 0.346 to 0.017 deaths per step throughout this initial phase, with notable rate transitions occurring at steps 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850. This phase reflects intensive hunting pressure from the initial cohort of 20 immortal foragers acting upon the starting population of 250 prey animals.

Between steps 850 and 1850, the rate of death accumulation decelerates substantially, entering a plateau phase where trends become statistically flat (no significant trend detected between steps 1160–1391 and 1624–1855). The cumulative death count reaches its maximum aggregate mean value of 240.33 at step 1876, indicating that nearly the entire initial prey population has been depleted. The median death count across all runs is 233, with a final mean of 235.00 observed at step 2312 (approximately 6.3 years).

In the terminal phase (steps 1850–2312), the aggregate mean exhibits a slight decreasing trend from the peak value down to 235, accompanied by low-amplitude oscillations (dominant period of 63 steps). This stabilization pattern, combined with the minimal variance in peak prominences, indicates that individual simulation runs are converging on maximum cumulative death counts between approximately 225 and 250 individuals.

**Interpretation and Implications**

The convergence of cumulative mortality on values approaching the initial population size (235–240 deaths from 250 initial prey) indicates that the parameterized hunting pressure is unsustainable and drives the prey population toward functional extinction within the simulated timeframe. Given that foragers are immortal and operate under optimal foraging constraints with energy storage, hunting pressure remains relentless and unbuffered by predator mortality. The two-year birth spacing (730 ticks) and high seasonality (0.8) appear insufficient to offset mortality rates, as the cumulative death count far exceeds what natural senescence (beginning at age 20) would produce in a 6-year period.

For decision makers, these results suggest that the current configuration of 20 immortal hunters invariably exhausts prey populations within 3–5 years, followed by a stagnant low-density equilibrium or collapse. To prevent extinction trajectories, managers should consider interventions that either reduce hunting pressure (e.g., hunter mortality, reduced forager numbers, or increased movement costs) or enhance prey resilience (e.g., reduced birth spacing, higher initial densities, or lower processing costs). The model demonstrates that under conditions of constant predation and environmental seasonality, megaherbivore populations lack the demographic capacity (given the 2-year interbirth interval) to withstand sustained hunting pressure, underscoring the vulnerability of slow-reproducing megafauna to persistent predation.
