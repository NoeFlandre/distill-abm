The aggregate energy available to forager bands exhibits a pronounced, sustained decline throughout the simulated period, initiating from a positive energy balance of 2000 units and terminating at approximately -38229 units after 2312 time steps. This trajectory represents a complete inversion from energy surplus to severe energetic deficit.

The decline proceeds through distinct phases marked by changes in the rate of energy loss. An initial phase spanning roughly the first 650 ticks shows a relatively moderate decrease, though punctuated by minor fluctuations, carrying the aggregate energy into negative territory by approximately tick 370. Following this, the system enters subsequent phases at roughly ticks 650, 1075, 1500, and 1925, where the rate of energy depletion intensifies substantially. The mean rate of decline stabilizes at approximately 20 energy units per tick during the intermediate and late phases, nearly double the initial rates observed in the earliest simulation periods.

Superimposed upon this long-term decline is a periodic oscillation with a dominant cycle of 63 ticks, particularly evident in the later stages of the simulation. This rhythmic pattern, manifesting between ticks 2170 and 2265 and likely extending throughout the series, reflects the strong environmental seasonality parameter creating pulsed availability of vegetation and consequently prey accessibility.

The energetic trajectory indicates that despite the capabilities of foragers to store food and avoid mortality from starvation, the collective energy balance deteriorates continuously. The progression from positive to deeply negative values suggests that foraging costs—comprising movement expenditures across the landscape and processing costs for captured prey—increasingly outweigh energy returns from hunting as prey populations decline. The accelerating rate of energy loss during middle phases likely corresponds to declining prey density forcing hunters to expend greater movement costs per unit of captured prey, creating a positive feedback loop of increasing energetic inefficiency until the system settles into a terminal phase of maximum depletion by tick 2310.
