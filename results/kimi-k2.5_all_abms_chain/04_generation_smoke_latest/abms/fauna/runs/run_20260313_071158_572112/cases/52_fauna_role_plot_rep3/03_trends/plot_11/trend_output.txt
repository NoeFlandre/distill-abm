The total energy contained in the male prey population initiates at approximately 12,000 to 12,500 energy units across all simulation replicates. This metric undergoes a steep, monotonic decline throughout the simulation period, ultimately collapsing to zero or near-zero values in all runs.

During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, total male energy decreases rapidly from ~12,500 to values predominantly ranging between 0 and 5,000. This interval exhibits the highest degree of inter-simulation variability, with some replicates falling below 1,000 energy units by step 500, while others maintain residual levels between 3,000 and 5,000.

Between steps 500 and 1,000, the decline continues with most simulations converging toward zero. By step 1,000, the majority of replicates have reduced male energy to below 500 units, with most approaching zero while a few maintain small residual pools between 100 and 500 units.

From step 1,000 to approximately step 1,500, nearly all simulations reach complete depletion of male energy. One outlier replicate maintains minimal residual energy below 500 units until approximately step 2,200, at which point it too reaches zero.

This consistent pattern of collapse across all replicates indicates that under the specified parameter configuration—with 20 human foragers applying continuous hunting pressure and males providing high nutritional returns (food-value-males = 30)—the male subpopulation experiences functional extinction within approximately 1,000 to 1,500 time steps (roughly 3-4 years given the 365 ticks-per-year calibration). The stepwise, staircase-like nature of the decline reflects discrete hunting mortality events where individual males are removed from the population. Despite the higher processing cost for males (6 units versus 3 for females), the combination of high food value and the presence of immortal foragers with food storage capabilities drives rapid depletion. The substantial variability in the timing of collapse during the intermediate phase (steps 300-800) suggests stochastic differences in hunter-prey encounter rates across simulation runs, but the eventual outcome of male population elimination is deterministic under these parameter settings. The persistence of minimal residual energy in one replicate until step 2,200 represents a scenario with lower encounter rates, yet even this delayed trajectory ultimately results in complete male energy depletion.
