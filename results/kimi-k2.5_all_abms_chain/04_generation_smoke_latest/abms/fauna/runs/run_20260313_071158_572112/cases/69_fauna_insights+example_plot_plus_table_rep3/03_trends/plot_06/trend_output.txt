Following this delay, reproduction commences around step 700, marked by inflection points at steps 613 and 733 and a significant change point at step 725. After step 1700, the trend reverses, with statistically significant decreasing slopes (Mann-Kendall tau = -0.495 and -0.453) observed between steps 1624-1855 and continuing through the remainder of the simulation. This decline in the aggregate metric likely reflects differential extinction dynamics across the simulation ensemble, wherein runs with higher cumulative reproductive output terminate due to hunting pressure or environmental constraints, leaving only runs with lower birth counts active by the conclusion.

The high variance in terminal values—ranging from herds that produced zero births to those achieving ten cumulative births—indicates substantial stochastic divergence in demographic trajectories under identical parameter settings. Key transition points at steps 1325, 1850, and 2050 demarcate shifts between increasing and decreasing birth accumulation rates, suggesting that herd persistence and reproductive success are nonlinear processes sensitive to feedback between hunting pressure and herd viability.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The extended initial period of reproductive inactivity (700+ steps) demonstrates that monitoring programs must account for long maturation and interbirth intervals when assessing megaherbivore population viability, as early absence of recruitment does not necessarily indicate system failure. The mid-simulation peak followed by aggregate decline warns against interpreting early reproductive success as indicative of long-term persistence, particularly under hunting pressure. Finally, the high variance across identical simulation conditions emphasizes the critical need for replicated experimental runs when evaluating extinction risk, as stochastic interactions between hunters and herds generate widely divergent outcomes that single-run analyses would fail to capture.
