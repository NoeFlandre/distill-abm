The total energy available in all prey animals begins at approximately 25,000 energy units across simulation runs. This initial value reflects the starting configuration of 250 prey herds with assigned food values for males (30 units), females (27 units plus additional value from nursing calves), and associated energy stores.

The system exhibits a pronounced declining trajectory toward extinction. Within the first 400 to 500 time steps, total energy undergoes a steep reduction, with most simulations falling below 5,000 energy units by step 500 and several approaching zero. This rapid depletion corresponds to initial hunting pressure from 20 foragers operating under the specified parameters, where hunters pursue prey based on optimal foraging calculations involving the moderate processing costs (3 for females, 6 for males) and movement costs of 1 unit per step.

Between steps 500 and 1,000, trajectories display high variability. Some runs reach near-zero energy by step 600, while others maintain between 2,000 and 8,000 energy units through step 800, exhibiting step-like plateaus before resuming decline. This divergence reflects stochastic variation in herd movement patterns across the 211×211 matrix, local grass depletion given the 0.33 initial grass proportion and 0.8 seasonality setting, and demographic fluctuations influenced by the 730-tick birth spacing and age of senescence at 7,300 ticks.

After approximately 1,200 steps, most simulations converge toward minimal energy values, though some persist with low residual energy (under 2,000 units) until 1,500 to 2,000 steps before final collapse. This temporary persistence suggests intermittent equilibrium conditions where hunting pressure momentarily balances with herd reproduction and grass regrowth, before ultimately overwhelming the population.

The complete depletion of energy to zero across all runs indicates that, under these parameter settings—including immortal foragers with food storage capability, the specified mortality rates (0.025 internal, 0.0025 external), and prey life history constraints—the hunting pressure creates a deterministic extinction dynamic despite stochastic variation in individual run trajectories.
