The cumulative mortality of postpartum female ungulates initiates at zero and follows a saturating growth trajectory across the temporal domain, ultimately converging at a mean terminal value of 117 fatalities per simulation, with individual realizations ranging between approximately 100 and 121. This progression manifests distinct kinetic regimes demarcated by quantitative change points and inflections that reflect the dynamics of predator-prey interaction under the specified energetic constraints.

During the initial phase spanning approximately 0 to 475 steps, the mortality accumulation exhibits explosive growth, advancing with maximum statistical intensity (Mann-Kendall tau = 1.000, slope ≈ 0.22 fatalities per step) through the first 695 steps. By the first structurally detected change point at step 475, the aggregate mortality has already accrued roughly 80–90 individuals, representing a substantial fraction of the eventual total. This rapid initial harvest indicates immediate, intense predation pressure on nursing females as the forager agents (initial population n=20) optimize their energy intake by targeting high-value reproductive individuals (food-value-females = 27) before significant prey depletion occurs.

Following the initial surge, the system enters a transition phase between steps 475 and 850, marked by additional change points at 625 and 850. Throughout this interval, the rate of accumulation decelerates precipitously; statistical slopes drop from 0.059 to 0.015 and finally to 0.003 fatalities per step. This deceleration corresponds to the depletion of the accessible postpartum female pool within the initial prey population of 250, forcing hunters to expend greater search effort (foraging-radius = 6) or target alternative demographic classes, thereby reducing encounter rates with reproductive females.

Beyond step 850, the cumulative mortality enters an asymptotic plateau phase. The mean series exhibits marginal increases (slope ≈ 0.001 in the 1160–1391 window) before stabilizing entirely (no significant trend detected between steps 1392 and 1623). The global maximum observed mean value of 119 fatalities occurs at step 1826, after which the series maintains a quasi-steady state. Late-stage oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 steps emerge after step 2170, reflecting minor stochastic fluctuations in the timing of final kills across the ensemble as individual simulations reach their respective asymptotes at different times.

Considerable variability exists across the 40 simulation runs, evidenced by a standard deviation of 30.0 and a left-skewed distribution where the mean (102.4) falls below the median (116.9). This dispersion indicates that while most trajectories saturate at high values (115–121), a subset of runs plateaus prematurely at lower cumulative counts (approximately 100–110) due to stochastic differences in hunter-prey encounter probabilities or spatial heterogeneity in vegetation distribution (grass-proportion = 0.33).

For decision-makers interpreting these results, the data demonstrate that postpartum females represent a finite, rapidly exhaustible resource under sustained hunting pressure, with approximately 70% of total exploitable mortality realized within the first quarter of the simulation period. The early saturation suggests that conservation interventions or management strategies targeting the protection of reproductive females would require immediate implementation at simulation onset, as the demographic window for effective intervention closes rapidly within the initial 500–600 steps. Furthermore, the asymptotic ceiling of roughly 120 individuals represents the maximum sustainable cumulative harvest from the initial population of 250 given the specific energetic costs and foraging efficiencies imposed on the agent populations.
