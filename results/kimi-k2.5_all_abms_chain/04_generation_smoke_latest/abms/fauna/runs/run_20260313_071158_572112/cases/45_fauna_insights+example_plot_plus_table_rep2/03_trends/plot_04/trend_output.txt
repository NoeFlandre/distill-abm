The cumulative number of female prey killed initiates at zero and exhibits pronounced logistic growth, accumulating rapidly to approximately 60–80 individuals within the first 375–475 time steps. This initial phase demonstrates the highest hunting efficiency, with accumulation rates exceeding 0.22 kills per step between steps 232 and 463, reflecting abundant prey availability and high encounter rates.  

Subsequent inflection points at steps 625, 850, and 1,600 mark progressive deceleration, with per-step accumulation rates declining from 0.06 to 0.002 as the cumulative count approaches 100–110 individuals by step 1,200. The system reaches a saturation point at approximately step 1,826, achieving a maximum cumulative kill count of 123.7 females, after which the trajectory stabilizes.  

Terminal values across simulation runs range from 108 to 124, with the aggregate mean stabilizing at 123 by step 2,312. Change points at steps 1,600 and 2,310 delineate the final transition to a stable plateau where net accumulation effectively ceases. Oscillations with a periodicity of 63 steps characterize the late-stage dynamics, though these represent minor fluctuations around the saturation level rather than directional trends.  

These dynamics indicate that a cohort of 20 hunters operating under optimal foraging constraints with food storage capabilities can extract approximately 120–124 female prey from an initial population of 250 individuals within roughly 2,000 time steps (approximately 5.5 years). The steep early accumulation followed by rapid deceleration and hard saturation suggests that prey populations face accelerating Allee effects or absolute depletion, wherein reduced prey density below critical thresholds eliminates hunting viability despite continued hunter presence.  

For researchers and resource managers, this pattern underscores that extinction trajectories may be nonlinear, with populations appearing stable during early hunting phases before collapsing abruptly as cumulative mortality erodes reproductive capacity. The 63-step oscillation cycle suggests seasonal or demographic periodicities influence kill rates even during population decline. Interventions must occur before the inflection point at approximately step 850, after which decelerating kill rates mask irreversible demographic damage and the population lacks sufficient breeding females to recover, leading inexorably to the observed plateau at 123 cumulative kills.
