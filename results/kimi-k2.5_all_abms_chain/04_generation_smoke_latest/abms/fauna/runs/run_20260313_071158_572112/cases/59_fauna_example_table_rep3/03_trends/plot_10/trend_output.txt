The population initiates at approximately 167 individuals and undergoes a protracted decline to extinction by tick 2311. The trajectory is characterized by three distinct phases of contraction punctuated by two brief demographic recoveries.

The first phase (ticks 0–475) exhibits a steep decline, with significant structural changes occurring at ticks 125 and 475. During this interval, the population experiences its most rapid attrition, likely driven by initial encounter rates between naive prey distributions and hunter foraging efforts, compounded by the immediate activation of movement costs and patchy grass resource distribution across the landscape.

From ticks 475–1391, the rate of decline decelerates substantially, transitioning into a prolonged trough characterized by oscillations with approximately 63-tick periodicity. These fluctuations reflect seasonal resource pulses or reproductive cycles (consistent with the two-year birth spacing parameter), where temporary resource availability generates minor demographic plateaus. However, the amplitude of these oscillations damps progressively as the population dwindles below critical thresholds necessary for herd maintenance and effective spatial escape from hunting pressure.

Between ticks 1392–1855, the dynamics shift to a low-density equilibrium characterized by volatility. Two discrete periods of positive growth emerge: first between ticks 1392–1623, and again between ticks 1856–2087, wherein populations briefly stabilize or increase toward local maxima of approximately 9–11 individuals. These ephemeral recoveries likely represent successful reproduction in spatiotemporal refuges where grass regeneration temporarily exceeds exploitation rates, or where hunter energy constraints and differential processing costs (higher for males) create temporary windows of reduced predation risk.

The final phase (post-tick 2087) culminates in irreversible collapse. Despite the theoretical 28-year maximum lifespan and senescence threshold at 20 years, the population declines monotonically to zero by tick 2311. This terminal extinction suggests that at low densities, Allee effects—potentially driven by mate-finding limitations or the breakdown of herd defensive behaviors—combined with sustained hunting pressure, overwhelm the life history buffering capacity of the megaherbivore population, preventing demographic recovery even when seasonal resources become available.
