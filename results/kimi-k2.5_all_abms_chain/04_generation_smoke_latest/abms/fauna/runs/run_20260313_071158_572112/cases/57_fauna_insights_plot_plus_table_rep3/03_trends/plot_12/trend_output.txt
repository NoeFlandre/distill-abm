The aggregate energy of female megaherbivore populations exhibits a deterministic extinction trajectory under the modeled parameter configuration, declining from an initial value of 12,500 energy units to complete depletion at step 2,311. The temporal dynamics unfold across three characteristic phases with distinct rates of change.

During the initial collapse phase (approximately steps 0–500), the population experiences precipitous energy loss, plummeting from 12,500 to roughly 2,000–4,000 units. Statistical change points at steps 375 and 500 mark transitions in the decline regime, with the steepest decay rates occurring in the first 231 steps (slope of -22.6 units per step) and continuing at -26.7 units per step through step 463. This rapid depletion reflects the immediate demographic impact of hunting pressure from 20 foragers operating under optimal foraging constraints upon the initial population of 250 prey, compounded by seasonal vegetation limitations.

The middle phase (steps 500–1,200) exhibits moderated decay rates punctuated by transient demographic rebounds. Values oscillate with a dominant period of approximately 63 steps, likely corresponding to seasonal resource cycles. Brief population recoveries emerge between steps 1,160–1,391 and again at 1,856–2,087, where energy levels temporarily stabilize or increase slightly (slopes of +0.019 and near-zero positive trends respectively). During these intervals, aggregate energy fluctuates between approximately 100 and 3,000 units, suggesting temporary equilibria where reduced hunting efficiency at low prey densities and intermittent reproductive pulses (governed by 730-step birth spacing intervals) allow marginal energy accumulation. However, these rebounds remain insufficient to reverse the overall trajectory.

The terminal phase (steps 1,200–2,311) witnesses progressive attrition to zero. Despite the brief increasing trend between steps 1,856–2,087, the population cannot overcome cumulative mortality pressures. Inflection points at steps 1,308, 1,407, 1,679, and 2,040 mark continuing transitions in decline rates. The final change point at step 2,310 precedes complete energy depletion at step 2,311. The median energy value of 100 units across the full temporal range indicates that the system spends the majority of its duration in a depleted state, substantially lower than the arithmetic mean of 1,569 units which reflects the influence of high initial conditions.

Rolling trend analysis confirms predominantly negative dynamics throughout, with significant decreasing trends (p < 0.001) persisting through step 1,159, followed by weaker but still significant declines through step 1,623. The coefficient of variation reveals high volatility relative to the mean, consistent with predator-prey oscillations dampening toward extinction.

**Implications for decision makers:**

The simulation results demonstrate that a moderate hunter-to-prey ratio (20:250) combined with large herbivore life history constraints (2-year birth intervals, 28-year maximum lifespan, and sexual dimorphism in food value) generates extinction dynamics within approximately 6.3 simulation years. The brief demographic rebounds observed during the middle phase create potential false signals of population resilience; however, the long-term trajectory remains definitively negative with zero probability of long-term persistence under these parameter conditions.

This suggests that reactive management strategies relying on natural population recovery are likely insufficient. The rapid initial decline (loss of 80% of initial energy within the first 500 steps, approximately 1.4 years) indicates that intervention windows are narrow. Once populations fall below approximately 20% of initial energy levels (2,500 units), recovery probability diminishes significantly despite temporary demographic rebounds. The pronounced 63-step oscillation period further indicates that resource management strategies must account for temporal resource bottlenecks that amplify hunting impacts during specific seasonal phases. Proactive reduction of hunting pressure below the tested threshold appears necessary to prevent deterministic extinction of long-lived, low-fecundity megaherbivore populations.
