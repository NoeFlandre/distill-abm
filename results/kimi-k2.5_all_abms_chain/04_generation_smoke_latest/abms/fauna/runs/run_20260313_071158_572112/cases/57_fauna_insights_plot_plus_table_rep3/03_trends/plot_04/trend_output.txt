Cumulative female mortality begins at zero and exhibits a monotonic increase throughout all simulation runs, ultimately saturating at approximately 123 to 124 individuals. The accumulation follows a decelerating trajectory characterized by three distinct temporal phases.

During the initial phase spanning roughly the first 500 time steps, mortality accumulates at the highest rates, with rolling trend analysis indicating slopes of approximately 0.21 to 0.23 females killed per step. By step 500, cumulative mortality across runs ranges from approximately 80 to 120 individuals, representing the majority of total mortality observed throughout the entire simulation period. Statistical change points detected at steps 125, 375, and 475 correspond to accelerations and modulations in this rapid accumulation curve, while inflection points at steps 245, 262, and 285 mark the transition from exponential to linear growth patterns.

The intermediate phase, extending from approximately step 500 to step 1000, exhibits a marked deceleration in mortality accumulation. Rolling trend slopes decline to values between 0.02 and 0.06, and further inflection at step 1204 indicates progression toward saturation dynamics. During this interval, cumulative counts rise from roughly 100 to between 115 and 120.

Beyond approximately step 1000, the system enters a saturation phase where mortality accumulation substantially plateaus. Aggregate values stabilize between 120 and 124 females killed, reaching a maximum observed value of 123.67 at step 1826. Trend slopes approach zero during the window spanning steps 1392 to 1623, and while minor oscillations continue with peaks and valleys between steps 1164 and 1851, the overall trajectory remains flat. Late-stage inflection points at steps 1336, 1487, 1631, and 1725 correspond to final adjustments in individual simulation trajectories before stabilization.

Substantial heterogeneity exists across the 40 simulation runs. While the aggregate mean follows the described three-phase pattern, individual runs vary in their total duration and final cumulative counts. Some simulations conclude by step 600 to 800 with approximately 108 to 110 total female mortalities, whereas others persist through step 2200, achieving the upper range of 122 to 124 cumulative deaths. This variation correlates with the timing of inflection points beyond step 1200, with late inflections distinguishing extended runs from truncated ones. A statistically significant decreasing trend appears in the final observation window (steps 1856–2087), reflecting the minimal absolute variation inherent in the plateaued cumulative values.

Interpreted within the model context, the saturation at 123 to 124 cumulative deaths represents near-complete exploitation of the initial female prey population. Given the initialization of 250 prey individuals and assuming approximate sexual parity, this outcome indicates that hunters remove approximately 98 percent of available females over the simulation duration of roughly six years (2200 steps). The rapid early accumulation aligns with high initial hunting efficiency under parameters favoring female pursuit—specifically lower processing costs (3.0 versus 6.0 for males) and enhanced food value from nursing offspring—while the subsequent plateau suggests demographic collapse or complete depletion of accessible females. The extended tail of accumulation in some runs through step 1800 likely reflects the harvesting of females maturing into the population from births occurring after initialization, given the birth-spacing interval of 730 steps (two years) and the age-of-senescence parameter of 7300 steps (twenty years).

**Implications for Decision Makers**

These results demonstrate that the current parameter configuration generates unsustainable predation pressure specifically targeting reproductive females. The model predicts rapid attrition of the female demographic within the first 500 time steps (approximately 1.4 years), eliminating nearly the entire initial cohort and precluding population recovery. If the management objective involves preserving prey populations while maintaining hunting activity, interventions must reduce hunter density below the current level of 20 agents or increase female processing costs to reduce the effective preference for female prey. The absence of equilibrium conditions in the saturation phase—where mortality simply ceases rather than stabilizing at a sustainable harvest rate—indicates that this system lacks self-regulating feedbacks capable of preventing extirpation of females under existing hunting protocols. Decision makers should note that the oscillation pattern detected at steps 2170–2265, though small in magnitude (0.00 prominence), suggests potential for minor cyclic variations in late-stage hunting efficiency, but this does not alter the overall trajectory toward female demographic elimination.
