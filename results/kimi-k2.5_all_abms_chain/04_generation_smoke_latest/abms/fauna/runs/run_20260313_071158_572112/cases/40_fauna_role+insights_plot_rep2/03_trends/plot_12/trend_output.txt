The simulations commence with total female energy values ranging between approximately 12,000 and 12,500 energy units. Across all repeated runs, the metric exhibits a pronounced decline toward functional extinction, with the entire ensemble of trajectories converging to values near or at zero within the temporal window of 2,000 steps.

The decline follows a steep descent pattern during the initial phase. Between steps 0 and 300, most trajectories lose between 60% and 95% of initial energy, dropping from approximately 12,000 to values ranging between 0 and 4,000. This initial phase demonstrates high variability in collapse velocity: the most rapid declines reach minimal energy levels (below 500) by step 300, while the most gradual declines maintain energy reserves above 8,000 through step 300 and above 4,000 through step 500.

During the intermediate phase (steps 300-800), the trajectories display asynchronous fluctuations with temporary plateaus and minor recoveries in subset of runs, with energy levels ranging between 0 and 4,000. Several simulations exhibit brief stabilization or rebound events where energy increases by 500-1,500 units before resuming decline, particularly concentrated between steps 300-600.

In the terminal phase (beyond step 1,000), the vast majority of trajectories decline to negligible levels (below 500 energy units). By step 1,500, most simulations have reached energy minima approaching zero, with only isolated outliers maintaining residual energy above 200. Complete depletion or near-complete depletion (values below 100) is observed across all runs by step 2,000.

The temporal variance in collapse trajectories suggests that while the outcome of female population energy depletion is deterministic under these parameter settings, the rate of collapse is stochastic, likely influenced by spatial heterogeneity in hunter-prey encounters and seasonal resource fluctuations. The discrete stepwise nature of decline patterns indicates that mortality events occur as punctuated removals rather than gradual attrition.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The 300-500 step window represents a critical vulnerability period where interventions would be most urgent, as this is when the majority of reproductive capacity is lost. The asynchronous nature of collapse across simulations suggests that local population persistence is highly variable in the short term, but the deterministic convergence to zero indicates that without reduction in hunting pressure or implementation of spatial refugia, no population configuration under these immortal hunter parameters achieves stable coexistence. Conservation strategies should prioritize reducing hunter encounter rates or increasing vegetation productivity within the first year (365 steps), as delays beyond this window correspond to the steepest phase of energy depletion.
