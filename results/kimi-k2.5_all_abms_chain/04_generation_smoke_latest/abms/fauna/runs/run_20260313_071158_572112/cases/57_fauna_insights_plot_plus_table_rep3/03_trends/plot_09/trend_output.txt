The population of female megaherbivores exhibits a deterministic trajectory toward extinction over approximately 2311 simulation steps, initiating from a cohort of 125 individuals and terminating at complete elimination. The demographic decline manifests through three distinct phases: an initial precipitous reduction, an extended persistence phase characterized by marginal remnant populations, and eventual final extinction.

During the initial phase spanning roughly the first 305 to 475 steps, the population undergoes rapid attrition from 125 individuals down to approximately 20 or fewer, representing a decline of over 80%. This steep reduction indicates that hunting pressure immediately exceeds the population's reproductive replacement capacity given the specified biological parameters, including a biennial birth spacing interval of 730 ticks and seasonal resource constraints with an 80% seasonality index.

Following this initial collapse, the population enters a prolonged persistence phase lasting from approximately step 500 through step 2000, wherein the average population across simulation replicates settles near 16 individuals but with a median of merely 1, indicating highly right-skewed distributions where most instances maintain very small remnant herds while rare cases retain slightly larger groups. Throughout this interval, the population exhibits oscillatory dynamics with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 steps, likely corresponding to seasonal cycles of resource availability and reproductive timing synchronized with the 365-tick annual cycle. Minor fluctuations in abundance occur around steps 710-730, 800-820, and 900-1000, though these represent minimal recoveries to levels below 7 individuals, insufficient to reverse the overall trajectory.

Statistical change point analysis identifies step 475, step 600, and step 875 as critical transitions where the rate or nature of decline shifts. After step 875, the population enters a terminal decline phase characterized by consistently low values, predominantly fluctuating between 0 and 5 individuals until final extinction at step 2311. Brief intervals of statistically significant localized increase detected in rolling trend analyses (particularly around steps 1160-1391 and 1856-2087) reflect temporary demographic recoveries or reduced hunting success, but these prove insufficient to establish sustainable population growth given the continued hunting pressure from 20 active foragers operating under optimal foraging constraints with lower processing costs for females (3 units) compared to males (6 units).

The complete absence of equilibrium conditions throughout the simulation demonstrates that, under the specified parameterization with high seasonality, limited grassland productivity (33% coverage), and intensive hunting pressure, the megaherbivore population cannot maintain positive growth rates. The median persistence time of small remnant populations suggests that while extinction is inevitable, significant time lags exist between critical population reduction and final disappearance, creating an extinction debt phenomenon where populations persist below minimum viable thresholds for extended durations.

For decision makers, these findings indicate that hunting management strategies must intervene during the initial rapid decline phase, optimally before step 475, as populations below approximately 20 individuals lack the demographic resilience to recover even if hunting pressure temporarily abates. The prolonged persistence of small populations suggests that monitoring protocols relying on detection of rare individuals may provide false assurance of population stability when irreversible extinction is already determined. Additionally, the 63-step oscillation cycle identifies seasonal vulnerability windows when hunting impact may be amplified, suggesting that seasonal hunting moratoria could potentially extend population persistence if implemented early in the decline trajectory.
