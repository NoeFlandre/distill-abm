The percentage of vegetated landscape patches initiates at approximately 33%, consistent with the initial grass coverage configuration, and demonstrates long-term stability around this baseline throughout the simulation duration, terminating at nearly the identical value. A distinct minimum emerges at tick 365, where vegetation cover declines to approximately 24.9%, representing an annual seasonal trough attributable to the strong environmental seasonality combined with cumulative grazing pressure over the first yearly cycle.

Following this initial annual minimum, the system exhibits regular oscillatory behavior with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks, suggesting cyclical fluctuations between phases of vegetation depletion and recovery. These oscillations likely arise from the interplay between the rapid vegetation regrowth interval (every 3 ticks) and continuous foraging by the herbivore population. The trajectory includes multiple inflection points distributed throughout the temporal sequence, indicating periodic shifts between net vegetation loss and recovery regimes.

Trend analysis reveals predominantly increasing patterns in vegetation cover during earlier temporal windows (ticks 0 through approximately 1623), transitioning to decreasing trends in subsequent intervals (particularly from tick 1624 onward). This shift may reflect long-term adjustments in the grazing pressure dynamics as herbivore herds redistribute across the fixed initial vegetation patches or as cumulative consumption effects emerge. Despite the presence of 250 herbivores continuously consuming vegetation, the system maintains remarkable stability with modest variance (approximately 0.95 percentage points) around the 33% mean, indicating that the rapid regrowth mechanism effectively compensates for consumption pressure while the restricted regrowth pattern (limited to initially vegetated patches) prevents landscape-wide expansion beyond the established baseline.
