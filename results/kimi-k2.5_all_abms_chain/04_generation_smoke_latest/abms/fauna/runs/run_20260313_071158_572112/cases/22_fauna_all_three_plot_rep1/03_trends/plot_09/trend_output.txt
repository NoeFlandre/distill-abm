The number of female animals initiates at approximately 125 individuals in all simulation runs, representing roughly half of the initial ungulate population of 250 animals. During the first 500 time steps, the population experiences a rapid decline, with most simulations falling below 20 individuals by step 500, and several reaching near-zero levels by step 400. Considerable variation characterizes the rate of decline during this initial period: certain populations decrease sharply, approaching single-digit counts by step 300, while others maintain higher abundances, remaining above 100 individuals until approximately step 200 before undergoing steep declines.

In the subsequent phase spanning steps 500 to 1000, surviving populations typically persist at reduced levels, generally between 1 and 20 individuals, gradually declining toward extinction with occasional temporary plateaus. Several simulations maintain small remnant populations of fewer than 5 individuals for protracted durations, with extinction events occurring as late as step 1500 in some runs and extending to approximately step 2200 in others, at which point the final individuals are lost.

The data reveal a consistent pattern of population collapse across all runs, with no instances of demographic recovery or stabilization at sustainable population levels. The pronounced variability in the timing of collapse—particularly evident during the first 500 steps—suggests that stochastic elements in hunter-prey interactions and environmental conditions substantially influence the velocity of decline without altering the terminal outcome. The extended persistence of small populations at low densities in later stages reflects reduced encounter probabilities between human foragers and prey when population numbers fall below thresholds readily detectable within the specified foraging radius of 6 patches.

Insights for decision makers: The simulation outcomes indicate that a hunting pressure of 20 foragers, combined with high environmental seasonality (0.8) and moderate vegetation coverage (33%), generates unsustainable mortality that inevitably drives megaherbivore populations to extinction, though the temporal horizon for collapse varies significantly. The prolonged persistence of small remnant populations creates potential monitoring challenges, as populations may persist at critically low levels for hundreds of time steps before final extinction, potentially delaying recognition of conservation crises. The inverse relationship between population density and extinction velocity suggests that management interventions may be most urgent when populations first begin to decline rapidly, rather than waiting for the extended terminal phase of scarcity.
