The combined total energy available in the female prey population initiates at 12,500 units and declines rapidly over the first 375 time steps, falling to approximately 2,200 units. This steep initial depletion suggests that hunting pressure immediately exceeds the demographic replacement capacity of the herds, rapidly reducing the standing biomass of reproductive females.

Between steps 375 and 875, the decline transitions into an oscillatory pattern characterized by fluctuations with a dominant period of approximately 63 time steps. During this interval, energy levels exhibit repeated minor peaks and valleys—starting near 2,230 units and gradually stepping down—before entering a phase of continued descent. These oscillations likely reflect the interaction of hunting mortality with periodic recruitment events, potentially synchronized with the two-year birth spacing interval or seasonal resource cycles, creating pulsed recoveries in total herd energy that are subsequently dampened by sustained predation.

From step 875 onward, the total energy continues a general downward trajectory, though the rate of decline varies across distinct windows. Notable temporary increases occur between steps 1,160–1,391 and again near steps 1,856–2,087, where the energy briefly rises before resuming its descent. These transient recoveries may indicate periods where juvenile recruitment temporarily outpaced hunting losses, or where hunter energy constraints reduced predation pressure sufficiently to allow modest population rebounds.

The system reaches complete energy depletion at step 2,310, terminating at zero units. The substantial variance observed across the 40 simulation runs—evident in the high standard deviation relative to the mean—indicates that while all trajectories converge on extinction, the specific timing of decline phases and recovery pulses varies considerably depending on stochastic demographic and hunting interaction events. The median value of 100 units demonstrates that for the majority of the simulation duration, the female population persisted at severely reduced energy levels, operating far below the initial carrying capacity before ultimate collapse.
