The male megaherbivore population initiated at 125 individuals and underwent complete extinction by tick 2284, representing a collapse trajectory over approximately 6.3 simulated years. The temporal dynamics exhibited distinct phased behavior rather than a uniform decline.

During the initial 1,391 ticks (covering roughly the first 3.8 years), the population experienced a statistically significant monotonic decrease across six consecutive analytical windows. The steepest contractions occurred immediately after initialization, with maximum rates of decline measured in windows 1 through 3 (ticks 0–695), where slopes reached -0.149 to -0.244 individuals per tick. The rate of loss attenuated progressively through windows 4 (ticks 696–927, slope -0.029), 5 (ticks 928–1159, slope -0.009), and 6 (ticks 1160–1391, slope -0.002), suggesting diminishing hunting efficiency or density-dependent predator adaptation as absolute numbers fell.

A notable demographic reversal occurred during ticks 1392–1623, when the population exhibited a significant increasing trend (tau = 0.435, slope +0.001). This transient recovery phase interrupted the collapse trajectory, potentially reflecting a birth pulse of male offspring reaching tracking age or temporary shifts in hunter targeting behavior given the sexual dimorphism in food value (males: 30 energy units) versus processing costs (males: 6 units). However, this recovery remained insufficient to restore viability, peaking at values well below the initialization threshold.

Following this rebound, the population entered a stochastic fluctuation phase from ticks 1624–2087, showing no significant directional trend (p = 0.608 and p = 0.783 for respective windows). During this period, male prey oscillated with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks—likely corresponding to seasonal vegetation cycles interacting with the 0.8 seasonality parameter and 3-tick grass regrowth time—superimposed on an overall downward trajectory. Multiple inflection points detected between ticks 1262 and 2167 indicate complex, non-linear responses to the interaction of hunting pressure, energy dynamics (movement cost: 1 unit, gain from food: 1.5 units), and demographic stochasticity at low population sizes.

The final transition to extinction occurred between tick 2087 and 2284, with the last male prey recorded at tick 2284. Throughout the simulation, the population distribution was heavily right-skewed: while the arithmetic mean maintained 21.03 individuals, the median plummeted to 1.35, indicating that for the majority of the runtime, the population persisted at critically low densities vulnerable to Allee effects and stochastic extinction. The presence of change points at regular intervals (125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 875 ticks) suggests threshold-mediated transitions in population decline rates, potentially corresponding to discrete hunting events or seasonal bottlenecks imposed by the strong environmental seasonality (0.8) and the two-year interbirth interval (730 ticks) limiting recruitment capacity.
