Across all simulation runs, the total energy of prey animals follows a consistent trajectory of decline from initial values of approximately 25,000 energy units toward effective extinction (near-zero energy), with complete collapse occurring in all observed trajectories. The temporal progression of this decline exhibits substantial variation in timing but converges on the same endpoint.

During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, the system experiences rapid depletion of prey energy. By step 500, the majority of simulation runs have declined to below 5,000 total energy units, representing a biomass loss exceeding 80 percent. Several trajectories reach near-zero energy by this point, while others maintain intermediate values between 2,000 and 8,000 units.

In the intermediate phase extending from step 500 to approximately step 1,000, the remaining populations exhibit heterogeneous decline patterns. While many runs complete their descent to zero energy by step 600, a subset demonstrates slower depletion rates, maintaining energy levels between 1,000 and 5,000 units with occasional temporary plateaus. This variation reflects demographic stochasticity and spatial dynamics in hunter-prey encounters. One trajectory in particular maintains energy above 5,000 units until approximately step 800 before accelerating decline.

The final phase beyond step 1,000 captures the extinction of the most persistent populations. The longest-surviving trajectory maintains low but non-zero energy (below 1,000 units) until approximately step 2,200 before reaching extinction. By the conclusion of the observation period, all simulation runs have converged to zero or near-zero total prey energy.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The parameter configuration tested—with 20 hunters, sexual dimorphism in food value (males: 30, females: 27), processing costs (males: 6, females: 3), and seasonal environmental constraints (seasonality: 0.8)—produces deterministic extinction outcomes across all stochastic replicates. The absence of equilibrium conditions or recovery trajectories indicates that current hunting pressure exceeds the demographic replacement capacity of the prey population, which operates with a two-year interbirth interval (730 ticks) and senescence beginning at year 20 (7,300 ticks). Decision makers should recognize that the current hunter density and foraging efficiency create a "extinction vortex" with complete prey depletion occurring within 1.4 to 6 years (500-2,200 ticks). To achieve sustainable coexistence, interventions would need to substantially reduce hunter numbers, increase processing costs, or enhance prey fecundity beyond current life history constraints.
