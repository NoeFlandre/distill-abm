The cumulative number of postpartum females killed begins at zero across all simulation instances. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, the metric increases rapidly and monotonically, though exhibiting substantial variation in accumulation rates between independent runs. By step 500, values range between approximately 60 and 120, with the majority of trajectories falling between 80 and 110. A subset of trajectories shows delayed initiation, remaining below 20 kills until approximately step 200–300 before undergoing steep increases to converge with other runs.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of increase decelerates considerably for most trajectories. During this interval, cumulative values typically rise from their mid-phase levels to asymptotic ranges of 105–125. After approximately step 1000, accumulation becomes negligible, with trajectories stabilizing at terminal values between roughly 110 and 125, occasionally reaching slightly above 120. The trajectories demonstrate that hunting mortality of postpartum females is heavily concentrated in the early temporal phase, with approximately 80–90 percent of total cumulative kills occurring within the first 500–700 steps.

The convergence of disparate early trajectories toward similar asymptotic maxima suggests a saturation effect consistent with the finite size of the reproductive female cohort. Given the initial prey population of 250 animals and sexual dimorphism parameters, the terminal values approaching 120 killed postpartum females represent near-complete attrition of the reproductive female population, accounting for nearly all individuals who would enter the postpartum state during the simulation period.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

These trends indicate that under the specified hunting parameters (where females offer high nutritional return with relatively low processing costs), reproductive females face disproportionate and unsustainable offtake pressure. The temporal concentration of mortality within the first 500–1000 steps (approximately 1.4 to 2.7 years given the daily calibration) suggests that demographic damage to the breeding population becomes essentially irreversible within a brief initial window of human-megafauna interaction. The near-total depletion of postpartum females implies severe recruitment collapse and population trajectory toward extinction, highlighting the need for immediate intervention strategies—such as restricting hunting of reproductive females or reducing forager density—within the first few years of contact to prevent irreversible demographic consequences.
