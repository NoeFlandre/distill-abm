The total number of animals begins at approximately 250 individuals across all simulation runs. The population undergoes a steep decline during the first 500 steps, with the majority of runs decreasing from 250 to below 50 animals, and several trajectories reaching near-zero levels by step 400. This rapid initial collapse reflects intense predation pressure from the 20 hunter agents operating with energy storage capabilities and immortality, creating a constant harvest pressure that overwhelms the prey population's reproductive capacity given the 730-tick (2-year) birth spacing interval.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the decline continues but at a decelerated rate, with populations exhibiting high variability across runs. While most trajectories approach extinction (0–5 animals) by step 800, a subset demonstrates prolonged persistence, fluctuating between 5 and 40 animals until approximately step 1000. This variation likely stems from stochastic differences in herd movement patterns, temporary spatial refugia, and demographic stochasticity affecting herd survival during the bottleneck phase.

From step 1000 to 2000, the remaining populations converge uniformly toward extinction. By step 1500, nearly all runs reach zero animals, with only isolated outliers maintaining minimal populations of fewer than 5 individuals until approximately step 2100. The absence of stabilization or recovery indicates that the combination of foraging radius (6 patches), prey food values (30 for males, 27 for females), and processing costs (6 for males, 3 for females) generates a sustained harvest rate that exceeds the maximum sustainable yield for the ungulate life history parameters, particularly given the senescence threshold at 7,300 ticks and internal mortality rate of 0.025.

The consistent extinction outcome across all stochastic runs demonstrates that the current parameter configuration creates deterministic extinction dynamics. The immortal forager population ensures that hunting pressure does not diminish as prey become scarce, while the moderate seasonality (0.8) and grass regrowth time (3 ticks) provide insufficient environmental buffering to offset the demographic deficit caused by predation. The rapid collapse within the first 500 steps suggests that the initial encounter rate between hunters and the 250 initial prey saturates the foragers' energy requirements while depleting the prey base below critical recovery thresholds.

Implications for decision makers: The uniform trajectory toward extinction indicates that the current hunter density of 20 individuals exceeds the critical threshold for sustainable harvest of this megaherbivore population. To prevent extinction, management interventions must either reduce initial forager numbers substantially, increase processing costs to reduce hunting efficiency, or extend birth spacing intervals to enhance reproductive output. The rapid decline phase within the first 500 steps suggests that conservation measures must be implemented immediately upon hunter introduction; delayed intervention beyond this window appears ineffective as populations enter irreversible extinction vortices. The variability in extinction timing implies that while small population refugia may delay extinction temporarily, they cannot prevent it under current hunting pressure levels, indicating that spatial management alone is insufficient without reducing overall predation impact.
