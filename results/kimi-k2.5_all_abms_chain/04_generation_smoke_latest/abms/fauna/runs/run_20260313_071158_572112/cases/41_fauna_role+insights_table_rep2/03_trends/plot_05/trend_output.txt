**Implications for Decision Makers**

The rapid initial accumulation of postpartum female mortality suggests that early hunting pressure disproportionately targets reproductively active females, likely due to their nutritional value (food-value-females = 27) coupled with lower processing costs (processing-cost-females = 3) relative to males. The subsequent plateau and late-phase decline in the cross-simulation mean indicate that scenarios experiencing high early mortality of reproductive females tend toward earlier population collapse, effectively removing those simulation trajectories from the aggregate mean at later time points while low-impact scenarios persist. This survivor bias implies that sustainable populations are those that avoid the initial high-mortality phase; consequently, management strategies should prioritize protection of postpartum females during the initial establishment period of hunter-forager populations.

The 63-tick oscillation—approximately one-sixth of the annual cycle given the 365-tick yearly calibration—suggests predictable windows of elevated vulnerability synchronized with seasonal resource pulses (seasonality = 0.8). This periodicity offers opportunities for temporal management interventions, such as seasonal restrictions on hunting, that align with these vulnerability cycles to buffer reproductive females during critical intervals. The upper asymptote near 117–119 cumulative kills across the simulation ensemble likely represents a demographic tipping point beyond which postpartum female mortality precipitates broader population collapse, providing a quantitative threshold for monitoring sustainability in comparable systems.
