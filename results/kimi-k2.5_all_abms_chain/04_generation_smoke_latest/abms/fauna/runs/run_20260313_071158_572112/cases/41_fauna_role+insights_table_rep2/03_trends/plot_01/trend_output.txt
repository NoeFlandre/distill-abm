The population of human hunter-gatherer bands remained absolutely invariant throughout the observation period, maintaining a constant value of 20 active agents across all 2,312 recorded time steps. No temporal variation, directional trends, cyclical fluctuations, or distributional changes were exhibited; the metric held steady at its initialization value from the first observation through the terminal time step.

This static behavior reflects the specific model parameterization where forager agents are designated as immortal (imortal-foragers = True), thereby eliminating starvation-induced mortality, combined with the absence of recruitment mechanisms or hunter-loss processes that would otherwise alter population size. Consequently, the energetic dynamics of individual hunters—including energy storage, expenditure during foraging, and pursuit success—did not translate into demographic changes at the population level.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The invariant hunter population establishes a fixed predation pressure baseline, which aids in isolating the effects of environmental stochasticity and prey population dynamics from confounding variables related to hunter demographic feedback. However, decision makers should recognize that this configuration precludes analysis of potential regulatory mechanisms where prey scarcity might reduce hunting pressure through hunter mortality or emigration, or where hunting success might facilitate population growth. This design choice is appropriate for testing prey vulnerability thresholds under constant exploitation pressure, but limits inference regarding co-evolutionary dynamics or sustainable coexistence scenarios where predator and prey populations might interactively regulate one another.
