The combined total energy of all prey animals initiates at approximately 16,667 units, though individual simulation trajectories commence with values ranging up to 25,000 units depending on initial herd compositions. A steep decline characterizes the first 350 time steps, with aggregate energy falling by roughly 75% to approximately 4,100 units. This rapid depletion corresponds to the initial encounter phase between human hunters and the established prey population.

Between steps 350 and 500, the decline moderates but continues through a series of oscillations with a dominant period of 63 time steps, exhibiting local peaks at steps 385, 388, 402, 408, 433, and 440 (ranging from 3,700 to 4,100 units). These fluctuations suggest density-dependent feedback where reduced prey availability temporarily diminishes hunting success, allowing marginal demographic recovery before renewed pressure drives numbers lower.

Following the change point at step 500, the system enters a phase of decelerating decline marked by additional inflections at steps 513 and 625. A notable secondary peak emerges at step 730 (value ~1,083 units), precisely aligning with the two-year interbirth interval parameter, indicating a final cohort recruitment pulse before the population enters terminal decline.

After step 875, the total energy stabilizes at low levels (median 146 units) with reduced rates of change. Brief periods of slight increase occur between steps 1392-1623 and 1856-2087, potentially representing stochastic persistence of small remnant subpopulations or temporary spatial refugia effects. However, the overall trajectory remains downward.

By step 2310, the system undergoes its final change point, driving the aggregate prey energy to complete depletion (zero) by step 2311 across all simulation runs. The standard deviation of 4,361 units and high initial variance reflect divergent extinction trajectories among runs—some populations collapsing within 500 steps while others persist for over 2,000 steps—though all ultimately converge on extinction.
