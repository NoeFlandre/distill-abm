**Implications for decision makers:**
The consistent male population collapse across parameter configurations suggests that differential hunting selectivity based on energetic returns drives sex-biased extinction dynamics. Conservation strategies must account for hunter preference structures where higher-value individuals face disproportionate mortality risk. The lag time between population decline and final extinction (up to 2200 steps) indicates that Allee effects or demographic stochasticity may mask imminent extinction risks, warning against complacency when small populations persist. Recovery interventions should prioritize male protection during the critical early decline phase (first 500 steps), as delayed action results in irreversible demographic bottlenecks where even extended birth intervals cannot facilitate population rebound due to mate limitation effects. The immortal forager assumption implies that hunting pressure remains constant regardless of prey density, suggesting that sustainable harvest models must incorporate hunter population regulation or movement costs to prevent deterministic extinction outcomes.
