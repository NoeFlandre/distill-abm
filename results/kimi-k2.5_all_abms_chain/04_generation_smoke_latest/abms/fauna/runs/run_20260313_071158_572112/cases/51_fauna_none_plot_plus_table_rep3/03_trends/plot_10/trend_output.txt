The simulations consistently demonstrate a deterministic trajectory of megaherbivore population decline culminating in universal extinction across all experimental runs. The population dynamics initiate at approximately 250 individuals and follow a monotonically decreasing trend toward zero, with the rate of decline exhibiting distinct phase transitions over the temporal progression of the simulations.

The initial phase, spanning approximately the first 500 to 1000 temporal steps, is characterized by a steep, rapid decline in population numbers. During this period, the aggregated mean population across simulations drops precipitously from the initial 250 individuals to approximately 50 or fewer, representing an 80% reduction in total population size. This steep decline reflects high hunting efficiency and encounter rates when prey densities are relatively high, creating an overharvesting dynamic where removal rates substantially exceed the population's reproductive capacity.

Following the initial rapid decline, the population enters a prolonged terminal phase characterized by a decelerated rate of decrease. During this period, which may extend from approximately step 1000 to beyond step 2000 depending on the specific simulation run, populations persist at low densities—typically fewer than 50 individuals and frequently fewer than 10—before eventually declining to zero. The median population value across simulations remains below 1.5 individuals for the majority of the temporal sequence, indicating that most simulation runs spend the preponderance of their duration at critically low population levels prior to final extinction.

Substantial stochastic variation exists across individual simulation trajectories regarding the specific timing of extinction events. While all runs eventually reach zero population, some simulations exhibit rapid extinction occurring within the first 500 to 1000 steps, whereas others demonstrate prolonged persistence with populations fluctuating at low densities for 2000 or more steps before final collapse. This variability reflects the stochastic nature of demographic processes, hunting encounters, and herd movement patterns within the agent-based framework.

Statistical analysis confirms the deterministic nature of the decline, with rolling Mann-Kendall tests indicating statistically significant monotonic decreasing trends throughout the vast majority of the simulation duration across all temporal windows. The aggregated mean population across all 120 simulation runs follows a continuous decreasing trajectory to a terminal value of zero, with an overall mean of 25 individuals and a standard deviation of 44.9 individuals, indicating high variance driven by the asynchronous timing of extinction events across runs.

In the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these dynamics indicate that under the specified parameter configuration—including 20 initial human foragers, specific processing costs and food values for sexually dimorphic prey, maximum lifespans of approximately 10,220 ticks, and environmental conditions with seasonality—the hunting pressure exerted by human agents systematically exceeds the megaherbivore population's capacity for demographic replacement. The inevitable extinction outcome demonstrates that the interaction between human hunting efficiency, prey life history characteristics, and environmental constraints creates a non-equilibrium system where prey populations cannot sustain themselves against the continuous removal pressure imposed by the hunting agents.
