The combined energy available in female ungulate populations initiated at 12,500 units and followed a deterministic trajectory toward complete depletion by tick 2,311. The dynamics exhibited marked non-linearity across four distinct temporal phases.

During the initial phase (ticks 0–375), the metric underwent precipitous decline with instantaneous rates exceeding 22 units per tick, reflecting immediate hunting pressure from 20 foragers on the founding population. By tick 376, aggregate female energy had contracted to approximately 2,233 units, representing an 82% reduction from initial conditions. Between ticks 376 and 410, the series exhibited localized fluctuations with values oscillating between minima of approximately 2,094 units and maxima of 2,233 units, suggesting demographic inertia wherein surviving cohorts maintained brief energetic stability before succumbing to sustained predation.

The intermediate phase (ticks 375–875) featured continued decline punctuated by change points at ticks 500 and 625 that moderated the rate of depletion. Throughout this interval, the metric displayed a dominant oscillatory period of approximately 63 ticks, likely corresponding to seasonal resource cycles interacting with herd movement patterns. Values progressively eroded from roughly 2,100 units toward substantially lower levels by tick 875.

During the prolonged late-middle phase (ticks 875–1,856), the population entered a regime of low-density equilibrium punctuated by ephemeral recoveries. Notably, between ticks 1,160 and 1,391, the trend reversed temporarily to weak positive growth, indicating possible density-dependent relaxation of hunting efficiency or demographic compensation wherein reduced competition temporarily elevated recruitment rates. However, this recovery proved insufficient to reverse the overall trajectory.

The terminal phase (ticks 1,856–2,311) witnessed final collapse despite a brief resurgence between ticks 1,856 and 2,087. The metric ultimately declined to zero energy availability by tick 2,311, representing population extinction. Inflection points at ticks 1,308, 1,407, 1,470, and subsequent intervals throughout the simulation marked transitions in decline rates, corresponding to shifts in herd age structure as senescence-related mortality (activated at tick 7,300 equivalent) and birth spacing intervals (730 ticks) altered population demographic composition under continuous harvest pressure.

For decision makers, these dynamics demonstrate that megaherbivore populations subject to continuous hunting pressure exhibit asymmetric extinction trajectories characterized by rapid initial depletion followed by prolonged vulnerability during low-density phases. The presence of temporary recovery windows between ticks 1,160–1,391 and 1,856–2,087 suggests that short-term population rebounds provide unreliable indicators of sustainability. Management protocols must account for the 63-tick demographic cycle and implement harvest moratoria or density-dependent controls well before populations reach the low-energy thresholds observed after tick 875, as immortal forager populations inevitably drive prey to extinction under the specified life history constraints of two-year birth spacing and age-dependent senescence.
