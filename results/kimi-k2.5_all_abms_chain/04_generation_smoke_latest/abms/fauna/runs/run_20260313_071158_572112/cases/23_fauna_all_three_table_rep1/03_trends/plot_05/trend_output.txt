The cumulative mortality of postpartum female ungulates initiates at zero and undergoes rapid accumulation during the first approximately 850 time steps, approaching 117 individuals. This initial phase is characterized by strongly increasing trends with steep slopes exceeding 0.2 kills per tick in the earliest intervals (0–463), reflecting immediate forager success upon entering the landscape. Inflection points occurring between ticks 245 and 285 indicate an early transition from accelerating to decelerating accumulation rates as the most accessible nursing females are depleted or as prey populations adjust spatially to predation pressure.

By approximately tick 1200, the cumulative kill count stabilizes near 117, entering a prolonged plateau phase that persists with minor oscillations between 117 and 119 kills. The maximum cumulative mortality of 119 occurs around tick 1826, after which the metric exhibits slight variability without sustained growth. This plateau suggests that hunting pressure on postpartum females reaches an equilibrium where continued kills are balanced by demographic replenishment, or alternatively, that the effective population of breeding females has been reduced to a threshold limiting further hunting success.

In the final phase of the simulation, particularly after tick 1856, the trend exhibits a slight decline from the peak value, settling at 117 by termination. This late-stage decrease, coupled with oscillatory patterns detected in the interval between ticks 2170 and 2265 with a periodicity of approximately 63 time steps, indicates cyclical fluctuations likely driven by seasonal resource variability affecting either prey vulnerability or hunter foraging efficiency. The transition from strongly positive trends early in the simulation to negligible or slightly negative trends in later windows demonstrates a fundamental shift from an exploitation phase to a depleted or resource-limited regime.

**Implications for decision makers:** The rapid accumulation of mortality to a saturation point near 117 kills indicates that nursing females constitute a highly vulnerable demographic that can be quickly depleted under sustained hunting pressure, despite their lower energetic value relative to males. The pronounced processing cost advantage for females (3 versus 6 for males) appears to drive initial targeting, but the subsequent plateau suggests that continued extraction risks functionally extirpating the postpartum cohort. The late-stage oscillations reveal environmentally-driven windows of heightened risk occurring in approximately 63-tick cycles, indicating that management interventions targeting hunting restrictions during these predictable high-risk periods could prevent the removal of reproductive females and their dependent offspring, thereby preserving the population's reproductive capacity.
