The cumulative mortality count initiates at zero and exhibits a saturating growth pattern characterized by three distinct phases across all simulation replicates. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, deaths accumulate rapidly with steep trajectories, reaching values between 150 and 235 cumulative deaths in the majority of runs, though some trajectories show more moderate accumulation reaching only 125 to 150 deaths by this point. This initial surge reflects the immediate impact of combined mortality sources—including human hunting, starvation under seasonal resource constraints, external predation, and age-related senescence—acting upon the founding population of 250 animals.

During the intermediate phase from step 500 to 1000, the rate of mortality accumulation decelerates markedly as cumulative counts converge toward values between 215 and 245 deaths. This deceleration indicates population depletion, where the diminishing number of surviving animals reduces the absolute mortality rate even as per-capita risk remains high. The curves exhibit step-wise plateaus during this period, suggesting discrete mortality events separated by intervals of population persistence.

By approximately step 1000 to 1500, trajectories stabilize at final plateau values ranging from 225 to 250 cumulative deaths, with fastest runs achieving saturation by step 800 while slower runs continue modest increases through step 2000. The stabilization occurs because the cumulative total approaches the initial population size, indicating that between 90 and 98 percent of the founding cohort has been lost to mortality. The termination of accumulation implies effective population collapse, as insufficient animals remain to sustain further deaths or the population has reached extinction.

High inter-run variability characterizes the timing of mortality accumulation, with coefficient of variation increasing through the intermediate phase. While some simulations exhibit rapid population collapse approaching 200 deaths by step 400, others demonstrate prolonged persistence with gradual attrition that does not reach comparable levels until step 800 or later. This stochasticity likely derives from variation in hunter-prey encounter rates, spatial heterogeneity in grass distribution, and demographic stochasticity in small population fragments.

The consistent convergence of all trajectories toward cumulative mortality values near the initial population size indicates that under the tested parameter configuration—combining 20 human hunters, high environmental seasonality of 0.8, and vegetation recovery every 3 ticks—the megaherbivore population experiences deterministic collapse regardless of stochastic variation in the timing of decline.

For decision makers: The deterministic nature of population collapse across diverse stochastic trajectories suggests that current hunting pressure and environmental productivity levels exceed critical sustainability thresholds for megaherbivore persistence. Management strategies must substantially reduce human hunting pressure or significantly enhance habitat productivity to prevent extinction outcomes. The high variability in collapse timing implies that short-term population stability does not indicate long-term viability, as stochastic environmental factors may delay but cannot prevent population decline under current pressure regimes.
