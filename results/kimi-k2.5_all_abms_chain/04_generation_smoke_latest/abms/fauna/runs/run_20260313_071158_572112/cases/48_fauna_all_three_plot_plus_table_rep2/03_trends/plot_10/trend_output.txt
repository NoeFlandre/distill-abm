The ensemble of simulations reveals an inexorable decline in megaherbivore populations from an initial state of approximately 250 individuals toward complete extinction. This trajectory unfolds over 2,312 time steps (approximately 6.3 years), with the aggregate population mean falling from roughly 167 individuals to zero.

The decline proceeds through three distinct phases demarcated by significant change points. During the initial phase (steps 0–475), the population experiences a catastrophic collapse, dropping precipitously from its initial abundance to fewer than 50 individuals. This rapid attrition is statistically marked by change points at steps 125 and 475, indicating escalating and then slightly moderating rates of loss as the most vulnerable prey are depleted and hunters shift to scarcer targets.

In the intermediate phase (steps 475–1500), the decline continues but at a decelerated pace, with populations fluctuating between approximately 50 and 5 individuals. This period exhibits high variance across simulation runs, with some populations persisting longer due to stochastic factors in hunter-prey encounters and vegetation dynamics. Superimposed on this trend is a periodic oscillation with a dominant cycle of approximately 63 steps (roughly bimonthly), likely reflecting the strong environmental seasonality (parameterized at 0.8) affecting both grass regrowth and hunter foraging efficiency. Temporary plateaus or minor recoveries appear in the aggregate mean around steps 730 and 1400, though these represent asynchronous dynamics across runs rather than true population rebounds within individual simulations.

The terminal phase (steps 1500–2312) sees populations dwindle to fewer than 5 individuals, with sporadic persistence of tiny remnant groups. Despite brief statistically significant increases in the aggregate mean detected between steps 1392–1623 and 1856–2087, the overall trajectory remains terminal, culminating in universal extinction by step 2310.

The complete absence of stable coexistence or recovery trajectories across all 120 simulation runs indicates that the current configuration—featuring 20 immortal human hunters with food storage capabilities, high environmental seasonality, and moderate initial vegetation cover—creates fundamentally non-viable conditions for megaherbivore persistence. The high variability in extinction timing (ranging from approximately 500 to over 2000 steps) underscores the critical role of stochasticity in determining when, but not if, collapse occurs. Management interventions would need to substantially reduce effective hunting pressure—either by reducing hunter numbers, introducing hunter mortality, or significantly enhancing landscape productivity and refuge areas—to shift the system away from this deterministic path to extinction.
