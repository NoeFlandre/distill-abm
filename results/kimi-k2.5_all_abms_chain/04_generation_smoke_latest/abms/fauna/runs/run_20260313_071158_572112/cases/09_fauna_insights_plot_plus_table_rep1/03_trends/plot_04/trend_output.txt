Across all simulation runs, the cumulative number of female prey killed exhibits a consistent three-phase dynamic characterized by rapid initial accumulation, progressive deceleration, and eventual saturation. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 steps, the cumulative kill count rises steeply from zero to roughly 100 individuals, with the mean trajectory reaching approximately 120 kills by step 500. This represents the highest rate of female mortality, as foragers encounter abundant prey populations.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the accumulation rate decelerates significantly as the cumulative count approaches 115 to 125 individuals. Most simulation runs reach plateau conditions during this interval, though substantial variability exists across replicates. Some trajectories saturate as early as step 600 with approximately 105 kills, while others continue gradual accumulation beyond step 1500, reaching maximum values of approximately 124 individuals. One anomalous trajectory exhibits markedly delayed accumulation, reaching comparable plateau levels only after step 1500.

Beyond step 1000, the system enters a saturation phase where the cumulative count stabilizes around a median of approximately 119 individuals and a mean of 104.6, with a maximum observed value of 123.7 occurring near step 1826. Statistical trend analysis confirms significant monotonic increases through step 1855, followed by a declining trend in accumulation rates thereafter, effectively indicating cessation of meaningful hunting success. Notable change points in the accumulation dynamics occur at steps 125, 375, 475, 625, and 850, marking transitions in hunting efficiency, with inflection points around steps 245-285 indicating peak kill rates before deceleration.

These dynamics suggest that with 20 immortal foragers and initial prey populations of 250, hunting pressure rapidly depletes accessible females despite moderate processing costs (3 units) and food values (27 units) associated with female prey. The plateau at approximately 120-124 kills likely reflects either functional extinction of females from the huntable population, severe depression of encounter rates due to prey depletion, or spatial refugia formation where remaining females avoid detection. Given the two-year interbirth interval (730 steps) and senescence threshold (7300 steps), population recovery cannot compensate for the extraction rate observed in the initial 500 steps, leading to irreversible depletion of the reproductive segment of the herd within approximately three to five years.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The rapid saturation of female kills within the first 500-1000 steps indicates that current forager densities and hunting efficiencies quickly exhaust reproductive female populations, creating a demographic bottleneck that prevents population sustainability. The irreversible nature of the plateau suggests that reactive management strategies implemented after observing initial declines will be ineffective, as the critical threshold for population viability is crossed early in the interaction. Decision makers should prioritize establishing hunting quotas or spatial refugia before step 125, when the first significant deceleration in accumulation indicates emerging prey scarcity. The presence of delayed-accumulation trajectories suggests that heterogeneous landscape use or temporary refugia can buffer populations temporarily, indicating that managed spatial structure may be the most effective intervention to prevent rapid female depletion.
