The number of female prey animals begins at approximately 125 individuals at the start of the simulation. Across all simulation runs, the population exhibits a consistent downward trajectory from the initial time step, indicating progressive population decline under the modeled hunting and environmental pressures.

The rate of decline varies substantially across individual simulation runs, reflecting stochastic variation in agent interactions and environmental stochasticity. In the most rapid decline scenarios, the female population collapses to near-zero levels within approximately 400 to 500 time steps. In more gradual decline scenarios, small populations persist until approximately 1,000 to 1,500 time steps, with some runs maintaining residual populations of fewer than 5 individuals until around the 2,000 time step mark.

By the conclusion of the simulation period at 2,000 time steps, essentially all simulation runs converge on zero or near-zero female population levels. This pattern of complete or near-complete population extinction across all stochastic replicates suggests that the combination of 20 initial human foragers, the specified hunting returns (food-value-males at 30, food-value-females at 27), and the energetic costs of processing and movement creates unsustainable hunting pressure relative to the ungulate reproductive capacity (birth-spacing of 730 ticks) and population recovery potential.
