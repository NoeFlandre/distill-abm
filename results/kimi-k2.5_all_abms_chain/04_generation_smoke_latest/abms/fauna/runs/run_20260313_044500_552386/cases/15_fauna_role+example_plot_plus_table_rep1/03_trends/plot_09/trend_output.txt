The population of female megaherbivores initiates at 125 individuals and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction across all simulation replicates, reaching zero by the conclusion of the temporal sequence at 2,310 steps. This decline unfolds through three distinct temporal phases characterized by differing rates of attrition and degrees of inter-simulation variability.

During the initial phase from step 0 to approximately step 475, the population undergoes a precipitous collapse from 125 to roughly 5–20 individuals. A critical inflection point occurs at step 305, marking the transition from the steepest decline to a decelerating descent. Statistical validation confirms this period exhibits strongly monotonic decreasing trends with Kendall's tau approaching -1.0 and significant negative slopes exceeding -0.06 per step. By the first major change point at step 475, the majority of simulation runs have reduced the female cohort to single-digit numbers, reflecting the intense hunting pressure exerted by 20 human foragers operating under conditions of high seasonal resource fluctuation (seasonality = 0.8) and moderate vegetation regrowth cycles.

The intermediate phase spanning steps 475 to 1,000 exhibits high heterogeneity across simulation runs, with the aggregate mean hovering between 1 and 20 individuals while the standard deviation remains elevated at approximately 31.5. Change points at steps 600 and 875 demarcate shifts in the extinction dynamics. During this interval, approximately half of the replicate populations reach zero, while others persist through stochastic oscillations. Inflection points at steps 740 and 833 indicate temporary moderations in the decline rate, likely corresponding to seasonal resource pulses that briefly sustain remnant herds. The median population value drops to 1.0 during this phase, indicating that the majority of runs maintain only a handful of survivors, though the mean of 16.5 reflects the right-skewed distribution caused by outliers exhibiting prolonged persistence.

The terminal phase from step 1,000 to 2,310 witnesses the final extinction of remaining lineages. While most populations perish by step 1,000, several outliers persist until steps 1,500–2,000 before succumbing. These late-surviving cohorts display oscillatory dynamics with a dominant periodicity of 63 steps, suggesting synchronization with seasonal environmental fluctuations or reproductive cycles. Rolling trend analysis reveals intermittent, weakly increasing trends (tau ≈ 0.09–0.45) during windows 1,160–1,391 and 1,856–2,087, reflecting temporary stabilizations or minor recoveries in individual runs before the final decline. The ultimate change point at step 2,310 coincides with the extinction of the final female animal, completing the population collapse.

Throughout the simulation, the variance between runs increases substantially after the initial collapse, with asynchronous extinction times creating divergence in population trajectories. This variability likely stems from the interaction of demographic stochasticity in small populations, spatial heterogeneity in hunter-prey encounters, and the dynamic optimal foraging decisions of human agents who may switch between targeting males and females based on energetic returns. The consistent terminal value of zero across all 40 replicates indicates that under the specified parameter configuration of moderate hunting pressure, strong seasonality, and the given life-history traits, the megaherbivore population lacks a viable refugium from which to recover, resulting in deterministic extinction despite temporary stochastic fluctuations in the decline rate.
