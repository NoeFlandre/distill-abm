The cumulative number of prey deaths starts at zero and increases in a stepwise manner throughout the simulation runs. During the first 500 time steps, the death count rises sharply, accumulating between 150 and 230 deaths across different runs. This initial rapid accumulation suggests an immediate and severe impact on the prey population from the combined effects of persistent hunting pressure by the 20 immortal foragers and baseline natural mortality processes.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of death accumulation decelerates considerably, with cumulative counts increasing from approximately 200 to final values ranging between 225 and 250. This slowing reflects the declining prey population density as herds approach local extinction, resulting in fewer encounters between hunters and remaining prey individuals. The stepwise pattern of increase indicates discrete mortality events occurring as specific herd agents succumb to energy depletion, senescence (after reaching the threshold of 7300 steps), or predation.

By approximately 2000 steps, the cumulative death counts plateau across all simulation runs, stabilizing at values between 230 and 250. Given the initial prey population of 250 herds, this plateau at near-initial population levels indicates that the prey population has been driven to effective extinction, with insufficient demographic recruitment to sustain the population against the constant foraging pressure and environmental constraints.

There is notable variability in the specific trajectories between runs: some simulations reach their maximum cumulative death count by step 1000, while others continue accumulating sporadic deaths until step 2000 or beyond. This divergence in extinction timing likely stems from stochastic variation in herd movement patterns across the toroidal landscape, the spatial distribution of grass resources (operating at 33% coverage with seasonal regrowth cycles of 3 days), and probabilistic hunter-prey encounter rates. The spread in final values, ranging from approximately 225 to 250 deaths, reflects differences in the total number of offspring produced before population collapse, with higher final counts indicating brief periods of successful reproduction that extended the persistence of maternal lineages before eventual mortality.
