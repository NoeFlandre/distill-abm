The cumulative number of females killed exhibits a characteristic saturating growth pattern across the 40 simulation replicates, ultimately converging on an asymptotic value of approximately 123 individuals. The temporal dynamics unfold through three distinct phases.

During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 125 to 375 steps, the metric accumulates rapidly from zero, with the aggregate mean reaching roughly 60–80 females killed by step 500. This acceleration reflects high encounter rates between the initial forager population of 20 agents and the ungulate herds, with hunting pressure quickly depleting the female demographic segment. Statistical change points at steps 125, 375, 475, and 625 mark transitions in the rate of increase, with the steepest accumulation occurring between steps 245 and 285 where the inflection in the growth curve appears.

By approximately step 850, the majority of individual simulation trajectories begin plateauing, with cumulative counts stabilizing between 110 and 125 females killed. The aggregate mean across all runs reaches 104.6, while the median of 119.1 indicates that most simulations achieved near-maximum kill counts. The rolling trend analysis confirms sustained significant increases through step 1159, after which the rate of new kills diminishes substantially. A statistically significant decreasing trend emerges in the final temporal window (steps 1856–2087), reflecting the exhaustion of hunt-able females.

Between runs, substantial variation exists in the timing of saturation. Some replicates reach maximum cumulative kills by step 600–700 (stabilizing around 108 individuals), while others continue accumulating kills gradually until step 2000 or beyond, ultimately reaching 122–124 individuals. This variability likely stems from stochastic differences in herd movement patterns and vegetation distribution affecting hunter-prey encounters.

In the plateau phase (steps 1000–2300), small oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 steps superimpose the otherwise flat trajectory. These fluctuations suggest residual hunting activity during specific seasonal windows when remaining females become temporarily available or vulnerable, coinciding with the model's strong seasonality parameter creating pulsatile resource availability.

The asymptotic value of approximately 123 females killed represents near-complete extirpation of the female population, given the initial ungulate abundance of 250 total animals. The female-specific food value of 27 energy units and low processing cost of 3 energy units—compared to 30 and 6 for males—rendered females preferred targets, contributing to the rapid depletion observed. The plateau indicates population collapse, where insufficient females remain to sustain either continued hunting opportunities or population recovery through reproduction, despite the 730-tick birth spacing allowing for potential population maintenance under lower mortality regimes.

The standard deviation of 31.1 across samples primarily reflects the transition period between active accumulation and saturation rather than uncertainty in the final outcome, as all trajectories converge toward similar maximum values regardless of the temporal path taken.
