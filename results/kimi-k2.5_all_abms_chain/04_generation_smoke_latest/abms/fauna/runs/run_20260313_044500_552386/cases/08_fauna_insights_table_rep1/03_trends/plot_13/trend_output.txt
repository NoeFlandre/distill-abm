The simulated megaherbivore populations exhibited a trajectory of progressive energy depletion over approximately 6.3 simulation years, transitioning from an initial state of maximum biomass energy (16,667 units) to complete exhaustion (0 units) by tick 2,311. The decline followed a markedly non-linear pattern characterized by distinct phases of decelerating loss superimposed with periodic fluctuations.

During the initial 350 ticks—roughly the first year—the system experienced rapid biomass reduction with depletion rates exceeding 24 units per tick, as the initial cohort of 250 prey animals faced intensive foraging pressure from 20 human hunters operating under optimal foraging constraints. This steep decline moderated through successive transition periods around ticks 500, 625, and 875, where the rate of energy loss decelerated progressively from approximately 36 units per tick to less than 1 unit per tick, suggesting density-dependent constraints on hunting efficiency as prey populations thinned.

Throughout the entire sequence, the total available energy oscillated with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks (roughly bi-monthly cycles), reflecting interactions between high environmental seasonality (0.8 parameter setting) and grass regrowth dynamics (3-tick regeneration cycles) that created rhythmic variations in forage availability. These oscillations generated temporary stabilization points where energy levels briefly plateaued—evidenced by recurring local peaks and valleys—but maintained an overall statistically significant decreasing trend across all observation windows.

By approximately tick 1,600, the system entered a terminal low-biomass phase (median energy 146 units) characterized by high volatility and demographically-driven fluctuations. Brief periods of localized energy increase occurred around ticks 1,624–1,855 and 1,856–2,087, likely corresponding to seasonal demographic rebounds or reduced hunting pressure during resource-scarce periods, yet these proved insufficient to reverse the overall trajectory. The population crossed critical viability thresholds approximately 500–600 ticks prior to final extinction, entering an irreversible decline as reproductive intervals (730 ticks) exceeded the remaining temporal window for population recovery, ultimately resulting in complete energy depletion at tick 2,311.
