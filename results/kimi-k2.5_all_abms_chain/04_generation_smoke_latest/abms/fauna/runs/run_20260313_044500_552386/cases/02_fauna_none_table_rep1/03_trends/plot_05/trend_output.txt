The cumulative count of postpartum female prey animals killed by forager bands exhibits a rapid initial accumulation followed by prolonged stabilization with minor oscillatory fluctuations. Beginning at zero, the metric increases steeply through the first 850 ticks, with distinct inflections in the rate of increase occurring between ticks 245 and 285, and significant trend transitions at ticks 475, 625, and 850. During this initial phase, the cumulative kills rise sharply toward approximately 115, reflecting intensive targeting of reproductive females who offer high net energetic returns due to combined adult and offspring food value relative to processing costs.

After tick 850, the accumulation decelerates substantially, entering a plateau phase that persists through approximately tick 1855. Throughout this interval, values stabilize within a narrow range of roughly 116.9 to 119.0, with the maximum value of 119.0 reached around tick 1826. The trend becomes statistically flat during portions of this plateau (ticks 1392–1623), suggesting that hunting pressure on postpartum females reaches an equilibrium state. This stabilization may indicate that forager success becomes limited by reduced prey encounter rates, demographic shifts in the herd structure, or optimal foraging decisions that moderate further accumulation.

From tick 1856 onward, the series exhibits a slight declining trend, decreasing from the peak of 119.0 to finish at 117.0 by tick 2312. This late-phase decrease coincides with detected oscillatory behavior possessing a dominant period of 63 ticks (approximately two months), visible in the clustered oscillation indices between ticks 2170 and 2265. These fluctuations likely reflect seasonal rhythms in herd movement, calving synchronization, or resource availability affecting hunter accessibility to reproductive females. The overall pattern—rapid saturation of cumulative reproductive female mortality within the first three to five years followed by stabilization—suggests that under the configured hunting pressure and life history parameters (including two-year birth spacing and high seasonality), the system converges toward a persistent equilibrium rather than exhibiting continuously accelerating predation pressure on the breeding population.
