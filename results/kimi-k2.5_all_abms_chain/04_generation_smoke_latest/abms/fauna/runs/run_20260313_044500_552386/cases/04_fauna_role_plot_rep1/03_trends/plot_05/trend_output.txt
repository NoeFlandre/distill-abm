Across the repeated simulation runs, the cumulative mortality of postpartum female ungulates exhibits a consistent pattern of rapid initial accumulation followed by asymptotic stabilization. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 400 to 600 time steps, the metric increases steeply from an initial value of zero, with most trajectories accumulating between 80 and 100 kills by step 600. This early phase displays considerable stochastic variation across replicates, with some simulations reaching 60 cumulative kills as early as step 300, while others show delayed accumulation with fewer than 20 kills until step 200 before accelerating rapidly.

Following the initial rapid increase, the rate of accumulation decelerates markedly during the intermediate phase spanning steps 600 to 1000. The trajectories transition to saturation plateaus ranging between approximately 105 and 125 total kills, with the majority of runs clustering around values of 115 to 120. The timing of stabilization varies substantially across stochastic replicates: some simulations reach their asymptotic values by step 600, while others continue gradual increases until steps 1500 to 1800. A small subset of trajectories exhibits extended accumulation periods, with final stabilization occurring near step 2000 at values approaching 120.

The discrete increments in the cumulative count reflect the event-based nature of hunting mortality in the agent-based framework, with each step-wise increase representing successful hunting events. The consistent upper bound of approximately 120 kills approaches the scale of the initial female population, suggesting that hunting pressure effectively depleted the cohort of reproductively active females within the first 3 to 5 years of simulation time. The subsequent stabilization indicates cessation of hunting mortality for this demographic, likely resulting from the exhaustion of available postpartum females through either direct mortality or population-level suppression of reproduction as the ungulate population responded to predation pressure.
