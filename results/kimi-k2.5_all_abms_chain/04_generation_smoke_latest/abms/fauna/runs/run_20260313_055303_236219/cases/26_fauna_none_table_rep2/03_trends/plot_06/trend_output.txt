The reproductive dynamics of the prey population exhibit distinct phased behavior across the simulation span. Initially, birth counts remain at zero during the earliest time steps, reflecting a demographic lag as the founding population of 250 individuals matures toward reproductive capability. Between indices 0 and approximately 725 (corresponding to roughly the first 63 years of the 200-year simulation), reproductive output remains minimal as herds establish and individuals reach sexual maturity given the 730-tick (2-year) birth spacing constraint.

Following this establishment phase, reproductive output enters a sustained growth period characterized by monotonically increasing trends from index 725 through 1624. During this interval, birth counts rise from near-zero values to a maximum of approximately 5.6 births per sampling interval, achieved around indices 1673-1702 (approximately years 145-147). This growth phase contains multiple local peaks of increasing magnitude: approximately 2.875 at index 1073 (year 93), 3.45 at index 1214 (year 105), and 4.3 at index 1392 (year 120), interspersed with valleys at indices 1097, 1281, and 1422. These oscillations occur with a regular periodicity of approximately 50 sampling intervals (equating to roughly 4.3 years), likely reflecting the interaction between the enforced 2-year birth spacing, seasonal resource limitations (seasonality parameter 0.8), and grass regrowth dynamics (3-tick regrowth time). The strong positive trends during windows spanning indices 696-1623 (tau values 0.78-0.85) indicate exponential-like population growth as the initial cohorts successfully reproduce and offspring survive to breeding age.

A critical transition occurs around index 1624 (year 140), marking the onset of a decline phase that persists through the remainder of the simulation. Birth counts decrease significantly from the maximum of 5.6, exhibiting negative trends from indices 1624-2087 (tau values -0.45 to -0.50). This decline proceeds through identified change points at indices 1325, 1850, 2050, and 2310, with inflection points at indices 1777, 1930, and 2195 indicating shifts in the rate of decrease. By the simulation terminus at index 2312 (year 200), reproductive output normalizes to a value of 1.0, representing a substantial reduction from peak productivity.

Interpreted within the MHPM framework, these trends suggest an initial colonization and population expansion phase where prey herds capitalize on available grassland resources (33% grass cover) and relatively low initial hunting pressure from the 20 forager agents. The peak reproductive output around year 145 likely represents the maximum sustainable breeding rate under the combined constraints of environmental carrying capacity and predation pressure. The subsequent decline indicates that hunting pressure—governed by optimal foraging logic considering the differential food values and processing costs between sexes (males: 30 food value, 6 processing cost; females: 27 food value, 3 processing cost)—eventually overwhelms the prey population's reproductive capacity. The persistent oscillatory pattern superimposed on both growth and decline phases reflects the model's seasonal breeding constraints and the biennial birth spacing parameter, creating pulsed recruitment events that become progressively dampened as the population contracts toward the end of the simulation period.
