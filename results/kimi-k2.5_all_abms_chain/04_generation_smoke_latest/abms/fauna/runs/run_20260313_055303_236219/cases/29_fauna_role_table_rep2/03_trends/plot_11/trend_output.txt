The aggregate energy stored within the male prey population initiates at 12,500 units and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction, reaching zero availability at approximately tick 2,284 and remaining absent through tick 2,312. 

The temporal dynamics divide into four distinct phases. The initial phase (ticks 0–400) exhibits high-amplitude oscillations superimposed upon a gradual decline. These fluctuations display a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks, peaking at tick 65 with residual energy near 11,608 units before descending. This rhythmic pattern, consistent with strong environmental seasonality (0.8), reflects pulsed resource availability affecting male condition and aggregation, while predation pressure from energetically optimizing hunters imposes an immediate net negative trend.

The second phase (roughly ticks 125–875) is characterized by serial regime changes marked by inflection points at ticks 316, 453, 516, 625, and 759, transitioning to 824. During this interval, the rate of energy depletion attenuates sequentially from steep initial losses (exceeding 14 units per tick) to moderating declines (approaching 0.8 units per tick). Change points at ticks 350, 500, and 875 demarcate shifts in the velocity of collapse, suggesting an initial overharvesting of high-value males (food value 30, processing cost 6) followed by diminishing returns as hunter effort encounters increasingly sparse targets.

A transient recovery interrupts the decline between ticks 1,392 and 1,623, wherein the trend reverses to a significant increase (tau = 0.548). This temporary resurgence likely reflects density-dependent ecological release or seasonal recruitment pulses that briefly elevate male energy reserves before predation pressure reasserts dominance.

The terminal phase (post-tick 1,856) exhibits negligible directional trends until the final collapse. The median energy value across the entire series (134.5 units) falls substantially below the mean (1,984 units), indicating a profoundly right-skewed distribution dominated by high initial biomass with prolonged low-energy conditions preceding extinction.

This extinction trajectory emerges from the interaction of sexually dimorphic hunting value—where males provide higher energetic returns (30 versus 27 for females) despite greater processing costs—and the presence of immortal foragers capable of indefinite energy accumulation. The combination eliminates demographic buffering, converting seasonal environmental oscillations into an irreversible depletion of male biomass despite vegetation regrowth capabilities (3-tick recovery) and moderate initial population density (250 animals).
