The cumulative number of female prey killed begins at zero across all simulation runs. During the initial approximately 500 time steps, the cumulative count increases rapidly and nonlinearly, with individual trajectories exhibiting stepwise increments of varying magnitude as discrete hunting events occur. By step 500, most trajectories have accumulated between 80 and 120 females killed, though considerable variation exists across runs regarding the specific rate of accumulation, with some runs showing steep increases of over 100 individuals within the first 400 steps while others remain below 50 until step 600.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of increase decelerates markedly for most runs, with many trajectories exhibiting extended plateaus where no additional females are harvested for significant durations, interspersed with occasional smaller increments of 1-5 individuals. After approximately step 1000, the majority of trajectories stabilize at asymptotic values ranging between approximately 108 and 125 total females killed, with only a minority of runs continuing to accumulate kills at reduced rates beyond this point, extending in some cases to step 2000 or later.

A subset of runs exhibits markedly delayed accumulation patterns, remaining at values below 20 until approximately step 300-400 before undergoing rapid increases that ultimately converge with the main cluster of trajectories. The final values demonstrate relatively tight convergence toward the 115-125 range despite substantial divergence in the timing and rate of accumulation during earlier phases, suggesting that the system encounters a consistent limiting constraint—likely the depletion of accessible female prey populations below viable hunting thresholds or the energetic saturation of forager bands given processing costs and movement requirements.

This pattern indicates an initial phase of intense hunting pressure exploiting the initial female herd population of 250 individuals, during which hunters pursue females due to their favorable food-value-to-processing-cost ratio (27:3) and the presence of nursing offspring increasing food value. The subsequent plateauing reflects a depletion phase where remaining prey become increasingly difficult to locate within the effective foraging radius or the expected energetic returns fail to justify pursuit costs, ultimately resulting in a quasi-equilibrium where female predation effectively ceases as the prey population is reduced below critical encounter probabilities or foragers switch to alternative prey strategies.
