**Temporal Dynamics of Reproductive Output in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model**

The simulation output reveals a three-phase pattern in ungulate reproductive activity over the 2,312-step observation period, characterized by an initial reproductive quiescence, a subsequent expansion phase, and a terminal decline. 

**Initial Latent Phase (Steps 0–725)**
For approximately the first 725 steps (nearly equivalent to the two-year interbirth interval of 730 ticks specified in the model parameters), the aggregated birth count remains at zero across all simulation runs. This plateau reflects the biological delay inherent in the system: the initial population of 250 animals requires time to reach reproductive maturity and complete the first gestation period before the first births can occur.

**Expansion Phase (Steps 725–1624)**
Following this lag, reproductive output increases significantly. From step 725 through approximately step 1624, the system exhibits statistically significant monotonic increases in birth frequency (Mann-Kendall tau values ranging from 0.392 to 0.851, p<0.001). The aggregate mean number of births rises from zero to a maximum value of 5.6, recorded at step 1673 (approximately 4.6 years into the simulation). Superimposed on this growth trend are regular oscillations with a dominant period of 50 steps, likely corresponding to seasonal reproductive pulses driven by the high seasonality parameter (0.8) and fluctuating vegetation availability. During this phase, individual simulation trajectories diverge considerably, with cumulative birth counts across the 40 runs ranging from near zero to approximately 10 individuals per run by step 2000.

**Decline Phase (Steps 1624–2312)**
After reaching peak productivity, the system enters a regime of decline. From step 1624 through step 2087, the birth count exhibits statistically significant decreasing trends (Mann-Kendall tau = -0.495 to -0.453, p<0.001), with the aggregate mean falling from the maximum of 5.6 to a terminal value of 1.0 by step 2312 (approximately 6.3 years). This reduction of over 80% from peak reproductive output suggests that the combined pressures of human hunting (20 foragers) and strong environmental seasonality progressively suppress recruitment, potentially driving populations toward reproductive failure or extinction in many runs.

**Heterogeneity in Outcomes**
While the aggregate mean follows this trajectory of rise and fall, individual simulations display substantial variability in cumulative reproductive success. By the end of the observation window, total births per run span from effectively zero to ten, indicating that certain combinations of stochastic events, hunter foraging decisions, and environmental fluctuations allow some lineages to persist while others collapse completely.

**Key Transition Points**
Change point detection identifies step 725 as the onset of reproduction, step 1325 as the transition to peak productivity, step 1850 as the initiation of the downward trend, and step 2310 as the approach to minimal reproductive output, marking distinct biological transitions in the coupled human-megafauna system.
