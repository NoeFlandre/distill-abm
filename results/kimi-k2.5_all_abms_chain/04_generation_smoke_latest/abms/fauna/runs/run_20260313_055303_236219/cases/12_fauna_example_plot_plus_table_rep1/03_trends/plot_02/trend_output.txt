The total energy available to all forager bands initiates at 2000 and declines continuously throughout the simulation, terminating at approximately -38,229 after 2310 time steps. This trajectory indicates a sustained energetic deficit where movement and hunting costs consistently exceed energy acquisition from prey.

The rate of energy depletion varies across three distinct phases. During the initial approximately 350 time steps, energy decreases at a moderate rate of roughly 11 units per step. Between time steps 232 and 463, the decline briefly slows to approximately 9 units per step, reaching a local plateau around -2000. Subsequently, the rate accelerates significantly, intensifying to roughly 16 units per step by time step 650, then to approximately 18-20 units per step by time step 1160. This accelerated rate of approximately 20 energy units per step stabilizes and persists through the remainder of the simulation.

Minor fluctuations interrupt the decline transiently around time step 55, where energy momentarily reaches approximately 1263 before resuming its descent, with additional brief hesitations around time steps 370 and 394. Despite these minor interruptions, the Mann-Kendall trend analysis confirms a monotonically decreasing pattern across all time windows (p < 0.001), with Kendall's tau values approaching -1.0.

The transition from moderate initial decline to sustained rapid depletion after time step 1160 suggests that forager bands are unable to locate sufficient prey to offset their metabolic and activity costs, leading to accumulated energy debt. Given that foragers are immortal and possess energy storage capabilities, the increasingly negative values represent progressive energetic impoverishment rather than mortality, indicating that the parameterized hunting success rates, prey densities, and processing costs create irreversible energetic deterioration over time.
