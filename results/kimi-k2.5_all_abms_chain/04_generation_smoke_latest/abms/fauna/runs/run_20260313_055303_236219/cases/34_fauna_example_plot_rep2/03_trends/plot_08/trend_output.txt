The number of male prey animals begins at approximately 125 individuals. Over the first 400 to 500 time steps, the population undergoes a rapid decline, decreasing to roughly 10 to 40 individuals depending on the specific simulation run. This steep initial reduction likely reflects intensive hunting pressure from the initial cohort of 20 foragers, who target males due to their higher food value relative to females (30 versus 27) despite the higher processing costs involved. 

Between steps 500 and 1000, the decline continues but at a decelerating rate, with most simulation runs reaching 10 or fewer males by step 1000. This slowing decline suggests that as male density decreases, encounter rates between hunters and male prey drop, temporarily buffering the remaining individuals. However, because foragers are immortal and can store energy, hunting pressure persists indefinitely without natural attrition of the hunter population.

By approximately step 1500, the majority of simulation runs reach zero male animals, though some runs exhibit extended persistence with small populations of 1 to 5 individuals lasting until steps 1200 to 1600, and rare cases showing survival until beyond step 2000. The eventual extinction of males across all runs indicates that the combination of continuous hunting pressure, the absence of hunter mortality, and the finite reproductive capacity of the herd (governed by birth-spacing intervals of 730 ticks) creates conditions where male replenishment cannot compensate for hunting losses. The variance across runs, particularly pronounced between steps 300 and 800, likely stems from stochastic differences in herd movement patterns, forager search efficiency, and the demographic timing of male births and deaths.
