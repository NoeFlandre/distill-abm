Across all simulation replicates, the megaherbivore population exhibits a deterministic trajectory of decline from the initial abundance of 250 animals toward complete extinction. The temporal dynamics of this collapse vary substantially across stochastic realizations, with extinction times ranging from approximately 400 to 2,100 time steps.

Approximately one-third of replicates demonstrate rapid population collapse, declining from 250 to fewer than 10 individuals within 400 to 600 time steps and reaching zero abundance shortly thereafter. An intermediate group of trajectories shows more gradual decrease, maintaining populations of 30 to 80 individuals through step 500 before declining to extinction between steps 800 and 1,200. The most persistent replicates exhibit slow, prolonged declines, sustaining 10 to 30 individuals through step 800, subsequently declining to 5 or fewer animals by step 1,200, with small remnant populations persisting until final extinction occurs between steps 1,500 and 2,100.

By step 1,000, approximately 70-80% of replicates have reached effective extinction (0-5 individuals), while the remaining trajectories persist at critically low densities. The decline is largely monotonic across all replicates, though several trajectories exhibit temporary reductions in the rate of decline or minor fluctuations when populations reach intermediate densities (50-100 animals), typically occurring between steps 200 and 600. No replicates exhibit recovery trajectories or stabilization at positive equilibrium densities; all populations terminate at or near zero abundance by the conclusion of the simulation period.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The universal extinction outcome across all stochastic replicates indicates that the parameter configuration featuring 20 immortal foragers with food storage capability, operating in a highly seasonal environment (seasonality = 0.8) with limited vegetation cover (33% initial grass proportion), generates hunting pressure that fundamentally exceeds the maximum sustainable yield of the megaherbivore population. The 1,700-time-step variation in extinction timing reflects demographic stochasticity and spatial heterogeneity in hunter-prey encounters, but the absence of any persistent or recovering trajectories demonstrates that random variation cannot compensate for the deterministic overharvesting pressure. To achieve population stability or coexistence, interventions would need to substantially reduce hunting pressure through modifications such as enabling hunter mortality (disabling the immortal-foragers parameter), reducing initial hunter numbers below the threshold of 20, or decreasing foraging efficiency through reduced foraging radius or increased processing costs. The rapid extinction observed in the fastest-declining replicates (within 400-500 steps) suggests the system operates far from sustainability, indicating that marginal adjustments to parameters would likely be insufficient to alter the outcome.
