The aggregate energy status of forager bands exhibits a consistent and substantial decline throughout the temporal sequence, initiating at 2000 energy units and deteriorating to approximately -38229 units by tick 2310. This trajectory represents a systematic accumulation of energy debt across the population of 20 hunters.

The rate of energy depletion accelerates markedly over the course of the simulation. During the initial phase from tick 0 to approximately 650, the decline proceeds at a rate of roughly 11 units per tick. Between ticks 650 and 1075, the slope moderates slightly to approximately 8.8 units per tick, but subsequently steepens dramatically. From tick 1160 onward, the rate of loss stabilizes at approximately 20 units per tick—a value corresponding exactly to the collective baseline movement costs of the 20 forager agents, suggesting that energy acquisition from hunting effectively ceases to offset metabolic expenditures.

Superimposed upon this downward trajectory are regular oscillations with a dominant period of 63 ticks, persisting throughout the entire sequence including the terminal phase between ticks 2170 and 2265. These cyclic fluctuations modulate the underlying decline, creating local extrema at regular intervals.

Key temporal milestones illustrate the progression: by tick 394, aggregate energy has declined to approximately -2006; by tick 1594, it reaches -23807; by tick 1826, approximately -28403; and by tick 2284, roughly -37713. Inflection points at ticks 358, 1238, 1329, 1397, 1402, 1422, and subsequent intervals mark transitions in the curvature of the decline trajectory. Change points detected at ticks 650, 1075, 1500, and 1925 demarcate distinct phases in the rate of energy loss.

Statistical analysis across 40 replicated series confirms a monotonically decreasing trend throughout all temporal windows (Kendall's tau approaching -1.0, p < 0.001), with the median value of -15095.8625 indicating that the typical energy state is deeply negative.

In the context of the model parameters, this pattern indicates that despite immortality and food storage capabilities enabling negative energy balances, foragers are unable to achieve sustainable energy budgets. The 63-tick oscillatory component likely reflects seasonal resource fluctuations (consistent with the 0.8 seasonality setting and 365-tick annual cycle) modulating hunting success rates, while the initial phase of slower decline corresponds to a transient period of higher prey availability before population depression.
