After approximately step 1500 to 1700, most trajectories stabilize at constant values, indicating cessation of reproductive activity. This stabilization persists through the end of the observation period at step 2100, with final cumulative birth counts spanning a wide range from approximately 2 to 10 births across different runs. A subset of runs maintains reproductive activity longer, with births continuing until steps 1800 to 2000, suggesting variable persistence times for reproductive-age females across stochastic realizations.

The variance in final outcomes—an order of magnitude separating the lowest and highest cumulative birth counts—demonstrates high demographic stochasticity under the current parameter configuration. The stepwise pattern with extended plateaus between jumps reflects the rigid birth-spacing constraint of 730 steps, which prevents continuous recruitment and creates discrete reproductive waves even in populations that persist longer.

Implications for decision makers:

The mandatory 750-step reproductive lag creates a critical vulnerability window where prey populations cannot offset mortality through births, exposing them to extinction risk from hunting pressure during the initial two-year period. The high variability in final birth outcomes indicates that 20 foragers with the specified processing costs and foraging radius generate unpredictable demographic dynamics, where stochastic factors determine whether populations persist or collapse rather than deterministic equilibrium processes. The early cessation of reproduction in most runs by step 1500 suggests the current hunting pressure likely exceeds sustainable thresholds, as populations fail to maintain reproductive activity beyond approximately four years. To improve conservation outcomes, managers should consider reducing hunter density, increasing initial prey population sizes beyond 250 individuals, or evaluating modifications to birth-spacing parameters that could accelerate reproductive output during the critical early period. The discrete nature of the stepwise increases confirms that small changes to inter-birth intervals could substantially impact total reproductive success before population depletion occurs.
