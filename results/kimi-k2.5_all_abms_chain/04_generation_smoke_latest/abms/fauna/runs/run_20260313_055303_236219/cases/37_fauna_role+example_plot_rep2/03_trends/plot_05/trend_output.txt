The cumulative number of postpartum female prey killed begins at zero across all simulation runs. During the initial approximately 400 to 600 time steps, the metric increases rapidly in a stepwise fashion, with most trajectories accumulating between 60 and 100 kills by step 600. This early phase reflects intensive hunting pressure on nursing females, who represent high-value targets due to their favorable energy return relative to processing costs.

Between approximately 600 and 1,000 time steps, the rate of increase decelerates markedly across all runs. The trajectories begin to diverge during this transitional phase, with some simulations continuing to accumulate kills at a moderate pace while others approach saturation. By step 1,000, cumulative kills range from approximately 90 to 115 across the ensemble of runs.

After approximately 1,000 to 1,200 time steps, the metric stabilizes into a plateau phase that persists through the remainder of the simulation period. Final cumulative values converge between approximately 105 and 125 postpartum females killed, with most trajectories settling between 110 and 120. The plateau indicates that the population of postpartum females available for hunting becomes effectively depleted or inaccessible, halting further accumulation despite continued forager activity.

The substantial variance observed across simulation runs—particularly in the timing of plateau onset and final cumulative totals—reflects stochastic variation in spatial encounter rates between hunters and prey, as well as demographic stochasticity in the production and survival of postpartum females across different random seeds.
