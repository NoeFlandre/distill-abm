The total energy contained within the male animal population initiates at approximately 12,500 units, representing the collective energetic reserves of the initial male cohort. This metric undergoes an immediate and precipitous decline throughout the first 125 time steps, with statistical tests confirming a strongly significant decreasing trend (slope -14.798) as the system responds to initial hunting pressures. By approximately step 350, the aggregate energy has fallen below 4,500 units, with localized fluctuations appearing between steps 377 and 408 where transient peaks and valleys emerge around values of 3,900–4,000 units, suggesting brief periods of energetic stability or demographic buffering before further depletion.

A second phase of rapid decline occurs between steps 350 and 500, characterized by multiple inflection points at steps 316, 453, and 516 that delineate shifts in the rate of energy loss. By step 625, the total energy has diminished substantially, continuing to decline through step 875 with progressively shallower slopes (transitioning from -28.062 to -10.723 and eventually to -0.831), indicating a slowing of energy depletion as the population nears critically low numbers. 

Between steps 1,392 and 1,623, the metric exhibits a temporary statistically significant increasing trend (slope 0.091, p<0.001), representing a brief recovery or stabilization of male energetic reserves before resuming its terminal decline. The population ultimately reaches zero total energy by step 2,284, indicating complete extinction of the male cohort or total energetic exhaustion thereof.

Throughout the simulation, the trajectory demonstrates high variability across the 40 replicated runs (standard deviation 3,478), with the most pronounced divergence occurring during the middle phase between steps 300 and 800. This variability reflects the stochastic nature of hunting success, forager decision-making under optimal foraging theory, and environmental seasonality effects. The oscillation pattern with a dominant period of 63 steps suggests cyclical fluctuations potentially linked to seasonal resource availability (seasonality parameter 0.8) or reproductive cycles affecting male energy accumulation.

The steep initial decline aligns with the higher food value assigned to males (30 units) compared to females (27 units), rendering them preferred targets for human foragers operating under energy-maximizing strategies, despite the associated higher processing costs. The stepwise nature of the decline, marked by six distinct change points and multiple inflection points, reflects the complex interplay between predation pressure, energetic gain from vegetation (1.5 units per consumption event), movement costs, and demographic stochasticity in the agent-based system.
