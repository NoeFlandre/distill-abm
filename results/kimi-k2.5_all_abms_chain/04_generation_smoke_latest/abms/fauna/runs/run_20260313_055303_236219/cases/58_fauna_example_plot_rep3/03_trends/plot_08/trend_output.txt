The number of male animals initializes at approximately 125 individuals across all simulation runs, consistent with the starting prey abundance and sexual dimorphism parameters. Over the first 400 to 500 steps, this metric undergoes a steep, sustained decline across all trajectories, though the specific rate of decrease diverges substantially between runs. By step 500, remaining male counts range from zero to approximately sixty individuals distributed stochastically, indicating the onset of high variability in demographic outcomes as hunting pressure and environmental constraints take effect.

Following this initial collapse, surviving populations continue to attenuate, with the majority reaching extinction between steps 600 and 1000. However, certain simulations exhibit markedly prolonged persistence, maintaining small cohorts of fewer than ten males for extended durations. These residual populations display highly variable extinction chronologies, with final declines to zero males occurring sporadically across a wide temporal window spanning roughly step 1000 to step 2150. No simulation maintains a male population beyond approximately 2200 steps.

This pattern of rapid initial depletion followed by stochastic persistence at low densities suggests that hunting pressure is immediately intensive and highly effective against the initially abundant male cohorts. The divergence in extinction timing likely reflects the interaction of several stochastic processes: probabilistic hunter-prey encounter rates within the foraging radius, herd movement dynamics across the grassland matrix, and demographic stochasticity when populations fall below critical thresholds. The eventual extinction of all male populations—despite variation in persistence time—indicates that under the current parameterization, which includes immortal foragers with energy storage capabilities and optimal foraging logic targeting high-value male prey, the rate of anthropogenic mortality significantly exceeds the maximum potential reproductive recruitment rate of the megaherbivore population, resulting in inevitable demographic collapse irrespective of transient stochastic buffering.
