At initialization, the number of female animals begins at approximately 120 to 125 individuals across all simulation runs. Immediately thereafter, all trajectories exhibit a marked and persistent decline in female population numbers. During the initial phase spanning roughly the first 500 steps, the decrease is particularly steep, with most simulation runs falling to between 0 and 40 females by step 500.

Following this rapid initial decline, the rate of decrease attenuates considerably. Between steps 500 and 1000, many trajectories continue to decline gradually toward zero, though substantial variation exists across simulation runs regarding the exact timing of population collapse. By approximately steps 1000 to 1500, the majority of runs have reached extinction, with female counts declining to zero.

A subset of simulation runs exhibits extended persistence, maintaining small populations of fewer than five females for several hundred additional steps, with the longest-running simulations extending beyond step 2000 before ultimately declining to zero. This variation in persistence times reflects the stochastic nature of demographic processes and hunting encounters within the agent-based framework.

In the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these trends indicate that under the specified parameter configuration, hunting pressure generates unsustainable mortality rates that consistently drive megaherbivore populations toward extinction. The initial rapid decline suggests that hunting pressure immediately exceeds the population's reproductive replacement capacity. The long tail of persistence in some runs demonstrates that stochastic factors occasionally allow small populations to persist temporarily, but eventual extinction remains the deterministic outcome across all replicates.
