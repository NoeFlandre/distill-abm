The cumulative mortality of postpartum females across replicate simulations exhibits a saturation pattern characterized by rapid initial accumulation followed by asymptotic stabilization. At initialization, the count begins at zero. During the first 500 time steps, accumulation occurs at the highest rates, with most simulations recording between approximately 60 and 120 postpartum female deaths by step 500. The most rapid accumulations achieve 80-100 kills by step 300, while slower progressing simulations reach 60-80 kills by the same temporal benchmark.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of increase diminishes substantially for the majority of simulation runs. The cumulative count transitions from linear growth to saturation, with most trajectories stabilizing between 110 and 125 individuals by step 1000. Final asymptotic values range from approximately 105 to 125 postpartum females killed.

A subset of simulations exhibits extended accumulation dynamics, with counts continuing to rise gradually until steps 1500-2000 before reaching stability, whereas the majority plateau between steps 600 and 1200. This variability suggests stochastic differences in encounter rates between foragers and prey herds across the 211-by-211 patch landscape.

Within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, the saturation pattern between 105-120 cumulative deaths reflects the exhaustion of the postpartum female demographic from the initial population of 250 ungulates. With 20 human foragers operating under optimal foraging protocols (foraging radius of 6 patches, processing cost of 3 energy units, food value of 27 energy units), postpartum females represent high-return prey items. The steep initial mortality trajectory indicates efficient harvesting of this demographic during early simulation phases when prey densities are highest.

The stabilization of cumulative mortality near 120 individuals suggests that hunters deplete nearly the entire standing crop of postpartum females that enter this reproductive state during the first 2000 time steps. Given the interbirth interval of 730 ticks (2 years), the pool of postpartum females is replenished through reproduction, but hunters successfully intercept approximately 90-95% of females that enter the postpartum state. The plateau indicates that after step 1000-2000, either the remaining female population has dropped below critical thresholds for reproduction, the age structure has shifted to preclude postpartum status, or encounter rates with this demographic become negligible due to prey population collapse or spatial refugia effects.

The secondary peak in variability around steps 500-800, where some simulations lag behind the main cluster before eventually catching up or stabilizing at lower asymptotes (105-115 versus 120-125), likely reflects heterogeneity in vegetation distribution (grass proportion 0.33, regrowth time 3 ticks) and seasonal dynamics (seasonality 0.8) affecting herd movement patterns and forager search efficiency across different landscape configurations.
