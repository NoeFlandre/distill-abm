The total energy of female animals initializes at approximately 12,000 units, representing the collective energetic biomass of the starting cohort. This aggregate metric undergoes a precipitous decline during the first 400 to 500 steps, though specific trajectories diverge substantially across simulation runs. While some populations experience continuous monotonic decreases reaching minimal energy levels by step 400, others exhibit stepped decline patterns characterized by temporary plateaus at intermediate values ranging from 2,000 to 4,000 units that persist until approximately step 800.

Between steps 500 and 1,500, the remaining populations continue to decrease, with energy levels dropping below 2,000 units and approaching zero. By step 2,000, all trajectories converge to effectively zero, indicating complete depletion of female energetic reserves.

The substantial variance observed during the initial 400 steps—with some runs maintaining over 10,000 units while others approach zero—reflects stochastic variation in hunter-prey encounter probabilities under high seasonality conditions (0.8) and the spatial configuration of the 211-by-211 patch grid. The intermittent plateaus evident in certain trajectories suggest temporary periods where surviving female aggregations access spatial refugia or where hunting efficiency decreases due to reduced prey density and the foraging radius constraints of human agents operating under optimal foraging theory.

The ultimate convergence toward zero across all replicates, despite divergent intermediate pathways, indicates that the combination of 20 human foragers with food storage capabilities and immortality, operating under high environmental seasonality and the specified ungulate life history parameters, leads to deterministic extinction of the female population within approximately 2,000 time steps (roughly 5.5 years), though the specific pathway to collapse varies stochastically based on initial spatial distributions and encounter rates.
