The cumulative birth record across the 40 replicate simulations exhibits a pronounced temporal structure initiated by an extended reproductive latency period. For approximately the first 725 time steps (roughly two years), births remain absent, consistent with the imposed interbirth interval of 730 ticks and the maturation requirements of the initial population of 250 prey. Following this establishment phase, reproductive output accelerates significantly, attaining a maximum mean value of 5.6 births near step 1702 during a sustained expansion period characterized by statistically significant increasing trends from step 696 through step 1855. 

This growth proves transient, as the trajectory subsequently reverses, exhibiting statistically significant decreasing trends from step 1856 forward. By the terminal observation at step 2312, the mean cumulative count declines to 1.0, indicating a collapse in reproductive recruitment despite the initial reproductive success. Superimposed upon these broad trends, the system manifests regular oscillatory behavior with a dominant periodicity of approximately 50 time steps, likely reflecting the influence of the imposed seasonal environmental cycle (seasonality parameter: 0.8) on reproductive timing. 

Substantial heterogeneity characterizes the individual simulation trajectories, with cumulative birth counts ranging from zero to values exceeding 10 across the replicates. This wide dispersion indicates high sensitivity to stochastic demographic processes and localized extinction events, suggesting that while populations initially capitalize on available resources to reproduce, they subsequently face mounting constraints—potentially arising from the interactive effects of hunter foraging behavior, limited grassland productivity (grass proportion: 0.33), and density-dependent feedback—that ultimately curtail recruitment below sustainable levels.
