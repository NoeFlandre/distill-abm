The cumulative mortality among the prey population initiates at zero and follows a saturating trajectory over the approximately 2000 time steps observed (representing roughly 5.5 years in the modeled system). During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, deaths accumulate rapidly, with most simulation runs recording between 150 and 230 cumulative fatalities by this point. This early surge reflects the immediate impact of the initial hunting pressure from 20 human foragers acting upon the starting population of 250 animals, compounded by baseline physiological mortality and environmental stressors including high seasonality (0.8) and limited vegetation recovery.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of mortality accumulation decelerates markedly across all simulation runs. During this interval, cumulative death counts increase from approximately 200 to between 220 and 245. This deceleration likely stems from prey depletion effects—as the population density declines, remaining animals become more difficult for hunters to encounter, and the absolute pool of animals susceptible to natural mortality diminishes.

After approximately 1000 to 1500 time steps, the cumulative death trajectories stabilize and plateau at final values ranging from approximately 220 to 250. This saturation suggests that the system has reached a demographic endpoint where either the local prey population has been driven to effective extinction, or the remaining animals exist in such low densities that further mortality events become exceedingly rare. The plateau levels approaching the initial population size of 250 indicate substantial turnover or collapse of the founding cohort within the first 5-6 years of simulated time.

Considerable variability exists across simulation runs, particularly evident during the intermediate phase between steps 300 and 800. Some trajectories exhibit rapid mortality accumulation reaching 200 deaths by step 500, while slower runs record as few as 75 deaths by this point and do not reach comparable cumulative totals until approximately step 1000. This divergence reflects stochastic variation in agent-level interactions, including the spatial patterning of hunter-prey encounters, herd movement dynamics across the 211×211 landscape, and the patchy distribution of vegetation resources. By the end of the observation window, most trajectories converge on similar plateau values, suggesting consistent long-term outcomes despite variable transient dynamics.
