The simulations consistently resulted in the extinction of female megaherbivore populations, initiating from a cohort of 125 individuals and declining to zero across all 40 replicate runs. The temporal dynamics exhibited a pronounced non-linear decay characterized by distinct phases of population reduction rather than a uniform decline.

During the initial phase (approximately steps 0–305), populations underwent a precipitous collapse, with numbers plummeting rapidly from the starting value of 125. This sharp reduction reflects the immediate impact of hunting pressure from the initial forager population of 20 hunters applying optimal foraging strategies on the virgin prey population. The presence of inflection points at steps 305 and 740 indicates transitions in the rate of decline, with the trajectory moderating after the initial crash but continuing downward through significant change points identified at steps 475 and 600.

Following the initial reduction, populations entered a prolonged persistence phase at critically low densities, evidenced by a median abundance of merely 1 individual across all time steps, contrasted against a mean value of approximately 16.5. This disparity indicates highly skewed distributions where most simulation time was spent at near-extinction levels interspersed with occasional small aggregations. During this phase, which extended from roughly step 600 onward through multiple inflection points (833, 1309, 1410), populations exhibited oscillatory dynamics with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 steps—corresponding to roughly two-month cycles given the 365 ticks-per-year calibration. These oscillations likely reflect seasonal resource fluctuations (seasonality parameter 0.8) modulating the interaction between surviving females and hunting pressure, creating brief windows of marginal recruitment followed by renewed hunting mortality.

Statistical analyses confirmed significant monotonic declines across all early temporal windows, with Mann-Kendall tests revealing strong decreasing trends (tau approaching -1.0, p < 0.001) throughout the first 1,159 steps (approximately three years). Later periods showed weakened or statistically complex patterns, including brief intervals of population increase (steps 1160–1391 and 1856–2087, tau = 0.093 and 0.453 respectively), representing stochastic fluctuations or temporary escape from predation pressure at low densities.

Terminal extinction occurred asynchronously across replicates, with different simulation runs reaching zero females between approximately steps 1000 and 2200, indicating substantial stochasticity in the precise timing of final population loss despite deterministic overall outcomes. The final change point at step 2310 marks the extinction of the last persisting lineages, representing roughly 6.3 years of simulation time before complete elimination of the female cohort.

This pattern of rapid initial collapse followed by prolonged low-density persistence and eventual inevitable extinction suggests that under the modeled combination of hunting pressure (20 foragers), sexual dimorphism (females providing 27 food units with only 3 processing cost versus males at 30/6), and high environmental seasonality (0.8), female megaherbivore populations face an extinction debt where demographic stochasticity and sustained predation pressure preclude recovery. The processing efficiency advantage for females likely maintained hunting interest in this sex even at critically low population levels, preventing the formation of viable refugia necessary for long-term persistence. The 63-day oscillation cycle during the extinction phase indicates that seasonal vegetation dynamics continued to modulate population viability even as the overall trajectory moved inexorably toward zero.
