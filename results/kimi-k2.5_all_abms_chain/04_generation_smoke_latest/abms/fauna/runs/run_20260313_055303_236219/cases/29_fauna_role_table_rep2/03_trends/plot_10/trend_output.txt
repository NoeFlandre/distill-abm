The simulated megaherbivore population initiates at a mean abundance of approximately 167 individuals, immediately subject to intensive hunting pressure from 20 human foragers operating under conditions of energy surplus accumulation and immortality. The trajectory exhibits monotonic decline punctuated by seasonal oscillations, ultimately terminating in extinction at approximately 6.3 years (tick 2311).

During the initial 125 ticks (first 4 months), the population undergoes precipitous decline from 167 individuals toward levels below 100, driven by the confluence of human hunting, baseline mortality (2.5% per tick), and predation (0.25% per tick). This rapid contraction continues with accelerating severity through tick 463 (month 15), representing the primary collapse phase where recruitment through reproduction—constrained by 730-tick (2-year) interbirth intervals and nutritional limitations—proves inadequate to offset mortality.

Subsequent to this initial crash, the system enters a prolonged period of oscillating decay between ticks 475 and 1391 (years 1.3-3.8), characterized by 63-day periodic fluctuations reflecting strong environmental seasonality (0.8 intensity) and vegetation regrowth dynamics (3-tick recovery cycles). Within this phase, ephemeral recoveries generate secondary peaks at approximately years 1.9 (tick 709, ~9 individuals) and 2.0 (tick 730, ~11 individuals), with successively attenuated maxima occurring at years 2.2, 2.4, and beyond, each rally failing to reestablish viable population sizes above 12 individuals.

Two brief demographic reversals occur in years 3.8-4.4 (ticks 1392-1623) and 5.1-5.7 (ticks 1856-2087), where mean growth rates turn marginally positive, suggesting temporary alignments of favorable seasonal conditions, vegetation availability, and reduced hunting mortality that allow marginal population increases. However, these periods of increase cannot sustain momentum against the constant foraging pressure from immortal hunters, and the population resumes decline.

Throughout the trajectory, the distribution of abundances remains heavily right-skewed, with median values falling to 1.5 individuals despite a mean of 25, indicating that after the initial collapse, the population persists primarily at critically low densities where stochastic effects dominate. The final extinction event occurs uniformly across simulation replicates by tick 2311, demonstrating that under the parameterization of moderate sexual dimorphism (male food value 30 vs. female 27, processing costs 6 vs. 3), high seasonality, and continuous hunter presence without density-dependent feedback on hunter mortality, megaherbivore populations inevitably collapse despite intermittent reproductive pulses and temporary stabilization phases.
