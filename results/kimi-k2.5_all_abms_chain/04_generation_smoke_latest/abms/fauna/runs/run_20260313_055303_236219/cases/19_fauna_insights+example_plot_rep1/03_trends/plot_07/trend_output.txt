The cumulative number of prey deaths initiates at zero and undergoes rapid acceleration during the first 400 to 600 time steps, escalating to values ranging between approximately 200 and 240 individuals. This steep initial trajectory reflects immediate hunting pressure on the initial population of 250 prey animals, compounded by natural mortality factors and environmental constraints.

Between 600 and 1200 steps, the accumulation rate diminishes substantially, with the metric increasing gradually toward asymptotic levels. During this interval, substantial variation emerges across simulation runs, with some scenarios stabilizing near 220 total deaths while others continue increasing toward 245 to 250 deaths. This divergence highlights the influence of stochastic factors—such as hunter-prey encounter rates, spatial distribution of grass resources, and herd movement patterns—on the tempo of population decline.

Beyond 1500 steps, the majority of runs reach stable plateaus, with final cumulative values clustering between 220 and 250 deaths. The convergence near the initial population size of 250 suggests extensive depletion of the prey base, accounting for both the original cohort and limited offspring production governed by the two-year birth spacing interval. The presence of continued, albeit slowed, mortality accumulation in some runs until approximately 2000 steps indicates that certain populations persisted longer before succumbing to the sustained foraging pressure exerted by the constant hunter population.

The pronounced heterogeneity in the timing of mortality accumulation—particularly evident between steps 300 and 900—demonstrates significant sensitivity to initial conditions and random interactions within the spatial environment. However, the consistent approach toward high mortality endpoints across all runs reveals a deterministic trajectory toward population exhaustion under the current configuration of immortal foragers, seasonal resource constraints, and life history parameters.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The data indicates that current hunting pressures and life history constraints produce an inevitable progression toward near-complete prey mortality within approximately five to seven years. While stochastic variation creates uncertainty in the specific timing of extinction events, the absence of scenarios showing sustained population persistence suggests that the current system lacks sufficient buffering capacity against hunting pressure. Interventions to reduce foraging efficiency or enhance prey recruitment would need to be implemented early, within the first 300 steps, to alter the trajectory before the majority of mortality occurs. The plateau behavior observed after step 1000 in most runs implies that once the population is reduced below critical thresholds, recovery becomes improbable regardless of subsequent management actions, emphasizing the importance of preventive rather than reactive conservation strategies.
