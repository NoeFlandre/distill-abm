The percentage of vegetated landscape patches initiates at approximately 33%, consistent with the initial grass proportion parameter setting. Within the first 50 time steps, this metric undergoes a modest decline to approximately 32%, followed by a gradual recovery to 32.5–33% by step 300. This early stability reflects an initial equilibrium between vegetation coverage and herbivore grazing pressure from the starting population of 250 ungulates. At approximately step 350, the system experiences a catastrophic vegetation crash across simulation runs, with the percentage of green patches plummeting to between 23% and 30%. The most severe drops reach approximately 23%, representing a loss of nearly one-third of the initial vegetated landscape. This collapse corresponds to the first annual cycle's resource minimum, where high seasonal stress (seasonality = 0.8) coincides with accumulated grazing pressure to temporarily exceed the vegetation's regrowth capacity. Following this crash, rapid recovery occurs, with values returning to 32–33% by step 400 and stabilizing near 33% by step 700. This resilience demonstrates the grassland's capacity to regenerate given the fast regrowth parameter (3 ticks). A second vegetation decline occurs around step 700–750, with values dropping from 33% to approximately 29–32%. While significant, this second crash is notably less severe than the first, indicating system adjustment to grazing pressure. The pattern repeats with a third decline around step 1100–1200, where the percentage falls to approximately 31–33%, showing further attenuation of seasonal vegetation fluctuations. In the late phase (steps 1200–2200), vegetation coverage stabilizes primarily around 32.5–33%, with substantially reduced seasonal amplitude. Some simulation runs exhibit minor step-like variations between steps 1400–2200, briefly declining to approximately 32.5% before recovering, while others maintain constant values near 33%. The progressive dampening of seasonal crashes over the 2000+ time steps suggests the system evolves toward a dynamic equilibrium where herbivore grazing pressure and vegetation regrowth become synchronized. The initial crash represents a transient overshoot where herbivore populations exert pressure exceeding seasonal carrying capacity, while subsequent years show improved stability as the system adjusts. Implications for decision makers: The model indicates that grassland systems with rapid regrowth capacity can recover from severe overgrazing events, but the first year of herbivore population establishment may trigger vegetation losses of up to 30%. Management interventions should prioritize the initial adjustment period (first 365–400 time steps) to prevent catastrophic resource depletion. The high seasonality setting creates predictable annual stress points, but the system's resilience suggests that megaherbivore populations and grassland productivity can achieve stable coexistence after approximately 3–4 years (1000–1200 ticks). The divergence in late-phase behavior across runs indicates that stochastic factors may lead to slightly different stable states, suggesting that monitoring during the critical first year is essential for long-term system stability.
