Over the initial 400 to 500 time steps, the male population undergoes a steep decline, dropping from 125 to between 0 and 40 individuals depending on the specific stochastic realization. By step 500, some populations have already crashed to zero, while others retain 20–30 males, indicating high variability in the early response to hunting pressure and environmental constraints.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the decline continues but at a reduced rate for populations that persist. During this interval, counts typically range from 0 to 25 males, with a gradual downward trajectory punctuated by occasional plateaus where populations briefly stabilize before resuming their decline. This suggests intermittent periods where hunting pressure is temporarily relaxed or resource availability allows for slight demographic recovery, though these reprieves prove insufficient to reverse the overall trend.

After approximately 1000 to 1200 steps, nearly all simulation runs converge toward extinction, with male counts dropping below 10 individuals and approaching zero. A small number of runs exhibit extended persistence, maintaining low numbers of males (1–5 individuals) until approximately 1500 to 2200 steps, but ultimately these populations also succumb.

The variance across simulation runs is moderate in the initial 300 steps but increases substantially between steps 400 and 800, reflecting divergent outcomes based on stochastic encounters between hunters and prey, variations in resource distribution, and demographic stochasticity in reproduction. As populations approach critical thresholds below 10 individuals, variance decreases as most runs reach the absorbing state of extinction.

This pattern indicates that under the specified parameter configuration—combining 20 human hunters with strong seasonal resource variation (0.8 seasonality) and the defined sexual dimorphism in food value—the male segment of the megaherbivore population faces deterministic decline driven by unsustainable hunting mortality. The higher nutritional value assigned to males (30 vs. 27 for females) likely contributes to their elevated vulnerability, accelerating their removal from the population relative to females. The extended tail of some temporal patterns suggests that under certain stochastic conditions, small remnant populations can persist for extended periods before final extinction, representing a long-term decline phase rather than immediate collapse.

**Implications for decision makers:** The consistent downward trajectory demonstrates that the current hunting pressure and environmental configuration drive male megaherbivore populations toward extinction within approximately 500 to 1500 time steps (roughly 1.4 to 4.1 years given 365 ticks per year). The high variability in extinction timing suggests that while outcomes are inevitable, precise timelines depend on stochastic factors. Conservation interventions would need to reduce hunter numbers or encounter rates substantially to prevent collapse, as the current configuration exceeds sustainable mortality thresholds for males. The sexual dimorphism in hunting value creates a male-biased harvest that could destabilize population structure even before total numbers reach critical lows, potentially requiring sex-specific harvest regulations to maintain breeding populations.
