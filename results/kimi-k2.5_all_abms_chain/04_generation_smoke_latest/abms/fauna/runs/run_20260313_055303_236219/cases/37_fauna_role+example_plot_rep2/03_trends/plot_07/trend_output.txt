The cumulative mortality of megaherbivores begins at zero and exhibits a rapid, concave-downward increase throughout the initial phase of the simulation. Within the first 500 time steps, the total number of deaths accumulates to values ranging between approximately 100 and 230 individuals. This initial surge reflects the immediate impact of hunting pressure from the 20 forager agents on the initial population of 250 ungulates, compounded by natural mortality factors including baseline physiological death and external predation.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of mortality accumulation decelerates considerably, with cumulative deaths rising from the initial range of 100-230 to approximately 210-250 individuals. This slowing trajectory suggests diminishing returns in hunting efficiency as prey population density declines, forcing foragers to expend greater movement costs to locate remaining animals, while the ungulate population simultaneously experiences density-dependent feedbacks in resource competition.

After approximately 1000 to 1500 time steps, the cumulative mortality trajectories plateau across nearly all simulation runs, stabilizing at final values between 225 and 250 deaths. This asymptotic behavior indicates that the prey population has been driven to effective extinction or reduced to such low densities that further mortality events become exceedingly rare. The plateau levels approaching the initial population size of 250 suggest that the majority of founding individuals ultimately perish, with limited recruitment of new individuals into the population insufficient to sustain the prey base against the combined pressures of human hunting and environmental constraints.

Substantial variation exists across simulation runs, particularly evident in the intermediate phase between steps 500 and 1000, where trajectories diverge markedly before reconverging at similar final plateau values. This stochasticity reflects the agent-based nature of the model, wherein individual-level variation in spatial distribution, foraging success, reproductive timing, and demographic stochasticity generates divergent population trajectories despite identical initial parameters. The convergence of final mortality totals across runs suggests that while the specific temporal pathway to population collapse may vary, the ultimate outcome of near-complete mortality of the initial cohort is robustly determined under the specified parameter configuration of moderate hunting pressure and high environmental seasonality.
