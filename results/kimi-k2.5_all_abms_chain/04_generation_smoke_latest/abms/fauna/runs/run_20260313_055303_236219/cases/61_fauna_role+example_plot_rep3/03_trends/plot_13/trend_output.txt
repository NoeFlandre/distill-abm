The combined energy reserves stored in the total ungulate population initiate at approximately 25,000 units across simulation replicates. This metric undergoes a severe, rapid decline during the first 500 time steps, with most trajectories plummeting by 80 to 100 percent as human hunters systematically deplete the prey base. This initial collapse reflects the immediate impact of optimal foraging behavior by 20 human agents operating without mortality constraints and with unlimited food storage capacity, allowing sustained, intensive predation pressure on the megaherbivore population.

The decay trajectories exhibit substantial heterogeneity across simulation runs. While some replicates exhaust available animal energy by step 300 to 400, descending to near-zero levels, others maintain substantial reserves—ranging from 5,000 to 8,000 units—until approximately step 600 to 700. A distinct subset of runs displays prolonged decline curves, sustaining residual energy between 1,000 and 3,000 units through step 800 to 1,000 before final depletion. This variation likely stems from stochastic differences in hunter-prey encounter rates, spatial heterogeneity in grassland vegetation patches, and demographic stochasticity affecting herd reproduction and survival.

Beyond step 1,000, total animal energy asymptotically approaches zero across all scenarios. Scattered runs exhibit trace persistence of energy reserves—typically remaining below 1,000 units—extending to approximately step 1,500 or 2,000, representing the final survival of isolated individuals or small remnant groups before elimination. Given the temporal calibration of 365 ticks per year, the majority of prey biomass is removed within the first 1.4 years of human hunting pressure, with complete population collapse occurring universally across replicates by approximately 5.5 years (2,000 time steps). The deterministic trajectory toward zero energy indicates that under the specified parameter configuration of immortal foragers with food storage, megaherbivore populations face inevitable extirpation regardless of initial stochastic variation in decline rates.
