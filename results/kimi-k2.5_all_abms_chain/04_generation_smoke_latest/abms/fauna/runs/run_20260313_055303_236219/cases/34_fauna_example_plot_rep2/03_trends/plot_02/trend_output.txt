The total energy available to all forager bands begins at approximately 1,500 units. Over the first 500 time steps, this value declines rapidly to approximately -4,000 to -5,000 units, with notable variation across simulation runs during this initial phase. Some runs exhibit steeper declines than others, suggesting stochastic differences in early hunting success and energy acquisition.

From approximately 500 to 1,000 time steps, the decline continues but the trajectories begin to converge, with most runs clustering between approximately -10,000 and -12,500 units by step 1,000. After this point, the decline becomes remarkably linear and consistent across all simulation runs.

Between 1,000 and 2,000 time steps, the total energy decreases steadily from approximately -12,500 to around -30,000 units. By the end of the observation period at approximately 2,200 time steps, values reach between -32,000 and -37,000 units. The variance across runs is substantially lower in this later phase compared to the initial 500 steps.

This pattern indicates that forager energy expenditure consistently exceeds energy acquisition throughout the simulation. The initial variability suggests that early hunting encounters differed stochastically across runs, but the subsequent linear convergence indicates that all forager populations eventually face uniform conditions where movement costs are not offset by successful hunting returns. The steady rate of decline, approximately 15 to 20 energy units per time step in aggregate, aligns closely with the cumulative movement costs of the forager population, implying minimal to no successful prey acquisition in the later phases.
