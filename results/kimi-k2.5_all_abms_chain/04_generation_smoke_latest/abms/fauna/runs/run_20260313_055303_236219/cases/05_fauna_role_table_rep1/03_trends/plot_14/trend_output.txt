The percentage of vegetated landscape patches exhibits remarkable stability throughout the simulation, maintaining an equilibrium very close to the initial value of approximately 33%. Over the course of 2,312 time steps (ticks), the metric demonstrates a mean value of 32.66% with minimal overall drift, concluding at 32.998%—a negligible decrease of merely 0.002 percentage points from the initialization.

Distinct cyclical patterns dominate the temporal dynamics. A pronounced annual oscillation manifests clearly, with the absolute minimum vegetation coverage of 24.91% occurring precisely at tick 365 (corresponding to exactly one year given the daily time-step calibration), followed by recovery. Superimposed upon this annual cycle is a secondary oscillation with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks (roughly two months), creating regular fluctuations around the equilibrium value throughout the simulation.

Trend analysis reveals non-stationary behavior across different temporal windows. Rolling Mann-Kendall tests indicate significant increasing trends (p < 0.001) in early and middle periods (approximately ticks 0–1,600), suggesting initial vegetation accumulation or equilibration from starting conditions. However, this pattern reverses in later windows (approximately ticks 1,600–2,300), where significant decreasing trends emerge, indicating a gradual long-term decline in vegetation coverage during the latter portion of the simulation despite the continued stability of the overall mean.

Multiple statistically significant change points occur at ticks 150, 300, 475, and 725, marking discrete transitions in system behavior, with a final change point near the simulation conclusion at tick 2,310. The coefficient of variation remains low (standard deviation approximately 0.95 percentage points), confirming that despite the identified seasonal and multi-monthly cycles, the vegetation coverage remains tightly constrained around the 33% equilibrium throughout the experimental duration.
