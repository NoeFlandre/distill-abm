The number of female animals initiates at approximately 125 individuals and exhibits a consistent downward trajectory across all simulation runs, ultimately approaching or reaching zero within the 2,200-step observation period. The decline manifests in two distinct phases: an initial rapid depletion occurring within the first 300 to 500 steps, during which populations typically fall from 125 to between 20 and 40 individuals, followed by a more gradual attrition phase extending to step 1000, where counts diminish to fewer than 10 individuals.

Significant stochastic variation characterizes the extinction dynamics across runs. Some trajectories exhibit steep mortality rates, declining to fewer than 5 individuals by step 400, while others demonstrate prolonged persistence, maintaining 10 to 20 individuals through step 800 and gradually declining to reach minimal population levels between steps 1500 and 2200. By step 1000, the majority of runs have converged to populations below 5 females, with several collapsing to zero, though a minority sustain remnant populations of 1 to 3 individuals for extended durations.

This pervasive population decline indicates that under the specified parameters of constant hunting pressure from 20 immortal foragers, sexual dimorphism favoring female processing efficiency, and life history constraints including 730-step birth spacing, the prey population experiences negative growth. The stepwise nature of the decline suggests discrete mortality events corresponding to individual hunting successes rather than mass die-off events. The differential extinction timing across runs reflects variability in spatial patterns of hunter-prey encounters, stochastic demographic fluctuations, and potentially differential exposure to environmental seasonality effects on grassland productivity.
