The simulation initialized with a total prey energy of 16666.67, representing the starting population of sexually dimorphic ungulate herds distributed across the grassland environment. From this initial state, the system underwent a prolonged trajectory toward complete extinction, reaching zero available prey energy at tick 2311. While the overall trend was decisively downward, the dynamics exhibited complex non-linear patterns rather than a simple monotonic decline.

The initial phase (ticks 0–350) featured a rapid decrease in aggregate prey energy, with particularly steep declines documented through tick 231 (with rates exceeding 24 energy units per tick) and continuing through tick 463, where the pace of depletion reached its maximum intensity. During this early period, high-frequency oscillations occurred around ticks 385–440, where total energy fluctuated between approximately 3700 and 4100 despite the downward trend. These fluctuations suggest initial instabilities in the predator-prey dynamics as hunters commenced optimal foraging operations and herds began responding to both predation pressure and environmental constraints.

Between ticks 500 and 875, marked by significant change points, the rate of decline moderated considerably. The degradation slowed from slopes of approximately -36 to -10, then to -3 energy units per tick, eventually approaching a near-stable state between ticks 928 and 1391 where decline rates dropped below 1 unit per tick. This deceleration indicates a potential equilibrium phase where hunting pressure temporarily balanced against ungulate fecundity and survivorship, or where hunter populations faced energetic constraints limiting further depletion.

Notably, the system exhibited two distinct periods of temporary recovery during the late stages. Between ticks 1392 and 1623, aggregate prey energy increased significantly (tau = 0.206, slope = 0.034), followed by another increase between ticks 1856 and 2087 (tau = 0.445). These resurgences occurred despite the overall extinction trajectory and suggest demographic rebounds potentially enabled by the 730-tick birth-spacing parameter allowing brief population growth when hunting pressure momentarily lagged, or by seasonal resource pulses (operating at seasonality 0.8) temporarily reducing juvenile mortality.

Following these temporary increases, the final decline accelerated toward extinction. The last significant change point occurred at tick 2310, immediately preceding total collapse. Throughout the simulation, superimposed oscillations with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks created regular fluctuations in available biomass, likely reflecting the interplay between seasonal grass regrowth cycles (regrowth-time set to 3 ticks), hunter movement patterns (foraging radius 6), and herd dispersal behaviors.

The median aggregate energy of 146.375, substantially lower than the mean of 2368.77, reflects the strongly right-skewed distribution dominated by high initial values followed by prolonged residence at low population levels.
