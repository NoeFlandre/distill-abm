Across multiple simulation replicates, the cumulative number of male animals killed by hunting exhibits a consistent two-phase pattern over the first approximately 2,000 time steps. Beginning from zero mortality at initialization, the trajectories increase rapidly and steeply, accumulating between 100 and 125 male deaths within the first 500 to 1,000 steps. Following this initial surge, the rate of accumulation decelerates substantially, with curves flattening into stable plateaus between steps 800 and 2,000. Final cumulative kill counts observed by step 2,000 range from approximately 100 to 122, with the majority of replicates stabilizing between 110 and 120. Variation exists across stochastic runs: some replicates cease accumulating male deaths as early as step 900 with totals near 105, while others continue incremental increases until step 2,000, reaching approximately 121. The trajectories display discrete stepwise increments rather than smooth acceleration, reflecting the event-based nature of hunting mortality in discrete time steps.

Within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these patterns indicate that human foragers rapidly deplete the male component of the prey population. Given an initialization of 250 total ungulates and the temporal calibration of 365 steps per year, the asymptotic values approaching 120 deaths within approximately 5.5 years suggest that hunters successfully kill the vast majority of available males. The plateauing occurs because the male population becomes functionally depleted—either through direct mortality or reduced to densities too low to sustain encounter rates with hunters—rather than because hunters cease activity, as the immortal-foragers parameter ensures persistent hunting pressure. Since males and females provide equivalent net energetic returns after processing costs (approximately 24 energy units), the rapid male depletion suggests either preferential targeting of males by foragers or higher encounter probabilities with male groups due to movement patterns or herd structure. The variation across replicates reflects stochastic differences in agent movement, herd distributions, and encounter probabilities on the 211 by 211 patch landscape.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**\nThe trajectories reveal that under moderate hunting pressure (20 foragers) with efficient hunter adaptations (immortality, food storage, 6-patch foraging radius), male megaherbivore populations face near-total depletion within less than six years. This rapid sex-specific mortality creates severely male-depleted populations that may trigger reproductive Allee effects or genetic bottlenecks, potentially leading to population collapse even if female numbers remain temporarily viable. Conservation managers should recognize that traditional abundance monitoring may fail to detect critical population vulnerability, as the removal of males occurs faster than overall population decline. Management strategies must prioritize sex-specific protection protocols or rapid intervention thresholds, as the window for preventing functional male extirpation is substantially shorter than the timeframe for total population collapse.
