The cumulative number of female ungulates killed begins at zero and exhibits discrete, stepwise increases as individual hunting events occur. During the initial 200 steps, fatalities accumulate rapidly, reaching values between 20 and 60 across different simulation runs. This rapid accumulation continues through step 500, at which point most trajectories record between 80 and 120 fatalities. Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of increase decelerates substantially, with many runs approaching asymptotic values ranging from 115 to 125 total fatalities. By step 1500, the majority of trajectories have plateaued, indicating cessation of additional female mortality, though some runs continue gradual increases extending to approximately step 2200, ultimately reaching 122 to 125 fatalities. Terminal values cluster tightly between 118 and 125 for most simulations, with several outliers stabilizing between 108 and 115. High variance characterizes the accumulation rates during the early phase through step 400, reflecting stochastic variation in hunter-prey encounter frequencies, while terminal convergence toward maximum values near 125 occurs consistently across runs regardless of these early differences.

Given the initial prey population of 250 individuals and assuming roughly equal sex ratios, the terminal values approaching 125 indicate near-complete extirpation of the adult female cohort through hunting pressure. The rapid accumulation of 60 or more fatalities within the first 200 steps corresponds to high initial encounter rates when prey density is maximal. The subsequent deceleration after step 500 reflects increasing search costs and declining prey availability as the female population becomes depleted. The eventual plateau observed in most trajectories suggests functional extinction of females or population levels insufficient to sustain further hunting mortality.

**Implications for decision makers:** The data indicate that megaherbivore populations face critical risk of demographic collapse within approximately 500 to 1000 time steps (roughly 1.4 to 2.7 years given the 365-step annual calibration), with female mortality reaching thresholds likely to preclude population recovery. The consistent convergence toward terminal values near 120 fatalities across stochastically varying trajectories suggests that female cohort extinction represents a robust outcome under the modeled hunting pressure rather than a rare stochastic event. The substantial variance in early kill rates highlights that population assessments conducted immediately following hunter introduction may underestimate long-term extinction risk, as slower initial hunting success does not prevent eventual demographic collapse.
