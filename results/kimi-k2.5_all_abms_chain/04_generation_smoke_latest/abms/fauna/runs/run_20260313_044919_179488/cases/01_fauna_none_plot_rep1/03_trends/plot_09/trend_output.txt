All simulation runs begin with approximately 120 to 125 female animals, representing roughly half of the initial prey population of 250 individuals. Across all runs, the number of female animals exhibits a monotonic decline from the initial state toward local extinction, with no recoveries or stable equilibria observed.

The rate of decline is characterized by a rapid initial decrease followed by a slower approach to zero. Within the first 500 steps, most trajectories decline from approximately 125 females to fewer than 20, with many runs reaching zero within this interval. The decline rate varies stochastically across runs: the fastest declines reach zero by approximately step 400, while slower declines maintain 20 to 40 females at step 500.

Following the initial rapid decline, a subset of runs exhibits extended persistence at very low population sizes. Between steps 500 and 1000, several trajectories maintain between 1 and 5 female animals, with individual runs persisting at these low levels until approximately steps 1500 to 2200 before reaching zero.

In the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these trends indicate that hunting pressure by 20 initial foragers, operating under optimal foraging constraints with the specified energetic and demographic parameters, consistently drives the prey population to extinction. The variation in extinction timing reflects stochastic processes in hunter-prey encounters and herd demographic dynamics. The prolonged persistence of small populations at low densities suggests demographic stochasticity or Allee effects operating near the extinction threshold, with some populations avoiding final extinction for extended periods despite severe hunting pressure.
