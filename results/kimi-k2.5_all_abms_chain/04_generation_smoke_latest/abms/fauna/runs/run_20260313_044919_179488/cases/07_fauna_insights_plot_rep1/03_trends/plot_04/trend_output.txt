The rate of accumulation varies considerably, with some runs showing steep, consistent increases while others exhibit more stepwise, intermittent progression.

During the middle phase (steps 500–1000), most trajectories begin decelerating as they approach terminal values. The late phase (steps 1000–2200) is characterized by asymptotic behavior, with all runs eventually reaching stable plateaus. Final cumulative values cluster predominantly between 115 and 125 females killed, though several runs stabilize at lower levels of 108–115. The timing of stabilization varies markedly: some runs plateau as early as step 600–800, others between steps 900–1400, and the slowest-extending runs continue accumulating kills until steps 1800–2200.

The discrete, staircase-like increments indicate that hunting events are sporadic rather than continuous. The convergence of nearly all runs on maximum values near 120—approximately half the initial prey population of 250—suggests that hunters effectively deplete the entire female cohort. The cessation of additional kills following plateau indicates that females are hunted to functional extinction, with encounter rates dropping to zero or near-zero as the population is exhausted.

**Implications for decision makers:**
The model consistently predicts total female depletion under the current parameterization, with stochastic factors affecting only the timing, not the outcome, of extinction. The rapid accumulation of 60–100 kills within the first 500 steps (approximately 1.4 years) indicates that hunting pressure from 20 foragers is immediately unsustainable for prey with biennial birth spacing. The preferential targeting of females—despite males having higher gross food value—reflects the influence of lower female processing costs (3 vs. 6) and the nutritional bonus from nursing offspring, creating a nutritional incentive structure that selectively removes reproductive individuals.

To prevent population collapse, intervention must occur before step 300 (roughly 8 months), as delayed management fails to prevent the cumulative mortality that drives extinction. Effective strategies would require either reducing forager density by 50% or more, implementing spatial refugia protecting reproductive females, or manipulating processing costs to shift hunting selectivity toward males. The deterministic nature of the outcome—total female elimination within 6–20 years despite parameter stochasticity—indicates that the current configuration creates an extinction vortex that cannot be buffered by the prey species' demographic resilience alone.
