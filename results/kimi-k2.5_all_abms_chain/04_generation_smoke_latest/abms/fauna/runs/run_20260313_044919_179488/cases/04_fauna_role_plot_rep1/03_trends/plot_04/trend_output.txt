Across replicate simulation runs, cumulative mortality of female ungulates due to hunting exhibits a characteristic three-phase trajectory: an initial period of rapid acceleration, followed by progressive deceleration, and ultimate asymptotic stabilization near maximum values. During the first approximately 500 time steps, cumulative kills increase sharply from zero to between 80 and 120 individuals, representing the primary phase of female population depletion. This rapid accumulation reflects the foraging behavior of 20 initial hunter agents operating with a foraging radius of 6 patches, targeting prey with a food value of 27 and processing cost of 3.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of mortality accumulation diminishes substantially as the curves begin to flatten, indicating declining encounter rates with surviving females. By approximately 1000 to 1500 steps, the trajectories stabilize completely, reaching final asymptotic values predominantly between 110 and 125 females killed. Individual simulation runs exhibit stochastic variation in both the timing of saturation and final cumulative totals, with some runs plateauing as early as step 800 at values near 102, while others continue accumulating kills until approximately step 1800, reaching 124 to 125 individuals.

This saturation pattern suggests that hunting pressure effectively extirpates the female cohort from the initial population of 250 ungulates. Given that processing costs yield equal net energetic returns for both sexes (24 units after subtracting respective processing costs of 3 for females and 6 for males), hunters pursue females opportunistically until their availability is exhausted. The final asymptotes approaching 125 individuals indicate near-complete mortality of the female subpopulation, effectively eliminating further hunting opportunities on this sex and likely precipitating overall herd collapse given the reproductive biology parameters, including birth-spacing of 730 ticks and the critical role of females in population maintenance.
