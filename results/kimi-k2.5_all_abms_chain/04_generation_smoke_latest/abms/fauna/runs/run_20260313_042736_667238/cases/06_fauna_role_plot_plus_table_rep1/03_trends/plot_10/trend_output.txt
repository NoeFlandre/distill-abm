The megaherbivore population exhibits a universal trajectory toward complete extinction under the combined pressures of human hunting and seasonal environmental variation. Initialized at 250 individuals, the population undergoes immediate demographic collapse, with aggregate mean abundances plummeting rapidly during the first 500 ticks. By approximately tick 500, the majority of simulation replicates have declined to fewer than 10 individuals, with many trajectories reaching local extinction or persisting only as small remnant groups.

During the intermediate persistence phase spanning ticks 500 to 1500, surviving populations display oscillatory dynamics superimposed on the overall declining trend. These fluctuations manifest a dominant periodicity of 63 ticks, producing secondary peaks at tick 730 (11.37 individuals), tick 803 (7.42), tick 820 (6.92), and tick 893 (4.63). The primary peak at tick 730 coincides precisely with the 730-tick interbirth interval parameter, suggesting temporary demographic rebounds synchronized with reproductive pulses in surviving females. Corresponding valleys occur at ticks 708, 729, 802, and 819, reflecting the oscillatory nature of recruitment and mortality in depleted populations.

Statistical trend analysis confirms significant monotonic decreases across early temporal windows (ticks 0-1391), with particularly steep negative slopes in windows 1-3 (ticks 0-695). Brief periods of increasing trends emerge between ticks 1392-1623 and 1856-2087, indicating temporary demographic recoveries or slowed attrition in subsets of replicates. However, these positive deviations prove insufficient to reverse the overall trajectory. Multiple inflection points detected at ticks 357, 747, 825, and 1285 mark transitions in the rate of decline, while change points at ticks 125, 475, 625, and 875 delineate distinct phases of the collapse dynamics.

The terminal extinction phase spans ticks 1500 to 2311, characterized by the final attrition of the last surviving individuals. Complete population extinction occurs universally across all 120 replicates by tick 2311, with the aggregate mean reaching zero. The median population size across the full simulation duration is 1.48 individuals, reflecting the extended period during which small remnant populations persist at densities highly vulnerable to stochastic extinction. The coefficient of variation and high standard deviation (44.9) relative to the mean abundance (25.0) indicate substantial heterogeneity in the timing of collapse among replicates, though the zero endpoint is invariant.

The observed 63-tick oscillation period likely reflects the interaction between strong seasonal vegetation cycles (seasonality parameter 0.8) and the two-year reproductive interval, generating pulses of recruitment that temporarily slow but cannot prevent ultimate extinction under the foraging pressure exerted by 20 human hunters operating with energy storage capabilities and sex-biased prey selection preferences.
