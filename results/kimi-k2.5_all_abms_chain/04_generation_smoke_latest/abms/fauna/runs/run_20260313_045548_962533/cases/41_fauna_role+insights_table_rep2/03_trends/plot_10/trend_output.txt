Across the 120 simulation runs, the megaherbivore population commenced at a mean of approximately 167 individuals and underwent a precipitous collapse within the first two years (approximately 730 daily time steps), plummeting to fewer than 12 animals. This initial crash represents the most dramatic phase of decline, with statistically significant decreasing trends occurring throughout the first 1,160 ticks. The steepest rates of depletion occurred between change points at tick 125 and tick 475, followed by continued rapid contraction through tick 625.

Following the initial collapse, the population entered a prolonged period of persistence at critically low densities, fluctuating between approximately 2 and 11 individuals from tick 700 onward. These fluctuations exhibited a regular oscillatory pattern with a dominant period of approximately 63 days (roughly two months), superimposed on a general downward trajectory. The system displayed multiple inflection points—particularly at ticks 357, 747, and 825—indicating shifting velocities of decline as the population oscillated. Brief demographic recoveries occurred during intervals 1392–1623 and 1856–2087, where mean population counts temporarily increased, though these rebounds remained below 12 individuals and failed to restore viability.

The population ultimately declined to extinction by tick 2312 (approximately 6.3 years), with the final decrease occurring between change points at tick 875 and tick 2310. Throughout the low-density phase from tick 700 to 2312, the average population remained below 15 individuals, with the terminal 500 ticks characterized by increasingly volatile oscillations between 0 and 3 animals before the final extinction event.

**Interpretation and Implications**

This trajectory suggests that the configuration of 20 human foragers with energy storage capabilities and immortal status, combined with high environmental seasonality (80% variation) and baseline vegetation coverage of 33%, created an unsustainable harvest regime. The rapid initial decline indicates that hunting pressure immediately exceeded the population's maximum sustainable yield, while the subsequent six-year persistence at low densities represents an extinction debt where small remnant populations oscillated toward inevitable collapse. The 63-day oscillation period likely reflects the interaction between seasonal resource pulses (within the 365-day annual cycle) and optimal foraging behavior by human agents.

For decision makers, these results indicate that current parameter settings produce rapid extinction within a decade, suggesting critically unsustainable management practices. The significant lag between initial population impact (first two years) and final extinction (year six) implies that monitoring programs must track populations for extended periods to detect hunting-driven collapse, as small remnant populations may persist transiently while being demographically non-viable. Furthermore, the brief recovery intervals observed even at low densities suggest that hunting moratoria or seasonal restrictions during resource bottlenecks could potentially alter the trajectory, provided intervention occurs before the population enters the final low-density oscillation phase below 10 individuals.
