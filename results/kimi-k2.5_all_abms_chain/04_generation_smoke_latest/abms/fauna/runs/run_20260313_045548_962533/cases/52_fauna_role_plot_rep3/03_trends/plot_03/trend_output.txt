The cumulative number of male ungulates killed by human foragers exhibits a consistent pattern of rapid initial accumulation followed by deceleration and eventual stabilization across all simulation runs. At initialization (step 0), the cumulative kill count begins at zero. During the first approximately 500 time steps, the trajectories display steep, nearly linear increases, with most runs accumulating between 80 and 115 male kills by step 500. This phase represents a period of high hunting efficiency where the initial population of 250 ungules provides abundant encounter opportunities for the 20 forager agents.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of increase slows markedly across all trajectories, with cumulative kills rising from approximately 100 to final plateau values ranging between 105 and 125 individuals. The deceleration is particularly pronounced after step 600, where the stepwise nature of the cumulative counts becomes evident as longer intervals elapse between successive hunting events.

After approximately step 1000, most trajectories reach asymptotic stability, with cumulative counts remaining constant for extended periods. Some runs stabilize as early as step 800 with cumulative totals near 108 males killed, while others continue accumulating kills gradually until approximately step 2000, reaching maximum values of 122-125 individuals. The variation in final cumulative totals and the timing of stabilization reflects stochastic differences in forager movement patterns, encounter rates, and optimal foraging decisions across simulation replicates.

The plateauing dynamics suggest that hunting pressure progressively reduces male ungulate availability to the point where encounter rates drop below sustainable hunting thresholds, or foragers switch to alternative prey (females and calves) as predicted by optimal foraging theory given the males' higher processing cost (6 energy units) compared to females (3 energy units). The maximum cumulative kills of approximately 120-125 individuals represents roughly 48-50% of the initial ungulate population, though accounting for natural mortality, starvation, and the sex-specific hunting pressure, this suggests near-complete depletion of the male segment of the population within the first 500-1000 time steps of the simulation.
