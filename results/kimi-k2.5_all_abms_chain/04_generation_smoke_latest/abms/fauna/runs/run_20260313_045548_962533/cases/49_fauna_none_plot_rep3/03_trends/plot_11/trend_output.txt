Across simulation replicates, total energy aggregated across all male animals commences at approximately 12,000 to 12,500 energy units. During the initial 500 steps, this collective metric undergoes a steep, monotonic decline characterized by discrete downward steps indicative of individual mortality events. By step 500, most trajectories have fallen below 2,000 units, with several having reached zero, representing a population reduction of roughly 85 to 100 percent from initial levels.

Between steps 500 and 1000, populations retaining residual energy continue to decline, though at a decelerated rate compared to the initial phase, typically decreasing from the low thousands to levels approaching zero. The majority of simulation runs reach effectively zero total male energy between steps 1000 and 1500, indicating functional elimination of the male prey demographic.

Substantial stochastic variation exists across replicates. While the bulk of runs exhaust male energy reserves by approximately step 1200, certain trajectories persist considerably longer, with final exhaustion occurring as late as step 2200 in one instance. The stepwise descent pattern reflects discrete hunting events where individual male animals are successfully pursued and harvested by forager agents.

Under the parameterization where male animals present a net energy return of 24 units (food value of 30 minus processing cost of 6), hunting pressure from 20 forager agents systematically depletes the male ungulate population. The temporal variation in extinction timing suggests stochastic elements in hunter-prey encounter rates, movement patterns, or foraging success generate differential depletion speeds, though the eventual outcome of male population elimination remains consistent across runs.
