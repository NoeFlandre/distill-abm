The combined total energy available in the population of female prey animals initiates at approximately 12,000 to 12,500 units across all simulation runs. Within the first 200 steps, this metric undergoes a steep decline, with most populations losing between 50 and 75 percent of initial energy reserves, falling to values ranging from 3,000 to 6,000 units.

Between steps 200 and 800, trajectories diverge substantially across repeated runs. While some populations continue declining rapidly to near-zero energy by approximately step 400 to 500, others exhibit more gradual depletion or temporary stabilization, maintaining energy levels between 1,000 and 4,000 units through step 600 to 800. Several populations display stepwise decreases during this interval, suggesting discrete energy loss events interspersed with periods of relative stability.

From approximately step 800 onward, nearly all populations approach zero energy. The majority reach complete depletion between steps 1,000 and 1,500, though a minority persist with minimal residual energy until steps 1,800 to 2,200.

This pattern indicates that under the specified parameters—which include high environmental seasonality (0.8), moderate initial hunter density (20 foragers), favorable processing economics for females (processing cost of 3 versus food value of 27–30 including nursing calves), and immortal hunters—female ungulate populations experience unsustainable energy extraction rates that consistently lead to biomass elimination. The variation in depletion timing suggests that stochastic elements in hunter-prey encounters, movement patterns, and environmental feedback generate differing extinction trajectories even under identical starting conditions. The consistent endpoint of zero total energy demonstrates that the current configuration leads to effective elimination of female prey availability.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The reliable progression toward zero energy indicates that current hunting pressure and processing efficiency create conditions where prey populations are driven to extinction rather than achieving sustainable equilibrium. The high variability in extinction timing—spanning from step 400 to over 2,000 across runs—suggests that deterministic population models may underestimate extinction risk in stochastic real-world systems. Interventions should consider reducing hunter density, increasing the processing cost differential between sexes, or enhancing habitat productivity through increased grass proportion or reduced seasonality to buffer against the observed depletion dynamics.
