The total energy contained within the male prey population begins at approximately 12,000 to 12,500 units across all simulation replicates. The rate of depletion follows a stepped pattern, characterized by discrete downward jumps interspersed with brief periods of relative stability, reflecting episodic hunting events rather than continuous decline. The most rapid phase of depletion occurs within the first 300 to 500 steps, during which the majority of simulations plummet from initial values to below 2,000 units, with many falling below 500 units.

Subsequent dynamics reveal considerable variability in extinction timing across replicates. While most simulations reach near-zero energy levels (below 200 units) by step 600 to 800 and effectively reach complete depletion by step 1000 to 1200, a minority of trajectories demonstrate extended persistence. In these cases, total energy continues to decline gradually, maintaining low residual values (ranging from approximately 100 to 500 units) that persist until approximately step 1400 to 1600, with extreme outliers maintaining trace energy levels extending to step 2200 before ultimately reaching zero.

By step 1500, the overwhelming majority of replicates have achieved complete energetic depletion of the male prey population. The terminal state across all simulations is effectively zero energy, indicating functional extinction of male animals within the modeled timeframe.

This pattern indicates that the combination of 20 immortal foragers with energy storage capabilities, combined with male prey food values of 30 units and processing costs of 6 units, creates predation pressure that systematically exceeds the population's capacity for demographic replacement or energy maintenance. The stepped decline trajectory is consistent with optimal foraging behavior, where hunters target male prey when energetically favorable, while the variability in extinction timing suggests stochastic elements in encounter rates, herd movement patterns, or demographic fluctuations affect the rate but not the ultimate outcome of population depletion.

**Implications for Decision Makers**: The deterministic trajectory toward male population extinction within 500-1000 steps (with maximum extension to ~2200 steps) indicates that current parameter configurations produce unsustainable hunting pressure. Decision makers seeking to prevent megaherbivore extinction should prioritize interventions that alter the fundamental economics of hunting, such as reducing hunter density below the threshold of 20 immortal foragers, eliminating hunter immortality to allow natural mortality to limit predation pressure, or adjusting the food-value-to-processing-cost ratio to make male prey less attractive targets. The fact that stochastic variation only delays but does not prevent extinction suggests that passive management relying on natural demographic variability will be insufficient to maintain viable male populations under these conditions.
