The cumulative count of female ungulates killed by hunter agents exhibits a consistent pattern of rapid saturation across all simulation replicates. Beginning from zero at initialization, the metric increases steeply during the initial phase, with most trajectories accumulating between 60 and 80 kills within the first 300 to 500 time steps. This initial accumulation rate varies substantially across replicates, with some simulations reaching 60 kills as early as step 300 while others attain only 20 to 40 kills by this point, indicating high stochasticity in early encounter rates between foragers and female prey.

Following this rapid accumulation phase, the rate of increase decelerates markedly between steps 500 and 1000, with trajectories asymptotically approaching terminal values ranging from approximately 108 to 125 individuals. By step 1000, the vast majority of replicates have ceased accumulating additional female kills, and from approximately step 1000 through the observation limit at step 2000 and beyond, the cumulative counts remain static with zero additional mortality recorded.

The narrow clustering of terminal values around 110-125 kills suggests convergence toward a hard ceiling representing the effective extirpation of the female subpopulation. Given the initial ungulate population of 250 individuals and assuming roughly equal sex ratios, this saturation point corresponds to the elimination of nearly the entire breeding female population from the system. The consistency of this asymptote across stochastic replicates, despite variation in the timing of accumulation, indicates that hunter foraging behavior under the specified parameters inevitably leads to complete liquidation of available females within the first 500-1000 time steps (approximately 1.4 to 2.7 simulated years).

One notable outlier trajectory exhibits a delayed onset pattern, showing minimal accumulation until approximately step 300, followed by a steep compensatory increase that ultimately converges on the same asymptotic range as other replicates by step 800. This suggests that while initial encounter stochasticity can delay hunting pressure, it cannot prevent the eventual depletion of the female cohort.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The rapid saturation of female mortality at levels indicating near-complete subpopulation elimination suggests that current hunting parameters generate unsustainable extraction rates that exhaust the reproductive capacity of the megaherbivore population within approximately two to three years. Decision makers should recognize that the cessation of female kills after step 1000 does not represent sustainable management but rather population collapse, as the asymptote corresponds to the elimination of the breeding stock rather than equilibrium harvesting. The narrow variance in final kill counts (108-125) across highly stochastic agent movements indicates that outcomes are robustly deterministic: female extinction is the inevitable consequence of the current forager density (20 hunters), encounter radius (6 patches), and prey nutritional value configuration. Interventions to prevent population collapse must either reduce hunter numbers, restrict hunting during critical periods, or modify forager decision algorithms to avoid targeting reproductive females, as the current system exhibits no self-regulating feedback that would prevent breeding population elimination.
