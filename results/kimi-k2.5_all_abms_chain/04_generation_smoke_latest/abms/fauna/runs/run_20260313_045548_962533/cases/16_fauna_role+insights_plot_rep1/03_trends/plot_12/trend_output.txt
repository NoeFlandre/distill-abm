The simulations demonstrate a consistent pattern of rapid depletion in the aggregate energy reserves of female ungulate populations, followed by eventual collapse to zero across all experimental runs. Initial conditions establish a collective energy pool ranging between approximately 12,000 and 12,500 units. Within the first 400 time steps—which, given the calibration of 365 ticks per year, represents approximately the first 13 months—this energy base undergoes a steep decline, with most trajectories falling below 2,000 units and several approaching zero.

A subset of simulations exhibits temporary stabilization or oscillatory behavior between steps 400 and 800, maintaining residual energy levels between approximately 1,000 and 4,000 units. These fluctuations likely reflect intermittent successful reproduction or temporary reductions in hunting pressure as prey densities decline, creating brief periods where female energy acquisition balances or exceeds depletion from mortality and metabolic costs. However, these plateaus prove unsustainable.

Between steps 800 and 1,500, the remaining populations exhibit continued attrition, with energy levels drifting downward in stepwise decrements characteristic of discrete mortality events. By step 1,500, the vast majority of simulations have converged to zero or near-zero energy states. A small number of trajectories persist with marginal energy reserves (under 500 units) until approximately step 2,000, after which complete depletion occurs uniformly.

The variance in time-to-collapse across simulations—ranging from roughly 500 to 2,000 steps—indicates significant sensitivity to stochastic factors such as hunter-prey encounter rates, spatial distribution of vegetation, and demographic stochasticity in female reproduction and survival. Notably, no simulation maintains a stable positive energy state beyond approximately 2,000 ticks, indicating that the combination of 20 human foragers, high seasonal resource variability (seasonality parameter 0.8), and the specified life history parameters reliably drives female megaherbivore populations toward functional extinction within six years.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The complete erosion of female energy reserves across all stochastic realizations suggests that the current parameter configuration creates an unsustainable harvest pressure that eliminates reproductive capacity before the maximum simulation horizon. For conservation or management applications, these results indicate critical thresholds where even moderate hunter densities (20 individuals) in seasonal environments with 33% initial vegetation cover result in deterministic extinction of breeding females within a short temporal window. Management strategies should prioritize reducing hunter encounter rates or enhancing resource availability during critical bottleneck periods (the initial 400 steps) to prevent the depletion of reproductive capital. The absence of any stable trajectories implies that minor adjustments to hunting pressure or carrying capacity may be insufficient; substantial parameter modifications would be required to achieve demographic viability.
