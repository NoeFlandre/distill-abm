The combined energy of the prey population begins at approximately 16,667 units, representing the initial biomass of 250 ungulate herds distributed across the landscape. This initial state reflects a system stocked with megaherbivores at carrying capacity prior to the onset of intensive hunting pressure.

Over the first approximately 350 time steps, the total prey energy undergoes a steep decline, dropping sharply from initial levels. This rapid decrease corresponds to the initial encounter phase where 20 human hunters, operating under optimal foraging constraints with energy storage capabilities, begin exploiting the naive prey population. The decline accelerates through an inflection point around step 309, indicating increasing per-capita hunting efficiency as hunters locate and deplete high-energy herds.

Between approximately steps 350 and 750, the trajectory exhibits damped oscillations superimposed on the downward trend, with notable local maxima occurring around steps 385-440 (ranging from approximately 3,700 to 4,100 energy units) and again near steps 709-730 (ranging from approximately 880 to 1,080 energy units). These fluctuations correspond to approximately 63-tick periodic cycles, likely reflecting the interaction between seasonal resource availability (given the high seasonality parameter of 0.8) and prey demographic responses, including the 730-tick (2-year) birth spacing interval allowing for limited recruitment between hunting events. The temporary rebounds suggest brief periods where prey recruitment temporarily outpaced predation mortality, though hunters' immortal status and food storage capabilities prevent sustained relaxation of hunting pressure.

Following step 875, the system enters a terminal decline phase characterized by increasingly severe fluctuations but a consistent downward trajectory. Brief stabilization attempts occur around steps 1390-1620 and 1850-2090, where the rate of decline temporarily slows or slightly reverses, corresponding to windows where prey populations might have experienced reduced encounter rates with hunters or temporary refuge in suboptimal foraging patches. However, the fundamental asymmetry between predator mortality (none, due to immortal foragers) and prey replacement rates proves insurmountable.

By step 2311, the total prey energy reaches zero, indicating complete megaherbivore extinction. The median energy level of approximately 146 units across the simulation run indicates that for the majority of the experiment, the prey population existed at drastically reduced biomasses compared to initial conditions, spending most of its time below 10% of starting values before final collapse.

The oscillatory nature of the decline, combined with the eventual extinction despite temporary recoveries, suggests that while the 2-year birth spacing and seasonal environmental constraints allowed for brief population rebounds, the constant hunting pressure from a non-depensatory predator population (enhanced by energy storage capabilities that buffer hunters against temporary prey scarcity) inevitably drove the system to exhaustion.
