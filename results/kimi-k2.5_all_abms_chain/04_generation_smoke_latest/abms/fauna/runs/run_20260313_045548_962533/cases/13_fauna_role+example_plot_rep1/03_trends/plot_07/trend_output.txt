The cumulative number of deaths across all simulation runs begins at zero and exhibits a monotonic increase throughout the temporal range, characterized by a rapid initial acceleration followed by a marked deceleration as the simulations progress. Within the first 500 steps, the cumulative death count rises steeply from 0 to approximately 150–200, indicating a phase of intense mortality driven by the initial interaction between the 250 introduced prey animals and the 20 human foragers. This early mortality accounts for roughly 60 to 80 percent of the initial population, suggesting immediate and substantial hunting pressure alongside baseline environmental mortality.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of accumulation slows considerably, with cumulative deaths increasing from approximately 200 to between 220 and 240. By step 1500, most trajectories approach or enter a plateau phase, where the cumulative death count stabilizes between 230 and 250. This plateauing indicates that by approximately 1500 to 2000 steps, mortality events have effectively ceased or been reduced to negligible levels, consistent with the near-total depletion of the initial prey population given the starting size of 250 animals.

Notable variability exists across simulation runs regarding the timing of mortality accumulation. While most runs follow the steep early trajectory described, a subset of simulations exhibits a distinctly slower accumulation pattern, reaching only approximately 225 cumulative deaths by step 1600 to 1700, compared to the 240 to 250 deaths observed in other runs by that time. This divergence suggests that stochastic factors, such as spatial heterogeneity in hunter-prey encounters or localized resource availability, can temporarily buffer certain subpopulations from predation pressure, delaying but not ultimately preventing the population collapse observed across all runs.
