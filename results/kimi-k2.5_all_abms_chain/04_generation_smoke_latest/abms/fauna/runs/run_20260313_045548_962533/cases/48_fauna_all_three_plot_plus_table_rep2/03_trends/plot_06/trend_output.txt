The cumulative number of births remains at zero across all simulations for an initial latency period spanning approximately the first 700 time steps. This reflects the reproductive immaturity of the initial population and the 730-tick interbirth interval governing the model, during which no females complete their first reproductive cycle. Around tick 725, the first birth events occur, initiating a divergence in demographic trajectories across the 40 simulation runs. Some populations accumulate births rapidly, reaching cumulative totals of 8 to 10 by ticks 800-1000, while others exhibit minimal reproductive success, maintaining counts of 1 to 2 births throughout the observation period. This high variance suggests strong sensitivity to stochastic interactions between hunter foraging success, seasonal resource availability, and herd movement patterns. The mean cumulative births across active simulations increases to a maximum value of 5.6 around tick 1673, after which it declines steadily to approximately 1.0 by tick 2312. This decline occurs because populations with higher reproductive output tend to experience earlier collapse and drop out of the average, leaving only populations with suppressed birth rates persisting toward the simulation terminus. Rolling trend analysis identifies four distinct demographic phases: an initial growth period from ticks 696-1391 showing strongly increasing birth rates (Kendall's tau ranging from 0.833 to 0.392), continued acceleration to tick 1623 (tau = 0.784), followed by a sharp transition to decline between ticks 1624-1855 (tau = -0.495), and sustained decreasing trends thereafter (tau = -0.453). Superimposed on these trends are regular oscillations with a dominant period of 50 ticks, consistent with seasonal forcing of reproductive output under the 0.8 seasonality parameter. The system exhibits a demographic paradox where higher birth rates correlate with reduced population persistence, as rapid growth leads to earlier exposure to overexploitation and resource depletion. For decision makers, this indicates that managing hunter pressure to maintain moderate rather than maximal birth rates may paradoxically enhance long-term population viability, though the high variance across simulations suggests substantial unpredictability in individual outcomes regardless of initial conditions.
