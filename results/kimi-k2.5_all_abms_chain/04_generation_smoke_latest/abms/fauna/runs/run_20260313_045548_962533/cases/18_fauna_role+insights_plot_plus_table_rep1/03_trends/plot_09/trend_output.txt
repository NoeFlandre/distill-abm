The simulation reveals a deterministic trajectory toward extinction of the female megaherbivore population under the specified hunting and environmental regimes. Initially comprising 125 individuals, the population undergoes a precipitous decline characterized by multiple phases of varying decay rates. Within the first 305 time steps (approximately 0.8 years), the population experiences its most rapid reduction, falling sharply from initial levels. By step 475 (~1.3 years), the decline rate moderates but continues steadily, with distinct inflections occurring around steps 740 and 875 (roughly 2.0 and 2.4 years) indicating shifts in population loss dynamics.

Throughout the decline, the population exhibits oscillatory behavior with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 steps (bi-monthly cycles), likely reflecting the strong seasonal resource fluctuations (seasonality = 0.8) interacting with hunting pressure. The mean population size across all temporal samples registers at approximately 16.5 individuals, though the median value of 1.0 indicates that most time points feature near-extinction conditions. Complete population collapse (zero females) occurs across all replicates by step 2311 (approximately 6.3 years), though the majority of simulation runs reach extinction earlier, between steps 1000 and 1500 (2.7 to 4.1 years).

Stochastic variation between replicates produces variable extinction times, with some populations persisting until beyond step 2000 while others collapse before step 500. Despite temporary stabilization attempts evidenced by minor increasing trends in specific rolling windows between steps 1160-1391 and 1856-2087, the overall trajectory remains significantly decreasing throughout (p < 0.001). Critical change points at steps 475, 600, and 875 mark transitions where mortality pressures from hunting, starvation (threshold = 25 energy units), and external predation (rate = 0.0025) intensify relative to reproductive recruitment, which is constrained by the 730-step (2-year) interbirth interval and nutritional stress in a system where females provide hunters with 27 units of food value.

Implications for decision makers: The inevitability of extinction within 2-6 years under current hunting pressure (20 foragers) and environmental conditions suggests that immediate intervention is required to prevent population collapse. The oscillatory nature of the decline indicates that population monitoring must account for seasonal fluctuations to avoid false signals of recovery during high-resource periods. The protracted tail of the extinction process, with some populations persisting beyond 2000 steps despite being functionally non-viable, implies that detection of demographic collapse may lag significantly behind the onset of irreversible threats. Management strategies should prioritize reducing hunting pressure before step 300 (year 1), when the population enters a phase of rapid decline, and must account for the biological constraint of a 2-year interbirth interval that prevents rapid demographic recovery even if hunting pressure is later alleviated.
