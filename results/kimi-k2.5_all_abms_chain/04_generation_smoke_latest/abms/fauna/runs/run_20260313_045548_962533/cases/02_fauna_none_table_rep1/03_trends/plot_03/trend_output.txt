Between approximately 850 and 1856 time steps, the accumulation entered a plateau phase characterized by stabilization around values of 118 to 120 individuals, with intermittent fluctuations. This late-phase reduction suggests stochastic variation across simulation ensembles or the cessation of hunting activity in individual runs following local prey depletion.

Throughout the entire sequence, and particularly prominent during the late phase between approximately 2170 and 2265 time steps, the data exhibited cyclical oscillations with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 time steps. Given the model's high seasonality parameter and the 365 ticks-per-year temporal scaling, these oscillations likely reflect seasonal resource availability patterns influencing hunter foraging efficiency and prey encounter rates.

In the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these dynamics suggest that forager bands initially established substantial hunting pressure on male prey, exploiting the higher food value of males relative to processing costs. The subsequent plateau and eventual decline correspond to demographic constraints imposed by the prey species' two-year birth spacing and twenty-year senescence threshold, which limited population recovery, combined with optimal foraging decisions that may have shifted toward alternative prey as male densities decreased.
