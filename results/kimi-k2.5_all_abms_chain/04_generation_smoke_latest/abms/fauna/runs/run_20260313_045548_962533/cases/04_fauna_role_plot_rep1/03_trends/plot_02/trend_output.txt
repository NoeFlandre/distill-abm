The simulations reveal a consistent pattern of energy depletion across all stochastic runs. Initially, at step 0, the total energy available to forager bands begins at approximately 1,000 to 1,500 energy units. This early phase shows divergent trajectories, suggesting stochastic variation in initial hunting success and energy acquisition rates.

After approximately step 500, the simulations converge toward a uniform linear decline. From this point forward, all runs follow nearly identical downward trajectories with a constant slope, indicating a steady rate of energy loss. By step 1,000, total energy reaches approximately -12,000 to -13,000 units. This linear depletion continues through step 2,000, where values approach -30,000 to -32,000, and persists until the simulations terminate around step 2,200 with final energy values between -32,000 and -38,000, with one extreme case reaching approximately -40,000.

This pattern indicates that forager populations operate under persistent negative energy balance throughout the simulation. The initial 500 steps represent an adjustment phase where stochastic factors create variation in energy acquisition, but once the system stabilizes, the rate of energy expenditure consistently exceeds intake. The linear nature of the decline after step 500 suggests that movement costs and metabolic maintenance requirements create constant energy drains that are not compensated by successful hunting returns, leading to progressive depletion of stored energy reserves across all simulation runs.
