The cumulative number of males killed begins at zero and undergoes a rapid, approximately linear increase during the initial 200 to 400 time steps, reaching values between 20 and 40. This initial phase reflects the onset of foraging activity as hunters encounter and target male prey. Between approximately step 400 and step 800, the cumulative count continues to rise steeply but with increasing variability across simulation runs, climbing from roughly 40 to between 80 and 100 individuals killed. This period represents the primary hunting phase where foragers actively exploit the male segment of the prey population.

After approximately step 800 to 1000, the curves begin to flatten markedly as the system approaches saturation. The rate of increase diminishes significantly, with most trajectories stabilizing between 105 and 125 cumulative kills by steps 1200 to 1500. This plateau indicates the depletion of the available male prey population to levels where few additional males remain accessible for hunting. The persistence of hunting pressure from immortal foragers with food storage capabilities means that once the male population is reduced below a critical threshold, further kills become rare events.

Final values at termination (between steps 1500 and 2000) range from approximately 105 to 125 males killed across different simulation runs. This variation reflects stochastic differences in spatial encounter rates, forager movement patterns, and the demographic composition of the prey population at initialization. The dynamics demonstrate that given the specified sexual dimorphism in food value (males providing 30 units versus 27 for females) and processing costs, male-targeted hunting rapidly depletes the male cohort despite equal net energetic returns between sexes, leading to male-biased mortality patterns that stabilize only when the male population is substantially reduced.
