The cumulative mortality of female ungulates exhibits a saturating growth pattern across the 2,312 time steps of the simulation series. Initiating at zero fatalities, the metric undergoes rapid acceleration during the first approximately 285 ticks, with inflection points detected at indices 245, 262, and 285 indicating the first major deceleration in kill rates. This early phase is characterized by steep monotonic increases, transitioning through change points at tick 125, 375, 475, 625, and 850, which demarcate shifts in the velocity of accumulation as the initial population of vulnerable females encounters the forager bands.

Following this establishment period, the series enters a prolonged deceleration phase lasting until approximately tick 1,826, where the mean cumulative fatalities reach a maximum value of 123.67 across the 40 simulation runs. During this interval, additional inflection points at indices 1,204, 1,336, 1,487, 1,631, 1,725, and 1,800 mark progressive steps toward saturation as the rate of new kills diminishes. The mean value across all observations stands at 104.56, with a median of 119.06, indicating that the distribution of cumulative kills over time is right-skewed toward the upper asymptote.

The terminal phase of the simulation, spanning roughly ticks 1,856 through 2,312, demonstrates stabilization around a plateau of approximately 123 individuals, with the final recorded mean value at 123.00. Statistical analysis of rolling temporal windows reveals persistent significantly increasing trends from ticks 0 through 1,855, with Kendall's tau values exceeding 0.12 and slopes declining from 0.213 to 0.000 as the system approaches equilibrium. The final temporal window (ticks 1,856–2,087) exhibits a significant decreasing trend (tau = -0.453, p < 0.001), coinciding with minor oscillations detected at indices 2,170 through 2,265 with a dominant periodicity of 63 ticks, suggesting micro-fluctuations around the asymptotic limit.

The step-wise deceleration evident at change points and inflection indices tracks the progressive exhaustion of female prey availability, while the high seasonality parameter (0.8) and grassland dynamics likely contribute to the stochastic variation observed in the timing of kills across individual simulation runs before the final convergence to maximum cumulative mortality.
