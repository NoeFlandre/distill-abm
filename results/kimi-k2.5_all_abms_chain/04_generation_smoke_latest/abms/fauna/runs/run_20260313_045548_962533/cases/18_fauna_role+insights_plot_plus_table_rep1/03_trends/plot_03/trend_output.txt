Across the 80 simulation replicates, the cumulative mortality of male ungulates follows a characteristic saturation trajectory. Beginning from zero at the initialization of hunting activity, the metric exhibits a pronounced logistic-like growth pattern, accumulating approximately 60 kills within the first 500 ticks and reaching roughly 100 kills by tick 850. The rate of increase decelerates markedly after approximately 362 ticks, as evidenced by the first major inflection in the aggregate series, transitioning from rapid acceleration to a slower approach toward maximum values.

By tick 1000, the series approaches an asymptotic ceiling near 118 to 120 individuals, with the maximum observed mean value of 120.17 occurring at tick 1876. Between ticks 1160 and 1391, and again between 1624 and 1855, the trend stabilizes statistically, showing no significant directional movement. This plateau indicates the effective extirpation of the harvestable male population, as cumulative counts cease increasing despite continued hunting activity. The final recorded mean value at tick 2312 stands at 117.5, suggesting that nearly all males present in the initial population of 250 ungulates succumb to hunting pressure over the course of the simulation.

Rolling trend analysis reveals nine distinct phases of accumulation, with strongly significant positive slopes (p < 0.001) dominating the first 1159 ticks, followed by periods of stagnation and eventual slight decline in the terminal phase (ticks 1856–2087). This late decrease, coupled with oscillatory behavior detected around ticks 2170–2265, reflects stochastic variation as the last remaining males are pursued across varying landscape conditions and seasonal cycles (operating at the calibrated 0.8 seasonality parameter with 365 ticks per year).

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The rapid saturation of male mortality indicates that current hunter density (20 foragers) and target preferences (males valued at 30 energy units versus 27 for females) create unsustainable pressure on the male subpopulation, leading to functional elimination within approximately three to five simulated years. Wildlife managers should recognize that sex-biased harvesting at these parameters results in near-complete demographic collapse of males, potentially impairing herd reproductive viability long before the maximum simulation timeframe. To prevent population collapse, intervention strategies must either reduce forager numbers, lower the nutritional valuation of males relative to processing costs, or implement seasonal restrictions that align with critical reproductive periods.
