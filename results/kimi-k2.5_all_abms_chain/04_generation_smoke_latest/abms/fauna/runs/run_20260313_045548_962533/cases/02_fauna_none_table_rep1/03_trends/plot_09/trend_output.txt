The female prey population initiated at 125 individuals and underwent a protracted decline toward extinction over 2,311 time steps. During the first approximately 475 time steps, the population decreased rapidly from 125 toward intermediate abundance levels. This steep decline moderated after time step 475 but continued with significant volatility through approximately time step 1,159, characterized by progressively diminishing peak abundances.

Between time steps 1,160 and 1,391, the trajectory reversed temporarily, exhibiting a statistically significant increasing trend that briefly interrupted the long-term decline before the population resumed its downward path. During the subsequent period (approximately time steps 1,392–1,855), abundance continued to decrease, maintaining values generally below 5 individuals after approximately time step 700, with temporary fluctuations peaking at roughly 6.5 individuals around time step 730 and declining to below 2 individuals by time step 1,000.

A second transient recovery occurred between time steps 1,856 and 2,087, showing another statistically significant increasing trend from the depleted base levels established in the prior phase. Following this brief resurgence, the population declined irreversibly, reaching zero individuals by time step 2,311. Throughout the latter portion of the simulation (after approximately time step 700), the system exhibited persistent oscillations with a period of approximately 63 time steps superimposed on the declining trend, with the median abundance across the full simulation duration falling to 1 individual, indicating that the population persisted at critically low levels for the majority of the observation period.

This extinction trajectory reflects the demographic pressure exerted by constant hunter presence (with immortal foragers maintaining sustained predation pressure) combined with life history constraints including a two-year birth interval (730 time steps) and senescence at 20 years (7,300 time steps). The temporary increases in abundance suggest brief demographic windows where recruitment temporarily exceeded mortality, possibly corresponding to cohorts of females reaching reproductive age or temporary spatial dynamics that reduced hunting efficiency. The oscillatory dynamics likely reflect interactions between high environmental seasonality (0.8), grass regrowth cycles, and reproductive periodicity, while the asymmetry in processing costs between sexes may have contributed to sustained targeting of females despite their only marginally lower food value relative to males, ultimately precluding population recovery.
