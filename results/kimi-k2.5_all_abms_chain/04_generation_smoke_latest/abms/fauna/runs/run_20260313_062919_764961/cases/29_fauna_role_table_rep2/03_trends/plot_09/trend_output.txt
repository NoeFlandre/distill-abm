The female megaherbivore population initiated at 125 individuals and underwent a deterministic collapse to extinction by tick 2311. Across the 2,312 time steps, the population exhibited a strongly right-skewed distribution with a mean of approximately 16.5 females but a median of 1, indicating that the system spent the majority of its duration at near-extinction densities following an initial rapid demographic contraction.

The trajectory comprises five distinct phases. During the initial collapse (ticks 0–305), the population declined precipitously from 125 females, passing an inflection point at tick 305 that marked the transition from the steepest descent to a decelerating—but continued—decline. Structural shifts in the rate of decrease occurred at ticks 475 and 600, after which the population stabilized into a low-density equilibrium oscillating between approximately 5.0 and 6.5 individuals from ticks 700 through 900. These oscillations displayed a regular periodicity of roughly 63 ticks, with local maxima reaching approximately 6.5 females at tick 730 and 4.4 females at tick 803, interspersed with troughs near 4.6–5.0 females.

Following this unstable persistence, the population entered a transient recovery phase between ticks 1160 and 1391, during which it exhibited a statistically significant positive trend despite low absolute abundance. This respite proved temporary, as the subsequent interval (ticks 1392–1855) reverted to significant decline, punctuated by inflection points at ticks 1410, 1469, 1594, 1679, and 1763 indicating continually shifting rates of decrease. A second, more pronounced recovery attempt occurred between ticks 1856 and 2087, showing a strong positive trend in the endgame sequence, yet this ultimately failed to prevent final collapse. The population reached zero females at tick 2311, immediately following a change point at tick 2310 that marked the definitive extinction event.

Throughout the low-density phases, the system exhibited high variance (standard deviation of 31.55) relative to the mean, reflecting extreme demographic volatility characteristic of small populations subject to Allee effects, stochastic hunting mortality from constant human predation pressure, and strong environmental seasonality. The oscillatory dynamics superimposed on the declining trend suggest that brief recruitment pulses—likely tied to the 730-tick interbirth intervals and seasonal vegetation cycles—periodically offset harvest pressure, but insufficiently to overcome the continuous predation by efficient, energy-storing foragers operating with a perception radius of 6 patches and no mortality risk.
