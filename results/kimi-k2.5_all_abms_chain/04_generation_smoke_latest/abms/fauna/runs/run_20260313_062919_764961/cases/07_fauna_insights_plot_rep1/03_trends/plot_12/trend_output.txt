The total energy available in the female prey population initiates at consistent levels between approximately 12,000 and 12,500 units across simulation runs. During the initial phase spanning roughly the first 500 time steps, the metric undergoes rapid depletion in most iterations, with numerous trajectories declining to values below 2,000 and several approaching zero. However, substantial variability characterizes the rate of decline during this period: while some simulations exhaust female energy reserves entirely by step 400 to 500, others maintain intermediate levels of 3,000 to 4,000 units through step 500, with individual runs briefly sustaining values near 10,000 before experiencing sudden crashes. Beyond step 500, populations retaining residual energy exhibit continued stepwise declines, descending gradually from several thousand units toward zero over subsequent hundreds of steps. By step 1000, the majority of simulations have reduced female total energy to values below 500 units. Between steps 1500 and 2000, nearly all trajectories converge at or near zero, indicating complete depletion of energy stores in the female population. The staircase pattern of decay suggests discrete mortality events rather than continuous exponential decline. Under the parameter configuration tested—incorporating 20 human foragers, high environmental seasonality (0.8), female food value of 27, and birth spacing of 730 ticks with nursing offspring increasing target value—these dynamics indicate persistent hunting pressure drives female megaherbivore populations toward inevitable demographic collapse despite stochastic variation in extinction timing.
