The repeated simulations reveal a consistent pattern of linear energy depletion among forager bands, with the aggregate hunter population experiencing a steady decline in total stored energy throughout the temporal extent of the model runs.

During the first approximately 500 time steps, the total energy exhibits substantial inter-simulation variability, with initial values clustering near 1,500 energy units and declining to values ranging between approximately -2,000 and -6,000 by step 500. This early variability likely reflects stochastic differences in initial spatial distributions of hunters and prey, as well as variable early hunting success before the system stabilizes.

After approximately step 500, the trajectories converge and follow a highly consistent linear decline. From step 500 to step 2000, the total energy decreases at a rate of approximately 15 to 17 energy units per step, falling from roughly -5,000 to between -32,000 and -35,000 by the termination of most runs. One simulation extends to approximately step 2200, reaching nearly -38,000 energy units.

Given the parameter configuration with 20 initial foragers, movement costs of 1 energy unit per step per agent, and the activation of energy storage mechanisms, the observed decline rate of approximately 15-17 units per step suggests that the forager population consistently fails to achieve energy balance. With baseline movement costs alone accounting for 20 units of expenditure per step (20 foragers × 1 unit), the observed depletion rate indicates that energy acquisition through hunting returns only 3-5 units per step on average across the population, resulting in a chronic net deficit of approximately 75-85% of baseline metabolic requirements. The reduction in variability after step 500 indicates that initial transient conditions give way to a steady-state system where hunting success becomes predictable but insufficient. The activation of immortal-foragers prevents population collapse despite the severe energy deficit, but the linear decline suggests that without external supplementation or behavioral adaptation, the energy reservoir would theoretically deplete indefinitely.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
These results suggest that under the modeled environmental conditions—including high seasonality (0.8) and the specified vegetation dynamics—human foraging populations of 20 individuals face unsustainable energetic constraints when hunting megaherbivore populations of 250 individuals with the given life history parameters. Decision makers should note that sustainable coexistence may require either reduced hunter numbers, expanded foraging radii beyond the current setting of 6 patches, or alternative subsistence strategies not captured in the current model configuration. The consistency of the linear decline across multiple runs indicates this outcome is robust to stochastic variation in initial conditions, suggesting that the current parameter combination lies firmly within a regime of chronic hunter energy deficit rather than equilibrium or surplus.
