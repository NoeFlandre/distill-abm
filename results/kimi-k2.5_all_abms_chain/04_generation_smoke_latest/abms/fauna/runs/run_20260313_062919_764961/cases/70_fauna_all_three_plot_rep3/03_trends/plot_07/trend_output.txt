The cumulative death count begins at zero and rises sharply during the initial phase of the simulation. Within the first 500 steps, mortality accumulates rapidly, reaching between approximately 100 and 230 deaths across different simulation runs. This initial surge reflects the immediate impact of 20 human foragers operating under optimal foraging strategies upon the initial population of 250 ungulates, compounded by baseline physiological mortality, external predation, and potential starvation under high seasonal resource fluctuation.

Between steps 500 and 1000, the rate of mortality increase decelerates considerably as the susceptible population dwindles. Most outcomes accumulate between 220 and 245 deaths by step 1000, approaching the limit of the initial cohort size. The variation in timing during this phase, with some runs reaching these mortality levels by step 800 while others require the full 1000 steps or more, indicates stochastic variation in hunter-prey encounter rates and the spatial heterogeneity of vegetation resources.

After approximately 1000 steps, the cumulative mortality stabilizes between 245 and 250 deaths across all simulation outcomes. Some trajectories achieve this asymptotic state as early as step 800, whereas others continue to record occasional mortality events extending beyond step 2000. This prolonged tail reflects rare survival of individual animals that evade detection or maintain favorable energetic status, though ultimately the entire initial cohort succumbs to the combined mortality pressures.

The convergence toward 250 total deaths—equivalent to the initial population size—demonstrates deterministic population collapse under the specified parameter configuration. The temporal variation in the approach to extinction, spanning from approximately 800 to over 2000 steps, suggests that while final elimination is consistent across stochastic realizations, the rate of decline remains sensitive to individual-level variation in movement patterns, foraging success, and encounter probabilities.

From a management perspective, the consistency of total population elimination across stochastic runs indicates that the current combination of 20 human foragers and high environmental seasonality represents an unsustainable pressure regime for the megaherbivore population. Decision makers should note that while the timing of final extinction varies considerably, the trajectory toward zero survival appears inevitable without intervention. The rapid accumulation of 40 to 90 percent of total mortality within the first 500 steps suggests that early warning indicators of population stress are visible well before final extinction, providing a critical window for management action. The substantial variation in extinction timing across otherwise identical conditions further implies that short-term population assessments may be unreliable predictors of long-term persistence, necessitating extended monitoring periods to accurately assess conservation status.
