The cumulative number of prey deaths initiates at zero and undergoes a rapid, sigmoid-like accumulation over the first 850 time steps, subsequently stabilizing into a prolonged plateau phase with minor oscillations through the remainder of the simulation period, ultimately converging at values between 220 and 248 deaths.

During the initial phase, spanning approximately the first 125 to 500 time steps, mortality accumulates aggressively. Statistical change points are detected at 125, 250, 375, and 500 steps, marking successive transitions in the accumulation rate. Rolling trend analysis confirms a highly significant monotonic increase throughout this interval, with slopes decreasing progressively from 0.346 to 0.017 deaths per tick across consecutive windows, indicating that while the cumulative count rises steeply, the instantaneous mortality rate begins moderating as the prey population responds to foraging pressure. An inflection point at step 362 demarcates the transition from accelerating to decelerating growth.

Between steps 500 and 850, the accumulation continues but at a markedly reduced velocity, with additional change points identified at 625 and 850. During this transitional phase, the slope of increase diminishes further to approximately 0.059 and then 0.017, reflecting the depletion of susceptible individuals or the establishment of defensive herd behaviors.

Following step 850, the cumulative death count enters a saturation phase characterized by negligible net increase. Rolling trend analysis indicates no significant trend between steps 1160–1391 and 1624–1855, with values stabilizing around a median of 233 deaths and oscillating within a narrow band. The maximum aggregate value of approximately 240 deaths occurs around step 1876. Subsequent windows (1856–2087) exhibit a slightly negative trend (tau = -0.446, slope = 0.000), indicating that new mortality events effectively cease and the cumulative count stabilizes. Minor oscillations with a periodicity of approximately 63 steps persist throughout this plateau, likely reflecting seasonal environmental fluctuations affecting mortality patterns.

Across the 40 simulation replicates, substantial variance exists in the temporal trajectories. While most runs converge to final values between 235 and 245, individual simulations exhibit divergent dynamics: some achieve rapid saturation near 230 deaths by step 1000, while others demonstrate extended accumulation phases continuing beyond step 1500, terminating at lower final values near 200–220. This heterogeneity suggests sensitivity to stochastic variation in hunting encounters or vegetation regrowth patterns.

In the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, these dynamics illustrate the demographic impact of sustained foraging pressure on an initial cohort of 250 prey. The rapid early accumulation represents the initial exploitation phase, where 20 human foragers encounter naïve prey, resulting in approximately 200 deaths within the first 1000 steps. The subsequent plateau indicates that the system has reached a quasi-equilibrium where prey births balance deaths from hunting and natural causes, or alternatively, that surviving prey have adopted spatial distributions that minimize encounter rates with hunters. The high terminal cumulative values—approaching 96% of the initial population—demonstrate substantial cohort turnover over the simulated interval, consistent with continuous predation pressure, baseline mortality rates of 0.025, and senescence effects emerging after 7,300 ticks, notwithstanding the replenishment of the population through reproduction at 730-tick birth intervals.
