The cumulative number of postpartum females killed begins at zero across all simulation runs. During the initial approximately 500 time steps, this metric increases rapidly, with values reaching between 60 and 100. This early acceleration reflects high encounter rates between hunter agents and vulnerable postpartum females when prey populations are near initial capacity, combined with the elevated food value of nursing females that makes them preferred targets under optimal foraging logic.

Between approximately 500 and 1000 time steps, the rate of increase slows considerably as cumulative values approach the range of 100 to 120. By approximately 1000 to 1500 time steps, the cumulative count stabilizes and reaches a plateau across most runs, with final values converging between approximately 105 and 125. This plateau indicates that hunting of postpartum females effectively ceases, likely due to depletion of this demographic class through direct mortality or broader collapse of the prey population below viable reproductive thresholds, potentially exacerbated by the two-year birth spacing interval limiting the recruitment of new postpartum females.

Variation across simulation runs is most pronounced in the timing of accumulation during the initial 500 steps, with some runs showing delayed onset of hunting success while others progress rapidly, though final cumulative values remain relatively consistent across the ensemble. Minor increases continue in some runs until approximately 2000 steps, but the majority of postpartum female mortality is concentrated in the first 1000 steps of the simulation.
