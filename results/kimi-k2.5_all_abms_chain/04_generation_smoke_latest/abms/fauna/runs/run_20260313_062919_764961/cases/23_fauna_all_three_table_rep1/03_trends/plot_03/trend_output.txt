The cumulative mortality of male ungulates begins at zero and undergoes a rapid accumulation phase throughout the initial 850 ticks, reaching approximately half of the terminal value by tick 500. This phase is characterized by steep monotonic increases, with the rate of accumulation being highest in the interval between ticks 232 and 463. Multiple inflection points detected at ticks 362, 500, and 625 indicate successive shifts in the acceleration of hunting mortality, likely corresponding to the establishment of forager settlement patterns and the initial encounter phase between human agents and naive prey populations.

Between ticks 850 and 1856, the accumulation rate decelerates substantially, transitioning from rapid growth to a quasi-stable plateau oscillating around 118 to 120 individuals. The series exhibits no significant directional trend between ticks 1160 and 1391, followed by a modest resurgence in accumulation between ticks 1392 and 1623, suggesting temporary intensification of male-targeted hunting or demographic recovery of male herds. The maximum cumulative mortality of 120.17 occurs at tick 1876, representing the saturation point of hunting pressure on males across the simulation ensemble.

Following this peak, the metric enters a distinct declining phase from tick 1856 through 2087, ultimately terminating at 117.5 by tick 2312. Late-period oscillations with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks emerge after tick 2170, superimposed over the downward trajectory. This late-stage decline indicates that in a subset of simulation runs, cumulative male mortality effectively stabilizes or reverses as populations become extirpated or hunters abandon male pursuit due to declining encounter rates, with the cross-simulation mean reflecting the cessation of hunting activity in a growing proportion of replicates.

The trajectory reveals an S-curve dynamic: explosive initial exploitation, followed by resource stabilization, then exhaustion. The pronounced gap between the median (116.5) and mean (100.4) values indicates high variance across simulation runs, with some experiments experiencing dramatically lower cumulative mortality than others, suggesting that stochastic factors or threshold effects determine whether male populations sustain prolonged hunting pressure or collapse early. The 63-tick oscillation cycle visible in the terminal phase aligns with the strong seasonal forcing (0.8 seasonality) parameterized in the model, implicating pulsed resource availability in driving episodic hunting patterns even as overall viability declines.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The rapid accumulation to near-maximum levels within the first 1,000 ticks suggests that male megaherbivore populations face immediate existential risk upon hunter colonization, with little lag time between initial contact and unsustainable mortality. The plateau phase around 118–120 individuals likely represents a critical threshold where male densities become too low to support continued hunting profitability, forcing behavioral switching or forager abandonment. Conservation interventions would need to occur within the first 500 ticks to prevent irreversible male depletion, as the late-stage oscillations and decline indicate that population recovery mechanisms are insufficient to counteract cumulative mortality once the exploitation trajectory peaks.
