The cumulative mortality of male megaherbivores initiates at zero and undergoes rapid accumulation during the first approximately 850 time steps, rising sharply as forager bands encounter and pursue available male prey. This initial phase exhibits a decelerating rate of increase, with the accumulation slope declining sequentially through change points at 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850, suggesting diminishing hunting success as prey density decreases or forager energy constraints become limiting.

Following this establishment phase, the system enters a period of variable accumulation characterized by fluctuations rather than steady growth between approximately ticks 1160 and 1856. Notable inflection points at 1241, 1330, 1397, 1455, 1474, and 1595 indicate repeated adjustments in the rate of male mortality accumulation, potentially reflecting dynamic interactions between herd movement patterns, seasonal environmental constraints (with an observed oscillation period of roughly 63 ticks), and shifting forager pursuit decisions based on optimal foraging calculations.

The cumulative kill count reaches its maximum value of approximately 120 individuals around tick 1876, after which it exhibits a slight downward trend, declining to 117.5 by tick 2312. This late-phase decrease, occurring alongside oscillatory behavior through indices 2170-2265, may indicate that in some simulation instances, herd extinction events terminate hunting opportunities, or that sample-wide hunting pressure diminishes as surviving herds adopt more successful avoidance strategies or occupy less accessible areas.

Implications for decision makers: The rapid initial accumulation of male mortality suggests that male megaherbivores face acute vulnerability to hunting pressure early in the interaction period, with populations potentially losing over 100 reproductive-age males within the first several years of simulation time. The subsequent plateau and decline indicate that hunting pressure either self-limits through prey depletion or enters a dynamic equilibrium state. The persistent oscillatory patterns throughout the time series imply that managers should expect cyclical fluctuations in hunting success rather than linear progression toward extinction, with critical periods occurring at approximately 63-tick intervals that may correspond to seasonal resource availability or reproductive cycles.
