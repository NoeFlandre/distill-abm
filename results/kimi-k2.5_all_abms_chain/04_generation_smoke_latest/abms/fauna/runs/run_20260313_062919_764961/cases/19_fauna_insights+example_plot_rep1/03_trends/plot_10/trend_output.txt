The total prey population initiates at 250 individuals across all simulation instances. Within the first 500 time steps, the population undergoes a precipitous decline, with most trajectories falling below 50 individuals and several reaching counts below 10. This rapid decrease reflects the immediate demographic impact of 20 foragers operating under optimal foraging strategies, combined with baseline mortality rates and the patchy grassland environment with seasonal constraints on resource availability.

Between 500 and 1,000 steps, the decline continues with increasing variability between runs. While some populations reach effective extinction (0-2 individuals) by step 600, others maintain persistent small groups of 10-20 individuals until approximately step 900. Following this period, the majority of trajectories converge near zero by step 1,200, though several runs exhibit extended persistence tails where 1-5 individuals survive beyond step 2,000.

The extinction dynamics display substantial stochastic variation in timing, with complete extinction occurring as early as step 400 in some instances and persisting beyond step 2,100 in others. This variance emerges from the interaction between the fixed hunting pressure from immortal foragers, demographic stochasticity in herd reproduction constrained by 730-tick birth spacing intervals, and spatial heterogeneity in the 211×211 wrapped environment affecting encounter rates between hunters and the diminishing prey population.

Overall, the population trajectories demonstrate deterministic extinction outcomes under the current parameter configuration, where the combination of constant hunting mortality and life history constraints (limited fecundity and senescence effects after 7,300 ticks) prevents population recovery or stabilization. The final decline phases, where small populations persist at very low densities, indicate the difficulty of complete extinction when prey utilize spatial refugia, though eventual extinction remains inevitable given the sustained predator presence.

For decision makers: These results suggest that megaherbivore populations face certain extinction when subjected to continuous hunting pressure from as few as 20 human foragers under the modeled life history constraints. Conservation strategies must prioritize reducing hunting pressure below critical thresholds or enhancing reproductive rates through supplementary feeding or reduced interbirth intervals. The observed variability in extinction timing implies that field monitoring must account for extended periods of low population density that may precede final extinction, avoiding premature conclusions about population viability during temporary persistence phases.
