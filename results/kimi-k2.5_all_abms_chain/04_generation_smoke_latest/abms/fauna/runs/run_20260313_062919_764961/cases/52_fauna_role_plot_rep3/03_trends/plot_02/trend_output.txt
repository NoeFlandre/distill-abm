Across all simulation replicates, total forager energy exhibits a consistent trajectory of decline from initial positive values to substantial negative values. The metric initiates at approximately +2,000 energy units. During the initial phase spanning roughly the first 400–600 steps, trajectories display moderate inter-simulation variability, with values declining to between approximately -4,000 and -6,000.

Subsequently, from approximately step 600 through the remainder of the simulation, trajectories converge tightly and follow a steep, essentially linear decline with a rate of approximately 18–20 energy units per step. This rate corresponds closely to the aggregate movement costs of the 20 foragers, each incurring a cost of 1 energy unit per tick, indicating minimal net energy acquisition from hunting during this sustained phase.

By step 1,000, total energy reaches approximately -12,000. The decline continues uniformly, reaching roughly -22,000 by step 1,500. Simulation runs terminate at varying time points between approximately steps 2,000 and 2,200, with final total energy values ranging from approximately -32,000 to -38,000.

The transition from variable initial dynamics to a tightly constrained linear decline suggests a systematic shift in forager-prey interactions, whereby energy expenditure from movement consistently exceeds energy gain from hunting. This results in the accumulation of energy debt at a predictable rate determined primarily by forager movement costs rather than variable hunting success, consistent with the model parameterization where foragers are immortal and can accumulate negative energy balances indefinitely.
