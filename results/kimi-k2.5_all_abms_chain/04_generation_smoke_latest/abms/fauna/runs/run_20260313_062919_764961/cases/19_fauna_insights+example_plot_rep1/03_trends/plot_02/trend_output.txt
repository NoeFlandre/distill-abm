By step 1,000, total energy reaches approximately -12,000; by step 1,500, approximately -20,000; and by step 2,000 to 2,200, values range from -30,000 to -37,000.

Variation across simulation runs is highest during the first 500 steps, with deviations of approximately 2,000 energy units between the highest and lowest values. After step 500, the variance diminishes substantially, with the different simulation runs converging into nearly parallel trends that maintain consistent spacing of only a few hundred units through the remainder of the simulation. This convergence indicates that despite initial stochastic differences in forager movement and prey encounter patterns, the system rapidly settles into a predictable equilibrium of energy depletion.

The persistent negative slope indicates that forager bands are operating under chronic energy deficits, where movement costs consistently exceed energy acquisition from hunting. Given the parameter configuration where foragers are immortal and can store energy, the steady decline into increasingly negative values suggests that prey availability or accessibility is inadequate to support the energetic demands of the foraging strategy, resulting in a continuous degradation of the collective energy status.

**Implications for decision makers**: The reliable linear decline and low variance after step 500 indicate that the current parameter combination produces a deterministic outcome of energy unsustainability. The forager population cannot achieve energetic equilibrium under these conditions, suggesting that either prey densities are insufficient, encounter rates are too low, or the net energy return per hunt is inadequate to compensate for search costs. To prevent this energy deficit spiral, decision makers should consider increasing prey initial densities, reducing forager movement costs, or enhancing prey food values to improve the energetic balance of hunting activities.
