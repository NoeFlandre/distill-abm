The total energy contained within female animal populations initiates at 12,500 units and follows a deterministic trajectory toward extinction (zero) across all simulation replicates, though the temporal progression exhibits substantial heterogeneity. The decline occurs in three primary phases characterized by distinct rates of energy depletion and demographic volatility.

The initial phase spans approximately the first 375 steps, during which the metric undergoes its most precipitous decline, plummeting from 12,500 to roughly 2,200 units. This represents a loss of over 80% of initial biomass within the first 16% of the total simulation duration. The trajectory is monotonically decreasing with high statistical significance (Mann-Kendall tau approaching -1.0), indicating consistent, rapid removal of individuals. This pattern suggests intense initial hunting pressure on a naive population, where hunter success rates are maximized due to high prey densities and minimal prey avoidance learning or spatial refuge effects.

The second transitional phase, extending from step 375 to approximately step 875, exhibits moderated decline rates interspersed with localized fluctuations. Between steps 376 and 410, the series registers temporary plateaus with values oscillating between approximately 2,097 and 2,233 units, suggesting brief windows where energy loss from hunting mortality is partially offset by reproductive recruitment or herd aggregation behaviors. However, these fluctuations represent local optima rather than recovery trends, as the overall trajectory remains negative with significant change points detected at steps 500, 625, and 875. The persistence of oscillatory patterns with a dominant period of 63 steps throughout this phase likely reflects the interaction between seasonal resource availability (seasonality parameter set to 0.8) and hunting pressure, creating regular but insufficient demographic fluctuations.

The terminal phase initiates around step 875, wherein residual energy levels (typically below 1,000 units) undergo gradual attrition toward complete depletion by step 2,311. This phase is characterized by high variance across simulation runs, with the median energy level (100 units) falling substantially below the mean (1,569 units), indicating a right-skewed distribution where most populations persist at minimal energy levels while a minority of replicates survive for extended durations (some persisting beyond step 2,000). Brief periods of statistically significant localized increase occur between steps 1,160–1,391 and 1,856–2,087, suggesting that remnant populations occasionally achieve marginal positive growth or temporary relief from hunting pressure. Nevertheless, these episodes prove insufficient to prevent ultimate extinction, as the population density has apparently fallen below critical thresholds necessary for demographic viability, likely encountering Allee effects related to mate limitation and reduced herd defensive capabilities.

The high standard deviation (3,083 relative to a mean of 1,569) across the 40 simulation runs indicates that stochastic variation in herd movement patterns, local resource competition, and encounter rates with hunters creates unpredictability in exact extinction timing, despite the deterministic outcome. Notably, no simulation maintains energy above 2,500 units beyond step 500, establishing this threshold as a demographic point of no return under the current parameter configuration.

For decision makers, these results indicate that hunting pressure from 20 immortal foragers utilizing energy storage creates inevitable extinction pressure on megaherbivore populations with 730-tick birth spacing and senescence at 7,300 ticks. The critical intervention window occurs within the first 375 steps, before populations lose 80% of their initial energy; once the metric falls below 2,500 units, recovery becomes demographically unfeasible despite continued reproduction attempts. Conservation strategies must account for high variability in persistence times—a population might collapse rapidly or persist for over 2,000 steps—requiring robust monitoring rather than reliance on average extinction estimates. The oscillatory patterns suggest that seasonal resource management could temporarily buffer populations but cannot alone prevent extinction under sustained hunting pressure.
