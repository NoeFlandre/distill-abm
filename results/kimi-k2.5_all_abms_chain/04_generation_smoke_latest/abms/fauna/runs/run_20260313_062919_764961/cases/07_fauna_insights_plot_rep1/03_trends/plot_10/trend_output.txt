The data comprise multiple simulation trajectories tracking total prey animal counts over discrete time steps. At initialization (step 0), the population consistently begins at approximately 250 individuals across all runs, corresponding to the initial prey parameter setting.

During the initial phase spanning roughly the first 500 steps, all trajectories exhibit a steep, monotonic decline. By step 500, population levels vary stochastically across runs but generally fall between approximately 10 and 100 individuals, representing a decline of 60 to 96 percent from the initial stock.

From step 500 to approximately step 1000, the decline continues but at a reduced rate for some trajectories, while others approach zero more rapidly. By step 1000, most populations have fallen below 20 individuals, with many reaching single-digit counts or zero.

Between steps 1000 and 1500, the majority of trajectories converge toward extinction, defined as zero or near-zero population counts. A minority of runs exhibit prolonged persistence, maintaining small populations of 1 to 5 individuals until approximately step 1500 to 2000, though these eventually decline to zero as well. Given the calibration of 365 ticks per year, this extinction dynamic unfolds over approximately 4 to 5.5 years.

The consistent pattern across all stochastic realizations indicates that under the current parameter configuration—featuring 20 initial hunters with immortal status, specified food values and processing costs favoring hunting efficiency, and constrained environmental carrying capacity—the prey population faces inevitable extinction. The lack of any stable equilibrium or recovery trajectory suggests that hunting pressure substantially exceeds the maximum sustainable yield of the prey population's reproductive capacity.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The data indicate that the current hunting pressure creates an unsustainable extraction rate that inevitably leads to prey extinction within approximately 4 to 5.5 years. Decision makers should recognize that reducing hunter numbers, limiting hunter lifespan (mortality), or decreasing hunting efficiency (by adjusting food values, processing costs, or foraging radius) would be necessary to prevent population collapse. Alternatively, increasing the prey's reproductive rate (shortening birth spacing) or environmental carrying capacity (increasing grass proportion or regrowth) could potentially offset hunting pressure, though the current trajectory suggests the deficit between harvest and recruitment is substantial. Immediate intervention is required to avoid ecosystem collapse.
