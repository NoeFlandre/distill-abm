The total megaherbivore population begins at 250 individuals across all simulation runs. The population exhibits a consistent downward trajectory throughout the temporal sequence, ultimately declining to near-zero levels in all replicates. The rate of decline varies substantially across runs, with the fastest declines reducing populations to fewer than 10 individuals within approximately 400-500 ticks, while the slowest declines maintain populations above 50 individuals until roughly 800-1000 ticks before eventual collapse.

During the initial phase (approximately 0-300 ticks), all populations experience rapid reduction, typically falling below 150 individuals. This steep initial decline suggests immediate pressure from the 20 human foragers exploiting the initially dense prey population, combined with the high encounter rates typical of optimal foraging behavior when prey are abundant. The variation in decline rates during this phase likely reflects stochastic differences in hunter-prey spatial overlap and early hunting success.

Between approximately 300-800 ticks, the decline rate slows in most runs but remains negative, with populations typically dropping from 150 to below 50 individuals. This deceleration may correspond to reduced hunting efficiency as prey density decreases and foragers must expend more energy searching, though the continued downward trend indicates that hunting mortality exceeds the combined recruitment from reproduction (governed by the 730-tick interbirth interval) and survival from other causes.

After approximately 1000 ticks, the vast majority of simulation runs reach populations of fewer than 10 individuals, with complete extinction or near-extinction (1-3 individuals) occurring by 1500 ticks in most cases. A small subset of runs maintains trace populations (5-20 individuals) until 1800-2200 ticks, suggesting rare configurations where spatial refuge or demographic stochasticity temporarily buffers against extinction. The complete collapse across all replicates indicates that the combination of 20 human hunters, high seasonality (0.8), and the specific life history parameters creates an unsustainable harvesting pressure that overwhelms the ungulate population's maximum growth potential.
