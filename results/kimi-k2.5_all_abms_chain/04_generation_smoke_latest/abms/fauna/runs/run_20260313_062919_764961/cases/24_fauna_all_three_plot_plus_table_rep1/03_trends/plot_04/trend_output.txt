The cumulative mortality of female megaherbivores initiates at zero and undergoes a rapid accumulation phase during the first approximately 500 time steps, reaching values near 100 individuals. This initial phase represents the period of highest hunting intensity, where human foragers encounter and target female prey at maximum rates, driven by the initial population of 250 ungulates distributed across the grassland landscape.

Between approximately 500 and 1000 time steps, the rate of accumulation decelerates substantially, with cumulative mortality increasing from roughly 100 to approximately 115–120 individuals. This deceleration suggests a declining encounter rate between hunters and females, potentially driven by prey depletion, altered herd movement patterns as populations decline, or shifts in hunter targeting behavior toward alternative prey categories as the optimal foraging calculus adjusts to reduced female availability.

After approximately 1000 time steps, the cumulative mortality stabilizes near a maximum value of approximately 123 individuals, exhibiting minimal additional accumulation through the remainder of the simulation period extending to 2312 time steps. This plateau indicates the effective cessation of female hunting mortality, likely resulting from the functional extinction of accessible females within the foraging radius of hunter bands or the abandonment of female targeting by foragers due to diminished returns and processing costs relative to search effort.

The temporal dynamics exhibit periodic fluctuations with a dominant oscillation period of approximately 63 time steps, consistent with seasonal environmental variation given the 365-tick annual calibration and the seasonality parameter set at 0.8. These oscillations reflect cyclical changes in hunting success rates corresponding to seasonal pulses in grass availability and prey vulnerability, creating regular windows of elevated and depressed female mortality risk.

Statistical change points in the accumulation trajectory occur at approximately 125, 375, 475, 625, and 850 time steps, marking discrete transitions in the hunting pressure dynamics. The most pronounced inflections occur between 245–285 time steps and again at 1204 and 1336 time steps, indicating periods of significant acceleration or deceleration in female mortality rates as the system transitions from exploitation to depletion phases.

For decision makers: The trajectory indicates that approximately 80% of cumulative female mortality occurs within the first 1000 time steps (approximately 2.7 years), suggesting a narrow critical window for intervention before demographic collapse. The pronounced seasonal oscillation of 63 ticks indicates predictable periods of elevated vulnerability that could inform targeted protection measures during high-risk seasons. The stabilization at approximately 123 individuals suggests a hard limit on sustainable female mortality under these parameter conditions, beyond which the population segment becomes functionally unavailable to hunters, indicating that continued hunting pressure beyond this threshold yields no additional harvest and may signal local extirpation requiring population recovery periods or hunting moratoria.
