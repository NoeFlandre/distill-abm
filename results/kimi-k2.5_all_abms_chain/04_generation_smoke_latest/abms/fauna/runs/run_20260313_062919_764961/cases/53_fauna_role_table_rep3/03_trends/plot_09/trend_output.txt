The population trajectory of female megaherbivores reveals a collapse dynamic spanning approximately 6.3 years (2,311 daily ticks), commencing with 125 individuals and terminating in complete extinction. Following an initial steep decline during the first 475 ticks (1.3 years), the population decelerated through successive phases of attrition, ultimately persisting at a median abundance of just one female for the majority of the simulation duration.

The initial phase (ticks 0–475) witnessed a rapid monotonic decrease from 125 females toward single-digit abundance, with statistical trend analysis confirming significant negative slopes exceeding -0.06 per tick. This precipitous collapse occurred despite the absence of hunter mortality from energy depletion, suggesting that the combination of 20 active foragers employing a 6-patch foraging radius and the high processing efficiency for females (cost of 3 energy units versus 6 for males) exerted immediate unsustainable pressure on the founding population of 250 ungulates.

Between ticks 475 and 875 (years 1.3–2.4), the decline decelerated markedly yet remained directional, transitioning through change points at ticks 475, 600, and 875 that demarcate shifts in population viability. During this interval, the population first dropped below sustainable breeding thresholds defined by the 730-tick (2-year) interbirth interval, creating a demographic bottleneck where the remaining females could not replace losses through reproduction.

A regime of low-density oscillation dominated the subsequent four years, with the population fluctuating between 0 and 7 individuals across a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 ticks (bi-monthly cycles). These oscillations align with the imposed seasonality intensity of 0.8 and rapid vegetation regrowth (3 ticks), creating brief windows of resource availability that allowed marginal survival but insufficient recovery. Notable secondary peaks emerged at tick 730 (year 2, value ~6.5) and tick 820 (year 2.25, value ~4.1), potentially representing limited reproductive output from the initial cohort before age-related senescence (commencing at tick 7,300) could manifest.

Two temporary reversals in the extinction trajectory occurred: first between ticks 1,160–1,391 (years 3.2–3.8) showing a slight increasing trend, and again between ticks 1,856–2,087 (years 5.1–5.7). These brief recoveries likely reflect stochastic reductions in hunting pressure or seasonal resource pulses coinciding with the 730-tick reproductive cycle. However, the population remained below critical thresholds where Allee effects and demographic stochasticity override positive growth rates.

The final phase (post-tick 2,087) exhibited renewed decline punctuated by inflection points at ticks 2,156 and 2,194, culminating in irreversible extinction at tick 2,311. Throughout this terminal period, the population exhibited extreme variance (standard deviation of 31.6 around a mean of 16.5), indicating high volatility during the final collapse sequence. The complete elimination of females by year 6.3—despite immortal hunters maintaining constant predation pressure, seasonal grass regeneration, and sexual dimorphism favoring slightly lower hunting value for females (27 vs. 30)—demonstrates that the initialized parameters created an inherently unstable predator-prey ratio where environmental seasonality could not compensate for the energetic efficiency of human foraging strategies.
