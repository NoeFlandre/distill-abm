The percentage of landscape patches carrying vegetation initiates at approximately 32.5 percent across all simulation runs, maintaining values between 32.0 and 33.0 percent through the first 200 time steps. This initial equilibrium reflects the initialization parameter setting grass coverage at 0.33 of the available patches.

Between time steps 300 and 400, the system experiences a synchronized collapse in vegetated cover across all replicates, with values plummeting to between 23 percent and 25 percent at the nadirs, representing declines of 7 to 9 percentage points. This depletion likely corresponds to intensive grazing pressure from the initial ungulate population of 250 animals concentrated in accessible patches. Recovery commences immediately, with vegetation returning to baseline levels of 32-33 percent by approximately step 500, consistent with the rapid regrowth time of 3 ticks enabling swift vegetation renewal.

A second distinct depletion event occurs around time step 700-750, though attenuated compared to the first, with values dropping to approximately 29-31 percent before recovering to 32.5 percent by step 800. From step 800 through 1100, the system maintains relative stability at 32.5-33 percent, punctuated by a minor dip to approximately 31 percent around step 1100 in several runs.

After step 1400, most simulations stabilize near 33 percent, though individual runs exhibit divergent behaviors including isolated drops to 30-31 percent and one trajectory showing continued oscillation with intermittent declines to 32 percent and recovery to 33 percent through the end of the observation period at step 2200.

The pronounced variance across runs during collapse events, contrasted with convergence during recovery phases, indicates that while grazing pressure stochastically triggers vegetation depletion, the deterministic regrowth mechanism consistently returns the system to baseline. The dampening oscillation amplitude over time suggests that redistribution of ungulate herds or shifting foraging patterns may reduce the probability of intensive localized grazing as the simulation progresses.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The rapid vegetation recovery capacity prevents permanent habitat degradation, but the periodicity of depletion events creates recurrent nutritional bottlenecks for megaherbivores. Management strategies must account for temporary resource scarcity events occurring at 300-400 tick intervals initially, even in systems with high intrinsic regrowth rates. The convergence of runs during recovery phases suggests that intervention timing is less critical during regrowth periods, but monitoring should focus on preventing herd concentrations that trigger the severe drops observed in early simulation phases. Conservation efforts should prioritize maintaining dispersal corridors that prevent excessive spatial concentration of the 250-animal founding population, as the synchronized nature of the initial crash across all replicates indicates that localized overgrazing poses systemic risks to habitat carrying capacity.
