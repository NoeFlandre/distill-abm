The cumulative mortality of female megaherbivores due to human hunting initiates at zero and undergoes rapid accumulation during the first 500 time steps, reaching approximately 100 individuals. This initial phase represents intensive exploitation where forager bands efficiently locate and process female prey, leveraging the lower processing costs relative to males despite equivalent net energetic returns. The rate of increase attenuates significantly between steps 500 and 1500, with cumulative mortality rising from roughly 100 to 120 individuals, indicating resource depletion as the accessible female population diminishes.

By approximately step 1200, the accumulation curve exhibits an inflection point marking the transition to a saturation phase. The mean cumulative kill count peaks at approximately 123.7 individuals near step 1826, representing roughly five years of simulated time given the calibration of 365 ticks per year. Subsequent to this peak, the trajectory stabilizes with minor oscillations before registering a slight decline in the terminal phase beyond step 1850, suggesting heterogeneity in extinction timing across simulation runs.

Multiple statistically significant change points in the accumulation rate occur at steps 125, 375, 475, 625, and 850, corresponding to strategic shifts in forager behavior as prey densities decline. The early inflection points clustered between steps 245 and 285 reflect the exhaustion of high-vulnerability female prey, while later inflections at steps 1204, 1336, and 1631 indicate episodic hunting success as remaining females are encountered sporadically.

The oscillatory pattern detected in the late phase, with a dominant period of 63 steps, likely reflects seasonal variation in hunting pressure aligned with the strong seasonality parameter, where grass availability fluctuations influence herbivore aggregation patterns and hunter success rates. The final plateau at approximately 123 cumulative deaths—approaching the estimated initial female population size given the starting parameters—suggests that sustained hunting pressure drives female megaherbivores to near-extinction within the simulated landscape, with hunting mortality effectively ceasing only when the prey base is exhausted.

For resource managers and conservation decision-makers, these results indicate that even moderate initial forager populations can rapidly deplete female megaherbivore populations when processing costs favor females, achieving approximately 80% of total cumulative mortality within the first 500 time steps. The plateau effect suggests that once female populations fall below critical thresholds, hunting pressure shifts or ceases, but this occurs only after severe demographic contraction. The strong early inflection points signal limited windows for intervention—approximately 125-285 time steps—before hunting efficiency drives irreversible population decline. Managers should note that seasonal resource fluctuations create predictable pulses in hunting mortality that could inform targeted protection measures during high-vulnerability periods.
