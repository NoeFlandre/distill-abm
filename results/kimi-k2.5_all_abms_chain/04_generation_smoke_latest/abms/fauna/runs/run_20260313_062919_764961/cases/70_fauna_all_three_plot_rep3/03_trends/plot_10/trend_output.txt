The total number of prey animals begins at approximately 250 individuals across all simulation runs. Over the first 500 time steps, the population undergoes a rapid and substantial decline, with many trajectories falling below 50 animals and several reaching near-zero levels by step 500. This initial collapse reflects the immediate impact of hunting pressure from 20 human foragers combined with strong seasonal resource fluctuations (seasonality set to 0.8), creating a bottleneck where high encounter rates with hunters coincide with periods of nutritional stress.

Between steps 500 and 1500, the remaining populations continue to decline, though at variable rates across different simulation runs. Some trajectories persist in the range of 10-40 animals during this intermediate period, while others gradually approach zero. This variability suggests that stochastic factors—such as the spatial distribution of remaining herds, individual movement patterns, and the timing of reproduction relative to seasonal resource pulses—create divergent demographic outcomes even under identical parameter settings.

By step 2000, nearly all simulation runs converge toward zero animals, with only isolated trajectories showing minimal persistence at levels below 5 individuals. The eventual extinction or near-extinction across all runs indicates that with immortal hunters (who do not die from energy depletion) and food storage capabilities, hunting pressure remains constant and unsustainable for the prey population, ultimately overwhelming the species' reproductive capacity (governed by a 730-tick interbirth interval) and ability to recover from demographic stochasticity.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**
The results indicate that megaherbivore populations face extreme vulnerability under sustained hunting pressure, particularly when combined with strong environmental seasonality. Conservation or management interventions would need to be implemented within the first 500 time steps to be effective, as populations rapidly pass through critical thresholds where recovery becomes demographically impossible. The high variability in persistence times suggests that small population refugia may temporarily delay extinction, but cannot prevent it under constant hunting pressure without active intervention to reduce hunter success rates or increase prey reproductive output.
