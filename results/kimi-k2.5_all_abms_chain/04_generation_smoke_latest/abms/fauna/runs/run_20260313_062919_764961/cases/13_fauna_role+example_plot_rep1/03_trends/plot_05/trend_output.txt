The cumulative number of postpartum females killed begins at zero across all simulation runs. During the initial phase, roughly spanning the first 200 to 500 steps, the count increases rapidly in a near-linear fashion, rising sharply from 0 to approximately 80–100 individuals. This steep ascent reflects the efficient detection and pursuit of postpartum females by forager agents during the early stages, when the population of vulnerable individuals with high nutritional value (females accompanied by calves) is abundant and concentrated.

Following this initial surge, the rate of increase begins to decelerate noticeably between steps 400 and 800, entering a transitional phase where the slope of the cumulative trend gradually flattens. During this period, the count progresses from approximately 100 to between 110 and 125 individuals, with the pace of killing slowing considerably. This deceleration suggests the depletion of the postpartum female subpopulation through mortality, or alternatively, a shift in forager behavior as the remaining prey become scarcer, more dispersed, or harder to locate due to behavioral adaptations such as increased vigilance or movement away from foraging zones.

Eventually, the cumulative count stabilizes and reaches an asymptote, with most simulation runs plateauing between 115 and 125 total postpartum females killed, though the exact timing of this plateau varies substantially across runs. Some trajectories level off as early as step 600–800, while others continue to show slow, incremental increases until approximately step 1500–2000. The final plateau indicates that the pool of postpartum females available to hunters has been effectively exhausted, or that the remaining individuals have become inaccessible. The variability in the timing of the asymptote reflects the stochastic nature of agent interactions, landscape heterogeneity, and demographic fluctuations inherent in the individual-based modeling approach. The relatively tight clustering of final values around 120 individuals suggests a consistent carrying capacity or maximum impact threshold for postpartum female mortality under the specified parameter configuration of 250 initial prey and the defined reproductive biology parameters.
