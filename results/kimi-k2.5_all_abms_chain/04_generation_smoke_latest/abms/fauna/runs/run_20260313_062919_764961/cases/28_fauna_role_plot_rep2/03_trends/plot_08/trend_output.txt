All simulations initiated with approximately 125 male ungulates. Male populations consistently declined across all runs, eventually reaching extinction, though the timing of extinction varied substantially among simulations due to stochastic demographic processes. During the first 500 time steps, populations declined rapidly, with male counts falling to between 0 and approximately 30 individuals across different runs. By time step 1000, most simulations had reached extinction (0 males), though several runs maintained small persistent populations of 1 to 5 individuals. A subset of simulations exhibited prolonged persistence, with small male populations surviving until approximately time step 1500, and in one case, until approximately time step 2200, before eventual extinction.

This pattern reflects intense hunting pressure from the initial cohort of 20 human foragers operating under dynamic optimal foraging theory, with immortal hunters and food storage capabilities ensuring sustained predation pressure throughout the simulation period. The variation in extinction timing illustrates demographic stochasticity inherent in agent-based systems, where chance events in hunting success and reproduction allow some populations to persist longer than others before stochastic extinction inevitably occurs. Mortality occurs discretely as individual agents are removed from the system, resulting in abrupt reductions in population counts.
