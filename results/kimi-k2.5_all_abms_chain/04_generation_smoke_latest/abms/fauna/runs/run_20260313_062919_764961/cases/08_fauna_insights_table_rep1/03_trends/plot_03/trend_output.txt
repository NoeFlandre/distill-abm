**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The decelerating accumulation pattern during the initial phase suggests that hunting pressure on male megaherbivores is self-limiting through prey depletion rather than sustainable harvest, indicating that male-selective hunting strategies rapidly exhaust target populations. The prolonged plateau periods demonstrate that male populations can persist at low densities under chronic hunting pressure, creating a "rare but persistent" state that may mask imminent extinction risk in monitoring programs. The 63-tick seasonal oscillation implies that conservation interventions targeting male protection would achieve maximum efficacy if synchronized with seasonal lows in hunting activity. Finally, the terminal decline in aggregated cumulative metrics highlights the stochastic nature of extinction trajectories, underscoring the necessity of ensemble-based risk assessments rather than single-simulation projections when evaluating megaherbivore viability under hunting pressure.
