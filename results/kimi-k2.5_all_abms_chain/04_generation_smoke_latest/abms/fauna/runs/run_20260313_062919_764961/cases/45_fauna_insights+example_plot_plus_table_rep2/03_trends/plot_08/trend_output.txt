The male megaherbivore population initiates at 125 individuals and undergoes a pronounced decline throughout the simulation, ultimately reaching extinction. During the first 500 time steps (approximately 1.4 years), the population experiences a steep collapse, plummeting from 125 to roughly 10 individuals. This rapid reduction reflects intense hunting pressure from the forager population operating under optimal foraging constraints, overwhelming the species' reproductive capacity despite the two-year birth-spacing interval.

Between time steps 500 and 1000, the decline continues at a moderated pace, with the population fluctuating between approximately 2 and 11 individuals. Small peaks emerge around time step 730 (reaching about 10.5 individuals) and time step 803 (approximately 6.8 individuals), likely corresponding to recruitment pulses from births occurring at the 730-tick birth-spacing interval. However, these reproductive events prove insufficient to counteract sustained hunting mortality, and the downward trajectory persists.

After time step 1000, the population enters a terminal phase, dropping below 5 individuals and continuing to erode toward zero. The final extirpation occurs across all simulation runs by approximately time step 2284, after which no males remain in the system.

Statistical analysis reveals a significant monotonic decreasing trend for the first 1159 time steps, with the steepest rate of decline occurring between time steps 232 and 463. A brief, weak increasing trend appears between time steps 1392 and 1623, suggesting a temporary, marginal recovery attempt, but this quickly reverses to a stable near-zero state after time step 1856. Oscillations with a period of approximately 63 time steps (bi-monthly given the annual cycle of 365 ticks) persist throughout the decline, indicating that seasonal environmental variations continue to affect demographic dynamics even as the population approaches extinction.

For decision makers, these results indicate that the current hunting parameters create an extinction vortex that eliminates male megaherbivores within approximately six to seven years. The inability of the population to recover during recruitment pulses suggests that hunting pressure consistently exceeds the maximum sustainable yield throughout the simulation period. To prevent extinction, management strategies must significantly reduce forager density, alter the cost-benefit structure of hunting males relative to females, or implement restrictions during critical breeding periods to allow population recovery.
