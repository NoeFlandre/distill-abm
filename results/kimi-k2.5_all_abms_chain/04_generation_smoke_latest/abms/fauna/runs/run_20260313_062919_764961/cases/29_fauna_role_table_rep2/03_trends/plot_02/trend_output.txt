The aggregate energy dynamics of the forager population exhibit a pronounced monotonic decline over the simulation duration, transitioning from an initial surplus of 2000 energy units to a terminal deficit of approximately -38229 units. This trajectory indicates a systematic erosion of hunter energetic status despite the absence of mortality from starvation, reflecting a landscape undergoing progressive prey depletion under sustained hunting pressure.

The decline occurs in distinct phases characterized by varying rates of energetic loss. During the initial approximately 650 ticks, the forager bands experience a moderate decrease averaging between -8.8 and -11.1 units per tick. Subsequently, the system undergoes a marked acceleration in energy loss around tick 650, with slopes intensifying to -15.7 units per tick, followed by a further escalation at tick 1075 to rates exceeding -19.5 units per tick. Between ticks 1160 and 1855, the decline stabilizes at its maximum velocity of approximately -20.0 units per tick, representing the phase of most rapid energetic deterioration. A final change point at tick 1925 precedes the asymptotic approach to the minimum value near tick 2310.

Superimposed upon this secular decline, regular oscillations with a dominant period of 63 ticks permeate the entire series. These cycles likely correspond to seasonal pulses in resource availability (consistent with the high seasonality parameter of 0.8) or rhythmic variations in hunting success as foragers track prey across the grassland matrix. The persistence of these oscillations even as total energy plummets suggests that hunters continue to exploit ephemeral opportunities despite the collapse of baseline prey populations.

Within the context of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model, this pattern illustrates the consequences of immortal forager agents operating under optimal foraging theory in a closed system. The initial positive energy balance reflects successful exploitation of the founding ungulate population (n=250), while the subsequent nosedive indicates the crossing of critical thresholds in prey density where search costs overwhelm nutritional returns. The terminal value of -38229 represents an extreme energetic debt accumulated because hunters cannot die yet face vanishingly low encounter rates with prey, forcing them to expend movement costs (1 unit per patch) without compensatory gains. The stabilization of decline rates in later phases suggests the system reaches a quasi-equilibrium of extreme scarcity, where the combination of rapid grass regrowth (3 ticks) and residual prey movements permits sporadic foraging success sufficient to slightly buffer the rate of energetic freefall, but insufficient to arrest the overall trajectory of collapse.
