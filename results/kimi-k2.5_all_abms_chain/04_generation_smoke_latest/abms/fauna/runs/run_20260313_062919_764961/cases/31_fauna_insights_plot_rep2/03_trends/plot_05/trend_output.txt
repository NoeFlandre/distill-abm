All simulation runs demonstrate a monotonic increase in the cumulative count of postpartum females killed, beginning from zero and eventually plateauing at asymptotic values ranging approximately between 105 and 125 individuals. During the initial phase, roughly spanning the first 500 time steps, all trajectories exhibit rapid growth, though with substantial inter-run variation in accumulation rates; some runs accumulate over 100 kills within this period while others reach only 60 to 80. Between approximately 500 and 1000 steps, most trajectories begin decelerating as they approach their respective plateaus, with numerous runs stabilizing completely within this interval at values typically between 100 and 115. In the extended phase beyond 1000 steps, a subset of runs continues gradual accumulation, eventually plateauing at later time points extending up to approximately 2200 steps; these late-plateauing runs frequently achieve higher final counts, often between 115 and 125. The final asymptotic values demonstrate considerable variation across the ensemble, with the lowest plateau observed near 105 and the highest near 125, while the majority cluster between 110 and 120. These plateaus indicate that the hunting process eventually exhausts the available supply of postpartum females within each simulation run. The observed variation in the timing of saturation and the final cumulative counts reflects inherent stochasticity in the model, including spatial heterogeneity in herd distribution, probabilistic foraging success, and demographic variation in herd reproduction and mortality schedules.
