The cumulative mortality of the prey population follows a sigmoid trajectory characterized by three distinct phases: rapid initial accumulation, progressive deceleration, and ultimate stabilization. Beginning from zero deaths at the start of the simulation, the metric exhibits sharp acceleration during the first 500 steps, punctuated by change points at regular 125-step intervals (125, 250, 375, 500, 625) and a major inflection around step 362. During this initial phase, cumulative deaths increase rapidly, typically reaching between 200 and 230 individuals as hunting pressure from the forager population establishes and encounters between hunters and prey herds intensify.

Between approximately step 500 and step 1200, the rate of mortality accumulation decelerates substantially. While the trend remains significantly increasing through step 1159, the slope diminishes considerably after step 850, with statistical windows between steps 1160-1391 showing no clear trend as the system approaches saturation. During this transitional period, cumulative deaths continue to rise but at a markedly reduced rate, climbing from approximately 220 toward the upper 230s.

Beyond step 1200, the mortality curve stabilizes into a plateau, with cumulative deaths asymptoting at values between 233 and 240 individuals across the ensemble of simulations. The median terminal value stands at 233 deaths, while the aggregate mean across all time steps is approximately 200.8, reflecting the heavy weighting toward earlier time points before the plateau is achieved. The maximum recorded cumulative death count reaches approximately 240.3 individuals around step 1876, representing roughly 96% of the initial prey population of 250 individuals.

Considerable stochastic variation exists in the timing of mortality saturation across the 40 simulation replicates. While the aggregate data extends to step 2312, individual runs exhibit plateaus commencing anywhere from step 1000 to step 2000, indicating variable extinction dynamics despite consistent final outcomes. This variability suggests that while the combination of immortal foragers, high environmental seasonality (0.8), and moderate grass availability (0.33) reliably drives the megaherbivore population toward near-total collapse, the precise timing of final mortality events depends on stochastic spatial interactions between hunters and the diminishing prey herds. The survival of approximately 10-15 individuals from the initial population of 250 across most simulations implies either the formation of small population refugia or simulation termination prior to the location of the final surviving herd members.
