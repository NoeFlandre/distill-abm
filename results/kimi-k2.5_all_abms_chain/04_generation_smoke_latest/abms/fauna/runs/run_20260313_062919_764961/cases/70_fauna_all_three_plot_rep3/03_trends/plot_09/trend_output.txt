The number of female animals initiates at approximately 125 individuals, representing the starting demography of the ungulate population. This initial count remains stable only for the earliest time steps before entering a phase of rapid decline.

During the interval from step 0 to step 500, the population undergoes a precipitous collapse across all simulation runs. By step 200, counts have typically fallen to between 60 and 100 females, with substantial variation emerging between runs. By step 400, the majority of populations have declined to fewer than 40 individuals, and by step 500, most counts range between 0 and 20 females. This steep reduction indicates that the initial encounter rates between human hunters and prey, combined with environmental pressures from high seasonality and limited vegetation coverage, generate mortality rates that far exceed the reproductive capacity of the herds. The reproductive interval of 730 ticks cannot offset the losses sustained during this initial phase, particularly given the presence of 20 immortal foragers with food storage capabilities.

From step 500 to step 1000, the decline continues though at decelerating rates for some runs while others proceed rapidly to extinction. By step 750, most populations have diminished to fewer than 10 females, with many runs recording zero individuals. However, several simulations maintain small remnant populations of 5 to 15 individuals that persist through this middle period, showing temporary plateaus in the decline curves before eventual collapse. These plateaus likely reflect brief periods where small herds avoid detection by hunters or exploit localized resource patches, but they do not indicate recovery or demographic stability.

Beyond step 1000, the trend converges toward universal extinction. By step 1500, the vast majority of simulation runs record zero female animals, with only isolated exceptions maintaining 1 to 3 individuals. The final extinction events occur between step 1500 and step 2200, at which point all remaining populations cease to exist. The extended duration required for complete extinction in some runs, compared to the rapid collapse in others, demonstrates high sensitivity to stochastic demographic and spatial factors when population sizes become critically small.

The variance across simulation runs increases significantly during the middle phase (steps 300-800), where extinction trajectories diverge most dramatically. Some populations collapse completely by step 400, while others maintain 20 or more individuals until step 600-700. This variability underscores the role of chance events in determining the timing of local extinction, even when the ultimate outcome of population collapse is consistent across all runs.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that the current model configuration produces deterministic extinction of the female megafauna population within approximately 2000 time steps, with median extinction occurring much earlier (around step 500-800). The absence of any stable low-density equilibrium suggests that small population management or refugia creation would be insufficient to prevent collapse under the existing hunting pressure and environmental parameters. Interventions would need to address either the hunting mortality rate (currently driven by immortal foragers with food storage capabilities) or significantly enhance the reproductive capacity and survival rates of the prey population to alter the fundamentally unsustainable trajectory observed across all stochastic replicates.
