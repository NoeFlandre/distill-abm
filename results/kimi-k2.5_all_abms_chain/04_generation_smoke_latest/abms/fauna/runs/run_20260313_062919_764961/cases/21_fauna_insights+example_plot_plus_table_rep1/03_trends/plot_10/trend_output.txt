**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The simulation outcomes demonstrate that a forager population of 20 individuals generates sufficient hunting pressure to drive megaherbivore populations to extinction within approximately 6.3 years (2,310 ticks), despite environmental conditions supporting grass regrowth and prey species having 20-year lifespans. The steep initial collapse indicates that hunting pressure is highly effective when prey densities are moderate, while the oscillatory late-phase dynamics and eventual extinction suggest that low-density populations cannot recover due to demographic constraints and persistent predation. The 63-tick oscillation cycle (approximately bi-monthly) likely reflects the interaction between seasonal resource availability and breeding intervals. These results indicate that moderate human hunting populations can precipitate megaherbivore extinction even without environmental degradation, highlighting the vulnerability of slow-reproducing large ungulates to sustained predation pressure. Conservation strategies should prioritize reducing hunter density below critical thresholds or implementing seasonal hunting moratoriums to prevent irreversible population collapse.
