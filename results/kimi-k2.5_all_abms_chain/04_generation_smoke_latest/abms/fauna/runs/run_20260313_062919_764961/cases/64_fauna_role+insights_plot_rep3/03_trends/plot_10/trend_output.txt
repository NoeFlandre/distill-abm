Across all simulation replicates, megaherbivore populations consistently initiated at 250 individuals and exhibited deterministic trajectories toward extinction. During the initial phase spanning approximately the first 500 time steps, populations underwent rapid collapse, declining from 250 to intermediate levels ranging between 0 and 80 animals, with the majority falling below 100 individuals. This phase was characterized by steep negative slopes indicating mortality rates substantially exceeding reproductive recruitment, driven by the energetic demands of 20 human foragers operating under optimal foraging strategies.

Following the initial collapse, populations entered a variable persistence phase between steps 500 and 1000, during which abundance continued to decrease but at decelerating rates. Remaining groups typically declined from 50-80 individuals to fewer than 20 animals. Heterogeneity among simulation runs became most pronounced during this interval, with stochastic demographic processes causing some populations to reach zero by step 400 while others maintained small groups of 10-30 animals until approximately steps 800-1000.

The terminal extinction phase occurred between steps 1000 and 2100, during which the remaining small populations succumbed to the combined pressures of continued hunting, demographic stochasticity, and Allee effects. No simulation achieved stable coexistence or population recovery; all trajectories terminated at or approaching zero animals. The latest extinctions occurred around step 2100, representing approximately 5.7 years of daily time steps, while the earliest collapses completed within the first year.

These patterns reflect the demographic consequences of persistent hunting pressure from immortal foragers with energy storage capabilities, targeting populations with a two-year interbirth interval (730 ticks) in environments with high seasonality (0.8) and moderate vegetation regrowth (3 ticks). The consistent extinction outcome across stochastic replicates indicates that mortality from hunting, compounded by seasonal resource bottlenecks and the species' slow life history traits (10,220 tick maximum lifespan, 7,300 tick senescence onset), exceeded maximum sustainable yield thresholds.

**Implications for Decision Makers**  
The absence of sustainable equilibria suggests that hunting pressure from as few as 20 efficient foragers constitutes a deterministic extinction threat for megaherbivore populations when combined with seasonal environmental variability and slow reproductive rates. Conservation interventions must prioritize reducing hunter efficiency or access well before populations decline below 100 individuals, as the data indicate demographic momentum prevents recovery once populations enter terminal decline phases below 50 animals. Given the two-year interbirth interval and high energetic investment in offspring, management should either maintain hunter densities substantially below this threshold or implement seasonal restrictions that eliminate hunting during critical reproductive periods when resource scarcity and predation pressures compound. The model results further suggest that allowing hunter numbers to reach 20 individuals in landscapes supporting only 250 megaherbivores results in inevitable extinction within approximately 6 years, providing a clear quantitative boundary for sustainable harvest management.
