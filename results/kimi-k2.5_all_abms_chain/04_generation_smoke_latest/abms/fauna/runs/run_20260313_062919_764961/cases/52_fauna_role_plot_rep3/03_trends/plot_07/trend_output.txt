Across the ensemble of simulation runs, cumulative prey mortality exhibits sigmoid growth trajectories characterized by three phases: rapid initial accumulation, progressive deceleration, and ultimate stabilization. During the initial interval spanning approximately steps 0-500, death counts increase steeply, with most trajectories accumulating 150-230 deaths by step 500.

The rate of mortality accumulation decelerates markedly after step 500-800, as trajectories bend toward horizontal asymptotes. Final cumulative death counts converge within the range of approximately 225 to 250 individuals, consistent with near-total mortality of the initial population of 250 prey.

Substantial stochastic variation characterizes the temporal dynamics of population depletion. The most rapid trajectories achieve asymptotic mortality levels of 240-250 deaths by steps 600-800, while progressively slower trajectories continue accumulating deaths through steps 1000-1200 (reaching 230-245 deaths) and beyond. The slowest realizations extend the mortality accumulation phase to approximately steps 1500-2100, with several trajectories plateauing at values between 225 and 240. One notable realization exhibits markedly delayed early mortality, accumulating fewer than 50 deaths by step 300 compared to 80-170 in contemporaneous runs, before accelerating to reach an asymptote of approximately 235 deaths near step 1000.

The universal convergence to plateau values approximating the initial population size, combined with the cessation of mortality accumulation despite continued simulation runtime, indicates that the prey population is driven to effective depletion across all stochastic realizations. The extended persistence of low-level mortality in slower trajectories (continuing through steps 1500-2100) suggests that small numbers of prey individuals survive for extended periods due to stochastic variation in spatial distribution or energetic states before eventually succumbing to hunting or natural mortality. The presence of 20 immortal foragers with food storage capability, operating under high seasonality (0.8) and rapid vegetation regrowth conditions, generates sufficient hunting pressure to overwhelm the megaherbivore population's reproductive capacity (governed by 730-tick interbirth intervals), resulting in population collapse within 3-6 years across all runs.
