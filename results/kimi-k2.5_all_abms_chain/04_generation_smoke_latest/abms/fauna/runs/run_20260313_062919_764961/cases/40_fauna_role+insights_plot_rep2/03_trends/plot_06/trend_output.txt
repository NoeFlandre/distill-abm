Across the replicate simulations, cumulative births remain at zero for an initial latent period of approximately 730 steps, consistent with the parameterized interbirth interval of 730 ticks. This quiescence reflects the biological constraint that females in the initial cohort of 250 animals require approximately two years to produce their first offspring.

Following the onset of reproduction around step 730, birth events occur sporadically, generating stepwise increases in the cumulative count. The trajectories exhibit substantial heterogeneity in both magnitude and duration. Final cumulative birth tallies range from a minimum of 1 birth to a maximum of 10 births. The highest value of 10 is achieved by step 1500 in one replicate, while other simulations terminate with 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, or 8 births.

Temporal persistence varies considerably among replicates. Several populations cease reproduction and collapse within 300-400 steps after the first births (terminating between steps 1000-1100), accumulating only 1-2 births. Others demonstrate moderate persistence, accumulating 5-8 births before collapsing between steps 1400-1700. One replicate maintains reproductive activity through step 2000, achieving 6 cumulative births.

The stepwise accumulation pattern indicates discrete reproductive events consistent with the birth-spacing interval, while the early termination of accumulation in most simulations suggests rapid population collapse. The maximum reproductive output of 10 births from an initial population of 250 animals over approximately 5.5 years represents an extremely low reproductive rate of roughly 0.8% population growth through reproduction, indicating that mortality pressures severely constrain recruitment.

**Implications for decision makers:** The high variability in outcomes suggests that population persistence is highly sensitive to stochastic demographic events. Even with identical initial conditions of 250 animals, outcomes range from immediate collapse to modest persistence. The consistent 730-tick delay before any recruitment occurs creates a critical vulnerability window where populations face extinction before producing offspring. Management strategies should prioritize protecting reproductive females during the initial two-year period, as populations that fail to produce sufficient births in this window face near-certain collapse. The low maximum birth count (10) relative to the initial cohort size indicates that current parameter settings create an unsustainable system where extinction is the probable outcome.
