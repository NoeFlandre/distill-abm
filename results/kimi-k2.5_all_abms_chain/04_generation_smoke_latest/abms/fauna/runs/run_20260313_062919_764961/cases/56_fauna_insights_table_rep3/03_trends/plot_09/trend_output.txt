The population of female ungulates initiated at 125 individuals and underwent complete extinction over approximately 6.3 years (2,312 ticks). The trajectory exhibited a general pattern of precipitous decline characterized by distinct phases of varying intensities, intermittent stabilization attempts, and periodic fluctuations superimposed upon a terminal trajectory toward zero.

Initially, the population experienced a steep collapse during the first 1.3 years (0-475 ticks), with high rates of decrease observed through the first two years. By approximately the second year (710-730 ticks), the population had already crashed to minimal levels, fluctuating around 5-6 individuals, representing a 95% reduction from initial conditions. This early rapid collapse suggests that hunting pressure from the initial cohort of 20 foragers, combined with environmental constraints (limited grass availability with high seasonality and rapid regrowth cycles), overwhelmed the species' reproductive capacity, exacerbated by the 2-year interbirth interval that limited population recovery potential.

Following this initial crash, the dynamics entered a prolonged period of volatility at critically low densities between years 2 and 3.5 (ticks 730-1,300), characterized by alternating slight recoveries and continued declines. Notable inflection points in the rate of change occurred at approximately 10 months, 2 years, and 2.3 years (ticks 305, 740, and 833), indicating shifts in the factors driving population change—possibly reflecting hunter behavioral adjustments based on optimal foraging theory, seasonal resource availability affecting both prey condition and hunter success, or demographic stochasticity as the population entered an Allee effect regime where mate-finding became difficult.

Between years 3.2 and 3.8 (ticks 1,160-1,391), a statistically significant temporary increase occurred, marking the only substantial recovery attempt during the observation period. However, this resurgence proved insufficient to overcome the population's compromised demographic structure, and the trend reversed into decline between years 3.8 and 4.5 (ticks 1,392-1,623). A second minor resurgence appeared between years 5.1 and 5.7 (ticks 1,856-2,087), but like the previous attempt, failed to establish sustained growth, likely due to the continued presence of hunting pressure and the inability of the small remaining population to achieve positive growth rates given life history constraints.

Throughout the entire trajectory, oscillations with a dominant periodicity of approximately 63 days (two months) persisted, likely reflecting the strong seasonality parameter affecting grass regrowth and consequently herbivore movement patterns, energy status, and hunter encounter rates. These oscillations became more pronounced relative to population size as densities approached zero, indicating intense pressure on the remaining few individuals.

The final extinction event occurred at approximately 6.3 years (tick 2,310), with the last individuals disappearing after a final detectable change point at roughly year 6 (tick 2,194). The population spent the majority of the observation period at or below 2 individuals, with a median abundance of 1 across the entire simulation timeframe.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The rapid collapse to near-zero levels within the first two years, followed by a prolonged but ultimately unsuccessful struggle against extinction over the subsequent four years, indicates that hunting pressure at the specified density constitutes an unsustainable harvest rate for this megaherbivore life history strategy. The brief recovery phases between years 3-4 and 5-6 suggest that temporary reductions in hunting pressure or favorable environmental windows cannot rescue populations once they have declined below critical demographic thresholds, particularly given the species' extended 2-year birth spacing and the high processing efficiency for females relative to males. Conservation or management strategies must prioritize maintaining populations above critical density thresholds—likely well above the 5% of initial capacity observed here—rather than relying on seasonal reprieve or temporary hunting moratoria, as recovery potential appears severely limited once populations fall into the single-digit range. The persistent two-month cyclical fluctuations suggest that while seasonal resource management influences population dynamics, it cannot offset continuous hunting pressure without addressing the fundamental imbalance between harvest rates and the species' slow reproductive rate.
