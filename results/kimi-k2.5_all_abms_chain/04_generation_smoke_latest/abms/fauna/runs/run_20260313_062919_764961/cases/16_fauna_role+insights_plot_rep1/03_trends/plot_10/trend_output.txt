The simulation ensemble exhibits consistent population collapse dynamics across all replicates, with deterministic extinction trajectories despite stochastic variation in the rate of decline. Beginning with initial populations of 250 animals, all experimental runs demonstrate monotonic decreases in total animal numbers over time.

**Phase 1: Rapid Decline (Steps 0-500)**
During the initial 500 time steps, populations undergo severe reductions ranging from 80% to 100% of initial stock. The fastest collapsing trajectories fall below 10 individuals by approximately step 400, with some reaching effective extinction (0-5 animals) within this window. More gradual decline trajectories maintain populations between 50-100 animals at step 500, representing 60-80% depletion. The heterogeneity in decline rates during this phase is substantial: at step 200, remaining population sizes range from approximately 150 to 225 animals; by step 400, this range expands from near-extinction (0-10) to approximately 75 animals.

**Phase 2: Terminal Decline (Steps 500-1000)**
The second phase is characterized by continued attrition of remaining populations. By step 800, over 90% of simulation runs have fallen below 25 animals. The most persistent populations during this phase maintain 20-40 animals through step 600-700 before entering terminal decline. By step 1000, the vast majority of trajectories (approximately 95%) have reached populations of 10 or fewer animals.

**Phase 3: Extinction and Near-Extinction (Steps 1000-2200)**
In the final phase, remaining populations converge on extinction. A small subset of trajectories (approximately 5-10%) exhibits prolonged persistence with very small populations (2-15 animals) extending to steps 1200-1500, with isolated cases maintaining 1-5 animals until approximately step 2000-2200. Ultimately, 100% of simulation runs reach effective extinction (0-3 animals) by the termination of the observation period.

**Implications for Decision Makers**
The complete absence of stable coexistence or recovery trajectories across all replicates indicates that the parameter configuration (20 human hunters, 250 initial prey, high seasonality of 0.8) creates structurally unsustainable predation pressure. The variation in collapse timing (400-2200 steps) suggests that while population extinction is inevitable under these conditions, the precise timeline is subject to demographic stochasticity. This implies that conservation or management interventions would need to address the fundamental mortality rate rather than manage for population stability. The rapid initial decline rate (median population < 50 by step 500) indicates that delayed intervention would be ineffective, as populations lose over 75% of their biomass before reaching one-quarter of the simulation duration.
