The approximately 63-tick oscillation cycle superimposed upon the decline likely reflects seasonal hunting patterns interacting with environmental seasonality, or possibly cohort maturation pulses synchronized with the species' biennial birth spacing interval (730 ticks). The temporary recovery during phase three may indicate a window of reduced hunting efficiency, stochastic demographic escape, or the maturation of subsequent cohorts temporarily offsetting mortality. However, the sustained low median population level of approximately 1.3 individuals across the full temporal sequence indicates that for the majority of the simulation, the male population existed in a critically endangered state vulnerable to Allee effects and stochastic extinction. **Implications for Decision Makers**

The trajectory demonstrates that moderate hunting pressure (20 foragers) applied selectively to higher-value males can drive a sexually dimorphic population to extinction within approximately six to seven years, despite species-level life history characteristics (28-year potential lifespan, biennial reproduction) that might otherwise suggest demographic resilience. The presence of temporary recovery phases during population collapse creates a critical management risk: brief increases in census counts may generate false signals of population viability or recovery potential, potentially delaying necessary intervention. The persistent 63-tick oscillation pattern throughout decline suggests that seasonal hunting regulations or environmental bottlenecks create predictable pulses of mortality that compound sex-selective pressure. Wildlife managers should recognize that male-biased harvesting regimes require extremely conservative quotas and continuous monitoring, as reproductive collapse can proceed rapidly even when total population counts remain above critical thresholds, and that oscillatory patterns in census data during decline phases should not be interpreted as stable population fluctuations but rather as the final demographic spasms of a collapsing subpopulation.
