The combined energetic reserves of female ungulate populations initiated at 12,500 units and underwent a deterministic collapse to extinction by tick 2,311 (approximately 6.3 years), exhibiting complex non-linear dynamics throughout the trajectory. 

The initial phase (ticks 0–463) displayed precipitous decline characterized by steep negative slopes exceeding 22 units per tick, during which aggregate energy plummeted despite transient oscillatory peaks reaching approximately 2,230 units around tick 376. These early fluctuations manifested with a dominant periodicity of 63 ticks (roughly bimonthly cycles), superimposing seasonal rhythms upon the underlying collapse trajectory. 

Between ticks 464 and 1,159, the rate of decline attenuated substantially to gentler slopes ranging from 5.7 down to 0.56 units per tick, suggesting temporary equilibration between energetic gains from foraging and losses from mortality. However, this moderation proved deceptive, as the trend remained significantly negative throughout.

A brief demographic recovery emerged between ticks 1,160 and 1,391, wherein aggregate female energy exhibited a weak but statistically significant increasing trend. This respite was short-lived; subsequent intervals (ticks 1,392–1,855) renewed the declining trajectory with accelerating intensity.

Notably, a second recovery period occurred between ticks 1,856 and 2,087, displaying the strongest positive trend observed in the entire series with a pronounced upward slope. Despite this temporary accumulation of energetic reserves, the population entered a terminal collapse phase after tick 2,087, declining rapidly to zero by tick 2,311. Change points detected at ticks 375, 500, 625, and 875 marked discrete shifts in decline velocity, while the final change point at tick 2,310 denoted the extinction threshold.

**Implications for Decision Makers:**

The data indicate that female megaherbivore populations face existential risk within approximately six years under sustained hunting pressure, even when environmental productivity is high (rapid grass regrowth) and seasonality creates regular resource pulses. The bimonthly oscillation cycles suggest that population monitoring must account for seasonal energetic bottlenecks that could mask underlying decline. Critically, intermittent recovery periods—observed here at years 3 and 5—may generate false perceptions of population resilience, potentially delaying conservation interventions until irrecoverable thresholds are crossed. The vulnerability of females appears heightened by their lower processing costs (3 units versus 6 for males), rendering them persistently profitable targets despite marginally lower energetic returns (27 versus 30 units). Management protocols should prioritize protecting female cohorts during identified recovery intervals to prevent the terminal collapses observed in later simulation stages.
