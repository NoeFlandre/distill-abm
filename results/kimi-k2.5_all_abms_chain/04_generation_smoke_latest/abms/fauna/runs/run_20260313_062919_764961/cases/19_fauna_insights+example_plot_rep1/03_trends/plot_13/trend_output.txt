The total energy of all animals starts at approximately 25,000 units across all simulation runs. It declines rapidly over the first 500 time steps, dropping to values between 5,000 and 8,000 for most runs, with some falling near zero. This initial decline can be attributed to the immediate impact of hunting pressure combined with natural mortality and the inability of prey populations to reproduce quickly enough to offset losses. Between 500 and 1,000 steps, the decline continues but at a significantly slower rate, with substantial variance across simulation runs; some populations persist with fluctuating energy levels between 2,000 and 8,000, while others collapse completely. This variability likely reflects stochastic elements in animal movement, reproduction timing, and hunting success. From 1,000 to 2,000 steps, the remaining energy reserves continue to deplete, approaching zero in most runs by approximately 1,500 steps, though a few simulations show minimal persistence extending toward 2,200 steps. The simulation ends with total energy near zero across all runs, indicating inevitable extinction of the prey populations under the current parameter configuration.

The implications for decision makers suggest that with the current hunting pressure, prey life history traits, and environmental conditions, megafauna extinction is unavoidable without intervention. The rapid initial decline indicates that conservation measures would need to be implemented extremely early in the process—within the first 500 time steps—to have any potential to alter the trajectory, as delayed action faces a system already in terminal decline with minimal remaining biomass to sustain recovery.
