**Implications for Decision Makers**

The rapid initial depletion within the first 350 ticks (approximately the first year of simulation time) indicates that intervention thresholds must be established and triggered almost immediately upon detection of hunting activity to prevent irreversible biomass loss. The mid-series recovery phase demonstrates that male populations retain biological capacity for rebound even after substantial decline, suggesting that temporary hunting moratoria or strict male harvest quotas implemented before tick 1,400 could potentially restore population viability. However, the ultimate extinction outcome despite this resilience implies that sustained hunting pressure—even with constant hunter numbers and theoretically optimal foraging behavior—creates an extinction debt that manifests after approximately 6.25 years (2,284 ticks), rendering short-term recovery indicators unreliable for assessing long-term sustainability. Management protocols must account for the persistent 63-tick cyclical fluctuations to distinguish true population trends from seasonal or demographic noise, particularly when biomass falls below 1,000 units where stochastic variation risks masking the irreversible slide toward extinction.
