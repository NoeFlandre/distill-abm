The cumulative number of births across the prey population remains at zero for an extended initial period lasting approximately 750 time steps. This prolonged reproductive quiescence corresponds to the interbirth interval parameter of 730 ticks, indicating that the initial cohort of females requires this duration to complete their first reproductive cycle after simulation onset.

At approximately step 750, a synchronized reproductive event occurs across all simulation runs, with cumulative birth counts instantaneously jumping to values ranging between 2 and 8 offspring. This synchronized pulse reflects the initial reproductive output of the founding population of 250 animals, after which trajectories diverge considerably across runs.

Following this initial birth pulse, the various simulation runs exhibit highly variable reproductive trajectories. Several runs plateau at low cumulative birth counts—specifically at 2, 3, 4, and 5 births—for the remainder of the simulation period, suggesting that breeding females in these runs failed to produce subsequent offspring. One run reaches exactly 8 births at step 750 and maintains this count through approximately step 1400. Another trajectory demonstrates step-wise progression from 5 births around step 750, to 6 births near step 1000, 9 births around step 1100, and finally reaching the maximum observed value of 10 births by approximately step 1500, where it stabilizes.

The most successful run achieves only 10 total births over approximately 2000 time steps (equivalent to roughly 5.5 years), despite beginning with a founder population of 250 animals. This remarkably low reproductive output, combined with the high variance across runs—where cumulative births range from as few as 1 to as many as 10 by the end of the observation period—indicates substantial mortality pressure on the breeding population. The stochastic variation in subsequent birth events after the initial pulse suggests that which specific females survive hunting pressure to produce second or third litters is highly variable and unpredictable, with many runs experiencing effective reproductive failure after the initial birth cohort. The step function nature of the curves, with long plateaus punctuated by sudden increases, reflects the discrete reproductive events of individual females following their interbirth intervals, moderated by high mortality rates that prevent most females from contributing multiple times to the cumulative birth count.
