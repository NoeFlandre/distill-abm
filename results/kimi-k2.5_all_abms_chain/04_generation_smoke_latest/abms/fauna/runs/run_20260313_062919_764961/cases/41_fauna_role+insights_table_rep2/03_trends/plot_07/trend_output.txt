The cumulative mortality of megaherbivore populations exhibits a characteristic trajectory marked by rapid initial accumulation followed by deceleration and eventual plateau. Beginning from zero at the simulation onset, mortality accumulates steeply through the first 850 time steps, with the rate of increase gradually diminishing but remaining strongly positive. This early phase is punctuated by inflection points at approximately 125, 250, 375, 500, 625, and 850 ticks, indicating successive shifts in the accumulation dynamics as the population responds to initial hunting pressure and environmental constraints.

Between ticks 850 and approximately 1855, the accumulation of deaths transitions to a more variable regime. The trend exhibits periods of stability interspersed with modest increases, including a notable plateau between ticks 1160 and 1391 where the cumulative count remains relatively static, followed by a slight resumption of increase. This middle phase suggests a temporary equilibrium where mortality rates stabilize, possibly reflecting reduced hunting efficiency or population adjustments to forager presence.

The cumulative mortality reaches its maximum value of approximately 240 individuals around tick 1876, after which the metric exhibits a declining trend through the remainder of the simulation, concluding at 235 deaths by tick 2312. This late-phase decrease in cumulative mortality indicates a cessation of new deaths sufficient to cause the running total to decline when averaged across simulation runs, implying population collapse or extreme rarity of surviving individuals that precludes further mortality events.

Superimposed upon these broad trends is a cyclical oscillation with a dominant period of approximately 63 ticks, particularly evident in the later stages of the simulation. This periodicity likely reflects the strong environmental seasonality driving pulsed resource availability and corresponding fluctuations in mortality risk throughout the annual cycle.

For decision makers, these results indicate that megaherbivore populations under moderate hunting pressure and high environmental seasonality face rapid initial mortality accumulation that quickly constrains population viability. The emergence of mortality oscillations suggests that intervention timing should account for seasonal windows when animals are most vulnerable. The ultimate plateau and decline of cumulative mortality signals that without management adjustments, the system progresses toward population collapse rather than sustainable coexistence, emphasizing the need for early regulatory action before tick 850 when mortality acceleration is most pronounced.
