In all simulation runs, total forager energy initiates at values between approximately 0 and +1,500 energy units. During the initial 500 steps, the metric declines rapidly with substantial variation across runs, ranging from approximately −3,000 to −6,000 energy units by step 500. Following this transient phase, the trajectories converge and exhibit a consistent linear decline from step 500 through step 2,000, decreasing at a rate of approximately 18 energy units per step. By step 1,000, total energy reaches approximately −12,000 to −13,000 units; by step 1,500, approximately −20,000 to −21,000 units; and by step 2,000, most runs terminate with values between −28,000 and −33,000. One simulation continues to approximately step 2,200, reaching −38,000 energy units.

The sustained negative trajectory indicates that forager energy expenditure consistently exceeds energy acquisition throughout the simulation. With forager immortality enabled, the population does not die off from energy depletion but instead accumulates increasing energy debt. The convergence of trajectories after step 500 suggests that stochastic variation in early hunting success gives way to a predictable steady-state energy drain rate determined by the balance between movement costs and hunting returns.

**Implications for Decision Makers:** The parameter configuration produces an unsustainable energetic regime where hunters cannot achieve energy balance. Decision makers should recognize that under these conditions, foragers face a structural deficit that would be fatal without the immortality override. To achieve viable hunting sustainability, adjustments are needed to increase prey encounter rates, reduce forager movement costs, increase prey food values, or decrease processing costs. The consistent linear decline rate after step 500 provides a reliable metric for predicting long-term energy debt accumulation, useful for calibrating extinction timelines or determining critical thresholds for forager population viability.
