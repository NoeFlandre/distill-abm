The data indicate a universal decline in female prey abundance across all simulation runs, initiating from approximately 125 individuals. The rate of population decrease varies substantially across stochastic realizations. The most rapid declines reach near-extinction levels (fewer than 5 individuals) by approximately step 400, while the most gradual declines maintain small populations of 1 to 5 individuals until beyond step 2000.

By step 500, the majority of simulations have reduced female populations to between 0 and 30 individuals. By step 1000, nearly all runs have declined to fewer than 10 females, with most populations at or approaching zero. The decline is monotonic across all runs, with no instances of population recovery or sustained equilibrium observed.

This pattern demonstrates that under the specified parameter configuration—including 20 initial human foragers, sexual dimorphism in food value and processing costs, and the defined life history parameters—the female megafaunal population undergoes deterministic collapse. The variation in extinction timing reflects sensitivity to stochastic processes including demographic fluctuations, spatial encounter rates between hunters and prey, and individual variation in energy management, though the ultimate outcome of population elimination remains invariant across runs.

For decision makers, these results indicate that the current hunting pressure and environmental parameters create conditions incompatible with megafaunal persistence. The rapid decline to extinction—typically occurring within 1000 steps (approximately 2.7 years given 365 ticks per year)—suggests that intervention strategies would require immediate implementation to prevent irreversible population collapse. The consistency of extinction across all stochastic realizations indicates that the system is not resilient to demographic or environmental stochasticity under current parameter settings, necessitating substantial reductions in hunting pressure or improvements in habitat quality to achieve viable population dynamics.
