The combined energy reserves of male megaherbivores initiated at 12,500 units and underwent a deterministic progression toward complete depletion by tick 2,284 (approximately 6.3 years), exhibiting complex transitional dynamics rather than monotonic decay. The trajectory divides into four principal phases with distinct energetic signatures.

The initial phase (ticks 0–350) exhibited the most precipitous decline, with aggregate male energy plummeting from 12,500 to approximately 4,000. Within this interval, a transient oscillation peaked at 11,608 units around tick 65 before resuming descent. The rate of depletion was steepest during the interval spanning ticks 232–463, characterized by a loss rate exceeding 28 units per tick, suggesting intense initial hunting pressure on the male subpopulation immediately following hunter initialization.

Following the initial collapse, the system entered an extended phase of gradual attrition (ticks 350–1,400) marked by pronounced oscillatory behavior superimposed on a diminishing trend. These fluctuations exhibited a dominant periodicity of 63 ticks, approximately corresponding to bimonthly cycles within the annual environmental regime (365 ticks per year), reflecting seasonal variation in resource availability modulated by the high seasonality parameter (0.8). During this interval, the rate of decline attenuated substantially, with losses slowing to less than one unit per tick.

A notable demographic rebound occurred between ticks 1,392 and 1,623, wherein aggregate male energy exhibited a statistically significant increasing trend (approximately 0.09 units per tick). This temporary recovery suggests a period of reduced hunting mortality or improved foraging success, possibly arising from density-dependent release or favorable seasonal resource pulses.

However, this stabilization proved ephemeral. After tick 1,624, the trend resumed decremental drift, ultimately reaching zero energy reserves at tick 2,284, indicating complete extirpation of the male cohort. The final phase showed minimal oscillation amplitude as the population approached the energetic threshold for viability termination.

Throughout the simulation, the distribution of male energy remained heavily right-skewed, with the median standing at 134.5 units—substantially below the temporal mean of 1,984—consistent with rapid initial depletion followed by prolonged low-energy persistence before ultimate collapse.
