The total number of prey animals starts at 250 and declines rapidly over the first 500 time steps. This steep decline indicates high initial predation pressure combined with natural mortality factors reducing the herd size quickly. By approximately 1000 time steps, the population levels off to a minimal range between 0 and 20 animals. This stabilization at near-zero levels signifies a functional extinction event within the simulation environment. There is significant variance in the rate of decline among the simulation runs, with some populations collapsing within 400 steps while others persist with small numbers until 1500 steps. These differences suggest that stochastic interactions between forager movements and prey density fluctuations play a critical role in determining the precise timing of population collapse. For decision makers, the rapid initial loss implies that protective measures would need to be implemented immediately to prevent irreversible population crashes, while the variance highlights the importance of managing environmental buffers to delay extinction under optimal conditions.
