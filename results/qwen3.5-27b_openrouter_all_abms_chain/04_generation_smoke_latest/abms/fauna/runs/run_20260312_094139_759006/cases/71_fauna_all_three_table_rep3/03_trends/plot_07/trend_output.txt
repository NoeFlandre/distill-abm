The cumulative mortality trajectory for the megaherbivore population initiates at zero and exhibits a sustained upward trend throughout the majority of the observation period. The metric reaches a peak mean value of approximately 240.33 deaths at tick 1,876. Given the initial prey population is set at 250, this value indicates a mortality rate exceeding 95% of the starting cohort. Following this peak, the aggregate mean stabilizes, ending at a value of 235.00 at the final recorded time step. The statistical variance across the 40 simulation runs is substantial, with a standard deviation of 65.03, implying significant divergence in outcomes between individual model runs despite the shared parameter configuration.

This periodicity may correspond to seasonal cycles or resource regeneration dynamics influencing late-stage mortality events. The transition from significant growth to no trend observed after tick 1,159 suggests that the surviving population becomes too sparse to sustain high encounter rates with foragers.

For decision makers, these trends highlight a high-risk scenario where the configured hunting pressure (20 foragers) combined with natural mortality leads to near-total elimination of the initial megaherbivore stock within approximately five years (1,876 ticks at 365 ticks per year). To prevent extinction, parameters governing forager density or hunting efficiency would likely require reduction to allow for population recovery and birth rates to outpace mortality.
