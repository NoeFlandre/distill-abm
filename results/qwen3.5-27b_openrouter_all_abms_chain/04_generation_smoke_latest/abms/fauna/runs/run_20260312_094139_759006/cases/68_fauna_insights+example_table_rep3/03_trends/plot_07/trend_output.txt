The cumulative number of deaths within the prey population initiates at zero and exhibits a distinct multi-phase trajectory over the simulation period. The metric rises sharply during the initial 1,159 time steps, a phase characterized by a strong, statistically significant increasing trend across the majority of rolling windows. This rapid accumulation indicates an initial surge in predation pressure from the forager population, consistent with a scenario where foragers actively hunt against a background of natural mortality.

Around time steps 1,241 and 1,330, distinct inflection points mark a modulation in the accumulation rate. Following this period, the curve approaches a peak average magnitude of approximately 240.33 at time step 1,876. Although the metric represents a cumulative total, statistical analysis identifies a period of no significant trend between steps 1,160 and 1,391, and a subsequent signal indicating a reduction in the mean value between steps 1,856 and 2,087. This suggests that the rate of new mortality events has effectively ceased or stabilized across the simulation runs as the accessible prey base is depleted.

Superimposed on this primary upward trend is a dominant oscillation with a period of approximately 63 steps. Given the model parameters set the ticks-per-year to 365 and seasonality to 0.8, these fluctuations likely correspond to sub-annual environmental cycles influencing forage availability and, consequently, forager movement and hunting success. The simulation concludes with a mean cumulative death count of 235.00, a value very close to the peak, indicating a high level of total mortality by the end of the run.

For decision-makers, the rapid initial slope signals a high vulnerability of the prey population to immediate hunting pressure. The eventual plateau suggests that the system naturally self-regulates through population depletion, reaching an equilibrium where further losses are negligible. This highlights the critical window at the start of the simulation as the most sensitive period for population collapse, implying that early intervention or management strategies would have the most significant impact on population survival.
