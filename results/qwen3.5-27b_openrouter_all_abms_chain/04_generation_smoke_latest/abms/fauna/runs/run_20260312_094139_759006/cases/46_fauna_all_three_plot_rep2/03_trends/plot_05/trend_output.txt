The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted begins at zero and increases rapidly over the first 400 time steps, reaching approximately 100 in most simulation runs. This sharp initial rise indicates an intense early phase of predation targeting females with offspring, likely driven by the high nutritional value associated with both adult females and their young. The rate of increase slows significantly between 400 and 800 steps as the curves approach an asymptote, stabilizing between 110 and 125. Variance among simulation runs is relatively low during the initial 200 steps but widens between 200 and 600 steps, suggesting that stochastic interactions between foragers and prey herds introduce variability in hunting outcomes once the initial population pressure is established. After 800 steps, the cumulative count remains largely static, indicating a depletion of accessible postpartum females or a behavioral shift by foragers toward other prey categories due to diminishing returns. The cessation of new kills implies a critical threshold where human hunting pressure exhausts the vulnerable segment of the herd, potentially signaling long-term instability for population recovery.
