The number of male prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model starts at an initial value of approximately 125. Over the first 500 time steps, this value declines rapidly, with most simulations showing a sharp decrease to around 20 male animals. This decline is primarily attributed to the combined effects of hunting pressure and natural mortality, as male prey are targeted due to their higher food value (30 units compared to 27 units for females). By 1,000 time steps, the number of male animals in most simulations has dropped to near zero, indicating a significant depletion of the male population. However, some simulations show a slight stabilization or minor recovery in the male population between 500 and 1,000 steps, likely due to variations in foraging behavior and herd dynamics. After 1,000 steps, the male population remains critically low across all simulations, with only a few runs showing a marginal presence of male animals. This trend suggests that under the current parameter settings, the model predicts a high risk of extinction for male megaherbivores, driven by intense hunting pressure and environmental constraints. Decision-makers should consider adjusting hunting strategies or prey protection mechanisms to prevent the complete loss of male animals, which could have cascading effects on herd reproduction and stability.
