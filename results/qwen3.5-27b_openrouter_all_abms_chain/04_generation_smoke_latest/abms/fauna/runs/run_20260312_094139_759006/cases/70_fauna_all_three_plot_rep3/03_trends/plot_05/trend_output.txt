The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted begins at zero and increases rapidly within the first 400 time steps, reaching approximately 100 in most simulation runs. This sharp initial rise indicates an intense early phase of predation targeting females with offspring, likely driven by the high energy value assigned to female prey (27.0) and the presence of calving events early in the simulation. Between 400 and 700 time steps, the rate of accumulation slows, with values stabilizing between 110 and 125 across the majority of runs. Some simulations exhibit minor increases up to 1200 time steps, but the overall trend plateaus by 1500 time steps. The final cumulative values range from 105 to 122, indicating that hunting pressure on postpartum females is concentrated in the early-to-mid phases of the simulation. The variability among runs, particularly between 500 and 1000 time steps, suggests stochastic elements in hunter-prey interactions or prey availability, which may affect the timing and intensity of hunting pressure. These trends imply that early intervention or regulation of hunting pressure could significantly mitigate impacts on reproductive female populations, which are critical for herd sustainability.
