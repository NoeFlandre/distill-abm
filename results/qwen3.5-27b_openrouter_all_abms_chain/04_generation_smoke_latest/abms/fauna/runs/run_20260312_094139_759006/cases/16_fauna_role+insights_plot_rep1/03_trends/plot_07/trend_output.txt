The cumulative number of prey deaths exhibits a rapid increase in the initial phase of the simulations, followed by a stabilization period. During the first 500 time steps, the total number of deaths rises sharply, reaching approximately 225 across multiple simulation runs. This steep incline suggests a high mortality rate early in the simulation, likely driven by predation and environmental stress on the prey population. After this initial surge, the rate of deaths slows significantly, and by around 1,000 time steps, the cumulative death count stabilizes within a narrow range between 230 and 250. This plateau indicates that the prey population has either reached a new equilibrium with mortality factors or has been reduced to a level where further significant decline is less pronounced. The convergence of death counts across different simulation runs implies a consistent outcome under the specified model parameters, highlighting the robust impact of the combined effects of foraging pressure and environmental constraints. For decision makers, this trend underscores the critical importance of the initial phase in managing prey populations, as the majority of mortality occurs rapidly after the introduction of human foragers and environmental challenges.
