The total reserve biomass across the grazing patches begins at 90,000 units and exhibits a downward trend during the early stages of the simulation. This decline persists until timestep 26, where the biomass reaches a minimum of 84,114 units. This initial reduction suggests that the initial livestock pressure exceeded the regeneration capacity of the grass, likely due to the starting livestock levels before farmers adjusted their herding strategies based on environmental feedback.

Following the minimum at timestep 26, the trajectory shifts toward recovery. Although the trend is not statistically significant between timesteps 22 and 32, a strong increasing pattern emerges from timestep 33 onwards. An inflection point at timestep 48 marks a consolidation of this growth, leading to a steady accumulation of reserve biomass for the remainder of the run. Statistical analysis confirms a significant positive trend from timestep 55 through the final step, indicating a consistent improvement in pasture condition.

The simulation ends with the reserve biomass reaching a maximum of 121,072 units at timestep 100, significantly surpassing the initial state. This substantial increase implies that the farmers' management decisions, influenced by their own experience and neighbor interactions, eventually reduced grazing pressure to a level that allowed the vegetation to recover and accumulate surplus biomass. The system demonstrates resilience, transitioning from an initial phase of depletion to a state of sustainable abundance by the end of the simulation period.
