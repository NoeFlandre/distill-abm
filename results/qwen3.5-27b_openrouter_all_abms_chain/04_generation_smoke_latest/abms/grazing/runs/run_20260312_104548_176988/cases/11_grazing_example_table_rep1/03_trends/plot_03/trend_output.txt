The average amount of reserve biomass available across all patches starts at 90,000 units. This metric declines steadily during the initial phase of the simulation, reaching a low point of 84,114 units at time step 26. This initial depletion is likely driven by the initial livestock allocation of 90 animals per household, which creates high grazing pressure before agents adapt their strategies. After step 26, the trend reverses, and the reserve biomass enters a consistent phase of recovery. The rate of increase accelerates notably between time steps 44 and 54, indicating a period of rapid regeneration as the population adjusts. Throughout the remainder of the simulation, the biomass continues to rise, albeit at a slowing rate, culminating in a final value of 121,072 units at time step 100. The final biomass level exceeds the starting condition by a substantial margin, suggesting that the heterogeneous population of learning agents, comprising baseline, learning-by-doing, and social learning types, successfully mitigates overgrazing over time. This trajectory reflects the effectiveness of adaptive management strategies in restoring pasture conditions without relying on enforced resting periods.
