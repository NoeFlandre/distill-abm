The number of households engaging in social learning begins at zero for all simulation runs and undergoes a rapid increase between time steps 5 and 30. During this initial phase, the aggregate average rises sharply, reaching a mean of approximately 27.4 households by time step 40, indicating a swift adoption of social learning strategies across the population. This rapid ascent is driven by households observing the states of neighbors and updating their strategies, a process facilitated by the specific configuration where social learning is a distinct behavioral type. Following this steep growth, the rate of increase slows considerably. The aggregate mean value plateaus between time steps 60 and 100, stabilizing around a mean of 32.1 households. While the average value levels off, there is significant divergence among individual simulation runs during the later stages. Some trajectories continue to climb, reaching counts as high as 54 households by time step 40, while others flatten out earlier at lower values, such as the range of 16 to 19 households. This variance suggests that while the initial diffusion of social learning is consistent, the long-term saturation levels of this behavior are sensitive to stochastic elements within the simulation environment. The maximum aggregate value observed is 32.07 households at time step 63, after which the trend remains relatively flat, suggesting that the system reaches a dynamic equilibrium regarding the prevalence of social learning agents.
