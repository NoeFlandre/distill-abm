The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock, measured by the Gini coefficient, starts at 0.0000 at the beginning of the simulation and exhibits a consistent and statistically significant upward trend throughout the 100 time steps, concluding at a value of 0.0726. In the initial phase, specifically the first 10 time steps, the coefficient increases steadily with a steep trajectory, indicating a rapid onset of disparity among the 60 households. The mean value during the entire simulation is 0.0302, with a median of 0.0229, reflecting the skewed nature of the increasing inequality. A statistically significant change point occurs at time step 51, where the rate of increase shifts, followed by another shift at time step 71, suggesting that the dynamics of livestock accumulation and loss become more variable in the middle to late stages of the simulation. By the final steps, the system reaches its peak inequality, with the rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirming an increasing trend (tau=1.000) across all intervals from step 21 to 100. The variance in the data increases towards the end of the simulation, as indicated by the standard deviation of 0.0239 and the divergence of individual simulation runs in the later stages. This pattern implies that as the simulation progresses, the differences in livestock management strategies—whether baseline, self-learning, or social-learning—lead to increasingly unequal outcomes in herd health among the households.
