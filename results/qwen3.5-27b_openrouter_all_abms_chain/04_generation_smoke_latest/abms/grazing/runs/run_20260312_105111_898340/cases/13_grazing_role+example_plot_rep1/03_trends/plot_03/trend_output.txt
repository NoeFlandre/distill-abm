The mean reserve biomass starts at approximately 90,000 units across all simulation runs. During the initial phase, up to step 15, there is a consistent decline in reserve biomass across all runs, reaching a minimum of approximately 80,000 units by step 40. This reduction in biomass likely reflects initial overgrazing or high livestock pressure as the farmers' adaptive behaviors have not yet fully stabilized the grazing system. From step 40 to step 60, there is a significant and rapid increase in reserve biomass, with levels rising from around 80,000 to over 120,000 units. This sharp increase suggests that the learning behaviors of the farmers, particularly those adapting based on their own experience or neighboring strategies, begin to effectively manage grazing and allow the biomass to recover. Beyond step 60, the biomass levels stabilize and show minor variations, with most runs maintaining levels between 110,000 and 128,000 units through step 100. The final range indicates that the system has reached a relatively stable equilibrium, although slight differences persist among the simulation runs. These variations could reflect the influence of individual learning strategies or local interactions on long-term grazing management outcomes.
