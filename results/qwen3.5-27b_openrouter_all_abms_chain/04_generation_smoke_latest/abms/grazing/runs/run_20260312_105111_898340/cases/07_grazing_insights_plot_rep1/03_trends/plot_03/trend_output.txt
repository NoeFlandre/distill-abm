The average reserve biomass across all patches begins near 90,000 units at the start of the simulation. Within the first 30 time steps, values consistently decline, reaching a low of approximately 80,000 units between time steps 30 and 40. Following this minimum, a significant and rapid increase occurs, with values rising from approximately 80,000 units at step 40 to exceeding 110,000 units by step 60. After step 60, the rate of increase slows, and the biomass stabilizes into a plateau phase. By the end of the simulation at step 100, the average reserve biomass remains elevated, settling in a range between 111,000 and 128,000 units. These trends indicate that the initial grazing pressure reduced the resource base, but the introduction of behavioral adaptations facilitated a robust recovery, eventually sustaining a significantly higher biomass condition than at the start of the simulation. This suggests that the specific combination of behavioral types and environmental parameters supports long-term pasture sustainability after an initial period of resource stress.
