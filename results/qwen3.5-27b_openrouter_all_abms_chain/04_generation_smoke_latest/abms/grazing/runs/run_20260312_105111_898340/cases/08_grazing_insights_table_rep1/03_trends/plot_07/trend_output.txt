The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households follows a consistent increasing trend throughout the simulation period. The metric begins at a value of approximately zero at the first timestep and rises steadily to reach 0.0726 by the 100th timestep. The mean value across the entire duration is 0.0302. Statistical analysis confirms a significant positive trend in every ten-timestep window examined, indicating that the divergence in livestock health is persistent and directional. While the overall trajectory is upward, specific inflection points occur at timesteps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, 37, and 57, marking variations in the rate of change. Furthermore, structural change points are identified at timesteps 51 and 71. In the context of the model, this progression occurs within a population of 60 households employing heterogeneous behavioral strategies, such as baseline behavior, learning-by-doing, and social learning, alongside varied risk attitudes. The emergence of inequality suggests that differences in risk perception and learning processes cause households to accumulate livestock health assets at different rates over time. For decision makers, this indicates that introducing diverse behavioral strategies into the system may lead to uneven outcomes in household livelihoods, suggesting a need for monitoring equity metrics to ensure that long-term social-ecological performance remains balanced across the population.
