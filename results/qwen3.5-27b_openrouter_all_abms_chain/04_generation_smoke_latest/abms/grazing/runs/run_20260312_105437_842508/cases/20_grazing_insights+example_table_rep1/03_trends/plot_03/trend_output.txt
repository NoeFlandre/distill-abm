The average reserve biomass across all patches initiates at 90,000 units. During the initial phase, the metric experiences a significant decline, decreasing consistently until reaching a minimum value of 84,114 units at time step 26. This early reduction suggests a period of heightened grazing pressure relative to vegetation growth as the household population establishes their initial stocking levels. Following this trough, the system enters a sustained recovery phase. From time step 33 onwards, the reserve biomass shows a strong increasing trend, which remains significant through the end of the simulation. The rate of accumulation is particularly pronounced between time steps 44 and 54. By time step 100, the average reserve biomass reaches a maximum of 121,072 units, substantially exceeding the starting condition. This long-term growth indicates that the inclusion of learning capabilities facilitates better alignment between livestock numbers and pasture carrying capacity over time. For decision makers, the initial drop highlights a window of ecological stress requiring attention, while the eventual recovery underscores the potential of adaptive management strategies to enhance pasture resilience and biomass reserves in the long run.
