The simulation tracks the prevalence of households adhering to the E-RO strategy, which represents agents operating without adaptive learning mechanisms in the grazing system. At the onset of the observation period, the count stands at exactly 20.0 households. During the initial phase between timesteps 0 and 10, the metric exhibits a statistically significant increasing trend, reaching a local maximum of 21.1 households at timestep 8. Following timestep 10, a distinct regime shift occurs. Between timesteps 11 and 65, the observations reveal a consistent and statistically significant downward trajectory. Multiple rolling window analyses confirm decreasing trends during this period, with a notable change point identified around timestep 20. By timestep 44, the count drops to approximately 15.57 households. The decline continues steadily until the system stabilizes from timestep 66 through the end of the simulation at timestep 100. Statistical tests indicate no significant trend during the final windows. The metric settles near its minimum observed value of 14.67 households at timestep 92, ending the simulation at 14.70 households. The overall mean across the simulation is 16.37 households.

In the context of grazing land management, this trajectory implies that households utilizing the baseline non-learning strategy become less dominant over time. The initial stability gives way to a sustained reduction, suggesting that environmental feedback or social dynamics favor alternative behavioral types over the long term. For decision makers, this indicates that assuming a static population of non-adaptive farmers is likely inaccurate. Policies should anticipate a shrinking baseline cohort, potentially necessitating a focus on supporting the transition to adaptive strategies. The stabilization in the latter third suggests a new equilibrium where the non-learning strategy persists at a lower, stable prevalence.
