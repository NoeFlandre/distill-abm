The simulation tracks the average available green biomass across the pasture patches over 100 timesteps. Initially, at timestep 0, the biomass stands at 45000 units. During the early phase, specifically between timesteps 11 and 21, the system experiences a significant decrease in biomass. This decline bottoms out at timestep 27, where the biomass reaches its minimum value of approximately 42057. This initial reduction suggests that livestock stocking levels exceeded the immediate regenerative capacity of the pasture, potentially driven by initial behavioral adjustments among the heterogeneous household population comprising E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 agents.

Following the minimum at timestep 27, the trajectory shifts. Between timesteps 33 and 43, a significant increasing trend begins, marking a recovery phase. This upward momentum accelerates notably between timesteps 44 and 54, coinciding with the first identified change point at timestep 50. The biomass continues to rise steadily through the remainder of the simulation, although the rate of increase slows in the final periods from timestep 77 to 98. By the final timestep 100, the available green biomass reaches its peak value of 60535, substantially exceeding the initial conditions.

In the context of the model, this trajectory indicates that the inclusion of learning mechanisms allows the household population to adapt their grazing strategies over time. The initial dip represents a period of adjustment where uncertainty in sensing or risk attitudes may have led to overgrazing. However, the sustained recovery demonstrates that the mixed behavioral types effectively manage the resource dynamics, eventually achieving a state of higher pasture condition. For decision makers, this suggests that while short-term ecological stress may occur during the introduction of new management strategies or behavioral changes, long-term adaptive capacity within the farmer population can lead to improved resource conditions. Policymakers should allow time for these learning processes to manifest before evaluating the success of intervention programs.
