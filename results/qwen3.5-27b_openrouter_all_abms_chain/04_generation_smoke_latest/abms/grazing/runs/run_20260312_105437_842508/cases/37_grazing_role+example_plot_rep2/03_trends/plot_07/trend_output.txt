The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock starts at 0 across all simulation runs at timestep 0, indicating an initially equal distribution of livestock among households. Over the first 40 timesteps, the Gini coefficient remains relatively low, with values generally below 0.015, suggesting that inequality in livestock distribution increases slowly during this period. This gradual increase may reflect the early stages of behavioral adaptation as households begin to adjust their livestock management based on their own experiences or observations of neighbors. After timestep 40, the Gini coefficient rises more sharply, reaching values between 0.03 and 0.09 by timestep 100. This acceleration in inequality likely corresponds to divergent outcomes among households, as some accumulate more healthy livestock while others struggle due to differences in learning strategies or environmental feedback. The variability among simulation runs becomes more pronounced after timestep 60, with some runs showing a steeper increase in inequality than others. This divergence could be attributed to the stochastic nature of rainfall and its differential impact on household grazing practices. By the end of the simulation, the highest Gini coefficient reaches approximately 0.09, indicating significant inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households. This final state suggests that the learning dynamics and environmental factors lead to substantial disparities in livestock outcomes among smallholder farmers over time.
