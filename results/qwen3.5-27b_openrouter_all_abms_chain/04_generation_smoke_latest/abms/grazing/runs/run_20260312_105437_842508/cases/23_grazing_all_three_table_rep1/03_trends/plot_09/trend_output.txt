The cumulative volume of livestock destocking across the farming community follows a consistent upward trajectory over the 100-step simulation horizon. Initiating at zero, the total count of destocked animals climbs to approximately 96.78 by the final time step. The rate of this accumulation is not constant; the steepest increase occurs immediately, with the slope of accumulation reaching approximately 5.0 units per step in the initial 10 time steps. This indicates an immediate and aggressive adjustment of herd sizes at the outset of the simulation. As the simulation progresses, the rate of destocking decelerates. Between steps 1 and 32, the accumulation rate drops significantly, settling into a more gradual increase. By the second half of the simulation, specifically after step 40, the slope stabilizes at approximately 0.31 per step. Distinct structural shifts in this trend are observable around time steps 25 and 60. The transition at step 25 marks the move from the initial high-velocity adjustment to a moderate management phase. The final value of roughly 97 cumulative sales reflects a system in continuous, albeit stabilizing, flux. Underlying this monotonic increase is a cyclic component with a dominant period of approximately 49 steps, suggesting longer-term environmental or social learning cycles influencing when farmers choose to release livestock. For decision-makers, this pattern implies that while the system settles into a steady state of maintenance destocking, the initial conditions trigger a rapid realignment of herd sizes. The stability of the positive trend indicates that continuous, modest offtake is required to maintain pasture health and economic balance among the 60 participating households.
