The average total number of livestock, including destocked animals, starts at 90 and declines sharply over the first 30 time steps, reaching a low point of approximately 41 at step 37. This rapid decrease likely reflects an initial adjustment phase where households reduce their herds to align with available forage or risk management strategies in a challenging grazing environment. After the decline, there is a period of relative stability until step 45, followed by a gradual increase that continues through the remainder of the simulation. By the end of the 100 time steps, the average livestock number has risen to about 47.5. The recovery phase suggests that as households adapt through learning from their own experiences or observing neighbors, they gradually rebuild their herds, balancing production goals with land sustainability. The variability among different simulation runs becomes apparent after step 40, indicating that behavioral diversity among farmers influences the herd recovery trajectories. For decision-makers, the initial herd reduction underscores the importance of risk mitigation in uncertain grazing systems, while the subsequent recovery highlights the potential benefits of adaptive learning strategies. Policymakers could support such adaptation through targeted information-sharing platforms that encourage best practice adoption across smallholder communities.
