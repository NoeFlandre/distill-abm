The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero across all simulation runs and increases significantly during the initial phase of the simulation. This rapid increase suggests that early in the simulation, there is a high propensity for households to observe and adopt behaviors from their neighbors, likely driven by the immediate benefits perceived from the actions of the E-RO-SL1 behavioral type. 

Between time steps 10 and 40, the rate of increase slows but remains steady. By time step 40, the mean number of households with active social learning stabilizes around 27. This plateau indicates that the diffusion of the social learning strategy is nearing saturation within the population, or that a dynamic equilibrium has been reached where the rate of new adopters balances out with households potentially reverting or switching strategies. The statistical analysis identifies a significant change point at time step 40, marking the transition from this active adoption phase to a more stable state.

From time step 40 through the end of the simulation at time step 100, the number of households engaging in social learning remains relatively constant, with a final mean value of approximately 32. The variance stabilizes after the initial surge, showing low fluctuation in the later stages. This suggests that once a critical mass of households has adopted the social learning strategy, the population structure regarding this behavior becomes robust. For decision-makers, this implies that introducing social learning mechanisms yields the highest returns in terms of adoption during the early stages of intervention, with diminishing returns as the system matures.
