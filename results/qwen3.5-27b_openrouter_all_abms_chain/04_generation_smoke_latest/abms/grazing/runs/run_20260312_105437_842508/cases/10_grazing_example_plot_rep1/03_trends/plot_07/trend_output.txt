The Gini coefficient for total healthy livestock begins near zero at time step 0, indicating an initial state of nearly perfect equality in livestock distribution among the 60 households. From time steps 0 to approximately 40, the coefficient increases gradually across all simulation runs, reaching a range between 0.01 and 0.02. This slow growth reflects modest divergence in livestock holdings, possibly due to initial variations in decision-making among the three behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) or minor stochastic effects in the model’s ecological dynamics. Between time steps 40 and 60, the rate of increase accelerates significantly, with the Gini coefficient spanning from 0.02 to 0.04. This marks a transition phase where disparities in livestock holdings widen, likely driven by compounding differences in grazing strategies and resource utilization across behavioral types. After time step 60, the divergence becomes even more pronounced, with the Gini coefficient ranging from 0.03 to 0.09 by time step 100. This widening spread suggests that learning-by-doing (E-LBD) and social learning (E-RO-SL1) strategies lead to increasingly heterogeneous outcomes, where some households accumulate significantly more healthy livestock than others. By the final time step, the highest values of the Gini coefficient approach 0.09, indicating substantial inequality in livestock distribution. This outcome highlights the long-term social-ecological consequences of adaptive learning behaviors under the heterogeneous risk attitudes (r-parameter) and ecological conditions of the model. The overall trajectory shows a clear trend toward increasing inequality in livestock distribution, reflecting the complex interplay of behavioral strategies and environmental variability in the common property grazing system.
