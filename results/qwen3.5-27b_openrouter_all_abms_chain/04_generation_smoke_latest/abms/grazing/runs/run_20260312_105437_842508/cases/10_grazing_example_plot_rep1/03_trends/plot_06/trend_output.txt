The average number of livestock destocked by households begins near zero and rises sharply in the first 5 time steps, peaking at approximately 7.5. This sharp increase is likely due to initial resource scarcity, as households with high initial livestock counts face immediate pressure to reduce their herds in response to insufficient pasture biomass. From time step 5 to 20, the mean destocking declines rapidly from its peak to a range of approximately 0 to 3, as households adjust their livestock numbers in response to the initial resource constraints. This decline may reflect the initial stabilization of herd sizes and the gradual adaptation of household decision-making to the prevailing ecological conditions. Between time steps 20 and 100, the mean destocking stabilizes within a lower range, oscillating between 0 and 1.5, with noticeable fluctuations. These oscillations suggest ongoing dynamic adjustments in household behavior and resource availability, as the system seeks a new equilibrium. The fluctuations could be attributed to the interaction of heterogeneous behavioral types, such as baseline behavior (E-RO), learning-by-doing (E-LBD), and social learning (E-RO-SL1), alongside varying risk attitudes and ecological conditions. Variability among simulation runs increases over time, indicating that individual household trajectories diverge as they respond differently to resource constraints and decision-making processes. By the end of the simulation, the mean destocking stabilizes near zero to 0.5, indicating that most households have successfully adjusted their livestock numbers to sustainable levels. This stabilization aligns with the model’s goal of evaluating the long-term social-ecological consequences of different behavioral strategies under conditions of ecological uncertainty.
