The average total number of livestock, including destocked animals, starts at 90 and declines sharply over the first 20 time steps. This rapid initial decrease suggests a significant adjustment period where households likely reduce their livestock numbers, possibly in response to initial grazing pressure or to align with the established carrying capacity of the land. 

The decline continues at a slower rate until time step 37, where the average number of livestock reaches a minimum of approximately 41. This minimum represents a point of stability or a 'floor' for herd sizes under the baseline behavior model (E-RO), where farmers primarily rely on their own past experiences without learning from neighbors. 

Around time step 40, the trend reverses, and the average number of livestock begins to increase. This upward trend continues for the remainder of the simulation, ending at approximately 47.5 by time step 100. The increase after the minimum indicates that households, particularly those using the learning behaviors (E-LBD and E-RO-SL1), begin to rebuild their herds as they gain more experience or share knowledge about sustainable practices. The divergence of the lines in the plot after time step 40 highlights the impact of different behavioral types on herd size management, with some households successfully increasing their livestock numbers while others maintain lower levels. 

The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis supports this interpretation, showing a significant decreasing trend in the early stages and a significant increasing trend in the later stages. The change points at 20, 55, and 100 mark transitions in the trend dynamics, reflecting shifts in the dominant behaviors or environmental conditions affecting livestock management. The oscillation pattern with a dominant period of 49 suggests cyclical fluctuations in livestock numbers, likely driven by seasonal variations or periodic adjustments in herd sizes by farmers. 

For decision-makers, these findings suggest that initial herd reductions are necessary to align with the land's capacity, but with appropriate learning and adaptation, farmers can sustainably rebuild their livestock populations. The divergence in outcomes based on behavioral types highlights the importance of promoting knowledge sharing and adaptive management practices to support long-term livestock and land sustainability.
