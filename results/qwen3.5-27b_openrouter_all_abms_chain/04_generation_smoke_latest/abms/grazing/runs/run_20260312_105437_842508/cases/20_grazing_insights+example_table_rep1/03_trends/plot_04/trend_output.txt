The average available green biomass across all patches begins at 45,000 units at the start of the simulation. During the initial phase, from time step 0 to time step 27, the biomass experiences a gradual decline, reaching a minimum value of 42,057 units. This early reduction reflects the initial grazing pressure exerted by the 60 heterogeneous households as they establish their livestock holdings before learning mechanisms fully adapt to the ecological conditions. The decrease is most pronounced within the first 21 time steps, suggesting that initial stocking rates temporarily outpace vegetation regrowth.

Following the minimum at time step 27, the system enters a transition period characterized by stability between time steps 22 and 32. Subsequently, a robust and sustained increasing trend emerges from time step 33 through to the end of the simulation. By time step 100, the biomass reaches its maximum recorded value of 60,535 units, significantly surpassing the initial level. This long-term recovery and growth are consistent with the activation of adaptive learning behaviors, such as learning-by-doing and social learning, which allow households to adjust livestock numbers based on observed pasture states and neighbor behavior. The trend remains consistently upward after time step 33, highlighting a stable shift towards pasture regeneration.

Minor fluctuations are observed around the mean, particularly between time steps 10 and 20, and again between 41 and 49, likely driven by stochastic rainfall variability and the discrete decision-making cycles of the agents. For decision makers, these trends suggest that while initial resource depletion is a risk when introducing diverse behavioral types into a common property system, long-term ecological resilience can be achieved through adaptive management strategies. Monitoring early biomass levels is critical to prevent irreversible degradation, but implementing learning mechanisms appears effective in promoting pasture recovery and enhancing biomass availability over the long term.
