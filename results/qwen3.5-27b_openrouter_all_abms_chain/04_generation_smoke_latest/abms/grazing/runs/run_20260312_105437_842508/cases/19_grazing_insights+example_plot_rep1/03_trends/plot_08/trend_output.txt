The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero across all simulation runs and begins to rise sharply between time steps 5 and 10. This rapid increase suggests an early onset of social learning adoption among the agent population. The count continues to climb steeply until approximately time step 30, where most simulations reach between 35 and 50 households. This pattern indicates that within the first thirty steps, social learning diffuses rapidly through the population, likely due to the influence of neighboring agents observing successful strategies. After time step 40, the growth rate decelerates, and the number of households stabilizes, with most runs plateauing between 30 and 54 households by the end of the simulation. The divergence in the final values across simulations reflects variability in how social learning propagates under different stochastic conditions, such as noise in information transmission or varying risk attitudes. This trend implies that while social learning adoption is robust in the short term, its long-term reach depends on initial conditions and interaction dynamics. Decision makers should consider the rapid early diffusion phase as a critical window for interventions to maximize social learning adoption and align incentives for sustainable practices.
