The mean weekly consumption of whole milk per agent starts at 2654.81 units and follows a consistent downward trajectory across the entire simulation period, ending at 253.10 units. The aggregate trend is statistically significant, as indicated by multiple rolling Mann-Kendall tests showing a 'decreasing' trend (tau = -1.000, p = 0.027) for the initial and middle phases (indices 0–14 and 20–24). The decline is steepest during the early phase: between indices 5 and 9, the mean consumption drops sharply with a slope of -221.111, reflecting a rapid shift away from whole milk. 

A plateau phase occurs roughly between indices 15 and 19, where the trend is no longer statistically significant (p = 0.086), suggesting a temporary stabilization of habits before consumption resumes its downward path. Later in the simulation, from index 25 to 29, the data again shows a non-significant trend, possibly indicating the saturation of market share for alternative milk types. Notable local variations include peaks at index 19 (773.96), index 27 (620.98), and index 30 (335.18), which may represent moments of temporary resistance to switching or localized social reinforcement of whole milk use. However, these do not alter the overarching negative trajectory. 

For decision-makers, this pattern suggests that early interventions promoting alternative milk types yield high rates of adoption due to weak initial habit strength or high social susceptibility. As the simulation progresses, the rate of change slows, implying that remaining whole milk consumers are entrenched in habits or less responsive to social influence. Strategies targeting late adopters may require stronger incentives or structural changes to overcome high incumbent habit levels (parameter set to 10).
