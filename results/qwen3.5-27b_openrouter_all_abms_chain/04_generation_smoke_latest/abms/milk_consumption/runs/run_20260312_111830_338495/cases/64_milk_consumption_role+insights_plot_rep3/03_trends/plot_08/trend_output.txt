The mean habit factor for whole milk exhibits a consistent upward trajectory across all simulation runs. At the initial step, the value stands at 1.0, indicating no habitual influence on purchasing decisions. By step 1, there is a sharp increase to approximately 1.02, suggesting a rapid initial adoption of habitual behavior. From step 1 to step 32, the habit factor steadily rises, reaching values between 1.04 and 1.06 by the final step. This gradual increase reflects the strengthening of habitual tendencies over time as agents continue to choose whole milk. The divergence in the trajectories across different runs indicates variability in individual habit formation dynamics, likely influenced by the interplay of social interaction parameters and individual susceptibilities. For decision makers, this trend underscores the importance of understanding how initial consumer behaviors can rapidly solidify into long-term habits, making them resistant to change. Strategies aimed at altering consumption patterns must consider the entrenched nature of these habits and potentially leverage social norms and health perceptions to induce shifts in consumer behavior.
