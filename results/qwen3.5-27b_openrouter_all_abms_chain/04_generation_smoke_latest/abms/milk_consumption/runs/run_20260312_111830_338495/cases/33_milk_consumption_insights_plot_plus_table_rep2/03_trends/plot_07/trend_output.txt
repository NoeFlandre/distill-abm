The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits three distinct behavioral phases. Initially, consumption rises rapidly from a baseline of 5.29 units, surging to approximately 1,200 units by time step 10, reflecting a strong early adoption dynamic likely driven by shifting social norms regarding health and the initial activation of agent dispositions to consider alternatives. Following this surge, the system enters a plateau phase spanning from time step 11 to 27, where consumption fluctuates between 1,183 and 1,265 units with no statistically significant trend, suggesting a period of stabilization where habitual consumption and social reinforcement maintain status quo levels. A second period of growth commences at time step 28, where consumption climbs sharply from approximately 1,220 units to a peak of 1,456 units by the final time step. This late-stage increase implies that agents who remained resistant to earlier shifts eventually modified their behavior, potentially due to the cumulative effects of social conformity or the reinforcement of health-based preference weights.
