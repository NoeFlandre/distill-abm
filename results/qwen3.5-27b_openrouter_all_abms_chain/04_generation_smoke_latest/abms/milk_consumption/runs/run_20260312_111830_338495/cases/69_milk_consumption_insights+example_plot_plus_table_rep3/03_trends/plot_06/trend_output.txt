The average weekly consumption of whole milk per person begins at approximately 2,655 units and exhibits a sharp decline through the early stages of the simulation. Between the initial time step and step 10, consumption drops significantly to roughly 980 units, indicating a rapid shift away from whole milk. This steep reduction suggests that initial dispositions to consider alternative milk types were strongly activated, likely driven by the high perceived health and environmental characteristics of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties, combined with moderate social influence and habit modification. 

From step 10 to step 25, the rate of decline slows, with consumption stabilizing in a range between 700 and 850 units. Multiple minor fluctuations occur during this period, including small peaks around steps 19 and 27, but no sustained reversal of the downward trend is observed. The persistence of a decreasing trend in this phase implies that agents who had not yet switched continued to evaluate alternatives and gradually adjusted their preferences, possibly influenced by evolving social norms or internal re-evaluation of prior choices.

A second acceleration in the decline occurs after step 25, with consumption falling rapidly from approximately 600 units to below 300 units by step 32. This late-stage drop suggests a tipping point was reached where the majority of agents had transitioned away from whole milk, reinforcing the adoption of lower-fat alternatives through cumulative social influence and habit formation.

For decision makers, these trends indicate that consumer shifts away from dominant products can occur rapidly when supported by aligned perception, social influence, and habit mechanisms. Interventions aimed at promoting alternative choices may be particularly effective in the early stages, as agents are more receptive to new information. However, sustained effort may be necessary to overcome entrenched habits, as evidenced by the period of slower decline in the middle stages. The eventual sharp decrease highlights the potential for system-wide transformation once a critical mass of consumers adopts new preferences.
