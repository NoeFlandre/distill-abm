The mean household risk attitude values begin near 0.0 at the start of the simulation and exhibit a rapid increase for nearly all trajectories within the first 20 to 30 time steps, where most values reach between 0.4 and 0.8. Following this initial surge, the trends diverge: approximately half of the trajectories stabilize at high risk attitudes (between 0.4 and 0.8) for the remainder of the simulation, while the other half show a gradual decline or a sharp drop around time steps 40 to 60, eventually settling at lower values, some approaching -0.3 by time step 100. The divergence suggests that agents adapt their risk attitudes differently over time, with some maintaining high risk tolerance and others becoming more risk-averse as the simulation progresses. These patterns indicate that the extension agents' learning mechanisms and uncertainty management strategies result in heterogeneous long-term behaviors, which could inform decision-makers about the potential variability in household responses to resource management interventions.
