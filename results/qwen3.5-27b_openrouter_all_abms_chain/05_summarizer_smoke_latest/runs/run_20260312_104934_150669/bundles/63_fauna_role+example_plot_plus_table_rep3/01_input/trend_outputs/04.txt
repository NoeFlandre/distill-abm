The cumulative number of female prey animals killed by forager bands begins at zero and increases steadily over the initial period, reaching approximately 60 individuals by step 200. A notable acceleration in the rate of increase occurs between steps 200 and 500, where the count rises from 60 to around 100. This phase aligns with a period of sustained predation pressure as the initial forager-prey interactions intensify. Following this rapid accumulation, the growth rate slows markedly between steps 500 and 1000, with the cumulative total rising from 100 to approximately 115. This deceleration suggests a reduction in available prey or saturation of foraging efficiency relative to prey population density. Between steps 1000 and 1500, the cumulative count approaches its maximum, stabilizing near 120 individuals, indicating that the total number of females susceptible to being hunted has been largely exhausted or the system has reached a steady state in predation dynamics. The final phase, extending beyond step 1500, shows negligible increase, with the count plateauing at approximately 123 by step 1826, and remaining stable through the end of the observation window at step 2312. The consistency of these trends across 40 simulation runs, evidenced by the low variance in mean values and the high statistical significance of increasing trends in early windows, reflects a robust outcome driven by the interaction of high hunting pressure, defined by initial forager counts and food value parameters, and the finite availability of female prey within the simulated environment.
