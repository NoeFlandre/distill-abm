The cumulative number of prey deaths begins at zero and exhibits a rapid, steep increase during the first 400 time steps, reaching approximately 180 to 220 deaths across the majority of simulation runs. This initial surge suggests an immediate, high-intensity interaction between the forager population and the prey herd, likely driven by the hunters' initial energy deficit and the availability of the initial 250 prey units on the landscape. The rate of mortality slows significantly after 600 steps, with the cumulative count rising more gradually to roughly 230 by step 1000. The mean value stabilizes near 233.0 by step 1500, with peaks recorded at step 1876 (240.33) and step 2040 (239.50). The final mean cumulative death count settles at 235.0. This trajectory implies that the prey population was subjected to significant pressure early in the simulation, but the rate of removal decreased over time, potentially due to the diminishing density of vulnerable prey or the accumulation of resources by foragers reducing immediate hunting pressure. The data indicates that under these parameters, nearly the entire initial prey cohort (250 individuals) is removed within approximately 2000 time steps, suggesting a system trending toward local extinction rather than equilibrium.
