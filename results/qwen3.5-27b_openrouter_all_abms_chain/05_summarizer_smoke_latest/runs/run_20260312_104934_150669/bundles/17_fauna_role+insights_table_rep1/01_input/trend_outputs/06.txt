The simulation of the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model reveals a complex trajectory in the prey population's reproductive metrics over the simulation duration. Initially, the birth metric begins at 0.0000. Statistical analysis identifies an early inflection point at time step 613, marking a shift in population dynamics. A period of significant growth follows, evidenced by rolling Mann-Kendall tests showing a significant increasing trend between time steps 696 and 1623. During this expansion phase, the metric reaches its maximum recorded value of 5.6000 at time step 1673. This peak represents the zenith of reproductive output or population viability under the configured parameters of 20 initial foragers and 250 initial prey.

Following the peak at time step 1673, the dynamics shift drastically. Change points detected at 1850 and 2050 coincide with a statistically significant decreasing trend identified between time steps 1624 and 2087. The metric declines from the peak of 5.6000 to an end value of 1.0000 at the final simulation step. This downward trajectory suggests a severe contraction in population capacity or a cessation of births, consistent with extinction scenarios driven by hunting pressure and environmental constraints. Oscillations with a dominant period of 50 time steps indicate recurring cyclical pressures, likely reflecting the seasonal parameters set at 0.8. The convergence of high seasonality and forager pressure appears to trigger a boom-and-bust cycle where initial population growth is unsustainable.

For decision makers, these trends imply that current parameter settings create a high-risk environment for megaherbivore persistence. The lag between the peak at time step 1673 and the final decline suggests that population collapse may not be immediate following pressure onset but follows a trajectory of resource depletion. Mitigation strategies should focus on reducing forager impact or increasing grass regeneration to prevent the observed post-peak crash.
