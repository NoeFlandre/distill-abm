The cumulative number of deaths among the prey animal population starts at zero and rises sharply, reaching between 150 and 240 deaths by approximately 600 time steps. This rapid increase in mortality is indicative of the combined effects of internal mortality, environmental factors, and foraging pressure by human hunters, as the initial population of 250 prey animals is quickly impacted by these dynamics. After this period, the rate of deaths slows significantly, with the cumulative total stabilizing between 220 and 250 deaths by around 2000 time steps. This plateau suggests that the population has either stabilized at a lower level due to the combined pressures or that the remaining prey population has reached a balance with the foraging behavior and environmental constraints. The variability in the trajectories across simulation runs highlights the influence of stochastic elements in foraging efficiency and prey movement strategies. This pattern implies that decision makers should consider the initial vulnerability of prey populations to hunting and environmental stressors and the potential for stabilization at lower population levels.
