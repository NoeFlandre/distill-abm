The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore. It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival. The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model suggests that the forager population is unable to maintain a positive energy balance. Despite the presence of food storage mechanisms and immortal foragers, the cumulative energy costs associated with movement and prey processing exceed the energy gained from hunting megaherbivores. The most extreme deficit is recorded at tick 2310, with a minimum value of -38233.00. The observed metric tracks the cumulative mortality of the megaherbivore population across forty simulation iterations. The trajectory begins at zero and follows a distinct pattern of rapid accumulation followed by stabilization. The overall pattern reflects a system where initial high hunting pressure extracts a significant portion of the reproductive base, followed by a plateau where prey availability constrains further mortality. The aggregate population trajectory follows a terminal decline, initiating from a collective mean abundance of 166.67 individuals at time zero. The female megaherbivore population initiates the simulation at 125 individuals and experiences a sustained decline throughout the observed period of 2312 ticks. Within the model context, the combined pressures of 20 immortal foragers, high seasonality, and specific processing costs exceeded the reproductive capacity defined by a 730-tick birth spacing. This pattern confirms that the configured hunting pressure of 20 foragers, interacting with environmental mortality factors, exceeds the population's reproductive and energy replenishment capacity.
