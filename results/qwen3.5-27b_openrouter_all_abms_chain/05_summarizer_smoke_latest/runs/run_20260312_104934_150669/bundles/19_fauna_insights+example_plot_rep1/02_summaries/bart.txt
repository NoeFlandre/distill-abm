Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Hunters follow dynamic optimal foraging theory, balancing energy status, prey value, density, and costs. Ungulates are sexually dimorphic, reproduce, age, and move as herds Number of male prey animals killed starts at zero and rises sharply, reaching approximately 110 to 120 individuals by step 500 across simulation runs. Trend plateaus between steps 500 and 1000, with only marginal increases in the number of males killed. Trend suggests hunting pressure on male prey is most intense in early stages, potentially leading to rapid population declines. The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model predicts a high risk of extinction for male megaherbivores. By 1,000 time steps, the number of male animals in most simulations has dropped to near zero. Decision makers should prioritize interventions such as reducing foraging pressure, increasing food resource density or adjusting herd life-history parameters to prevent total extinction in similar ecological models. By step 2,000, the total energy of female prey animals remains consistently at or near zero across all runs. The rapid initial decline and eventual stabilization at zero suggest that the system reaches a state where female prey populations can no longer sustain their energy levels.
