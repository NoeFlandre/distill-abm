The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock, measured by the Gini coefficient, starts at 0.0000 at the beginning of the simulation and exhibits a consistent and statistically significant upward trend across all 100 time steps, concluding at a value of 0.0726. The mean Gini coefficient across the simulation period is 0.0302, indicating that on average, there is a moderate divergence in livestock holdings among the households. The distribution remains extremely narrow during the initial phase, with the coefficient rising only slightly until approximately step 40, where the rate of increase begins to accelerate. Throughout the entire duration, the trend is strictly increasing as confirmed by rolling Mann-Kendall tests, with a slope that becomes more pronounced in the second half of the simulation (slope 0.001 compared to 0.000 in the first half). This trajectory reflects a systematic divergence in herd sizes among the agents. By the end of the simulation, the divergence has widened significantly, with the distribution of outcomes spreading out; while the aggregate trend shows a rise to 0.0726, individual simulation runs vary, ending in a range between approximately 0.03 and 0.09. The presence of change points at steps 51 and 71 corresponds to periods where the rate of inequality growth shifts, potentially driven by the compounding effects of the specific learning behaviors (baseline and learning-by-doing) and the heterogeneous risk attitudes of the households interacting with the variable rainfall environment. The final increase suggests that over time, the decision-making processes of the agents lead to greater disparity in economic assets, with some households accumulating significantly more healthy livestock than others.
