The population of female prey animals initiates at 125 individuals. A rapid decline is observed immediately, dropping below 20 individuals by approximately 600 time steps. This steep initial reduction suggests high pressure from external mortality, such as hunting or starvation, combined with internal mortality rates that exceed the birth spacing of 730 time steps (roughly two years) during the early phases. The rate of decline slows significantly after step 700, where the population fluctuates between 0 and 10 individuals until approximately step 2000. During this phase, minor oscillations occur, likely driven by the interplay between the 63-step dominant period and seasonal environmental factors. The population eventually reaches zero for all simulation runs by the final time step of 2310. This outcome indicates that under the specific parameter settings—particularly the high initial forager count relative to prey and the movement costs—the reproductive capacity of the herd is insufficient to sustain the population against the combined pressures of predation and environmental mortality.
