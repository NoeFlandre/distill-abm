The cumulative number of female prey killed by foragers increases sharply from the start of the simulation, rising from 0 to approximately 100 within the first 500 time steps. This rapid initial accumulation suggests that foragers efficiently locate and target female prey during the early phase, likely driven by high prey density and favorable foraging conditions. From step 500 to around 1000, the rate of increase slows, indicating that as prey numbers are reduced, the effort or success rate of hunting females diminishes. The cumulative count reaches a maximum of about 123.7 around step 1826 and stabilizes thereafter, with only minor fluctuations up to the end of the observed period. The variance among simulation runs is highest between steps 400 and 800, reflecting stochastic differences in forager-prey interactions during this critical transition phase. As the cumulative number of females killed plateaus, the population of female prey likely becomes too depleted or dispersed to sustain the same level of hunting success, signaling a potential shift in forager behavior or prey availability. For decision makers, this trend highlights the vulnerability of female prey populations to early, intensive hunting pressure, which can lead to rapid depletion and long-term population instability.
