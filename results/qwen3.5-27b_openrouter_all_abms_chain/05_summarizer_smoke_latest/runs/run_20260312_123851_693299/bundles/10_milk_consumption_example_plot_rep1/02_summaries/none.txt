Context and Goals:

The overall objective of the agent-based model is to reproduce adoption behaviours of milk consumption by the British public (1974-2005), by replicating individual preferences and decision factors. Specifically, the goal of the model is to explore the influence of perception, habits and social influence in an individual's decision-making process of milk choice. The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against observed macro level data.

In particular, the study conducts an experiment to compare the performance of two model variants in reproducing overserved milk consumption trends. These variants present different mechanisms for how agents become disposed to consider their choices, representing a threshold based, and a probability-based approach. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence, and finally, choice evaluation where agents consider the impact of their choices and may adjust their future decisions.

The agents in the model represent adult consumers who occupy a random position in an information environment. Each agent has a disposition to consider alternative milk choices. Two disposition mechanisms are tested in the model, a threshold-based approach, and a probability-based approach. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. These are computed at the initialisation of the simulation and then calculated at each time step. An agent's milk choice function is modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. Agents ascribe different relative importance to each constituent part of the choice function (health factors and environmental factors). If the disposition requirement has been met, consumption of each milk type is split proportionately by the size of each choice function, modulated by the other influences. If not, agents keep their existing choice.

Agents (n=1,000) start with an existing choice based on the dominant position of whole milk versus skimmed varieties in 1974 (start year of the data). All agents are part of a social network. Each agent in the network can sense and be influenced by the choice function of each milk alternative for other agents in their network. Links between agents are unidirectional, and influence occurs as a function of interaction probability, with the degree of influence characterised by agent susceptibility. Social norms are globally perceived by agents and impact the weightings of the choice function.

The average weekly consumption of whole milk starts at approximately 2,650 units per person and remains stable during the first time step. From time step 1, consumption begins a steep decline, dropping to around 1,200 units by time step 10. This rapid reduction suggests an initial phase of widespread agent re-evaluation or adoption of alternative milk types, driven by factors such as health or environmental perceptions. By time step 15, consumption further decreases to approximately 900 units, indicating a continued shift away from whole milk. Between time steps 15 and 25, the decline slows, with consumption stabilizing between 700 and 800 units, reflecting a period of relative equilibrium in decision-making patterns. After time step 25, consumption resumes a downward trend, reaching approximately 250 units by time step 32. This final decline may result from cumulative effects of social influence, habit formation, or evolving norms regarding milk preferences.

The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at zero and remains at that level during the first time step. From time step 1, consumption rises sharply, reaching approximately 1,200 by time step 10. This rapid increase suggests that agents quickly adapt to the new milk options, influenced by the initial perception of health benefits and environmental factors. The growth rate slows between time steps 10 and 27, where consumption fluctuates around 1,200 to 1,250. This period may reflect a balance between habit formation and social influence, as agents adjust their choices based on previous decisions and interactions within their social networks. After time step 27, there is another noticeable increase, with consumption rising to about 1,450 by time step 30. This final surge could indicate a reinforcement of social norms or a shift in collective perception regarding the benefits of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. Overall, the trends show a clear progression from initial non-consumption to significant adoption, with key transitions occurring around time steps 10 and 27, likely driven by the interplay of agent perception, habit, and social influence mechanisms.

The total number of times agents chose whole milk begins at zero at the start of the simulation and increases linearly over time. By step 10, the cumulative count of whole milk choices reaches approximately 800, rising steadily to around 1,600 by step 20. At step 30, the total number of whole milk choices is close to 2,400. The trajectory of the data remains consistent across simulation runs, indicating minimal variability in agent behavior regarding whole milk selection. This linear growth suggests that agents maintain a steady preference for whole milk throughout the simulation, with no significant shifts in choice patterns or influence from other milk types over time.

The total number of times agents chose skimmed or semi-skimmed milk begins at zero and increases steadily throughout the simulation. In the initial steps, from step 0 to step 5, the growth is minimal, with the number of choices staying below 500. This slow start suggests that agents initially favor their existing whole milk choices, possibly due to strong habits or limited disposition triggers. From step 5 onward, the trend shows a consistent upward trajectory. By step 10, the number of choices rises to approximately 4,000. This increase aligns with the model's parameters, as agents begin to consider alternatives based on perception, social influence, and habit adjustments. At step 15, the total reaches around 9,000, and by step 20, it climbs to approximately 14,000. The rate of increase remains stable, reflecting a gradual shift in consumer behavior as agents integrate new information about health and environmental factors. By step 30, the total number of choices surpasses 24,000, indicating a significant move away from whole milk toward skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The consistent upward trend across all runs suggests that the underlying mechanisms, including social conformity and habit modifications, effectively drive this behavioral shift over time.

The total number of milk choices made by all agents begins at zero at time step 0 and increases linearly as the simulation progresses. By time step 30, the total number of choices reaches approximately 25,000. The steady increase suggests that, on average, each of the 1,000 agents is making a choice at every time step. The uniformity in the rise indicates a consistent rate of decision-making across the population, possibly driven by the probability-based disposition mechanism allowing agents to frequently reconsider alternatives. This pattern highlights that social influence and habit parameters, such as the set interaction probability of 0.3 and susceptibility of 0.2, result in widespread and regular reconsideration of milk choices over time.

The mean whole milk quantity starts at approximately 2,650 at the initial step and declines rapidly, reaching around 1,250 by step 5. A more significant drop occurs between step 25 and step 30, reducing the quantity to around 400. By the final step, the mean whole milk quantity stabilizes at approximately 300. This trend aligns with the observed historical substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties, suggesting that increasing awareness of health factors and social influence drives the decline in whole milk consumption.

The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed starts at 0 and increases rapidly, reaching approximately 1250 units by time step 11. This sharp rise indicates a strong initial adoption of lower-fat milk types, likely driven by the perceived health benefits and the initial low habits of consuming whole milk. The quantity stabilizes between 1200 and 1250 units from time step 11 to 26, suggesting that the agents' choices have reached a temporary equilibrium as the effects of social influence and habit reinforcement balance out the initial surge in adoption. From time step 27 onwards, there is another significant increase, with the quantity reaching approximately 1450 units by time step 32. This second surge might reflect a renewed influence of health perception or environmental factors, as well as the cumulative effect of social influence as more agents adopt these milk types, further reinforcing the trend.

The mean habit factor for whole milk starts at 1.00 and increases sharply to approximately 1.02 within the first step. This gradual rise indicates a continuous reinforcement of habitual consumption of whole milk over time. The variance across different simulation runs remains relatively low, suggesting consistent behavior in the reinforcement of habits. The steady increase in the habit factor aligns with the model's objective of replicating the persistence of whole milk consumption patterns in the British public.

The mean habit factor for skimmed/semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 1.0 at time step 0, indicating no initial habit reinforcement. The progressive rise in the habit factor aligns with the mechanism where repeated choices for skimmed/semi-skimmed milk reinforce preference, modulated by social influence and cognitive dissonance dynamics.

The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at zero and rises sharply during the initial time steps, reaching approximately 0.56 by step five. This rapid increase suggests that agents are quickly developing divergent preferences for milk types based on their initial perceptions, habits, and social influences. Following this initial phase, the rate of growth slows significantly, with the metric increasing gradually to reach approximately 0.63 by step ten. The continued but slower growth indicates that while agents are still refining their choices, the initial divergence has largely been established. By step twenty, the value approaches 0.70, showing a steady convergence towards a stable state. The simulation concludes with the metric nearing 0.75 by step thirty-two, indicating that the agents' choice functions have reached a relative equilibrium with significant but stabilizing differences in their preferences across the population.

The mean value health deviation begins at zero at the initial time step, indicating that the agents' held health values for milk choices were aligned with their initial selections. From step 0 to approximately step 10, the deviation rises steadily, reaching values around 0.5. This sharp increase suggests a rapid divergence between the health values perceived by agents and the health values implied by their actual consumption choices during this early period. The increase may reflect agents adjusting their consumption away from their initial habits as social influence and other decision factors begin to take effect. Between steps 10 and 25, the mean deviation grows more slowly, climbing from approximately 0.5 to 0.6. This period of gradual increase indicates that while agents continue to adjust their choices, the rate of divergence between perceived and implied health values has slowed. The system appears to be approaching a state of relative equilibrium where the adjustments to consumption are becoming less pronounced. A noticeable upward trend resumes after step 25, with the mean deviation rising from approximately 0.6 to over 0.7 by step 32. This renewed acceleration in divergence suggests a shift in dynamics where agents' actual consumption choices are moving further away from their initial health perceptions. The simulation ends with the mean value health deviation stabilizing near 0.7, indicating a persistent and significant gap between the health values agents hold and those implied by their milk consumption patterns.

The mean value environmental deviation begins at zero at the initial time step, indicating that there is no discrepancy between the agents' held environmental values and the implied values of their milk choices at the simulation's start. As the simulation progresses, the mean value environmental deviation increases sharply, reaching approximately 10 by the 5th time step. This rapid rise suggests a quick divergence between agents' environmental values and their actual milk consumption behaviors as they adjust to the model dynamics. The deviation continues to grow but at a slower rate, reaching a peak of around 13.5 by the 10th time step. From this point onward, the mean value environmental deviation stabilizes, maintaining a value close to 13.5 throughout the remaining duration of the simulation. This stabilization indicates that the agents' milk choices have settled into a consistent pattern relative to their environmental values, with no further significant adjustments in behavior.
