This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success. The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock. The aggregate reserve biomass demonstrates a complex temporal evolution characterized by an initial depletion followed by sustained recovery. At timestep 0, the biomass stands at 90,000.00 units at the beginning of the simulation. The overall mean across the 101 samples is 103,366.2649 with a standard deviation of 16,057.2488. The Gini coefficient representing inequality in total healthy livestock demonstrates a consistent upward trajectory across the 100 timesteps of the simulation period. The average cumulative livestock destocking per household demonstrates a strictly monotonic increase over the simulation horizon. This pattern suggests that while initial livestock levels likely exceeded carrying capacity, leading to heavy destocking. A sustained and significant decrease is observed across multiple consecutive periods, indicating a systematic shift in farmer behavior. During this decline, the count drops to approximately 15.57 households by time step 44. This reduction reflects a transition away from the baseline non-learning approach, likely as farmers adopt strategies involving self-learning or social learning facilitated by the extension model.
