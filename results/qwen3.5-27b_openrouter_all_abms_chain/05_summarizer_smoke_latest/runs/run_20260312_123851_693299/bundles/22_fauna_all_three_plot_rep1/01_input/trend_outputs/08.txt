The number of male prey animals begins at approximately 120 and declines rapidly over the first 400 time steps, reaching below 40 in most simulation runs. This sharp initial decline suggests that hunting pressure and environmental constraints are highly effective in reducing the male population early in the simulation. By around 1000 steps, the number of male animals in most runs falls to near zero, indicating a critical threshold where male population collapse accelerates. Some runs show slightly delayed declines, with numbers persisting above 10 until approximately 1200 steps, before also reaching zero. The variation among runs after 400 steps reflects differences in the timing and intensity of hunting interactions and resource availability across simulations. This pattern implies that male megaherbivores are particularly vulnerable to early-stage extinction under the modeled conditions, driven by a combination of human predation and environmental stressors. Decision makers should consider that even moderate hunting pressure, coupled with environmental factors, can lead to rapid declines in male populations, which could destabilize the broader socio-ecological system by reducing herd sizes and reproductive potential.
