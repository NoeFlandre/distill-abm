The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted and killed increases sharply during the initial phase of the simulation, reaching approximately 120 by step 600 across most simulations. The rate of increase is rapid and steep from step 0 to 400, with variations among simulations showing some trajectories reaching 100 by step 300, while others lag, achieving the same milestone closer to step 500. After step 600, the cumulative count stabilizes, with minimal additional kills observed up to the maximum recorded step of approximately 2100. The final values cluster between 110 and 120, indicating a saturation point where hunting pressure no longer significantly adds to the cumulative total. This pattern suggests that postpartum females are heavily targeted early in the simulation, likely due to their lower processing costs and high food value relative to calves. The saturation implies either a depletion of vulnerable targets or behavioral adaptations reducing encounter rates. Decision makers should consider the early, intense predation on reproductive females as a critical factor accelerating population decline and potentially driving extinction under similar hunting pressures.
