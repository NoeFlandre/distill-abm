The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape. It simulates a socio-ecological system containing human foragers and sexually dimorphic megaherbivore herds. The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands shows a consistent upward trend across all simulation runs. After this point, the rate of increase slows down, and cumulative number stabilizes between 100 and 130 from 1000 steps onwards. These findings highlight the importance of understanding early hunting dynamics and environmental carrying capacities in predicting long-term outcomes. By step 1000, the number of male prey animals is near zero. This trend suggests that under the given conditions, human hunting pressure and environmental constraints effectively drive megaherbivore populations toward extinction. By step 1,000, the majority of simulation runs reach near-zero values. The percentage of green patches in the simulation initially fluctuates around 32% but shows a sharp decline to approximately 23% at around 400 steps. After 1200 steps, the percentage stabilizes with minor fluctuations. This suggests that after an initial period of intense consumption or environmental stress leading to reduced vegetation, the system adapts or recovers.
