The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households begins at 90.0 at the start of the simulation. This value declines rapidly over the first 40 time steps, reaching a minimum of approximately 40.0 by step 40. This initial sharp decrease could be attributed to the households' initial strategies in managing their livestock in response to pasture conditions and resource constraints. After reaching this minimum, the average total number of livestock starts to increase gradually. By step 60, the value rises to around 50.0, and by step 80, it stabilizes at approximately 60.0. This gradual increase suggests that households are adapting their strategies, possibly due to learning processes or changes in pasture conditions, leading to a more sustainable management of their livestock. The final value remains stable around 60.0 until the end of the simulation at step 100. There is noticeable variability among the simulation runs, particularly after step 40, indicating that different learning strategies and risk attitudes lead to diverse outcomes in livestock management over time.
