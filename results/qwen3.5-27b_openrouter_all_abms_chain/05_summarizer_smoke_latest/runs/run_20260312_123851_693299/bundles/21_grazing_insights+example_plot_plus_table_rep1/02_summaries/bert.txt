The rage model is a multi - agent simulation model that captures feedbacks between pastures, livestock and household livelihood in a common property grazing system. Three new behavioural types are created (which cannot be combined with the original ones) : e - ro (baseline behaviour), e - lbd (learning - by - doing) and e - ro - sl1 (social learning). The r - parameter is catch - all for various factors that form an agent's disposition to act in a certain way, such as : uncertainty in the sensing (partial observability of the resource system), noise in the information received, or an inherent characteristic of the agent, for instance, their risk attitude. The reduction in biomass during this initial phase suggests that livestock grazing pressure exceeded the vegetation regrowth rate, potentially driven by households maintaining high herd sizes relative to pasture capacity in the early years of the simulation. This surge in biomass accumulation indicates that the system is regenerating faster than it is being consumed, likely due to the behavioral strategies of the learning and baseline agents adjusting their herd sizes in response to resource states. Following this trough, the trajectory shifts around time step 40, entering a recovery phase characterized by a steady increase in livestock numbers until the end of the simulation at time step 100. Statistical analysis indicates a distinct change in trend around time step 20, marking the transition from decline to stabilization, and another shift at time step 55, where the rate of increase moderates into a slower growth phase. For decision makers, the data highlights a critical adjustment period within the first 40 years where resource pressures are highest, followed by a system resilience that supports gradual herd rebuilding, though long - term stability remains sensitive to individual household variations. The wide range of trajectories among individual simulation runs (visible in the data spread) implies that while the system - wide trend is toward stabilization, individual household outcomes vary significantly based on their specific risk attitudes and behavioral strategies.
