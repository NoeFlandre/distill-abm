The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits an initial rapid increase from a starting value of 5.29 to 1456.60 by time step 32. Policy interventions targeting the substitution of milk types may have the most significant impact during the early stages of adoption, where the rate of change is highest. The data indicates a dominant oscillation period of 15 time steps, suggesting cyclical patterns in consumer behavior. Decision makers should note the initial rapid adoption of lower-fat milk options, a period of stabilization where consumption habits appeared entrenched, and a subsequent resurgence in preference for skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Statistical analysis confirms a consistently increasing trend across all rolling windows (steps 0–4 through 25–29), with significant Tau values of 1.0 and p-values of 0.027. For decision-makers, this persistent and growing divergence implies that agents’ milk choices increasingly fail to reflect their perceived health priorities over time.
