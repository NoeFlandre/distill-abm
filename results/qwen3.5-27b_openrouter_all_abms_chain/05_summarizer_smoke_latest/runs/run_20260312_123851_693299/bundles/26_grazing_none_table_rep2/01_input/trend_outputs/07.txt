The distribution of healthy livestock across the household population begins with near-zero inequality at the initial time step, valued at approximately 0.0000. Throughout the 100-time-step simulation, the metric demonstrates a consistent and statistically significant upward trend, reaching a maximum value of 0.0726 at the final time step. The average inequality over the duration is 0.0302. Structural shifts in the rate of inequality accumulation are observed at specific intervals. Inflection points occur at time steps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, 37, and 57. Subsequently, change points are identified at time steps 51 and 71, with the final change point coinciding with the end of the simulation at time step 100. Rolling trend analyses indicate an increasing trajectory across all decadal windows from start to finish. Additionally, indicators of oscillation are present between time steps 78 and 93, suggesting localized variability within the broader increasing trend. Contextually, this divergence in livestock inequality arises from the interactions between three distinct behavioral types (E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1) and heterogeneous risk attitudes among the 60 households. While the system starts with a balanced distribution of assets, the combination of learning-by-doing, social learning, and varying risk dispositions leads to differentiated decision-making outcomes regarding herd placement and management. The gradual rise in the Gini coefficient indicates that these behavioral differences and environmental uncertainties cause household livelihood outcomes to become increasingly stratified over the long term.
