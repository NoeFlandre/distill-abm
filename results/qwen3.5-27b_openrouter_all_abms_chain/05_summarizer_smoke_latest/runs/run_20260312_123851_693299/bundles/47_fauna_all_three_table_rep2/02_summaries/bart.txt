The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival. Statistical validation via rolling Mann-Kendall tests confirms a robust negative trend across the entire timeline. This trajectory implies a systemic energy drain where expenditure consistently exceeds caloric intake from hunting. The configuration of 20 foragers results in a state where costs systematically outweigh gains, suggesting that without external subsidies, the forager group would face energetic collapse. The cumulative number of births begins at 0.0000 and exhibits a steady upward trend during the initial phase of the simulation. A significant increasing trend is observed between time steps 696 and 1391. This period reflects the initial reproductive expansion of the megaherbivore population within the prehistoric landscape. The aggregate mean peaks at approximately 240.33 deaths around time step 1876. The female megaherbivore population initiates at 125 individuals and terminates at zero, signaling complete extinction within the observed simulation window of 2311 ticks. For decision makers, these dynamics highlight that moderate predation pressure combined with high environmental seasonality can precipitate rapid extinction. Management strategies should focus on mitigating hunting pressure during early phases to prevent crossing this irreversible threshold. The combined energy available in the female prey population initiates at 12,500.00 units at the beginning of the simulation. This rapid reduction suggests that initial environmental constraints or predation pressure significantly deplete the energy reserves of the female cohort. By the 2311th step, the energy level is zero, marking functional extinction.
