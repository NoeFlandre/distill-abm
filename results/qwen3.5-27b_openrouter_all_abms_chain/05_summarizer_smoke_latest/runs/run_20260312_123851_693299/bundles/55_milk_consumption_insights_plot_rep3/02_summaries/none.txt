Context and Goals:

The overall objective of the agent-based model is to reproduce adoption behaviours of milk consumption by the British public (1974-2005), by replicating individual preferences and decision factors. Specifically, the goal of the model is to explore the influence of perception, habits and social influence in an individual's decision-making process of milk choice. The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against observed macro level data.

In particular, the study conducts an experiment to compare the performance of two model variants in reproducing overserved milk consumption trends. These variants present different mechanisms for how agents become disposed to consider their choices, representing a threshold based, and a probability-based approach. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence, and finally, choice evaluation where agents consider the impact of their choices and may adjust their future decisions.

The agents in the model represent adult consumers who occupy a random position in an information environment. Each agent has a disposition to consider alternative milk choices. Two disposition mechanisms are tested in the model, a threshold-based approach, and a probability-based approach. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. These are computed at the initialisation of the simulation and then calculated at each time step. An agent's milk choice function is modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. Agents ascribe different relative importance to each constituent part of the choice function (health factors and environmental factors). If the disposition requirement has been met, consumption of each milk type is split proportionately by the size of each choice function, modulated by the other influences. If not, agents keep their existing choice.

Agents (n=1,000) start with an existing choice based on the dominant position of whole milk versus skimmed varieties in 1974 (start year of the data). All agents are part of a social network. Each agent in the network can sense and be influenced by the choice function of each milk alternative for other agents in their network. Links between agents are unidirectional, and influence occurs as a function of interaction probability, with the degree of influence characterised by agent susceptibility. Social norms are globally perceived by agents and impact the weightings of the choice function.

The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent begins at approximately 2600 units during the initial time steps, specifically from time step 0 to 2. From time step 2 to 10, there is a steep decline in consumption, dropping from around 2600 units to approximately 900 units. This sharp decrease suggests a rapid shift in consumer preferences, potentially driven by changes in perceived health benefits, environmental concerns, or social influences. After time step 10, the decline continues but at a more moderate pace. By time step 20, consumption levels stabilize around 800 units, indicating a plateau in the rate of substitution. Between time steps 20 and 30, consumption fluctuates slightly but remains relatively stable, hovering around 600 to 800 units. This period of stability suggests that the market has reached a new equilibrium with a lower preference for whole milk. Finally, from time step 30 onwards, there is a noticeable drop again, bringing consumption down to approximately 300 units by the end of the simulation. This final decline could indicate a renewed shift in consumer behavior or external factors influencing milk choices. The overall trend shows a significant and sustained reduction in whole milk consumption over the simulation period, reflecting a broader societal shift towards alternative milk types. These insights highlight the importance of considering long-term behavioral changes and external influences when planning strategies for milk consumption patterns.

Decision-makers can leverage these insights to tailor public health campaigns that capitalize on existing trends and further promote healthier milk alternatives.

The mean value health deviation metric exhibits three distinct phases over the simulation period. Initially, from step 0 to step 10, the metric rises steeply, increasing from approximately 0.0 to a value near 0.52. This rapid ascent suggests that in the early simulation stages, agents' milk choices increasingly diverge from their perceived health attributes, indicating a period of significant adjustment or misalignment in decision-making. Between steps 10 and 27, the metric enters a phase of stability, fluctuating narrowly around 0.55 to 0.60. This plateau implies a temporary equilibrium where the divergence between health perceptions and actual consumption choices remains relatively constant. Finally, from step 27 to step 32, the metric resumes an upward trend, climbing from approximately 0.60 to over 0.70. This final increase indicates a growing inconsistency between what agents perceive as healthy and their selected milk types, potentially reflecting evolving external influences or habituation effects. For decision makers, these trends highlight critical periods where interventions targeting health perception or habit reinforcement could effectively align consumer choices with health goals, particularly during the initial adoption phase and the later stage of increasing divergence.

The mean value environmental deviation exhibits a sharp increase from step 0, starting at 0, and rapidly climbing to approximately 12.2 by step 5. Following this initial surge, the deviation stabilizes, reaching a plateau around 13.3 by step 10. From step 10 through step 32, the values remain consistent, fluctuating minimally between 13.2 and 13.4. This trajectory indicates that agents initially experience a significant misalignment between their held environmental values and the implied values of their chosen milk types, which resolves into a steady state of deviation within the first 10 time steps. For decision-makers, this suggests that the decision-making model parameters result in agents quickly settling into a stable behavior pattern where environmental considerations are consistently separated from their actual consumption choices, rather than gradually adapting to align better with their internal values.
