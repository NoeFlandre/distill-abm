Analysis of the male megaherbivore removal dynamics indicates a non-linear response to foraging pressure over the simulation horizon. The aggregate removal count initiates at zero and follows a trajectory of rapid initial accumulation, sustaining a significant positive trend for the initial phases. Statistical tests confirm this upward pressure is significant (p=0.000) across the first five 231-tick windows. The aggregate mean peak removal value reaches 120.17 units at tick 1876. Following this peak, the trajectory indicates a statistical shift. Between ticks 1160 and 1855, the trend significance wavers, indicating a plateau phase. In the final analyzed window (ticks 1856-2087), the trend tests register a statistically significant decreasing signal in the rate of accumulation. In the context of an ensemble of simulations, this signal suggests increasing divergence in population survival, where runs experiencing earlier extirpation affect the mean trajectory as the simulation horizon extends. Temporal autocorrelation analysis detects a dominant oscillation period of 63 time steps, reflecting the environmental seasonality and forage regeneration cycles influencing hunter success rates. The high standard deviation (32.52) relative to the mean (100.39) highlights substantial sensitivity to stochastic events. For management and conservation planning, the concentration of kill events within the first 1876 steps (representing the peak pressure point) signals a critical window of vulnerability for the herd. The results suggest that without mitigation of forager pressure, the male sub-population faces a critical bottleneck early in the simulation. The peak pressure point signals the window of maximum vulnerability for the herd before environmental constraints or resource depletion naturally dampens the hunting rate.
