Context and Goals

This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. In this version, each farmer stays on their own plot of land. They decide how many animals to keep based on how the grass is doing and what others are doing. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. There are three main ways farmers behave: baseline (E-RO), learning from their own results (E-LBD), and learning from neighbors (E-RO-SL1). The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock.

The average initial risk attitude of all social learning agents remains constant at 0.0074 across all 100 time steps. This metric, which reflects the agents' initial tendency to deviate from recommended livestock numbers, shows no variability or trend. The lack of change implies that, under the specified model parameters, social learning agents maintain a consistent initial disposition toward risk throughout the simulation. For decision-makers, this suggests that interventions aimed at modifying risk attitudes through social learning would not be necessary in this specific configuration, as the initial predisposition remains stable.

The average household risk attitude exhibits a sharp increase during the initial phase, rising from an initial value of approximately 0.007 at step 0 to a maximum peak of 0.381 around step 33. This period of significant growth is followed by a distinct downturn starting immediately after step 33, where the metric declines to a lower stable range. The data indicates an average value of 0.233 with a standard deviation of 0.094, reflecting substantial variability among the simulated households. By the conclusion of the simulation at step 100, the mean risk attitude settles at approximately 0.191. The trend reveals a clear shift from increasing risk tolerance in the early stages to a correction phase in the middle years, followed by a period of relative stability. This trajectory implies that households initially adapt their grazing strategies to take on more risk, likely expanding livestock numbers, before adjusting towards more conservative or stable risk management behaviors as the system evolves.

From step 50 onwards, the rate of increase slowed, and the system approached a stable plateau. The statistical analysis confirms a significant decreasing trend in the initial period, followed by a sustained significant increasing trend for the remainder of the simulation. This pattern indicates that the initial stocking levels resulted in overgrazing, but the subsequent behavioral adjustments by the agents led to a restoration of pasture capacity and a state of sustainable biomass levels.

The long-term stabilization at a low level implies that the system has reached a dynamic equilibrium where resource limitations are managed effectively, preventing significant loss of livestock.

The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock starts at 0.0 at timestep 1 and rises steadily to 0.0726 by timestep 100, with an overall mean of 0.0302 and a standard deviation of 0.0239. The increase is significant, as confirmed by rolling Mann-Kendall tests across all time windows (tau=1.0, p=0.000, slope=0.001). The trajectory shows an inflection around timestep 51, where the rate of increase accelerates, and another at timestep 71, with the final value reaching its peak at timestep 100. Oscillations are observed in the range of timesteps 78 to 93, though the trend remains strongly upward. This trend suggests that inequality in healthy livestock holdings among households grows consistently over time, becoming most pronounced in the later stages of the simulation. For decision makers, this indicates that without intervention, disparities in livestock health outcomes will naturally widen, potentially undermining community resilience and equitable development.

Initially, no households engaged in social learning. Starting around step 7, the number of households adopting social learning behaviors began to increase rapidly, with a significant rise observed between steps 22 and 28. By step 33, the rate of increase slowed, and the count stabilized around 30 households by step 57. Towards the end of the simulation, the number of households with social learning behaviors reached a steady state around 32 households. These trends suggest that social learning spreads quickly among households initially but eventually plateaus as the potential adopters are exhausted. Decision makers should focus on promoting social learning early on to maximize its spread and impact.

The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households showed a consistently increasing trend over the 100 time steps, rising from an initial value of zero to a final value of 96.78. The rate of increase was most pronounced during the first 25 time steps, with a sharp rise observed before the slope began to moderate. Between time steps 25 and 60, the growth trajectory remained steady but at a reduced pace compared to the initial period. From time step 60 to the end of the simulation, the cumulative destocking continued to climb linearly. Throughout the simulation, the data exhibited no significant oscillations or declines, indicating that destocking was a unidirectional process for the population. These patterns suggest that households progressively reduced their livestock numbers over time, potentially in response to environmental constraints or management strategies aimed at sustaining pasture health.
