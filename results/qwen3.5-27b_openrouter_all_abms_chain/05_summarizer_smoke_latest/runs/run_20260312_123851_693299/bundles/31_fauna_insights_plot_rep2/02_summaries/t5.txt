The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Initial rise suggests high hunting efficiency and a strong initial foraging pressure on male prey. After step 500, the rate of increase slows, and the cumulative number stabilizes between 110 and 125 by step 2000. This stabilization indicates that hunting pressure on postpartum females either saturates or that the male population becomes depleted, limiting further kills. By step 600, most simulation runs show the female prey count stabilizing at extremely low levels, typically below 5 individuals. After this initial phase, the population remains critically low, with no observable recovery or significant fluctuation up to step 2000. This trend suggests a high degree of sensitivity in the model's dynamics to the interplay of hunting pressure, resource availability, and demographic processes.
