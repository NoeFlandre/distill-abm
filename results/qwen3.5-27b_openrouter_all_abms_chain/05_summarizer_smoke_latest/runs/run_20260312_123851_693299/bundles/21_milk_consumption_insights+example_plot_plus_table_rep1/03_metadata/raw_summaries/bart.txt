 The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards . Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice . An agent's milk choice function is modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices .  Average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5.29 units per person at the start of the simulation, representing the low market penetration of lower-fat milk options in 1974 . A rapid and steep increase occurs over the first four time steps, reaching an inflection point around time step 4 where consumption values exceed 600 units .  The average weekly consumption of whole milk per person begins at approximately 2,655 units and exhibits a sharp decline through the early stages of the simulation . By step 12, the decline moderates, stabilizing around 900 units before resuming a downward trajectory . The simulation concludes with whole milk consumption at its lowest recorded level, approximately 253 units by step 32, indicating a significant and sustained transition away from whole milk .  The consistent upward trend over the entire simulation period implies that the mechanisms for choice evaluation and social influence are effective in driving agents towards more distinct preferences over time . Decision-makers can interpret this as a sign that interventions targeting social influence or habit formation could lead to significant shifts in consumer behavior .
