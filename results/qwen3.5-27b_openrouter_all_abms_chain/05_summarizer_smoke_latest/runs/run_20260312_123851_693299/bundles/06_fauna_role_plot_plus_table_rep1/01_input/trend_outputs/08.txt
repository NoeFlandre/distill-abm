The simulation demonstrates a robust trajectory of male prey population decline leading to extinction. The mean population begins at 125 individuals and follows a steep downward curve, exhibiting a high standard deviation of approximately 36 during the initial phase, which indicates significant variance in extinction timing across the different simulation runs. The population mean decreases rapidly until the 390th time step, marking the first major inflection point where the rate of decline shifts. The Mann-Kendall statistical test confirms a strictly decreasing trend during the first 755 time steps, with a significant negative slope. As the simulation progresses, the rate of decline attenuates; between time steps 755 and 1262, the population hovers near critical low levels before dropping closer to zero. The mean population value remains extremely low after the 1262nd time step, with the trend eventually becoming statistically non-significant as the population stabilizes near extinction. The aggregate data shows a median population of only 1.35 individuals throughout the duration of the simulation, highlighting that in the majority of time steps, the male population in most simulation runs had already been eliminated. The final count reaches zero, signifying the total collapse of the male prey sub-population under the applied hunting and environmental parameters.
