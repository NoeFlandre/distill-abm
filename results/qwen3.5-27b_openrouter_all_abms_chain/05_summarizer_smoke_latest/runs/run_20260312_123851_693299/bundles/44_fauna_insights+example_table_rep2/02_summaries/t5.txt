The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted begins at zero. The metric rises steadily, reaching approximately 118.5 by step 1,208. This phase reflects active foraging behavior where hunters successfully target male ungulates. Short-term oscillations with a dominant period of 50 ticks are observed around indices 625 to 690 and 785 to 810. These fluctuations align with the model's seasonality parameter. The shift from an increasing to a decreasing trend suggests that hunting pressure or internal mortality eventually outweighs reproductive gains. Between time steps 700 and 1000, the population fluctuates within a low range, reaching local peaks of approximately 6.5 at step 730. These fluctuations align with the observed oscillation period of 63 ticks, potentially reflecting seasonal variations in fodder density that temporarily buffer mortality rates. The total energy available in all prey animals begins at 16,666.67 units at the start of the simulation. This ultimate reduction indicates a complete depletion of the prey population, resulting in total extinction within the model environment.
