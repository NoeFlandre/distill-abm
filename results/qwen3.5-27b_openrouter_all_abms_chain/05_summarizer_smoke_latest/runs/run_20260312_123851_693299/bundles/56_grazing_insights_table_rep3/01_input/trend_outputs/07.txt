The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households demonstrates a consistent upward trajectory throughout the simulation period. Initially, the Gini coefficient starts at effectively zero, indicating perfect equality in herd sizes among the households at the outset. Over the course of 100 time steps, this metric rises steadily to a final value of 0.0726. Statistical analysis confirms a significant increasing trend across all decadal windows, with correlation coefficients remaining near unity, suggesting a robust divergence in economic outcomes among the agents.

Early in the simulation, several inflection points occur at time steps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, 37, and 57, indicating periods where the rate of change in inequality shifts as households adjust their livestock management strategies under varying risk attitudes and learning mechanisms. Structural change points are observed at time steps 51 and 71, marking transitions in how inequality accumulates during the mid-to-late phases of the run. Towards the end of the simulation, between time steps 78 and 93, oscillation indices suggest minor volatility, yet the overall trajectory remains positive.

This divergence implies that even when households begin with identical assets and land rights, the interaction of divergent behavioral strategies and risk sensitivities leads to stratification. Decision makers should note that private property rights combined with varying decision-making approaches do not guarantee equitable outcomes over time. Interventions focusing on leveling access to information or risk mitigation support may be necessary to prevent the accumulation of livestock wealth in the hands of fewer households, as natural dynamics appear to drive inequality upward without external stabilization.
