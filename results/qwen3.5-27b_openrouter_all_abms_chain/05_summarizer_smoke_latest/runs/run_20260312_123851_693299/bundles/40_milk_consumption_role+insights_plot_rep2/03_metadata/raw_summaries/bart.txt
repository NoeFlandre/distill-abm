 Model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms .  The data exhibits a steady upward trend in the cumulative number of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choices . The tight clustering of the simulation runs indicates high stability in these decision patterns . For decision makers, this implies that once a specific behavior is adopted by a critical mass of the population (around step 10), it becomes a dominant habit that persists and scales linearly over time .  The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 1.0 at the initial time step . This progressive rise indicates that agents' tendencies toward choosing skimmed milk strengthen over time . For decision-makers, this suggests that early interventions promoting these milk types may have compounding effects as habits solidify .
