 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore . It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival . The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape .  The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands begins at zero and rises sharply during the initial 500 time steps, reaching approximately 100 individuals in most simulation runs . The rate of increase slows significantly between 400 and 800 steps as the curves approach an asymptote, stabilizing between 110 and 125 individuals across most runs . After step 1500, the cumulative count remains nearly constant, with minimal additional hunting observed .  The number of male prey animals begins at approximately 125 in all simulation runs and declines sharply over the first 400 time steps . By step 750, the population in most runs drops below 10, and by step 1000, nearly all simulations approach extinction levels . The trajectory suggests that under these conditions, male prey populations are highly vulnerable and unable to sustain themselves .  By step 750, the total energy has diminished to near zero, indicating the complete energetic collapse of the male population . This collapse precedes total extinction in the population, as individuals lose the capacity for mobility and reproduction before perishing . For decision makers, this trend highlights the critical vulnerability of male megaherbivores to energetic stress . Management strategies aimed at preserving energy intake could be crucial in preventing this trajectory .
