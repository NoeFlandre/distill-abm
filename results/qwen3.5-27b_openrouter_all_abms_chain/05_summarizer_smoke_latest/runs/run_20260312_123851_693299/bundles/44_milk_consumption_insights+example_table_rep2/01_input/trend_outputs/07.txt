The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per person begins at 5.29 units at time step 0, reflecting the initial market dominance of whole milk. A rapid upward trajectory occurs during the first ten time steps, with significant increasing trends identified up to step 9. This initial surge indicates that agents quickly respond to perceived health and environmental characteristics of alternative milk types.

Between time steps 10 and 24, the growth stabilizes, with no significant trend detected in rolling analyses. Values oscillate during this period, reaching local peaks at step 11 (1265.47) and step 13 (1240.35), and dipping to valleys at step 18 (1183.39) and step 20 (1214.46). This plateau suggests a temporary equilibrium where habit strength and cognitive dissonance thresholds counteract the drive for substitution. Agents may be re-evaluating their choices without committing to a net increase in alternative consumption.

From time step 25 onwards, an increasing trend resumes, culminating in a maximum value of 1456.60 units at the final time step of 32. This final rise indicates that long-term social norms and cumulative influence eventually overcome initial habit resistance, successfully replicating the historical shift away from whole milk. For decision makers, the simulation suggests that while initial campaigns can trigger rapid adoption, sustaining growth requires mechanisms to break through habit inertia during stabilization periods. The distinct phases of growth, plateau, and resurgence imply that policy interventions should be adapted to the maturity of the adoption curve.
