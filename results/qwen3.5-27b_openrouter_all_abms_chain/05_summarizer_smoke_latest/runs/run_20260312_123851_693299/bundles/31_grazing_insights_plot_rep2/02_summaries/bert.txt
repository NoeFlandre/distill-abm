The rage model is a multi - agent simulation model that captures feedbacks between pastures, livestock and household livelihood in a common property grazing system. Three new behavioural types are created (which cannot be combined with the original ones) : e - ro (baseline behaviour), e - lbd (learning - by - doing) and e - ro - sl1 (social learning). The r - parameter is catch - all for various factors that form an agent's disposition to act in a certain way, such as : uncertainty in the sensing (partial observability of the resource system), noise in the information received, or an inherent characteristic of the agent, for instance, their risk attitude. The mean household initial risk attitude across all social learning agents (e - ro - sl1) remains constant throughout the 100 simulation time steps. Within the first 30 time steps, a slight decline occurs, with the lowest point reaching approximately 80, 000 units around time step 30. From this point, a sustained and significant increase in biomass is observed, accelerating sharply between time steps 40 and 60, during which levels rise from roughly 95, 000 to over 120, 000 units. By time step 80, the growth rate slows as the biomass levels off, reaching a plateau between 110, 000 and 128, 000 units by the final time step of 100. These trends suggest that initial resource depletion transitions into a strong recovery phase, eventually stabilizing at a high equilibrium level, indicating that the system supports effective biomass regeneration under the modeled behavioral dynamics. After timestep 40, a divergence occurs : some household groups show a gradual recovery, with livestock numbers rising between 45 and 50 by timestep 60 and stabilizing thereafter between 48 and 52 through timestep 100. These trends suggest that, while most households reduce their livestock numbers early on, certain learning behaviors may allow specific groups to partially recover herd sizes in the later stages of the simulation. This trajectory suggests that while resource scarcity forces significant livestock liquidation early in the simulation, the system adapts over time, leading to a stabilization of herd management strategies and reduced loss due to insufficient resources. This implies that while some households manage to maintain a relatively equitable distribution of livestock wealth, others experience substantial stratification, suggesting that learning behaviors (specifically e - ro, e - lbd, and e - ro - sl1 under these parameters) combined with risk attitudes and ecological noise can lead to varied social outcomes. The number of households engaging in social learning exhibits a rapid increase in the initial phase of the simulation, followed by a stabilization period. The data suggests that social learning adoption happens quickly within the first two decades of the simulation period and becomes entrenched, with the population converging to a stable state of adoption levels relatively early. The rapid increase in the initial phase suggests a high initial rate of destocking, which gradually slows over time for some households while continuing to rise for others. Between time steps 20 and 40, there is a noticeable divergence in trends : some simulations show sustained high numbers around 30 - 40 households, whereas others continue to decline, stabilizing around 5 - 15 households. After time step 40, the number of households following the e - ro strategy tends to stabilize in most simulations, with some maintaining levels between 20 - 25 households and others settling at lower values around 5 - 10 households.
