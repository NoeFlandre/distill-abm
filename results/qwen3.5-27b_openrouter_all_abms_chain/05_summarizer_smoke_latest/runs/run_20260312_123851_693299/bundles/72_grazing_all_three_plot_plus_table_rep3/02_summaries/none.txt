Context and Goals

This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. In this version, each farmer stays on their own plot of land. They decide how many animals to keep based on how the grass is doing and what others are doing. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. There are three main ways farmers behave: baseline (E-RO), learning from their own results (E-LBD), and learning from neighbors (E-RO-SL1). The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock.

The average initial risk attitude for social learning agents remains fixed at approximately 0.0074 throughout the entire simulation duration. This metric exhibits zero variance, with no fluctuations, peaks, or trend shifts observed from the first to the last time step. This static behavior indicates that the agents' inherent predisposition to deviate from livestock recommendations was established as a constant initialization parameter and was not subjected to dynamic updating or modification by the model's learning mechanisms during the 100 time steps. For decision makers, this stability implies that the initial behavioral calibration of the agents remains the sole determinant of their risk baseline, as no internal adaptation occurs to alter this specific trait over time.

The average household risk attitude begins at approximately 0.0074 and exhibits a distinct upward trajectory during the initial phase of the simulation. A rapid and statistically significant increase occurs between time steps 0 and 10, with the rate of change slowing slightly but remaining positive through time step 32. The metric reaches its maximum observed value of approximately 0.3814 around time step 33, indicating a peak in the population's overall willingness to accept risk. This initial growth phase suggests that farmers, driven by their specific behavioral strategies—ranging from reinforcement learning to social learning—are initially adjusting their herd sizes upward, likely responding to early positive feedback loops regarding pasture availability or livestock accumulation. Following this peak, the trend reverses significantly. From time step 33 to roughly time step 65, the average risk attitude shows a consistent downward trend, declining from the peak of 0.3814 to approximately 0.1911. This period of decline suggests a system-wide correction where risk aversion increases, potentially triggered by the accumulation of livestock leading to resource strain or negative outcomes from overgrazing. After time step 66, the statistical significance of the trend diminishes, and the metric stabilizes within a narrower range, oscillating around values between 0.1 and 0.2 for the remainder of the simulation. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of early intervention; the sharp rise in risk-taking behavior in the first 30 years indicates a period of high potential for pasture degradation, requiring monitoring or extension support to guide farmers toward sustainable stock levels before the system naturally corrects toward risk aversion.

The average reserve biomass across all patches begins at 90,000 units and experiences a decline in the initial phase, reaching a minimum of approximately 84,114 units by time step 26. This early reduction suggests that initial grazing pressure from the livestock, combined with the behavioral strategies of the farmers, led to a net consumption of biomass exceeding regeneration rates during the first 25 to 30 steps. Specifically, the decreasing trend is statistically significant during this period, indicating a consistent depletion of reserves across simulation runs. Following this initial drawdown, a brief stabilization or slight fluctuation occurs between time steps 26 and 40 before the system shifts decisively. 

From time step 40 onwards, the reserve biomass undergoes a rapid and substantial increase, accelerating most sharply between time steps 50 and 60. This growth phase is driven by a significant reduction in grazing pressure relative to grass growth, allowing the system to recover. The trend becomes strongly positive from time step 33 through 98, with the steepest rate of increase observed in the middle period. The biomass levels surpass the initial starting value around time step 55. By time step 70, the rate of increase slows considerably as the system approaches a new equilibrium, with the average reserve biomass stabilizing between 120,000 and 128,000 units for the remainder of the simulation. The final average value recorded is approximately 121,072 units. 

The wide divergence of individual trajectories in the later stages, particularly after time step 60, reflects the influence of heterogeneous farmer behaviors and stochastic factors, such as local rainfall variations, despite the global rainfall setting. For decision makers, the initial decline indicates a critical vulnerability window in the first year where pasture degradation is most likely if stocking rates are not adjusted. However, the system's strong recovery demonstrates that the current behavioral mix allows for self-correction, eventually leading to a state of high resource availability that exceeds the starting conditions. The divergence suggests that policy interventions should focus on supporting farmers during the early depletion phase to prevent irreversible land degradation or economic loss before the system naturally stabilizes.

The average number of livestock destocked by households begins at zero and spikes sharply to a maximum of 6.61 animals at time step 3, reflecting an initial period of intense resource stress. This surge likely results from the immediate overgrazing that occurs as the system starts with a high initial livestock count of 90 and farmers operating under the E-RO behavioral mode, who base decisions on recent past performance without anticipating future degradation. Following this peak, the destocking metric declines rapidly, falling to approximately 1.47 by time step 17, indicating that the initial shock to the system has prompted widespread herd liquidation. 

From time steps 22 through 65, the destocking rate fluctuates within a low range, oscillating between roughly 0.8 and 1.2 animals, with no statistically significant overall trend, suggesting the system has settled into a phase of minor, reactive adjustments. However, starting around time step 66, the trend shifts to a statistically significant increase, stabilizing at slightly higher levels between 0.5 and 0.8 animals through the end of the simulation. This late-stage uptick implies that despite the initial herd reductions, resource pressure has re-emerged, possibly due to the recovery of forage biomass leading to herd expansion that again outpaces available resources. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of early intervention; the sharp initial spike in destocking is the primary indicator of system strain, while the eventual stability suggests that households can eventually adapt their herd sizes to the carrying capacity, albeit with persistent low-level stress.

The coefficient remains low, never exceeding 0.073, indicating that while inequality is growing, the distribution of healthy livestock remains relatively equitable among households. Multiple inflection points are detected early in the simulation, suggesting shifts in the underlying dynamics driving inequality. For decision makers, the consistent rise in inequality suggests that without intervention, differences in livestock health outcomes between households will widen over time. This highlights the need for support mechanisms, such as shared grazing practices or early warning systems, to maintain equity in livestock health outcomes.

The number of households practicing social learning starts at zero across all simulation runs. A rapid adoption phase occurs between time steps 5 and 25, where the average count of households engaging in social learning surges from near zero to approximately 40. This rapid accumulation suggests that once a threshold of information or successful outcomes is observed by the initial E-RO households, social learning spreads quickly among the remaining farmers. The rate of adoption slows significantly after time step 25, with the curve continuing to climb more gradually until it begins to plateau around time step 40. By time step 60, the average number of households engaging in social learning stabilizes at approximately 50 out of 60 total households. The simulation ends with the metric maintaining this plateau, indicating that the vast majority of the farming population has shifted to social learning. The variance among individual simulation runs is highest during the early adoption phase, reflecting stochastic differences in which neighbors influence whom first. However, this variance converges as the system stabilizes, showing that the long-term state is largely independent of initial random fluctuations. Decision-makers can expect that introducing social learning mechanisms will result in rapid system-wide adoption, typically stabilizing within the first half of the planning horizon.
