The megafaunal hunting pressure model (mhpm) is an interactive, agent - based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival. Context : the model simulates a socio - ecological system containing human foragers and sexually dimorphic megaherbivore herds. It incorporates principles of optimal foraging theory, where hunters make decisions based on energy costs and food value. Goals : the primary goal is to determine conditions under which human hunting pressure, combined with environmental constraints, leads to the extinction of megafauna. The number of active human hunter - gatherer bands in the simulation is recorded at exactly 20 from the initial time step through the final recorded time step. This absolute stability occurs because the simulation configuration explicitly defines the forager agents as immortal, preventing any population loss due to mortality. This constant human population implies that hunting pressure on the megafauna remains uniform over time, meaning that extinction dynamics will be driven solely by the interaction of this fixed predation rate with prey reproduction rates and resource availability, rather than fluctuations in hunter numbers. The total energy of foragers starts at approximately zero and shows a steady decline throughout the simulation. This trend continues, with the energy reaching approximately - 10, 000 by step 1, 000 and further decreasing to around - 20, 000 by step 1, 500. By the end of the simulation at 73, 000 ticks, the cumulative number of males killed ranges from approximately 110 to 125, indicating that the population of male prey reached a point where further hunting yielded diminishing returns. The cumulative number of female prey animals killed starts at zero and rises sharply, reaching approximately 80 by step 200 and continuing to increase at a slightly decelerated rate until about step 500, where it reaches approximately 100. This deceleration implies a stabilization of the predation dynamics, possibly due to a reduction in prey availability or increased energy costs for foragers as the herd density decreases. For decision - makers, the rapid initial decline suggests that female prey are highly vulnerable in the early stages of interaction with forager populations, necessitating early conservation or management interventions if population sustainability is the goal. The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted begins at zero and increases rapidly within the first 400 time steps, reaching approximately 100 in most simulation runs. The final cumulative values range from 105 to 122, indicating that hunting pressure on postpartum females is concentrated in the early - to - mid phases of the simulation. The variability among runs, particularly between 500 and 1000 time steps, suggests stochastic elements in hunter - prey interactions or prey availability, which may affect the timing and intensity of hunting pressure. The stabilization of death counts by 1, 000 steps implies that the system has reached a state where few or no prey remain to die, reflecting conditions sufficient for near - total extinction under the given parameters. These findings highlight the model's sensitivity to early - stage pressures, and for decision - makers, emphasize the importance of mitigating hunting or environmental stressors during initial phases to prevent irreversible population loss. Between 400 and 800 time steps, the rate of decline slows, though the population continues to dwindle, indicating a period where the population stabilizes slightly under reduced predation pressure or increased resource availability. Variability across runs is highest during the initial 400 steps but diminishes significantly as the simulation progresses, converging toward complete population collapse. The consistent trajectory across simulations implies that under the current parameter settings, the socio - ecological system is not capable of sustaining a viable female megafauna population over time. Variability among simulation runs remains low during this period, suggesting deterministic dynamics driven by early predation events and resource limitations. The simulation ends with the total number of animals near zero across all runs, indicating that under these parameter settings, the combination of human hunting pressure and environmental constraints is sufficient to drive the megaherbivore population to extinction. By the final time steps, the total energy of the male population is consistently zero across all simulation runs, implying the complete extinction of male megaherbivores under the specified conditions. This total loss of energy and, by extension, the male population, implies that the current balance of hunting efficiency, reproductive rates, and environmental capacity is unsustainable, leading to the collapse of the male herd required for population recovery. The rapid depletion of energy implies that the female population, crucial for herd reproduction, was unable to sustain itself under the defined hunting intensity, leading to a swift and total population collapse. The total energy of all animals begins at approximately 25, 000 units and experiences a rapid decline over the first 500 time steps, dropping to around 5, 000 units. Decision - makers should note that while initial declines in prey energy are predictable, long - term outcomes depend heavily on the interplay of hunting intensity and environmental resource availability. This rapid reduction likely reflects intense foraging activity by the prey animals, potentially driven by seasonal resource availability and initial population dynamics. This pattern suggests periodic depletion events, possibly linked to cyclical foraging behaviors or localized resource exhaustion. These trends highlight the resilience of vegetation cover despite periodic foraging pressure, emphasizing the importance of regrowth time and grass proportion settings in maintaining long - term fodder availability for the megaherbivore population.
