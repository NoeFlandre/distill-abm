The number of female prey animals begins at approximately 125 in all simulation runs and declines sharply over the first 400 steps, reaching values below 20 in most cases. This rapid reduction suggests high mortality rates during the initial phase, likely driven by the combined effects of internal mortality, external mortality, and predation pressure from foragers. The decline continues more gradually between steps 400 and 1000, where the number of females stabilizes at very low levels, often below 5. By step 1500, nearly all runs show the female population at or near zero, indicating functional extinction of the breeding population. Variability across runs is highest during the initial 400 steps but diminishes significantly as the simulation progresses, converging toward complete population collapse. The consistent trajectory across simulations implies that under the current parameter settings, the socio-ecological system is not capable of sustaining a viable female megafauna population over time. Decision makers should consider that current predation and environmental parameters lead to rapid demographic collapse, highlighting the sensitivity of reproductive individuals to anthropogenic and natural pressures.
