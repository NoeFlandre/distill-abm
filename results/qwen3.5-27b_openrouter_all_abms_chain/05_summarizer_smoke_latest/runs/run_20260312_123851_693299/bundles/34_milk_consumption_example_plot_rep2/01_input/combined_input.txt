Context and Goals:

The overall objective of the agent-based model is to reproduce adoption behaviours of milk consumption by the British public (1974-2005), by replicating individual preferences and decision factors. Specifically, the goal of the model is to explore the influence of perception, habits and social influence in an individual's decision-making process of milk choice. The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against observed macro level data.

In particular, the study conducts an experiment to compare the performance of two model variants in reproducing overserved milk consumption trends. These variants present different mechanisms for how agents become disposed to consider their choices, representing a threshold based, and a probability-based approach. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence, and finally, choice evaluation where agents consider the impact of their choices and may adjust their future decisions.

The agents in the model represent adult consumers who occupy a random position in an information environment. Each agent has a disposition to consider alternative milk choices. Two disposition mechanisms are tested in the model, a threshold-based approach, and a probability-based approach. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. These are computed at the initialisation of the simulation and then calculated at each time step. An agent's milk choice function is modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. Agents ascribe different relative importance to each constituent part of the choice function (health factors and environmental factors). If the disposition requirement has been met, consumption of each milk type is split proportionately by the size of each choice function, modulated by the other influences. If not, agents keep their existing choice.

Agents (n=1,000) start with an existing choice based on the dominant position of whole milk versus skimmed varieties in 1974 (start year of the data). All agents are part of a social network. Each agent in the network can sense and be influenced by the choice function of each milk alternative for other agents in their network. Links between agents are unidirectional, and influence occurs as a function of interaction probability, with the degree of influence characterised by agent susceptibility. Social norms are globally perceived by agents and impact the weightings of the choice function.

The average weekly consumption of whole milk starts at approximately 2,650 units per person and remains stable during the first time step. From time step 1, consumption begins a steady decline, reaching around 1,200 units by time step 10. This rapid reduction suggests an initial phase where agents are actively reconsidering their milk choices, likely influenced by perceptions of health and environmental factors, as well as social norms promoting alternatives. By time step 15, consumption further decreases to approximately 900 units, indicating a continued shift away from whole milk. Between time steps 20 and 25, the decline slows, with consumption hovering around 700 units, reflecting a period of relative stability where habits and social influences may be reinforcing existing choices. A notable drop occurs around time step 28, where consumption falls sharply to below 500 units, potentially driven by a heightened awareness of health concerns or intensified social pressure to adopt alternatives. By the final time steps, consumption stabilizes at approximately 300 units, suggesting that the majority of agents have transitioned to alternative milk options. This trend aligns with the model's goal of capturing the substitution of whole milk with skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties over time.

The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at zero and remains at that level during the first time step. From time step 1, consumption rises sharply, reaching approximately 1,200 by time step 10. This rapid increase suggests that agents quickly adapt to the new milk options, influenced by the initial perception of health benefits and environmental factors. The growth rate slows between time steps 10 and 27, where consumption fluctuates around 1,200 to 1,250. This period may reflect a balance between habit formation and social influence, as agents adjust their choices based on previous decisions and interactions within their network. At time step 27, there is a notable rise in consumption, climbing to about 1,450 by time step 30. This uptick could be attributed to a cumulative effect of social norms and heightened susceptibility to social influence, driving more agents to adopt skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. By the end of the simulation, the consumption stabilizes near 1,450, indicating a significant shift in consumer preferences away from whole milk towards lower fat alternatives.

The total number of times agents chose whole milk begins at zero at the start of the simulation and increases linearly over time. By step 10, the cumulative count of whole milk choices is approximately 750, rising to around 1,500 by step 20. The simulation concludes with the total reaching between 2,000 and 2,300 by the final step, depending on the specific simulation run. This steady linear increase indicates that while agents may have initially held a preference for whole milk, the cumulative total of this choice consistently grew throughout the observation period, suggesting a persistent underlying demand for whole milk despite the historical shift towards semi-skimmed and skimmed varieties observed in macro-level data. The relatively low variance between simulation runs in the early steps widens slightly towards the end, reflecting the growing influence of individual differences in social susceptibility and habit formation as the simulation progresses.

The total number of times agents chose skimmed or semi-skimmed milk begins at zero and increases steadily throughout the simulation. In the initial steps, from step 0 to approximately step 5, the growth is minimal, with the number of choices remaining close to zero. From step 5 onwards, the number of choices increases at a linear and consistent rate. By step 10, the total number of choices reaches approximately 5,000, and this trend continues until step 30, where the total number of choices approaches 25,000. The steady increase reflects a growing adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk by agents over time, driven by social influence, habit, and the perception of health and environmental benefits. The model parameters, such as the probability of interaction (p-interact) and social susceptibility, suggest that these factors play a significant role in influencing the agents' decisions. The consistent linear growth indicates that the adoption process is stable and predictable over the course of the simulation.

The total number of milk choices made by all agents begins at zero at time step 0 and increases linearly as the simulation progresses. By time step 30, the total number of choices reaches approximately 25,000. The steady increase suggests that, on average, each of the 1,000 agents consistently makes a choice at every time step, or at a constant rate across the simulation duration. This indicates that the disposition mechanisms, whether threshold-based or probability-based, are effectively prompting agents to evaluate and select milk alternatives consistently over time.

The mean quantity of whole milk consumed by agents begins at approximately 2,600 units at the start of the simulation. A rapid decline is observed over the initial 10 time steps, with consumption dropping to approximately 1,000 units. This sharp decrease suggests that agents quickly shifted away from whole milk, likely due to evolving perceptions of health and environmental factors, as well as the influence of social norms favoring alternatives. From step 10 to around step 25, the decline in consumption continues at a slower, more gradual rate, stabilizing near 600 units. This slower phase may reflect agents adjusting their choices based on habit and social influence within the network. A more pronounced decline occurs between steps 25 and 30, where consumption falls sharply to approximately 300 units. This drop could indicate a tipping point in agent behavior, driven by stronger collective perceptions or heightened social conformity. The simulation concludes with consumption levels plateauing around 200 to 250 units, indicating a significant long-term shift in preferences away from whole milk.

The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed starts at 0 and increases rapidly until approximately step 11, reaching a peak value of around 1270. This initial rapid growth phase likely represents the period where agents begin to actively consider and adopt skimmed or semi-skimmed milk alternatives due to their initial perceptions of health and environmental characteristics, potentially amplified by early social influences. From step 11 to step 18, the mean quantity slightly declines and stabilizes around 1200. This plateau and minor decrease may indicate a temporary equilibrium where the rate of new adopters is balanced by those who maintain their current choices due to habit strength or insufficient perceived benefits to switch further. Between step 18 and step 27, the mean quantity fluctuates slightly but remains generally stable, hovering between 1200 and 1250. This period suggests a dynamic stability where social influence and evolving individual evaluations continue to impact choices, but not enough to cause significant shifts in aggregate consumption. From step 27 onwards, there is a distinct upward trend, culminating in a final value of approximately 1450 at step 32. This secondary increase could reflect a cumulative effect of social norms strengthening, agents overcoming higher habit thresholds as alternatives become more normalized, or a delayed response to the evolving perceptions of milk alternatives. The overall trajectory shows a transition from low consumption to significantly higher levels, with an initial surge, a period of consolidation, and a final phase of increased adoption.

The mean habit factor for whole milk begins at 1.00 and increases sharply to approximately 1.02 during the first time step. This immediate jump is indicative of the high initial incumbent habit value, which reinforces the existing preference for whole milk as agents start with established consumption patterns. Following this initial rise, the factor enters a phase of slow, steady growth. The factor increases linearly from 1.02 at time step 1 to approximately 1.05 by time step 32. This gradual upward trend reflects the accumulation of habit strength through repeated choice confirmation, suggesting that agents who continue to consume whole milk reinforce their existing behavior over time. The variance among simulation runs remains relatively low throughout the observed period, though the spread widens slightly as the simulation progresses, indicating consistent habit formation dynamics across different runs with minor deviations.

The mean habit factor for skimmed/semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 1.0 at time step 0. A slight increase occurs between steps 0 and 2, reaching a value of around 1.02. The factor remains relatively stable, hovering near 1.02, until approximately time step 6. From time step 7 onwards, a consistent upward trend is observed. This increase accelerates through time step 10, where the factor reaches approximately 1.1. By time step 20, the factor has risen to roughly 1.38. The growth continues at a slightly slower rate towards the end of the simulation, reaching a value of approximately 1.58 by time step 32. The data across simulation runs shows very little variance throughout the timeline, indicating a highly consistent pattern of habit formation for these milk types among the agents.

The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at zero at the start of the simulation. It increases sharply, reaching approximately 0.55 by time step five. This rapid rise suggests that agents quickly develop diverse evaluations of milk alternatives based on their individual perceptions, habits, and social influences. Following this initial surge, the rate of increase slows, with the mean deviation continuing to rise gradually to a value of approximately 0.76 by time step 32. This steady growth indicates that, while initial differences are established early, the divergence in choice function scores continues to widen over time, likely due to the cumulative effects of habit reinforcement, social influence, and individual learning within the simulation environment.

The mean value health deviation begins at zero at the start of the simulation. It increases rapidly over the first 10 time steps, reaching approximately 0.52. This initial steep rise suggests that as agents engage with the decision-making process, a significant gap quickly emerges between their internally held health valuations and the health values implied by their actual milk choices. From step 10 to approximately step 27, the rate of increase slows, with the metric plateauing slightly around 0.58 to 0.60. This period indicates a stabilization where the divergence between preference and action grows more slowly, potentially reflecting a temporary equilibrium in agents' behavioral adjustments. Following step 27, the deviation rises again more sharply, reaching a peak between 0.70 and 0.75 by step 32. This final upward trend implies that the mismatch between agents' health preferences and their choices continues to widen significantly in the later stages of the simulation, possibly driven by accumulated habit formation or increasing social influence effects that decouple initial health perceptions from final choices.

The mean value environmental deviation begins at zero at the initial time step, indicating that there is no discrepancy between the agents' initial environmental values and the implied values from their milk choices at the start of the simulation. The deviation rises sharply over the first few time steps, reaching approximately 13 by step 10. This rapid increase likely reflects agents adjusting their milk consumption choices in response to new environmental perceptions, which are then modified by habit and social influence mechanisms within the model. From step 10 onwards, the deviation stabilizes and remains relatively constant around 13, suggesting that agents have reached a new equilibrium where their environmental values and milk choices are consistently misaligned. This stabilization may indicate that the habit and social influence factors have effectively balanced the agents' environmental perceptions, leading to a stable state of environmental value deviation.
