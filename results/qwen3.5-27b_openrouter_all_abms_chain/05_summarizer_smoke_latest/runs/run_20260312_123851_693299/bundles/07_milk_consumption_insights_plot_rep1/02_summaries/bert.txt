Context and goals : the overall objective of the agent - based model is to reproduce adoption behaviours of milk consumption by the british public (1974 - 2005), by replicating individual preferences and decision factors. Decision - making follows a basic structure of : agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence, and finally, choice evaluation where agents consider the impact of their choices and may adjust their future decisions. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. This downward trend continues, with a more pronounced drop observed between steps 6 and 10, where the consumption falls to approximately 1000 units. A more significant reduction occurs between steps 26 and 30, bringing the consumption down to about 400 units. At time step 27, consumption begins to rise more significantly, reaching approximately 1, 250 by time step 28 and continuing to increase to around 1, 420 by time step 32. This steady growth implies that within the model's dynamics, agents continue to select whole milk at a relatively constant rate throughout the observation period, without significant shifts toward alternative milk types or saturation effects reducing the frequency of choice. As time progresses to step 20, the number of skimmed or semi - skimmed milk choices increases to about 12, 000. This indicates a continuous and significant increase in the preference for skimmed or semi - skimmed milk among the simulated agents over time. This steady increase suggests that agents are consistently making milk choices at each time step without any significant change in the rate of decision - making. The implication for decision - makers is that the decision - making process remains consistent and active throughout the simulation, with no indication of saturation or diminishing returns in terms of the number of choices made. Overall, the trends highlight a clear and steady decline in whole milk consumption over the simulation period, reflecting changes in individual preferences and decision factors influenced by health perceptions, social norms, and habit formation. Following this, consumption levels off slightly between steps 10 and 25, fluctuating around 1200 to 1250, indicating a period of stability or slower adoption rates. The mean habit factor for whole milk exhibits a consistent upward trend over the simulation steps. This steady increase suggests that the habit component reinforces the continued preference for whole milk among agents, potentially acting as a stabilizing force in the face of other decision factors like health perceptions and social influence. The consistent rise in the habit factor suggests that over time, the influence of habit on the choice of skimmed / semi - skimmed milk becomes increasingly pronounced. The rapid initial increase suggests a significant divergence in choice function scores among agents in the early stages of the simulation, indicating varied responses to milk consumption options. For decision makers, this trend highlights the importance of early intervention strategies to influence milk consumption habits, as initial variations in choice function scores are more pronounced.
