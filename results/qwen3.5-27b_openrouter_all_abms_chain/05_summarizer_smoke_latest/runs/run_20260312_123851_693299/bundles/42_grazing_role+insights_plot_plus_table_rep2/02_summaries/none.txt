Context and Goals

This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. In this version, each farmer stays on their own plot of land. They decide how many animals to keep based on how the grass is doing and what others are doing. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. There are three main ways farmers behave: baseline (E-RO), learning from their own results (E-LBD), and learning from neighbors (E-RO-SL1). The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock.

The average household risk attitude exhibits a sharp increase during the early phase of the simulation, rising from an initial value of approximately 0.007 at step 0 to a peak value of 0.381 around step 33. This period, spanning from the beginning through roughly step 40, represents a significant shift where households generally transitioned towards a more risk-neutral or risk-seeking posture, likely driven by initial observations of pasture performance and livestock outcomes. Following this peak, the aggregate attitude undergoes a distinct and sustained reversal. From step 33 to step 65, the mean risk attitude steadily declines, eventually falling below the initial starting value. After step 65, the trend flattens, with the average risk attitude stabilizing and remaining static through the remainder of the simulation, ending at approximately 0.19. These fluctuations imply that while farmers initially adjust their risk tolerance to exploit potential growth, prolonged exposure to the system leads to a retraction of this attitude, settling into a conservative equilibrium.
