The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5.29 units per person at the initial time step. This value rises sharply, reaching about 1265 units by the 11th time step, an increase of over 240-fold. The simulation concludes at step 32 with an average consumption of 253.10 units, representing a substantial long-term reduction in whole milk usage. Average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5.3 units at time step 0. Social influence and individual susceptibility appear to modulate the pace of change, leading to a more stable reduction in consumption. Social norms and cumulative habit changes reinforce the shift away from whole milk. The mean deviation between agents' held health values and those implied by their milk choices begins at 0.0 at the start of the simulation. The most significant increase occurs within the first five steps, indicating that agents quickly established consumption habits or choices that diverged from their internal environmental priorities. After the tenth step, the value stabilizes, hovering consistently around 13.4.
