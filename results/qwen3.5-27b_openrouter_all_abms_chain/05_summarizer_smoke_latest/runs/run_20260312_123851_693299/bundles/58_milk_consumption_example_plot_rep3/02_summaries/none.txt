Context and Goals:

The overall objective of the agent-based model is to reproduce adoption behaviours of milk consumption by the British public (1974-2005), by replicating individual preferences and decision factors. Specifically, the goal of the model is to explore the influence of perception, habits and social influence in an individual's decision-making process of milk choice. The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against observed macro level data.

In particular, the study conducts an experiment to compare the performance of two model variants in reproducing overserved milk consumption trends. These variants present different mechanisms for how agents become disposed to consider their choices, representing a threshold based, and a probability-based approach. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence, and finally, choice evaluation where agents consider the impact of their choices and may adjust their future decisions.

The agents in the model represent adult consumers who occupy a random position in an information environment. Each agent has a disposition to consider alternative milk choices. Two disposition mechanisms are tested in the model, a threshold-based approach, and a probability-based approach. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. These are computed at the initialisation of the simulation and then calculated at each time step. An agent's milk choice function is modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. Agents ascribe different relative importance to each constituent part of the choice function (health factors and environmental factors). If the disposition requirement has been met, consumption of each milk type is split proportionately by the size of each choice function, modulated by the other influences. If not, agents keep their existing choice.

Agents (n=1,000) start with an existing choice based on the dominant position of whole milk versus skimmed varieties in 1974 (start year of the data). All agents are part of a social network. Each agent in the network can sense and be influenced by the choice function of each milk alternative for other agents in their network. Links between agents are unidirectional, and influence occurs as a function of interaction probability, with the degree of influence characterised by agent susceptibility. Social norms are globally perceived by agents and impact the weightings of the choice function.

The average weekly consumption of whole milk starts at approximately 2,650 units per person and remains stable during the first time step. From time step 1, consumption begins a steep decline, reaching around 1,000 units by time step 10. This rapid decrease likely reflects an initial widespread shift in consumer preferences, possibly influenced by early adoption of alternative milk types as agents reassess their choices. Between time steps 10 and 18, the decline continues but at a slower rate, with consumption stabilizing around 750 units. This phase suggests that the most significant changes in consumer behavior occurred early, with remaining agents gradually adjusting their preferences. A slight uptick in consumption is observed around time step 27, where levels briefly rise to approximately 650 units before declining again. This minor fluctuation could be attributed to a subset of agents re-evaluating their choices, potentially due to social influence or evolving perceptions. From time step 28 onwards, consumption drops sharply once more, falling to approximately 300 units by the end of the simulation at time step 32. This final decline indicates a sustained and widespread transition away from whole milk, driven by the combined effects of habit formation, social norms, and perceived characteristics of alternative milk options. Overall, the data demonstrates a clear trend of decreasing whole milk consumption over time, consistent with the model's goal of simulating the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties.

The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at zero for the first two time steps. Following this initial period, consumption rises rapidly, reaching approximately 1250 units by time step 10. The rate of increase slows significantly after this point, with consumption levels fluctuating between 1180 and 1280 units until time step 27. This plateau period reflects a dynamic equilibrium where habit formation, social influence, and perceived health benefits balance the agents' inclination to switch milk types. Following time step 27, consumption trends upward again, surpassing the previous peak and stabilizing between 1400 and 1450 units by the final time step. This secondary increase suggests that the cumulative effect of social norms and the shifting perception of health characteristics eventually overcomes the initial inertia associated with incumbent whole milk habits. The variance across simulation runs is minimal throughout the entire timeline, indicating that the model parameters result in a highly robust and consistent convergence of consumer behavior.

The total number of times agents chose whole milk begins at zero at the start of the simulation and increases linearly over time. By step 10, the total whole milk choices reach approximately 750, and by step 20, they increase to around 1500. At the conclusion of the simulation at step 32, the total whole milk choices stabilize near 2250. This consistent linear growth suggests a steady, uniform rate of decision-making regarding whole milk consumption among the agents throughout the simulation period. The narrow range of variation across different simulation runs indicates that the dynamics governing the choice of whole milk are robust and less influenced by stochastic elements.

The total number of times agents chose skimmed or semi-skimmed milk begins at zero and increases steadily throughout the simulation. In the initial steps, from step 0 to approximately step 5, the growth is minimal, with only a slight rise observed. From step 5 onwards, the rate of increase accelerates, showing a linear upward trend. By step 32, the cumulative total reaches approximately 26,000 choices. The trajectories across different simulation runs exhibit very low variance, indicating a high degree of consistency in the adoption patterns of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk.

The total number of milk choices made by all agents begins at zero at time step 0 and increases linearly as the simulation progresses. By time step 30, the total number of choices reaches 30,000. This steady, linear increase suggests that each agent is consistently making one milk choice decision per time step throughout the duration of the simulation. This behavior is indicative of the disposition mechanisms, whether threshold-based or probability-based, being triggered regularly for all 1,000 agents. The uniformity of the slope implies a constant rate of decision-making across the agent population, likely reflecting that the combined influences of habit, social interaction, and perception of milk attributes are sufficient to prompt a consideration of choices at every step without significant fluctuation.

The mean whole milk quantity starts at approximately 2,650 at the initial time step. This value drops rapidly, reaching about 1,400 by the fifth time step, and falls further to just under 1,000 by the tenth time step. This steep decline reflects the initial and rapid shift in consumer preference away from whole milk. Following the tenth time step, the rate of decline moderates, with the mean quantity decreasing more gradually to approximately 750 by the twentieth time step. There is a slight plateau around the twentieth time step where the consumption hovers between 750 and 800. Subsequently, the decline resumes, dropping to roughly 650 by the twenty-fifth time step and reaching a minimum of approximately 250 by the thirty-second time step. This final phase suggests a continued but slower substitution of whole milk as the simulation progresses.

The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed starts at 0 and increases rapidly, reaching approximately 1250 units by time step 11. This sharp rise indicates a strong initial adoption of lower-fat milk types, likely driven by the perceived health and environmental characteristics of these alternatives, as well as early social influence from the network. Following this initial increase, the quantity stabilizes around 1250 units between steps 11 and 26, with minor fluctuations. This period of relative stability suggests that agents' habits and existing social norms temporarily balance the decision-making process, preventing further rapid shifts. After step 26, a secondary rise occurs, with the mean quantity increasing to approximately 1450 units by step 32. This second phase of growth could be attributed to cumulative social influence and evolving perceptions as agents evaluate their choices over time, leading to increased adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. The trends demonstrate a two-stage adoption process, reflecting the interplay of habit, social influence, and evolving perceptions in shaping milk consumption behavior.

The mean habit factor for whole milk begins at 1.00 and increases sharply to approximately 1.02 during the first time step. This immediate jump is indicative of the high initial incumbent habit value, which reinforces the existing preference for whole milk as agents start with established consumption patterns. Following this initial rise, the factor enters a phase of slow, steady growth. The factor increases linearly from roughly 1.02 at step 2 to approximately 1.05 by step 32. This gradual upward trend reflects the accumulation of habit strength through repeated reinforcement of the choice over time, as agents continue to select whole milk and the habit modifier incrementally boosts the choice function for the incumbent product. The variance among simulation runs is minimal in the initial steps but widens slightly as the simulation progresses, indicating that while the overall habit formation mechanism is consistent, individual agent-level variations begin to influence the aggregate mean later in the process.

The mean habit factor for skimmed/semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 1.0 at time step 0, indicating no initial habit reinforcement. Between steps 1 and 5, the factor slightly rises to just above 1.0, then stabilizes briefly before beginning a more pronounced upward trend starting around step 7. From step 10 onward, the factor increases steadily, reaching approximately 1.3 by step 15 and 1.5 by step 25. The trajectory remains smooth and consistent across simulation runs, with minimal divergence, suggesting robust dynamics in habit formation. By step 30, the mean habit factor approaches 1.6, continuing its upward progression. This trend aligns with the model's representation of growing habitual reinforcement as agents increasingly adopt skimmed and semi-skimmed milk, consistent with broader shifts in consumer behavior during the modeled period.

The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at zero at the start of the simulation. It increases sharply during the initial phase, reaching approximately 0.55 by step 5. This rapid rise likely reflects the early differentiation of agent perceptions and decision dynamics as agents begin to process health and environmental factors, along with the effects of habit and social influence. After step 5, the growth rate slows considerably, and the deviation continues to rise steadily, reaching around 0.62 by step 10, 0.68 by step 20, and finally stabilizing near 0.75 by step 30. The trend suggests that while initial decision variability is high, the divergence in choice function scores among agents increases more gradually over time, indicating a progressive stratification of preferences as agents interact and adapt within the social network.

The mean value health deviation begins at zero at the initial time step, indicating that the agents' held health values for milk choices were aligned with their initial selections. From step 0 to approximately step 10, the deviation rises sharply, reaching a value of around 0.52. This rapid increase suggests a period of significant adjustment where agents are beginning to re-evaluate the health attributes of their milk choices, likely influenced by the disposition mechanisms and social interactions within the network. Between steps 10 and 25, the deviation stabilizes, hovering around the 0.55 to 0.60 range. This phase indicates a period of relative equilibrium where the initial adjustments have settled, and agents are maintaining a consistent level of discrepancy between their perceived health values and their actual choices. After step 25, there is a noticeable uptick in the deviation, which continues to rise until the end of the simulation at around step 32, reaching a final value near 0.72. This final increase might be due to further refinements in agents' perceptions or social influences that continue to alter their evaluations over time. The low variance observed across simulation runs throughout the process suggests that the underlying mechanisms driving these changes are robust and consistent.

The mean value environmental deviation begins at zero at the initial time step, indicating a perfect alignment between agents' held environmental values and their milk choices. It rises sharply within the first three time steps, reaching approximately 12. The increase becomes less steep between time steps 3 and 7, where the deviation stabilizes near a value of 13. From time step 8 onwards, the metric plateaus, maintaining a value close to 13.2 with minimal variation until the simulation ends at 32 steps. This rapid early increase followed by a stable plateau suggests that agents quickly adjust their choices or perceptions to create a consistent, steady-state deviation between their internal environmental values and their observed milk consumption choices.
