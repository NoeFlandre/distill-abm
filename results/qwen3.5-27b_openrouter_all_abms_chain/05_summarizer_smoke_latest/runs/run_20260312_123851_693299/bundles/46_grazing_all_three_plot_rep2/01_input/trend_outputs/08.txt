The number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero across all simulation runs and begins to rise sharply between time steps 5 and 10. By time step 20, approximately half of the simulation runs show between 18 and 28 households actively learning from their neighbors. The growth rate of social learning slows down after time step 40, with many runs reaching a plateau between 18 and 24 households, while other runs continue to grow more steadily, stabilizing around 30 to 54 households by time step 100. This trend suggests that initial conditions or stochastic factors in the simulation lead to varied outcomes in how quickly and extensively farmers adopt social learning behaviors. The diversity in final counts of socially learning households indicates that some configurations allow for widespread adoption, while others limit it to a subset of the community. For decision makers, this highlights the importance of facilitating early interactions and creating conditions that encourage the spread of beneficial behaviors within the farming community.
