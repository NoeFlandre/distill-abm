Over the first 40 time steps, there is a noticeable decline in biomass, reaching a minimum value around 40,000 units. This reduction likely reflects the initial grazing pressure as farmers adjust their livestock numbers without extensive learning or adaptation. Following this decline, the biomass levels stabilize briefly before initiating a substantial increase starting around step 40. By step 60, the biomass has risen significantly, reaching values above 60,000 units. This increase may indicate that farmers have adapted their practices, possibly through learning or reduced grazing pressure, allowing the pasture to recover and regenerate. After step 60, the biomass levels plateau, with most simulation runs stabilizing between 61,000 and 63,000 units by the end of the simulation at step 100. The consistency in the final values suggests that the system has reached a new equilibrium where grazing practices and pasture health are balanced. For decision-makers, this trend implies that initial grazing strategies may lead to short-term depletion of resources, but adaptive behaviors can restore and sustain pasture health over time. Monitoring and supporting such adaptive strategies could enhance long-term sustainability.
