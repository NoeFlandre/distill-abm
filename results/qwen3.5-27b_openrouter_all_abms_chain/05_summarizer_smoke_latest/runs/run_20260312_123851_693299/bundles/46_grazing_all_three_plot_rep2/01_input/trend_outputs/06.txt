The average number of livestock destocked by households begins near zero, then sharply rises to a peak of approximately 7.5 animals at around time step 2. Following this initial surge, the average destocking number drops steeply, reaching approximately 2 animals by time step 20. The decline continues at a slower rate until time step 40, where the values stabilize at a range between 0 and 1.5 animals for the remainder of the simulation. Throughout the 100 time steps, significant variance is observed among individual simulation runs, particularly in the initial phase, indicating high heterogeneity in household outcomes despite uniform behavioral settings. This suggests that early in the simulation, resource pressure forces widespread destocking, likely due to the initial overstocking relative to the pasture's carrying capacity under the baseline extension model. As households adjust their herd sizes, the pressure on the pasture diminishes, resulting in minimal destocking in later years. The stabilization indicates that the system reaches a new equilibrium where grazing levels are sustainable under the current management rules. The persistence of some destocking, even in later stages, implies that occasional resource shortfalls or management miscalculations still occur. For decision makers, the sharp initial destocking signals a critical need for intervention during the early adoption phase of grazing systems, possibly through better initial livestock advice or temporary support to avoid excessive herd reductions. The variance underscores the importance of considering individual farmer contexts and learning curves in policy design to prevent extreme losses in vulnerable households.
