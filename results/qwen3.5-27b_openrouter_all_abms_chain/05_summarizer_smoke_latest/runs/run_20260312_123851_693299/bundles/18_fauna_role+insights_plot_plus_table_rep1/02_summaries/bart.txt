Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival. The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape. The cumulative number of male prey killed exhibits a rapid, monotonic increase starting from zero, reaching a saturation point between 110 and 120 individuals. Early in the simulation, energy decreases rapidly, passing through zero around tick 370, which coincides with the forager bands depleting their initial reserves. By the end of the observed period, the mean energy has plummeted to -15983, with individual simulations trending toward the minimum observed value of -38233. The mean cumulative death count across the simulation series is 200.79, with a median of 233.0. The final mean death count recorded at the termination of the simulation is approximately 235.0. The combined energy of male prey animals begins at approximately 12,500 units and exhibits a steep, monotonic decline, reaching total extinction (0 energy units) by tick 2284. Statistical analysis confirms a significant decreasing trend throughout the initial phases, with a Mann-Kendall slope of -22.6 during the first phase (ticks 0–231) The decline is characterized by a strong negative trend, evidenced by statistical analysis showing consistently decreasing slopes across the early temporal windows. By tick 744, the energy level drops precipitously, reaching zero by the final observation at tick 2311.
