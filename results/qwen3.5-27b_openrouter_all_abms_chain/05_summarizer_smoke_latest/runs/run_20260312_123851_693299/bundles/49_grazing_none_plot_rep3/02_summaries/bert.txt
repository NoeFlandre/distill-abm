The rage model is a multi - agent simulation model that captures feedbacks between pastures, livestock and household livelihood in a common property grazing system. Three new behavioural types are created (which cannot be combined with the original ones) : e - ro (baseline behaviour), e - lbd (learning - by - doing) and e - ro - sl1 (social learning). The r - parameter is catch - all for various factors that form an agent's disposition to act in a certain way, such as : uncertainty in the sensing (partial observability of the resource system), noise in the information received, or an inherent characteristic of the agent, for instance, their risk attitude. Following step 40, a divergence occurs : some agents experience a sharp decline in risk attitude, reaching as low as - 0. There is a slight increase in reserve biomass up to time step 10, after which it decreases, reaching a minimum value of around 80, 000 units by time step 30. This upward trend continues, albeit at a slower rate, until time step 100, where the reserve biomass stabilizes at values ranging between 110, 000 and 127, 000 units across the various simulation runs. Between time steps 0 and 10, a slight initial increase is observed, followed by a decline, reaching values near 41, 000 units around time step 30. Over the first 20 time steps, the average livestock count drops steeply across all agents, reaching a low of approximately 41 by timestep 40. After timestep 60, the average livestock counts stabilize, with values ranging between 40 and 62 by the final timestep of 100. The initial sharp decline suggests a significant reduction in livestock numbers, possibly due to resource constraints or risk aversion in the early stages of the simulation. The average number of livestock destocked by households exhibits a sharp initial increase in the first few time steps, reaching a peak value between 6 and 7 at approximately time step 2. Following this peak, the destocking levels show a rapid decline, decreasing to a range of 2 to 3 by time step 20. A rapid increase is observed between approximately timestep 5 and timestep 25, where the count rises from near zero to between 18 and 48 households depending on the simulation run. From timestep 25 to timestep 40, the growth rate slows, and the count stabilizes further, with most simulations reaching a plateau between timestep 30 and 60. The final state reveals two distinct clusters of outcomes : a lower cluster stabilizing between 16 and 20 households, and an upper cluster stabilizing between 30 and 54 households. In the initial phase, from step 0 to approximately step 20, all simulations exhibit a rapid increase in destocking, rising from an initial value of 0 to values between 70 and 80. Between steps 1 and 10, most simulations show a decline in the number of e - ro households, with some lines reaching as low as 2 households by step 10. However, there are also simulations where the count increases, peaking at around 39 households by step 5 before gradually declining again. From step 10 to step 40, the number of e - ro households generally decreases in most simulations, though some remain relatively stable. Others show a stabilization phase where the count remains relatively constant, ranging between 15 and 25 households from step 40 to 100. One simulation exhibits a sharp drop around step 45, reducing the count from approximately 35 to 25 households, after which it remains stable.
