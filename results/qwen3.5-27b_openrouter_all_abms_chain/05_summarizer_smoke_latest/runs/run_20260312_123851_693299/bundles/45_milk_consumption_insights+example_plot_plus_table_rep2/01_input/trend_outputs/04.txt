The total number of choices for skimmed or semi-skimmed milk begins at zero at the start of the simulation and exhibits a continuous upward trend throughout the 33 recorded time steps. During the initial phase, from time step 0 to 5, the rate of increase is relatively slow, reaching approximately 1,500 choices, indicating a gradual initial adoption phase where agents begin to consider lower-fat alternatives. As the simulation progresses through time steps 5 to 15, the accumulation rate accelerates significantly, with the metric surpassing 10,000 choices by step 15. This steepening trajectory suggests a reinforcing feedback loop where habit formation and social influence drive a rapid shift in preference away from the incumbent whole milk. By the end of the simulation at time step 32, the total number of choices reaches approximately 25,644. The trend remains consistently increasing without any significant peaks or valleys, characterized by a strong positive slope that stabilizes after the initial acceleration. The low variance observed across the runs implies that the model mechanisms for social influence and health perception produce highly deterministic outcomes regarding the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. For decision makers, this steady and accelerating uptake suggests that once the initial threshold for trying alternatives is met, the transition to lower-fat milk is rapid and robust, driven strongly by the compounding effects of habit and social norms.
