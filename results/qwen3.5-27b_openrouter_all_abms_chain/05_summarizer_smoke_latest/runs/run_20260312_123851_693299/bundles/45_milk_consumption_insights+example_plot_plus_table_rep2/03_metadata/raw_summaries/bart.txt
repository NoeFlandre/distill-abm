 The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards . The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person . Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives .  The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at 5.29 units per person at the start of the simulation, representing the low baseline consumption of these alternatives in the 1974 market . The data suggests that policy interventions or social marketing focused on health attributes are most effective in the early stages of shifting market dominance .  The average weekly consumption of whole milk per person begins at approximately 2,655 units and exhibits a sharp decline through the early stages of the simulation . This rapid reduction aligns with the model’s initial conditions where agents, heavily influenced by habit and social norms, gradually shift their preferences toward alternative milk types .  The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the implied values by their milk choices begins at 0.0 at time step 0 . The gap between perceived health attributes and the health implications of actual consumption choices widens over time . Decision-makers might infer that consumer preference for milk types will increasingly polarize as individuals become more attuned to health and environmental factors within their social circles .
