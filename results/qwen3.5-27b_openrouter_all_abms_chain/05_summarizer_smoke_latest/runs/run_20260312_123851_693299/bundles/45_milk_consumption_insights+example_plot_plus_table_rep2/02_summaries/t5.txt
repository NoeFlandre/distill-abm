The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. Consumption drops sharply to 390.11 units between time steps 26 and 28. This suggests a tipping point in the social network where a larger proportion of agents met the disposition threshold to consider alternatives, triggering a cascade of substitution. The average weekly consumption of whole milk per person begins at approximately 2,655 units. By step 12, the decline moderates, stabilizing around 900 units before resuming a downward trajectory. This late-stage decline suggests that accumulated changes in health and environmental perceptions eventually drive significant substitution away from whole milk. Metric rises gradually to 0.62 by time step 10 and continues to climb to 0.75 by step 32. Trend remains consistently upward throughout the simulation. Decision-makers might infer that consumer preference for milk types will increasingly polarize.
