The Gini coefficient for healthy livestock starts at 0 across all simulation runs at time step 0, indicating an initially equal distribution of livestock among households. Over the first 40 time steps, the Gini coefficient remains relatively low, with a maximum value of approximately 0.01, suggesting that the distribution of healthy livestock remains fairly uniform. From around time step 40 to 60, the Gini coefficient shows a gradual increase, with some runs exhibiting values up to 0.035. This rise implies a growing disparity in the distribution of healthy livestock. After time step 60, the increase in the Gini coefficient accelerates significantly, reaching values as high as 0.09 by time step 100 in some simulations. This sharp rise indicates a substantial divergence in livestock health across households. The variance among different simulation runs also becomes more pronounced, particularly after time step 60, reflecting the influence of stochastic factors or differing behavioral strategies. These trends suggest that over time, certain households accumulate healthier livestock while others lag behind, potentially due to variations in learning and decision-making processes. For decision makers, this implies a need to monitor and address emerging inequalities in livestock health to ensure sustainable and equitable outcomes.
