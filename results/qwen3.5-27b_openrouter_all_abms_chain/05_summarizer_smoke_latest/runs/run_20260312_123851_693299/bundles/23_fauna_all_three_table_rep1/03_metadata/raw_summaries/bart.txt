 The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction . It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival . The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape .  The cumulative number of female prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands initiates at zero and demonstrates a robust upward trajectory during the initial phases of the simulation . The aggregate trajectory of postpartum female prey animals hunted by forager bands begins at zero and demonstrates a rapid accumulation during the initial phase of the simulation . As the simulation advances through subsequent intervals, the rate of accumulation decelerates markedly . Management strategies should account for this saturation point to prevent overexploitation .  Under the current configuration of 20 foragers and specific processing costs, the system drives the megaherbivore population to near-extinction within approximately 2,000 time steps . Management strategies aiming to preserve the population would require significant reduction in forager numbers or adjustments to environmental carrying capacity .  The population count eventually reaches zero at time step 2311, confirming total extinction within the observed timeframe . For decision makers, this outcome indicates that under the specified configuration, including 20 immortal foragers with food storage enabled, the megafauna population is unsustainable . Even moderate human presence, when combined with high seasonality and optimal foraging strategies, can drive a population from an initial baseline to extinction in approximately six years .
