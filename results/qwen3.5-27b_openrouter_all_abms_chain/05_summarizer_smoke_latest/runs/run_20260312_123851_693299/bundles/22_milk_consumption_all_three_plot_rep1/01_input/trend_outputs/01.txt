The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent begins at approximately 2,600 units and shows a rapid decline over the first 10 time steps, reaching around 950 units. This sharp decrease can be attributed to the initial susceptibility of consumers to social influence and the perceived health benefits of alternative milk types, which are modeled with a mean initial health perception of 1.69. As agents interact within their social networks, characterized by a Watts-Strogatz structure with a network parameter of 8, the shift away from whole milk accelerates. Between 10 and 20 time steps, the decline slows, with consumption stabilizing slightly above 750 units. This period of slower change suggests a temporary equilibrium as habits, set with an initial threshold of 2, begin to solidify around new consumption patterns. From 20 to 30 time steps, the consumption continues to decrease gradually, with minor fluctuations, before dropping more sharply around time step 27, reaching approximately 300 units. This final decline indicates a tipping point where the combined effects of habit change, social conformity, and cognitive dissonance, set with thresholds of 0.15, lead to a significant reduction in whole milk preference. By the end of the simulation, consumption levels remain low, suggesting a strong shift towards skimmed or semi-skimmed milk alternatives. For decision makers, this trend underscores the importance of social norms and perceived health benefits in driving consumer behavior changes. Marketing strategies that leverage these factors could accelerate or sustain such transitions in product preferences.
