The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape. It simulates a socio-ecological system containing human foragers and sexually dimorphic megaherbivore herds. Cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands shows a consistent upward trend across all simulation runs. Initially, the numbers of males killed increases rapidly, reaching approximately 120 by around 500 steps. After this point, the rate of increase slows down, and the values stabilize, ranging between 110 and 125 male. Prey population has been reduced to a level that limits further increases in kills. The number of male prey animals in the simulation shows a rapid decline over the initial steps. By step 500, the population drops to an average range of 10 to 30. The decline accelerates between steps 500 and 1000. Decline continues but at a slower rate, with the total energy approaching near-zero levels by step 2000. This suggests that under the given parameters, the combined pressures of human hunting and environmental constraints lead to a rapid and unsustainable loss of energy resources available to prey animals.
