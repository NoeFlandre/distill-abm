The cumulative number of male prey animals killed starts at zero and rises sharply, reaching approximately 110 to 120 individuals by step 600 across all simulation runs. This rapid increase suggests that, during the initial phase, hunters actively target male prey, likely due to their higher food value compared to females and calves. The rate of accumulation slows significantly after step 800, as the number of males killed stabilizes between 100 and 125 for the majority of runs. This plateau may reflect a depletion of accessible male prey or a shift in hunting strategies as the population dynamics evolve. By step 2000, the total number of males killed remains constant, indicating that hunting pressure on males has effectively ceased, potentially due to the scarcity of males or saturation of forager needs. Variability among simulation runs increases after step 600, with some runs reaching higher cumulative totals, while others plateau earlier. This divergence may result from stochastic differences in herd composition, movement patterns, or forager behavior influencing the availability and targeting of male prey.
