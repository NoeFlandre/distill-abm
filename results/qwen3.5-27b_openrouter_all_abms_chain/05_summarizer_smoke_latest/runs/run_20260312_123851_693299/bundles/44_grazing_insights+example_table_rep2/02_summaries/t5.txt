The RAGE model is a multi-agent simulation model that captures feedbacks between pastures, livestock and household livelihood in a common property grazing system. It implements three stylized household behavioral types (traditional TRAD, maximizer MAX and satisficer SAT) these types can be compared regarding their long-term social-ecological consequences. The average initial risk attitude of social learning agents remains constant at 0.0074 from time step 0 to time step 100. There is no variation observed throughout the simulation, indicating that this metric serves as a static baseline rather than a dynamic variable. This stability ensures that any observed changes in herd management or pasture conditions are driven by the behavioral dynamics and learning mechanisms. The average number of livestock destocked across households begins at zero and experiences a sharp surge within the first few time steps, reaching a maximum of 6.6144 at timestep 3. This initial spike reflects an immediate mismatch between initial herd sizes and available pasture biomass before agents adjust their strategies. The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households initiates at zero and exhibits a monotonic increase over the 100 time steps. This rapid early accumulation suggests an aggressive initial response by households to reduce herd sizes, likely driven by risk aversion mechanisms.
