The simulation demonstrates a consistent upward trajectory in the mean deviation between agents' held health values and those implied by their milk choices. At the start of the observation period (step 0), the deviation is 0.0000, rising to a maximum of 0.7251 by step 32. Statistical validation via rolling Mann-Kendall tests across multiple windows confirms a significant positive trend (p=0.027). Notable inflection points appear at steps 4, 6, 16, 19, 22, 28, and 31, punctuating the general increase. A dominant oscillation period of 15 steps is also detected. In the context of the model, this widening gap indicates that as the simulation progresses, actual consumption choices drift further from the agents' internal health valuations. Given the model parameters emphasizing habit (initial habit strength 10) and social conformity (0.28), the data suggests that behavioral inertia and network effects increasingly mediate choices, decoupling them from pure health utility. For decision-makers, this underscores that long-term consumption trends are driven as much by social and habitual factors as by explicit health values, suggesting that interventions focusing solely on health metrics may fail to account for these diverging behavioral drivers.
