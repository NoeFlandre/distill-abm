The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against macro level data. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at 5.29 at time step 0, consistent with the historical dominance of whole milk in 1974. The overall trajectory from 2654.8100 to 253.1040 encapsulates the model's objective of replicating the long-term adoption of low-fat milk variants through mechanisms of perception, habit formation, and social influence. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk was 5.29 units at the initiation of the simulation, corresponding to the 1974 baseline, reflecting the initial dominance of whole milk consumption. From time step 0 to 9, the metric demonstrated a statistically significant increasing trend, rising rapidly. During the intermediate phase, spanning approximately time steps 10 to 24, the growth trajectory stabilized. The trend during this window showed no significant direction. The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the implied values of their milk choices exhibits a consistent upward trajectory across the 33 time steps representing the period from 1974 to 2005. The growing gap likely reflects the interplay of high initial habit strength and social influence mechanisms, which may cause consumption choices to lag behind or deviate from evolving health valuations.
