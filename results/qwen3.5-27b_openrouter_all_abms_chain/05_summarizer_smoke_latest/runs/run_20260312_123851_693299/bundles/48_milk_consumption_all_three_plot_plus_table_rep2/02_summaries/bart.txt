The agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms. The total number of milk choices made by all agents starts at 0.00 at the initial step and increases linearly, reaching 32,000.00 by the final time step. The trajectory shows a consistent upward trend with high linearity, supported by statistical measures indicating significant increasing trends across multiple rolling windows. The overall trend exhibits two distinct growth phases separated by a period of relative stability, reflecting the interplay between individual decision-making and collective behavior over time. For decision-makers, this data implies that once the initial barrier to trying alternative milk products is overcome (around step five), consumer habits form rapidly, making the alternative products a stable part of consumption behavior. Rolling Mann-Kendall analyses confirm a significant positive trend across all five-year windows examined. The absence of significant valleys or peaks beyond the endpoint suggests a monotonic divergence without major reversals. This pattern implies that as the model progresses, agents become less consistent in aligning their milk choices with their perceived health priorities. Decision makers may interpret this as evidence that external factors such as social norms are increasingly driving consumption choices away from internal health considerations.
