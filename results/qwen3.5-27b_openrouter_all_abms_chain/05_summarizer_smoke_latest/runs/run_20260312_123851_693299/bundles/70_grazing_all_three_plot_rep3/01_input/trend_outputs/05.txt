The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households starts at 90. It declines rapidly over the first 40 time steps, reaching a minimum around 40. This initial decline likely reflects the farmers' response to overgrazing or insufficient grass availability, leading them to reduce their livestock numbers to align with the carrying capacity of their land. After the 40th time step, the number of livestock begins to rise steadily, stabilizing between 45 and 60 by the 100th time step. This recovery could indicate that farmers adjusted their livestock management practices, possibly through learning from their own experiences or observing their neighbors, allowing them to maintain higher livestock numbers sustainably. The divergence in trends among the simulations after the 40th step suggests variability in individual farmer strategies, highlighting the importance of adaptive management and learning mechanisms in grazing systems. Decision makers should focus on promoting strategies that enhance learning and adaptation, as these appear to lead to more resilient and productive livestock management.
