The RAGE model is a multi-agent simulation model that captures feedbacks between pastures, livestock and household livelihood in a common property grazing system. It implements three stylized household behavioral types (traditional TRAD, maximizer MAX and satisficer SAT) these types can be compared regarding their long-term social-ecological consequences. The average household risk attitude initiates at a minimal level of approximately 0.0074 during the early time steps. From the beginning to the end of the simulation, the metric shows no deviation. This indicates that the agents' baseline disposition to deviate from livestock recommendations is static. Despite early resource depletion, social-ecological system stabilizes and improves over the long term. The presence of learning behavioral types, such as learning-by-doing and social learning, likely facilitates this recovery as households adjust livestock numbers in response to observed pasture states and neighbor behaviors. The number of households engaging in social learning begins at zero. The adoption rate is steep with a slope of 1.43, reflecting a rapid diffusion of social learning strategies among the agent population. This growth accelerates until inflection points appear between time steps 28 and 38, after which the rate of increase begins to moderate.
