The simulation tracks the count of households engaging in social learning over a 100-timestep horizon. At the onset, no households are recorded as engaging in social learning, with the value starting at zero. A consistent upward trajectory characterizes the entire duration, supported by statistically significant increasing trends identified in rolling Mann-Kendall analyses across multiple sequential windows. The mean number of households exhibiting this behavior across the simulation period is approximately 27.43, with a standard deviation of 8.92. The count rises steadily to reach a maximum value of approximately 32.07 at timestep 63. Following this peak, the metric stabilizes, ending the simulation at a nearly identical value of 32.07 at timestep 100. Several inflection points are observed at timesteps 7, 28, 30, 33, 38, 57, and 60, indicating fluctuations in the rate of adoption. A distinct change point occurs at timestep 40, suggesting a shift in the dynamic of adoption, while another is noted at the final timestep. Periods of oscillation are detected between timesteps 22 and 32 and again from 51 to 59, reflecting temporary variations in the growth rate. Within the context of the grazing model, this trend demonstrates that social learning behavior is adopted progressively by the smallholder population. Starting from an absence of social learning, the practice spreads until it encompasses more than half of the total household population by the end of the century, suggesting that learning from neighbors becomes a dominant strategy for managing livestock and pasture health over time.
