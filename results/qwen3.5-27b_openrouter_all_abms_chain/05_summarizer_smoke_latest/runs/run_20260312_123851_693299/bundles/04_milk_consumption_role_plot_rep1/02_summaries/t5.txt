Model measures the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. Each agent evaluates milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics. Decisions are influenced by habit strength, likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms. The data exhibits a steady upward trend, indicating a consistent increase in the total number of times agents selected skimmed or semi-skimmed milk. Starting from a near-zero value at the initial time step, the total choices gradually rise, surpassing 5,000 by step 10. The consistent growth suggests a cumulative shift in consumer preferences from whole milk towards healthier alternatives. The rate of increase in deviation accelerates, culminating in a final range of values between 0.70 and 0.75 by step 32. This pattern suggests an increasing misalignment between agents' internal health perceptions and the health implications of their actual milk choices.
