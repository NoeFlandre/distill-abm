The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across households exhibits a consistent upward trajectory over the simulation period. At the initial timestep, the Gini coefficient is recorded at 0.0000, indicating perfect equality in livestock health among the farming population. By the final timestep, this metric rises to 0.0726, signifying a measurable divergence in herd health outcomes. Statistical testing confirms this trend is robust, with rolling Mann-Kendall analysis showing significant increasing trends across all ten-year windows, supported by tau values approaching 1.000.

Early dynamics show multiple inflection points between timesteps 8 and 25, suggesting an initial phase of adjustment where household strategies begin to differentiate following learning interactions. A notable change point occurs at timestep 51, followed by another at timestep 71, marking shifts in the rate of inequality accumulation despite the persistent upward direction. Towards the end of the simulation, oscillation indices between 78 and 93 indicate minor fluctuations in the final phase, though the mean value remains stable around 0.0302 throughout the full period.

In the context of the grazing model, this trend implies that despite similar initial conditions, the interaction between risk modes, learning styles, and environmental stochasticity leads to unequal long-term outcomes. Decision makers should anticipate that management strategies which appear equitable initially will result in divergent livelihood success over time. Interventions may be required to support households falling behind in maintaining healthy livestock, as natural learning mechanisms alone do not prevent the emergence of inequality in this system.
