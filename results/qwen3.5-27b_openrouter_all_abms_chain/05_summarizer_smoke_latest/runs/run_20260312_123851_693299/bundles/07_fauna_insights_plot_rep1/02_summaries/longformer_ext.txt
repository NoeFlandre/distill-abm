The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The landscape consists of an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges on a 211x211 patch matrix. The goals are to manipulate socio-natural dynamics to observe how environmental conditions, human hunting, and animal life history interact to either accelerate or buffer extinction or create equilibrium conditions. Hunters follow dynamic optimal foraging theory, balancing energy status, prey value, density, and costs. Ungulates are sexually dimorphic, reproduce, age, and move as herds. The environment controls fodder density, regrowth, and seasonality. By approximately 400 steps, the population decreases to around 50. The decline continues, albeit at a slower rate, with the population dropping to less than 10 by about 800 steps. By 1200 steps, the number of prey animals approaches zero, indicating near-extinction. A few simulations show a slightly delayed decline, with some populations persisting until around 1600 steps, but ultimately, all simulations result in the complete or near-complete extinction of prey animals by 2200 steps.
