Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival. The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape. Early in the simulation, energy decreases rapidly, passing through zero around tick 370, which coincides with the forager bands depleting their initial reserves. As the simulation progresses, the energy deficit deepens, reaching -20059 by tick 394 and -23807 by tick 1594. The decline continues to accelerate, culminating at the minimum value of -38233 by tick 2310. The simulation demonstrates a robust and rapid decline in the male megaherbivore population, culminating in total extinction across all 40 experimental runs. Under the specified configuration of 20 foragers and 250 initial prey, the hunting pressure and environmental constraints are sufficient to guarantee the extinction of the male cohort in every iteration. The total energy of prey animals within the simulation exhibits a consistent and steep decline over the observed time steps. This pattern indicates a system-wide failure to sustain the female population under the combined pressures of environmental constraints and predation intensity. Decision makers should note that under these specific parameters, the system does not reach a sustainable equilibrium, and the extinction of the male cohort occurs rapidly. Vegetation levels are decreasing after the four-year mark. Internal dynamics, likely linked to prey density, have exerted a gradual but persistent downward pressure on vegetation availability, researchers say. Decreasing coverage after four years suggests that the decline in vegetation coverage is due to a decrease in vegetation availability.
