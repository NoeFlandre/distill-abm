Simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock. The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches begins near 45,000 units at the start of the simulation. The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and destocked animals, begins at 90 across all households. The overall trend indicates an initial period of resource depletion followed by recovery and stabilization as the system adapts. The number of households engaging in social learning begins at zero for all simulation runs at the initial time step. A rapid increase occurs between time steps 5 and 25, where the count of households with social learning rises sharply, reaching values between 15 and 45. The variability among simulation runs becomes more pronounced after timestep 60, with some runs showing a steeper increase in inequality.
