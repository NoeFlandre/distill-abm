The mean reserve biomass begins at approximately 90,000 units across all simulation runs. During the initial phase, spanning from step 0 to roughly step 40, the biomass levels generally decline, reaching a low point near 80,000 to 81,000 units. This early reduction suggests an initial phase of high utilization where livestock numbers or grazing intensity exceeded immediate regrowth capabilities, temporarily depleting the reserve stores. Variability among the simulation runs is visible during this phase, indicating differing rates of depletion. Following this trough, a distinct and rapid growth phase occurs between step 40 and step 70. During this interval, the reserve biomass increases sharply, surging from approximately 82,000 units to over 120,000 units. This significant rebound implies a shift in system dynamics, likely reflecting a reduction in grazing pressure relative to grass growth rates, possibly driven by behavioral adjustments in the farmer agents. From step 70 to step 100, the mean reserve biomass stabilizes at these elevated levels, hovering between 110,000 and 125,000 units. The system reaches a new equilibrium characterized by high reserve biomass, indicating a sustainable balance has been achieved where grass regeneration outpaces consumption. The divergence in final values among the runs suggests that while the general trend toward recovery is consistent, specific agent behaviors result in varying degrees of carrying capacity and biomass retention.
