The megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The count rises from 0 to approximately 110, reflecting active hunting pressure as foragers deplete available male prey within the environment. The growth in the number of males killed slows significantly, with values plateauing near 115 to 118. After 1,500 steps, the cumulative count stabilizes near 120, showing negligible growth until the end of the simulation. Rapid accumulation is indicative of a high mortality rate driven by immediate environmental pressures and active foraging. A significant structural shift occurs around step 1200, where the rate of increase diminishes markedly. The total energy of female prey animals starts at 12500 and declines rapidly over the first 400 time steps. By step 1500, the energy falls to near zero and remains at this level until the end of the simulation. The oscillatory pattern observed in the early stages, particularly around step 63, reflects the influence of seasonality.
