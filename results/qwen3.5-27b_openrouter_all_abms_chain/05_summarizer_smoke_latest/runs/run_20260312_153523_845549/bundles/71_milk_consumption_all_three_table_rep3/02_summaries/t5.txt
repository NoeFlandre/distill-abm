Model measures the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. Each agent evaluates milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics. Decisions are influenced by habit strength, likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms. Accumulated social influence eventually overcomes individual habit thresholds, leading to widespread adoption of lower-fat milk varieties. A temporary plateau in adoption rates should not deter long-term strategies aimed at shifting social norms. Between time steps 10 and 24, growth stagnates with statistical tests indicating no significant trend. From time step 25 onwards, the increasing trend resumes significantly, propelling the metric to its maximum value at the conclusion of the simulation. Upward trajectory is statistically significant across all analyzed windows. Inflection points are observed at time steps 7, 12, and 28, indicating moments where the rate of preference divergence shifts slightly, though the overall trend remains positive. This increasing deviation reflects the fragmentation of consumer perceptions.
