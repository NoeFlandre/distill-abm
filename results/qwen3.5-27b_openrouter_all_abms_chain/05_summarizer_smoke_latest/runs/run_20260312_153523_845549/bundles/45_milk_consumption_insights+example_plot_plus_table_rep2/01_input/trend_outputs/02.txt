The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5.29 units per person at the start of the simulation, representing the low baseline consumption of these alternatives in the 1974 market. Over the first 10 time steps, consumption increases rapidly, reaching a peak value of approximately 1265 units around time step 11. This initial surge suggests that agents quickly respond to perceived health and environmental benefits, modulated by social influence and habit formation. Following this peak, consumption experiences a period of stabilization with slight fluctuations, maintaining a level between 1200 and 1250 units until approximately time step 26. A second, significant increase occurs between time steps 27 and 32, driving consumption to a final value of approximately 1456 units. This late-stage growth implies a delayed reinforcement of social norms or habit strengthening among the agent population. The overall trend indicates a steady and robust shift in consumer preference away from whole milk toward lower-fat alternatives. For decision-makers, this behavior suggests that while initial market penetration of healthy alternatives can be rapid due to immediate social and perceptual factors, long-term market dominance requires sustained social reinforcement and habit consolidation to prevent stagnation.
