The total energy of male prey animals exhibits a rapid and consistent decline from the initial simulation step. At the start, the total energy is approximately 12,500. Between steps 0 and 200, the energy decreases sharply, dropping to roughly 6,000. This decline accelerates between steps 200 and 400, where values plummet to approximately 4,000 or lower depending on the specific simulation run. By step 1,000, the total energy for the majority of simulations has fallen below 1,000. After step 1,500, the total energy across all simulations stabilizes near zero, indicating a near-complete depletion of the male prey population or their energy reserves. The high variability among runs is most pronounced between steps 300 and 800, suggesting sensitivity to initial conditions or stochastic processes during the period of rapid population decline. For decision-makers, this trend implies that under the given parameters—specifically the high hunting pressure and limited fodder regrowth relative to consumption—the male prey population is unsustainable. The system rapidly moves toward an extinction equilibrium, highlighting the critical impact of hunting intensity on megaherbivore survival in this modeled environment.
