 The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards . Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence .  Average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at a minimal level of 5.29 units per agent at the start of the simulation . This low baseline reflects the initial dominance of whole milk preferences and high incumbent habit strength among the population . Between steps 15 and 19, the rate of change stabilizes, suggesting a period where habit persistence or existing social norms temporarily buffer against further substitution .  The average weekly consumption of whole milk per person begins at a high of 2654.81 units at the start of the simulation . The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents begins at 5.29 units . This low starting value reflects the historical context of 1974, where whole milk held a dominant position and agents possessed a high incumbent habit strength for existing choices .  The mean habit factor applied to the skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choice function initiates at 1.0000 and follows a sustained upward trajectory . This rising factor signifies that for the alternative options, the propensity to select them reinforces itself over time . The delay at the start followed by sustained growth aligns with the historical context of the 1970s-2005 period .  The mean deviation between agents' held environmental values and the implied values by their milk choices starts at 0.0 at the beginning of the simulation . It increases rapidly over the first 13 time steps, reaching a peak value of 13.4756. This initial rise indicates a swift divergence between the environmental preferences held by the agents and the environmental characteristics of the milk choices they are making .
