The average reserve biomass starts at 90,000 units and experiences an initial decline during the early stages of the simulation. This downward trend persists until approximately time step 21, reaching a minimum value of 84,114 at time step 26. This initial reduction suggests that the livestock pressure from the households, comprising E-RO, E-LBD, and E-RO-SL1 behavioral types, initially exceeds the immediate regeneration capacity of the pastures. As the simulation progresses, the trajectory shifts; between time steps 22 and 32, the trend stabilizes, indicating a period where grazing pressure and vegetation growth reach a temporary balance. Following this stabilization, a robust increasing trend begins at time step 33 and persists through the end of the simulation. By time step 100, the biomass reaches its peak value of 121,073, surpassing the initial starting conditions. Specific shifts in the rate of biomass change occur at time steps 8 and 48, likely corresponding to adjustments in household livestock decisions based on learning mechanisms.

For decision makers, this pattern indicates that while the introduction of learning agents may lead to short-term resource depletion as behaviors adapt, the long-term ecological outcome is positive. The sustained recovery after the initial dip suggests that the combination of learning-by-doing and social learning strategies effectively promotes pasture regeneration over time. Policymakers should consider supporting the initial transition period where biomass is lowest to prevent household livelihood collapse before the system stabilizes and improves.
