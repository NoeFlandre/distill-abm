Context and Goals:

The overall objective of the agent-based model is to reproduce adoption behaviours of milk consumption by the British public (1974-2005), by replicating individual preferences and decision factors. Specifically, the goal of the model is to explore the influence of perception, habits and social influence in an individual's decision-making process of milk choice. The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against observed macro level data.

In particular, the study conducts an experiment to compare the performance of two model variants in reproducing overserved milk consumption trends. These variants present different mechanisms for how agents become disposed to consider their choices, representing a threshold based, and a probability-based approach. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence, and finally, choice evaluation where agents consider the impact of their choices and may adjust their future decisions.

The agents in the model represent adult consumers who occupy a random position in an information environment. Each agent has a disposition to consider alternative milk choices. Two disposition mechanisms are tested in the model, a threshold-based approach, and a probability-based approach. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. These are computed at the initialisation of the simulation and then calculated at each time step. An agent's milk choice function is modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. Agents ascribe different relative importance to each constituent part of the choice function (health factors and environmental factors). If the disposition requirement has been met, consumption of each milk type is split proportionately by the size of each choice function, modulated by the other influences. If not, agents keep their existing choice.

Agents (n=1,000) start with an existing choice based on the dominant position of whole milk versus skimmed varieties in 1974 (start year of the data). All agents are part of a social network. Each agent in the network can sense and be influenced by the choice function of each milk alternative for other agents in their network. Links between agents are unidirectional, and influence occurs as a function of interaction probability, with the degree of influence characterised by agent susceptibility. Social norms are globally perceived by agents and impact the weightings of the choice function.

The average weekly consumption of whole milk starts at approximately 2,650 units per person and remains stable during the initial steps. This period reflects a steady state where consumers are entrenched in their existing habits, supported by the high incumbent habit value set at 10. A rapid decline occurs from step 3 to step 11, where consumption drops to roughly 950 units. This significant shift suggests the initial activation of the disposition mechanism, allowing agents to evaluate health and environmental characteristics, leading to substitution with skimmed or semi-skimmed alternatives. Following this, a slower, gradual decline is observed from step 11 to step 26, with consumption hovering around 650 units. This phase indicates the consolidation of the transition, where social influence and habit formation in the new choices stabilize the market. A further sharp drop occurs between steps 26 and 29, reducing consumption to approximately 300 units. This late-stage decline could reflect a threshold of social conformity being reached, where the majority adoption of lower-fat milk reinforces the shift through network effects. The simulation concludes with consumption stabilizing between 200 and 300 units. This final low level suggests that the market has transitioned significantly away from whole milk, aligning with the empirical data of the 1970s-2000s. For decision makers, the rapid initial phase highlights the sensitivity of consumer behavior to health and environmental factors once the initial habit is broken, while the subsequent fluctuations indicate the ongoing influence of social dynamics.

The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at zero and increases rapidly over the first 10 time steps, reaching approximately 1,200 units by step 10. This sharp rise suggests a swift transition in consumer behavior, likely driven by strong social influence and health perception dynamics within the model. Consumption levels remain relatively stable between steps 10 and 26, fluctuating between 1,200 and 1,250 units. This plateau phase indicates a period of equilibrium where consumer habits and social norms are balanced, resulting in steady adoption of the healthier milk options. A notable increase occurs after step 26, with consumption rising again to reach approximately 1,450 units by the end of the simulation at step 32. This final uptick may reflect a tipping point where cumulative social influence and habit reinforcement drive a broader shift in preferences. For decision makers, the rapid initial adoption highlights the importance of early interventions to promote healthier milk choices, while the mid-phase stability suggests the need for sustained strategies to maintain momentum. The late-stage increase underscores the potential for long-term reinforcement mechanisms to further consolidate behavioral change.

The total number of times agents chose whole milk begins at zero at the start of the simulation and increases steadily over the course of 32 time steps. At approximately step 16, the cumulative number of choices reaches around 1,100, and by step 32, the number of choices peaks at approximately 2,200. The trend is characterized by a consistent, linear-like increase with little variation between the multiple simulation runs during the initial 15 steps. As the simulation progresses beyond step 15, a slight increase in divergence is observed among the simulation runs, though the overall upward trajectory remains consistent. This steady increase implies that agents are continuously opting for whole milk, potentially indicating the strength of habit and initial dominance of whole milk in the simulated environment. Decision-makers could interpret this as evidence that, in the absence of significant social or health-related influences, agents tend to maintain their initial preference, reinforcing existing consumption patterns.

The total number of times agents chose skimmed or semi-skimmed milk starts at zero and increases steadily over the simulation period. This increase is nearly linear, reaching approximately 25,000 choices by the 32nd time step. The low variance across simulation runs indicates that the trends are robust, suggesting that the mechanisms driving the adoption of skimmed or semi-skimmed milk are consistent across different iterations. This steady rise implies that factors such as perceived health benefits and social influence are effectively encouraging agents to switch from whole milk. For decision-makers, this suggests that public health campaigns emphasizing the benefits of lower-fat milk can effectively drive consumption changes over time.

The total number of milk choices made by all agents begins at zero at step 0 and increases steadily and linearly over the duration of the simulation. By step 30, the total number of choices reaches approximately 25,000, and by the final step of 32, the total exceeds 30,000. This linear growth indicates that, on average, the number of agents actively re-evaluating and modifying their milk consumption choices remains constant throughout the simulation period. This consistency implies that the interaction between the disposition mechanisms, habit thresholds, and social influence parameters sustains a stable level of consumer churn. For decision-makers, this trend suggests that the market for milk alternatives is in a continuous state of flux, where agents are constantly open to reconsidering their choices rather than settling into permanent habits, which may facilitate the gradual adoption of new milk varieties.

This sharp decrease continues, with the quantity dropping to roughly 1,000 by step 10. From steps 10 to 20, the decline slows, with the mean whole milk quantity reducing more gradually from around 1,000 to approximately 800. A slight plateau occurs between steps 20 and 25, where the quantity remains relatively stable near 700. Beyond step 25, another decline begins, with the mean whole milk quantity falling to approximately 600 by step 28. This decline accelerates, and by the final steps, the quantity stabilizes around 250. These trends suggest that factors such as habit formation, social influence, and changing perceptions about health and environmental characteristics significantly impact the reduction in whole milk consumption over time. Decision makers should note the initial rapid change and subsequent stabilization phases, which may indicate critical periods for intervention strategies aimed at influencing consumer behavior.

The mean habit factor for whole milk starts at 1.00 and increases sharply to approximately 1.02 within the first step. This rapid initial increase indicates an immediate and consistent reinforcement of existing whole milk consumption habits among the agents. Following this initial jump, the mean habit factor rises gradually and steadily over the subsequent time steps, reaching a range between 1.05 and 1.06 by step 32. This consistent upward trajectory suggests that as agents continue to select whole milk, their predisposition or habit strength associated with this choice accumulates linearly over time. The variance between simulation runs increases slightly over time, though the overall trend remains highly predictable across all variants. For decision makers, this trend implies that once established, consumption habits for whole milk are self-reinforcing and strengthen with each repetition, making them a persistent barrier to changing consumer behavior toward skimmed or semi-skimmed alternatives without significant external intervention.

The mean habit factor for skimmed/semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 1.0 at time step 0, remaining nearly constant through time step 5. From time step 5 onward, the factor begins to rise gradually, accelerating noticeably after time step 10. By time step 20, the value reaches approximately 1.35, and continues climbing to approximately 1.58 by time step 32. Across all simulation runs, the lines exhibit minimal divergence, indicating high consistency in the growth of the habit factor over time. This steady increase suggests that repeated engagement with skimmed or semi-skimmed milk options strengthens habitual preference over time, reinforcing consumption patterns as agents adapt their choices. For decision-makers, this indicates that promoting initial trials of low-fat milk alternatives can lead to self-reinforcing adoption through habit formation, reducing the need for continued intervention once initial uptake is achieved.

The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at zero and rises sharply in the initial time steps, reaching approximately 0.5 by time step 4. This rapid increase suggests that agents quickly diverge in their evaluations of milk alternatives based on varying perceptions of health and environmental factors, influenced by initial habit and social cues. Between time steps 4 and 10, the rate of increase slows, stabilizing around a value of 0.6, indicating a temporary equilibrium in the differentiation of choice scores. After time step 10, the deviation resumes a gradual upward trend, reaching approximately 0.75 by time step 32. This slow but steady increase reflects the ongoing influence of habit, social conformity, and individual evaluations over time, leading to increasingly polarized choice functions. For decision makers, this divergence implies that interventions aimed at influencing milk consumption must account for the growing heterogeneity in consumer preferences and the reinforcing effects of social and habit-based mechanisms.

The mean value health deviation between agents' perceived health values and the implied health values of their milk choices begins at 0.0 at step 0, indicating no initial discrepancy between perception and choice. The metric rises sharply over the first 10 steps, reaching approximately 0.5 by step 10, suggesting that agents quickly begin making choices that diverge from their initial health-related perceptions. From steps 10 to 25, the growth rate slows, with the deviation plateauing between 0.55 and 0.6, reflecting a period of stability in consumer behavior and alignment between perceived health benefits and actual choices. At step 26, the deviation begins to rise again, reaching approximately 0.75 by the final observed step. This renewed increase suggests that external factors, such as shifting social norms or environmental pressures, influence agents to reconsider their milk choices, leading to a further divergence from initial health perceptions. For decision-makers, this pattern highlights the importance of monitoring both internal habits and external social cues to anticipate shifts in consumer behavior and ensure that health-related perceptions remain aligned with actual purchasing decisions.

The mean value environmental deviation begins at zero at the initial time step, indicating that agents' initial milk choices were perfectly aligned with their held environmental values. This deviation increases sharply over the first five time steps, reaching approximately 13 by step 10. This rapid rise suggests that as agents interact within the social network and evaluate alternatives, a significant misalignment emerges between their environmental preferences and their actual consumption choices. From step 10 onwards, the deviation stabilizes, maintaining a value around 13.5 through the end of the simulation. This plateau indicates that the system reaches a steady state where the gap between values and choices persists without further widening or narrowing. For decision-makers, this implies that while social and habit-based influences drive milk choices, they do not necessarily result in behaviors that reflect individual environmental priorities, suggesting a structural disconnect that remains unresolved over time.
