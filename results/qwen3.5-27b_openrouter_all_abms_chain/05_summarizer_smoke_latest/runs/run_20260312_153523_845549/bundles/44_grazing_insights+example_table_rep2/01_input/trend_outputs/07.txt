The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock, measured by the Gini coefficient, follows a distinct and consistent increasing pattern over the simulation period. Starting from a neutral value of 0.0000 at the initial step, the metric climbs steadily to reach 0.0726 by time step 100. This indicates that while the system begins with perfect equality in livestock holdings, a gradual divergence occurs among the 60 households as the model evolves.

Trend analysis across ten non-overlapping windows confirms a statistically significant upward trajectory throughout the entire 100-time-step run. Notable structural shifts in the rate of this inequality are observed at change points 51 and 71, with additional inflection points detected at steps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, 37, and 57. Following step 78, the system exhibits oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of 49 steps, suggesting cyclic adjustments in herd sizes likely driven by feedback loops between pasture quality and livestock decisions.

In the context of the RAGE model extension, this trend suggests that under the E-RO behavioral strategy with risk sensitivity enabled, asset concentration occurs slowly but inevitably without stronger adaptive learning mechanisms. For decision-makers, the key insight is that even with a shared environmental shock (global rainfall), individual risk attitudes (r-parameter) lead to a stratification of wealth over time. Although the final Gini coefficient of 0.0726 remains relatively low, indicating a relatively equal distribution, the consistent drift suggests that long-term equity may require active management or behavioral interventions beyond the baseline strategy.
