 The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards . Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence .  The data indicates a steady reduction in the incumbent habit of whole milk consumption throughout the simulation, replaced by alternative choices as agents update their decision functions over time . The total number of choices for skimmed or semi-skimmed milk begins at zero at the start of the simulation and exhibits a continuous, monotonic increase throughout the entire period .  The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 5.3 units at time step 0.3 . This metric exhibits a period of rapid and consistent growth during the first 11 time steps, reaching a peak value of roughly 1,265.5 . The data shows very low variance across the simulation runs, indicating that the model produces highly consistent dynamics in milk adoption trends .  The mean deviation between agents' held environmental values and their actual consumption choices begins at 0.0 at time step 0 . By time step 1, the deviation reaches approximately 2.0, and it continues to rise rapidly, crossing 10.0 by time step 3 and 12.0 . The rate of increase slows significantly between time steps 5 and 10 . The metric continues to climb, finishing at a peak value of 0.7251 at time steps 32 .
