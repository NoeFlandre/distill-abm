The average number of livestock destocked by households begins at zero and spikes sharply to a maximum of 6.61 during the third time step, indicating an initial crisis in resource availability. This peak is followed by a steep decline, with values dropping to approximately 3.37 by the ninth time step and further reducing to about 1.47 by the seventeenth time step. The trend continues to decrease significantly, with a notable reduction in volatility as the system progresses. By the 30th time step, the average destocking rate stabilizes significantly, hovering between 0.31 and 0.89 for the remainder of the simulation, ending at 0.31 at the final time step. Statistical analysis confirms a strong decreasing trend in the early phases, specifically within the first 32 time steps, suggesting that initial herd management strategies result in rapid destocking. After this period, the system reaches a quasi-equilibrium where destocking occurs at a low, steady rate. For decision makers, this implies that while initial resource shocks are severe, the system is resilient enough to adapt and stabilize herds relatively quickly, likely due to the adoption of sustainable herd sizes or pasture management adjustments.
