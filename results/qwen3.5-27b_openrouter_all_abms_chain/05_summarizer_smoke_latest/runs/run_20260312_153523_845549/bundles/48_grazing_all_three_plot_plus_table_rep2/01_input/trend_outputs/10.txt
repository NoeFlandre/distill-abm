The number of households following the non-learning E-RO strategy begins at 20 and experiences a marginal increase over the first 10 time steps, peaking at approximately 21.1. Following this initial rise, the count transitions into a sustained and significant decline, dropping to roughly 17 by time step 30. From time step 30 to time step 65, the downward trend persists, albeit at a slower rate, until the number of households stabilizes near 14.7 by the final time step. The data indicates that while individual simulation runs exhibit high variability—ranging from complete extinction of the strategy to stabilization at higher levels—the aggregate population of non-learning agents consistently decreases over the long term. For decision-makers, this implies that in a competitive environment where learning strategies are available, a static, non-adaptive behavioral strategy is generally unsustainable and will result in a gradual loss of population share over time.
