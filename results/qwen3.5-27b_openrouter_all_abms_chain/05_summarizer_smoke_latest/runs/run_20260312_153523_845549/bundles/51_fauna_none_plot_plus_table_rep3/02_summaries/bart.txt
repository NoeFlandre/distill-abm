The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model is an interactive, agent-based model designed to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Hunters follow dynamic optimal foraging theory, balancing energy status, prey value, density, and costs. Ungulates are sexually dimorphic, reproduce, age, and move as herds. The Megafaunal Hunting Model starts at 125 and declines monotonically across all time steps. The number of male prey animals hunted has effectively stabilized or the available population of males has been depleted to a point where further hunting is negligible. The cumulative number of births in the prey population exhibits stepwise growth over time. At time step 730, which corresponds to two years given the model’s tick-per-year setting of 365, the population remains extremely low, indicating intense pressure from hunting and environmental factors. By time step 2284, the male population reaches zero, signifying complete extinction. Total energy remains close to zero until the final recorded time step, where the value is exactly 0.0.0. Inflection points observed throughout trend indicate shifts in the rate of decline, suggesting potential changes in the interaction dynamics between the foragers and the prey or environmental factors influencing prey energy accumulation.
