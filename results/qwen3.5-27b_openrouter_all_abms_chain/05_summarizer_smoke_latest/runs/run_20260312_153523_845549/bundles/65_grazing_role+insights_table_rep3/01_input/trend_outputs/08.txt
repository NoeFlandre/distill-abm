The simulation output indicates a robust and statistically significant increase in the number of households engaging in social learning behaviors throughout the 100-year period. The metric begins at zero households at timestep 0 and climbs steadily, reaching a mean value of approximately 27.43 households across the simulation duration. A peak engagement of 32.07 households is observed at timestep 63, with the value remaining stable at this maximum level by the end of the simulation at timestep 100.

Statistical analysis confirms a persistent increasing trend across all rolling windows, with significant slopes ranging from 1.430 in the initial phase (timesteps 0-10) to 0.008 in the later phase (timesteps 55-65), suggesting a rapid initial adoption followed by a gradual saturation. Inflection points identified at timesteps 7, 28, 30, 33, 38, 57, and 60 mark shifts in the rate of adoption, while a structural change point occurs at timestep 40. Notable oscillations in the engagement count appear between timesteps 22 and 32, and again between 51 and 59, indicating periods of fluctuation in social learning activity despite the overall upward movement.

In the context of grazing management, this trend suggests that social learning strategies become increasingly critical or attractive to smallholder farmers as the simulation progresses. The rise in engaged households surpasses the initial baseline allocation of 20 households assigned to the social learning behavioral type, implying that farmers from other behavioral groups are adopting or activating social learning mechanisms over time. This diffusion of knowledge sharing behavior could enhance collective adaptation to grazing constraints. For decision makers, the early rapid adoption phase highlights a window for initial interventions or extension services to catalyze community learning, while the stabilization around 32 households suggests a potential equilibrium in the proportion of the community actively relying on peer information for livestock management decisions.
