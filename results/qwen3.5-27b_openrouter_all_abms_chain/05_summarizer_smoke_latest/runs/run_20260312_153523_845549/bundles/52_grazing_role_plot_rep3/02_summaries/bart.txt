Simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock. The average total number of livestock, including both healthy livestock and those that have been destocked, across all households, starts at 90 at step 0. The average number of households engaging in social learning starts at zero, with a rapid increase in the number of people engaging in learning and adaptation strategies. By step 80, the average reserve biomass stabilizes at approximately 125,000, maintaining this level through the end of the simulation at step 100. The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households increases over the 100 timesteps. The spread among the simulated runs widens over time, indicating variability in the cumulative destocking among different household behaviors or conditions. The number of households following the E-RO strategy exhibits a range of behaviors across the simulation steps.
