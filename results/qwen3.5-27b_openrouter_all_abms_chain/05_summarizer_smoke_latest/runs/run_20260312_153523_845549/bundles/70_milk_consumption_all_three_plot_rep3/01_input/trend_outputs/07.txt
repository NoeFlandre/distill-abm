The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed by agents starts at zero and increases rapidly during the first 10 time steps, reaching approximately 1,250 units. This initial surge suggests a strong adoption phase, possibly driven by agents' initial susceptibility to social norms and perceived health benefits. Following this, the consumption stabilizes between 1,200 and 1,300 units for about 15 time steps, indicating a period of equilibrium where habit strength and social influence balance out decision-making. A slight dip around step 18 reflects a minor adjustment in consumption, potentially due to cognitive dissonance or fluctuations in individual evaluations. From step 25 onwards, consumption rises again, peaking at approximately 1,450 units by step 30. This final increase may result from reinforced social influence or updated perceptions of health benefits. The low variance across simulation runs in the early steps implies consistent initial behaviors, while increased variability later suggests evolving dynamics due to individual dispositions and network interactions. These trends highlight the importance of social influence and habit formation in driving long-term consumer shifts, providing actionable insights for decision makers targeting similar behavioral changes.
