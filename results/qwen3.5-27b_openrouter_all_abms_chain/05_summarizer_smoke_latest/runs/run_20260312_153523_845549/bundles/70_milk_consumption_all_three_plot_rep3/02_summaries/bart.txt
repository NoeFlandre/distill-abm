Model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms. The total number of milk choices made by all agents begins at zero at step 0 and increases steadily over the subsequent 32 time steps. The rise suggests that factors such as habit strength, social influence, and health perceptions are effectively driving the adoption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk. For decision-makers, this suggests that interventions promoting healthier milk choices can yield reliable and consistent results over time. The trend indicates a consistent strengthening of habitual behavior associated with whole milk consumption over time. The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk starts at 1.0 and remains relatively stable for the first 5 time steps. The low variance across simulation runs throughout the timeline indicates consistent behavior in the model's response to habit formation.
