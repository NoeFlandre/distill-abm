Context and Goals:

The overall objective of the agent-based model is to reproduce adoption behaviours of milk consumption by the British public (1974-2005), by replicating individual preferences and decision factors. Specifically, the goal of the model is to explore the influence of perception, habits and social influence in an individual's decision-making process of milk choice. The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against observed macro level data.

In particular, the study conducts an experiment to compare the performance of two model variants in reproducing overserved milk consumption trends. These variants present different mechanisms for how agents become disposed to consider their choices, representing a threshold based, and a probability-based approach. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence, and finally, choice evaluation where agents consider the impact of their choices and may adjust their future decisions.

The agents in the model represent adult consumers who occupy a random position in an information environment. Each agent has a disposition to consider alternative milk choices. Two disposition mechanisms are tested in the model, a threshold-based approach, and a probability-based approach. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. These are computed at the initialisation of the simulation and then calculated at each time step. An agent's milk choice function is modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. Agents ascribe different relative importance to each constituent part of the choice function (health factors and environmental factors). If the disposition requirement has been met, consumption of each milk type is split proportionately by the size of each choice function, modulated by the other influences. If not, agents keep their existing choice.

Agents (n=1,000) start with an existing choice based on the dominant position of whole milk versus skimmed varieties in 1974 (start year of the data). All agents are part of a social network. Each agent in the network can sense and be influenced by the choice function of each milk alternative for other agents in their network. Links between agents are unidirectional, and influence occurs as a function of interaction probability, with the degree of influence characterised by agent susceptibility. Social norms are globally perceived by agents and impact the weightings of the choice function.

The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent begins at approximately 2,600 units and remains stable for the first time step. From steps 2 to 10, consumption declines rapidly, reaching approximately 1,000 units by step 10. This sharp decrease suggests that agents are quickly transitioning away from whole milk, likely influenced by initial perceptions of health and environmental factors as they evaluate alternatives. The rate of decline moderates significantly between steps 10 and 25, where consumption gradually decreases to around 600 units. This slower phase may indicate the stabilization of consumer habits or the gradual influence of social norms and peer effects within the network. A final period of more pronounced decline occurs from steps 26 to 32, with consumption dropping to approximately 300 units. This late-stage reduction could reflect cumulative effects of social influence or the strengthening of habits favoring skimmed or semi-skimmed milk as these alternatives gain acceptance. The consistent convergence of multiple simulation runs across all phases highlights the robustness of these trends under the tested parameters. For decision-makers, the rapid initial decline underscores the effectiveness of early interventions promoting health or environmental benefits in shifting consumption patterns, while the later steady reductions highlight the importance of sustained efforts to maintain momentum and reinforce social norms supporting alternative choices.

The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at zero and increases rapidly over the first 10 time steps, reaching approximately 1,200 units by step 10. This sharp rise suggests a swift transition in consumer preferences, likely driven by the combined effects of health perception, social influence, and the initial activation of habit mechanisms. Between steps 10 and 25, consumption stabilizes, fluctuating slightly around the 1,200 to 1,250 unit range, indicating a period of equilibrium where consumer choices become more consistent despite ongoing interactions and evaluations. A renewed increase occurs after step 27, with consumption climbing to approximately 1,450 units by step 33. This secondary surge may reflect a delayed response to accumulated social influence or shifts in perceived environmental and health attributes as agents adjust their behaviors over time. The overall trend demonstrates a clear and sustained shift away from whole milk, aligning with the model’s objective to replicate historical adoption patterns of skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. For decision makers, this trajectory highlights the importance of early interventions to accelerate behavior change, followed by targeted strategies to sustain momentum and prevent stagnation in adoption.

The growth is nearly linear, with only minor divergence among simulation runs, indicating consistent patterns in agent behavior across replications. This trend suggests that, under the given parameters, agents are increasingly selecting whole milk despite the historical context of declining whole milk consumption. This could imply that the mechanisms driving disposition to consider alternatives, such as habit strength or social influence, may need refinement to better reflect the substitution behavior observed in empirical data. For decision makers, this highlights the importance of tuning parameters like habit thresholds or social susceptibility to ensure the model accurately captures the shift toward skimmed and semi-skimmed milk over time.

The mean habit factor for whole milk starts at 1.00 and increases sharply to approximately 1.02 within the first step. This rapid initial increase indicates an immediate and consistent reinforcement of existing whole milk consumption habits among the agents. Following this initial jump, the mean habit factor rises gradually and steadily over the subsequent time steps, reaching a range between 1.05 and 1.06 by step 32. This consistent upward trajectory suggests that as agents continue to select whole milk, their predisposition or habit strength associated with this choice accumulates linearly over time. The variance between simulation runs increases slightly over time, though the overall trend remains highly predictable across all variants. For decision makers, this trend implies that once established, consumption habits for whole milk are self-reinforcing and strengthen with each repetition, making them a persistent barrier to changing consumer behavior toward skimmed or semi-skimmed alternatives without significant external intervention.

The mean habit factor for skimmed/semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 1.0 at the initial step, indicating that agents initially possess little or no established habit strength associated with these milk types. This trend suggests that as agents interact within the model, the habitual preference for skimmed/semi-skimmed milk strengthens over time. The low variance across runs implies that this strengthening of habit is a consistent and robust outcome regardless of specific simulation variations. For decision makers, this highlights the importance of habit formation in shaping long-term consumption patterns of healthier milk alternatives, indicating that interventions targeting habitual behavior could significantly influence consumer choices.

The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at zero and rises sharply in the initial time steps, reaching approximately 0.5 by time step 4. This rapid increase suggests that agents quickly diverge in their evaluations of milk alternatives based on varying perceptions of health and environmental factors, influenced by initial habit and social cues. After time step 10, the deviation resumes a gradual upward trend, reaching approximately 0.75 by time step 32. The sustained rise in choice function deviation underscores the growing heterogeneity in agent preferences, which may challenge decision makers aiming to design uniform public health or sustainability interventions for milk consumption behaviors.

The mean value environmental deviation begins at zero at the initial time step, indicating that agents' initial milk choices were perfectly aligned with their held environmental values.
