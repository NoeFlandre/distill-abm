The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics of each alternative. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at zero and increases rapidly over the first 10 time steps. This sharp rise suggests a swift transition in consumer preferences, likely driven by the combined effects of health perception, social influence, and the initial activation of habit mechanisms. The overall trend demonstrates a clear and sustained shift away from whole milk. The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed starts at 0 and increases rapidly, reaching approximately 1250 units by time step 11. This sharp rise indicates a strong initial adoption of lower-fat milk options, possibly driven by shifting health perceptions and social influences within the model. Decision makers can infer that interventions promoting healthier choices and leveraging social influence can significantly alter consumption behaviors over time. The mean value environmental deviation begins at zero at the initial time step, indicating that agents' initial milk choices were perfectly aligned with their held environmental values. This rapid increase suggests that agents quickly shifted their milk choices away from their initial environmental preferences. Intervention or information campaigns could most effectively influence milk choice behavior.
