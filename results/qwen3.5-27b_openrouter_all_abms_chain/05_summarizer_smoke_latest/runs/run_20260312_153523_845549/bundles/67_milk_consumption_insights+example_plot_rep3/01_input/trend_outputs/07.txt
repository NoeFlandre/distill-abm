The mean quantity of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk consumed starts at 0 and increases rapidly, reaching approximately 1250 units by time step 11. This sharp rise indicates a strong initial adoption of lower-fat milk options, possibly driven by shifting health perceptions and social influences within the model. Following this surge, consumption levels stabilize between 1200 and 1250 units from time steps 12 to 26, suggesting a temporary equilibrium in consumer behavior. A second significant increase occurs around time step 27, with consumption rising to approximately 1450 units by time step 32. This renewed growth phase may reflect cumulative effects of habit formation, social norms, or updated health evaluations influencing agent decisions. Throughout the simulation, the trend is consistent across multiple runs, implying robustness in the modeled behavioral mechanisms. These findings suggest that consumer preferences for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk are not only responsive to initial health trends but also susceptible to long-term social and habitual reinforcement, offering insights for policymakers aiming to promote dietary changes through targeted health messaging and social engagement strategies.
