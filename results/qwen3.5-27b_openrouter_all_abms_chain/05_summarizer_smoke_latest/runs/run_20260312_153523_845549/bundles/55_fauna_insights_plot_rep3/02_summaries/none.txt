The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. The landscape consists of an abstract square universe that wraps at the edges on a 211x211 patch matrix. The goals are to manipulate socio-natural dynamics to observe how environmental conditions, human hunting, and animal life history interact to either accelerate or buffer extinction or create equilibrium conditions. Hunters follow dynamic optimal foraging theory, balancing energy status, prey value, density, and costs. Ungulates are sexually dimorphic, reproduce, age, and move as herds. The environment controls fodder density, regrowth, and seasonality.

The number of human hunter-gatherer bands in the simulation remains constant at 20 throughout the entire duration of the simulation, spanning from step 0 to 2300. This stability is consistent with the model parameter setting 'imortal-foragers' to true, which prevents forager bands from dying or being removed. For decision-makers, this indicates that the human pressure on the megafauna remains static over the modeled timeframe. Consequently, any changes in prey populations observed in the broader model results would be driven by prey demography, environmental conditions, or the efficiency of the hunting strategy, rather than fluctuations in the human population size.

The total energy of forager bands exhibits a consistent and significant decline over time. Initially, the total energy stands at approximately 500 units. By step 500, it decreases to around -2000 units, indicating a net loss of energy. This trend continues, with the energy reaching approximately -10000 units by step 1000. The decline accelerates, and by step 1500, the energy is approximately -20000 units. At step 2000, the energy level drops to around -30000 units, and the downward trajectory persists, reaching approximately -40000 units by step 2500. This pattern suggests a sustained negative energy balance for the forager bands, likely due to high movement costs and insufficient hunting success to offset these costs. Decision-makers should consider strategies to optimize foraging efficiency and reduce movement costs to mitigate this energy deficit.

The cumulative number of male prey animals killed by forager bands increases steadily from the start, reaching approximately 100 by step 500. This rapid increase indicates high hunting efficiency during the early phase, likely driven by abundant prey availability and optimal foraging behavior. From step 500 to step 1000, the growth rate slows, with values climbing from 100 to about 115. This plateau suggests a reduction in hunting rates, potentially due to declining prey density or increased competition among foragers. After step 1000, the cumulative count stabilizes, fluctuating minimally between 115 and 120 until the end of the simulation. This stabilization reflects a balance between prey availability, forager efficiency, and herd demography, leading to an equilibrium in hunting activity. For decision-makers, this trend underscores the importance of monitoring prey density and forager behavior to ensure sustainable hunting practices and avoid overexploitation.

The cumulative number of female prey animals killed by forager bands increases rapidly from the start, reaching approximately 100 individuals by step 500 across all simulation runs. By step 1000, the majority of simulations stabilize, with cumulative kills ranging between 110 and 125. From step 1000 onward, there is minimal to no further increase in the number of females killed, indicating that the system reaches a state of equilibrium or exhaustion of available targets. A few simulations show slightly higher cumulative kills, peaking around 125, while others plateau earlier at lower values, such as 110. This pattern suggests that hunting pressure is initially high but quickly diminishes as the prey population declines or as foragers shift their strategy due to changing environmental or prey density conditions. These trends imply that female prey, likely critical for herd reproduction, are heavily targeted early in the simulation, potentially accelerating population decline. Decision makers should consider the long-term implications of such selective hunting on prey population sustainability and ecosystem balance.
