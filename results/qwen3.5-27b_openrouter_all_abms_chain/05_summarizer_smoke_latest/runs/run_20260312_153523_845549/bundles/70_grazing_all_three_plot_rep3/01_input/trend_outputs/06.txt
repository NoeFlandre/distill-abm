The average number of livestock destocked by households begins near zero, then sharply rises to a peak of approximately 7.5 animals at around time step 2. Following this initial surge, the mean destocking value declines steadily, reaching roughly 2 animals by time step 20 and continuing to decrease to about 1 animal by time step 40. Between time steps 40 and 100, the values stabilize, hovering between 0.5 and 1 animal per household with minimal variation. This early peak in destocking suggests an initial adjustment period where households likely react to resource constraints before adapting their livestock management strategies. The subsequent decline and stabilization indicate that households progressively optimize their stocking levels relative to pasture capacity, reducing the need for destocking over time. For decision-makers, this pattern highlights the importance of supporting households during the initial years to manage resource shocks, while long-term interventions should focus on sustaining adaptive practices to maintain low destocking rates.
