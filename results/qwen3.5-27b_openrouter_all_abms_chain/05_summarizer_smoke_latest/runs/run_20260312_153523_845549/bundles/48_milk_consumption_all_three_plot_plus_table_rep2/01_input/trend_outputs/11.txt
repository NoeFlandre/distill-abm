The mean deviation between agents’ held health values and the implied health values from their milk choices begins at 0.0000 and rises steadily, reaching a final value of 0.7251 by the 32nd time step. This upward trajectory indicates a growing misalignment between consumer health perceptions and their actual purchasing decisions over the simulated period. The trend is marked by a steep increase in the first 10 steps, climbing from 0.0000 to approximately 0.52, suggesting that early in the simulation, agents quickly adapt their choices to new health-related cues while their internal valuations lag behind. From steps 11 to 26, the rate of increase slows, with values plateauing around 0.55 to 0.60, which may reflect a phase of partial adjustment or stabilization in consumer behavior. A second phase of acceleration occurs after step 26, pushing the deviation upward again, culminating in the peak of 0.7251 at step 32. Inflection points are detected at steps 4, 6, 16, 19, 22, 28, and 31, highlighting moments where the pace of divergence shifts, potentially due to changes in social influence, habit re-evaluation, or external triggers within the model. Rolling Mann-Kendall analyses confirm a significant positive trend across all five-year windows examined, reinforcing the persistent increase in deviation. The absence of significant valleys or peaks beyond the endpoint suggests a monotonic divergence without major reversals. This pattern implies that as the model progresses, agents become less consistent in aligning their milk choices with their perceived health priorities. Decision makers may interpret this as evidence that external factors such as social norms or marketing influences are increasingly driving consumption choices away from internal health considerations, potentially indicating a gap in the effectiveness of health-focused interventions in shifting purchasing behavior over time.
