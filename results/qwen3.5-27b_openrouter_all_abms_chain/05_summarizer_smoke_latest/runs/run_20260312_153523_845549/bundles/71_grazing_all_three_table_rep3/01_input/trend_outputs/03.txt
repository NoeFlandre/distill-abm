The average reserve biomass across all patches begins at 90,000 units and follows a distinct U-shaped trajectory over the simulation period. The metric decreases consistently during the first 26 time steps, reaching a minimum value of approximately 84,114 at time step 26. This initial reduction likely corresponds to a period of adjustment where livestock pressure exceeds regenerative capacity before management strategies adapt.

Following the minimum, the system exhibits a sustained recovery. From time step 33 onwards, the trend shifts to increasing, maintaining positive growth through the remainder of the simulation. The biomass rises steadily, passing an inflection point around step 48, and culminates at the final time step with a maximum value of approximately 121,072. This end value exceeds the starting biomass by over 30%, indicating a net accumulation of reserve resources over the 100-year period.

These dynamics suggest that while initial grazing pressure depletes pasture reserves, the implemented learning mechanisms enable farmers to optimize herd sizes effectively over time. The transition from depletion to regeneration highlights the efficacy of experience-based and social learning in restoring land health. For decision makers, this implies that policies promoting information sharing among households can buffer against early biomass loss and ensure long-term pasture sustainability. The initial dip represents a necessary calibration phase rather than systemic failure.
