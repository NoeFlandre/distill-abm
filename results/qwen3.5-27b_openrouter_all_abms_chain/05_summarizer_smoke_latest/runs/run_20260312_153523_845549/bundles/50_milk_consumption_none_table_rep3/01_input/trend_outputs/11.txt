The mean deviation between agents' held health values and the implied values of their milk choices exhibits a consistent upward trajectory across the 33 time steps representing the period from 1974 to 2005. At the initialization of the model, corresponding to the year 1974, the deviation is exactly 0.0000, indicating perfect alignment between initial health perceptions and the dominant milk choice of whole milk. As the simulation progresses, this deviation increases steadily, reaching a maximum value of 0.7251 at the final time step. The trend remains increasing throughout the entire duration, with positive slopes observed in every analyzed segment. Several inflection points occur at time steps 4, 6, 16, 19, 22, 28, and 31, suggesting shifts in the rate at which this divergence accumulates. Additionally, oscillatory behavior with a dominant period of 15 time steps is observed, particularly evident in indices 18 through 20 and 27 through 31. In the context of the model, this rising trend suggests that over the simulation period, the alignment between individual health perceptions and actual consumption behaviors deteriorates. This growing gap likely reflects the interplay of high initial habit strength and social influence mechanisms, which may cause consumption choices to lag behind or deviate from evolving health valuations as the market shifts from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. The average deviation across the simulation period is recorded at 0.4965, underscoring that the misalignment becomes a persistent feature of the agent population's decision-making dynamics by the end of the observed period.
