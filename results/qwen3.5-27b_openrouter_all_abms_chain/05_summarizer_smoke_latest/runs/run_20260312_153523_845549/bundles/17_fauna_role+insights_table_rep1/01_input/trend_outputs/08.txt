Simulation results indicate a definitive extinction trajectory for the male megaherbivore subpopulation. The population initiates at 125 individuals at index 0 and concludes at 0. Over the 2312 observed time steps, the aggregate mean population size across 40 runs is 21.03, contrasting sharply with a median of 1.35. This discrepancy indicates a rapid initial decline from 125 to low single-digit numbers, following which the population persisted at critically low densities. Notable fluctuations occurred, with a local peak of approximately 10.5 individuals observed around index 730, and another around index 803 with a value near 6.7. However, these were insufficient to arrest the downward trajectory, and the population count reached the minimum value of 0 at index 2284.

Statistical trend analysis confirms a sustained negative pressure. Rolling Mann-Kendall tests for the first six windows (indices 0 to 1623) returned significant decreasing trends with p-values of 0.000. While a temporary non-significant phase appeared later (windows 8-9), the primary dynamic was collapse. 

Cyclic behavior is present with a dominant period of 63 ticks. Multiple inflection points, such as those at indices 390, 755, and 817, indicate structural breaks in the trajectory.

Implications for management highlight the fragility of the population under the simulated hunting and environmental pressures. The long tail of low abundance (evidenced by the low median) suggests that once the population falls below a critical threshold, functional extinction precedes numerical extinction. Decision makers should interpret the rapid drop in the initial phase as a critical warning signal, as the system shows limited capacity for recovery even in the presence of environmental carrying capacity.
