 Model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms . The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent begins at approximately 2,600 units .  The number of milk choices made by all agents begins at zero and increases steadily over the simulation period . The trend reflects a gradual shift in consumer preferences toward lower-fat milk varieties, driven by factors such as health awareness and social influence . For decision-makers, this trend highlights the importance of monitoring evolving consumer preferences over time .  The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at 0.0 at time step 0 and shows a rapid increase, reaching approximately 0.55 by time step 5 . From approximately step 5, the mean habit factor begins to rise gradually, reflecting a growing influence of habit on the preference for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk . This trend highlights the critical role of habit formation in driving the historical shift in milk consumption patterns .
