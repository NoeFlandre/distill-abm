Context and Goals

This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. In this version, each farmer stays on their own plot of land. They decide how many animals to keep based on how the grass is doing and what others are doing. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. There are three main ways farmers behave: baseline (E-RO), learning from their own results (E-LBD), and learning from neighbors (E-RO-SL1). The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock.

The mean household initial risk attitude for social learning agents remains constant across all simulation steps, ranging from 0 to 100. The values observed span approximately -0.10 to 0.18, with no changes or trends over time. This indicates that the initial risk attitude of these agents does not evolve or adapt during the simulation. For decision makers, this implies that the predisposition of social learning agents to deviate from the recommended number of livestock remains fixed throughout the study period, suggesting that their risk management behavior is static and unaffected by experience or social interactions within the simulated timeframe.

The mean household risk attitude values begin near 0.5 at the initial step and increase over the first 20 steps, with values diverging across agents. By step 20, the values range from approximately 0.2 to 0.8. Between steps 20 and 40, some agents stabilize at higher risk attitudes, while others show fluctuations or begin to decrease. After step 40, a subset of agents experiences a sharp decline in risk attitude, with some dropping below 0, while others remain relatively stable. By step 60, the divergence is pronounced, with risk attitudes ranging from approximately -0.3 to 0.8. From step 60 to 100, the trends stabilize, with minimal changes observed in the risk attitudes of most agents. The initial increase suggests a collective shift toward higher risk-taking behavior, possibly due to early positive outcomes or external influences. The subsequent divergence and stabilization indicate varying individual experiences and learning processes, leading to differentiated risk management strategies. Decision makers should consider the potential for heterogeneous responses to interventions and the importance of targeted support to guide risk management practices.
