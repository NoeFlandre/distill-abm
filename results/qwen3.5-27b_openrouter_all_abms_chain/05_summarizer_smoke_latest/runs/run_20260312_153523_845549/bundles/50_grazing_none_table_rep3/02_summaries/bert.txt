The rage model is a multi - agent simulation model that captures feedbacks between pastures, livestock and household livelihood in a common property grazing system. Three new behavioural types are created (which cannot be combined with the original ones) : e - ro (baseline behaviour), e - lbd (learning - by - doing) and e - ro - sl1 (social learning). The r - parameter is catch - all for various factors that form an agent's disposition to act in a certain way, such as : uncertainty in the sensing (partial observability of the resource system), noise in the information received, or an inherent characteristic of the agent, for instance, their risk attitude. Throughout the 100 - step simulation, the average initial risk attitude of the social learning agents remained perfectly constant at a value of 0. Rolling window analyses detected no significant trends, indicating that this metric does not fluctuate in response to the model's internal dynamics. This stability is consistent with the variable's definition as an initial condition ; unlike dynamic state variables such as herd size or pasture biomass, the initial risk attitude serves as a fixed baseline for agent behavior and is not updated by the ecological or social learning feedback loops during the run. The overall pattern reflects an initial period of exploration or reaction to environmental variability, followed by adaptive convergence toward a new equilibrium state where agents manage their herds with a moderate, persistent deviation from the recommended levels. While the slope of increase moderates in the later stages, specifically between time steps 77 and 98, the upward trajectory remains significant until the end of the simulation. In the context of the model, which simulates a population of 60 households comprising baseline, learning - by - doing, and social learning strategies under variable rainfall conditions, the long - term increase in biomass implies that the adaptive agents successfully adjusted their livestock management decisions over time. The shift from depletion to accumulation suggests that the learning mechanisms enabled the population to align grazing pressure with pasture capacity, leading to improved ecological conditions by the end of the simulation period. Subsequently, starting from approximately timestep 44, the average total livestock count enters a sustained phase of recovery, characterized by a significant increasing trend that continues until the simulation concludes at timestep 100. Overall, despite the recovery in the latter half of the simulation, the final livestock level remains significantly lower than the initial endowment, representing a net reduction of nearly half over the 100 - timestep period. In the context of the extended rage model, this pattern suggests that the heterogeneous population of agents, comprising baseline (e - ro), learning - by - doing (e - lbd), and social learning (e - ro - sl1) types, initially responded to environmental variability and risk constraints by destocking. This upward trajectory is consistent across all measured windows, with specific shifts in the rate of change detected at time steps 8, 13, 15, 20, 25, 37, and 57. The trend maintains an increasing status across the first half of the period, with shifts in growth dynamics occurring around timesteps 7, 28, 30, 33, and 38. The progression suggests that social learning behavior activates and diffuses within the population after an initial period, eventually reaching a steady state where slightly more than half of the agents consistently engage in social learning strategies. The slowing rate of increase after timestep 40 indicates a saturation point for the adoption or activation of this behavioral mode under the given ecological and social conditions. Within the context of the adaptive management model, this trajectory suggests that households engage in significant livestock reduction early in the timeline, potentially as a response to initial uncertainty or resource constraints given the risk - sensitive decision - making framework.
