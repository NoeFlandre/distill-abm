The mean whole milk quantity consumed by agents in the agent-based model exhibits a clear downward trend over time, reflecting a shift in consumer behavior toward alternative milk types. At the initial step, the mean whole milk quantity is approximately 2,600 units, indicating a high level of consumption at the start of the simulation. This value declines sharply in the first 10 steps, dropping to around 900 units. This rapid decrease suggests a strong initial transition away from whole milk, likely driven by the parameters influencing health perceptions and habit strength. Between steps 10 and 25, the rate of decline slows, with the mean quantity stabilizing around 700 to 800 units. This period indicates a phase of adjustment where some agents continue to reduce their whole milk consumption, while others maintain their habits. After step 25, the decline accelerates again, reaching approximately 300 units by step 30. This final drop may reflect the cumulative impact of social influence and norms, as well as the triggering of agents' dispositions to consider alternatives. The overall trend implies that the model effectively captures the historical shift from whole milk to lower-fat alternatives, with key drivers being perceived health benefits, social dynamics, and habit formation. For decision makers, this underscores the importance of health-related messaging and social norms in influencing consumer preferences.
