The average available green biomass across all patches initiates at 45,000 units. Over the initial phase, the metric declines, reaching a minimum value of 42,057 units at time step 27. This initial drawdown reflects the period where households place livestock on their private pastures based on initial risk attitudes and preferences prior to significant behavioral adaptation. A transition period follows, characterized by a stabilization of grazing pressure around time step 32.

At time step 50, a distinct shift occurs, initiating a sustained recovery phase. From this point, the biomass exhibits a consistent increasing trend throughout the remainder of the simulation. The value climbs steadily, overcoming the earlier deficits to reach a maximum of 60,535 units at time step 100. This trajectory suggests that the integration of learning mechanisms, such as learning-by-doing and social learning within the agent population, eventually leads to modified livestock management decisions that favor pasture regeneration. The final biomass level exceeds the starting condition, indicating a net positive ecological outcome driven by the adaptive strategies employed by the agents over the long term. Inflection points observed at time steps 9 and 49 further delineate the shift from initial resource depletion to long-term ecological restoration.
