Simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock. The average reserve biomass across all patches begins at 90,000 units and experiences a decline in the initial phase. This early reduction suggests that early grazing pressure from livestock, combined with the behavioral strategies of the farmers, led to a net consumption of biomass exceeding regeneration rates during the first 25 to 30 steps. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of early intervention. The number of households practicing social learning starts at zero across all simulation runs. The average cumulative number of livestock destocked by households begins at zero and shows a rapid increase during the initial phase, reaching approximately 60 units by time step 25. The rate of destocking slows significantly, suggesting a stabilization in herd management decisions as the system adapts to prevailing conditions. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of early intervention. Observers eventually outperform static management approaches, leading to a shift in the dominant behavioral profile of the community.
