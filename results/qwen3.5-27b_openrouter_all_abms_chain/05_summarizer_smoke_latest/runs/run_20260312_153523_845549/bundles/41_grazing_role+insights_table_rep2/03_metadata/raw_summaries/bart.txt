 Simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors . The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time . The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock .  The simulation tracks the average available green biomass over 100 timesteps . At the onset, biomass stands at 45,000.0 at time step 0.0 . The trajectory implies that the mixed population of farmers, utilizing baseline, experience-based learning, and social learning strategies, initially degrades the pasture but eventually adapts their livestock holdings to allow for regrowth .  Towards the end of the simulation timeline, specifically between timesteps 66 and 76, a statistically significant increasing trend emerges . This suggests a potential resurgence of pressure on grazing resources . Policies should focus on reinforcing adaptive capacity to prevent destocking levels from rising again in the mature phase of the grazing system .  Simulation tracks prevalence of households adhering to E-RO strategy, which represents agents operating without adaptive learning mechanisms in the grazing system . In the context of grazing land management, this trajectory implies that households utilizing the baseline non-learning strategy become less dominant over time . Policies should anticipate a shrinking baseline cohort, potentially necessitating a focus on supporting the transition to adaptive strategies .
