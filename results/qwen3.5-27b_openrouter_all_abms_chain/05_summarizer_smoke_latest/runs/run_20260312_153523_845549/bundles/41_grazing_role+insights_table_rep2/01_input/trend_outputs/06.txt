The average number of livestock destocked across households follows a trajectory characterized by an initial sharp peak followed by a sustained decline and eventual stabilization. At the onset of the simulation, the metric starts at zero but surges to a maximum value of approximately 6.61 animals per household by timestep 3. This initial spike indicates an acute phase of resource pressure where significant herd reductions were necessary. Subsequently, the system enters a period of rapid adjustment. Statistical analysis confirms a statistically significant decreasing pattern across the first thirty timesteps, with the rate of destocking slowing considerably after timestep 20.

During the intermediate phase from timestep 30 to 65, the destocking levels fluctuate around a low mean without a consistent directional trend, suggesting the system has reached a dynamic equilibrium. The standard deviation of 1.43 relative to a simulation mean of 0.96 underscores that livestock removal remains episodic rather than uniform across the population. Towards the end of the simulation timeline, specifically between timesteps 66 and 76, a statistically significant increasing trend emerges. This suggests a potential resurgence of pressure on grazing resources. Despite this late-stage increase, the metric concludes the 100-timestep period at a low value of 0.31 animals.

For decision makers, the early spike highlights the critical importance of supporting farmers during the initial implementation or shock period, as this is when resource deficits are most acute and livestock losses are highest. The subsequent stabilization demonstrates that the integrated behavioral strategies effectively manage herd sizes to match carrying capacity over the medium term. However, the late-stage increasing trend serves as an early warning signal for potential long-term sustainability issues. Policies should focus on reinforcing adaptive capacity to prevent destocking levels from rising again in the mature phase of the grazing system.
