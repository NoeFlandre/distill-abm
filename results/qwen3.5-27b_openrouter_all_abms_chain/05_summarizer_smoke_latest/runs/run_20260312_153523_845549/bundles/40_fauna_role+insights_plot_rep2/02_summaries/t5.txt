The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape. It simulates a socio-ecological system containing human foragers and sexually dimorphic megaherbivore herds. Cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands shows a consistent upward trend across all simulation runs. By 500 steps, the rate of increase slows, and the cumulative number reaches approximately 60 in most simulations. The stabilization suggests a depletion of accessible female prey or changes in forager behavior over time. Female prey animals exhibit a sharp decline from an initial average of approximately 125 individuals at step 0, dropping to fewer than 25 animals by step 400. This rapid reduction suggests that the combined effects of human predation pressure and environmental constraints are critical in driving megafaunal extinction. Decision makers should consider adjusting hunting parameters or environmental conditions to ensure population sustainability. Vegetation coverage begins to recover, reaching a plateau near 33% by 1000 steps. This pattern suggests a transient environmental stress event early in the simulation. Decision makers should focus on the initial decline to understand potential causes.
