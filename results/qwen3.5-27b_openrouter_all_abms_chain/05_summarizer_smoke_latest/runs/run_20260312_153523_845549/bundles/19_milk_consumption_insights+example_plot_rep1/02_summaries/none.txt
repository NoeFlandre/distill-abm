Context and Goals:

The overall objective of the agent-based model is to reproduce adoption behaviours of milk consumption by the British public (1974-2005), by replicating individual preferences and decision factors. Specifically, the goal of the model is to explore the influence of perception, habits and social influence in an individual's decision-making process of milk choice. The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against observed macro level data.

In particular, the study conducts an experiment to compare the performance of two model variants in reproducing overserved milk consumption trends. These variants present different mechanisms for how agents become disposed to consider their choices, representing a threshold based, and a probability-based approach. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence, and finally, choice evaluation where agents consider the impact of their choices and may adjust their future decisions.

The agents in the model represent adult consumers who occupy a random position in an information environment. Each agent has a disposition to consider alternative milk choices. Two disposition mechanisms are tested in the model, a threshold-based approach, and a probability-based approach. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. These are computed at the initialisation of the simulation and then calculated at each time step. An agent's milk choice function is modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. Agents ascribe different relative importance to each constituent part of the choice function (health factors and environmental factors). If the disposition requirement has been met, consumption of each milk type is split proportionately by the size of each choice function, modulated by the other influences. If not, agents keep their existing choice.

Agents (n=1,000) start with an existing choice based on the dominant position of whole milk versus skimmed varieties in 1974 (start year of the data). All agents are part of a social network. Each agent in the network can sense and be influenced by the choice function of each milk alternative for other agents in their network. Links between agents are unidirectional, and influence occurs as a function of interaction probability, with the degree of influence characterised by agent susceptibility. Social norms are globally perceived by agents and impact the weightings of the choice function.

The average weekly consumption of whole milk starts at approximately 2,650 units per person and remains stable during the initial steps. This period reflects a steady state, where most agents maintain their initial choice, influenced by strong habits and social norms favoring whole milk. Around step 2, a significant decline begins, indicating a shift in consumer preferences towards other milk types. This rapid decrease could be attributed to the agents' evolving perceptions of health and environmental factors, coupled with the influence of social networks. By step 10, the consumption drops to about 1,000 units, showing a marked transition in consumption patterns. Following this, the decline rate slows, and consumption stabilizes between steps 15 and 25, hovering around 800 units. This period may represent a new equilibrium where agents' choices are influenced by a balance of habit, social influence, and evolving perceptions. A noticeable dip occurs around step 28, reducing consumption to approximately 300 units, suggesting a further shift in preference or an external factor influencing the decision-making process. The simulation ends with whole milk consumption stabilizing around 250 units per person, indicating a long-term trend towards reduced whole milk consumption. For decision makers, this trend highlights the impact of changing health and environmental perceptions on consumer behavior, suggesting that future policies should consider these evolving factors to effectively influence milk consumption trends.

The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent begins at zero and increases rapidly from step 1, reaching approximately 1,250 by step 10. This initial surge suggests a strong initial responsiveness among agents to factors such as perceived health benefits or social influence. From steps 11 to 27, consumption stabilizes around 1,220 to 1,250, with minor fluctuations, indicating a temporary equilibrium where the adoption rate slows, possibly due to habit formation or saturation of early adopters. At step 28, consumption rises sharply again, reaching approximately 1,450 by step 32, the end of the simulation. This late-stage increase may reflect cumulative social influence, evolving norms, or a re-evaluation of existing choices by agents. The trend implies that initial adoption drivers differ from those sustaining long-term consumption, suggesting that decision-makers should consider both immediate and delayed mechanisms to encourage continued behavioral change.

The total number of times agents chose whole milk begins at zero at the start of the simulation and increases steadily over the course of 32 time steps. At approximately step 16, the cumulative number of choices reaches around 1,100, and by the final time step of 32, the cumulative total ranges between 2,000 and 2,300 across all simulation runs. The trajectory is consistently linear with minimal variance between individual simulation runs throughout the process. This pattern suggests that the rate at which agents collectively choose whole milk remains remarkably stable and predictable. For decision-makers, this implies that, under the simulated conditions, the underlying factors influencing the decision to consume whole milk operate with a high degree of consistency, indicating that consumer preference retention is strongly influenced by the initial dominance and habit factors set at the start of the simulation.

The total number of times agents chose skimmed or semi-skimmed milk starts at zero and increases steadily over the simulation period. By step 5, the count rises to approximately 1,000, indicating initial adoption driven by agent perceptions and social influence. This trend accelerates, reaching about 5,000 by step 10, suggesting a growing shift in consumer preferences. The count continues to climb linearly, reaching around 10,000 by step 15 and 20,000 by step 25. By the final step of 32, the total reaches approximately 26,000. This steady increase implies that over time, a significant portion of the population adopts skimmed or semi-skimmed milk, potentially due to evolving health perceptions or social norms. Decision-makers can infer that consistent social influence and habit formation mechanisms are effective in driving long-term behavioral change.

The total number of milk choices made by all agents begins at zero at step 0 and increases steadily and linearly over the duration of the simulation. By step 30, the total number of choices reaches approximately 25,000, and by the final step of 32, the total exceeds 30,000. This linear growth indicates that, on average, the number of agents actively re-evaluating and modifying their milk consumption choices remains constant throughout the simulation period. This consistency implies that the interaction between the disposition mechanisms, habit thresholds, and social influence parameters sustains a stable level of consumer churn. For decision makers, this trend suggests that the underlying factors driving the switch from whole milk to lower-fat alternatives result in a predictable and continuous rate of adoption across the population, rather than occurring in sudden bursts or phases of stagnation.

The mean whole milk quantity starts at approximately 2,650 at the initial step and shows a rapid decline over the first 10 steps, dropping to around 950. This sharp decrease suggests that many agents quickly adjust their consumption habits, possibly due to heightened awareness of health and environmental factors or strong social influences. The decline then slows, with the quantity falling gradually to approximately 800 by step 18. This period of slower decline indicates a stabilization phase where agents’ choices are influenced by established habits and social norms, reducing the rate of change. After step 18, the quantity continues to decrease, with minor fluctuations observed between steps 20 and 28, eventually reaching around 300 by step 32. The final steady decline suggests a long-term shift in preference away from whole milk, likely driven by cumulative effects of changing perceptions and social dynamics. These trends imply that policymakers and marketers should focus on the initial period of rapid change, where interventions could have a significant impact on shaping long-term consumer behavior.

The mean consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at zero and increases rapidly over the first 10 time steps, reaching approximately 1200 units. This initial surge suggests that agents quickly adopted low-fat milk options, likely driven by strong initial perceptions of health benefits and social influence. Following this rapid adoption, consumption stabilizes around 1250 units between steps 11 and 26, indicating that consumer behavior reaches a temporary equilibrium. During this phase, the influence of habit and social norms appears to sustain current consumption patterns without significant shifts. Between steps 27 and 32, consumption rises again, reaching a peak of approximately 1450 units. This secondary increase may reflect heightened awareness or stronger social conformity pushing agents to align more closely with emerging norms favoring low-fat milk. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of initial health messaging and social influence in driving consumer behavior, as well as the potential for sustained shifts when these factors are reinforced over time.

The mean habit factor for whole milk starts at 1.00 and increases sharply to approximately 1.02 within the first step. This rapid initial increase indicates an immediate and consistent reinforcement of existing whole milk consumption habits among the agents. Following this initial jump, the mean habit factor rises gradually and steadily from 1.02 to approximately 1.05 over the subsequent 30 steps. This continuous upward trend suggests that as long as agents continue to select whole milk, their habit strength accumulates, thereby increasing the resistance to considering alternative milk types. The low variance observed across the simulation runs implies that this reinforcement mechanism is robust and consistent across different network realizations. For decision-makers, this highlights that entrenched habits for whole milk are difficult to disrupt without a significant initial intervention to break the choice cycle, as the model shows a self-reinforcing dynamic where continued consumption strengthens the habit further.

The mean habit factor for skimmed/semi-skimmed milk begins at approximately 1.0 at the initial step, indicating minimal influence of habit at the start of the simulation. Between steps 1 and 5, the factor slightly increases, reaching about 1.02, before stabilizing near this level until step 10. This plateau suggests that early in the process, the habit mechanism for skimmed/semi-skimmed milk does not yet play a significant role in consumer decision-making. Starting around step 10, the factor begins to rise steadily, reaching approximately 1.1 by step 15, 1.3 by step 20, and ultimately peaking near 1.58 by step 32. This upward trend reflects a growing impact of habit formation over time, reinforcing the preference for skimmed/semi-skimmed milk among consumers. The consistent increase without significant deviations across simulation runs suggests that habit formation is a robust driver in the adoption of these milk types. For decision-makers, this trend implies that interventions aimed at fostering early habits around skimmed/semi-skimmed milk could accelerate their long-term adoption by strengthening consumer loyalty and reducing reliance on external influences such as social norms or environmental concerns.

The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at zero and rises sharply in the initial time steps, reaching approximately 0.5 by time step 4. This rapid increase suggests that agents quickly diverge in their evaluations of milk alternatives based on varying perceptions of health and environmental factors, influenced by initial habit and social cues. Between time steps 4 and 10, the rate of increase slows, stabilizing around a value of 0.6, likely as agents' decision-making processes become moderated by habit persistence and social influence. From time step 10 onwards, the deviation continues to rise steadily but gradually, reaching a final value near 0.75 by time step 32. This sustained upward trend indicates increasing heterogeneity in choice function scores over time, reflecting persistent differences in individual preferences and the cumulative effects of social interactions within the network. For decision-makers, this progression underscores the importance of targeting both individual-level factors (e.g., health and environmental perceptions) and social dynamics to influence milk consumption behaviors effectively.

The mean value health deviation begins at zero at the initial time step, indicating a perfect alignment between agents' perceived health values and their milk choices at the start of the simulation. Over the first 10 time steps, this metric rises rapidly, reaching approximately 0.5. This steep increase suggests that agents quickly adjust their milk preferences in response to the modeled health characteristics of whole, skimmed, and semi-skimmed milk, reflecting the model's incorporation of health as a primary driver in decision-making. From step 10 to step 25, the deviation stabilizes, fluctuating minimally around the 0.55 mark. This plateau indicates a period of equilibrium where agents' internal health valuations and their consumption behaviors reach a relative balance, moderated by habit and social influences. In the final segment, from step 25 to step 32, the mean value health deviation increases again, climbing from approximately 0.6 to a final value near 0.75. This renewed growth implies that agents continue to reassess and modify their preferences over time, likely driven by evolving perceptions of health or social norms. The steady rise without a plateau suggests that the equilibrium is dynamic rather than static, with continuous adjustments in milk choices as agents adapt to changing conditions. For decision makers, these trends highlight the importance of ongoing health messaging and social influence strategies to guide milk consumption patterns effectively. The rapid initial shift underscores the immediate impact of health information, while the later fluctuations emphasize the need for sustained interventions to maintain long-term behavior changes.

The mean value environmental deviation begins at zero at the initial time step, indicating that agents' initial milk choices were perfectly aligned with their held environmental values. This deviation increases sharply over the first five time steps, reaching approximately 12.5. This rapid increase suggests that agents quickly shifted their milk choices away from their initial environmental preferences, possibly due to the strong influence of habit or social factors overriding environmental considerations. From time step 10 onwards, the deviation stabilizes around 13.5 and remains consistent through the end of the simulation at step 32. This plateau implies that the population reached a steady state where the gap between actual choices and environmental values became constant. The convergence of multiple simulation runs indicates robustness in this outcome regardless of initial stochastic variations. For decision-makers, this persistent deviation implies that even with environmental awareness, external pressures like habit formation and social influence may dominate long-term consumption behaviors, suggesting that interventions to promote sustainable milk choices must address these deeper behavioral drivers.
