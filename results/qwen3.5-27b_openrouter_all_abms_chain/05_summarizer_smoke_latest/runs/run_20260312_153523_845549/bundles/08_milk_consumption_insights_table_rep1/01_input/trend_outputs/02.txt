The simulated trajectory of average weekly consumption for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk reveals a non-linear adoption pattern over 33 time steps. Consumption initiates at a baseline of 5.29 and undergoes a rapid expansion phase, reaching a local maximum of 1265.47 by time step 11. This initial surge indicates a high responsiveness to initial perception factors regarding health and environmental characteristics. Following this spike, the consumption metric stabilizes between time steps 12 and 24, oscillating within a range bounded by 1183.39 and 1240.35. During this interval, directional trends are absent, suggesting a temporary equilibrium where habit persistence and social conformity modulate further adoption. From time step 25, the growth trajectory resumes, culminating in a peak value of 1456.59 at time step 32. This late-stage acceleration reflects the long-term internalization of social norms and the overcoming of habitual inertia. For policy makers, the dynamics suggest that while initial market signals drive quick substitution, sustained growth requires interventions that address habit formation during stabilization periods to ensure continued transition away from incumbent whole milk products.
