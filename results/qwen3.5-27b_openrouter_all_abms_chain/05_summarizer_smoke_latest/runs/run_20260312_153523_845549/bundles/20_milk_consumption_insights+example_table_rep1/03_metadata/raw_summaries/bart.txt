 The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards . Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence .  The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk per agent initiates at 5.29 units during the first time step . This initial value aligns with the historical context of whole milk dominance in 1974 . A rapid increase occurs over the first ten time steps, driven by agents responding to perceived health characteristics and social norms .  The average weekly consumption of whole milk begins at 2654.81 units at the initial time step . This value declines steadily throughout the simulation period, reaching 253.104 units at time step 32 . For decision makers, this stable engagement pattern suggests that consumer attention to milk selection remains steady over time .  The mean habit factor applied to the skimmed and semi-skimmed milk choice function starts at 1.0000 at the initial time step . From step 5 onwards, the metric enters a phase of consistent growth, maintaining an increasing trend through step 29 . For decision makers, the persistence and growth of this habit factor imply that consumer inertia is a significant barrier to market shifts .  As the gap between health perception and actual choice widens, interventions must account for the reinforcing effects of habit and social networks that decouple consumption behavior from health motivations . Aligning social norms with health goals could help reduce this deviation . For decision makers, the rapid initial divergence and subsequent stability imply that behavioral drivers likely override environmental values in daily food choices .
