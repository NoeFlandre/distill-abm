The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. Rapid initial decline suggests early interventions targeting health perceptions are highly effective in displacing established habits. However, stabilization periods indicate sustained engagement is necessary to prevent resistance from social norms or habit inertia. The average weekly consumption of whole milk begins at 2654.81 units at the initial time step. This value declines steadily throughout the simulation period, reaching a final value of 253.104 units at time step 32. The mean habit factor for the whole milk choice function starts at 1.0000 at the initial time step and follows a consistent upward trajectory. The growth is steady with minimal fluctuation, evidenced by a standard deviation of 0.0110 across the simulation runs. This pattern indicates that the influence of habit on the incumbent milk choice strengthens rather than diminishes over time. Trend is consistently upward across all measured intervals, indicating a growing divergence between what agents perceive as healthy and what they actually choose to consume. Further changes in the trajectory appear at time steps 16, 19, and 22, suggesting periods where social influence or habit formation may have accelerated. The presence of oscillation, particularly between time steps 18 and 31, suggests periodic adjustments in agent behavior.
