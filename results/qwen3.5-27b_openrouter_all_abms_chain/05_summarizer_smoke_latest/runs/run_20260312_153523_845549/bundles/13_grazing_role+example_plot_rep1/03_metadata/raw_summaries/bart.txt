 Simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors . The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time . The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock .  The average amount of actual available green biomass across all patches begins near 45,000 units at the start of the simulation . The average total number of livestock, including healthy and destocked animals, begins at 90 across all households . Within the first 20 time steps, this value declines sharply to approximately 40.0 at the first time step, peaking sharply to around 7.5 shortly after . By time step 60 onwards, the growth rate slows, and the biomass levels off .  The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across all households starts at zero and rises sharply in the initial phase, reaching approximately 80 by step 20 . From step 60 onward, the curves diverge significantly, with some trajectories continuing to rise steadily, while others plateau around 60 to 80 . This divergence indicates that different learning strategies or behavioral types result in varying levels of livestock management .
