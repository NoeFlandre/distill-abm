The average total number of livestock, including healthy and destocked animals, begins at 90 across all households. Within the first 20 time steps, this value declines sharply to approximately 40. This rapid decrease likely reflects an initial overgrazing event where livestock levels exceed the land's carrying capacity, leading to destocking or mortality. Between time steps 40 and 60, there is a gradual increase in livestock numbers for most simulation runs, stabilizing around 50 by step 80. This recovery suggests that farmers adjusted their stocking rates in response to the initial decline, achieving a more sustainable balance. However, there is significant divergence in outcomes by the end of the simulation. Some simulations stabilize around 40, while others reach levels as high as 62. This variability indicates that different learning strategies or local conditions lead to varying degrees of sustainability. The overall trend shows an initial crash followed by a partial recovery, highlighting the challenges of maintaining livestock populations under grazing pressure and the role of farmer behavior in determining long-term outcomes.
