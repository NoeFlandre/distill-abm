Simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock. The overall mean mean biomass across the simulation period is 103,366.26 units, with a standard deviation of 16,057 units, reflecting the variability inherent in the adaptive decision-making processes and environmental stochasticity over the simulation horizon. This recovery pattern suggests that the mixture of farmer behavioral strategies, including those based on learning from own results and neighbors, eventually leads to sustainable management practices. The number of households engaging in social learning initiates at 0.0 and demonstrates a sustained increase over the 100 time steps. Growth is most pronounced in the initial phase, with a steep slope in the first decade. The rate of adoption decelerates markedly, suggesting farmers rapidly adjust herd sizes in response to initial environmental conditions. The number of E-RO households settles at 14.7 by the conclusion of the 100-timestep run. This progression illustrates an initial period of volatility and reduction in the non-learning agent cohort, which eventually transitions into a stable long-term equilibrium.
