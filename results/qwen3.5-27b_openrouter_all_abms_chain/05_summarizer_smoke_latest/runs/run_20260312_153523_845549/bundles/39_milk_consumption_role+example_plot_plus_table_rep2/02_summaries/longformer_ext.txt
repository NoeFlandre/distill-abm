Context and Goals: This agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. We aim to explore how individual perception, established habits, and social influence drive decision-making processes. Statistical analysis confirms a significant increasing trend across all rolling windows, with slopes ranging from 64.1 to 76.2 and p-values consistently at 0.027. This suggests that the accumulation of whole milk choices is driven by stable underlying mechanisms rather than random fluctuations. Inflection points detected at indices 1, 7, and 31 suggest minor shifts in the rate of growth, though no peaks or valleys are observed. The steady increase implies that, under the given parameter settings, consumer preference for whole milk remains persistent and grows cumulatively over time, likely due to the strong initial habit strength and limited susceptibility to alternative options. This suggests that consumer preference for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk is likely to continue to grow over time, as agents adapt to changing perceptions and social influences. These fluctuations indicate a phase of adjustment where consumer habits are being consolidated under the influence of established social norms and individual susceptibility. This late-stage acceleration implies a secondary wave of behavioral change, potentially driven by the cumulative effect of social networks or the overcoming of initial resistance to changing milk habits.
