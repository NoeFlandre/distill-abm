Context and Goals

This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. In this version, each farmer stays on their own plot of land. They decide how many animals to keep based on how the grass is doing and what others are doing. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. There are three main ways farmers behave: baseline (E-RO), learning from their own results (E-LBD), and learning from neighbors (E-RO-SL1). The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock.

The mean household initial risk attitude for social learning agents remains constant at 0.5 across all 100 time steps. This indicates that, within this simulation, the initial disposition to deviate from the recommended number of livestock does not change over time. Decision makers should consider that initial risk attitudes are fixed and may influence the stability of livestock management practices and pasture health outcomes.

From step 20 to step 40, the values stabilize, with slight fluctuations around the established levels. After step 40, a divergence occurs where some trajectories remain stable while others decline significantly, with values dropping as low as -0.3 by step 60. Decision-makers should consider these diverging risk profiles to tailor interventions that support sustainable grazing practices across different behavioral groups.
