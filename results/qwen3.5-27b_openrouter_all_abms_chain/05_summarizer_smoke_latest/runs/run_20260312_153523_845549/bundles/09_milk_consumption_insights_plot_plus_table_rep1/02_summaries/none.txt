Context and Goals:

The overall objective of the agent-based model is to reproduce adoption behaviours of milk consumption by the British public (1974-2005), by replicating individual preferences and decision factors. Specifically, the goal of the model is to explore the influence of perception, habits and social influence in an individual's decision-making process of milk choice. The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against observed macro level data.

In particular, the study conducts an experiment to compare the performance of two model variants in reproducing overserved milk consumption trends. These variants present different mechanisms for how agents become disposed to consider their choices, representing a threshold based, and a probability-based approach. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence, and finally, choice evaluation where agents consider the impact of their choices and may adjust their future decisions.

The agents in the model represent adult consumers who occupy a random position in an information environment. Each agent has a disposition to consider alternative milk choices. Two disposition mechanisms are tested in the model, a threshold-based approach, and a probability-based approach. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. These are computed at the initialisation of the simulation and then calculated at each time step. An agent's milk choice function is modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. Agents ascribe different relative importance to each constituent part of the choice function (health factors and environmental factors). If the disposition requirement has been met, consumption of each milk type is split proportionately by the size of each choice function, modulated by the other influences. If not, agents keep their existing choice.

Agents (n=1,000) start with an existing choice based on the dominant position of whole milk versus skimmed varieties in 1974 (start year of the data). All agents are part of a social network. Each agent in the network can sense and be influenced by the choice function of each milk alternative for other agents in their network. Links between agents are unidirectional, and influence occurs as a function of interaction probability, with the degree of influence characterised by agent susceptibility. Social norms are globally perceived by agents and impact the weightings of the choice function.

The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent exhibits a significant and consistent downward trend over the simulation period. Starting at 2654.81 units, consumption drops sharply, reaching 1020.22 units by time step 10, representing a reduction of more than 60% within this initial phase. This decline continues, though the rate slows, until time step 19 where the value stands at 773.96. Following this period, the decrease accelerates again, falling to 335.18 units by time step 30. The simulation concludes at time step 32 with a final consumption level of 253.10 units, indicating a total reduction of approximately 90% from the starting value. Statistical analysis of the series confirms a significant decreasing trend across the majority of the timeline, with the final change point identified at the last time step. These results imply that the modeled mechanisms for habit change and social influence effectively drive a substantial long-term shift away from the initial dominant product choice.

The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits an initial rapid increase from a starting value of 5.29, reaching approximately 1220 by time step 11, followed by a period of fluctuation where values oscillate between roughly 1183 and 1265 until time step 26. Subsequently, a second phase of increase occurs, driving consumption up to a final value of 1456.59 by time step 32. This trajectory suggests that agents initially adopted alternative milk options quickly, likely driven by early shifts in social influence or perception, before settling into a transitional stability where habit and social conformity balanced out new adoption. The final acceleration in consumption indicates a renewed shift in decision-making dynamics, potentially reflecting stronger social conformity or changing environmental perceptions. For decision makers, this pattern implies that interventions to promote alternative milk types might be most effective during the initial phase of change or following periods of stagnation where underlying factors are re-evaluated.

The total number of whole milk choices exhibits a consistent and monotonic increasing trend over the entire simulation period, starting from 0 at step 0 and reaching a maximum of approximately 2195 at step 32. The mean value across the simulation trajectory is 1130.88. Statistical analysis confirms this trend is strictly increasing without any intervening peaks, valleys, or oscillations, as evidenced by significant positive rolling Mann-Kendall trends throughout the timeline (p=0.027). The growth rate appears roughly linear, with calculated slopes varying only slightly between approximately 64 and 76 units per time step across different rolling windows. There is a detected change point at step 32, coinciding with the simulation's end and the maximum value. For decision-makers, this linear accumulation implies that under the current model parameters—specifically with a high initial incumbent habit of 10—agents are persistently selecting whole milk as they engage in choice evaluation, rather than rapidly switching to alternatives or reaching a saturation point.

The total number of choices for skimmed or semi-skimmed milk starts at 0 and increases steadily over the simulation steps, reaching 25643.83 by the final step. The trend is consistently upward without any detected peaks or valleys, indicating a continuous shift in consumer preference away from whole milk. The growth rate appears to stabilize after an initial period, with a mean value of 11157.35 and a median of 10679.43 across all steps. Statistical analysis confirms a significant increasing trend throughout the simulation, with no significant changes in direction. The consistent rise suggests that factors such as habit formation, social influence, and perceived health or environmental characteristics effectively drive the adoption of lower-fat milk options among the simulated population. For decision-makers, this trend implies that public health campaigns and social norms can play a critical role in shaping long-term dietary behaviors.

The total number of milk choices made by agents exhibits a consistent linear increase from 0 at the initial step to 32,000 at step 32, reflecting a constant rate of 1,000 choices per step. This trend persists across all statistical windows, indicating that the aggregate decision-making activity within the agent population remains steady over time, with each agent contributing proportionately to the overall choice volume as the simulation progresses.

The average weekly consumption of whole milk per person started at 2654.81 units at time step 0 and showed a consistent decline over the period observed. By time step 10, the value had dropped significantly to 968.40 units, indicating a sharp reduction in consumption. The decline continued, though at a slower rate, reaching 760.00 units at time step 18 and 620.98 units by time step 27. A notable decrease occurred around time step 29, where consumption fell to 323.46 units. By the final time step, 32, the consumption had reached its lowest recorded value of 253.10 units.

Inflection points were observed at several key times, including steps 5, 13, 16, 19, 24, 25, 27, and 30, suggesting shifts in the rate of decline or periods of stabilization. The Mann-Kendall test indicated a decreasing trend across most of the analyzed windows, particularly during the intervals from time steps 0-4, 5-9, 10-14, and 20-24, confirming a robust downward trajectory in consumption. However, some windows, such as 15-19 and 25-29, showed no significant trend, highlighting potential periods of fluctuation.

These trends imply that factors influencing milk choice, such as perception of health and environmental attributes, habit strength, and social influence, played critical roles in reducing whole milk consumption. Decision-makers could interpret these findings to understand how shifts in consumer perception and social dynamics may drive changes in dietary habits, suggesting targeted interventions could further influence consumer choices.

The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits three distinct behavioral phases. Initially, consumption rises sharply from a starting value of approximately 5.29 at step 0, accelerating rapidly through the first half of the simulation. This period of exponential growth is statistically significant, characterized by a steep positive slope until step 9. Around step 11, consumption reaches a preliminary local maximum of approximately 1265.47 before entering a period of consolidation. During the middle phase, spanning roughly from step 11 to step 27, the growth trend flattens into a plateau. Values oscillate within a relatively narrow band between approximately 1183 and 1265, indicating a temporary market equilibrium or saturation point for the alternative milk types. This middle phase is characterized by statistically insignificant trends, suggesting that factors driving the initial rapid adoption have temporarily stabilized. In the final phase, beginning around step 27, the consumption trend resumes its upward trajectory with high statistical significance. The average quantity increases sharply from this second plateau to reach a maximum value of 1456.59 at the final step. This secondary surge implies that agents overcame previous barriers or established new habits later in the simulation, leading to renewed and substantial market penetration of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk.

The mean habit factor for whole milk exhibits a consistent upward trend, starting at an initial value of 1.0 and gradually increasing to a final value of 1.0502. This growth is statistically significant throughout the entire observed period, characterized by a steady positive slope and a lack of any significant peaks or valleys. The trend is marked by a very sharp initial increase occurring at the second time step, reaching a value of approximately 1.02, followed by a more gradual and linear ascent for the remainder of the simulation. Minor fluctuations and potential points of inflection are observed around time steps 7 and 12, but these do not disrupt the overall positive trajectory. This pattern indicates that the initial strong preference for whole milk rapidly solidifies into a persistent habit that continues to strengthen linearly over time, reinforcing the difficulty of consumer substitution away from incumbent products.

The mean habit factor for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at 1.0 at the initial time step, reflecting the baseline influence of habit at the start of the simulation, which corresponds to 1974 when whole milk was the dominant choice. From time step 0 to approximately time step 5, the habit factor remains nearly constant, fluctuating minimally around 1.0 to 1.02, suggesting limited initial reinforcement of habit for the alternative milk types during the early simulation period. Starting around time step 10, a noticeable upward trend begins. By time step 15, the mean habit factor reaches approximately 1.25, indicating a growing influence of habit as agents increasingly incorporate skimmed or semi-skimmed milk into their regular consumption patterns. This acceleration aligns with the transition period in the historical data where consumer shifts toward lower-fat milk options gained momentum. From time step 20 to 30, the growth rate remains significant, with the habit factor rising steadily to about 1.5 by step 30. The trajectory is smooth and consistently increasing throughout this phase, without reversals or plateaus. By the final recorded time step, time step 32, the mean habit factor reaches its peak at approximately 1.59, showing that over the simulated period, the behavioral reinforcement of consuming skimmed or semi-skimmed milk becomes substantially stronger. The increase in the habit factor suggests that repeated exposure and choice consistency for alternative milk types lead to increased reliance on habitual decision-making rather than active evaluation. For decision-makers, this trend highlights the potential effectiveness of interventions that promote consistent initial trials of skimmed or semi-skimmed milk, as the resulting habit formation can significantly support long-term adoption without requiring continuous active persuasion. The model indicates that habit strength grows predictably as these alternatives become more normalized over time.

The mean percentage difference in choice function scores among agents exhibits a clear upward trend over time. Starting at 0.0000, the value rises sharply in the initial steps, reaching approximately 0.6000 by step 5. This rapid increase indicates that agents quickly begin to diverge in their evaluations of milk choices as they process information influenced by habit, social networks, and perceived attributes. After this initial phase, the rate of increase slows down, continuing to rise gradually to reach a final value of 0.7508 by step 32. The presence of inflection points at steps 7, 12, and 28 suggests shifts in the dynamics of how agents update their preferences, likely linked to evolving social influences or habit adjustments. The trend remains consistently increasing across all analyzed periods, indicating a growing polarization or differentiation in milk preferences among the simulated population. For decision makers, this implies that interventions aimed at altering milk consumption patterns should account for increasing heterogeneity in consumer preferences over time, necessitating targeted strategies rather than one-size-fits-all approaches.

The mean deviation between agents’ held health values and the implied health values by their milk choices begins at 0.0 at the start of the simulation and exhibits a consistent, overall increasing trend, reaching a final value of approximately 0.73. During the initial phase, specifically between time steps 0 and 4, the metric rises sharply with a calculated slope of 0.063, indicating a rapid divergence between agents' internal health valuations and the health implications of their current milk consumption. This rapid growth continues through time step 9, where the rate of increase moderates slightly to a slope of 0.041. Between time steps 10 and 19, the growth rate slows considerably, with slopes dropping to 0.012 and subsequently 0.003, suggesting a period of relative stability where agents' choices and health perceptions are in closer alignment. However, from time step 20 onwards, the trend accelerates again, culminating in a final surge to 0.73 at time step 32. This sustained upward trajectory and the presence of multiple inflection points imply that as the simulation progresses, agents increasingly experience cognitive dissonance regarding the health attributes of their milk choices, suggesting that habitual persistence or social influences may be preventing agents from fully aligning their consumption behaviors with their personal health priorities.

The mean deviation between agents’ held environmental values and the implied environmental values from their milk choices begins at 0.0 at step 0, rises rapidly during the initial phase, reaches a peak of 13.4756 at step 13, and then remains relatively stable with minor fluctuations until step 32, where it ends at 13.4186. The increase between steps 0 and 13 indicates a significant divergence between perceived environmental values and actual choices during early adaptation, likely reflecting agents adjusting to new milk options or social influences. After step 13, the deviation stabilizes around 13.4, suggesting that agents’ choices have largely aligned with their environmental value perceptions within the constraints of habit, social influence, and choice functions. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms significant upward trends in the first two time windows (steps 0-4 and 5-9), followed by periods of no statistically significant trend, highlighting a rapid initial adjustment phase followed by behavioral stabilization. This pattern implies that while environmental considerations influence milk choice, long-term decisions are moderated by entrenched habits and social dynamics.
