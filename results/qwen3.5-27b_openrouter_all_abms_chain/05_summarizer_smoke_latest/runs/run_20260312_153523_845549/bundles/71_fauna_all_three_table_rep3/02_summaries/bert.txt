The megafaunal hunting pressure model (mhpm) is an interactive, agent - based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival. Context : the model simulates a socio - ecological system containing human foragers and sexually dimorphic megaherbivore herds. It incorporates principles of optimal foraging theory, where hunters make decisions based on energy costs and food value. Goals : the primary goal is to determine conditions under which human hunting pressure, combined with environmental constraints, leads to the extinction of megafauna. The number of active human hunter - gatherer bands starts at 20 and remains constant at 20 throughout the simulation duration. There is no variation in this metric, with the standard deviation recorded at zero across the entire timeline. This absolute stability reflects the parameter setting where foragers are immortal and initialized without demographic change mechanisms, ensuring a static hunting pressure baseline. For decision makers, this indicates that extinction drivers in this specific scenario are independent of human population growth, suggesting that conservation or mitigation strategies should prioritize environmental carrying capacity or foraging efficiency rather than human band size. The transition from significant growth to no trend observed after tick 1, 159 suggests that the surviving population becomes too sparse to sustain high encounter rates with foragers. For decision makers, these trends highlight a high - risk scenario where the configured hunting pressure (20 foragers) combined with natural mortality leads to near - total elimination of the initial megaherbivore stock within approximately five years (1, 876 ticks at 365 ticks per year). Statistical analysis of the simulation runs reveals a precipitous and monotonic collapse in this demographic cohort. There is a brief interval (steps 1392 – 1623) where a statistically significant but minor upward fluctuation is recorded, yet this is insufficient to reverse the terminal trend. This deceleration suggests a shift to a low - density persistence phase where the reduced prey density decreases encounter efficiency or highlights the struggle of the remaining individuals against environmental stochasticity. For decision makers, these dynamics highlight that extinction trajectories in this system are not always linear ; early rapid depletion is often followed by a prolonged period of low - density persistence that can misleadingly suggest stability. Conservation strategies focusing solely on initial population abundance may fail if they do not account for this prolonged extinction vortex, where even minor increases in hunting pressure or environmental degradation can prevent recovery once the population drops below a critical threshold.
