The average amount of reserve biomass starts at approximately 90,000 units across all patches. Following this, from approximately time step 30 to 60, there is a significant and rapid increase in reserve biomass, reaching values between 110,000 and 130,000 units. This sharp growth indicates a period of substantial resource recovery, likely due to the behavioral adjustments made by the agents in response to the observed conditions. By time step 60, the reserve biomass levels begin to stabilize, with most lines converging and showing minimal fluctuation up to the end of the simulation at time step 100. This stabilization suggests that the system has reached a new equilibrium where resource usage and regeneration are balanced. The observed trend implies that the learning capabilities of the agents, particularly the E-LBD and E-RO-SL1 types, have led to more sustainable grazing practices over time. Decision-makers can infer that fostering learning and adaptive strategies among agents can significantly improve resource management and ecological outcomes in similar grazing systems.
