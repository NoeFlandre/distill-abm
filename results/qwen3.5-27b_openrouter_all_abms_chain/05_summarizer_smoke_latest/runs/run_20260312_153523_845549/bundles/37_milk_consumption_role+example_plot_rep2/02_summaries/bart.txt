The agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005. The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties. Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms. The total number of milk choices made by all agents begins at zero at time step 0 and increases linearly. By step 5, the cumulative choices reach approximately 1,000. The rate of increase accelerates noticeably between steps 5 and 15, with cumulative choices rising to around 7,500 by step 15. This period reflects a significant shift in consumer preferences, possibly driven by heightened social influence and evolving health perceptions. The mean percentage difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents begins at 0.0 at time step 0. The trend across all simulation runs remains highly consistent, with minimal variance between them throughout the entire period. The mean deviation between agents' held environmental values and the implied values by their milk choices begins at zero and shows a rapid increase.
