The total energy of all animals begins at approximately 25,000 units and experiences a rapid decline over the first 500 time steps, dropping to around 5,000 units. This sharp decrease likely reflects the initial impact of human foraging pressure combined with environmental constraints, as the foragers exploit available prey populations efficiently. Around 500 time steps, a brief stabilization or minor fluctuation in energy levels occurs, possibly due to temporary reductions in hunting pressure or localized resource availability. Following this, the decline continues at a slower rate until approximately 1,000 time steps, where the total energy approaches near-zero levels. After this point, the system stabilizes with negligible energy remaining, indicating the effective extinction of the megaherbivore population. The consistency of this trend across multiple simulation runs suggests that under the given parameters, human hunting pressure and environmental factors are sufficient to drive megaherbivore populations to extinction. For decision makers, this highlights the critical threshold at which human predation, even at moderate levels, can lead to irreversible ecological consequences when combined with seasonal variability and limited prey reproduction rates.
