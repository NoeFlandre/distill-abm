The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk begins at 5.29 units per person at the start of the simulation. A rapid and steep increase occurs over the first four time steps, reaching an inflection point around time step 4 where consumption values exceed 600 units. This initial surge suggests early adoption mechanisms rapidly destabilized the initial equilibrium of whole milk dominance. The mean value across all steps is 16,000, with a standard deviation of 9,521.90. There are no detected peaks, valleys, or inflection points in the series. This consistent growth suggests that decision-making mechanisms driving agents to consider and adjust their milk choices remain uniformly active throughout the simulation period. The mean difference between the maximum and minimum choice function scores among agents starts at 0.0 at time step 0 and shows a rapid increase, reaching approximately 0.55 by time step 5. This initial surge suggests a quick divergence in agent preferences, likely driven by the early activation of disposition mechanisms and the immediate influence of social networks and habit formation.
