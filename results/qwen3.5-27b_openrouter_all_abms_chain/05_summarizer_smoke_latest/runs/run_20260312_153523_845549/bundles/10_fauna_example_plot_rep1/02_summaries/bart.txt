Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based model designed to conduct experiments to test megaherbivore extinction hypotheses. The model considers environmental change, human hunting behavior, herd demography, herd movement, animal sexual dimorphism, and animal life history. Hunters follow dynamic optimal foraging theory, balancing energy status, prey value, density, and costs. Ungulates are sexually dimorphic, reproduce, age, and move as herds The cumulative number of postpartum female prey animals hunted begins at zero and shows a rapid increase during the initial 300 steps. From steps 300 to 1000, the rate of deaths slows, with the cumulative number reaching approximately 225. Variability among simulation runs is noticeable throughout the timeline, highlighting the robustness of the underlying dynamics. The number of prey animals in the simulation begins at approximately 120 and declines rapidly over the first 500 time steps. The rate of decline slows after 500 steps, indicating a reduction in mortality or predation pressure. By around 1000 steps, the number of female animals drops to near zero, suggesting that the population has reached a critical point where reproductive rates cannot sustain the herd. The percentage of green patches in the simulation begins near 33% at the initial time step. By approximately step 20, this percentage decreases slightly to stabilize around 32%. A sharp decline occurs around step 380, where the percentage of vegetation coverage drops precipitously to a minimum value near 23%. Following this depletion, the vegetation coverage recovers rapidly, returning to the baseline of approximately 33% by step 600.
