The cumulative number of male prey animals killed starts at zero and rises sharply, reaching approximately 110 to 120 individuals by step 600 across all simulation runs. This rapid increase suggests that, during the initial phase, hunters actively target male prey, likely due to their higher energy value compared to females. The rate of male kills slows significantly after step 600, with the cumulative count plateauing between 105 and 125 for most simulations. This leveling off may indicate that the available male population has been reduced to a point where hunting males becomes less efficient or unsustainable for the forager bands. By step 2000, most simulations show no further increase in the cumulative count, implying that hunting pressure on males has stabilized or that the remaining male population is either too sparse or too costly to hunt under the given socio-natural conditions. Variability among simulation runs is notable, especially after step 600, suggesting that factors such as herd density, seasonal changes, or individual hunting strategies introduce differences in the long-term hunting dynamics.
