Across the 100-timestep simulation period, the average number of livestock destocked per household exhibits a distinct non-stationary pattern. The metric begins at 0.0000 at time step 0. A sharp peak occurs early, reaching a global maximum of 6.6144 at time step 3. Following this surge, the metric declines generally, with diminishing secondary peaks observed at time step 9 (value 3.3733) and time step 12 (value 2.7406). By the final time step 100, the value has settled at a low level of 0.3111.

Statistical evaluation reveals significant decreasing trends during the initial phase, particularly within the first 32 time steps, supported by rolling Mann-Kendall tests with p < 0.05 in multiple early windows. Between time steps 33 and 43, the trend becomes statistically insignificant, suggesting a transition to stability. While a minor increasing trend is detectable between steps 66 and 76, the absolute values remain low relative to the initial peak.

From a management perspective, the initial spike suggests a necessary period of rapid adjustment or destocking to align herd sizes with forage availability. The subsequent sustained decline toward near-zero values indicates that the system reaches an equilibrium where livestock numbers are better balanced with the carrying capacity, minimizing the need for forced removals.
