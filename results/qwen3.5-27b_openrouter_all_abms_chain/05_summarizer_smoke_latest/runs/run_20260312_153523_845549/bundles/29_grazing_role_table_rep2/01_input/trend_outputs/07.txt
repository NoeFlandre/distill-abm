The Gini coefficient representing inequality in total healthy livestock demonstrates a consistent upward trajectory across the 100 timesteps of the simulation period. The metric commences at a value effectively zero at the initial timestep and rises to a maximum of 0.0726 by the final timestep. Statistical analysis indicates a robust positive trend, with rolling Mann-Kendall tests confirming significant increases across all decadal windows, evidenced by Tau values approaching 1.000 and p-values at 0.000. Although the progression is predominantly monotonic, the rate of change is not uniform, characterized by multiple inflection points occurring between timesteps 8 and 57. Significant structural shifts in the trend are detected at change points located at timesteps 51, 71, and 100. Additionally, a phase of oscillation is identified between timesteps 78 and 93, suggesting temporary fluctuations in the rate of inequality accumulation during the final quarter of the simulation. The overall mean value for the coefficient is 0.0302. In the context of the grazing model, this sustained increase implies that the distribution of healthy livestock among the 60 households becomes progressively less equal over time. Initially, livestock health is uniformly distributed, but divergent management outcomes lead to stratification. By the end of the simulation, the variation in household success suggests that factors such as learning styles, risk exposure, or stochastic environmental interactions result in some farmers maintaining healthier herds than others, driving the measured inequality upward.
