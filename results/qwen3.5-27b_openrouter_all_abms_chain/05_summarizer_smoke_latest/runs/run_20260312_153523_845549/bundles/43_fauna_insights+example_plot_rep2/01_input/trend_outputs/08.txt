The number of male prey animals in the Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model starts at an initial value of approximately 125. Over the first 500 time steps, this value declines rapidly, with most runs showing a reduction to near 20 individuals. This sharp decrease is likely due to the combined pressures of high hunting activity, which aligns with the foragers' optimal foraging strategy targeting high-value male prey, and internal mortality. Between 500 and 1000 time steps, the decline continues, though at a slower rate, as the remaining population becomes more dispersed or harder to target. By 1500 steps, the number of male prey animals stabilizes at very low levels, often approaching zero across multiple simulation runs. This suggests that under the given parameter settings, male prey populations are highly vulnerable to extinction pressures. For decision-makers, this indicates that strategies aimed at preserving male megafauna must account for high hunting pressures and mortality rates. Conservation efforts might need to focus on reducing hunting intensity or enhancing habitat carrying capacity to buffer against such rapid declines.
