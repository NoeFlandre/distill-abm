 This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors . The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time . The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock .  The average total number of livestock across all households begins at 90.0000 at the start of the simulation . A pronounced decline occurs over the first 37 time steps, reducing the average stock to a minimum of 41.0089 . This significant reduction represents a decrease of approximately 54 percent from the initial stock level . By time step 100, the average total livestock count reaches 47.5472 .  The inequality in the distribution of healthy livestock across all households begins at zero at the first time step, indicating an initial state of perfect equity where every household starts with an identical number of animals . The number of households engaging in social learning initiates at zero and exhibits a strong upward trajectory throughout the simulation . For decision makers, this trend highlights the importance of preparing for initial herd losses when establishing grazing systems under variable rainfall conditions .  The population of households adhering to the E-RO strategy initiates at 20.1 . An early fluctuation occurs within the first 10 time steps, where the count rises slightly to a maximum of 21.1 at time step 8 before reversing direction . From time step 11 onwards, a statistically significant downward trend dominates the system dynamics .
