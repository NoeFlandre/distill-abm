The average reserve biomass begins at 90,000 units and undergoes a significant reduction during the early stages of the simulation. This decline continues until time step 26, where the metric reaches its lowest point of approximately 84,114 units. Statistical tests confirm a strong decreasing trend during the first 21 time steps, reflecting an initial period of grazing pressure or system adjustment as households manage their livestock. Following this valley, the biomass stabilizes briefly before initiating a recovery phase.

Starting from time step 33, the system enters a period of consistent growth that lasts until the simulation concludes. This increasing trend is statistically significant across all subsequent 10-step windows analyzed between steps 33 and 98. By time step 100, the reserve biomass achieves its maximum value of approximately 121,073 units. An inflection point around time step 48 marks the transition into this sustained accumulation phase.

These trends indicate that the initial deployment of diverse learning behaviors among the farmers leads to temporary vegetation stress. However, the robust recovery suggests that the adaptive strategies, including learning from experience and neighbors, eventually optimize herd sizes relative to the carrying capacity. For decision makers, this highlights the importance of allowing an adjustment period where short-term biomass depletion may occur, as the long-term outcome supports enhanced pasture health and resilience through adaptive management practices.
