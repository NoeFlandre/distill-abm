 The agent-based model simulates the purchasing behavior of British adults regarding milk consumption from 1974 to 2005 . The primary goal is to replicate the historical shift from whole milk to skimmed and semi-skimmed varieties . Each agent evaluates milk choices based on perceived health and environmental characteristics . Decisions are influenced by habit strength, the likelihood of interacting with others, and susceptibility to social norms .  The simulation output tracks the mean quantity of whole milk consumed by agents over 33 time steps, corresponding to the period from 1974 to 2005 . The trajectory reflects the enduring influence of established habits despite shifting social and health norms . The rate of growth shows a gradual deceleration, with the slope declining from 70.808 in the earliest period to 64.067 in the final period .  The consumption metric for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk demonstrates a pronounced upward trajectory throughout the simulation period . At the initial time step, the value stands at 5.29, indicating low initial market penetration driven by strong incumbent habits . The simulation results illustrate a macro-level transition driven by individual decision-making processes under influence of social norms and perceived health benefits .  The initial zero deviation reflects a population anchored by strong incumbent habits . As the simulation progresses, the increasing divergence suggests that agents are increasingly differentiating their evaluations based on social susceptibility, habit thresholds, and cognitive dissonance mechanisms . The rise to 0.7508 by the final step implies that by the end of the period, the population has fragmented into distinct segments .
