The average household risk attitude exhibits a distinct three-phase trajectory over the 100-timestep simulation period. Initially, the metric begins at a low value of 0.0074. From the start until timestep 33, there is a significant upward trend, with the risk attitude increasing steadily. Statistical analysis confirms this phase as a significant increasing trend across the first three rolling windows, culminating in a peak value of 0.3814 at timestep 33. Several local peaks are observed around this maximum, with values remaining high between timesteps 21 and 37, hovering above 0.35. Following the peak at timestep 33, the metric enters a period of decline. From approximately timestep 33 to timestep 65, the average risk attitude decreases significantly. Rolling Mann-Kendall tests for this period show strong negative trends, with Tau values reaching -0.964. By timestep 64, the value drops to approximately 0.1913. In the final phase, from timestep 66 to the end of the simulation at timestep 100, the metric stabilizes. The rolling trend tests indicate no significant trend during this interval. The final value at timestep 100 is 0.1911, which is consistent with the stabilized level observed from timestep 64 onwards. In the context of the grazing model, this pattern suggests an initial period of experimentation where farmers adjust their risk perceptions regarding livestock management, possibly due to the introduction of different learning strategies such as E-RO and E-LBD. The subsequent decline indicates a convergence in risk attitudes as farmers gain experience or align with neighbors, leading to more consistent decision-making regarding herd sizes. The stabilization in the later years implies that the system has reached a dynamic equilibrium where risk attitudes remain relatively constant, balancing the extension recommendations with on-farm learning outcomes.
