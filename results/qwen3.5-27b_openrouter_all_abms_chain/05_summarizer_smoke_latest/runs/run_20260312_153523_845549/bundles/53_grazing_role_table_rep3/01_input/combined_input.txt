Context and Goals

This simulation extends the RAGE model to study how smallholder farmers manage grazing land when they can learn from their own experience or from neighbors. In this version, each farmer stays on their own plot of land. They decide how many animals to keep based on how the grass is doing and what others are doing. The goal is to see how different learning styles affect the health of the pasture and the farmers' success over time. There are three main ways farmers behave: baseline (E-RO), learning from their own results (E-LBD), and learning from neighbors (E-RO-SL1). The model runs for a set number of years to track changes in grass and livestock.

Over the course of the 100-step simulation, the average initial risk attitude of the farming households remains entirely constant at a mean value of 0.0074. Statistical analysis reveals zero variance and no significant trends across the entire 101-sample duration. Within the context of the grazing management model, this indicates that the risk attitude variable functions strictly as a static initial condition assigned to agents at the start of the run. Despite the dynamic environment involving variable rainfall, grass growth, and varying social learning strategies (E-RO, E-LBD, E-RO-SL1), this specific metric does not adapt or evolve. It confirms that while livestock decisions and pasture health are subject to feedback loops, the underlying initial risk disposition for this population remains a fixed attribute rather than a state-dependent variable.
