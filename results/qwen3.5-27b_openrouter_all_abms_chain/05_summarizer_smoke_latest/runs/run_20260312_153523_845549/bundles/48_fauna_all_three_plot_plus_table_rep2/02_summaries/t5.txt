The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape. It simulates a socio-ecological system containing human foragers and sexually dimorphic megaherbivore herds. A dominant oscillation period of 63.0 steps is detected in the data. The cumulative number of male prey killed by foragers increases sharply from 0 to approximately 100 over the first 500 time steps. This initial surge likely reflects the high availability of adult males. The mean value across all simulations settles at approximately 102.4, while the median reaches 116.9, suggesting a slight skew in individual run outcomes. The rolling Mann-Kendall analysis confirms a significant increasing trend up to step 1623 followed by a statistically insignificant plateau and a minor decreasing trend in the final phase. The population reaches absolute extinction (zero individuals) by approximately time step 2300. The population is effectively extinct for the remaining duration of the simulation. This pattern indicates that the combined pressures of human hunting and environmental constraints rapidly exceeded the reproductive capacity of the prey population. The total energy available in all prey animals begins at approximately 16,667 units. This precipitous drop occurs primarily within the first 500 time steps. The energy metric decreases by more than 90%, suggesting a system-wide failure of the prey population to maintain biomass.
