The simulation output demonstrates a pronounced and sustained reduction in the average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent across the modeled timeframe. Initial consumption at index 0 was recorded at 2654.81 units, establishing a high baseline consistent with historical 1974 preferences. By index 32, consumption had fallen to 253.10 units, indicating a near-complete substitution of whole milk with other varieties by the end of the period.

Trend analysis confirms a statistically significant decreasing trajectory during the initial phases. Between indices 0 and 14, the trend remained consistently downward, with steepest slopes observed between indices 5 and 9. However, the rate of decline exhibited variability in later stages. Periods between indices 15 and 19 and again between 25 and 29 showed intervals where the trend was not statistically significant, suggesting temporary stabilization or fluctuation in adoption rates. Despite these pauses, the overall direction remained negative.

Specific local fluctuations punctuated the general decline. Local peaks occurred at index 19 (773.96 units), index 27 (620.98 units), and index 30 (335.18 units). Conversely, local minima were identified at index 18 (760.00 units), index 26 (594.35 units), and index 29 (323.46 units). These oscillations align with a dominant periodicity of approximately 15 time steps, suggesting cyclical adjustments in consumer behavior driven by the interaction of habit, social influence, and perception. Multiple inflection points, including indices 5, 13, 16, 19, 24, 25, 27, and 30, mark shifts in the momentum of consumption change.

The substantial drop from the maximum value of 2654.81 units to the minimum of 253.10 units reflects the model's capacity to replicate the historical shift in milk preferences. For decision makers, the persistent downward trend underscores the strength of the transition away from whole milk, likely driven by entrenched social norms and health perceptions. The periods of stabilization indicate that while the long-term trend is clear, short-term consumption levels can resist change due to habit thresholds. Strategic interventions would need to account for these cyclical dynamics and the high initial inertia of established consumption habits to effectively influence future choices.
