 The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards . The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person . Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives .  The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits an initial rapid increase from a starting value of 5.29 to 1220 by time step 10 . The Mann-Kendall test confirms a significant decreasing trend in the early and late stages of the simulation, while the middle phase (time steps 15-19 and 25-29) shows periods where trend is statistically less distinct .  The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits three distinct behavioral phases . The data indicates a robust downward trend throughout the entire period, with statistical significance in the initial and final phases of decline, suggesting a strong shift in preference away from whole milk . For decision makers, this implies that the substitution of whole milk for skim and skimmed varieties is a highly probable outcome .  The mean deviation between agents’ held environmental values and the implied environmental values from their milk choices begins at 0.0 at step 0, rises rapidly during the initial phase . The metric exhibits a dominant oscillation period of 15.0, with observed cycles around steps 5–9, 16–20, and 26–30 . This pattern suggests an initial and rapid phase of cognitive dissonance as agents, initially committed to whole milk, encounter environmental trade-offs in their choices .
