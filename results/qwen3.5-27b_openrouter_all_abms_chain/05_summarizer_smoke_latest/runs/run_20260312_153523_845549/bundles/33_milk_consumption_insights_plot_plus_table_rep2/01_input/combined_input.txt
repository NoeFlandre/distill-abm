Context and Goals:

The overall objective of the agent-based model is to reproduce adoption behaviours of milk consumption by the British public (1974-2005), by replicating individual preferences and decision factors. Specifically, the goal of the model is to explore the influence of perception, habits and social influence in an individual's decision-making process of milk choice. The model looks to replicate the substitution of whole milk for skimmed and semi-skimmed milk from the 1970s onwards. The main outcome reported here is the average weekly consumption of each milk type per person. The simulation uses both a theoretical grounding and empirical data to inform the ABM, with calibration performed against observed macro level data.

In particular, the study conducts an experiment to compare the performance of two model variants in reproducing overserved milk consumption trends. These variants present different mechanisms for how agents become disposed to consider their choices, representing a threshold based, and a probability-based approach. Decision-making follows a basic structure of: agent perception of choice characteristics, the triggering, or not, of disposition to consider alternatives, a set of scored choice functions made up of the perceived characteristics and modulated by habit and social influence, and finally, choice evaluation where agents consider the impact of their choices and may adjust their future decisions.

The agents in the model represent adult consumers who occupy a random position in an information environment. Each agent has a disposition to consider alternative milk choices. Two disposition mechanisms are tested in the model, a threshold-based approach, and a probability-based approach. Each agent makes a choice of milk selection based on a function for each alternative, made up of the perceived health and environmental characterises of each choice. These are computed at the initialisation of the simulation and then calculated at each time step. An agent's milk choice function is modified by habit, social influence, and evaluation of previous choices. Agents ascribe different relative importance to each constituent part of the choice function (health factors and environmental factors). If the disposition requirement has been met, consumption of each milk type is split proportionately by the size of each choice function, modulated by the other influences. If not, agents keep their existing choice.

Agents (n=1,000) start with an existing choice based on the dominant position of whole milk versus skimmed varieties in 1974 (start year of the data). All agents are part of a social network. Each agent in the network can sense and be influenced by the choice function of each milk alternative for other agents in their network. Links between agents are unidirectional, and influence occurs as a function of interaction probability, with the degree of influence characterised by agent susceptibility. Social norms are globally perceived by agents and impact the weightings of the choice function.

The average weekly consumption of whole milk per agent begins at approximately 2,655 units at the start of the simulation. A steep and rapid decline is observed immediately, with consumption dropping to 1,221 units by time step 5 and further reducing to 964 units by time step 10. This initial phase characterizes a substantial shift in consumption behavior. The rate of decline slows significantly after time step 10, transitioning into a period of gradual reduction. Between time steps 10 and 25, consumption falls from 964 to 667 units. The data indicates a temporary plateau or stagnation around time step 18, where consumption levels are 760 units, before resuming a downward trajectory. The final phase of the simulation, from time step 25 onwards, shows a sharp drop in consumption, decreasing from 667 units to a final value of 253 units at time step 32. Statistical analysis identifies inflection points at time steps 5, 13, 16, and 19, signaling transitions in the rate of decline. The Mann-Kendall test confirms a significant decreasing trend in the early and late stages of the simulation, while the middle phase (time steps 15-19 and 25-29) shows periods where the trend is statistically less distinct. The overall pattern reflects a rapid substitution of whole milk in the early period, a long period of gradual decline, and a final significant reduction in consumption.

The average weekly consumption of skimmed and semi-skimmed milk exhibits an initial rapid increase from a starting value of 5.29, reaching approximately 1220 by time step 10. A period of fluctuation and relative stability occurs between time steps 11 and 26, where values hover around 1200, with specific local peaks at time steps 11 (1265.47) and 13 (1240.35), and a local minimum at time step 18 (1183.39). A secondary phase of growth initiates after time step 26, culminating in a final value of 1456.59 at time step 32. This trajectory indicates that the transition from whole milk is not linear but occurs in phases, suggesting that initial adoption is rapid, followed by a period of social consolidation or habit reinforcement before a final surge in consumption driven by sustained social influence or changing norms.

The metric tracking the total instances of agents choosing whole milk exhibits a consistent and uninterrupted upward trajectory across the entire simulation duration. Starting from an initial baseline of 0 at time step 0, the value rises to a mean of approximately 1130 by the midpoint of the simulation. By the final time step 32, the metric reaches a maximum value of 2194.57. Statistical analysis confirms a robust positive trend throughout the 33 time steps, with no significant periods of decline or stagnation observed. While the overall progression is linear, statistical inflection points are noted at time steps 1, 7, and 31, suggesting subtle variations in the rate of accumulation. The data shows a high degree of consistency in the trend, characterized by an average slope of roughly 65 to 75 units per time step across different rolling windows. Minor oscillations are detected near the end of the simulation, specifically between steps 27 and 30. This pattern indicates a steady cumulative growth in the selection of whole milk, implying that under the specified parameters—specifically a high initial habit strength for the incumbent and moderate social influence—the mechanism for switching to alternative milk types is insufficient to displace whole milk consumption within this timeframe. For decision-makers, this suggests that relying solely on the modeled levels of social susceptibility and environmental perception is not enough to drive market shifts away from established habits; significantly stronger interventions or different disposition mechanisms may be required to alter the adoption trajectory.
