RAGE model is a multi-agent simulation model that captures feedbacks between pastures, livestock and household livelihood in a common property grazing system. It implements three stylized household behavioral types (traditional TRAD, maximizer MAX and satisficer SAT) These types can be compared regarding their long-term social-ecological consequences. Simulation tracks the average available green biomass across the pasture patches over 100 timesteps. During the first phase, spanning from the start to approximately timestep 26, the system experiences a significant decline in biomass condition. Initial low deviation suggests baseline adherence to extension guidance. System demonstrates resilience, eventually surpassing initial biomass levels, suggesting that policies supporting these learning behaviors can lead to improved ecological outcomes. The simulation dynamics display a pronounced initial decline in average herd size, starting from 90.00 head at step 0 and bottoming out at 41.01 head by step 37. By the final timestep 100, the available green biomass reaches its peak value of 60535, substantially exceeding the initial conditions. For policy and land management, this trend indicates that learning-enabled households tend to self-regulate towards lower stocking levels. The average cumulative number of livestock destocked across households begins at 0.0 and rises monotonically to 96.78 by timestep 100. Multiple inflection points detected at time steps 7, 28, 30, 33, 38, 57, and 60, indicating phases of varying adoption rates before stabilization. This trajectory indicates that social learning strategies successfully propagate through the agent population.
