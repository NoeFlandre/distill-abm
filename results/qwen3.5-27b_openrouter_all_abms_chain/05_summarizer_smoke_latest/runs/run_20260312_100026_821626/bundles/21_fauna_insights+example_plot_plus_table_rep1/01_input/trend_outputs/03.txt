The cumulative number of male prey killed by foragers increases sharply from 0 to approximately 100 over the first 500 time steps, averaging a rise of about 0.2 animals per step. This initial surge likely reflects the high density of available prey early in the simulation and the efficient foraging behavior of the agent bands under optimal conditions. The rate of accumulation slows significantly between steps 500 and 1000, with the average increase dropping to 0.03 per step, indicating that the most accessible male prey have been depleted. By step 1200, the number of male kills stabilizes around 118 to 120, with only minor fluctuations observed thereafter. The final cumulative count reaches approximately 117.5 across runs, suggesting that the forager populations have exhausted the viable male prey base or that hunting pressure is being offset by low prey encounter rates. This pattern implies that under the given parameters, the model system reaches a steady state where further increases in male mortality are negligible. Decision makers should note that hunting pressure is most intense early on and diminishes rapidly as prey availability declines, which could be a critical factor when assessing long-term sustainability of prey populations.
