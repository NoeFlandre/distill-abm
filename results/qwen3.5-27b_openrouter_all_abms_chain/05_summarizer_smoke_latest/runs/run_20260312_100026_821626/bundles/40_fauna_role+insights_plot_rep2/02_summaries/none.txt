The Megafaunal Hunting Pressure Model (MHPM) is an interactive, agent-based simulation designed to test hypotheses regarding megaherbivore extinction. It allows users to simulate a prehistoric landscape to observe how environmental factors and human predation interact to influence population survival. The model runs on a 211x211 patch matrix representing a toroidal landscape.

Context:
The model simulates a socio-ecological system containing human foragers and sexually dimorphic megaherbivore herds. It incorporates principles of optimal foraging theory, where hunters make decisions based on energy costs and food value. The environment is dynamic, featuring grass growth and seasonal changes.

Goals:
The primary goal is to determine conditions under which human hunting pressure, combined with environmental constraints, leads to the extinction of megafauna. By manipulating parameters, users can test scenarios of environmental degradation and predation intensity.

This steady state indicates that, under the current model parameters, the human population neither grows nor declines. For decision makers, this implies that the initial conditions and parameters set for forager dynamics, such as movement cost, food storage, and immortality, create a balanced scenario where human population pressure is static and does not escalate over time, potentially stabilizing the pressure on megaherbivore populations.

The total energy of foragers exhibits a consistent downward trend, starting from an initial value close to 0 and decreasing steadily over time. By approximately step 2000, the total energy reaches values near -35,000. This decline reflects a continuous energy deficit for the forager bands, likely due to high movement costs, processing costs, and insufficient hunting success relative to energy expenditure. The linear and pronounced decrease suggests a lack of compensatory mechanisms, such as improved hunting efficiency or increased food availability, to offset the energy costs. For decision-makers, this indicates that the current parameter settings result in unsustainable foraging conditions, emphasizing the need to adjust parameters such as food value, hunting efficiency, or movement costs to achieve a viable energy balance.

The cumulative number of male prey animals hunted and killed by forager bands shows a consistent upward trend across all simulation runs. Initially, the number of males killed increases steadily, with the rate of increase being higher in the early stages. By approximately 500 steps, the cumulative number of males killed reaches around 60 in most simulations. Between 500 and 1000 steps, the rate of increase slows, and the cumulative number reaches approximately 110. After 1000 steps, the growth rate further decelerates, with most simulations plateauing between 110 and 125 males killed by 2000 steps. The variation in the final values among simulations suggests differences in specific environmental or interaction dynamics within each run. For decision makers, this pattern indicates that initial hunting pressure is high but diminishes as the simulation progresses, likely reflecting the depletion of easily accessible prey or changes in forager behavior over time.
